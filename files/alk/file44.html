    Abstract

    ecto-5′-Nucleotidase (eN, CD73) catalyzes the hydrolysis of extracellular AMP to adenosine. eN inhibitors have potential for use as cancer therapeutics. The eN inhibitor α,β-methylene-ADP (AOPCP, adenosine-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid]) was used as a lead structure, and derivatives modified in various positions were prepared. Products were tested at rat recombinant eN. 6-(Ar)alkylamino substitution led to the largest improvement in potency. N6-Monosubstitution was superior to symmetrical N6,N6-disubstitution. The most potent inhibitors were N6-(4-chlorobenzyl)- (10l, PSB-12441, Ki 7.23 nM), N6-phenylethyl- (10h, PSB-12425, Ki 8.04 nM), and N6-benzyl-adenosine-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] (10g, PSB-12379, Ki 9.03 nM). Replacement of the 6-NH group in 10g by O (10q, PSB-12431) or S (10r, PSB-12553) yielded equally potent inhibitors (10q, 9.20 nM; 10r, 9.50 nM). Selected compounds investigated at the human enzyme did not show species differences; they displayed high selectivity versus other ecto-nucleotidases and ADP-activated P2Y receptors. Moreover, high metabolic stability was observed. These compounds represent the most potent eN inhibitors described to date.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    ecto-5′-Nucleotidase (ecto-5′-NT, eN, CD73, EC 3.1.3.5) is a member of the group of ecto-nucleotidases that dephosphorylate extracellular nucleotides.(1)eN catalyzes the dephosphorylation of nucleoside monophosphates, and its main substrate is AMP.(1, 2) Other members of the membrane-bound group of ecto-nucleotidases are the nucleoside triphosphate diphosphohydrolases (NTPDases; subtypes 1–3 and 8), the nucleotide pyrophosphatase/phosphodiesterases (NPPs 1–4), and the alkaline phosphatases (APs; tissue nonspecific, intestinal, placental, and germ cell).(3-8) NTPDases and NPPs are ATP- and ADP-hydrolyzing ecto-nucleotidases that reduce the concentration of extracellular ATP and/or ADP and thus the activation of P2X and P2Y receptors by these nucleotides.(6)eN elevates extracellular concentrations of adenosine.(7) The membrane-bound vertebrate enzyme hydrolyzes 5′-AMP to adenosine and phosphate but cannot hydrolyze adenosine 2′- and 3′-monophosphates. The hydrolysis of 5′-AMP is stereoselective, i.e., only the d-enantiomer is a substrate.(1, 8)
    eN is a Zn2+-binding glycosylphosphatidylinositol- (GPI-) anchored protein, with its catalytic domain facing the extracellular medium. It can be cleaved off the anchor and released as a soluble protein.(7) Eukaryotic eN functions as a noncovalent homodimer with two structural domains: the N- and C-terminal domains are connected by a small hinge region.(9, 10) This hinge region enables the enzyme to undergo large domain movements and switches between the open and closed conformations.(9) Recent studies on eN knockout mice and eN overexpressing tissues have provided evidence for possible therapeutic applications of eN inhibitors under various pathological conditions.(11-13)eN and adenosine A2A receptors have been found to be coexpressed in various tissues and can thereby activate the receptors in a concerted action.(9) 2-Substituted AMP derivatives, which serve as eN substrates and are converted to A2A adenosine receptor agonists, can serve as site-specific A2A agonist prodrugs that are targeted to sites of elevated eN expression, e.g., under inflammatory conditions.(12, 13)
    eN inhibitors have potential applications as novel therapeutics for melanomas as well as lung, prostate, and breast cancers.(14-16)eN favors tumor cell growth by inhibiting T cell activity and promoting angiogenesis.(17, 18) It has been shown that inhibition of eN with a monoclonal antibody, siRNA, or small molecules delays tumor growth and metastasis.(19, 20) The same effects were observed in eN knockout mice due to decreased adenosine production.(21) High eN expression has been reported in triple-negative breast cancers (TNBC), and it has also been demonstrated that targeted blockade of CD73 significantly prolonged the survival in anthracycline-resistant animal models of cancer.(16)
    Inhibitors of eN, therefore, have potential for use as novel drugs for cancer therapy.(15-18)eN inhibitors reduce extracellular adenosine levels, resulting in an indirect blockade of adenosine (P1) receptor activation. In contrast to direct receptor–ligand interaction, enzyme inhibitors are indirect antagonists that exhibit site- and event-specific effects since they are active only in the presence of enzyme, substrate, and receptor. ADP and ATP are competitive inhibitors of the enzyme, with inhibition constants in the micromolar range.(1, 6, 8) However, these inhibitors are not ideal, as they themselves are subjected to enzymatic degradation by NTPDases, NPPs, and alkaline phosphatases.(20, 21) Adenosine-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] or [{5-(6-aminopurin-9-yl)-3,4-dihydroxyoxolan-2-yl}methoxyhydroxyphosphoryl]methylphosphonic acid (I, α,β-methylene-ADP, AOPCP), a more stable analogue of ADP, is one of the most potent competitive inhibitors of eN known to date.(22) In addition to this nucleotide analogue, only anthraquinones,(24) sulfonamides,(25) various polyphenols,(26) and some polyoxometalates (POMs)(27) are currently known to potently inhibit eN. Among them, the most potent inhibitor is the anthraquinone derivative IV (Figure 1).(24) Anthraquinones and sulfonamides were found to display a competitive mechanism of inhibition, whereas polyphenols and POMs were shown to be noncompetitive inhibitors.
    The number of structure–activity relationship studies (SARs) of eN inhibitors is very limited. We therefore started a program to develop potent eN inhibitors based on nucleotide as well as non-nucleotide scaffolds. However, our efforts to improve the potency of non-nucleotide-based eN inhibitors have so far been met with limited success.(24, 25) Thus, in the present study, we chose the competitive inhibitor AOPCP as a lead structure, for which, to the best of our knowledge, no SARs have been described. AOPCP is the most potent and selective eN inhibitor known to date.(22, 23) It has a much higher metabolic stability as compared to that of the physiological eN inhibitors ADP and ATP due to replacement of the di- (or tri-) phosphate by a methylenediphosphonate residue.(8, 24, 25) AOPCP derivatives and analogues will be deprotonated at physiologic pH values and display high water solubility, making them suitable for in vivo target validation studies and also for parenteral application in cancer therapy. Moreover, the crystal structure of AOPCP in complex with the human enzyme has been published (PDB: 4H2I) and provides information for the design of derivatives and analogues with improved potency.(9) Moreover, we designed and synthesized compounds that were expected to exhibit high metabolic stability and will not be metabolized to adenosine receptor agonists.

    Chemistry

    For the preparation of AOPCP derivatives and analogues 9a–d and 10a–w substituted at N6, O6, S6, and C-8, a convergent synthetic strategy was applied. It started with the synthesis of the intermediate nucleosides (6a–n, 6p–r, 6w, 6v, 7a–d, 8b, 8c), which were subsequently reacted with methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) in trimethyl phosphate (see Schemes 1–3; for more details, see the Supporting Information). Disubstituted derivative 10x was synthesized from intermediate nucleoside 8d by reaction with methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) (see Scheme 4). Further derivatives (12a–c) with substitution in the methylenebisphosphonate side chain of AOPCP were synthesized by nucleophilic displacement of 2′,3′-O-isopropylidene-5′-O-tosyl-adenosine (11b) with tris(tetra-n-butylammonium) salts of substituted bisphosphonic acids (see Scheme 5).
    For the preparation of N6-substituted intermediate nucleosides (6a–n), 6-chloropurine riboside was used, which was synthesized from inosine according to a reported procedure(28, 29) with minor modifications (Scheme 1). The chlorination of the keto function at the 6-position of inosine requires protection at the 2′-, 3′-, and 5′-hydroxyl groups, as they are all susceptible to chlorination. There are several reported protecting groups, such as acetyl or benzoyl residues. We chose acetyl groups for protection, as they can be conveniently removed under mild alkaline conditions. Protection of the 2′-, 3′-, and 5′-hydroxyl groups of inosine (1) was carried out using acetic anhydride and pyridine. Chlorination of protected derivative 3 was achieved using phosphorus oxychloride in the presence of N,N-dimethylaniline.(28) Subsequent deprotection was performed using a solution of 7 N ammonia in methanol for 24 h, yielding 4 in a good yield of 70%.
    N6-substituted adenosine derivatives 6a–n were obtained by reaction of 6-chloropurine riboside (4) with N-monoalkylamine–HCl, monoar(alk)ylamine, N,N-dialkylamine–HCl, or N,N-diar(alk)ylamine, respectively.(30, 31) Phosphorylation in the 5′-position of nucleosides proceeds without side-product formation at the 2′- and 3′-positions if the 2′- and 3′-hydroxyl groups are protected. Frequently, the nucleosides are treated with p-methoxybenzaldehyde in tetrahydrofuran (THF) in the presence of zinc chloride, yielding acetal-protected nucleosides.(32) However, protection of the N6-substituted nucleosides with p-methoxybenzdehyde did not work in our hands. Therefore, we introduced the classical 2′,3′-O-isopropylidene protecting group using acetone and 2,2-dimethoxypropane under strong acidic conditions to give nucleosides 7a–d.(33)
    For the preparation of nucleoside-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid], there are several commonly used multistep methods: (i) the protected nucleoside may be reacted with activated bisphosphonates, e.g., (4-nitrophenyl)ethyl phosphonates,(33, 34) (ii) the protected nucleoside may be reacted with phosphonates via a Mitsunobu reaction,(35) (iii) the protected nucleoside may be esterified with phosphonic acid in trichloroacetonitrile in the presence of dicyclohexylcarbodiimide, or (iv) the protected nucleoside-5′-sulfonyl ester may be prepared followed by nucleophilic substitution with alkylammonium salts of bisphosphonic acid.(36) All of these methods utilize protected nucleosides and afford products only in low yields. In the early 2000s, two separate groups reported the preparation of nucleoside-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid](37, 38) by phosphorylation of nucleosides with methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) followed by hydrolysis with TEAC buffer (triethylammonium hydrogen carbonate).(39) The formation of the nucleoside phosphonate proceeds via intermediates (i.e., nucleoside-O-[{chloro(methyl)phosphoryl}phosphonic dichloride]); in addition to 5′-phosphonylation, there is an equal risk of reaction at the 2′- and 3′-positions. Apart from this, there is also a high probability of the formation of nucleoside-3′,5′-cyclomethylenebis(phosphonic acid) and dinucleoside-bisphosphonic acid derivatives. The formation of many byproducts will complicate the purification process. Therefore, we decided to start from the 2′,3′-O-isopropylidene-protected nucleosides to prevent 2′- and 3′-phosphonylation and the formation of cyclic phosphonates. To increase the yield of nucleoside-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] formation using protected nucleosides, we optimized: (i) the amount of methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) used during the reaction and (ii) the reaction time. Optimized reaction conditions included the use of 5 equiv of methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) and a reaction time of 30 min, followed by hydrolysis with TEAC, producing only nucleoside-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] as a single product. (Scheme 1; for detailed information on the optimization of the reaction, see the Supporting Information). Protected nucleotides 9a–d were deprotected by trifluoroacetic acid to give nucleotides 10a–d in good yields. While the initial phosphonylation reactions were carried out using protected nucleosides, we were able to employ the same optimized reaction conditions with unprotected nucleosides 6e–n, yielding nucleoside-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] 10e–n as the main product. Nucleotide 10o was synthesized from inosine (1). All final compounds were purified by anion exchange chromatography and/or reverse-phase C18 HPLC.
    For the synthesis of 6-(ar)alkoxy-substituted purine nucleoside derivatives (6p, 6q), 6-chloropurine-β-d-riboside (4) was reacted with sodium (ar)alkoxide in the corresponding (ar)alkyl alcohol according to a reported procedure(40, 41) (Scheme 2). The 6-benzylthio-substituted derivative (6r) was synthesized from 6-chloropurine riboside (4), first converting it into 6-thiopurine-riboside(29, 42) followed by reaction with benzyl chloride. 6-Chloro-, 6-(ar)alkoxy-, and 6-benzylthio-purine ribosides were phosphonylated as described above by reaction with methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) followed by hydrolysis with TEAC (Scheme 2). The optimized reaction conditions yielded mainly the desired products, which were purified by reverse-phase C18 HPLC.
    For the synthesis of 8-substituted derivatives (10t–w), commercially available adenosine (8a) was brominated using bromine–water solution according to a reported method to give 8-bromoadenosine (8b).(43) 8-Thioadenosine was prepared from 8-bromoadenosine (8b) by reaction with thiourea and was used without further purification.(29, 42) 8-Chloroadenosine (8c) was prepared from 8-thioadenosine by reaction with N-chlorosuccinimide (Scheme 3). 8-Methylaminoadenosine (6v) was synthesized from 8-chloroadenosine by treating it with methylamine.(44, 45) 8-Thioadenosine was reacted with ethyl iodide to give 8-ethylthioadenosine (6w).(46) These 8-substituted adenosine derivatives were subsequently reacted by applying the earlier optimized method using 5 equiv of methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) to give nucleoside 5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] 10t–w as the final products (Scheme 3). The final phosphorylation reaction yielded mainly the desired products with very low amounts of side products. The products were purified by anion exchange chromatography followed by reverse-phase C18 HPLC. The yields of 8-substituted nucleotides were low compared with those of 6-substituted nucleotides (for details, see the Supporting Information).
    Disubstituted nucleotide derivative 10x was synthesized by reaction of nucleoside 8d with methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride), applying the previously optimized reaction conditions (Scheme 4).
    The reactions for the synthesis of methylene diphosphonate side-chain-modified analogues (12a–c) were also started from the commercially available adenosine (8a) (Scheme 5). Adenosine was protected on the 2′- and 3′-hydroxyl groups to give 2′-,3′-O-isopropylidene-adenosine (11a) followed by tosylation at the 5′-position to yield intermediate 11b. Substituted bisphosphonic acid derivatives were converted to the corresponding tris(tetra-n-butylammonium)salts by trituration with tetra-n-butylammonium hydroxide. Then, tris(tetra-n-butylammonium) dibromomethylenebis(phosphonic acid), tris(tetra-n-butylammonium) dichloromethylenebis(phosphonic acid), or tris(tetra-n-butylammonium)-1-hydroxyethane-1,1-diyldiphosphonic acid, respectively, was reacted with 2′-,3′-O-isopropylidene-adenosine 5′-O-tosylate (11b), providing phosphonylated intermediates 11c–e. After purification of the intermediates, they were subsequently deprotected by treatment with 6–8% trifluoroacetic acid in dichloromethane to obtain desired products 12a–c.(34-36) All diphosphate side-chain-substituted nucleotides were purified by anion exchange chromatography followed by reverse-phase C18 HPLC. Product 12c was obtained as a mixture of diastereomers, which was not separated.

    Pharmacological Evaluation

    The compounds’ potency to inhibit eN was determined by a radiometric eN assay using [3H]AMP as a substrate, which was separated from the enzymatic product [3H]adenosine by precipitation with lanthanum chloride followed by filtration through glass fiber filters.(22) Recombinant rat eN expressed in Sf9 insect cells was employed.(47) For all compounds, full concentration–response curves were determined in at least three separate experiments using 10 different concentrations. Ki values were calculated from the obtained IC50 values using the Cheng–Prusoff equation.(48, 49) Results are summarized in Tables 1–4, and selected concentration–inhibition curves are shown in Figure 2.
    For two of the most potent compounds, inhibitory potency at human eN was additionally determined using a radiometric assay based on separation of substrate and products by thin-layer chromatography (TLC).(50) Measurements were performed on recombinant human enzyme and also on native eN present in human blood serum (see below).(51)

    Structure–Activity Relationships

    A series of 31 derivatives (compounds 9a–d, 10a–x, and 12a–c) was evaluated for inhibition of rat eN (see Tables 1–4).
    The lead compound, AOPCP (I), a competitive inhibitor of rat eN, showed a Ki value of 197 nM, determined under the same conditions. N6-substitution generally increased potency of lead structure I. Monosubstitution displayed the following rank order of potency: 2-phenylethyl (10h, Ki 8.04 nM) ∼ benzyl (10g, 9.03 nM) > phenyl (10f, 36.8 nM) ≥ ethyl (10b, 43.8 nM) > methyl (10a, 104 nM) > H (I, 197 nM). These results showed that large, hydrophobic N6-substituents were beneficial for high inhibitory potency at eN. N6-disubstitution was also tolerated. Among the symmetrically disubstituted derivatives, the N,N-diethyl-substituted compound (10d, 68.0 nM) was slightly superior to the dimethyl derivative 10c (86.0 nM) and the dibenzyl-substituted compound 10j (92.5 nM). However, the N6-monoethyl derivative was superior to the diethyl-substituted compound (compare 10b/10d), and the difference was even more pronounced for the larger N6-monobenzyl-substituted derivative 10g in comparison with the N6-dibenzyl derivative 10j (ca. 10-fold difference). These results indicate that only one large N6 substituent was well-tolerated, but two voluminous residues at the exocyclic N atom were not. In contrast, in the case of N6-methyl substitution, the N6-disubstituted derivative 10c was even somewhat more potent than the monomethyl-substituted compound 10a, indicating that a second small substituent at N6 was well-tolerated and might even increase potency. We therefore prepared compounds with two different N6-substituents, 10e and 10i. In fact, combination of an ethyl and a methyl substituent at N6 (10e) increased potency, as compared to the monoethyl-substituted compound 10b, by about 2-fold (10e, 23.6 nM). In the case of 10i, in which a benzyl residue was combined with an ethyl substituent at N6, a large drop in affinity was observed in comparison with the monobenzyl-substituted derivative 10g (10i, 76.4 nM; 10g, 9.03 nM). These results indicate that disubstitution at N6- may increase the compounds’ potency and that, although one of the N6-substituents may be large, the size of the second substituent is more limited and should preferably be a methyl group.
    Since N6-benzyl-AOPCP 10g had so far been one of the most potent derivatives, we investigated compounds with various para-substituted benzyl residues (10k, 10l, 10m, 10n). The following rank order of potency for N6-substituents was observed: p-chlorobenzyl (10l, 7.23 nM) ≥ benzyl (10g, 9.03 nM) > p-sulfamoylbenzyl (10n, 14.2 nM) ≥ p-nitrobenzyl (10m, 17.6 nM) > 6-aminobenzyl (10k, 29.0 nM). Para-substituents were tolerated but did not lead to a significant increase in inhibitory potency.
    To rationalize the effects of the N6-substituents, we studied possible interactions of the substituents by molecular modeling based on the crystal structure of human eN in complex with AOPCP in the closed conformation (PDB: 4H2I). Rat and human eN display 90% sequence identity, and their active sites differ in only one amino acid, F500, which is involved in nucleotide base stacking in human eN is replaced by a tyrosine residue in the rat enzyme. In the human eN crystal structure, the terminal phosphate group of AOPCP is coordinated by two zinc ions and the nucleobase is sandwiched via π-stacking interactions between the side chains of F417 and F500. In addition, a hydrogen-bonding network with water molecules and side chains of the N- and C-terminal domains further stabilizes the interaction.(9)
    The 6-amino group (N6) of AOPCP points toward the solvent and is not involved in any interaction with the protein or water molecules.(9) Therefore, small as well as large substituents are not expected to interfere with adjacent residues important for binding of AOPCP. Residues L184, S185, N186, D121, and N122 of the N-terminal domain of eN are at a distance of 4–11 Å to the N6 and thus constitute potential interaction partners for the N6 substituents. Figure 3 (left) shows the modeled binding mode of the most potent compound, 10l. While the overall binding mode of the AOPCP scaffold was stable in the energy minimization, further hydrophobic interactions might be formed by the benzyl group with the backbone atoms of L184 and S185 of the N-terminal domain. This also applies to other voluminous, but more flexible, hydrophobic substituents that might interact additionally with D121, N122, or N186 (Figure 3). Furthermore, para substituents at the aromatic ring (10k–n) may result in polar interactions with the carbonyl oxygen of S185, the side chain of N186, or water molecules. Smaller hydrophobic groups at N6 (compounds 9a–10e) also result in increased binding affinity, probably via hydrophobic interactions with F417 and F500. The modeled binding mode of 10j shows that both aromatic rings at N6 have enough space without causing steric clashes but that only one benzyl group is involved in favorable hydrophobic interactions with the protein (Figure 3, right ).
    We also tested a few compounds that were modified at the ribose moiety, 2′,3′-isopropylidene-substituted nucleotides 9a–d (Table 1), which had been obtained as intermediate products during the preparation of 10a–d. The isopropylidene-substituted compounds were 5–37-fold less potent than their unsubstituted counterparts. Unsubstituted 2′,3′-hydroxyl groups appear to be important, since they form hydrogen bonds with aspartate and arginine residues in the substrate binding site of the enzyme, as evidenced by crystal structures (PDB: 4H2I).(9, 19, 24, 25) The isopropylidene-substituted nucleotides showed a different rank order of potency with regard to the N6 substitution than that observed with the compounds with free hydroxyl groups in the 2′- and 3′-positions. This indicates different binding modes of the 2′,3′-isopropylidene-substituted nucleotides as compared to that for unsubstituted nucleotides. The most potent compound among the isopropylidene-substituted nucleotides was N6-ethyl-derivative 9c (365 nM), which was 8-fold weaker than its analogue 10b (43.8 nM).

    Analogues of the very potent 6-benzylamino-substituted derivative 10g in which the 6-amino group (N6H) was replaced by an oxygen (10q) or a sulfur atom (10r) were also investigated. Both compounds were equally potent as their N6 analogue. This offers the possibility to move away from adenine nucleotide derivatives, which might be metabolized to (N6-substituted) adenosine derivatives after hydrolysis of the ester bond between the ribose and the diphosphonate moiety. Certain N6-substituted adenosine derivatives are known to potently activate adenosine A1 receptors and may thereby induce negative inotropic and chronotropic effects, which might even lead to cardiac arrest.(52, 53)
    Next, we investigated derivatives of lead structure I bearing substituents in the 8-position. The following rank order of potency was observed: Cl (10u, 93.5 nM) > H (I, 197 nM) > Br (10t, 491 nM) > NHMe (10v, 1720 nM) > SEt (10w, 3610 nM) (Table 3). These results showed that only small substituents were tolerated at the purine 8-position. The smaller chlorine atom increased potency of I by 2-fold, but the larger bromine atom already led to a significant reduction.

    To further investigate and explain the influence of substituents in the 8-position, a model for the binding mode of 10w was generated (Figure 4). Substituents in the 8-position result in close contacts of the substituent with the terminal phosphate group in the eN–AOPCP complex structure. The coordination of the phosphate group by zinc ions plays a decisive role for the inhibitory binding mode of AOPCP to eN.(9) Thus, small shifts in the position of the terminal phosphate probably result in a loss of inhibitory potency for larger substituents such as thioethyl (10w). Only for the smaller Cl substituent might minor shifts of the nucleobase be sufficient to compensate for the close contact (10u). Possibly, halogen bonding interactions of the Cl atom with the phosphate oxygens contribute to the improved affinity of 10u. Due to their larger size, the bromo (10t) and thioethyl substituents (10w) likely result in steric clashes with the phosphate oxygens. Another possible explanation for our findings may be that larger 8-substituents can induce a conformational change from the anti- to the syn-conformation around the nucleosidic bond, which is unfavorable for binding to the enzyme.(54, 55)
    In order to further study the effect of 8-substitution, we synthesized hybrid molecule 10x, which combines an N6-benzyl substitution (one of the best substituents at the 6-position) with a bromo-substitution at the 8-position (a substituent that had decreased potency in AOPCP). For 10x, a Ki value of 20 nM was observed, which can be compared to the corresponding 8-unsubstituted N6-benzyl derivative (10g, 9.03 nM). A 2-fold decrease in potency was observed for 10x as compared to 10g, which was similar to the 2-fold decrease for 8-bromo derivative 10t as compared to AOPCP (I).
    Moreover, some derivatives with substitution in the methylenediphosphonate side chain of AOPCP were synthesized and investigated. Adenosine-5′-dibromomethylenediphosphonic acid (12a), adenosine-5′-dichloromethylenediphosphonic acid (12b), and adenosine-5′-(1-hydroxy)ethane-1,1-diphosphonic acid (12c) were tested under same assay conditions. All substitutions resulted in a reduction of potency as compared to that of AOPCP; however, 12b was more potent than 12a and 12c (Table 4). An analysis of the environment of substituents at the methylene bridge of AOPCP shows that both positions have a very polar environment (Figure 5). One position is in hydrogen-bonding distance to a zinc-coordinated water molecule, but it is at quite a short distance to the carbonyl group of H243 (1.71 Å distance when the substituent is OH, before energy minimization). This would require some rearrangements in the positions of the phosphate groups. Substituents larger than OH cannot be accepted at this position. The second substituent atom is within hydrogen-bonding distance to water molecules. Thus, a methyl group is not a suitable substituent at the methylene bridge of AOPCP. A single hydroxyl group or a hydroxyl group and a second polar substituent may be more favorable.

    Selectivity

    Lead structure I and selected derivatives were investigated for inhibition of further ecto-nucleotidases, including human NTPDase 1–3 and human NPP 1–3. Compound I showed a moderate inhibition of NPP 1 (Ki 16 500 nM),(57) but otherwise we observed no inhibition by I or any of the selected derivatives, 10e, 10g, 10h, 10l, and 10r, at a high test concentration of 10 μM at any of the ecto-nucleotidases (see Supporting Information, page S20). Furthermore, the ADP analogues were investigated on ADP-activated G protein-coupled receptors, namely, P2Y1 and P2Y12 receptors. None of the selected compounds (10b, 10e, 10g, 10l) activated or inhibited P2Y1 or P2Y12 receptors at the high concentration of 10 μM (see Supporting Information, page S22).

    Metabolic Stability

    The most potent and selective eN inhibitors, 10g and 10l, as well as the lead compound, AOPCP, were further investigated for their stability in rat liver microsomes in order to investigate potential metabolic degradation by liver enzymes. Compound 10g and AOPCP (but not compound 10l owing to its lower stability as compared to that of 10g) were further investigated for their stability in human blood plasma. The experiments were performed as described in the Experimental Section. Samples were incubated at 37 °C and subsequently analyzed by HPLC coupled to electrospray ionization mass spectroscopy (HPLC-ESI-MS).
    Incubation of the compounds with rat liver microsomes showed that 10g and 10l were quite stable. Only a small percentage (<23%) of 10g was metabolized under the applied conditions (see Experimental Section), whereas >77% of the compounds was recovered unchanged after a long incubation for 8 h (Figure 7). Compound 10g was somewhat less stable, and 56% was metabolized within 8 h. Hydrolytic cleavage of the glycosidic bond, which is a typical phase I reaction of nucleosides and nucleotides, was observed as the main reaction. Under same conditions, AOPCP was cleaved much faster, and hydrolysis was complete within 15 min.
    Incubation of the compounds in human blood plasma showed that inhibitor 10g was highly stable. Only a negligible percentage was metabolized under the applied conditions (Figure 7) during an incubation that lasted for 5 h. Again, AOPCP was significantly less stable, and ca. 50% of the compound was degraded under the same conditions.

    A series of 31 nucleotide derivatives was synthesized by multistep reactions via intermediate nucleosides and obtained in good yields and high purity. The AOPCP-derived nucleotides were modified at the N6-, C8-, and both positions of the adenine moiety of AOPCP and/or substituted at the methylene group of the side chain. The synthesized nucleotides were evaluated as inhibitors of eN. Analysis of structure–activity relationships allowed them to be optimized, leading to the development of inhibitors with Ki values in the low nanomolar range. In particular, N6-benzyl, N6-(2-phenylethyl), N6-(4-chlorobenzyl), and O6-benzyl substitution increased potency. The most potent inhibitors were N6-(4-chlorobenzyl)- (10l, PSB-12441, Ki 7.23 nM), N6-phenylethyl- (10h, PSB-12425, Ki 8.04 nM), and N6-benzyl-adenosine-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] (10g, PSB-12379, Ki 9.03 nM). Replacement of the 6-NH group by O (e.g., 10q, PSB-12431) or S (10r, PSB-12553), both analogues of 10g, yielded equally potent inhibitors (10q, 9.20 nM; 10r, 9.50 nM). Selected compounds tested on human eN did not show species differences. The compounds displayed high selectivity versus other ecto-nucleotidases (NTPDase 1–3 and NPP 1–3) and ADP-activated P2Y (P2Y1 and P2Y12) receptors. They represent the most potent eN inhibitors known to date, showing high selectivity and metabolic stability. Thus, they are likely to become useful pharmacological tools to further elucidate the enzyme’s (patho)physiological role and its potential as a drug target.

    General

    All reagents were commercially obtained from various producers (Acros, Aldrich, Fluka, Merck, and Sigma) and used without further purification. The purity of all compounds including starting material was greater than 95%. Commercial solvents of specific reagent grades were used, without additional purification or drying. The reactions were monitored by thin-layer chromatography (TLC) using aluminum sheets with silica gel 60 F254 (Merck) and using dichloromethane/methanol (9:1, 5:1, or 3:1) and n-butanol/acetic acid/water (2:1:1) as the mobile phase. Column chromatography was carried out with silica gel 0.060–0.200 mm, pore diameter ca. 6 nm. Mass spectra were recorded on an API 2000 (Applied Biosystems, Darmstadt, Germany) mass spectrometer (turbo ion spray ion source) coupled with a Waters HPLC system (Agilent 1100) using a Phenomenex Luna 3 μ C18 column. The LC-MS samples were prepared by dissolving 1 mg/mL of compound in H2O/MeOH (1:1) containing 2 mM ammonium acetate. A sample of 10 μL was injected into an HPLC instrument, and elution was performed with a gradient of water/methanol (containing 2 mM ammonium acetate) from 90:10 to 0:100 for 20 min at a flow rate of 250 μL/min. UV absorption was detected from 190 to 400 nm using a diode array detector. 1H, 31P, and 13C NMR spectra were performed on Bruker Avance 500 and 600 MHz spectrometers. DMSO-d6, MeOD-d4, or D2O was used as solvent. 31P NMR spectra were recorded at room temperature; orthophosphoric acid (85%) was used as an external standard. Shifts are given in ppm relative to the external standard (in 31P NMR) or relative to the remaining protons of the deuterated solvents used as internal standard (1H, 13C NMR). Melting points were determined on a Buchi 530 melting point apparatus and are uncorrected. For the microwave reactions, a CEM-focused microwave synthesis Discover apparatus was used. For lyophilization, a freeze dryer (CHRIST ALPHA 1-4 LSC), for HPLC purification, a Knauer Smartline 1050 HPLC system, and for ion exchange chromatography, a FPLC instrument from Amersham Biosciences were used, respectively.

    Preparation of Triethylammonium Hydrogen Carbonate Buffer (TEAC)

    A 1 M solution of TEAC was prepared by adding dry ice slowly to a 1 M triethylamine solution in water for several hours until a pH of approximately 7.4–7.6 was indicated using a pH meter.

    Purification of Nucleotides

    Ion Exchange Chromatography

    The crude nucleoside-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] derivatives were purified by ion exchange chromatography on an FPLC instrument (AKTA FPLC, from Amersham Biosciences) with an XK 26 mm × 20 cm length column (Pharmacia). The column was packed with Sephadex DEAE (diethylaminoethyl) A-25 gel, HCO3– form, swelled in a 1 M solution of TEAC at 4 °C for 48 h. Before running purification, the column was washed and equilibrated with deionized water. The sample was prepared by dissolving crude product in 5 mL of aqueous triethylammonium hydrogen carbonate buffer. Separation was achieved by running a solvent gradient of 0–900 mM TEAC buffer using approximately 3000 mL of solvent to elute the nucleotides. The UV absorption was detected at 254 nm. Fractions were collected, and appropriate fractions were pooled, diluted in water, and lyophilized.

    Preparative HPLC

    Lyophilized nucleoside-5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] was dissolved in 5 mL of deionized water and injected into a RP-HPLC column (Knauer 20 mm i.d., Eurospher-100 C18). The column was eluted with a solvent gradient of 0–65% acetonitrile in 50 mM aqueous NH4HCO3 buffer for 40 min at a flow rate of 10 mL/min. The UV absorption was detected at 254 nm. Fractions were collected, and appropriate fractions were pooled, diluted with water, and lyophilized several times to remove the NH4HCO3 buffer, yielding the nucleotides as white powders.

All of these compounds, with two exceptions (inosine derivative 10o and dichloromethylene derivative 12b), are new compounds that have not been previously described in the literature. Compounds 10o and 12b have previously been described, but no detailed analytical characterization and purity measurement for these compounds have been reported.     General Procedure for the Synthesis of Nucleotides 9a–d

    A solution of methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) (2 mmol) in trimethyl phosphate (2 mL), cooled to 0 °C, was added to a suspension of corresponding N6-substituted nucleosides 7a–d (1 mmol) in trimethyl phosphate at 0 °C. The reaction mixture was stirred at 0 °C, and samples were withdrawn at 10 min intervals to check for the disappearance of nucleosides by TLC. After 1 h, following the disappearance of the nucleosides, 7 mL of cold 0.5 M aqueous TEAC solution (pH 7.4–7.6) was added. The mixture was stirred at 0 °C for 15 min followed by stirring at rt for 1 h. Trimethyl phosphate was extracted using (2 × 100 mL) tert-butyl methyl ether, and the aqueous layer was lyophilized. The mixture of nucleotide and dinucleotide was separated by ion exchange chromatography on DEAE Sephadex (A-25, HCO–3 form) using a linear gradient of aqueous TEAC. Fractions containing the product were pooled and evaporated to dryness, with ethanol added repeatedly to remove TEAC buffer. The compound was then purified by RP-HPLC using a gradient of 50 mM ammoniumbicarbonate/ACN from 100:0 to 40:60, and suitable fractions were pooled and lyophilized to obtain the final product as a glassy solid.

    General Procedure for the Synthesis of Nucleotides 10a–d

    2′,3′-Isopropylidene-nucleoside 5′-O-[(phosphonomethyl)phosphonic acid] 9a–d (100 mg) was dissolved in 4.5 mL of dichloromethane; then, 0.5 mL of water and 0.65 mL of trifluoroacetic acid was added to it. The mixture was stirred at room temperature for 3 h. After completion of the reaction, the mixture was evaporated, and the solid was precipitated by adding diethyl ether. Then, the crude solid product was dissolved in 6 mL of water and 0.6 mL of methanol. It was then purified by RP-HPLC using a gradient of H2O/MeOH from 100:0 to 0:100, and, finally, the appropriate fractions were pooled and lyophilized to obtain the final product.

    General Procedure for the Synthesis of Nucleotides 10e–x

    A solution of methylenebis(phosphonic dichloride) (5 mmol) in trimethyl phosphate (2 mL), cooled to 0 °C, was added to a suspension of corresponding nucleosides 6e–x (1 mmol) in trimethyl phosphate at 0 °C. The reaction mixture was stirred at 0 °C, and samples were withdrawn at 10 min intervals to check for the disappearance of nucleosides by TLC. After 30 min, following disappearance of the nucleosides, 7 mL of cold 0.5 M aqueous TEAC solution (pH 7.4–7.6) was added. The mixture was stirred at 0 °C for 15 min, followed by stirring at room temperature for 1 h. Trimethyl phosphate was extracted using (2 × 100 mL) tert-butyl methyl ether, and the aqueous layer was lyophilized. The crude product was then purified by ion exchange chromatography and/or RP-HPLC using a gradient of 50 mM ammoniumbicarbonate/ACN from 100:0 to 60:40 to obtain the final product.

    Procedure for the Synthesis of Nucleotides 12a–c

    Phosphorylating reagents, tris(tetra-n-butylammonium)-dibromomethylenebis(phosphonic acid), tris(tetra-n-butylammonium)-dichloromethylenebis(phosphonic acid), and tris(tetra-n-butylammonium)-1-hydroxyethane-1,1-diyldiphosphonic acid, were prepared by adding dibromomethylenediphosphonic acid, clodronic acid, and etidronic acid dissolved in 25 mL of methanol to a tetra-n-butylammonium hydroxide solution in water (10 mL), followed by evaporation of methanol and lyophilization of the resulting solution.     Pharmacological Assays

    Rat ecto-5′-Nucleotidase Assay

    The assay was performed as previously described(46) using recombinant rat eN expressed in Sf9 insect cells.(47)
    The assay was performed by adding 10 μL of compound solution to 70 μL of assay buffer (25 mM Tris, 140 mM sodium chloride, and 50 mM sodium phosphate, pH 7.4), 10 μL of [3H]AMP (Hartmann Analytic, Germany) at a final substrate concentration of 5 μM, and 10 μL of the enzyme solution (final concentration 0.3 ng/μL). The mixture was incubated for 25 min at 37 °C. The reaction was stopped by adding 500 μL of ice-cooled precipitation buffer containing 100 mM aqueous lanthanum chloride and 100 mM aqueous sodium acetate solution (pH 4.0). After 30 min, the precipitate was filtered through GF/B glass fiber filters (M-48, Brandel, Gaitherburg, MD, USA). After washing three times, 4 mL of the scintillation cocktail ULTIMA Gold XR was added, and then the signal quantified by scintillation counting (TRICARB 2900 TR, Packard/PerkinElmer). Three separate inhibition assays were performed in triplicate, concentration–response curves were fitted, and Ki values were calculated.

    Human ecto-5′-Nucleotidase Assays

    Blood was taken from healthy volunteers by venipuncture and allowed to clot at room temperature for 30 min. Blood samples were centrifuged for 15 min at 2000g followed by freezing of the serum at −70 °C.
    Serum samples (4–7 μL), or recombinant human eN (purified as previously described)(51) were incubated for 1 h at 37 °C. The enzyme assays were carried out in a final volume of 80 μL of RPMI-1640 medium containing 5 mM β-glycerophosphate and 40 μM unlabeled AMP containing tracer [2-3H]AMP (18.6 Ci/mmol; Quotient Bioresearch, GE Healthcare) as a substrate. The products were separated by TLC on Alugram SIL G/UV254 plates (Machery-Nagel, Düren, Germany) (eluent: 1-butanol 1.5 equiv, isoamylalcohol 1 equiv, diethylene glycol monoethyl ether 3 equiv, ammonia solution 1.5 equiv, pure water 2.5 equiv) and quantified by scintillation β-counting as previously described.(50) Concentration–inhibition curves were performed in 2–4 separate experiments.

    Molecular Modeling

    The program Molecular Operating Environment (MOE)(58) was used for the molecular modeling calculations. Starting from the crystal structure of AOPCP complexed to human eN (PDB: 4H2I),(9) the substituents were added in low-energy conformations that did not exhibit strong clashes with the protein or the rest of the AOPCP ligand. For energy minimization, the structures were prepared as follows: water molecules that showed close contacts to the introduced substituents were excluded from the model. Protons were added via the Protonate 3D option at pH 7.4 and 310 K. The ligand and protein residues within an 8 Å sphere around the ligand were subjected to energy minimization in the MMFF94 force field. The atoms in the 8 Å sphere were restrained to their starting positions of the crystal structure. Inhibitor conformations were analyzed with TorsionAnalyzer.(59) Figures were prepared using PyMOL.(60)

    Stability of Inhibitors in Rat Liver Microsomes

    Rat liver microsomes were prepared as previously described.(32) Compounds were incubated with rat liver microsomes (2 mg of protein per vial) at a concentration of 100 μM in a final volume of 1 mL of Tris-HCl buffer solution. The samples were incubated for different time intervals at 37 °C in a water bath. At different time points, 100 μL of the sample was taken and mixed with 100 μL of ice-cold acetonitrile (LC-MS quality from Sigma-Aldrich) to stop the enzymatic reaction. After centrifugation at 14 000g, the supernatant was analyzed by LC-MS. Three separate experiments were performed in duplicate.

    Stability of Inhibitors in Blood Plasma

    Blood from healthy donors was obtained from the blood bank, University Clinic Bonn. The blood (100 mL) was mixed with 70 mL of a stabilizing solution, containing citric acid monohydrate (327 mg), sodium citrate (2.63 g), sodium dihydrogen phosphate (251 mg), and dextrose monohydrate (2.55 g) in water (aqua ad injectabilia).(13) A solution of test compound in water was added to give a final concentration of 100 μM. The obtained solution was pipetted into vials (1 mL per vial) and incubated at 37 °C. At different time points (1, 5, 10, 30, 60, 120, and 300 min), 100 μL was taken and mixed with 300 μL of ice-cold acetonitrile (LC-MS quality from Sigma-Aldrich) to stop the reaction. The samples were sonicated in an ultrasonic bath for 5 min followed by centrifugation at 4000 rpm for 10 min. The supernatants were transferred to LC-MS autosampler vials and quantified. Three separate experiments were performed in duplicate.
