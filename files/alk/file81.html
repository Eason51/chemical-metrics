    Abstract

    In continuation of our effort to optimize the pharmacological profile of [1,2-diamino-1,2-bis(4-fluorophenyl)ethane]dichloridoplatinum(II) complexes, we synthesized [1,2-diamino-1-(4-fluorophenyl)alkanol]dichloridoplatinum(II) analogs. The aim of this study was to evaluate the influence of hydroxyl groups at the C2 moiety on aqueous solubility, lipophilicity, cellular platinum accumulation, and cytotoxicity against MDA-MB-231, U-937, RAJI, and SC-1 cells as well as against cisplatin-sensitive and cisplatin-resistant A2780 and A2780cisR ovarian carcinoma cells. As expected, the OH groups improved the water solubility and decreased the lipophilicity of the neutral ligands, resulting in complexes with favorable pharmacokinetic properties. The cellular uptake of the compounds in MDA-MB-231 and U-937 cells proved to depend on the configuration and showed only a slight correlation with lipophilicity. The most active complexes were R,R/S,S configured, which points to a carrier-mediated mode of action. [threo-1,2-Diamino-1-(4-fluorophenyl)propan]dichloridoplatinum(II) and [threo-2,3-diamino-3-(4-fluorophenyl)propan-1-ol]dichloridoplatinum(II) possessed only low cross-resistance to cisplatin and were up to 10-fold more active in lymphoma cell lines.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Since the serendipitous discovery of the cytotoxicity of cisplatin by Rosenberg et al.,(1) platinum complexes have represented important drugs that are used in the first-line therapy against a number of solid tumors, including testicular, ovarian, and nonsmall cell lung cancer. Cisplatin and carboplatin are the most marketed metal complexes for oncological purposes (Figure 1). However, the therapy is not without obstacles. Intolerable side effects, such as nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, and myelosuppression, a limited spectrum of activity, and the development of resistant cancer cells as well as unfavorable pharmacokinetic profiles limit their therapeutic use.(2-4) Because overcoming the resistance and improving the pharmacological properties of the currently used platinum drugs is absolutely essential, the design and synthesis of novel platinum anticancer drugs remains an important field in medicinal chemistry. Various structure-activity relationship studies showed that the nature of the neutral ligand substantially influences the efficacy of platinum(II) complexes as well the extent and mechanism of cellular uptake. Thus, structural alteration of this part of the molecule is an interesting approach to achieve selective enrichment in tumor cells and avoid resistance, which frequently is related to reduced drug accumulation.(4)
    Furthermore, structural modification at the carrier ligand also modifies the physicochemical properties of the complex and is therefore a promising strategy in drug design.
    Exchange of the NH3 ligands in cisplatin by 1,2-diaminocyclohexane and varying the leaving group (Cl2 to oxalate) resulted in oxaliplatin, the third platinum complex approved in Europe for cancer treatment.(5) It is used in patients with colon carcinoma that are resistant to cisplatin.
    Interestingly, no platinum complex is used in the therapy of tumors located in the breast.(6) Both hormone-dependent and hormone-independent mammary carcinomas seem to be resistant to cisplatin and carboplatin. To overcome these resistances, many carrier ligands were developed and coordinated to platinum. In particular, hormones and antihormones have been frequently combined with the cytotoxic PtCl2 moiety.(7, 8)
    In our group, we employed the synthetic estrogen hexestrol as the lead structure for the design of selectively acting platinum complexes (Figure 2). Exchange of the ethyl side chains by amino groups resulted in the 1,2-diamino-1,2-bis(4-hydroxyphenyl)ethane, which is suitable to be coordinated to PtCl2.(9) However, with this modification, the hormonal potency is lost. Nevertheless, the obtained meso/rac-configured [1,2-diamino-1,2-bis(4-hydroxyphenyl)ethane]platinum(II) complexes are more effective in vivo against murine breast tumors than cisplatin.(10) The carrier ligands can be optimized further by exchanging the 4-hydroxyl group with a fluorine substituent.(9, 11) The [1,2-diamino-1,2-bis(4-fluorophenyl)ethane]dichloridoplatinum(II) complexes meso-4F-PtCl2/rac-4F-PtCl2 (Figure 2) are about 25-fold higher in their accumulation in MCF-7 breast cancer cells than cisplatin.(12, 13) However, the neutral ligands mediate high lipophilicity and heavily reduce the water solubility. Therefore, we decided to modify the structure of the complexes systematically to investigate the relevance for antitumor activity and accumulation in tumor cells.
    In a first attempt, we varied the leaving groups.(12, 14-16) Unfortunately, the most promising but highly reactive aquasulfatoplatinum(II) complexes in vitro showed strong side effects in vivo at higher doses. The introduction of a more stable bound cyclobutane-1,1-dicarboxylato group (see carboplatin) did not fully optimize the properties of the complexes.(12) Thus, we tried to reduce the strong hydrophobic interactions in the crystals that are responsible for the low water solubility(17) by exchanging one aromatic ring in meso-4F-PtCl2/rac-4F-PtCl2 by an alkyl chain.(18-20) It was also expected that the reduced size of the alkyl chain would increase the binding kinetic to the DNA because of a reduced hindrance during a nucleophilic attack at the platinum(II). Indeed, the growth-inhibitory effects of the resulting dichloridoplatinum(II) complexes depended on the substituent at C2 in the following manner: 4-fluorophenyl ≤ methyl < ethyl ≈ propyl ≈ butyl.
    Although the elongation of the alkyl moiety at C2 from ethyl to n-propyl did not enhance cytotoxicity, the introduction of an isopropyl substituent at this position led to favorable effects on in vitro antitumor activity. The [(R,R)-1,2-diamino-1-(4-fluorophenyl)-3-methylbutane]dichloridoplatinum(II) was about 10-fold more active than cisplatin.(20)
    These investigations clearly demonstrated that the antitumor effects of [1,2-diamino-1-(4-fluorophenyl)ethane]platinum(II) complexes depend on the substituent at C2. Unfortunately, the low water solubility remained nearly unchanged. Therefore, further optimization of the pharmacokinetic properties is required. On one hand, an increase of the lipophilic character of platinum complexes facilitates drug accumulation via the lipid bilayer of the cell membrane. On the other hand, a decrease of aqueous solubility impedes drug application.
    To cope with these discrepancies, we decided to hydroxylate the C2 alkyl group of the diastereomeric [1,2-diamino-1-(4-fluorophenyl)propane/butane]dichloridoplatinum(II) complexes, and we studied the influence of this structural modification on lipophilicity, aqueous solubility, cytotoxicity, and antiproliferative efficacy on an enhanced panel of tumor cell lines. Because the separation into enantiomers brought only a negligible advantage,(18-21) all experiments were performed with the racemic mixtures of the novel complexes.
    To obtain deeper insight into the mode of action, the cellular accumulation and the induction of apoptotic effects were determined in MDA-MB-231 and U-937 cells. Furthermore, cisplatin-sensitive (A2780) and cisplatin-resistant (A2780cisR) ovarian cancer cells were used to determine whether the complexes show cross-resistance to cisplatin.

    Synthesis

    ARTICLE SECTIONS

    Meso-4F-PtCl2,(22)rac-4F-PtCl2,(22)4F/H-PtCl2,(23)e-4F/Me-PtCl2,(18) and t-4F/Me-PtCl2(18) were synthesized as previously described. The neutral ligands of e-4F/MeOH-PtCl2 (i.e., erythro-21), t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2 (i.e., threo-21), e-4F/EtOH-PtCl2 (i.e., erythro-20), and t-4F/EtOH-PtCl2 (i.e., threo-20) were synthesized starting from the corresponding 4-fluorophenyl substituted alkenes (E/Z-6 and E-7).
    The reaction steps toward the required alkene precursors E/Z-6 and E-7 are depicted in Scheme 1. For the synthesis of E/Z-3, a Wittig reaction(24) was performed. The required educt (3-hydroxypropyl)triphenylphosphonium bromide (1) resulted from the reaction of triphenyl phosphine with 3-hydroxypropyl bromide in boiling benzene and could be isolated by suction filtration. The subsequent deprontonation of 1 using NaH in absolute THF did not yield the expected ylene, but the 2,2,2-triphenyl-1,2-oxaphospholan (2),(25) which was separated and characterized by IR and 1H NMR spectroscopy. At its melting temperature (120 °C), the 1,2-oxaphospholan 2 is in equilibrium with the phosphonium ylide, which then forms an oxaphosphetane upon reaction with 4-fluorobenzaldehyde. From this intermediate, the required 3-(4-fluorophenyl)prop-2-en-1-ol 3 was eliminated in an E/Z ratio of 62:38 (according to 1H NMR data). The stereoisomers were not separated and were employed in the next reaction step as recovered.
    Unfortunately, the described approach failed in the preparation of E-5. The reaction of (2-hydroxyethyl)triphenylphosphonium bromide with NaH gave indeed the 2,2,2-triphenyl-1,2-oxaphosphetane, but it decomposed to triphosphine oxide and ethylene. Therefore, in an alternative synthetic route, 4-fluorocinnamic acid (E-4), obtained via a Knoevenagel reaction(26) of 4-fluorobenzaldehyde and malonic acid in pyridine, was reduced with LiAlH4 to the corresponding alcohol E-5.(27) It is worth noting that the respective Z-configured isomer was not built. Finally, the alkenols 3 and 5 were reacted with 3,4-dihydro-2H-pyran (DHP) to E/Z-6 and E-7, respectively, to protect the aliphatic hydroxyl function.
    The synthetic routes to obtain the 1,2-diaminoalkanols are depicted in Scheme 2. 1,2-Diaminoethanes with different substituents at the ethane bridge, in this case a 4-fluorophenyl moiety at C1 and a hydroxyalkyl chain at C2, can be easily synthesized using aziridines as intermediates.
    For this purpose, E/Z-6 was treated with N,N′-dichlorourethane (DCU) to give a mixture of ß-chloro carbamate isomers (erythro/threo-8). Although the ring closure of ß-chloro carbamates with ethanolic KOH proceeds stereospecifically,(28) the isomers were separated only at the aziridine stage (cis-9and trans-9) by column chromatography. The subsequent stereospecific ring-opening of the aziridine with sodium azide (cis-9 to threo-10 and trans-9 to erythro-10) yielded the 1-azido-2-aminoethanes, which were reduced to the respective 1,2-diaminoethanes (erythro-11 and threo-11) with LiAlH4. Finally, etheric HCl was added to cleave the tetrahydropyranyl ether. The obtained dihydro chlorides erythro-20 and threo-20 were recrystallized from methanol/diethyl ether to obtain analytically pure products.
    For the synthesis of erythro-21 and threo-21, stereospecific pathways were used. In the first pathway, addition of IN3 to E-7 gave exclusively the erythro-configured iodo/azido adduct erythro-12, which allowed, after reduction of the azido group with LiAlH4, a stereoselective ring closure (erythro-12 → trans-13). The subsequent reaction steps to erythro-21 were the same as described above for erythro-20.
    In the second pathway, E-7 was transformed with m-chloroperoxybenzoic acid to the oxiran trans-16 according the method of Anderson et al.(29) Then, stereoselective ring-opening with sodium azide resulted in the ß-azidoalcohol erythro-17, which was mesitylated (erythro-18) and converted in an SN2 reaction with sodium azide to the 1,2-diazidoethane (threo-19). Reduction of the azido groups with LiAlH4 (threo-15) and deprotection of the hydroxyl group with etheric HCl yielded the isomerically pure threo-21. Both the aziridines and the 1,2-diaminoalkanols were characterized by 1H NMR spectroscopy.
    The assignment of the aziridines either to the cis- or the trans-isomer can be made by coupling constant analysis of the vicinal protons at the three-membered ring. According to Brois et al.(30) and Hassner et al.,(31) the coupling constant of 6.5 Hz of cis-9 (Ar-CH, δ = 3.28) correlates with a cis-configuration, whereas the coupling constant of 2.3 Hz determined for trans-9 (Ar-CH, δ = 2.76) and trans-13 (Ar-CH, δ = 2.91) points to a trans-configuration. The following stereoselective reactions retained the configuration of the 1,2-diaminoalkanols.
    Besides the configurative assignment, the 1H NMR spectra gave information about the conformative behavior of the alkanols. The coupling constants between the methine protons in erythro-20 (Ar-CH, 3J = 9.9 Hz, δ = 4.54) and erythro-21 (Ar-CH, 3J = 9.4 Hz, δ = 4.62) indicate a predominant antiperiplanar arrangement. In contrast, the coupling constant of threo-21 (Ar-CH, 3J = 7.0 Hz, δ = 4.68) corresponds more with a synclinal orientation. Unfortunately, the overlap of the respective signal in the spectrum of threo-20 by the solvent peak did not allow the analogous conformative determination.
    Scheme 3 illustrates the three-step reaction to the dichloridoplatinum(II) complexes. By applying this approach, high yields of pure final products were attained, which can be used without further purification. In the first step, the respective dihydrochloride was dissolved in water and combined with an aqueous solution of K2PtI4, prepared from K2PtCl4 and KI. The pH was adjusted to 7.0 several times with 0.1 N NaOH until the precipitation of the diiodoplatinum(II) complex was completed. The complex was vacuum filtered and dried over P2O5. Subsequent reaction with Ag2SO4 in water (second step) resulted in the water-soluble diaquaplatinum(II) sulfates, which were not isolated. The AgI was separated, and the aqueous solution was treated with 2N HCl to precipitate the dichloridoplatinum(II) complex (third step). Characterization of the complexes was achieved by 1H NMR and IR spectroscopy, electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESI–MS), and elemental analysis.
    The coordination of the 1,2-diaminoalkanols to platinum was confirmed by 1H NMR spectroscopy. Because of the binding to platinum, the amino protons become diastereotopic and split into four separated signals that are broadened by the reciprocal coupling and the coupling to platinum. The methine protons that showed the same broadening are suitable to analyze the conformative behavior of the five-membered chelate ring.(32)
    Dynamic effects and the position of the aromatic ring in relation to the alkanol moiety could influence the physicochemical properties and consequently the cytotoxicity. Therefore, the coordination to platinum was performed in D2O, leading to an NH–ND exchange allowing coupling constant analysis. The five-membered chelate ring is puckered and could exist in a δ or λ conformation. The interconversion between both conformations or the existence of a predominant conformation depends on the configuration and the substituents at the chelate ring. The splitting of the benzylic proton by the methine proton and 195Pt is dependent on the dihedral angle. In the threo-series, 3JH–H ≈ 10.9 Hz and 3JH–Pt ≈ 0 Hz points to a predominant conformation with the aromatic ring and the alkanol in an equatorial position. A conversion to a conformation with bisaxial-positioned substituents can be excluded.
    In the spectra of e-4F/MeOH-PtCl2, the signal of the benzylic protons shows platinum satellites at a distance of about 70–80 Hz. This coupling together with the coupling of the Ar–CH to the methine proton (JH–H = 4.8 Hz) also indicates a restricted δ−λ interconversion.(32) In the predominate conformation, the 4-fluorophenyl ring adapts an equatorial position and the alkanol is axially positioned.
    In both diastereomers, the hydroxyl groups are incorporated in H-bridges. The OH stretching vibration appeared in the IR spectra at 3443 (e-4F/MeOH-PtCl2) and 3563 cm–1 (t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2), indicating a stronger network in the case of the erythro-configured diastereomer. The axial position of the CH2OH moiety allows not only an interaction with the amino groups but also with the axial coordination sphere above the platinum. Such an arrangement was already postulated for the cis-bis(2-hydroxypropylamine)dichloridoplatinum(II) complex.(33)

    Lipophilicity and Aqueous Solubility

    The octanol/water partition coefficient (log Po/w) is a physicochemical measure to quantify the lipophilicity of a substance. In other words, it describes the relative solubility of the drug in n-octanol (representing the lipid bilayer of the cell membrane) and water (representing the aqueous fluid in and out the cells). Consequently, the log Po/w value is a key indicator of a compound’s ability to pass through the cell membrane. In this study, log Po/w values of the novel platinum complexes were determined using the shake-flask method.(34, 35) The compounds were distributed between n-octanol and an aqueous 0.9% NaCl solution. The NaCl solution was selected as aqueous phase to prevent aquation of the complexes during the analyses. The platinum content in both layers was quantified using graphite furnace atomic absorption spectrometry (GF-AAS). The resulting log Po/w values are reported in Table 1.

    Additionally, log Po/w values were calculated using ALOGPS 2.1, a program which was developed for the prediction of lipophilicity of Pt(II) complexes.(36) However, the program does not distinguish between diastereomers. Interestingly, the experimental data confirmed this finding. Only in the case of [erythro- and threo-3,4-diamino-4-(4-fluorophenyl)butanol]dichloridoplatinum(II) was a clear difference observed: e-4F/EtOH-PtCl2 (log Po/w = −0.45) proved to be much more hydrophilic than t-4F/EtOH-PtCl2 (log Po/w = −0.24).
    Although a good overall correlation between experimental and theoretical data could be found (R2 = 0.9122), it must be noted that calculated and experimental data strongly differ in the case of the alkanol-substituted complexes. Although e-4F/MeOH-PtCl2 and t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2 were not as hydrophilic as expected, the elongation of the alkanol moiety by a methylene group did not result in complexes with medium hydrophobicity (log Po/w ≈ 0). Cisplatin is a hydrophilic complex, and the obtained log Po/w (−2.25) is consistent with the literature data.(37) Exchange of the ammine ligands by rac/meso-1,2-diamino-1,2-bis(4-fluorophenyl)ethane resulted in hydrophobic complexes with log Po/w values of 1.03 (meso-4F-PtCl2) and 0.90 (rac-4F-PtCl2). Exchange of one 4-fluorophenyl ring by H drastically reduced the hydrophobic character of the complexes. 4F/H-PtCl2 showed a log Po/w of −0.13. A methyl group at C2 slightly increased the hydrophobicity, which is more pronounced if the complex is threo-configured (log Po/w = 0.14). Finally, the introduction of an alkanol group at C2 gave the complexes a slight hydrophilic character with log Po/w values between −0.24 and −0.45.
    The solubility in water did not correlate with the log Po/w values but showed a clear dependence on the substituent at C2 as well as on the configuration at the diaminoethane bridge. The saturation level of Cisplatin was 1182.3 μg in 1 mL of a 0.9% (w/v) NaCl solution. The 1,2-diamino-1,2-bis(4-fluorophenyl)ethane ligand reduced the solubility to 0.4 (rac-4F-PtCl2) and 28.2 μg/mL (meso-4F-PtCl2), respectively. If one aromatic ring is exchanged by H or CH3, then the solubility increases only slightly (4F/H-PtCl2: 4.5 μg/mL; e-4F/Me-PtCl2: 43.1 μg/mL; t-4F/Me-PtCl2: 1.4 μg/mL). On the contrary, OH groups at the C2-alkyl residue mediated relatively high water solubility, especially if the ligand is erythro-configured (e-4F/MeOH-PtCl2 (271.6 μg/mL); e-4F/EtOH-PtCl2 (399.0 μg/mL)). The threo-configured complexes t-4F/MeOHPtCl2 (45.8 μg/mL) and t-4F/EtOH-PtCl2 (27.7 μg/mL) are only sparingly soluble in water.
    The lower solubility of complexes with rac- or threo-configured ligands might result from the predominant equatorial arrangement of the substituents at the 1,2-diaminoethane chelate ring. This conformation allows a closer packing in the crystals, with strong intermolecular interactions (NH2–Cl and Pt–Pt) and hydrophobic contacts between the aromatic rings. If the C2-residue is predominant axially oriented, then the contacts between the complex molecules are lower, and by hydroxylation of this moiety the single molecules can be easily solvated.

    Cellular Accumulation (Cellular Uptake)

    An important parameter for the discussion of the cytotoxicity of platinum complexes is the cellular platinum concentration. The first step in drug action is the passage through the cell membrane. The reports on platinum uptake are somewhat contradictory in the literature. On one hand, for platinum complexes, a passive transport is reported that correlates with the lipophilicity of the complexes.(38) On the other hand, during the past years, the human copper transporter 1 (hCTR1) became of interest because it mediates the uptake of cisplatin and carboplatin.(39) In [1,2-diamino-1,2-diarylethane]dichloridoplatinum(II) and [1,2-diamino-1-arylalkane]dichloridoplatinum(II) complexes, the neutral ligands are more sophisticated; thus, it is very likely that a carrier system can recognize this part of the molecule.
    To investigate the influence of the neutral ligand on the cellular uptake of the respective complex, cellular accumulation studies of our [1,2-diaminoethane]dichloridoplatinum(II) complexes were conducted on the MDA-MB-231 mammary carcinoma and the U-937 histiocytic lymphoma cell lines. The cellular platinum concentration was determined after 1, 4, 8, and 24 h of exposure to the respective platinum compound at a concentration of 5 μM.
    The degree of platinum accumulation is best expressed by the accumulation factor (f) which is defined as the ratio of the mean intracellular molar platinum concentration and the molar platinum concentration of the cell culture medium (5 μM).

    The results of our cellular accumulation studies are graphically presented in Figure 3. As expected, cisplatin was accumulated only sparingly in MDA-MB-231 cells. An accumulation factor of only 1.2 was determined after 24 h of incubation. Our lead compounds, rac-4F-PtCl2 and meso-4F-PtCl2, reached their maximum accumulation already after about 8 h of incubation. At the end of the experiment (24 h), meso-4F-PtCl2 (f = 9.4) was accumulated about 8-fold higher in the cells than cisplatin, and with rac-4F-PtCl2 (f = 70.3), an even higher 59-fold platinum concentration was achieved. This discrimination of diastereomers clearly indicates that the 1,2-diamino-1,2-(4-fluorophenyl)ethanes act as carrier ligands because both complexes possess comparable lipophilicity.
    As depicted in Figure 3a, with exception of 4F/H-PtCl2 all complexes showed the same accumulation profile, but only t-4F/Me-PtCl2 (f = 78.6) reached the value of rac-4F-PtCl2. Its diastereomer, e-4F/Me-PtCl2 (f = 2.1), was taken up to a minor extent than meso-4F-PtCl2, demonstrating that the threo-1,2-diamino-1-(4-fluorophenyl)propane acts as carrier ligand as well.
    Interestingly, a hydroxyl substituent at the C2 methyl group drastically reduced the accumulation of t-4F/Me-PtCl2 (f = 78.6 → 3.1 (t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2)), whereas the properties of e-4F/Me-PtCl2 after hydroxylation remained unchanged (e-4F/MeOH-PtCl2, f = 2.1). The elongation of the side chain from hydroxymethyl to hydroxyethyl additionally reduced the intracellular Pt concentration (for threo, from f = 3.1 to 2.2, and for erythro, from f = 2.1 to 0.9).
    A totally different accumulation profile was observed for 4F/H-PtCl2. The initial accumulation factor of 32.9 (1 h) decreased with time to 10.1 (4 h), 5.2 (8 h), and finally to 2.8 (24 h). An explanation for this finding can be resistance phenomena such as an active efflux of the complex.
    In U-937 cells (Figure 3b), the complexes were generally taken up to a higher extent than in MDA-MB-231 cells. The general trends observed in the mammary carcinoma cell line were also found for lymphoma cells: threo/rac-configured complexes were accumulated to a higher degree than the erythro/meso ones. Extraordinarily high values were measured after 24 h for rac-4F-PtCl2 (f = 409.7) and t-4F/Me-PtCl2 (f = 881.4). Rac-4F-PtCl2 showed a 39-fold and t-4F/Me-PtCl2 an 84-fold higher accumulation grade than cisplatin (f = 10.5). The maximum of intracellular Pt was determined for rac-4F-PtCl2 (f = 419.2), t-4F/Me-PtCl2 (f = 1 714.3), and 4F/H-PtCl2 (f = 133.5) after 1 h of incubation. In the case of t-4F/Me-PtCl2 and 4F/H-PtCl2, the accumulation grade decreased with time. The highest accumulation grade in this study was measured for t-4F/Me-PtCl2 after an incubation of 1 h, which was 163-fold higher than that of cisplatin at the end of the experiment.
    Again, hydroxyl groups at the side chain reduced the accumulation grade, especially in the case of t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2 (from f = 1714.3 (t-4F/Me-PtCl2) to 33.5 (t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2)). Nevertheless, t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2 showed the third highest Pt concentration after 24 h.
    The values of rac-4F-PtCl2 and t-4F/Me-PtCl2 point to very fast cellular uptake of complexes with R,R/S,S-configured non-hydroxylated ligands, whereas those with R,S/S,R-ligands were continuously accumulated. Interestingly, the diastereomeric complexes with C2 alkanols were continuously taken up. Therefore, it can be concluded that the accumulation profile depends on the configuration and the C2 substituent.
    The results obtained in both cell lines do not give a clear correlation between lipophilicity and cellular uptake. However, it can be deduced that the increased hydrophilicity because of the C2 alkanol moiety decreased the cellular platinum amount. The participation of a yet unknown carrier system is very likely because of the strong differences between complexes with diastereomeric ligands even though they exert nearly identical hydrophobic character.

    Cytotoxicity and Antiproliferative Efficacy

    To analyze the potential of the novel platinum complexes as antitumor agents, their antiproliferative and cytotoxic effects were evaluated in a panel of representative human cancer cell lines (i.e., mammary carcinoma (MDA-MB-231), ovarian carcinoma (A2780), and lymphoma (U-937, RAJI, SC-1) cell lines).
    Initially, complexes were screened for antiproliferative potency, characterized by the inhibition of [3H]thymidine incorporation into the cellular nucleic acid, at concentrations of 0.1, 1.0, and 5.0 μM. The results are depicted in Figure 4. All compounds caused a dose-dependent inhibition of proliferation in all cell lines studied. In general, the lymphoma cell lines were more sensitive to compound treatment than the mammary carcinoma cell line. Nevertheless, at a concentration of 5 μM, all complexes nearly abolished proliferation in all cell lines, which was independent of the C2 substituent and the presence of a hydroxyl group. Furthermore, all complexes were more effective than cisplatin.
    The observed decrease in proliferation was accompanied by a decrease in metabolic activity. Metabolic activity was determined via a modified MTT assay, which detects the reduction of tetrazolium salts into formazan derivatives, a process that requires functional mitochondria. Hence, this assay discriminates between living and dead cells and may therefore be used as a marker for measuring cytotoxicity of compounds.     To confirm the cytotoxicity of the compounds, their capacity to induce programmed cell death was determined by an single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) apoptosis ELISA, which is based on the selective denaturation of DNA in apoptotic cells by formamide and the subsequent detection of the denatured DNA with a monoclonal antibody to ssDNA.
    The induction of apoptosis after a 72 h incubation was investigated in MDA-MB-231 and U-937 cells at compound concentrations of 1.0 and 5.0 μM (Figure 5). In MDA-MB-231 cells, all complexes with erythro-configured ligands and cisplatin did not induce apoptosis, whereas meso-4F-PtCl2, rac-4F-PtCl2, and t-4F/EtOH-PtCl2 were slightly, but statistically significant, more active at 5 μM. The highest apoptosis rates were determined for t-4F/Me-PtCl2 and t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2, which already induced significant apoptosis at 1 μM. The proapoptotic effect of 5 μM of t-4F/Me-PtCl2 (Figure 6a–d) and 5 μM of t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2 (Figure 6e–h) is also clearly depicted by morphological changes of the MDA-MB-231 cells during a 24 h incubation period with the respective compound. After seeding and allowing the cells to adhere for 24 h, the MDA-MB-231 cells represent a quite homogeneous population of spindle-shaped cells. Treatment for 6 and 12 h with t-4F/Me-PtCl2 and t-4F/MeOH-PtCl2, respectively, induced rounding of the cells, which finally led to complete detachment from the surface after 24 h of incubation. It should be mentioned that further apoptosis-specific changes are not visible because of the limited magnification of the microscope.
    In U-937 cells, however, all complexes induced apoptosis comparable to cisplatin and no dependence on the used ligand was detected (Figure 5b). Hence, these data are in agreement with the [3H]thymidine assays where strong antiproliferative activity was measured irrespective of the C2 substituent and the presence of a hydroxyl group.

    Conclusions

    In this structure-activity study, we demonstrated that it is possible to optimize the physicochemical properties of [1,2-diamino-1-(4-fluorophenyl)ethane]dichloridoplatinum complexes by the introduction of an alkane or an alkanol moiety at C2. The in vitro effects strongly depended on this substituent as well as on the configuration at the 1,2-diaminoethane ligand, which points to a carrier-mediated mode of action. The complexes with R,R\S,S-configured ligands were highly active, especially against lymphoma cells, and showed low cross-resistance to cisplatin in the A2780cisR cell line.

    General Method for the Preparation of Diiodoplatinum(II) Complexes

    K2PtCl4 (1 mmol, 0.42 g) and potassium iodide (10 mmol, 1.60 g) were dissolved in 10 mL of water and stirred for 30 min under the exclusion of light, whereupon the reddish solution blackened. Subsequently, this mixture was added slowly to a solution of the respective diamine dihydrochloride (1 mmol) in water (15 mL). During this procedure, the pH of the reaction mixture was maintained at approximately 6 by the successive addition of 0.1 N NaOH. A constant pH value indicated the completion of the reaction. Thereafter, the mixture was acidified with 1.0 N HCl (pH 2) and stirred for an additional 30 min. The resulting precipitate was collected by suction filtration, washed thoroughly with warm water, and dried over P2O5 in vacuo.

    Determination of Aqueous Solubility

    Compounds were dissolved in 1 mL of a 0.9% (w/v) NaCl solution to saturation. The samples were vortexed vigorously for 20 s and allowed to stand for 24 h at rt. The suspensions were filtered (0.2 μm), and the platinum content was quantified using GF-AAS.

    Partition Coefficient Measurements

    Log Po/w values of the platinum compounds were determined using the shake-flask method.(35) The aqueous (0.9% w/v sodium chloride) and organic (n-octanol) phases were saturated with each other for 1 week. Drugs were dissolved in the aqueous phase, an equal volume of saturated n-octanol was added, and the solution was mixed vigorously for 30 min (1400 rpm). Thereafter, samples were centrifuged (15 min, 1500 rpm), and the platinum content of the organic and aqueous phases was determined by GF-AAS. The log of the ratio of platinum concentration in the organic and aqueous phases were calculated to determine log Po/w. The results are the mean of three independent experiments. To ensure the accuracy of log Po/w measurements, the platinum amount in the aqueous phase was determined before the beginning of the experiment and compared to the sum of the measured platinum amounts in both solvents at the end of the experiment. The discrepancies ranged from 0.3 to 19.1%.

    Biological Methods

    The mammary carcinoma cell line MDA-MB-231 was obtained from the American Culture Collection (ATCC, Rockville, MD). The human lymphoma cell lines U-937 (origin: patient with histiocytic lymphoma), SC-1 (B cell lymphoma), and RAJI (Burkitt lymphoma) were purchased from the German Collection of Microorganisms and Cell Cultures (DSMZ, Braunschweig, Germany). The ovarian carcinoma cell line A2780 and its cisplatin-resistant counterpart A2780cisR were kindly provided by Dr. Heidi Fiegl, Department of Gynecology, Medical University of Innsbruck. All cell lines were cultured in RPMI 1640 without phenol red supplemented with l-glutamine (2 mM), penicillin (100 U/mL), streptomycin (100 mg/mL), and fetal calf serum (FCS, 10%; all from PAA Laboratories, Pasching, Austria) using 75 cm2 flasks in a water-saturated atmosphere (5% CO2/95% air) at 37 °C. The cells were serially passaged twice a week. To maintain the cisplatin resistance of A2780cisR, cisplatin (1 μM) was added every third passage. Mycoplasma contamination was routinely monitored, and only mycoplasma-free cultures were used. Drugs were freshly prepared as stock solutions in DMF and diluted with complete cell-culture media when used for the biochemical experiments (final DMF concentration 0.1% (v/v)).

    Analysis of Metabolic Activity

    Exponentially growing cells were seeded in triplicate in 96-well microtiter plates at a density of 104 cells/well (100 μL). Compounds were added at appropriate concentrations 2 h thereafter, and the cells were incubated for 72 h at 37 °C in a 5% CO2/95% air atmosphere. Subsequently, cultures were analyzed for cellular growth using a modified 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay (EZ4U kit; Biomedica, Vienna, Austria) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. To exclude unspecific staining by FCS-containing medium, the optical density (OD) of the respective medium was subtracted. Metabolic activity in the absence of the compound was set at 100%. The IC50 values were calculated with Prism (GraphPad, San Diego, CA) using nonlinear regression and the log of the inhibitor versus variable slope response equation. The bottom constraint was set to 0.

    Analysis of Proliferation

    The rate of cellular proliferation was measured by the incorporation of [3H]thymidine into cellular nucleic acids. For this purpose, cells were plated similarly to the metabolic activity assay described above and incubated with the respective compound for 72 h. For the last 12–16 h of the incubation, cells were pulsed with 2 μCi of [3H]thymidine (specific activity 74 GBq/mmol, Hartmann Analytic, Braunschweig, Germany), harvested using a semiautomated device, and the amount of radiolabeled [3H]thymidine incorporated into DNA expressed in counts per minute was measured in a liquid scintillation counter (Microbeta Trilux; PerkinElmer Life Sciences, Boston, MA). For all cell lines, proliferation in the absence of drugs was set at 100%. The drug activity was calculated as the percentage of the control.

    Analysis of Apoptosis

    The plates for apoptosis were prepared analogously as that described above for the metabolic activity assay and were evaluated with an ELISA using the ssDNA apoptosis kit (Chemicon International, Hofheim, Germany) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The apoptosis rate was evaluated by dividing the OD of the wells measured in the plates for ssDNA apoptosis by the OD of the wells analyzed with the modified MTT assay. Apoptosis in the absence of the compounds was set at 1.

    Detection of Morphological Changes

    MDA-MB-231 cells (3 × 104 cells/well) were seeded in a flat-bottomed microtiter plate and incubated for 24 h at 37 °C in a humidified 5% CO2/95% air atmosphere to let the cells adhere. Thereafter, compounds were added (final concentration 5 μM), and the cells were cultivated for another 24 h. During this incubation time, a picture was taken by the JuLI Smart fluorescent cell analyzer (Digital Bio; NanoEnTek Inc., Seoul, Korea) every 30 min.

    Statistical Analysis

    The Mann-Whitney U test was used to analyze the differences between the activity in the absence and the presence of the respective concentration of the test compounds in the [3H]thymidine incorporation and ssDNA apoptosis assays. Statistical significance was defined as a P value of <0.05. Statistical analysis was performed using NCSS software (Kaysville, UT, USA).
