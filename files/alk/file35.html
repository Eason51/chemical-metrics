    Abstract

    Cocaine is one of the most widely abused drugs in the industrial world. Substantial evidence has accumulated that the dopamine transporter (DAT) is a key target for cocaine regarding its reinforcing effects. This work describes the application of chemometric methods to a data set of 54 N1-benzhydryl-oxy-alkyl-N4-phenyl-alk(en)yl-piperazines (GBR compounds) and chemically related mepyramines as putative candidates in cocaine abuse therapy. The aim of the study is to gain insight into the structural requirements that determine the affinity of the data set molecules to the DAT and the serotonin transporter (SERT) as well as their inhibitory potency on dopamine uptake. The compounds in the dataset are described using the recently developed GRID independent descriptors (GRIND), which allow one to obtain fast three-dimensional quantitative structure−activity relationship models without the need of aligning and superimposing the structures; the results are interpreted in a convenient pharmacophoric-like fashion. In the first part of the work, the selectivity of the database molecules for DAT binding vs dopamine reuptake inhibition is investigated. In the second part, the selectivity of the compounds for DAT binding vs SERT binding is studied. In both cases, significant models are obtained, which define the structural features responsible for the respective selectivity profiles. Moreover, the information has potential interest for the design of new derivatives with improved selectivity.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Cocaine, one of the most widely abused drugs today,1 is pharmacologically a nonselective drug interacting at a variety of distinct sites. Cocaine binds with high affinity to the transporter proteins for the neurotransmitters dopamine (DAT), serotonin (SERT), and noradrenaline thereby blocking the reuptake of these amines into the presynaptic terminals. With less affinity, cocaine binds to sodium channels as well as opioid, muscarinic, cholinergic, and σ-receptors.2
    The behavioral effects of cocaine have been attributed to its ability to inhibit the reuptake of dopamine, referred to in the literature as the “dopamine hypothesis”.1 According to this hypothesis, the above-mentioned interaction of cocaine with the DAT and the concomitant block of dopamine uptake into dopaminergic neurons results in increased synaptic dopamine levels. Increased levels of dopamine interact with dopamine receptors on postsynaptic neurons and result in a number of clinically observable effects such as reinforcement and locomotor stimulation. There are several lines of experimental evidence3 supporting the dopamine hypothesis:  (i) ligand binding to the DAT is inhibited by cocaine and related compounds at potencies that correlate with the potencies of the same compounds in behavioral studies of drug reinforcement; (ii) in microdialysis studies, cocaine mediates a dose-dependent increase in extracellular dopamine levels in limbic areas, consistent with the effect of cocaine as a dopamine uptake blocker; (iii) selective dopaminergic receptor blockers attenuate the reinforcing properties of cocaine; and (iv) the destruction of dopaminergic neurons disrupts cocaine self-administration.
    However, the ability of the dopamine hypothesis to explain the acute euphoric effects of cocaine in humans is unclear.4 Recent studies showed that DAT knockout mice self-administer cocaine,5 indicating that in the absence of the DAT, cocaine can still establish rewarding effects. Additionally, several dopamine uptake inhibitors, such as mazindol, do not display significant abuse liability,6 probably due to differing pharmacokinetic behavior. With cocaine, for example, its rapid onset of action and its relative short half-life might be partially responsible for its addictive potential. It is notable that some success has been achieved using slow onset sustained action agonists in the treatment of addiction. Accordingly, a “rate hypothesis” of addiction has been proposed, which predicts that the abuse liability of a compound depends on the strength of its positive psychological response and is proportional to the rate-of-onset of these effects.7 This brief summary of the pharmacological background of the dopamine hypothesis underlines the urgent need for further investigations.
    The DAT protein is a member of a family of Na+/Cl--dependent transporters, located on presynaptic nerve terminals.8 Rat and human DAT have been cloned, and the coding sequence has been determined.9 The primary sequence contains 620 amino acid residues; via hydropathy analysis, 12 transmembrane spanning regions are predicted with an extracellular loop between domains 3 and 4. N and C termini are located intracellularly. Regarding three-dimensional (3D) structure only, computer-generated molecular models have been developed so far.10
    The DAT has served as the primary molecular target in the research of pharmacologic treatment of cocaine abuse. However, to further investigate the dopamine hypothesis and to advance in the research of candidates useful in therapy, it is needed to obtain compounds with a more defined pharmacologic profile. In this respect, compounds with a high affinity for the DAT and a weak reuptake inhibition seem one of the most promising pharmacological approaches for the therapy of cocaine abuse. Another aspect open to investigation is to know whether “pure” ligands for the DAT or “mixed” compounds, exhibiting a more or less equilibrated affinity to both dopamine and SERTs, would be preferred in the therapy of cocaine abuse.
    There are plenty of chemically heterogeneous DAT ligands; cocaine analogues, benzotropines, mazindol, methylphenidate, rimcazol, meperidine, and the GBR compounds (N1-benzhydryl-oxy-alkyl-N4-phenyl-alk(en)yl-piperazines) are some representative examples.11 In literature, a lot of papers dealing with qualitative structure−activity relationships (SAR) of GBR compounds can be found.12-24 Major results of these investigations are summarized in Figure 1.
    In this context, we describe the application of chemometric methodology on a data set of GBR compounds and chemically related mepyramines. The aim is, more than obtaining more potent compounds, to gain insight in the structural requirements that are determinant for their pharmacologic profile and for the selectivity with respect to their interaction with different amine transporters. In the first part of the work, the selectivity of the database molecules for DAT binding with respect to dopamine reuptake inhibition is investigated. Later on, the selectivity of the compounds for the DAT vs the SERT is studied.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Selectivity of DAT Binding vs Dopamine Reuptake Inhibition. As stated above, one of the envisaged approaches in the therapy of cocaine abuse is the search for compounds that potently bind to the DAT but rather weakly inhibit the reuptake of dopamine. One of the possible rational approaches to understand the chemical prerequisites for selectivity is to carry out comparative quantitative structure−activity relationships (QSAR) studies involving both the DAT binding and the dopamine reuptake. Then, from the analysis of the results, information can be gathered potentially applicable for the design of new compounds with improved selectivity.
    The present study is focused on a series of 56 GBR compounds (for structures, see Table 1) and chemically related mepyramines (Table 2). Their biological activities, i.e., DAT affinity (inhibition of [125I]RTI-55 binding to human DAT in clonal cells) and dopamine uptake inhibition ([3H]dopamine uptake in HEK293 cells expressing recombinant DAT) are compiled in Table 3. In the models, inhibition of [125I]RTI-55 binding to human DAT is used as pKD values, and inhibition of [3H]dopamine uptake is used as log 1/C with C representing the half-maximal inhibitory concentration. For both activities, the test molecules cover a spectrum of roughly 3 log units between the most and the least active compound. In the case of inhibition of [125I]RTI-55 binding, the GBR compounds 17 and 21 exhibit the highest affinity with a pKD value of 8.41 and the compound with the lowest affinity is the mepyramine 38 with a pKD value of 5.66. The strongest inhibitory potency in the assay of [3H]dopamine uptake was found for 24 (log 1/C = 8.38), which is permanently charged due to N4 methylation of the central piperazine moiety. The weakest inhibition of dopamine uptake was measured for compound 7 (log 1/C = 5.22) in which the N4 nitrogen is involved in an amide function. Compounds 9, 27, 35, 38, 40, and 42 did not show appreciable inhibition of dopamine uptake at concentrations of up to 10 μM.

    A preliminary inspection of the structures and the activities allows one to obtain some qualitative SAR:  for binding inhibition and DAT affinity, GBR compounds are in general slightly more potent than the mepyramines; involvement of the N4 nitrogen in an amide function is detrimental for both activities. Quaternization of the N4 nitrogen produces different effects:  uptake inhibition is significantly improved, whereas DAT affinity is much less influenced.
    To gain further insight into the relationships between structure and activity, the series was also analyzed using 3D QSAR. First, suitable 3D structures were obtained for the compounds using molecular mechanics and semiempirical AM1 geometrical optimization in aqueous solvent. The method for generating and refining the 3D structures of the compounds is also described in detail in the Experimental Section. All compounds were analyzed except 24 and 26, since their permanent charge makes them too different from all of the remaining compounds in the series. The other 54 compounds were then described using the recently published GRID25 independent descriptors (GRIND).26 GRIND allows one to obtain fast 3D QSAR models without the need of aligning and superimposing the structures; the results are interpreted in a convenient pharmacophoric-like fashion. In few words, GRIND starts from molecular interaction fields (MIF) obtained with GRID, describing regions where the ligand can produce energetically favorable interactions with probes representing potential groups of the receptor. Then, the method transforms a number of these MIF in a small number of variables describing the existence at a certain distance of couples of points representing favorable interactions. The variables are grouped in blocks (correlograms) depending which kind of favorable interactionsuch as hydrophobic, hydrogen bond (HB) donor or HB acceptor interactionsis represented for each one of the points. Further details about the GRIND method can be found in the Experimental Section and in the original reference.26
    The GRIND obtained from this series consisted of a total of 360 descriptors, describing the interaction of the compounds with three probes:  the DRY probe (which represents hydrophobic interactions), the O probe (carbonyl oxygen, representing HB donor interactions), and the N1 probe (amide nitrogen, representing HB acceptor interactions). As stated above, each variable represents the presence of a couple of points separated by a certain distance where one such probe produces a favorable interaction for a certain compound. The value given to this variable corresponds to the product of the energies of interaction of the probe with the ligand in both points. For example, a certain GRIND variable can represent the presence of two hydrophobic regions separated by 10 Å or the presence of HB donor and HB acceptor favorable regions separated by 5 Å.
    With the above-described GRIND variables, we obtained an X matrix of 360 columns (variables) and 54 rows (compounds). From this matrix, the GBR compounds 9 and 27 as well as the mepyramines 35, 38, 40, and 42 were removed, to avoid the negative impact of missing Y data. Thus, the final data set used for the remaining QSAR calculations of this first part of the study comprises 48 structures.
    PLS2 analysis,27 performed on both activity values simultaneously, resulted in a three component model with an r2 of 0.67 for the variance in Y1 (DAT affinity) and an r2 of only 0.38 for Y2 (dopamine uptake inhibition). The cross validation of the model produced small positive q2 values. Leave one out (LOO) cross validation yielded q2 values of 0.41 and 0.20 for Y1 and Y2, whereas using a more stringent method (5 random groups, see the Experimental Section for details) we obtained slightly smaller values of 0.39 and 0.13 for Y1 and Y2. These results indicate that the model has modest quality and is not suitable for explaining the activity of the compounds in full but is still suited for obtaining some qualitative information. The validity of the model is further supported by an additional analysis applied to data sets with randomly scrambled activity values (results not shown), where the original model exhibited a significantly higher quality than any other model obtained with the randomized data.
    A close inspection of the PLS2 model, described above, showed that dopamine uptake inhibition is better explained by the third component, whereas DAT affinity is better explained by the first and second component. Thus, some differentiation between the chemical prerequisites for DAT binding and uptake inhibition is possible. This fact is illustrated in Figure 2, which represents a plot of the partial PLS weights (w) of the third vs the first principal component (PC). The points in this plot represent the original X variables in a position corresponding to their contribution to the PC. In few words, X variables with high positive or negative weights contribute significantly to a certain PC and therefore can be seen as highly correlated with the Y variables (biological activities). A closer inspection of Figure 2 allows one to detect those X variables or chemical descriptors that have a strong direct (positive weights) or inverse (negative weights) impact on DAT affinity and on dopamine uptake inhibition. The number represents the distance between two nodes showing favorable interactions with the respective probes and can be converted in distances in Ångström multiplying the number by two parameters of the method:  the grid spacing and the smoothing window (which are 0.5 and 0.8 Å in this analysis, as reported in the Experimental Section). Therefore, the variable DD-6 represents for instance an interaction between nodes separated by approximately 2.4 Å.
    According to the dopamine hypothesis, compounds are demanded that combine high affinity to the DAT with a weak inhibition of dopamine uptake. In our series, the compounds with higher DAT affinity are characterized by having short interactions at short distances of 2.4−3.6 Å, as documented by GRIND variables DD-6−DD-9. These are characteristic interactions of the GBR compounds, as exemplified for compound 11 in Figure 3A. They appear in the space formed between the two aromatic rings of the benzhydryl moiety; therefore, the variables DD-6−DD-9 can explain the high DAT affinity of these compounds. On the other hand, regions favorable for HB donor groups separated by 4.4−5.6 Å (GRIND variables NN-11−NN-14) seem to be characteristic of compounds with low DAT affinity (Figure 3B, compound 36). This is typical for mepyramines with an ethylene linker (Y in Table 2) like compound 36 (pKD = 6.0), whereas in GBR compounds this interaction was less intensely produced between the piperazine N and the ether O; in mepyramines with a propylene Y linker like present in compound 43 (pKD = 8.1), this interaction is not present.
    Regarding dopamine uptake inhibition, the model explains much less variation; therefore, its interpretation should be expected to be less consistent and to have less general value than the previous model. For example, it can be noticed that some of the less active compounds (e.g., 3, pKD = 5.4; 5, pKD = 5.9; 7, pKD = 5.2; and 25, pKD = 5.24) have an amide linker between the piperazine and the phenyl ring. In these compounds, long distance interaction between the hydrophobic regions at both ends (approximately at 19 Å) and represented by variables DD-47−DD-50 is either absent or very weak. The carbonyl oxygen of this linker is also producing the regions represented by the variables NN-27−NN-34, again present in the less active GBR compounds 5, 7, and 25 and shown in Figure 3C. Unfortunately, it is difficult to affirm if the real effect involved in the loss of activity of such compounds is due to the change on the geometry of the compounds, the presence of an unfavorable polar interaction, or the change in the characteristics of the piperazine nitrogen, since all three of these effects are correlated. In any case, the model highlights the effects as highly relevant and should be investigated in more depth.
    Selectivity of DAT Binding vs SERT Binding. The second part of this study concerns a putative selectivity of the database molecules for the DAT vs the SERT. It is not clear yet whether pure ligands for the DAT or mixed compounds, exhibiting a more or less equilibrated affinity to both DAT and SERT, would be preferred in the therapy of cocaine abuse. Knowledge on the chemical prerequisites for binding to the transporters could help to design compounds with different affinity profiles, which in turn could be used as tools in studies on cocaine abuse therapeutics.
    For the entire set of 56 molecules, the biological data for SERT binding (inhibition of [125I]RTI-55 binding to human SERT in clonal cells) are listed in Table 3 and given as pKD values, covering a spectrum of roughly 2.5 log units between the most active (24) and the least active compound (55). Even a first glance at the data indicates that the involvement of the N4 nitrogen in an amide function is advantageous for SERT binding, whereas it is detrimental for DAT binding.
    For the derivation of QSAR models, the entire database except the two permanently charged compounds was used. For the GRIND description, the parameters were changed slightly. First, the probe O, which was found to contribute only marginally, was removed to simplify the description. Then, the number of selected nodes was increased from 100 to 150, to cover better the effects of the hydrophobic regions. Finally, the compounds were described by 180 variables grouped in three blocks:  DRY−DRY (DD), N1−N1 (NN), and DRY−N1 (DN).

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    This study has presented some models describing the selectivity of GBR compounds and mepyramines studied with respect to DAT binding and dopamine reuptake, in the first part, and with respect to DAT and SERT binding in the second part. The aim of these models is not to predict the selectivity of novel molecules but, more than that, to highlight the structural characteristics that are common to most selective compounds with the idea of designing new more selective ligands.
    The interpretation of the models showed some ambiguities, in the sense that the true interactions responsible for the structural features identified as important are still unclear. For example, the high SERT−DAT selectivity of compound 25 is justified in the model by the presence of peculiar hydrophobic and HB acceptor regions (variables DD-26 and NN-33, respectively), both representing the effect of the amide group in fragment C. Such ambiguities are not actually an error but simply reflect the limitation of the QSAR analysis imposed by the structural diversity of the compounds studied. Much on the contrary, the GRIND method does a good job of identifying separately such features allowing us a more detailed interpretation and showing that the general validity of these findings has to be tested in a structurally more diverse set of compounds.

    A. Chemistry. GBR compounds (1−30) were synthesized by van der Zee et al.,12 and mepyramines (31−56) were synthesized by Timmerman et al. (paper in preparation).
    B. Biological Methods. lnhibition of [125I]RTI-55 Binding to hDAT and hSERT in Clonal Cells. Cell Preparation. HEK293 cells expressing hDAT or hSERT inserts are grown to 80% confluence on 150 mm diameter tissue culture dishes and serve as the tissue source. Cell membranes are prepared as follows:  medium is poured off the plate, and the plate is washed with 10 mL of calcium- and magnesium-free phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Lysis buffer (10 mL; 2 mM HEPES with 1 mM EDTA) is added. After 10 min, cells are scraped from plates, poured into centrifuge tubes, and centrifuged at 30 000g for 20 min. The supernatant fluid is removed, and the pellet is resuspended in 20−32 mL of 0.32 M sucrose using a Polytron at setting 7 for 10 s. The resuspension volume depends on the density of binding sites within a cell line and is chosen to reflect binding of 10% or less of the total radioactivity.
    Assay Conditions. Each assay tube contains 50 μL of membrane preparation (about 15 μg of protein), 25 μL of test compound or buffer (Krebs-HEPES, pH 7.4; 122 mM NaCl, 2.5 mM CaCl2, 1.2 mM MgSO4, 1 μM pargyline, 0.2% glucose, and 0.02% ascorbic acid, buffered with 25 mM HEPES), 25 μL of [125 I]RTI-55 (40−80 pM final concentration), and additional buffer sufficient to bring up the final volume to 250 μL. Membranes are preincubated with test compounds for 10 min prior to the addition of the [125 I]RTI-55. The assay tubes are incubated at 25 °C for 90 min. Binding is terminated by filtration over GF/C filters using a Tomtec 96 well cell harvester. Filters are washed twice with 10 mL of ice-cold saline. Scintillation fluid is added to each square, and radioactivity remaining on the filter is determined using a Wallac μ- or β-plate reader. Specific binding is defined as the difference in binding observed in the presence and absence of 5 μM mazindol. Two or three independent competition experiments are conducted with duplicate determinations. GraphPAD Prism is used to analyze the ensuing data, with lC50 values converted to pKD values using the Cheng−Prusoff equation.
    Inhibition of [3H]Dopamine Uptake in HEK293 Cells Expressing hDAT. Cell Preparation. Cells are grown to confluence as described above. The medium is removed, and cells are washed twice with PBS at room temperature. Following the addition of 3 mL of PBS, the plates are warmed in a 25 °C water bath for 5 min. The cells are gently scraped and then triturated with a pipet. Cells from multiple plates are combined. One plate provides enough cells for 48 wells, which is required to generate data on two complete curves for the test compounds.
    Assay Conditions. The assay is conducted in 96 1 mL vials. Krebs-HEPES (350 μL) and test compounds (50 μL) are added to vials and placed in a 25 °C water bath. Specific uptake is defined as the difference in uptake observed in the presence and absence of 5 μM mazindol. Cells (50 μL) are added and preincubated with the test compounds for 10 min. The assay is initiated by the addition of [3H]dopamine or [3H]serotonin (50 μL, 20 nM final concentration). Filtration through Whatman GF/C filters presoaked in 0.05% polyethylenimine is used to terminate uptake after 10 min. IC50 values are calculated applying the GraphPAD Prism program to triplicate curves made up of six drug concentrations each. Two or three independent determinations of each curve are made.
    C. Generation of the 3D Structures. Three-dimensional structures of the data set molecules (neutral form except the permanently charged 24 and 26) were first generated and minimized in Sybyl29 using MMFF force field30 parametrized in vacuo. The structures were further optimized by semiempirical quantum mechanics in a simulated aqueous environment, using AMSOL 6.5.3.31 Charge model SM5.4A was used, as conversion method DERISA and as solvent WATER. The final structures obtained showed a considerable consistence in the conformations found for the different compounds of the series.
    D. Structural Descriptors and Chemometric Analy-sis. GRIND26 was used as implemented in the program ALMOND.32 In the first model reported, ALMOND was used with probes DRY, O, N1, and default parameters:  0.5 Å grid spacing, 100 nodes, 50% of field importance, smoothing window 0.8. In the second model, only probes DRY and N1 were used, with the following parameters:  0.5 Å grid spacing, 150 nodes, 50% of field importance, smoothing window 0.8. The PCA and PLS analysis of the descriptors was also made within ALMOND software. No scaling was applied. The optimal dimensionality of the PLS models was chosen according to the results of the cross validation analyses. Cross validation was made using LOO or assigning the compounds randomly to five groups, making cross validation on these groups, and then repeating the whole procedure 20 times. No relevant differences were found between both validation methods. All computations were run on SGI O2 R10000 and R12000 workstations.
