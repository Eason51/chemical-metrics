    Abstract

    Growing interest in the use of both the logarithm of the partition coefficient of the neutral species in the alkane/water system (log PNalk) and the difference between log PNoct (the logarithm of the partition coefficient of the neutral species in the n-octanol/water system) and log PNalk (Δlog PNoct-alk) in the early stages of drug design has stimulated development of a computational tool based on the Volsurf software to predict virtual (=of each conformer) log PNalk and virtual log PNoct. From these two pieces of data, it is then possible to calculate Δlog PNoct-alk for a given compound as the difference between log PNoct and log PNalk. Once the pKa is known and the legitimacy of neglecting the contribution made by the ionized species has been checked,   it is also possible to calculate log DpHalk, which might be an important lipophilicity descriptor in absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) prediction, from log PNalk.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The important role played by lipophilicity and the related descriptors in governing pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic events has been extensively stressed over recent years, and thus, there have been a number of attempts to use various algorithm to predict log P.1-3 It is important that the partition coefficients expressed as log P be specified (log PN for neutral forms and log PI for ions) because log P values are valid for a single electrical species. In addition, where there is no subscript, it is generally assumed that the considered descriptor refers to the standard n-octanol/water system.
    Among log PN predictive tools, the molecular lipophilicity potential (MLP), developed by Gaillard et al.,4 is noteworthy in that it can take into account molecular flexibility and thus intramolecular effects. In particular, for a given molecule, MLP can be used to calculate the lipophilicity of each conformer (virtual log P). It is well-known that flexible compounds with suitable moieties may exhibit hydrophobic collapse in polar solvents and hydrophilic folding in lipidic environments.5 These conformational changes are postulated to allow flexible compounds to adapt and to mimic their environment.
    Unfortunately, the MLP only works in the n-octanol/water system, whereas today there is considerable demand for methods able to calculate log PN in various solvent systems and in particular in the alkane/water systems; log PNalk is very important alone and in combination with log PNoct (Δlog PNoct-alk = log PNoct − log PNalk) to investigate those inter- and intramolecular hydrogen bonding (HB) properties that determine many pharmacokinetic processes.6-8 In particular, besides the relevance of Δlog PNoct-alk in determining brain penetration,9-11 a close correlation has also been found between log PNalk and permeability, as measured through artificial membranes.12,13
    VolSurf is a computational procedure to produce 2D molecular descriptors from 3D molecular interaction fields (MIFs) obtained employing the GRID force field.14-17 VolSurf compresses the information present in 3D fields into a small number of 2D numerical descriptors that are very simple to understand and to interpret. By use of multivariate statistics and their related plots, valuable insights for drug design, pharmacokinetics profiling, and screening have been obtained.18-20
    Since VolSurf descriptors quantitatively characterize polarity and hydrophobicity (as well as size and shape), which are the main factors governing lipophilicity,21,22 we used them to obtain a partial least squares (PLS) model with which to calculate virtual log PN and the lipophilicity range of molecules in an alkane/water system. We also checked the chemical interpretation of the PLS model. We then applied the same approach to estimate log PNoct and thus Δlog PNoct-alk, which was calculated as the difference between log PNoct and log PNalk. Finally, log PNalk was used as the starting point to calculate log DpHalk, which might be a relevant absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) descriptor.

    Volsurf Model. The first investigation looked for a relation between the Volsurf descriptors (a total of 93 descriptors, 40 from the water (OH2) probe, 28 from the hydrophobic (DRY) probe, 4 OH2-DRY mixed descriptors, 16 additional descriptors for the carbonyl (O) probe, and 5 others not depending on GRID maps18-20), and the 152 experimental log PNalk was looked for. A PLS model was found (see Table 3). Three components explained about 76% of the total variance. The predictive capability of the model (q2 = 0.71) was evaluated using the cross-validation technique (CV) and in particular the leave one out (LOO) procedure. A standard deviation of errors of prediction (SDEP) value of about 0.8 is in line with experimental errors occurring in the alkane/water system (because of the difficulties encountered in manipulating alkanes, the uncertainty affecting log PNalk is greater than that affecting log PNoct).

The reasons for their anomalous behavior will now be discussed. Basically, three main reasons may underlie the poor agreement between experimental and calculated data:  (a) compound flexibility (calculation is made on a conformer that is not the “active” conformer); (b) limits of the method (the software cannot correctly recognize certain chemical moieties; i.e., they are not parametrized by the GRID force field); (c) unreliability of the experimental values.
    Since all the outliers have rotatable bonds, their log PN values could vary in different conformations, and thus, they were first submitted to conformational analysis and then for each compound the lipophilicity of each conformer (virtual log P) is calculated. From an operative standpoint, the final conformers obtained from molecular dynamics simulations were considered as a new test set to be submitted to Volsurf. The results showed that for acetanilide and ibuprofen an analysis of their lipophilicity range (virtual log P of the most lipophilic conformer minus virtual log P of the most hydrophilic one) explains their initially poor prediction. For these two drugs the lipophilicity range (symbolized in Figure 1A by the horizontal segments joining the minimum and the maximum log PNalk values) indicates that a few virtual log PNalk are in the statistically acceptable limits. Conversely, flexibility cannot explain the deviant behavior of 2-methoxybenzoic acid and phenazopyridine.
    To better investigate why experimental and calculated values for these two compounds differ, a Volsurf model on the same 152 compounds was also produced using log P data determined in the standard n-octanol/water system and recovered from the same sources (in their absence, log PNoct values were taken from Avdeef29). The statistics of this second PLS model are shown in Table 3. With the most recent version of the software, Volsurf, version 4.1.2, log PNoct can be computed automatically; however, for the purposes of this study it seemed more rigorous to generate a new model for log PNoct data based on the same 152 compounds used to estimate log PNalk. Nevertheless, the linear correlation between log PNoct obtained by Volsurf on the two different data sets was verified (r2 = 0.80; data not shown).
    Since 2-methoxybenzoic acid and phenazopyridine were not outliers in this model (see Figure 1B), it was possible to exclude errors made by GRID in handling the chemistry of these molecules; we thus tested the experimental validity of the data through the Absolv calculation,30 this being the only method currently available to obtain a prediction of log PNalk. The good agreement (−1.11 and −1.73, respectively, for the log PNalk calculated by Absolv; −0.66 and −1.38 from our prediction; 2.08 and 0.42 from the experimental data) between the two in silico methods based on two different approaches (Absolv31,32 is based on factorizing log PN by solvatochromic equations) suggests that the experimental log PNalk for 2-methoxybenzoic acid and phenazopyridine could be affected by experimental uncertainty.

Absolv could estimate only log PNalk for cicletanide (the chemical structure of guanabenz was too different from those of the training set for Absolv to estimate its solvatochromic descriptors safely), again confirming our hypothesis of experimental uncertainty (0.95 is the Absolv result, 0.43 is our prediction, and −1.73 is the experimental log PNalk). In the absence of other evidence, the same hypothesis was accepted for guanabenz.
    Interpretation of PLS Models. To interpret the statistical model correctly, it is necessary to combine the variable importance in the projection (VIP) plot with the PLS coefficient table. The VIP plot (Figure 4) displays the VIP values as a column plot sorted in descending order with confidence intervals derived from jack-knifing.33 The VIP values reflect the importance of terms in the model both with respect to Y and with respect to X,33 but they do not consider the sign of the coefficients. Conversely, the PLS coefficients (Table 4) represents the contribution of each single descriptor to the model only with respect to Y. Coefficients with positive values increase log PNalk (log PNoct), and the reverse is true for coefficients with negative values. In practice, the VIP plot shows which are the most important descriptors, while the PLS coefficients table indicates which of them increases or decreases log PNalk (log PNoct).

    For the sake of clarity, we compared the VIP plot and the PLS coefficients found for the model, taking log PNalk as the dependent variable, with the analogous results obtained using the PLS model generated by the same 152 compounds, taking log PNoct as the dependent variable. Selecting 1.5 as the starting VIP value, it is clear from Figure 4 that the most relevant descriptors to predict log PNalk (Cw2OH2, Cw3OH2, Cw4OH2, Cw5OH2, D1DRY, Cw6OH2, BV11OH2, and BV12OH2) are different from the most important descriptors to predict log PNoct (D1DRY, ROH2, D2DRY, D3DRY, Iw1OH2, POL, VOH2, MW, SOH2, Emin1DRY, Iw2OH2, D4DRY, W1O).
    Cw1OH2−Cw6OH2 are capacity factors and represent the ratio between the hydrophilic regions and the molecular surface. Since they are positive and their coefficients are negative (Table 4), the larger is the descriptor, the lower is the log PNalk. The same is true for BV11OH2 and BV12OH2 (the volumes of the hydrophilic zones that make the most significant contribution to the total volume at −1 and at −3 kcal/mol; they are close to the total volume, they indicate that a single zone governs the total volume with the probe considered). Conversely D1DRY (the hydrophobic regions at −0.2 kcal/mol) contributes to increasing log PNalk.

    Taken together, these findings suggest that Volsurf descriptors can describe partitioning of neutral species both in n-octanol/water and in alkane/water but their contribution varies with the system investigated. In particular, the significant contribution of the water-derived probe descriptors to decreasing log PNalk could confirm that alkane is less able than n-octanol in accepting polar groups. In addition, the crucial role played by volume descriptors in predicting log PNoct could be ascribed to the comparable HB acceptor/donor properties of n-octanol and water. These results are in agreement with solvatochromic equations, which are the most widely used approach to factorizing log PN data and indicate that log PNalk is generally lower than log PNoct, depending on the difference in hydrogen-bond donor capacity coefficients of the solute (α, −0.15 in n-octanol/water and −3.54 in alkane/water) and on the hydrogen-bond acceptor capacity (β, −3.51 in n-octanol/water and −5.35 in alkane/water). 22
    Volsurf descriptors for warfarin and desipramine (for chemical structures, see Figure 2) are given in Table 4. Warfarin has high log PNoct (3.54) and low log PNalk (0.05); conversely, desipramine has high log PNoct (3.79) and high log PNalk (3.38). As shown above, the difference in log PNalk should be chiefly due to differences in Cw1OH2−Cw6OH2 descriptors, which are larger for warfarin than for desipramine (Table 4). Large Cw1OH2−Cw6OH2 values indicate that the hydrophilic regions are widely spread over the molecular surface. This is true for warfarin but not for desipramine, for which the hydrophilic zone is localized around the lateral chain (Figure 5 shows the GRID 3D molecular field for the water probe for the two compounds at −2 kcal/mol). On the other hand, the similar volume and surface values for the two compounds might explain their similar log PNoct values.
    Which Virtual log P? By their very definition, methods to calculate 3D/4D log P are able to estimate a large number of virtual log P values for each compound.
    For rigid and semirigid structures virtual log P values are very close, and thus, the log P range is small, i.e., comparable to the standard error affecting an experimental determination. For flexible compounds, the log P range may be very wide; i.e., it may cover some log P units, provided that flexibility considerably alters the distribution of intramolecular interactions5 by hydrophobic collapse or by hydrophilic folding.3 In general, flexible compounds with suitable moieties may exhibit hydrophobic collapse (conformational change whereby a solute maximizes the superposition of hydrophobic interactions) in polar solvents and may exhibit hydrophilic folding (conformational change whereby a solute maximizes both the number and the strength of internal electrostatic interactions, mainly H-bonds) in nonpolar environments. Both phenomena are responsible for an increase in the log P range.
    After a certain drug is fixed, which pool of conformers is responsible for a given pharmacokinetic event? Or in other words, to prepare the matrix for an ADME prediction study in the absence of any experimental aid, which conformer and which corresponding virtual log P should we use for a flexible molecule? We propose a strategy to determine the correct selection. First, “averaged conformers” (reasonably averaged 3D structures generated by CONCORD or CORINA algorithms and minimized by the MMFF94x force field) should be generated for the whole database and tested. For most chemicals, the averaged conformer is sufficiently accurate for the compound to be accepted in the final statistical model. If the averaged conformer is an outlier, the whole lipophilicity range of the molecule must be calculated and the conformer with the virtual log P closest to that predicted by the model should be carefully analyzed in comparison with the most stable (the lowest energy) conformer and also with the averaged conformer. Any differences in intramolecular effects must be evidenced and indicated as being responsible for the peculiar pharmacokinetic behavior. A final remark concerns the minimization method used to obtain the most stable conformer. As recently shown,34 at least two optimal geometries must be sought:  the first should simulate the most stable conformer in apolar solvents and thus must be obtained under vacuum conditions; the second should simulate the most stable conformer in water and thus require the consideration of the solvent effect (see Methods).
    A detailed study of the dependence of the log P range on the number of rotatable bonds and the nature of the biphasic system is being undertaken in our laboratories, and results will soon be published.
    Calculation of Δlog PNoct-alk. As already mentioned, Δlog PNoct-alk is a good measure of solute H-bonding and related biological phenomena. Since tedious experimental work is required to obtain Δlog PNoct-alk, considerable interest surrounds methods that can predict this descriptor and more generally H-bonding properties. We were here able to estimate both log PNalk and log PNoct and thus to calculate their difference, Δlog PNoct-alk. Because of Volsurf's assumptions, it would also have been possible to make a PLS model using Δlog PNoct-alk as a dependent variable, but because of error propagation, we preferred not to develop this model.
    For Artursson's well-known series of compounds,35 we found a relationship between Δlog PNoct-alk, calculated as described here (Table 5), and the fraction of dose absorbed orally in humans (Abs, %) (Figure 6). A sigmoidal relationship of good statistical quality (r2 = 0.80) links the absorption descriptor with HB parameters. This result confirms the findings obtained a few years ago by some of us using the molecular hydrogen bonding potentials (MHBPs):36,37 for the same series of compounds we showed37 that the H-bonding donor capacity (MHBPdo) appeared to be better able than the H-bonding acceptor capacity (MHBPac) to predict oral absorption through a sigmoidal relationship similar to that shown in Figure 6. This is absolutely reasonable because Δlog PNoct-alk is mainly a function of the HB donor properties of the solutes (for this series of compounds the linear relationship with MHBPdo shows r2 = 0.80; data not shown).

    Conclusion

    A simultaneous optimization of pharmacodynamics and pharmacokinetics at a very early stage of drug discovery is currently a fundamental request in drug design. Lipophilicity parameters (not only the standard log DpHoct but also log DpHalk and Δlog PNoct-alk) play a crucial role in governing the behavior of drugs and/or drug candidates in both pharmacodynamic and pharmacokinetic phases.
    The study shows how to predict a number of lipophilicity descriptors by using the same computational approach (Volsurf coupled with molecular dynamics simulation). This strategy presents a number of advantages:  (a) being obtained by the same approach, lipophilicity data concerning different solvent systems can safely be compared with one another; (b) the influence of compounds' flexibility on their lipophilicity is taken into consideration; (c) the chemical parametrization is founded on the GRID force field that is in continuous development by integration of experimental crystallographic data (e.g., the N-oxide and sulfonamides functions are now correctedly treated), and thus, the prediction of log P of compounds containing uncommon chemical moieties can easily be achieved; (d) the statistical approach to generate PLS models is strong enough to guarantee a prediction of high precision (and thus a careful check of experimental data reliability that in alkanes/water systems is often poor).
    The major drawback of the method is represented by its failure when applied to charged species, and in fact, work is in progress in our laboratories to overcome this limitation.

    Computational Procedure.3D-Structures Generation. The molecules were built using SMILES as an input in the MOE molecular package (MOE, version 2003.04, software available from Chemical Computing Group Inc., 1010 Sherbrooke Street West, Suite 910, Montreal, H3A 2R7, Canada; http://www.chemcomp.com). After MOE conversion from 2D to 3D, the 3D structures obtained were first optimized by MMFF94x and then optimized by AM1.
    VolSurf v412 Descriptors Calculation. The GRID16 3D molecular interaction fields (MIFs) were calculated between the molecules in the data set and three different probes (water, hydrophobic, and carbonyl). MIFs were automatically converted into molecular descriptors using a standard procedure called VolSurf, which has been presented in detail elsewhere.19,20 A GRID box with a 0.5 Å spacing that extended 5 Å beyond the analyzed molecules was used. AM1 option was used to calculate molecular charges.
    Chemometric Analysis. PCA and PLS analysis tools are implemented in the VolSurf v412 software. In addition, SIMCA-P33 was used to obtain the VIP plots.
    Molecular Dynamics Simulation. The conformational hypersurface of compounds was explored by a hybrid Monte Carlo (HMC) module implemented in MOE as described in detail elsewhere.34 HMC conditions were the following:  iterations = 20000; save period = 100; MD steps = 1, MD time step = 0.005 ps; temperature = 2000 K; equilibration steps = 100. Minimization was performed under in vacuo conditions using the MMFF94x force field, and to investigate the influence of the medium on conformation preferences, the effect of solvation was also tested in the minimization step by including the energy of solvation associated with the molecule being in a continuum solvent model. A set of electrostatic corrections are calculated with the GB/SA method39 and applied to the MMFF94x force field as implemented in MOE.34
    All calculations described herein were performed on a Linux based biprocessor Appro 1124 server and on a standard IBM compatible computer.
