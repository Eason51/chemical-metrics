    Abstract

    c-Met has emerged as an attractive target for targeted cancer therapy because of its abnormal activation in many cancer cells. To identify high potent and selective c-Met inhibitors, we started with profiling the potency and in vitro metabolic stability of a reported hit 7. By rational design, a novel sulfonylpyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridine 9 with improved DMPK properties was discovered. Further elaboration of π–π stacking interactions and solvent accessible polar moieties led to a series of highly potent and selective type I c-Met inhibitors. On the basis of in vitro and in vivo pharmacological and pharmacokinetics studies, compound 46 was selected as a preclinical candidate for further anticancer drug development.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    c-Met, also termed as hepatocyte growth factor receptor (HGFR), belongs to a unique subfamily of receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs).(1-3) Since its discovery in 1984 as an oncogenic fusion protein TPR-MET,(4) c-Met was extensively investigated for its structure and functions. Studies revealed that c-Met forms a heterodimer by connecting a short extracellular α chain and a membrane spanning β chain through a disulfide bond.(5, 6) Binding of its natural ligand—hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) to c-Met initiates its kinase phosphorylation activity and then triggers a series of downstream signaling pathways including PI3K-AKT-mTOR, Ras-MEK-ERK.(7-10) Physiologically, the HGF/c-Met axis plays important roles in mammalian development and tissue homeostasis.(11, 12) Abnormal activation of c-Met has been reported in many types of cancers, occurring as a consequence of gene amplification or rearrangement, transcriptional regulation, as well as autocrine or paracrine ligand stimulation.(13-16) Importantly, overexpression of HGF and/or c-Met has been associated with poor prognosis or metastatic progression. In addition, Met amplification is observed in 20% of acquired resistance to EGFR inhibitors.(17) Tumor microenvironment elicits innate resistance to BRAF inhibitors via HGF secretion.(18) Thus, c-Met has emerged as an attractive target for targeted cancer therapy.(19)
    To disrupt the c-Met signaling, several strategies were exploited, including antibodies targeting HGF/c-Met and small molecular inhibitors binding to the kinase catalytic domain of β chain of c-Met.(20, 21) Since the activation of c-Met signaling pathway can be HGF-independent,(22, 23) the small molecular inhibitor targeting the intracellular kinase domain was predicted to have its unique pharmacological mechanism and may cover a potentially larger patient population than that of HGF/c-Met antibody treatment, which is only responsible for HGF-dependent cases. On the basis of the analysis of binding modes, the small molecular inhibitors of c-Met can be roughly classified into three types: type I c-Met inhibitors bind to the ATP binding pocket in “U” shape, which usually interacts with the residue Met1211 at the hinge part to anchor the inhibitor and forms a typical π–π stacking interaction with residue Tyr1230. Figure 1 lists some type I inhibitors, including a marketed drug crizotinib (1),(24) and two phase II clinical trial compounds (2, 3).(25, 26) Implied by the unique U-shaped binding mode, type I inhibitors all show good selectivity for c-Met, which is thought to give less side effects in cancer treatment. Type II c-Met inhibitors usually adopt an extended conformation, starting from the solvent-accessible part to hinge and further stretching to the deep hydrophobic Ile1145 subpocket near the C-helix region. As exemplified by one marketed drug (cabozantinib, 4),(27) these compounds are multikinase inhibitors and usually strongly inhibit VEGFR and other homological kinases as well. Except for above-mentioned well classified inhibitors, there are other atypical c-Met inhibitors (all classified as type III c-Met inhibitors), such as MK-2461 (5)(28) and ARQ-197 (6).(29) As revealed from a cocrystal structure of an analogue of 5 bound to c-Met, the small molecule occupies the subpocket from gatekeeper Leu1157 to solvent exposed area. Although it adopted a U-turn at sulfonamide and made hydrogen bonding with Asp1222, it has no π stacking moiety as in type I inhibitors. For ARQ-197, it was initially discovered with a phenotype cell viability assay and later was identified to be a non-ATP competitive c-Met inhibitor.
    In 2009 Porter et al. reported the discovery of 4-azaindoles as c-Met inhibitors through a high throughput screening campaign and structure-based optimization.(30) We noticed the moderate cellular activity of this interesting scaffold and decided to investigate this structure type in detail. Toward this end, we assessed the scaffold utility from the perspective of enzymatic activity and metabolism. A novel more metabolically stable scaffold was discovered, and further optimization resulted in a series of high potent, selective c-Met inhibitors. On the basis of the extensive in vivo pharmacology and pharmacokinetics studies, compound 46 was selected as promising candidate and subjected to further development.

    Inhibitor Design and Lead Identification

    Sulfonyl-4-azaindoles acting as novel c-Met inhibitors were discovered by Porter et al. from a high throughput screening campaign.(30) The most potent compound in the series was reported to have IC50 of about 9 nM in the enzymatic assay. As demonstrated by the cocrystal structure (PDB entry code 2WD1) of a representative compound 1-((2-nitrophenyl)sulfonyl)-1H-pyrrolo[3,2-b]pyridine-6-carboxamide with c-Met, the inhibitor binds at the ATP binding site in a typical U-shape: the pyrrolo[3,2-b]pyridine ring interacts with the hinge segment around residue Met1160, and the nitrophenyl group forms face to face π–π stacking with the residue Tyr1230, further enhancing the affinity by making a hydrogen bond between the nitro group and the backbone NH of Asp1222. The middle sulfonyl group makes two aromatic ring systems forming a right angle and perpendicular to each other. Although the series of pyrrolopyridine compounds is unique as c-Met inhibitors and demonstrates its utility with high potency in enzymatic assay, it only showed moderate cellular activities with GI50 about 0.1 μM in MKN45 and A549 cell lines. We thought this unique binding pattern deserves to be further explored, especially for the optimization of druglike properties. Thus, compound 7 with this 1-sulfonylpyrrolo[3,2-b]pyridine scaffold was synthesized to reinspect the utility of the scaffold. As shown in Figure 2, compound 7 showed a good 92.8% inhibition at 1 μM concentration in enzymatic assay. To assess its druglike property, the metabolic stability of 7 was measured in the human liver microsome assay. The results indicated that it is not so stable, only with an intrinsic clearance rate of 88 μL min–1 mg–1. After detailed analysis of the intermediate metabolites by incubating compound 7 in human liver microsome, it was shown that the pyrrolo[3,2-b]pyridine ring was easily hydroxylized. This is consistent with the works by Dalvie et al.,(31) who reviewed the biotransformation of common aromatic heterocycles and showed the pyrrole group is electron rich and prone to react with metabolic enzymes especially at the C2 position. Therefore, the more metabolically stable pyrazole ring was selected to replace the pyrrole and two compounds (8 and 9) were designed and synthesized accordingly. After testing two compounds in enzymatic assay, the more active compound (9) was studied in the human liver microsome assay, and the result indicated it was much more stable than compound 7. Hence, compound 9 was selected as a novel starting point for further modification.

    After selected the scaffold structure, the initial optimization was focused on enhancing the π–π stacking interaction with the residue Tyr1230, which is confirmed to be the dominant factor for selectivity and potency of type I c-Met inhibitors. As shown in Table 1, a variety of substituted arylsulfonylpyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridines were prepared and tested in c-Met enzymatic assay. Compared with compound 9, the unsubstituted phenyl derivative (10) was less active, which may be caused by losing a hydrogen bond with Asp1222. To include this hydrogen bonding feature into the molecules, we prepared compounds with CF3 (13–15), cyan (16), methoxyl (17), or carbonyl (18) substituted phenyl ring. As demonstrated by compounds 11–15, the position of the substitution at benzene group was important for the inhibitory activity: the meta-substituted fluorobenzene 11 was more potent than the para-substituted one (12). Similarly, the compound with ortho- or meta-substituted trifluoromethylbenzene group was at least 12-fold more potent than the compound with para-substitution. This trend was also supported by the 4-acetylphenylsulfonyl derivative with reduced IC50 of 4.8 μM. Interestingly, as demonstrated by compound 21 (IC50 = 4.2 nM), replacing monocycle with bicycle groups improves the activity. By comparing compounds 20 and 21, it is found that in the ring substituted with the sulfonyl group, 21 has a nitrogen atom that could form a hydrogen bond with Asp1222. It is not trivial, since this important substitution pattern pointed to a different direction. On the basis of the similarity of the 6-chloroimidazo[2,1-b]thiazole to other corresponding π stacking rings in various type I c-Met inhibitors, we can hybridize this series of sulfonylpyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridines with some well-explored type I c-Met inhibitors.(20)
    Following this direction, we prepared three analogs (22–24) and measured their enzymatic activities. As listed in Table 1, these three compounds all showed high potency, with IC50 lower than 40 nM. According to the synthetic accessibility and binding affinity, compound 22 was selected to check whether it is suitable to be advanced as lead compound.
    Compound 22 was tested in non-small lung cancer (NSCLC) EBC-1 cells for antiproliferation effect, and we found that it showed high potency with IC50 of 55.5 ± 13.6 nM. Since specificity is an important issue in current kinase drug development, a panel of other 16 diversified kinases was set up to assess the enzymatic selectivity. As indicated in Table 2, compound 22 is a selective inhibitor toward c-Met, only showing moderate inhibition for RON, a c-Met homology kinase in the same subfamily, and weak effects on other 15 kinases with less than 50% inhibition at the concentration of 1 μM.
    The inhibitor was further subjected to in vitro metabolic stability assays and in vivo pharmacokinetic test. From the multispecies liver microsome stability study, compound 22 showed excellent stability with clearance rate of 9, 4, 10 μL min–1 mg–1 in human, rat, and dog liver microsomes, respectively. Compared to the starting point compound 7, the metabolic stability of 22 in human liver microsome was increased considerably, which recapitulated the utility of sulfonylpyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridine scaffold. In addition to the liver microsome assays, five cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes commonly metabolizing exogenous chemicals were used to test the direct inhibition of compound 22. As listed in Table 3, the compound showed favorable metabolic properties, as the inhibition ratio for five CYPs was less than 50% even at the compound concentration of 10 μM.
    The selected compound 22 was further evaluated in the preliminary pharmacokinetic study in mice (see Table 3). Oral dosing with compound 22 at the level of 10 mg/kg shows high exposure (AUC0–∞ = 4098.7 ng/(mL·h)) and acceptable half-life about 1.45 h. The compound has a high Cmax about 2131.4 ng/mL. These desirable PK properties strengthen our confidence of selecting compound 22 as a lead structure.

    Lead Optimization

    The lead compound 22 was subjected to further optimization, which focused on two ring systems connecting to the pyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridine scaffold. Utilizing the crystal structure, we docked the lead compound into the binding pocket of c-Met. The predicted binding conformation between 22 and c-Met revealed several expected essential interactions, including π–π stacking and two hydrogen bonding interactions: one is between N4 of pyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridine and the hinge part (Met1160), and another is between N1 of imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine and Asp1222. An obvious direction for further optimizing the π–π stacking part is substituting various groups at the 6-position of imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine ring, where a large room exists and connects to the solvent accessible range through a short channel.

    Incorporation of various groups to the imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine ring led to compounds 25–31 with diversified activities. As shown in Table 4, adding a bromine atom at the C6 position of the ring (25) decreases the activity more than 10-fold and substituted with acetamide (29) or alkylamine (30) attenuated the affinity considerably, with IC50 larger than 1 μM. Interestingly, introducing alkoxyl group (26) or ester groups (27, 28) at this position improves the binding activity. The most potent compound in Table 4 is 31, which was prepared by coupling a benzene moiety at C6 position of imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine and showed an intriguing enzymatic activity (IC50 = 0.1 nM). This instructed us to further explore the SAR around this position by decorating it with various aromatic functional groups.

    As listed in Table 5, various substituted benzene groups and aromatic heterocycles were selected to attach at C6 position of imidazopyridine ring. Compared to the inhibitor 31, additional monosubstitution at the para-position of the benzene moiety reduced the binding affinity toward c-Met, as exemplified by compound 36 with IC50 larger than 100 nM, as well as compounds 39 and 42 with IC50 larger than 1000 nM. Compound with two methoxyl groups substituted at meta- and para-position of the benzene (37) showed much higher potency to c-Met, compared with compound 36. This indicated that the meta-position is critical to the activity. This was verified by compound 35 (IC50 = 1.4 nM), a bioisosteric inhibitor of 37, and compound 38 with a meta-cyanophenyl fragment (IC50 = 1.5 nM). Adding difluoro group to the phenyl ring (40) results in a more than 10-fold loss of binding activity in the enzymatic assay. Surprisingly, meta-substitution with trifluoromethyl group decreased the activity. Changing the phenyl ring to other aromatic hetorocyles is tolerable, as indicated from the activities of compounds 32–34. Coupling the thiazole group to imidazopyridine results in the most potent compound 32 in this series, with the IC50 value of about 0.04 nM. When being compared to 31, the activity of methylpyrazole compound (33) slightly reduced to 0.5 nM, while the activity of fluoropyridine derivative (34) decreased about 100-fold. Comparing compound 43 to 33, it was shown that the large and polar piperidine substituted at pyrazole ring is not favorable for the binding. The compounds with enzymatic activity better than 100 nM of IC50 value were further assessed in cellular antiproliferation assay, and the results (Table 5) indicated only 33, 34, and 38 have good cellular activities, especially compound 33 with more than 50% inhibition ratio even at concentration of 1 nM. Therefore, the methylpyrazole group was fixed for late optimization.

    As revealed from the crystal structure and our docking study, the methylpyrazole in compound 22 pointed to the solvent exposure area in kinase domain of c-Met. This hydrophilic site is relevant to the optimization of druglike properties of kinase inhibitors as indicated in many cases of kinase drug development. Further modification was carried out at this portion to gain further insight on the potential of improvement of potency and solubility of the series. Listed in Table 6, 10 compounds with varied polar fragment attaching the C6 of the pyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridine were prepared and tested in both enzymatic and EBC-1 cell antiproliferation assay. All of these inhibitors showed very high potency toward c-Met, with the IC50 ranging from 3 to 0.3 nM. Compared to compound 33, removing the methyl group on the 6-pyrazolepyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridine (44) resulted in about 4-fold loss of c-Met enzymatic activity, while changing to piperidine (45) or isopropylpiperidine (46) or cyclopentylpiperidine (47) retains the enzymatic activity. Exposing NH of pyrazole or NH of piperidine (44 or 45) at this part is harmful for the cellular antiproliferation activity. Substituted phenyl group at 6-position of pyrazolo[4,3-b]pyridine (48) is tolerable but with a 13-fold loss in the cellular activity assay if comparing to compound 33. Further modification of the phenyl group by adding methylmorpholine (49) or acylmethylpiperazine (50) enhanced the potency to at least 0.4 nM in cellular assay. The nonaromatic tetrahydropyrdine or substituted compounds were also tested (51–53), showing a dramatic loss in cellular activity.
    In vitro metabolism studies were applied to four inhibitors with high potency in cellular assay. As shown in Table 7, four compounds showed relatively weak inhibition in five CYP enzymes at a compound concentration of 10 μM. Compounds 47 and 49 exhibited high clearance ratio in human/rat liver microsome, indicating that they may have unfavorable issues associated with metabolic stability. The time dependent inhibition assays of five CYPs were also performed on these compounds, showing no covalent binding except for compound 49. Taking together, compound 46 with clean DMPK properties was selected as candidate for detailed pharmacological and pharmacokinetic investigations.

    Profiling the Candidate Compound 46. Compound 46 Is a Selective c-Met Kinase Inhibitor

    By use of an ELISA-based enzymatic assay, compound 46 was distinguished for its high potency against c-Met kinase activity with an average IC50 value of 0.6 ± 0.1 nM. Then we were prompted to investigate whether this potency was specifically against c-Met. Compound 46 was evaluated against a panel of 19 diversified kinases. In contrast to its high potency against c-Met, compound 46 barely inhibited the kinase activity of other tested tyrosine kinases, including c-Met family member RON and highly homologous kinase Axl, Tyro-3 (IC50 > 1 μM) (Table 8), indicating that it is a selective c-Met inhibitor.

    Cellular effects of Compound 46. Compound 46 Effectively Inhibits c-Met Phosphorylation and Downstream Signaling

    To further evaluate the cellular activity of compound 46 targeting c-Met kinase, we analyzed its effects on the phosphorylation of c-Met and its downstream signaling components Akt and Erk by using gastric cancer cell MKN45 and non-small lung cancer (NSCLC) cell EBC-1 harboring MET gene amplification. As shown in Figure 3A and Figure 3B, compound 46 inhibited the phosphorylation level of c-Met, Akt, and Erk in a dose-dependent manner. Extending the study to NSCLC cell A549 that responds well to HGF stimulation, and model cell line BaF3/TPR-Met containing MET chromosomal rearrangement (TPR-MET), we found that a similar trend was followed (Figure 3C, Figure 3D). These results suggested that compound 46 exhibits an effective inhibition of c-Met activation and its downstream signaling, regardless of the mechanistic complexity in c-Met activation across different cellular contexts.

    Compound 46 Selectively Inhibits c-Met-Driven Cancer Cells Proliferation via G1/S Arrest

    To elucidate the impact of compound 46 on c-Met mediated cancer cell proliferation, a broad panel of human cancer cell lines harboring different backgrounds of c-Met expression/activation was used. As shown in Figure 4A, compound 46 significantly inhibited the cell proliferation of c-Met constitutively activated EBC-1, MKN45, SUN5, BaF3/TPR-MET cells, with the IC50 values of 0.27 ± 0.03 nM, 1.10 ± 0.12 nM, 1.15 ± 0.22 nM, 0.32 ± 0.04 nM, respectively. Otherwise, the compound showed much less potency in cancer cell lines with low c-Met expression or activation (IC50 > 10 μM). These results indicated that compound 46 specifically inhibits c-Met-dependent cancer cell growth, also implying it is not a cytotoxic agent.
    Blocking c-Met activation inhibits the cell proliferation via arresting cells in G1/S phase.(32, 33) Therefore, the cell-cycle distribution was analyzed by flow cytometry to assess the association. We observed that treatment with compound 46 induced a G1/S phase arrest in EBC-1 cell, with 90% of the cell population in G1 phase at the 20 nM concentration of the inhibitor (versus 50% in the vehicle control group) (Figure 4B, Figure 4C). No obvious sub-G1 cell population was observed upon compound 46 treatment (Figure 4B), largely excluding the occurrence of induced apoptosis.

    Compound 46 Inhibits c-Met-Dependent Cell Migration, Invasion, Scattering, and Invasive Growth

    In addition to the effect of promoting cell proliferation, c-Met activation by HGF also enhances cell motility and invasion, which is associated with increased tumor aggressive and extensive metastatic phenotype.(34) Thus, the impact of compound 46 was evaluated in this regard. NCI-H441 cells were treated with HGF in the presence of compound 46, and the migratory and invasive abilities were determined using Transwell-based assays. Compared to vehicle group, compound 46 dose-dependently suppresses HGF-induced cell migration and invasion and at the concentration of 20 nM could almost block the movement of NCI-H441 cells (Figure 5A–D).
    Activation of HGF/c-Met axis could also induce cell scattering, a phenomenon contributing to the invasiveness and metastasis of cancer cell.(35, 36) It has been well documented that MDCK cells, which normally grow in clusters, undergo colony dispersal and become migratory, fibroblast-like cells upon HGF stimulation. As shown in Figure 5E, we observed that treatment with compound 46 dose-dependently inhibited HGF-induced MDCK cell scattering, even reversing the scattering phenotype at a concentration of 4 nM.
    Activating c-Met triggers a complex genetic program termed invasive growth, which constitutes multiple biological processes including migration, matrix degradation, cell multiplication, and survival.(37, 38) In vitro, this morphogenetic program is recapitulated by stimulating cultured MDCK epithelial cells in suspension in a three-dimensional extracellular matrix (collagen) with HGF. As shown in Figure 5F, in the absence of HGF, MDCK cell only showed round cysts, while upon HGF stimulation, a multicellular branched growth phenotype was observed. Compound 46 significantly inhibited this branching morphogenesis of the MDCK cells in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 5F), demonstrating its suppression effect on HGF-stimulated c-Met-mediated invasive growth phenotype.

    In Vivo Antitumor Activity of Compound 46

    To assess the in vivo antitumor efficacy of compound 46, an EBC-1 xenograft mouse model, which is specifically driven by constitutive activation of c-Met, was used. Compound 46 was administered orally at doses of 50 or 10 mg/kg once daily for 21 consecutive days. The results showed that, compared with the vehicle group, compound 46 could suppress tumor growth in a dose-dependent manner, with tumor growth inhibitory rate of 104.67% and 69.71% at the doses of 50 mg/kg and 10 mg/kg, respectively (Figure 6A). Consistent with the suppressed tumor growth, a marked reduction in the intratumoral phosphorylation of c-Met and downstream Akt and Erk was observed at 1 h after oral administration of 10 mg/kg compound 46 (Figure 4B). Taken together, compound 46 showed a robust antitumor efficacy that was correlated with the inhibition of c-Met-mediated signaling in a c-Met-dependent tumor model. Therefore, compound 46 was selected as a promising c-Met inhibitor advanced into further drug development.

    On the basis of a reported HTS hit, we synthesized compound 7 to recheck the utility of its core scaffold.     ELISA Kinase Assay

    The effects of compound 46 on the activities of various tyrosine kinases were determined using enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays (ELISAs) with purified recombinant proteins. Briefly, 20 μg/mL poly(Glu, Tyr)4:1 (Sigma, St. Louis, MO, USA) was precoated in 96-well plates as a substrate. A 50 μL aliquot of 10 μmol/L ATP solution diluted in kinase reaction buffer (50 mmol/L HEPES [pH 7.4], 50 mmol/L MgCl2, 0.5 mmol/L MnCl2, 0.2 mmol/L Na3VO4, and 1 mmol/L DTT) was added to each well; 1 μL of various concentrations of compound 46 diluted in 1% DMSO (v/v) (Sigma, St. Louis, MO, USA) was then added to each reaction well. DMSO (1%, v/v) was used as the negative control. The kinase reaction was initiated by the addition of purified tyrosine kinase proteins diluted in 49 μL of kinase reaction buffer. After incubation for 60 min at 37 °C, the plate was washed three times with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) containing 0.1% Tween 20 (T-PBS). Anti-phosphotyrosine (PY99) antibody (100 μL; 1:500, diluted in 5 mg/mL BSA T-PBS) was then added. After a 30 min incubation at 37 °C, the plate was washed three times, and 100 μL of horseradish peroxidase-conjugated goat anti-mouse IgG (1:2000, diluted in 5 mg/mL BSA T-PBS) was added. The plate was then incubated at 37 °C for 30 min and washed 3 times. A 100 μL aliquot of a solution containing 0.03% H2O2 and 2 mg/mL o-phenylenediamine in 0.1 mol/L citrate buffer (pH 5.5) was added. The reaction was terminated by the addition of 50 μL of 2 mol/L H2SO4 as the color changed, and the plate was analyzed using a multiwell spectrophotometer (SpectraMAX 190, from Molecular Devices, Palo Alto, CA, USA) at 490 nm. The inhibition rate (%) was calculated using the following equation: [1 – (A490/A490 control)] × 100. The IC50 values were calculated from the inhibition curves in two separate experiments.

    Cell Culture

    The human gastric cancer cell lines SNU-5; the human prostate cancer cell line DU-145; the human lung cancer cell lines A549, NCI-H441, and NCI-H1299, H460; the human breast cancer cell lines MDA-MB-231; the human colon cancer cell lines HCT-116, SW116; the human hepatic cancer cell HepG2, BEL-7404; the human pancreatic cancer cell PANC-1; and the Madin–Daby canine kidney (MDCK) cell line were purchased from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA). The hepatic carcinoma cell line SMMC- 7721 was obtained from the Institute of Biochemistry and Cell Biology, Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). The human gastric cell line MKN45 and the human NSCLC cell line EBC-1 were purchased from Joint Conference of Restoration Branches (JCRB). All the cell lines were routinely maintained in media according to the suppliers’ recommendations. A cell line, derived from the parental cell line BaF3, was constructed to stably express a constitutively active TPR-Met and was designated as BaF3/TPR-Met.

    Western Blot Analysis

    A549 cell was serum-starved for 24 h, treated with the indicated dose of compound 46 for 1 h at 37 °C, stimulated with HGF (100 ng/mL, PeproTech, Rocky Hill, NJ, USA) for 15 min, and then lysed in 1× sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) sample buffer. EBC-1, MKN45, and BaF3/TPR-Met cells were treated with the indicated dose of compound 46 for 1 h at 37 °C and then lysed in 1× SDS sample buffer. The cell lysates were subsequently resolved by 10% SDS–PAGE and transferred to nitrocellulose membranes. The membranes were probed with the appropriate primary antibodies [c-Met and cyclin E (Santa Cruz, CA, USA), phospho-c-Met, phospho-ERK1/2, ERK1/2, phospho-AKT, AKT (all from Cell Signaling Technology, Beverly, MA, USA), and GAPDH (KangChen Biotech, Shanghai, China)] and then with horseradish peroxidase-conjugated anti-rabbit or anti-mouse IgG. The immunoreactive proteins were detected using an enhanced chemiluminescence detection reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Rockford, IL, USA).

    Cell Proliferation Assay

    Cells were seeded in 96-well tissue culture plates. On the next day, the cells were exposed to various concentrations of compounds and further cultured for 72 h. Cell proliferation was then determined using sulforhodamine B (SRB, from Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) or the thiazolyl blue tetrazolium bromide (MTT, from Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) assay. The IC50 values were calculated by concentration–response curve fitting using the four-parameter method.

    Cell Cycle Distribution Assay

    EBC-1 cell (2 × 105) were seeded in six-well plates overnight and treated with different concentrations of compound 46 on the following day. After 24 h, the cells were collected by EDTA-free trypsinization, centrifuged at 900 rpm for 5 min, and fixed in 70% ethanol at −20 °C for 1 h. The cells were then centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 5 min, resuspended in 500 μL of PBS containing 20 ng/mL RNase and 10 ng/mL propidium iodide, incubated in the dark for 30 min at room temperature, and analyzed using flow cytometry (FACsCalibur, from BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA). The data were analyzed using Flowjo software.

    Migration Assay and Invasion Assay

    For the migration assay, NCI-H441 cells suspended in serum- free medium (1.5 × 105 cells per well) were seeded in 24-well Transwell plates (pore size, 8 μm; Corning). The bottom chambers were filled with serum-free medium supplemented with HGF (100 ng/mL), and 0.8, 4.0, and 20 nmol/L compound 46 was added to both sides of the membrane. The cultures were maintained for 24 h. The migrating cells were fixed in precold alcohol (95%) at 4 °C and stained with crystal violet (0.1%) for 15 min at room temperature, followed by the removal of nonmotile cells at the top of the filter using a cotton swab. The dye that was taken up by the cells bound to the membrane was released by the addition of 100 μL of 10% acetic acid, and the absorbance of the resulting solution was measured at 600 nm using a multiwell spectrophotometer (Spetra-MAX 190, from Molecular Devices, Palo Alto, CA, USA). The assay was performed in triplicate.
    For the invasion assay, NCI-H441 cells were cultured in the top chambers containing Matrigel-coated membrane inserts (Matrigel, BD). The ensuing procedure was identical to the migration assay. The assay was performed in triplicate. Images were obtained using an Olympus BX51 microscope.

    Cell-Scatter Assay

    MDCK cells (1.5 × 103 cells per well) were plated in 24-well plates and grown overnight. Different concentrations of compound 46 and HGF (100 ng/mL) were added to the appropriate wells, and the plates were incubated at 37 °C and 5% CO2 for 24 h. The cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 15 min at room temperature and then stained with 1% crystal violet, washed with water, and dried. Images were obtained using an Olympus IX51 microscope.

    Cell Branching Morphogenesis Assay

    MDCK cells at a density of 2 × 104 cells/mL in DMEM medium were mixed with an equal volume of collagen I (BD, 354236) solution and plated at 0.1 mL/well in a 96-well culture plate. After incubation for 1 h at 37 °C and 5% CO2 to allow the collagen to gel, HGF (100 ng/mL) with or without compound 46 at various concentrations dissolved in 100 μL of growth medium was added to each well. The medium was replaced with fresh growth medium every 2 days. Images were obtained using an Olympus IX51 microscope after 5 days.

    In Vivo Antitumor Activity Assay

    Female nude mice (4–6 weeks) were housed at five or six mice per cage in a specific pathogen-free room with a 12 h light/dark schedule at 25 ± 1 °C; the animals were fed an autoclaved chow diet and water ad libitum. All the animal experiments were performed according to the institutional ethical guidelines of animal care.
    EBC-1 cells at a density of (5–10) × 106 in 200 μL were first implanted sc into the right flank of each mouse and then allowed to grow to 700–800 mm3, which was defined as a well-developed tumor. The well-developed tumors were cut into 1 mm3 fragments and transplanted sc into the right flank of nude mice using a tracer. When the tumor volume reached 100–150 mm3, the mice were randomly assigned into control and treatment groups (n = 6 per group). The control groups were given vehicle alone, and the treatment groups received compound 46 at the indicated doses via oral administration once daily for 21 weeks. Crizotinib was a positive drug.(39, 40) The sizes of the tumors were measured twice per week using microcalipers. The tumor volume (TV) was calculated as follows: TV = (length × width2)/2. The tumor volume shown was obtained on the indicated days as the median tumor volume ± SEM for indicated groups of mice. The percent (%) inhibition values were measured on the final day of the study for the drug-treated mice compared with the vehicle-treated mice and were calculated as 100 × {1 – [(treated final day – treated day 0)/(control final day – control day 0)]}. Significant differences between the treated versus the control groups (P ≤ 0.05) were determined using Student’s t test.

    Statistical Analysis

    Data from the in vitro assays are presented as the mean ± SD. While in the in vivo assay, data are presented as the mean ± SEM. The statistical difference between multiple treatments and control was analyzed using Student’s t test. P < 0.05 vs control group was considered statistically significant.

    In Vitro Metabolic Stability Study

    Microsomes (human microsome, Xenotech, lot H0610; rat microsome, Xenotech, lot R1000) (0.5 mg/mL) were preincubated with 1 μM test compound for 5 min at 37 °C in 0.1 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) with 1 mmol of EDTA and 5 mmol of MgCl2. The reactions were initiated by adding prewarmed cofactors (1 mmol NADPH). After 0, 5, 10, and 30 min incubations at 37 °C, the reactions were stopped by adding an equal volume of cold acetonitrile. The samples were vortexed for 10 min and then centrifuged at 10000g for 10 min. Supernatants were analyzed by LC/MS/MS for the amount of parent compound remaining, and the corresponding loss of parent compound was also determined by LC/MS/MS.
    The CYP enzymatic activities were characterized based on their probe reactions: CYP3A4 (midazolam), CYP2D6 (dextromethorphan), CYP2C9 (diclofenac), CYP1A2 (phenacetin), and CYP2C19 (mephenytoin). Incubation mixtures were prepared in a total volume of 100 μL as follows: 0.2 mg/mL microsome (human microsome, Xenotech, lot H0610), 1 mmol NADPH, 100 mmol phosphate buffer (pH 7.4), probe substrates cocktail (10 μM midazolam, 100 μM testosterone, 10 μM dextromethophan, 20 μM diclofenac, 100 μM phenacetin, 100 μM mephenytoin), and 10 μM tested compound or positive control cocktail (10 μM ketoconazole, 10 μM quinidine, 100 μM sulfaphenazole, 10 μM naphthoflavone, and 1000 μM tranylcypromine) or negative control (PBS). The final concentration of organic reagent in incubation mixtures was less than 1% v/v. There was a 5 min preincubation period at 37 °C before the reaction was initiated by adding a NADPH-generating system. Reactions were conducted for 20 min for CYPs. For each probe drug, the percentage of metabolite conversion was less than 20% of substrate added. The inhibition rate was calculated as [(the formation of the metabolite of probe substrates with 10 μM tested compound)/(the formation of the metabolite of probe substrates with PBS)] × 100.
