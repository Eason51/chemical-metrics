    Abstract

    Computational fragment mapping methods aim to predict hotspots on protein surfaces where small fragments will bind. Such methods are popular for druggability assessment as well as structure-based design. However, to date researchers developing or using such tools have had no clear way of assessing the performance of these methods. Here, we introduce the first diverse, high quality validation set for computational fragment mapping. The set contains 52 diverse examples of fragment binding “hot” and “warm” spots from the Protein Data Bank (PDB). Additionally, we describe PLImap, a novel protocol for fragment mapping based on the Protein–Ligand Informatics force field (PLIff). We evaluate PLImap against the new fragment mapping test set, and compare its performance to that of simple shape-based algorithms and fragment docking using GOLD. PLImap is made publicly available from https://bitbucket.org/AstexUK/pli.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Identifying fragment binding sites on a protein target of interest is a common first step in rational fragment-based drug discovery (FBDD).(1) Due to the small size and low complexity of the fragments screened in an FBDD campaign, chemical space can be sampled efficiently with a relatively small library(2) and the fragment hits frequently show relatively high ligand efficiency.(3) To fully leverage the advantages of FBDD, even smaller (solvent-sized) organic molecules could be used to probe the target protein. To this end, Ringe et al. pioneered the Multiple Solvent Crystal Structure (MSCS) method wherein crystals of the target protein are soaked in high concentration solutions of a variety of solvent-sized molecules.(4, 5) MSCS provides vital information on the protein of interest by identifying sites where multiple solvent-sized molecules bind. Such sites should correspond to fragment binding “hot” spots (i.e., high-affinity sites where one would also expect hits from a “regular” fragment screen).(6, 7) In theory, exposing a protein to such high concentrations of very small fragments should not only identify the fragment “hot” spots but could also identify lower affinity sites. Such sites, or “warm” spots, could yield useful information for a fragment-growing or general structure-based design campaign.
    Unfortunately, MSCS cannot be employed routinely because not all systems are amenable to the harsh conditions (e.g., 1M fragment concentrations). Additionally, many fragments are not soluble enough to achieve the required concentrations. Computational fragment mapping approaches, however, can be applied to any fragment–protein system with structural information. These approaches can be roughly divided into three categories: (i) methods that generate interaction maps of atomic probes, (ii) methods that generate individual poses for molecular probes, and (iii) simulation-based methods that generate ensembles of poses for molecular probes. Two well established programs of the first type, GRID(8-10) and SuperStar,(11, 12) generate maps of favorable positions for a variety of atomic probes on the target protein surfaces. GRID uses a force field based energy function,(8-10) whereas SuperStar(11, 12) uses interaction propensities derived from the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD)(13) and the Protein Data Bank (PDB; www.rcsb.org).(14) Hot spots on protein binding sites cannot always be directly identified from the atomic interaction maps because they are generated from single atom probes and hence the context of these atoms within a larger molecule is lost. To overcome this limitation, molecular probes are used to identify sites on the protein surfaces where small molecules bind favorably. The Multiple Copy Simultaneous Search (MCSS) technique places multiple copies of a probe molecule, which do not interact with each other, on the surface of a protein and subsequently energy minimizes them simultaneously to identify the local minima of the protein-probe complex.(15, 16) Another molecular probe based approach, FTMap, identifies low energy clusters of probe molecules on protein surfaces.(17) A set of 16 small molecules are used to probe the target protein surface and hotspots are then identified as sites where the majority of probe molecules bind.(18, 19) Recently, Radoux et al. described a fragment mapping approach that combines SuperStar maps with a measure of depth/burial and uses three molecular probes to identify fragment hot spots.(20) Finally, both Molecular Dynamics (MD) and Monte Carlo (MC) simulations are used widely to identify hotspots on protein surfaces.(21-25) Such molecular simulations allow the generation of thermodynamic ensembles from which one can not only infer favorable binding positions of a molecular probe but can also estimate binding free energies associated with a cluster of poses. However, such simulations are computationally too expensive to be routinely used.
    Despite the range of computational fragment mapping protocols described in the literature, a diverse validation set for evaluating and comparing different approaches is not available. Consequently, these methods have only been tested on a few, select fragment–protein examples. Unfortunately, there are insufficient examples of high concentration solvent soaks available in the PDB to construct a fragment mapping validation set directly. However, the PDB has a wealth of information in the form of a variety of ligands bound to the same site of a protein. This information can be used to identify hot or warm spots as sites where the same moiety is placed by multiple different ligands. For example, to date there are no structures in the PDB of the resorcinol molecule (i.e., 1,3-dihydroxybenzene) bound to the ATP site of HSP90A. However, there are dozens of examples of HSP90A ligands that contain a resorcinol moiety, and in all cases, the resorcinol group is presented to the ATP pocket in the same way.(26-30) For the purpose of this work we have assumed that, provided such moieties are placed in the same binding subpocket by chemically diverse ligands, such binding subpockets represent hot or warm spots for the fragment (i.e., resorcinol in the example above) binding. Hence, we set out to find all examples in the PDB where multiple dissimilar ligands place the same fragment in the same binding environment of a protein. The results of this exercise were systematically filtered and prioritized to construct the first high quality, diverse fragment mapping validation set, consisting of 52 protein–fragment complexes. Finally, we characterized each entry in the set as either a hot spot or a warm spot for fragment binding, based on the frequency with which the subsite is occupied by a ligand. Prepared structures of the fragment and the protein for each entry in the set are available for download from the Supporting Information (SI).
    Additionally, we introduce PLImap, a novel, standalone computer program for fragment mapping. PLImap uses a systematic search algorithm, combined with the Protein–Ligand Informatics force field (PLIff),(31) and is made publicly available from https://bitbucket.org/AstexUK/pli. We test the performance of PLImap against the new fragment mapping validation set and compare the results to those obtained with two simple shape-based algorithms and fragment docking using GOLD.(32) We focus here on identifying “hot” and “warm” spots within the confines of a given binding site of a protein, as this is of particular interest to us in terms of generating ideas for fragment growing and merging in FBDD projects. However, other researchers may use our set to test global search protocols for identifying fragment binding sites across an entire protein surface.

    Composition of the Validation Set

    A detailed description of the protocols we followed for deriving the fragment mapping validation set is given in the “Methods” section and in the SI, but in brief, we searched the PDB for examples where multiple diverse ligands present the same moiety (fragment) to the same subpocket of the same protein. During the automatic filtering and manual selection of protein–fragment complexes for the validation set, emphasis was given to constructing a diverse set. No more than one example of fragments having similar chemotypes bound to the same binding site was selected. For example, out of the 40 different clusters of 22 different fragments binding to proteins belonging to the kinase superfamily, only five clusters with chemically diverse fragments were selected. Similarly, only one example from the five clusters of carbonic anhydrase II (CA2) with different aryl-sulfonamides was selected. The final set comprises 41 unique fragments bound to 45 unique proteins (all pairwise sequence identities <50%) forming 52 unique fragment–protein complexes (see Figure 1 and Table 1). Moreover, these 45 unique proteins encompass 41 unique sets of Pfam domains.(33) The resulting set contains many examples of well-known key combinations of chemotypes and target classes, including isoxazole against a bromodomain, benzamidine against a serine protease, and pyridine against a kinase.

    The distributions of physiochemical properties of the fragments in the validation set are shown in Figure 2. It is clear that these are very small fragments with an average heavy atom count (HAC) of 8.3 (s = 1.7) and an average molecular weight (MW) of 120 (s = 25). Some of the fragments are quite lipophilic and will consequently have low aqueous solubility, which would preclude experimental fragment mapping using X-ray crystallography. We restricted the fragments to contain a maximum of one rotatable bond. As such, nine fragments in the set have one rotatable bond, and the remaining fragments have no rotatable bonds.

    Hot and Warm Spots

    We categorized each entry in our validation set as either a “hot” or a “warm” spot (see Table 1). In our classification hot spots are supposed to represent high affinity sites where one might also expect to obtain hits from a “regular” fragment screen and represent key recognition motifs of ligands binding to a site. Warm spots are lower-affinity sites, but still represent important areas where specific functional groups favorably interact with the protein. These warm spots can be very interesting for structure-based design, for example, during fragment-to-lead campaigns. The position and orientation of a warm spot fragment might not necessarily be conserved when it binds independently of the rest of the ligand, as more favorable nearby hot spots might drive the fragment binding toward them. In order to classify each entry in a relatively unbiased manner, we used ligand occupancy maps for inferring whether a fragment binding site is hot or warm. A ligand occupancy map simply describes the fraction of unique ligands in the PDB that occupies each position in the binding site. Highly occupied regions (occupancy ≥ 0.8) are classified as “hot”, whereas less occupied regions are classified as “warm”. The protocols we used here are described in more detail in the “Methods” section in the SI.
    An example of a hot spot in the validation set is the phenyl-sulfonamide binding subsite in CA2 active site (Figure 3a,c); the subsite has ligand occupancy of 0.95 meaning 95% of the CA2 active site ligands in the PDB occupy the subsite. This example represents a high-affinity site and is a well-known recognition motif in carbonic anhydrases. Phenyl-sulfonamide has an affinity against CA2 of 430 nM(34) and can easily be detected by X-ray crystallography against this target.
    However, the chloro-phenyl binding subsite near the Gly-rich loop of PKA-Akt chimera, a surrogate for Akt, is a warm spot (Figure 3b,d); the ligand occupancy for the spot is only 0.69. In a previous study, it was shown that correctly presenting a chloro-phenyl group to this subsite can provide a ∼170-fold jump in potency.(35) However, a 170-fold improvement in potency equates to only 3 kcal/mol gain in binding free energy. Hence, particularly taking the rigid-body entropic barrier to binding (approximately 4.2 kcal/mol as estimated by Murray and Verdonk(36)) and the solubility of chloro-benzene into account, it is highly unlikely that one would be able to detect the binding of this fragment in isolation. The hot spot in the PKA-Akt chimera (and Akt) binding site is the hinge-region, which preferably binds heteroaromatic rings (Figure 3b) and has a ligand occupancy of 0.99. Several experimental fragment hits have been identified binding to this hot spot region.(37)

    PLImap

    A detailed description of the PLImap protocol is provided in the “Methods” section in the SI. In brief, PLImap combines the PLIff energy function(31) with a systematic, grid-based search algorithm to identify favorable sites and poses for fragments binding to a protein. PLIff is routinely used at Astex for scoring protein–ligand complexes in molecular docking and generating atomic interaction fields. Recently, it has been shown that PLIff, when employed to rescore docking poses, performs at least as well as four other scoring functions including ChemPLP and GoldScore in molecular docking and virtual screening experiments on large public and internal validation sets.(31) However, this is the first time that we have evaluated the performance of PLIff as a scoring function when used for both driving the search and scoring the poses in the context of fragment mapping.

    The performance of PLImap, in terms of the three indicators recorded in Table 2, is similar to that of the GOLD protocols listed. However, the sampling of low-PLIff-energy solutions is significantly better in the case of PLImap (see Supporting Information). For example, for 40 out of the 52 entries in our fragment mapping set, PLImap produces a better-scoring solution than the GOLD-PLIff protocol. A detailed analysis of the results (see “Supplemental results and discussion” section in the SI) suggests that this increased sampling also leads to higher-quality binding mode predictions in the PLImap protocol. It is satisfying to see that the more sophisticated protocols (PLImap and GOLD) outperform the two shape-based methods in terms of predicting the locations of the fragment sites. As expected, all protocols perform better on the hot spots, compared to the warm spots in our new validation set (see “Supplemental results and discussion” section in the SI), but this can be alleviated by considering lower-ranking poses (e.g., the top three poses).
    Examples of successful predictions by AMIsite, GOLD, and PLImap are shown in Figure 4. AMIsite correctly identifies the hydrophobic pocket on K-Ras where the indole fragment binds (Figure 4a). An example of a successful prediction using GOLD docking driven by ChemPLP and rescored by Goldscore is the 4-oxo-3,4-dihydroquinazoline binding pose in the Tankyrase-1 binding site within 0.30 Å RMSD of the bound fragment (Figure 4b). Similarly, PLImap correctly identifies the binding mode of a benzoic acid fragment in the retinoic acid receptor beta (RARβ) binding site at the top rank (Figure 4c).
    An advantage of the systematic sampling employed by PLImap is that it also results in a map of the binding site, which provides a complete picture of where the fragment prefers to interact. This can be exemplified by the results on benzoic acid mapping of the all-trans retinoic acid binding site of RARβ. RARβ antagonists are stabilized via hydrogen bonding interactions formed by the benzoic acid moiety and by extensive van der Waals contacts (Figure 5a). Several selective and nonselective RARβ antagonists present a terminal phenyl (or other aryl) moiety to a hydrophobic subpocket.(44) The map of a benzoic acid probe against the RARβ binding site not only shows the lowest PLIff energies where the fragment binds, but also shows favorable energies overlapping with phenyl-carbonyl and the terminal phenyl subgroups in the ligand. Similarly, in the case of endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS), the map for the 4-methyl, 2-aminopyrimidine probe shows favorable energies at the centroid of the terminal 6-methyl, 2-aminopyridine in addition to the region where the fragment is bound (Figure 5b).

    Challenges in the Validation Set

    There are a few factors that contributed to a low overall performance but are not related to the mapping protocols themselves. Instead, these factors represent challenges that are inherent to the examples in the validation set or to the metric used for assessing the prediction. Here we describe these challenges that resulted in failures from the PLImap and GOLD protocols. One of the challenges is the use of RMSD as a success criterion, which has inherent limitations, especially in the case of lipophilic fragments. The top-ranking PLImap pose of three such lipophilic fragments (fluoro-phenyl (Figure 6a), 1,3-dichloro-phenyl, and adamantane), although they overlap nicely with the bound fragment, have RMSDs > 1.5 Å. These examples are successfully predicted in terms of their volume-overlap-based shape similarity with the bound fragment.
    As a second challenge, the correct binding modes of a few fragments could not be identified at the top rank because they form water-mediated hydrogen bonding interactions with the protein. Interactions with structural waters can contribute significantly to the binding free energy for a small molecule binding to a protein.(45) Nevertheless, we did not include any waters in the protein structures in our set as the locations of water molecules mediating hydrogen bonds may not be known in a prospective fragment mapping exercise. Ten of the fragments in the set for which a correct binding pose was not obtained at the top rank using PLImap form at least one water mediated interaction with the protein. An example is 3,5-dimethylisoxazole bound to bromodomain-containing protein 4 (BRD4). The ring oxygen and nitrogen in the experimental structure form hydrogen bonds with the Asn140 side-chain amide nitrogen and a tightly bound water molecule, respectively (Figure S8a in the SI). The top-ranking pose from PLImap perfectly overlaps with the bound fragment, but in a flipped orientation (Figure 6b). Since we discarded all water molecules from the protein structures, PLImap predicts that the ring nitrogen, not the oxygen, forms the hydrogen bond with Asn140. This is because the PLIff potentials correctly regard the isoxazole nitrogen as a better hydrogen-bond acceptor than the oxygen (also see Figure S8b in the SI).
    The third challenge that resulted in a low overall performance related to the choice of the representative PDB structure for the fragment–protein complex. In particular, the PDB structures chosen for the two examples of HSP90A in the set have an additional induced subpocket. This subpocket is very hydrophobic, and hence, several high-ranking poses of resorcinol (Figure 6c) and 2-amino-4-methyl-pyrimidine occupy this subpocket.
    Finally, for some examples, the top-ranking pose occupied an energetically favorable subsite other than the one where the fragment is bound as part of the ligand. This indicates that, in some cases, the binding position of the fragments as part of their parent ligands might not be conserved when they bind independently of the rest of the ligand. Kinesin-like protein KIF11, has two subpockets in the active site where aromatic rings bind; in this example, the top-ranking pose of 1,4-difluoro-phenyl occupies the “wrong” subpocket. As another example, chloro-phenyl is bound to a “warm” spot (ligand occupancy = 0.69) in the PKA-Akt chimera active site for which the top-ranking pose from GOLD docking (ChemPLP scoring function) occupies the “hot” spot where most ligands place a heteroaromatic ring (ligand occupancy = 0.99) (Figure 3d and Figure 6d). Other examples of this sort include phenyl with hematopoietic prostaglandin D synthase, 1,3-chloro-4-fluoro-phenyl with hepatocyte growth factor receptor, and 2-methylpyridine with TGF-beta receptor type-1.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this article we have introduced the first high-quality, relevant, and diverse validation set for evaluating the performance of fragment mapping algorithms. The approach we adopted here was to identify examples from the PDB where the same fragment (functional group) is presented to the same subsite, by at least three chemically diverse ligands. This resulted in a set of 52 examples, representing 45 unique targets and 41 unique fragments. We have classified each example as either a fragment “hot” spot or a fragment “warm” spot. A hot spot is a high-affinity, key recognition site, where one would expect to obtain hits from a “regular” fragment screen. A warm spot is a lower affinity site that represents an opportunity for improving the affinity of an existing hit or lead.
    Additionally, we have introduced PLImap, a newly developed standalone fragment mapping application (code freely available from https://bitbucket.org/AstexUK/pli). We have tested the performance of PLImap against the new fragment mapping test set and have compared the results against those obtained for GOLD fragment docking and two shape-based pocket finding algorithms. In terms of raw success rates, PLImap performs equally to the GOLD protocols tested (∼50% success rate for top-ranked solution having RMSD < 1.5 Å), but the systematic search algorithm in PLImap does appear to provide slightly higher quality binding modes. PLImap and GOLD performed significantly better than the shape-based algorithms. All protocols perform better on the hot spots, compared to the warm spots in our new validation set, but this difference can be alleviated by considering additional, lower-ranking poses.
    We have made the fragment mapping set freely available for download from the SI with the intention that other researchers can test their fragment mapping protocols against it. We focused here on predicting “hot” and “warm” spots within the confines of a given binding site of a protein, as this is of particular interest to us in terms of generating ideas for fragment growing and merging in FBDD projects. However, other researchers may use the set to test their global search protocols for identifying fragment binding sites across entire protein surfaces. We believe the fragment mapping validation set we introduced here will significantly improve our understanding of the utility of fragment mapping technologies.

    Construction of the Validation Set

    The validation set was constructed by a ligand “shredding” approach; the approach is briefly described here and each step is explained in detail in the “Supplemental methods” section in the SI. We searched the PDB for examples where multiple diverse ligands present the same moiety (fragment) to the same subpocket of the same protein (i.e., as determined by the Uniprot ID (www.uniprot.org)(46)). Certain quality measures were used to filter the PDB structures, and the bound ligands to obtain a set of high quality structures with relevant ligands. Each ligand in the curated PDB list was recursively shredded by breaking each single bond where at least one of the participating atoms is either a carbon bonded to three heavy atoms or a heteroatom. Bonds forming rings were not considered for shredding to avoid fragmenting rings. The bonded atom that is not part of the shred was stored as a dummy atom; this helps identifying the attachment point in the shred. All shreds were generalized to their canonical tautomeric form using the MolVS tool (http://molvs.readthedocs.io), which in turn uses the RDKit chemistry framework.(47) The shreds were filtered based on criteria described in the SI. In summary, only terminal shreds with a maximum of one rotatable bond and with heavy atom counts between five and 11 were considered. The shreds were further generalized based on their chemical structure and the generic atom type of the point of attachment in the PLIff formalism.(31) PLIff generalizes atoms based on their element type, number of hydrogens, and bonds the atom is involved in. This will, for example, generalize all toluene shreds that have an attachment point at any of the ring carbons to the same shred type, whereas the toluene shred with attachment point at the methylene carbon will be considered a different shred. Further filtering based on the number of times a shred occurred in PDB ligands, presence of a subshred in the set, and drug-likeness resulted in 616 unique shreds (Figure 7).
    Next, instances of each shred were clustered based on the protein residues the shreds are in contact with. This results in clusters of shreds in which individual member shreds interact with the same binding site of the same protein as determined by the Uniprot ID. Only one example of similar ligands placing the shred in the same protein binding site was kept. A second level of clustering was performed based on RMSD values between shreds in a cluster. All shred–protein complexes in a cluster were superimposed using KFIT, which performs a sequence alignment(48) based structure superposition,(49) and pairwise RMSDs between superposed shreds were calculated. Clusters of shred–protein complexes were then obtained using an RMSD cutoff of 1.5 Å. As the final automatic filter, clusters where none of the PDB structures had structure factors for generating electron density maps were discarded. This resulted in 237 unique shred–protein clusters where at least three dissimilar ligands place the shred in the same protein environment. Next, the clusters were manually filtered during which emphasis was given to constructing a pharmaceutically relevant, high-quality, diverse validation set. No more than one example of fragments having similar chemotypes bound to the same binding site was selected. Furthermore, the number of examples for a shred with different proteins was restricted to a maximum of four to avoid over-representation of a shred in the validation set. Care was taken to ensure that the manual selection was not biased on a perceived performance of a fragment mapping exercise against the shred–protein complexes. The final set contained 52 examples involving 45 unique proteins and 41 unique shreds.
    For each of the 52 clusters, protein and the bound fragment for only one representative PDB structure (best among all structures in terms of structural quality) were saved. Furthermore, the largest ligand in the cluster was also saved, which was used for defining the binding site in the fragment mapping experiments. Finally all representative structures in the set were prepared by removing waters and adding hydrogens using the Protonate3D utility(50) within the MOE(51) software with the default settings. Both the protein and the fragment were protonated together using the complex structure. All protein and fragment structures were then visually inspected and any incorrect tautomeric or charge state assignments were rectified.

    Characterizing Binding Subpockets of the Fragments

    Each fragment binding site in the validation set was categorized as either a “hot” spot or a “warm” spot. Ideally such a characterization should be based on binding affinities of the fragments with their targets. Alternatively, a difference between the binding affinities of the whole ligand and the ligand minus the fragment can be used as a surrogate for the binding affinity of the fragment. Unfortunately we found that these binding affinities were not available for almost all examples in the set. However, we can infer whether subsites are hot or warm by analyzing how often different ligands in the PDB place any atom in the subsites. We do this by generating ligand “occupancy” maps for the binding site for each of the examples in the set. All PDB structures of proteins that have a sequence similarity of at least 70% with the target protein and have any ligand bound in the fragment-binding site (not necessarily containing the fragment) were superimposed using an in-house program KFIT. Here, all ligand-bound protein structures irrespective of whether or not the ligand contains the corresponding fragment were considered. However, if multiple PDB entries have the same ligand in the same site, only one example of the ligand was kept for occupancy calculation. A 3D grid covering all aligned ligands was then placed on the active site. For each grid point, we count the number of ligands that occupy it. A grid point is considered occupied by a ligand if any atom in the ligand lies within a certain distance (2.0 Å for hydrogen bond donor or acceptor atoms; 1.5 Å for metals; van der Waals radius +0.8 Å for all other atoms). Finally, the occupancy counts are normalized by the number of the ligands used, resulting in so-called “ligand occupancy” maps. The occupancy value for each bound fragment in the validation set was then computed using the corresponding ligand occupancy maps as the average occupancy over all non-hydrogen atoms in the fragment. Here each atom in the fragment gets the occupancy value by trilinear interpolation of the occupancy map. Visually inspecting the ligand occupancy maps, we found that a cutoff of 0.8 seemed to discriminate well between “hot” and “warm” spots. All fragments for which the normalized occupancy value was below 0.8 (meaning on an average, each atom position in the fragment is occupied by less than 80% of the ligands in the site) were considered “warm”.
    Defining the boundaries of a site and identifying all ligands that bind to a site for generating ligand occupancy maps is not a trivial process. There are several issues associated with this, some of which are (1) several nonoverlapping small ligands can bind to the same binding site, and (2) a very large ligand may bind to two different sites. With this in mind we clustered all ligands of a protein into sites based on the protein residues they interact with. Two ligands were clustered to the same site if they interact with at least four common residues. Only ligands with a maximum of 30 heavy atoms were considered for this first round of clustering. Ligands with more than 30 atoms were then added to the site, which is defined by the highest number of residues that the large ligand interacts with. These large ligands were added to a new site if none of the sites identified in the first round have at least four common interacting residues. This results in multiple sites on protein structures with different ligands belonging to each of the sites. One of the sites identified this way belongs to the ligand that contained the bound fragment in the validation set; all unique ligands in this site are used to generate the ligand occupancy map.

    PLImap

    We developed a method for fragment mapping using the knowledge based scoring function PLIff (Protein Ligand Informatics force field).(31) Since the scoring of a protein–ligand complex using PLIff is very fast, we could afford to perform a systematic search of the whole binding site for finding energetically favorable positions of the probe molecule. During the systematic search, diverse clusters of low energy probe poses are saved and written to a file at the end of the run. In more detail, we place a three-dimensional regular grid with 0.15 Å spacing covering all protein atoms that lie within 6 Å of the site ligand for sampling translational space of the probe fragment in the binding site. Grid points that are more than 6 Å away from the site ligands were masked, and the fragment probes were not placed on them; this was done to have a site definition matching as closely as possible to the one used in the shape-based methods and GOLD runs (see below and the SI). Rotational space was sampled by generating 700 different orientations of the fragment quasi-uniformly distributed on a sphere. For the fragments with a rotatable bond, multiple conformations were generated for each orientation by uniformly sampling the dihedral angle in a step size of 30°. Next, on each grid point, the PLIff score for each orientation of the probe molecule was estimated using precalculated PLIff atomic fields. These estimated scores are used to filter out poses that do not undergo a full PLIff scoring, which requires computationally more expensive Voronoi partitioning(52) and contact area calculation. Only probe orientations that have a per-atom estimated score of ≤−0.2 kcal/mol were scored using PLIff. The poses were either saved in a diverse cluster or discarded based on the clustering algorithm described in “Supplemental methods” section in the SI. In the end, diverse clusters are ranked based on the best score of any of the poses in the cluster, and the top five diverse clusters were written to a file along with the scores of each individual pose in the cluster.

    PLImap Validation and Comparison to Other Fragment Mapping Approaches

    PLImap was evaluated in terms of its ability to predict the positions and binding modes of the fragments in the newly created fragment mapping test set. For comparison, we also evaluated the performance of GOLD docking and two shape-based pocket-finding algorithms (see Supporting Information for details on the protocols used for these methods). Up to three different metrics, centroid distance, RMSD, and volume-overlap-based shape similarity,(53) comparing the top-ranked pose with the X-ray binding mode of the fragment, were used for evaluating the success of the different fragment mapping protocols. Volume overlap is less strict than RMSD as it will have a high value even if a fragment binds in a different orientation than the bound fragment but occupies the same space in the active site. Similarly, centroid distance is also a less strict measure for assessing success and was employed here for allowing a comparison with shape-based methods. An RMSD of ≤1.5 Å and a volume-overlap-based shape similarity of ≥0.51 between the top-ranking pose of a cluster and the bound fragment were considered successes in terms of these two metrics. The cutoff of 0.51 for the volume-overlap-based shape similarity was used as this is the minimum similarity with which two poses of a fragment can have an RMSD of 1.5 Å in our validation set. Similarly, a cutoff of 1.5 Å in terms of the distance between the centroids of the top-ranking pose in a cluster (or the pocket volume) and the bound fragment was used for assessing the prediction success.
