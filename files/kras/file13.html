    Abstract

    Attempts to directly drug the important oncogene KRAS have met with limited success despite numerous efforts across industry and academia. The KRASG12C mutant represents an “Achilles heel” and has recently yielded to covalent targeting with small molecules that bind the mutant cysteine and create an allosteric pocket on GDP-bound RAS, locking it in an inactive state. A weak inhibitor at this site was optimized through conformational locking of a piperazine–quinazoline motif and linker modification. Subsequent introduction of a key methyl group to the piperazine resulted in enhancements in potency, permeability, clearance, and reactivity, leading to identification of a potent KRASG12C inhibitor with high selectivity and excellent cross-species pharmacokinetic parameters and in vivo efficacy.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    KRAS, NRAS, and HRAS are small GTPase proteins which act as molecular switches, cycling between inactive GDP-bound and active GTP-bound forms, transmitting signals from membrane bound receptors to regulate cellular processes. Nucleotide cycling is carefully controlled by guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs) which promote nucleotide exchange, and GTPase activating proteins (GAPs), which stimulate GTPase activity. RAS-GTP interacts with and regulates the activity of several important cellular effector proteins, the best characterized of these being the RAF kinases, regulating the RAF–MEK–ERK pathway, and the phosphoinositide 3-kinases (PI3K), regulating the PI3K–AKT–TOR pathway. KRAS is frequently mutated or amplified in human tumors with KRAS alterations being enriched in pancreatic adenocarcinoma (PDAC) (72%), colorectal cancer (CRC) (including 69% of the genomically stable subtype), and lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) (33%).(1) Oncogenic mutations of KRAS are usually found within the codons encoding glycine 12 (G12), glycine 13 (G13), or glutamine 61 (Q61), which are located within the active site of KRAS.(2) Mutations of these residues impair intrinsic GTPase activity, thereby increasing cellular KRAS-GTP levels and causing aberrant activation of downstream effector pathways, driving tumor cell proliferation and survival.(2,3) Cysteine mutations at codon 12 represent an oncogenic KRAS alteration. KRASG12C is the most common KRAS mutation found in LUAD, due to genetic aberrations driven by smoking, and is found in 13–14.5% of LUAD tumors (The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) and Foundation Medicine/Flatiron data sets). Although less frequent, KRASG12C mutations are also found in 2.6–2.9% of CRC patients (Memorial Sloan Kettering (MSK), Dana-Farber Cancer Institute (DFCI) and TCGA data sets), 1.1–2.1% of endometrial carcinomas (TCGA and MSK data sets) and 0.5–1.6% of pancreatic adenocarcinomas (Queensland Centre for Medical Genomics (QCMG) and TCGA data sets). Oncogenic KRAS including KRASG12C, has been shown in preclinical studies to be a key driver of tumor proliferation and survival.(4) Moreover, recent data reported from phase I clinical trials (NCT03600883) indicates inhibitors of KRASG12C may be effective in patients.(5)
    Despite a recent resurgence in approaches designed to directly target the oncogene KRAS with small molecules, progress to date has been modest, consistent with the established view that RAS is an “undruggable”, featureless protein, absent of discernible targetable binding pockets. Figure 1 shows RAS bound to its GEF Son-of-Sevenless (SOS), superimposed with ligands for which PDB depositions exist, and which therefore should be viewed as confirmed direct ligands for this important target. A wide diversity of binding sites, hit-finding techniques and potencies have been described for ligands against several RAS isoforms and include analogues which block binding of SOS to KRASQ61H (PDB 6FA1) derived from an intracellular antibody fragment displacement screen.(6) Fragment-based lead generation (FBLG) has been utilized to generate ligands which bind in a similar region to this and block binding of SOS to KRASG12D (PDB 6GJ8),(7) which are an iteration on earlier less potent binders at this same site (PDB 4EPV).(8) This pocket was in fact first reported by Genentech (PDB 4DSU)(9) following an NMR fragment screen and then later by AstraZeneca following a crystallographic cocktail soaking FBLG campaign against the HRAS–SOS complex (PDB 4URZ), one which also yielded ligands directly bound to SOS (PDB 4URU).(10) This same SOS binding site was subsequently observed to accommodate both agonists (PDBs 6D5G, 6EPM)(11,12) and antagonists of SOS (PDB 5OVI)(12) by independent groups. Large cyclic peptides have been shown to bind to inactive KRASG12D and act to block GEF binding (PDB 5XCO),(13) thereby preventing activation of KRAS. Three distinct covalent approaches have been described, two of which are specific to the G12C mutation; ligands which bind to KRASG12C in an allosteric pocket on the GDP bound form and are discussed below (PDB 5V6S)(14) and a covalent nucleotide mimetic approach based on modified guanine nucleotides (PDB 5KYK).(15) The final covalent approach adopted reactive fragments to target C118 on HRAS (but present in all RAS isoforms), which is becomes accessible upon SOS binding to RAS (PDB 4US2).(10)
    In a 2013 breakthrough disclosure, Shokat et al. described the elegant discovery of ligands that specifically target the codon 12 cysteine mutant of KRAS.(16) Crystallographic data highlighted the potential to covalently capture Cys12 of GDP-bound KRAS, with the creation of a new allosteric regulatory site under the switch II loop important for effector recognition, rendering KRAS locked in an inactive state. Reliance on this cysteine motif for binding has a number of advantages, both in the potency and subsequent selectivity that could be achieved for KRASG12C inhibition, notably over wild-type KRAS. A disulfide fragment-based screening approach was utilized to find hits that were capable of binding reversibly and induce this novel pocket, followed by iteration with more drug-like Michael acceptor warheads. Further optimization was conducted to generate agents capable of potently inhibiting KRAS function in a cellular setting, such as ARS-853, 1 (Figure 2),(17) followed by subsequent scaffold hopping to quinazoline-based inhibitors such as ARS-1620, 2, capable of delivering in vivo proof-of-concept activity for the first time.(18) In more detailed mechanistic studies, it was shown that compounds 1 and 2 possess only very weak reversible binding affinity for KRAS but that the rate of inactivation by covalent modification of Cys12 is rapid. This is despite the fact the electrophilicity of the warhead is not especially high, nor that Cys12 in KRAS is particularly nucleophilic. It was suggested that Lys16 of KRAS can direct and activate the acrylamide in 1 for nucleophilic attack by Cys12, effectively stabilizing the developing negative charge upon inactivation.(19) Such an observation is intriguing insomuch as this upends traditional thinking about the importance of reversible affinity in covalent inhibitor design and the fine-tuning of warhead reactivity required to achieve good pharmaceutical properties.(20) Following the disclosure of tool compounds like 1, new agents have subsequently been disclosed. KRASG12C inhibitors AMG 510,(21)3 (Amgen) and MRTX849 (Mirati/Array) have now entered first-in-human clinical testing and while the structure of MRTX849 is not yet reported, an earlier, related tool compound MRTX1257, 4, has been published.(22) Wellspring Biosciences recently announced the intent to develop ARS-3248, but it is unclear if this compound is related in structure to ARS-1620.

Accordingly, the synthesis of piperazine precursor 13 began with amide coupling of alanine methyl ester and CBz-protected serine 5 to give dipeptide 6, which is then cyclized to diketopiperazine 7 upon removal of the protecting group (Scheme 1). Borane reduction of 7 gives hydroxymethyl piperazine 8, which underwent a short sequence of protecting group manipulation to give differentially protected BOC/CBz-protected piperazine 10 via mono-BOC piperazine 9. Oxidation of 10 with sodium periodate to give acid 11 was followed by esterification 12 and CBz-removal by hydrogenation to give key intermediate 13. Separately, bromoaniline 14 (Scheme 2) was chlorinated to give a mixture of ortho- and para-chloroanilines, favoring the latter by a ratio of ∼3:1 (the mixture was carried through unresolved). Aniline 15 was then converted to quinolone 17 via high temperature cyclization of intermediate 16, which was isolated by filtration of the precipitated mixture. Any uncyclized products arising from the ortho-chlorination of starting aniline 14 remained soluble and were filtered out on quinolone 17 isolation. Nitration of 17 to give 3-nitroquinolone 18 was followed by chlorination to 4-chloroquinolone 19 using phosphoryl trichloride. Piperazine 13 underwent an SNAr reaction with chloroquinoline 19 to afford 20 and iron-mediated reduction of the nitro group resulted in direct intramolecular cyclization with the piperazine ester to give tetracyclic amide 21. Methylation to tertiary amide 22 occurred in high yield, and cross-coupling with fluorophenol boronic acid gave 8-arylquinoline 23. Removal of the BOC protecting group was followed by separation of the atropisomers of 24, which was then converted to covalent inhibitor 25 by reaction with acryloyl chloride.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The potency of inhibitors of KRASG12C was assessed by using a complex assay that featured multiple steps capable of measuring different modes of inhibition, specifically, compounds were incubated with GDP-bound KRASG12C then treated with the guanine nucleotide exchange factor SOS1 in the presence of excess GTP, with any remaining activated KRAS from this step being detected by treating with a fluorescently labeled RAF binding domain (RBD). Compounds that bound to KRAS-GDP and prevented nucleotide exchange would hold KRAS in its inactive state and thus prevent recognition by the RBD. A literature inhibitor of KRASG12C26 was synthesized(23) (Table 1) and confirmed as a modestly potent inhibitor with an IC50 of 0.496 μM in this assay format. An X-ray crystal structure of 26 in KRASG12C was solved in-house and provided insights into the binding mode of this series of compounds, which was in good agreement with the structure of related analogues that were subsequently reported (Figure 3).(24) The acrylamide moiety forms a covalent bond with Cys12, and Lys16 additionally forms a hydrogen bond to the acrylamide carbonyl group. His95, specific to Kirsten variants of RAS, forms a hydrogen bond to quinazoline N1. At C7 of the quinazoline, a single atropisomer of the indazole is observed, and it is this group that causes a shift in the switch II loop, forming a direct hydrogen bond to Asp69 and a water-mediated one to Ser65. A water molecule is observed close to the quinazoline C4 position that does not appear to engage the ligand. Computational solvent analysis using Grand Canonical Monte Carlo(25) indicated that this water molecule has the potential to be displaced, potentially offering a route to improved potency.(26,27)

    We were struck by the conformation of the piperazine relative to quinazoline, specifically the observed twist between these two groups in the active, bound state. Analysis of the predicted relative energy barriers for torsion around this bond proved informative (Figure 4). Three distinct low energy conformations are available to this 4-piperazinoquinazoline, at 60°, 150–210°, and 300°. The observed bioactive torsion at 312° is close to, but not exactly coincident with, just one of these minima, leading us to hypothesize that strategies to restrict or lock out the bioactive conformation might lead to enhanced potency. Conceptually at least, this could be achieved by a strategy of tethering the piperazine ring back onto the quinazoline core, via a tetracyclic ring system, and this was realized by the synthesis of a series of tetracyclic quinoline cores tethered with a fused morpholine ring, 27–30 (Table 1). Synthesis of all four possible diastereomers (from two asymmetric centers, chiral methyl and restricted rotation around the biaryl bond) revealed that one in particular, 29, showed a reasonable ∼5-fold improvement in potency relative to the untethered reference 26. It should be noted, however, that 26 is as a racemic mixture of its active and less active atropisomers and also that the log D has increased for 29 relative to 26 as a consequence of the linker and switch to quinoline from quinazoline. This increase in log D also elicits an erosion in in vitro Clint values in both rat hepatocytes and human microsomal systems, which in fact are worse for this diastereomer than the other less active three compounds 27, 28, and 30. An expansion of the tether to a 7-membered ring as in 31 and 32 was also investigated, although in this case both 7-membered ring enantiomers were inferior to 29 and equally suffered from metabolic liabilities. Encouraged by the gains in potency, notwithstanding the worsening properties, 29 was selected for further optimization.
    Switching the indazole for a fluorophenol, as demonstrated for compound 33 had a beneficial impact on lowering both lipophilicity and in vitro Clint values (Table 2). Unlike the indazole, however, this fluorophenol moiety existed as an interconverting mixture of atropisomers, which could be separated analytically using chiral SFC but rapidly equilibrated upon standing. To lock out the active phenol atropisomer, small substituents were explored at quinazoline C8, with the best of these being 8-fluoro 34. Compound 34 was a potent inhibitor of KRASG12C with an IC50 of 16 nM, and although log D was moderately high at 3.4, metabolism in both rat and human in vitro assays proved low. An 8-methyl analogue 35 was both less potent than fluorine and more lipophilic, resulting in a slight worsening of in vitro clearances. An 8-methoxy analogue 36 showed similar potency to methyl, but erosion of Clint values, despite a more favorable log D.

    Having established preference for a 6-membered tether, we then sought to further probe the nature of the new ring itself, electing to conduct this SAR exploration with a quinazoline 8-H substituent to simplify the chemistry, with the intention that an 8-fluoro substituent would subsequently be reinstalled for optimal compounds (Table 3). Switching from morpholine 33 to piperazine 37 resulted in a slight erosion of potency, although a modest reduction in log D saw a trend toward lowered Clint values accompanied by an erosion in permeability likely due to the additional donor present. Methylation of the piperazine nitrogen 38 gave a boost in potency but took log D above 4, with rat Clint values in particular suffering as a result. Amide linkers were also explored; secondary lactam 39 was both more potent and less lipophilic than other linkers, and in vitro clearances values were low. Despite this, efflux in Caco2 cells was substantially increased, which we attribute to the additional hydrogen bond donor. Tertiary lactam 40 proved particularly interesting, resulting in improved potency relative to the starting ether, with lower log D leading to low Clint values and very high permeability with minimal efflux observed.

    We were motivated to explore the impact of further substitution on the piperazine ring carrying the acrylamide, with simple methyl groups. In vitro metabolite ID studies had indicated that glutathione addition to the acrylamide was the major route of clearance in rat for compounds such as 33, and it was hoped that such routes of elimination might be minimized by steric crowding of the acrylamide moiety. An increase in mouse whole blood stability for compounds related to 26 has been reported using this approach.(28) Methylation of the methylene group opposite the tether was achieved, with R-enantiomer 41 showing a 4-fold enhancement in potency compared to unsubstituted 33 and S-enantiomer 42 showing a marked loss in potency (Table 4). A modest increase in lipophilicity for 33 was associated with a slight increase in in vitro Clint values. The addition of this R-Me group also contributed to a positive reduction in general reactivity with glutathione (416 min half-life versus 334 min) and improved passive permeability (AB Papp 47.9 versus 35.9). To help put these glutathione half-lives into context, the corresponding values for marketed reversible kinase inhibitors afatinib(29) and osimertinib(30) in this same in-house assay are 28 and 121 min, respectively. Both methyl and ether substituents on the piperazine of 41 sit in an axial orientation and potentially contribute to limiting access to the acrylamide from either face, with the methyl group helping to improve permeability by lipophilic shielding of the adjacent polar carbonyl group. Having earlier established that the tertiary amide tether (as in 40) was optimal, addition of the 8-fluoro group to lock out the active atropisomer gave compound 43 which showed an excellent balance of potency that was associated with reasonable lipophilicity, low Clint values, and high permeability. Reactivity of the acrylamide to glutathione was also further reduced (585 min half-life), which may simply be a consequence of the increased steric bulk of the amide relative to ether, blocking the lower face of the molecule more effectively. Applying the chiral methyl SAR to 43 gave compound 25, which showed a significant increase in potency, similar gains in permeability, and further reductions in glutathione reactivity (half-life of 929 min). In this instance, the increase in lipophilicity had no material impact on in vitro Clint values relative to 43.

    We had noted higher in vivo clearances for some compounds than would be predicted based on scaling from in vitro values that we attributed to extrahepatic glutathione-mediated clearance from blood and other tissues.(31) Glutathione S-transferase (GST) enzymes, which catalyze the addition of glutathione to reactive electrophiles in the body are present not just in the cytosol of hepatocytes but additionally in the gastrointestinal tract, heart, lung, skin, and blood (erythrocytes) and in certain cases may lead to additional, extrahepatic metabolism that is not accounted for simply by scaling from hepatic fractions. This extrahepatic component to clearance was particularly acute in compounds devoid of any tether such as literature compound 26 but was noted in tethered analogues to a lesser degree also. Literature inhibitor 44 (Table 5) had high in vivo clearance in rat which is not predicted from in vitro scaling (observed 97 mL/min/kg versus 35 mL/min/kg predicted, for a notional extrahepatic contribution of 62 mL/min/kg), resulting in a low bioavailability of 24%. Exposure in nude mouse following a single high oral dose of 100 mg/kg was also low, suggestive of similar clearance issues. The simple tethered analogue 33 in comparison showed reasonable in vivo pharmacokinetics in rat, with a moderate bioavailability of 64% and clearance of 35 mL/min/kg, which is much closer to the predicted value of 28 mL/min/kg. In mouse, significantly higher exposure was also seen. It is interesting to note that compounds 33 and 44, close in structure, of similar reactivity and metabolic fate (based on metabolite ID studies) should display such different extrahepatic liabilities. It is tempting to speculate that there may be an element of GST isoform specificity to their observed in vivo clearances because while GST-α and GST-μ are present in hepatocyte cytosol, it is the GST-π isoform that is ubiquitous in other tissues.(32) The impact of the piperazine chiral methyl group can be seen in the PK profile of 41. In vivo clearance was low and well predicted from hepatocytes, and this combined with improved permeability resulted in very high bioavailability, with similar improvements in mouse oral exposure. Similar benefits were seen when comparing amides 43 and 25. Amide 43 has low clearance which is well predicted from in vitro scaling, but methylation to give 25 resulted in further reductions in clearance and improvements to bioavailability. The impact that this methyl group conferred in this series is noteworthy, with gains in permeability, potency, reduced extrahepatic clearance, and reduced glutathione reactivity all combining to yield a potent inhibitor of KRASG12C25 with exceptional PK properties. Compound 25 was selected for more detailed profiling.

    An X-ray structure of compound 25 complexed with KRASG12C was obtained with a resolution of 1.4 Å (Figure 5). Consistent with previous observations the acrylamide moiety forms a covalent bond with Cys12, while Lys16 forms a hydrogen bond to the acrylamide carbonyl group. It is noteworthy that the amidic carbonyl on the tether forms clear water-mediated interactions back to the NH backbone of Cys12; a motif which is believed to contribute to the boost in potency relative to the ether-tethered analogues. The quinoline N1 forms an interaction to His95, while the C7-fluorophenol group engaged Asp69 on the α2-helix and formed water-mediated interactions to Ser65 on the switch II-loop.
    The chiral R-Me was found to induce a somewhat atypical diaxial conformation around the piperazine and was found to displace the water molecule previously identified bound to 29. Intrigued by the gain in potency for 25 in comparison to 43, we used low-mode molecular dynamics to explore the conformational landscape of these compounds. Compound 43 is predicted to adopt an equatorial linkage to the tether approximately 20% of the time, a conformation which is incompatible with the bioactive form where the tether is axial. In comparison 25 is predicted to exclusively sit in the bioactive diaxial conformation, an observation which is consistent with free-ligand NMR analysis and suggests that 25 can bind to KRASG12C with a minimal entropic penalty. The combination of this effect, alongside the displacement of an energetically unstable water molecule, explains the exquisite potency of 25.
    One theoretical concern with any agent designed to target noncatalytic cysteine residues in proteins is that of promiscuity. The specificity of 25 by global cysteine reactivity profiling was examined to assess selectivity against the wider cysteine-containing proteome (Figure 6). NCI-H358 cells which carry the G12C mutation were treated with 4 μM of compound 25 or vehicle for 4 h. Any remaining unreacted, solvent exposed cysteine residues were then reacted with iodoacetamide desthiobiotin and proteins were subsequently digested with trypsin. The resulting biotinylated peptides were enriched with streptavidin and analyzed by MS to determine which targets 25 was capable of binding to in addition to KRASG12C. Free cysteine profiling of compound 25 in this manner demonstrates highly specific labeling of KRASG12C; it is the only protein with significant >2-fold protein loss compared to the DMSO control.
    The cellular potency and selectivity of compound 25 was explored across a panel of KRASG12C, wild-type KRAS, and non-G12C mutant KRAS cells in both two-dimensional (2D) and three-dimensional (3D) proliferation assays, as differences in KRAS dependency in monolayer and anchorage indepent growth conditions have been reported.(7,33,34) Growth of most of the KRASG12C cells was potently inhibited by compound 25, with a reasonable association between sensitivity in the different assay formats. Minimal activity was observed in cell lines not carrying this specific KRAS mutation (Figure 7; Supporting Information). Differential antiproliferative activity was observed across the panel, with LU99 and SW1573 cells showing lack of sensitivity in both 2D and 3D assay formats (2D GI50 data available as Supporting Information). Differences in binding of compound 25 to KRASG12C are unlikely to explain this differential sensitivity, as similar levels of target engagement were achieved across the cell panel and is likely to reflect the importance of other drivers in the proliferation of these cell types. Daily oral dosing of mice with 100, 50, or 20 mg/kg of compound 25 was well tolerated and caused a dose dependent regression of KRASG12C mutant Miapaca2 xenografts (Figure 8). Biomarker analysis of Miapaca2 tumors from mice treated with compound 25 showed a robust inhibition of DUSP6, a well characterized transcript regulated by RAS/MAPK pathway activity(35,36) at 3 and 24 h postdose. Taken together, these data indicate that compound 25 is a potent and selective inhibitor of KRASG12C.

    Conclusions

    The discovery of compound 25, a potent and selective KRASG12C inhibitor, has been described. An initial key step in its discovery was the application of a conformational tether to lock the piperazine group at C4 of a quinazoline ring to derive a tetracyclic quinoline which unexpectedly also markedly diminished extrahepatic GST-mediated clearance in preclinical species observed for related literature compounds. Optimization of the series was further advanced by switching to an amide tether, and crucially, methylation of the piperazine gave simultaneous improvements in potency, permeability, and clearance and a reduction in general warhead reactivity. The potency gains obtained from this methyl group may be explained by additional conformational locking of the bioactive pose and entropic and enthalpic gains from loss of an unstable water molecule identified in initial X-ray structures. Across species, the compound demonstrated low clearance and high bioavailability. Compound 25 was highly selective for reaction with the cysteine in KRASG12C over other cysteine containing proteins and had a selective antiproliferative phenotype in cancer cells bearing the G12C mutation that led to strong tumor regressions in a mouse xenograft disease model. More detailed studies with compound 25 will be reported in due course.
