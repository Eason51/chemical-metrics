    Abstract

    The V600E missense mutation in B-Raf kinase leads to an anomalous regulation of the MAPK pathway, uncontrolled cell proliferation, and initiation of tumorigenesis. While the ATP-competitive B-Raf inhibitors block the MAPK pathway in B-Raf mutant cells, they induce conformational changes to wild-type B-Raf kinase domain leading to heterodimerization with C-Raf causing a paradoxical hyperactivation of MAPK pathway. A new class of inhibitors (paradox breakers) has been developed that inhibit B-RafV600E activity without agonistically affecting the MAPK pathway in wild-type B-Raf cells. In this study, we explore the structural, conformational, and cellular effects on the B-Raf kinase domain upon binding of paradox breakers and inducers. Our results indicate that a subtle structural difference between paradox inducers and breakers leads to significant conformational differences when complexed with B-Raf. This study provides a novel insight into the activation of B-Raf by ATP-competitive inhibitors and can aid in the design of more potent and selective inhibitors without agonistic function.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    B-Raf is a member of the Raf family of serine/threonine protein kinases. It plays a vital role in the Ras–Raf–MEK–ERK signal transduction pathway (also known as the mitogen-activated protein kinase or MAPK signaling cascade) and participates in cell proliferation and cell survival.(1) In the MAPK pathway, active Ras induces conformational changes in B-Raf upon binding. This promotes changes in its phosphorylation status triggering kinase activity. Phosphorylation of B-Raf activates the MEK protein, which in its turn activates the downstream ERK protein kinase that phosphorylates several substrates.(2, 3)
    The B-Raf kinase is frequently mutated in melanomas, and the V600E missense mutation is most commonly observed. This mutation causes B-Raf to signal independently from upstream regulation. Uncontrolled amplification of downstream signaling is linked with transformation, increase of proliferation and finally tumorigenesis.(4) In order to target the B-RafV600E mutant, ATP-competitive B-Raf inhibitors such as vemurafenib(5) (1), PLX4720(6) (2), sorafenib,(7) and dabrafenib(8) have been developed, which effectively block the MAPK signaling pathway and decrease tumor growth in cells expressing B-RafV600E. However, while these inhibitors effectively inhibit ERK signaling in tumor cells expressing B-RafV600E, they have unexpected agonistic functions in cells expressing wild-type B-Raf. In particular, inhibitors binding to the wild-type B-Raf monomer were shown to induce homo/heterodimerization with a second Raf protomer (B-Raf/B-Raf, B-Raf/C-Raf) in the presence of GTP loaded KRAS at the membrane. As recently published, the dimerized B-Raf has lower affinity for the inhibitors compared with ATP with still high kinase activity activating the downstream MAPK pathway. Because Ras-GTP levels are relatively much lower in B-RafV600E cells, dimer-mediated activation of the MAPK pathway is disfavored.(9) Consequently, in cells expressing wild-type B-Raf, binding of these inhibitors paradoxically activates the wild-type B-Raf kinase domain by inducing conformational changes and dimerization and triggering C-Raf activation, leading to MEK/ERK phosphorylation and eventually causing enhanced cell growth (Figure 1). Therefore, the ATP-competitive inhibitors can either inhibit or activate the MAPK pathway, depending on wild-type or V600E B-Raf expression in tumor cell lines.(10-12) It has been reported that the combination of a MEK inhibitor, PD184352, with the B-Raf inhibitor 1 can help to overcome the paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway.(13) Similar studies have led to combination therapy of the B-Raf inhibitor dabrafenib and the MEK inhibitor trametinib. While this combination therapy reduces the agonistic effects of dabrafenib and has modest clinical efficacy, it causes some side effects such as pyrexia, nausea, and fatigue for the patients.(14, 15) Hence, there is an urgent need to design B-Raf inhibitors that can overcome the agonistic functions of the existing inhibitors with minimal side effects.
    Current efforts focus on developing B-Raf inhibitors known as paradox breakers, which prevent activation of the MAPK pathway in cells expressing wild-type B-Raf.(16) These paradox breakers thus represent a new class of B-Raf inhibitors that inhibit the ERK1/2 pathway in mutant B-Raf melanoma cells and do not cause activation of this pathway in the cells expressing wild-type B-Raf. One reported paradox breaker, PB04 or PLX7904, is a vemurafenib analogue that was shown to potently block ERK1/2 activation in vitro in mutant B-Raf melanoma cells and to reduce B-Raf dimerization and paradoxical activation of the downstream signaling.(17) Another paradox breaker is a pan-Raf inhibitor, TAK-632, which binds to the DFG-out inactive conformation of B-Raf and induces Raf dimerization but inhibits the kinase activity of the dimer with minimal paradoxical activation. In fact, TAK-632 exhibits a long residence time in purified wild-type B-Raf, and due to its slow off-rate effect, it persistently occupies both protomers in Raf dimers, thereby inhibiting the kinase activity of these dimers.(18, 19) This observation is consistent with another Raf inhibitor AZ-628, which binds to the inactive form with a slow off-rate and no significant paradoxical activation.(9, 10) Type I ATP-competitive inhibitor induction of dimerization/oligomerization is not unique to B-Raf complexes but was also observed in IRE1α kinase, where inhibitors favor the formation of dimeric and possibly oligomeric states of IRE1α.(20) Similarly, an increased JAK2 kinase heterodimerization and sustained JAK2 activation were observed after treatment with a JAK2 inhibitor.(21)
    While the mechanism of the paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway has been elucidated, the structural and conformational causes remain unclear. In order to further study the paradoxical activation of wild-type B-Raf by ATP competitive inhibitors, we studied four protein/ligand complexes such as wild-type B-Raf bound to 1 and three of its structural analogues, 2, P-0012 (3), and P-0013 (4) (Figure 2). Compounds 3 and 4 have recently been reported as B-Raf inhibitors,(16) and based on their reported cellular activity data (Table 1), these inhibitors are potential paradox breakers. In contrast to 2, 3 and 4 do not cause phosphorylation of ERK in IPC-298 cell activation activity, a tumorigenic B-Raf wild-type (B-RafWT) melanoma cell line with Ras mutations, but still show growth inhibitory activity in A375, a human melanoma cell line with B-Raf V600E mutation. Based on the chemical nature of the substituent attached to the 7-azaindole scaffold, 4 and 3 are closer structural analogues of 1 and 2 bearing 4-chlorophenyl- and chloro-substituted moieties, respectively (Figure 2). On the other hand, 3 and 4 both contain a sulfonylurea motif in the hydrophobic binding pocket, while 1 and 2 carry a sulfonamide motif in this region. Taken together, the biochemical activity data and the chemical structure of these compounds show that 1 and 2 cause paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway in B-Raf wild-type cells (paradox inducers, Pi), while 3 and 4 appear to block this effect (paradox breakers, Pb). Therefore, it seems that a subtle change in the molecular structure of these B-Raf inhibitors, upon binding, can lead to significant differences in terms of the described agonistic function.

    In the present work, we seek to study the paradox inducing and breaking effects of B-Raf ATP-competitive inhibitors on the wild-type B-Raf kinase using a recently described limited proteolysis approach coupled to stable isotope labeling mass spectrometry(22) and molecular dynamics simulations. More in detail, we focused on the characterization and identification of the structural elements that may act as discriminatory factors between the two types of inhibitors.

    Materials and Methods

    Limited Proteolysis and Stable Isotope Labeling Mass Spectrometry

    Recombinant human full-length wild-type B-Raf protein was provided by Janssen R&D, a division of Janssen Pharmaceutica NV. The purity was verified by SDS-PAGE, and the nucleotide status was analyzed by RP-HPLC analysis.(22) Limited proteolysis coupled to stable isotope labeling mass spectrometry was performed as previously described.(22) Briefly, B-Raf (60 pmol for limited proteolysis experiments and 360 pmol for complete digestion) was incubated for 4 h at 30 °C with either 1% DMSO (vehicle), ADP, ATP, or AMP-PNP (each time 10 nmol), or inhibitors (1 nmol) in 20 mM triethylammonium bicarbonate buffer (pH 8.5) containing 1 mM MgCl2. Limited proteolysis was performed with trypsin (sequencing-grade modified trypsin, Promega, Madison, WI, USA, 1:200, enzyme/substrate) for 10 min at 30 °C. The full digested sample was obtained by denaturing the B-Raf protein for 5 min at 95 °C and, following cooling on ice for 10 min, incubating the denatured protein with trypsin (1:20, enzyme:substrate) for 4 h at 37 °C. The generated peptide mixtures were labeled for further quantification using N-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) esters of different butyric acid forms: 12C4-butyric acid (light label) and 13C4-butyric acid (heavy label). The light labeled, limited proteolysis sample and the heavy labeled, full digested sample were pooled (100:3 light labeled/heavy labeled sample in order to finally achieve peptide intensity ratios close to one) and analyzed by LC-MS/MS using an Ultimate 3000 RSLC nano (Dionex, Amsterdam, The Netherlands) in-line connected to an LTQ Orbitrap Velos mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Bremen, Germany) that was operated as previously described.(22) The obtained MS/MS spectra were searched against an in-house database consisting of the sequence of the wild-type B-Raf protein in a background of Spodopterafrugiperda proteins (UniProtKB/Swiss-Prot protein database, release of 15/02/2012, containing 488 sequence entries). Peptides were identified and quantified using the Mascot Distiller Toolbox, version 2.3.2.0 (Matrix Science, London, UK). Peptide ratios were calculated using the areas under the light and heavy isotopic envelopes of the corresponding peptide and were further normalized to the control, being the B-Raf protein incubated with vehicle DMSO. Statistical analyses were assessed by a Student’s two tailed t test, after transformation of the peptide ratios to log2 values.

    Compounds

    Compounds 1 and 2 were obtained from Selleck. Compounds 3 and 4 were synthesized following the procedure reported in ref 16.

    Preparation of B-Raf Kinase Domain Models for Molecular Dynamics Simulations

    The crystal structures of human wild-type B-Raf kinase domain (KD) receptors present in the Protein Data Bank (PDB) have been mutated at multiple sites in order to improve expression to allow for further crystallization. In fact, 16 mutations are present in the crystal structure of the B-Raf active KD, PDB entry 3C4C (chain A). The starting B-Raf KD model in its active (DFG-IN) conformation was prepared using the Homology Modeling function in MOE2012.(23) The sequence of the B-Raf KD was obtained from its UniProt entry (BRAF_HUMAN, P15056, isoform 1). The crystal structure of the B-Raf KD inactive state (3C4C.A) was used as a template for building the B-Raf KD model. This structure is in a DFG-IN/αC-helix-OUT conformation. The cocrystallized ligand 2 in the template binding site was included as environment for induced fit for the generation of the final models. Ten intermediate models were generated with the side-chain sampling at 300 K. Finally, the model was refined using the Amber12EHT force field with Born solvation, and hydrogens were added using Protonate3D function.

    Protein–Ligand Docking

    The crystal structures of 1 and 2 were retrieved from PDB entries 3OG7 and 3C4C, respectively. The other two inhibitors to be tested were sketched in the 2D format and converted to 3D using LigPrep from Schrodinger. After minimization of the 3D structures, the inhibitors were docked in their respective receptor models. The Standard Precision algorithm of the Glide docking(24) tool was used to dock the inhibitors to the B-Raf KD active receptor model. The binding site for the B-Raf receptor models was defined by the centroid of the ATP-binding pocket residues.

    Molecular Dynamics Simulations

    Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were carried out on the B-Raf-inhibitor complex models that were obtained as described above. All simulations were carried out using the Amber12 suite(25) with the Amber12SB force field parameters. The parameter and coordinate files for the inhibitors were prepared using the antechamber utility(26) of AmberTools12. GAFF force field parameters were used for the inhibitors, and the partial charges were calculated using the AM1-BCC method. The topology files for the protein–inhibitor complexes were prepared using the tleap utility of AmberTools12. All the systems were then solvated using a TIP3P solvent box of 9 Å radii from protein surface and neutralized by adding counterions. In order to remove any possible structural artifacts resulting from model-building, before running the MD simulations, the solvated systems were subjected to minimization. This was performed in two steps. In the first step, positional restraints on the position of the solute molecules were applied, and only the solvent and ion molecules were allowed to minimize for 2500 steps using the steepest descent method followed by 2500 steps using the conjugate gradient method. In the second step, the entire system was subjected to minimization for 2500 steps using the steepest descent method followed by 2500 steps using the conjugate gradient method. Following the minimization step, the systems were heated from 0 to 300 K using a Langevin thermostat, at a constant volume, for 1000 ps with time step of 2 fs. A weak restraint of 5 kcal/(mol·Å2) was applied on the solute during this run. Once the system was heated to 300 K, the solute restraints were removed, and the systems were equilibrated over a period of 1000 ps with a time step of 2 fs under constant pressure conditions in order to relax the solvent density. Finally, the production runs were carried out using the NPT ensemble for 100 ns with a nonbonded cut off of 8 Å and time steps of 2 fs at 300 K. During the equilibration and production steps, bonds involving hydrogens were constrained using the SHAKE algorithm. Long range electrostatic interactions were treated using the particle mesh Ewald (PME) method.(27) All the simulations were carried out using the PMEMD module of the Amber12 suite, which is the reimplementation of Sander utility, with improved performance.

    Trajectory Analysis

    The RMSD and RMSF analyses of the MD trajectory were performed using cpptraj(28) of AmberTools12. The hydrogen bond analysis was performed using the HBond plugin of VMD(29) using a distance cutoff of 3.5 Å and angle cutoff of 65°. The principal component (PC) and dynamics cross-correlation matrix (DCCM) analysis were performed using the R based Bio3D(30) package and Pymol.(31) For the DCCM analysis, the following approach was designed to obtain the delta correlation matrix:where Cij1 = cross correlation matrix of B-Raf/1 complex, Cij2 = cross correlation matrix of B-Raf/2 complex, Cij3 = cross correlation matrix of B-Raf/3 complex, Cij4 = cross correlation matrix of B-Raf/4 complex, CijPi = averaged cross correlation matrix of Pi complexes, and CijPb = averaged cross correlation matrix of Pb complexes.

    Cell Viability

    SW620 and A375 cells were obtained from ATCC. Cell viability assays (MTT) were carried out as described in ref 32. In brief, cells were seeded at 4000 and 1000 per well (96 well format), respectively, and treated with compounds the next day for 96 h. The compounds’ impact on cell viability was analyzed via the MTT assay.

    Immunoblotting

    SW620 and A375 cells were seeded at 750 000 and 300 000 cells per well (six well format) and 48 h later (75% confluence) treated with increasing compound concentrations for either 1 or 4 h. Cells were lysed in lysis buffer (10 mM Tris-HCL, 1% SDS, and 20 mM Na3VO4) and 10–25 μg of proteins from each sample were loaded, separated by SDS-PAGE, and transferred to PVDF membranes. Membranes were incubated with the indicated primary antibodies and analyzed by the Odyssey detection system from LI-COR. Used primary antibodies for MEK, phospho-MEK, and phospho-ERK were purchased from Cell Signaling, B-Raf from Santa Cruz, and actin from Sigma. Corresponding secondary antibodies were used from Rockland and Invitrogen.

    Cell Viability and Downstream Signaling Confirm the Activation Behavior of Pi and Pb Complexes

    In order to investigate the effect of the four studied B-Raf inhibitors in living cells, the human colon cancer cell line SW260 expressing wild-type B-Raf and the malignant melanoma A375 cell line expressing B-RafV600E were treated with increasing concentrations of 1, 2, 3, and 4 inhibitors. The resulting MTT assays (Figure 3) showed that in the cells expressing B-RafV600E (A375 cell line) all four studied inhibitors resulted in inhibition of the proliferation, and the Pi inhibitors (1 and 2) were found to be more potent compared with the Pb inhibitors. In the cells expressing wild-type B-Raf (SW620 cell line), we observed a slight increase in proliferation at high concentration of the Pi inhibitors compared with the Pb inhibitors.
    The effect of the four B-Raf inhibitors on the MAPK signaling pathway was evaluated by measuring the phosphorylation of MEK and ERK. As expected, the treatment with both Pi and Pb inhibitors led to reduced phospho-MEK and phospho-ERK levels in cells expressing B-RafV600E (Figure 4). In the cells expressing wild-type B-Raf, both Pi inhibitors (1 and 2) induced the phospho-MEK and phospho-ERK levels due to paradoxical activation effects (Figure 4a,b), in good agreement with the previous literature data.(12) On the other hand, treatment with the Pb inhibitors at high concentrations led to reduced levels of phospho-MEK and notably phospho-ERK in the cells expressing wild-type B-Raf (Figure 4c,d) confirming their lack of paradoxical activation behavior.
    These results confirm that the Pi inhibitors lead to higher proliferation in the cells expressing wild-type B-Raf, and the Pb inhibitors prevent the paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway. Since the paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway is linked to the conformational effects on the wild-type B-Raf kinase domain, the prevention of this effect may also be due to the difference in the intradomain communication, within the kinase domain, between Pi and Pb complexes. This hypothesis was further investigated by conformational proteomics and in silico molecular dynamics simulations.

    Conformational Changes in B-Raf Induced by Paradox Breakers and Paradox Inducers

    To investigate the mechanistic basis of the paradoxical activation of wild-type, but not mutant, B-Raf by ATP-competitive inhibitors, we analyzed B-Raf conformational changes in response to small molecule binding. Recently, the Gevaert lab demonstrated that limited proteolysis coupled with quantitative mass spectrometry can be used to characterize the conformational changes induced by binding of small molecules to protein kinases and pseudokinases.(22) In this approach, a kinase complexed to a ligand is partially digested in non-denaturing conditions. In this manner, only solvent-accessible protease cleavage sites will be processed and the released peptides reflect the protein conformation as more stable protein regions were found to yield fewer peptides upon partial digestion. These peptides are next separated from the remaining undigested protein and labeled with light stable isotope-containing tags. In parallel, peptides obtained upon full digestion of a denatured sample of the same kinase are tagged with heavy stable isotopes. The light and heavy labeled peptides are then mixed and identified and quantified by LC-MS/MS. The light/heavy ratio of each identified peptide is representative of the abundance of that peptide in the limited proteolysis experiment and can consequently be used as a proxy for the conformational status of the kinase.
    We here applied this method to study conformational changes of B-Raf upon binding to nucleotides and different inhibitors; either Pi (1 and 2) or Pb (3 and 4). As expected and in agreement with our previous study,(22) binding of ATP or its nonhydrolyzable analogue AMP-PNP stabilized the overall B-Raf structure, including the kinase domain (KD) and the Ras-binding domain (RBD) (Table 2, Figure 5). In contrast, binding of ADP rendered the KD less resistant to limited proteolysis. In fact, the stabilization of the KD was observed for all compounds assayed, indicative of their effective binding. However, the stabilizing effect of the KD was more pronounced for both Pi compounds than the Pb compound 3. Surprisingly, compound 4 showed changes in the limited proteolysis pattern more similar to the Pi compounds than to those evoked by the other Pb compound 3, which instead revealed a stabilizing effect of the KD, similar to both Pi compounds. Furthermore, while compounds 1 and 4 also stabilized the RBD, this effect could not be observed for the compound 3 and to a lesser degree for compound 2, which rather seemed to destabilize this region, though the observed differences were not significant. In addition, compound 3 seems to further destabilize a region C-terminal to the KD. Despite the relatively satisfying degree of detail obtained at the domain level of the B-Raf protein, the data obtained by the limited proteolysis based approach did not allow an unambiguous discrimination between the two classes of inhibitors.

    In a next set of experiments, more detailed analyses were carried out on the kinase domain by MD to elucidate the structural changes induced by the Pi and Pb inhibitors.

    Average per-Residue Fluctuation Difference Reveals the Most Dynamic Regions in B-Raf Complexes

    The molecular dynamics simulations of B-Raf active complexed with compounds 1, 2, 3, and 4 were stable as revealed by total, kinetic, and potential energy analysis (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The root mean square deviation (RMSD) analysis for the four B-Raf complexes (1, 2, 3, and 4) was performed using the respective backbone heavy atoms from the starting structure extracted after the minimization step as the reference structure. The RMSD is used to measure the scalar distance between same atoms of two identical proteins, that is, the reference starting structure and each MD snapshot. The mean RMSD values (Figure S2, Supporting Information) show no significant differences among the four complexes. The overall RMSD profile also does not appear to be discriminatory among the paradox breaker and paradox inducer systems.
    The Cα per residue root mean square fluctuation (RMSF) values were calculated using the respective closest to average structures. The RMSF analysis provides the fluctuation values in separate regions of the B-Raf complexes, which can be indicative of a discriminative property among the Pi and Pb complexes. We observed differences in certain regions of the B-Raf protein between the Pi and Pb complexes. The αC-helix and some preceding neighboring residues have a higher per residue RMSF values in the Pi complexes (Figure 6a) compared with the Pb complexes (Figure 6b). A similar trend is observed for the residues belonging to (and around) the A-loop in the B-Raf/2 complex exhibiting the highest fluctuation. On the other hand, in the C-lobe region, the overall RMSF values are higher for the Pb complexes. In the P-loop region, we observed a trend similar to that in the C-lobe, although one of the Pb complexes, B-Raf/3, has a comparable RMSF value as that of the Pi systems. These observations are largely in agreement with our limited proteolysis results. The A-loop residues, with significant RMSF differences in the Pi and Pb systems, are reported to have somatic mutations, which highlight the biological importance of these residues (Table 3). No such mutations have been reported in the αC-helix, P-loop, and C-lobe residues, where the RMSF differences are significant.

    Ligand–Receptor Hydrogen Bonds Discriminate between Pi and Pb Complexes

    The B-Raf-inhibitor hydrogen bonds were analyzed in the four complexes for the entire duration of 100 ns of MD simulations. While all studied inhibitors are close structural analogues containing a 7-azaindole scaffold, a well-studied hinge binder in kinase research,(33)1/4 and 2/3 share the same structural features in the hinge binding region of the protein kinase. We observed that all the hinge-binding moieties of the four inhibitors bind well to B-Raf hinge residues Q530 and C532 (Figure S3, Supporting Information), which is also evidenced by high hydrogen bond occupancy between the inhibitors and these two residues (Figure 7a). This observation is in agreement with the crystal structure of B-Raf complexed with 2.
    The hydrogen bond analyses of the B-Raf-inhibitor complexes show that the Pi complexes have a significantly higher number of hydrogen bonds between the inhibitor and the gatekeeper residue T529. This interaction actually discriminates paradox inducers from breakers and can be quantified by comparing the hydrogen bond occupancy between the residue T529 and the respective inhibitors (Figure 7a). For both Pi complexes, we observe that one of the oxygen atoms of the sulfonyl group forms a significant number of hydrogen bonds with T529. In the Pi complex with 2, it was observed that after approximately 32 ns of the production run, the hydrogen from the T529 side-chain −OH flips toward the ligand, forming a stable hydrogen bond with one of the oxygen atoms of the sulfonyl group of 2, which remains intact until the end of the simulation (Figure 7b, left panel; Figure S4, Supporting Information). A similar behavior is also observed in the Pi complex B-Raf/1 where the aforementioned flip occurs in the beginning of the simulation production run. This particular hydrogen bond is not observed in the Pb complexes (Figure 7b, right panel).
    The gatekeeper residue is a crucial structural feature that has been known to affect the kinase activation.(34) Hence, it is conceivable that the introduction of the sulfonylurea group in the paradox breakers prevents this interaction with the gatekeeper residue T529 and causes an overall conformational change in the Pb complexes, inducing an open conformation (αC-helix OUT, A-loop toward the binding site), compared with the sulfonamide group in paradox inducers, which may introduce a closed conformation (αC-helix IN, A-loop away from the binding site). It should be noted that the closed conformation of B-Raf kinase favors dimerization,(35, 36) which may lead to paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway in the presence of type I ATP-competitive inhibitors. The gatekeeper residue also helps in stabilization of the hydrophobic C-spine by virtue of its location near the VAIK motif and can hence affect the activation of the protein kinase. Thus, the difference in binding of the inhibitor with the gatekeeper residue could affect the stabilization of the C-spine. However, the comparison of the van der Waals interaction energy between the residue pairs that constitute the hydrophobic spines did not reveal significant differences between the Pi and Pb complexes (Figure S5, Supporting Information) suggesting that mainly ligand–receptor hydrogen bond networks affect the paradoxical behavior.

    Conformational Sampling along First Principal Component Discriminates between Pi and Pb Complexes

    We studied the structural differences between the Pi and Pb complexes by performing principal component analysis (PCA) and interpreting the relationship between different conformations sampled over the course of the simulation. PCA relies on the assumption that major collective modes of fluctuation dominate the dynamics during the simulation. This technique helps to reveal the underlying structure in the data obtained from MD simulations, by plotting the projection of distribution onto the subspace defined by the largest PC modes (PC1 and PC2).The PCA was calculated with the combined MD trajectories of the four complexes in order to take out the possible rotation effects. Our results show that the mean conformational sampling for each system occupies one unique quadrant of the PC plane. However, with respect to PC1 (mode with maximum variance), both the Pi complexes lie in the negative PC1 region, while both Pb complexes lie in the positive PC1 region (Figure 8). This could be indicative of the differences between the paradox inducers and paradox breakers at the level of conformational sampling in the PC plane.
    In order to study this further, we mapped the trajectory PC1 modes onto the respective 3D models of B-Raf. We extracted the MD trajectories corresponding to the PC1 motion and mapped these onto the 3D structures for all four complexes giving us an insight into the most significantly dynamic regions in these complexes. In the Pi complexes (B-Raf/1 and B-Raf/2), the A-loop and the loop adjacent to the αC-helix appear to be the most dynamic. In contrast, in the Pb complexes (B-Raf/3 and B-Raf/4), these regions appear to be relatively stable (Figure 9). On the other hand, the P-loop and the C-lobe region appear to be most dynamic in the Pb complexes, while these regions are relatively stable in the Pi complexes. Further, the per-residue PC1 values were plotted separately for the Pi and Pb complexes. We observed a similar pattern as in the per-residue RMSF plots (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The different dynamic regions observed in the RMSF analysis and in the first two principal components are largely in agreement. Therefore, the difference in the location of dynamic regions appears to allow for discrimination between the Pi and Pb complexes. This is a significant observation, because it has been shown that the inhibitor binding to protein kinase can affect highly localized structural elements, which can lead to a stable kinase conformation.(37) Hence, the difference in the dynamic behavior of the individual regions in Pi and Pb B-Raf complexes, as shown by the RMSF and PC analysis, may be crucial in determining the conformational changes, which may be responsible for paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway. In particular, it is noteworthy that the A-loop and the loop preceding the αC-helix are the most dynamic structural elements of B-Raf in the Pi complexes. The role of the αC-helix related conformational changes in particular has been investigated recently. It has been proposed that the movement of the αC-helix to an out position appears to disfavor Raf dimerization, consistent with the αC-helix forming part of the dimerization interface.(9) This effect is involved with dimer related interactions between the positively charged residues R506 and R509, which form a symmetry related cluster in the dimer interface. The role of αC-helix shift in activation related conformational changes in protein kinase has also been recently emphasized by Jambrina et al.(38) It may be hypothesized that the movement of the loop preceding αC-helix can guide the orientation of the αC-helix itself due to their close sequence and structural proximity. The movement of the A-loop and the αC-helix could in turn affect the overall conformation of the kinase, that is move toward a closed conformation, affecting the assembly of the hydrophobic spines. As discussed above in the context of hydrogen bonding, the closed conformation is more likely to form a dimer, which is a crucial factor in the paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway. In the Pb complexes however these structural elements (A-loop and αC-helix) are relatively stable. There has been evidence that another paradox breaker, PB04 (another vemurafenib analogue), is able to prevent the paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway by preventing Raf dimerization.(17)

    Residue Pair Cross-Correlation Provides Clues to Structural Differences between Pi and Pb Complexes

    Dynamic cross-correlation map (DCCM) provides information about the average correlation between residues during the course of the simulation. A positive value for the correlation (positive correlation) implies that the residues are moving toward each other, while on the other hand, a negative value of correlation (negative correlation) implies that the residues are moving away from each other. Upon observing the average DCCM for the four complexes analyzed in this study, obtained from MD simulations, we again see a difference in the patterns for the Pi and Pb complexes. In both the Pi complexes, we observed a high overall correlation among all the residues, either positive or negative, whereas in the DCCM of the two Pb complexes, we observed that fewer residues are correlated. There are several points on the Pb cross-correlation maps that correspond to zero correlation (Figure 10).

    Conclusions and Perspectives

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, we have investigated the structural basis of the paradox inducer (Pi) and paradox breaker (Pb) B-Raf inhibitors in the context of paradoxical activation of the MAPK pathway. We confirmed the effects of these inhibitors by the means of cellular studies and compared the paradox inducer and breaker complexes by performing molecular dynamics simulations and limited proteolysis. We successfully characterized the conformational changes in the two types of complexes and identified the discriminatory residues and interactions. The limited proteolysis approach allowed us to detect subtle but likely functionally crucial conformational changes in the B-Raf kinase domain upon binding of Pi and Pb inhibitors. Of note is that although our limited proteolysis data covered the main B-Raf protein domains, the obtained quantitative peptide maps did not allow us to readily discriminate paradox inducers from paradox breakers. Nevertheless, the striking stabilization effect of the kinase domain induced by these inhibitors prompted us to perform a more targeted and detailed analysis of this region by MD simulations. In our hands, these two techniques were found to be complementary, since the limited proteolysis approach allowed us to have a complete vision of the changes in the overall protein kinase structure, including key catalytic and regulatory domains, while MD simulations resulted in a more pointed and focused analysis providing useful information on crucial residues of the KD. The presence of the RBD, with crucial regulatory functions, and of all the regions beside the KD, likely influence those sites that are first attacked by trypsin and thus the resulting overall peptide pattern, partly explaining some lacks in the correlation of the MD and the limited proteolysis data. With the help of MD simulations, we were able to identify key interactions between B-Raf residues (especially the gatekeeper residue) and the inhibitor(s), which are possibly a result of the subtle conformational changes in the pocket binding regions of the inhibitors. We have shown that the induced conformational changes are different for the Pi and Pb inhibitors. These conformational changes in the Pi complexes affect the αC-helix and A-loop, which are responsible for the conformation switch mechanism.
    Since Raf binding to K-Ras and subsequent dimerization is the central theme in the paradoxical activation of MAPK pathway by ATP competitive inhibitors, further analysis of this effect should involve the study of B-Raf complexes in its dimeric form. The importance of the alignment of the hydrophobic spines in symmetrical dimers in the regulation of protein kinase and its effect on inhibitor binding(35) can be further studied using MD simulations at longer time scales. These effects were not evident in our study. The results of this study provide a clearer picture of the structural basis of paradoxical activation of B-Raf by ATP competitive inhibitors and can help in designing more potent and selective inhibitors with minimal side effects.
