    Abstract

    An inverse agonist of estrogen-related receptor-γ (ERRγ), an orphan nuclear receptor encoded by Esrrg, enhances sodium iodide symporter-mediated radioiodine uptake in anaplastic thyroid cancer (ATC) cells, thereby facilitating responsiveness to radioiodine therapy in vitro. We synthesized potent, selective, and orally bioavailable ERRγ-inverse agonists and evaluated their activity by analyzing in vitro pharmacology and absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicity profiles. X-ray crystallographic analysis of the ligand and ERRγ complex showed that 35 completely binds to the target protein (PDB 6A6K). Our results showed improved radioiodine avidity in ATC cells through compound 35-mediated upregulation of iodide-handling genes, leading to enhanced responsiveness to radioiodine therapy in vitro. Importantly, in vivo 124I-positron emission tomography/computed tomography imaging revealed that 35 increases radioiodine avidity in CAL62 tumors. Collectively, these results demonstrated that 35 can be developed as a promising treatment for ERRγ-related cancer in the future.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The nuclear receptor (NR) superfamily consists of ligand-dependent receptors and orphan receptors for which no endogenous ligands have been identified yet and both of which are involved in cellular metabolism, energy expenditure, and inflammation.(1,2) In 2009, NR modulators accounted for approximately 13% of all U.S. pharmaceutical sales and a considerable global share.(3) Orphan receptors, which constitute approximately half of the NR superfamily, provide a rich source of targets suitable for the development of NR-based small-molecule therapeutics for various human diseases.(4−9) The estrogen-related receptor-γ (ERRγ), encoded by Esrrg, is an orphan NR that belongs to the NR3B subfamily of structurally related transcription factors.(10) The NR3B subfamily includes three members commonly known as estrogen-related receptors (ERRs; ERRα, β, and γ).(11,12) Among them, ERRγ was the last to be identified, whereas the other two members, ERRα and β, were identified first in a screen designed to clone the estrogen receptor α (ERα)-related steroid hormone receptors.(13−16) ERRγ is a constitutively active NR that interacts with either peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-γ coactivator 1α (PGC-1α) or the corepressors of small heterodimer partner, DAX-1, and SMILE, in a ligand-independent manner and controls gene expression in response to the binding of endogenous small lipophilic ligands, such as estrogen, to a specific sequence in the regulatory regions of a target gene.(17−21) ERRγ is abundantly expressed in various tissues associated with the CNS, circadian clock, and basal metabolic function, including the brain, heart, kidney, skeletal muscle, and liver;(22) thus, it not only regulates ion homeostasis in related tissues but also controls metabolic processes such as mitochondrial biogenesis and hepatic gluconeogenesis.(23−28)
    Anaplastic thyroid cancer (ATC) is a rare and deadly disease with a poor prognosis. ATC is the least common type of undifferentiated cancer and accounts for less than 2% of all thyroid cancers in the United States.(29,30) A clinical review of 1771 patients between 1949 and 2007 revealed that ATC patients have a median survival of only 5 months and their 1 year survival rate is approximately 20%.(31) However, a standard chemotherapy regimen for most ATC patients is not currently available, although several studies of radiosensitizing agents such as cisplatin or paclitaxel have reported better 1 year survival rates for patients with nonmetastatic ATC.(29,32,33)
    Radioactive iodine still remains the mainstay therapy for those with undifferentiated thyroid cancers including follicular and papillary thyroid cancers and those at high risk of recurrence.(34) Unfortunately, tumors in many ATC patients do not take up enough iodine for proper therapy, resulting in radioiodine refractory character and dismal prognosis. Sodium iodide symporter (NIS) is a transmembrane glycoprotein that induces iodide uptake in thyroid follicular cells by stimulating thyroid stimulating hormone (TSH).(35,36) Radioiodine therapy with a nuclear medicine imaging technique utilizing NIS has been widely used in clinical settings for the treatment and diagnosis of well-differentiated thyroid cancers and their metastasis with minimal adverse effects.(37,38) Practical application of radioiodine therapy is restricted to poorly differentiated thyroid cancers, such as ATC exhibiting a progressive dedifferentiation with resistance to anticancer treatments and aggressive metastasis to other tissues including the lung, bone, and regional lymph nodes because of insufficient expression and poor function of NIS. Thus, some attempts have been made to restore the NIS function in ATC and other dedifferentiated thyroid cancers, such as enhancing the poor responsiveness to radioiodine, which includes the introduction of gene delivery and some small-molecule agents such as demethylating agents, nuclear receptor agonists, kinase inhibitors, and histone deacetylase inhibitors.(36,39) However, satisfactory therapeutic outcomes have not been achieved so far.
    In addition to the involvement of ERRγ in various metabolic diseases and cancers, we have broadened the therapeutic scope of the selective inverse agonist of ERRγ, GSK5182 (1), based on our findings that it enhances the NIS-mediated radioiodine uptake in ATC cells with either KRAS or BRAF mutation, facilitating responsiveness to radioiodine therapy in vitro.(40)
    With the aim of discovering novel ERRγ inverse agonists, several repetitive lead optimization studies were performed including the synthesis and pharmacological evaluation of 4-hydroxytamoxifen (4-OHT, 2) analogs.(41−43) The interesting findings from these studies provided the rationale for the development of new small-molecule therapeutics that can effectively enhance the NIS function in vivo with the potential to provide a clinical treatment option for ATC patients who do not respond to radioiodine therapy. Recently, a potent inverse agonist of ERRγ, DY40 (3), that suppressed the transcriptional functions of both ERRγ and ERRβ with superior half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50, 0.01 μM) in a cell-based reporter assay system was identified (Figure 1).(44)
    From the perspective of medicinal chemistry, in this study, synthetic strategies for further optimization of B/C-phenyl rings of the core scaffold with focused substituents were mainly studied to improve the in vitro pharmacological and absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicity (ADMET) profiles for subsequent in vivo assessments. Therefore, our objective was to further optimize the new ERRγ inverse agonists that possess a tetrasubstituted scaffold similar to that of compound 2 with better druggablility profiles applicable for in vivo experiments.
    Herein, we report the discovery of a candidate ERRγ inverse agonist (35) that is highly selective and orally bioavailable with reasonably good biocompatibility. To explore the potential of the candidate in an ATC model in vitro and in vivo, we investigated changes in the expression of iodide-handling genes such as NIS, thyroid stimulating hormone receptor (TSHR), thyroperoxidase (TPO), and thyroglobulin (TG) at mRNA and protein levels in ATC cells. Furthermore, the effects of the novel ERRγ inverse agonist on radioiodine avidity as well as radioiodine therapy in vitro were assessed. Finally, 124I-positron emission tomography/computed tomography (PET/CT) imaging was performed to determine the change in radioiodine incorporation in ATC tumor by oral administration of the novel ERRγ inverse agonist.

    Structure–Activity Relationship (SAR) Exploration

    Optimization studies of novel ERRγ inverse agonists were performed to increase their inverse agonistic potency, druglike property, and selectivity. We recently reported the structure-based rational design and synthesis of novel ERRγ inverse agonists exhibiting excellent druglike profiles comparable to those of 4-OHT (2) based on in vitro assays and ADMET evaluations.(43) In the previous study,(41−43) we investigated well-defined structural modifications to advance the therapeutic index based on our results in hand while adhering to accessible modern synthetic strategies. In this study, novel and advanced ERRγ inverse agonists were synthesized and selected through two strategic goals. First, the focus of this study was to investigate the SAR of novel compounds with a tetrasubstituted backbone similar to that of GSK5182 (1) with increased in vitro potency and selectivity. Second, we explored the role of B- and C-ring substitutions in controlling the overall druglike property of the derivatives by applying other small functional groups such as −Br and −Me to the B-ring or altering the phenolic position in the B-ring and by incorporating various piperazine functionalities to the C-ring. As a result, novel ERRγ inverse agonists with good activity and selectivity that expressed better in vitro ADMET and in vivo pharmacokinetics (PK) properties were obtained. Ultimately, we synthesized more advanced ERRγ inverse agonists to validate efficacy in pathological disease models related to ERRγ.
    First, our aim was to fix the oxidized state (p-OH) of the B-ring in R1 of GSK5182 (1) and then optimize it according to the changes in R2 and R3 groups. The synthesis of an oxidized state (p-OH) of the B-ring, which is highly likely to be caused by a major phase I metabolic reaction by cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes, gives much information. As a result, these initial modifications produced several modest to potent ERRγ compounds 4, 7, 12, 17, 19–22, and 30, one of which exhibited promising binding potency against ERRγ (IC50 = 0.040 μM; compound 12 in Table 1). Detailed analysis of this series with R1 = p-OH group (4, 7, 12, 17, 19–22, and 30) gave an insight into the initial SAR of the R2 group of which the piperazine structure incorporated by short chain alkyl groups (7, 12, 17, 19; C1–C3) has a relatively good binding activity (IC50 = 0.040–0.060 μM) compared to that of the non (4)- or bigger cyclic alkyl (20–22)-substituted piperazine moieties in the C-ring. When a carboximidamide group was tried for R2 by adopting guanidine, which has a higher hydrophilic property, on this position (30), it resulted in poor binding and functional activity unlike the other p-phenolic compounds. This suggests that strong base or electron-rich functional groups should not be incorporated into piperazine for key interaction with the target protein.

    In the case of the SAR study in the R1 group, the substitution of p-OH by p-Br (5, 8, and 13) exhibited relatively good activity, although the IC50 trend of the corresponding R2 group was different. Especially, the p-Br-substituted compound 13 bearing the isopropyl R2 group showed the highest activity among them (IC50 = 0.021 μM; Table 1). The p-Me group substitution for R1 (6, 9, and 14) resulted in a higher binding potency (IC50 = 0.017–0.027 μM) in general. Among them, p-Me-substituted compound 14 with the isopropyl R2 group also showed the most excellent binding activity. For the compounds having an electron-withdrawing nitro R1 group on the B-ring, the binding assay showed only moderate activity for both methyl and isopropyl R2 derivatives (10 and 15). Next, the two aniline-type compounds of the B-ring (11 and 16) with an electron-donating effect were tested. Their binding potency was also only moderate, although the methyl R2 substitution gave a slightly better binding activity than that of the isopropyl R2 group in this case.
    To obtain more information on the functional group varieties of the B-rings, the R1 group was converted into several bioisosteres by simple modifications, keeping the isopropyl R2 group of the piperazine structure on the C-ring. The sulfonate group changes for R1 (23–25) were not found to be effective compared to those of the original oxidized p-OH derivatives of the B-ring. Amide group changes for R1 (26–28) also showed poor overall binding affinity even compared to that of the moderately active p-NH2 derivative (16). In addition, guanidine substitution for the R1 group led to nonbinding result for the assay of compound 29.
    As a prodrug approach, we additionally produced three derivatives of compound 7 by applying pivalate functional groups to R1 and/or R3 (compounds 31–33); this approach was not effective in the in vitro binding assay as expected, but it also did not work in further in vivo PK study (data not shown).
    A comprehensive SAR in the in vitro binding assay showed that the R1 modification of the phenolic group in the B-ring was tolerable to −Br and −Me groups and was generally most active when a middle-sized isopropyl group with piperazine functionality (12–14) was introduced into the C-ring. To further understand the SAR by the R1 substitution position, a small group of derivatives (34–36) of the two previous compounds (12 and 13) were synthesized. The C-ring functional group was fixed with the best isopropylpiperazine structure for direct comparison. Compounds 12 (p-OH) and 35 (m-OH) showed marginal differences in good binding potency, whereas the derivative with ortho-OH positional change (34) in the B-ring completely lost its activity (IC50 = 6.619 μM). The m-Br substitution for R1 of the B-ring (36) also showed good binding potency compared to that of the previously described p-Br compound (13).
    Finally, to look into the binding potency of the m-piperazine isomer rather than the para position to the C-ring, we also synthesized two compounds (37 and 38). The change of a piperazine position from para to meta in the C-ring did not yield good results and revealed that the p-piperazine-substituted structure showed the optimal efficacy overall.

Their IC50 criteria should also be less than those of the reference compound 1 (0.110 μM).     Improved Radioiodine Avidity by Compound 35

    Compound 35 increased radioiodine avidity in ATC cells (Figure 2A,B). The maximum iodide uptake of CAL62 and BHT101 cells was observed at 6 and 12 μM concentrations of compound 35, respectively. To examine whether the increased radioiodine avidity is related to modulation of NIS function by compound 35, KClO4, a specific inhibitor of NIS, was coincubated with compound 35-treated ATC cells and the change in radioiodine uptake levels was determined. The iodide uptake assay showed that the enhanced radioiodine uptake was completely inhibited by KClO4 treatments (Figure 2C,D). These results indicated that the enhanced radioiodine avidity is due to compound 35-mediated increase of NIS functional activity.

    Compound 35-Mediated Upregulation of Crucial Gene Expression Related to Iodine Metabolism and Increased Cytotoxicity of I-131 in ATC Cells

    We investigated the effects of compound 35 on the mRNA and protein levels of ERRγ in CAL62 and BHT101 cells. As shown in Figure S41A, a change in the mRNA expression level of ERRγ was not observed in 35-treated CAL62 and BHT101 cells. However, there was a dose-dependent reduction in the protein level of ERRγ in 35-treated CAL62 and BHT101 cells (Figure S41B), which is consistent with our previous reports.(40,43)
    Next, we examined whether the increase in radioiodine avidity is related to compound 35-mediated regulation of iodide-handling genes such as NIS, thyroid stimulating hormone receptor (TSHR), thyroperoxidase (TPO), and thyroglobulin (TG). Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (PCR) analysis demonstrated that compound 35 increased NIS, TSHR, TPO, and TG mRNA expression in CAL62 and BHT101 cells (Figure 3A,B). In the case of CAL62 cells, a dose-dependent increase in the mRNA expression was observed. For BHT101 cells, higher expression of the iodide-handling genes was observed at 6 μM than at 12 μM. Furthermore, western blot analysis demonstrated that compound 35 increased the protein expression of iodide-handling genes in ATC cells in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 3C). Importantly, compound 35 increased membranous NIS protein in ATC cells (Figure 3D). Next, we further investigated changes in iodide-handling-gene expression at various time points after compound 35 treatment. Quantitative polymerase chain reaction analysis showed a time-dependent increase in the mRNA expression of NIS, TG, TPO, and TSHR in compound 35-treated CAL62 cell and BHT101 cells (Figure S42), consistent with the results of western blotting analysis (Figure 3E).
    Most ATC cells do not express iodide-handling genes; therefore, effective radioiodine uptake cannot be achieved, thereby resulting in the failure of radioiodine therapy. Based on the effective increase in radioiodine uptake in compound 35-treated ATC cells, enhanced susceptibility of ATC cells to radioiodine therapy could be expected. The in vitro clonogenic assay revealed that 131I or compound 35 alone cannot decrease colony formation (Figure 3F). However, a drastic reduction in the colony formation of ATC cells was confirmed when I-131 was combined with compound 35. Collectively, these results indicate that compound 35 is an attractive re-differentiator that restores the downregulated iodide-handling genes in ATC, thereby enhancing radioiodine therapeutic effects.

    In Vivo Imaging of Compound 35-Mediated Increase in Radioiodine Avidity in CAL62 Tumors

    As shown in Figure 4A, compound 35 increased radioiodine avidity in CAL62 tumors as demonstrated by 124I-PET/CT imaging. Increased accumulation of iodine was not found in the tumor of the vehicle-treated group (Figure 4B). However, a significant increase in radioiodine avidity was detectable in the tumor of the compound 35-treated group (Figure 4C), implying that compound 35 does enhance the radioiodine avidity in vivo.

    ERRγ and Compound 35 Binding

    To understand how a ligand binds to the ERRγ ligand-binding domain (LBD) (222–458), we solved the ERRγ crystal structure in complex with compound 35 at 2.9 Å resolution by molecular replacement. The collected data were refined and converged to final Rwork = 20.09% and Rfree = 26.33% using cystallographic object-oriented toolkit (COOT) and Python-based hierarchical environment for integrated xtallography (PHENIX) (Table S1). All figures were generated by Chimera (version 1.11.2, build 41376).(45) Analysis of the structure confirmed that compound 35 binds to the ligand-binding pocket of ERRγ. The affinity of compound 35 for the ERRγ ligand-binding pocket is enhanced owing to its proximity to the key residues (Tyr326, Asn346, Asp273) of the ligand (Figure 5). The alcohol functional group of compound 35 interacts with Tyr326 at 2.08 Å and Asn346 at 2.31 Å by hydrogen bonding. Additionally, the benzene ring of compound 35 and His434 ring form π stacking in a parallel-displaced pattern, and the basic chain of compound 35 interacts with Asp273 at 3.0 Å. Previously, we solved the crystal structure of ERRγ in complex with DN200434 (the paper under revision), and Glu275 was one of the key residues binding the para-hydroxyl group of the phenyl ring in close proximity at 2.46 Å. In the case of ERRγ in complex with compound 35, the hydrogen interaction between Glu275 and the meta-hydroxyl group of the phenyl ring is weakened at 3.86 Å, and it induces the alcohol functionality of compound 35 to move toward Tyr326 and Asn346. Consequently, compound 35 interacts with the key ERRγ binding pocket residues via hydrogen bonding, ion interactions, and pi stacking, enhancing its potency.

    In summary, a tetrasubstituted olefin analogue (compound 35) was synthesized, and its SAR was studied to enhance its ERRγ inverse agonist potency, selectivity, and bioavailability. The analogue synthesized in this study showed better in vitro ADMET profiles and PK parameters than those of the reference compound GSK5182 (1). Compound 35 increased radioiodine avidity in ATC cells, and the mechanism underlying this was found to involve regulation of iodide-handling genes. It also increased avidity in CAL62 tumors, as demonstrated by in vivo nuclear medicine imaging. X-ray crystallographic analysis of the ligand–ERRγ complex clearly demonstrated that 35 bound to the ERRγ protein. Therefore, this compound can be used as a potential therapeutic agent for ERRγ-related cancer.

    ERRγ Binding Assay

    The ERRγ binding assay was conducted with a LanthaScreen assay system (Life Technologies, Grand Island, NY), which was based on TR-FRET, following manufacturer’s instruction. Briefly, compounds were serially diluted 2-fold starting from 10 μM and 10 μL of each diluent was added in 384-well plates. Then, glutathione S-transferase (GST)-conjugated ERRγ ligand-binding domain (LBD) was added to be 5 nM. Next, the mixture of fluorescein-conjugated coactivator PGC-1α (final concentration to be 500 nM) and Tb-a-GST antibody (final concentration to be 5 nM) was added. The reaction mixture was incubated at rt in a dark state for 1 h. TR-FRET activity was measured at 340 nm excitation and 495/520 nm dual emission using a microplate reader (Biotek, SynergyNeo, Winooski, VT), and IC50 value was calculated with Prism 6 software.(46−48)

    ERRα and ERRβ and ERα Binding Assay

    ERRs and ERα binding assay used the same assay system as that used by ERRγ’s, LanthaScreen assay system. Therefore, the procedure was the same as that for the ERRγ binding assay except tested enzymes (ERRα, ERRβ, ERα). Used reference compounds were 4-OHT (2) for ERα and the ERRβ binding assay and XCT790 for the ERRα binding assay.

    ERRγ Functional Assay

    AD293 cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM), high glucose, supplemented with 0.5% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (Hyclone, Logan, UT). After 24 h, culture media were replaced with DMEM, high glucose, containing 10% FBS. DNA constructs, which include pCMX-Gal4-ERRγ, pFR-luciferase reporter plasmid, and pCMV-β-gal, were transiently transfected into AD293 cells using the TransIT-LT1 transfection reagent (Mirus, Madison, WI) for 24 h. Then, transfected cells were treated with compounds for 24 h and harvested for detection of luciferase activity and β-gal activity. All cells were incubated at 37 °C under 5% CO2 in a humidified incubator.(27)

    125I Uptake Assay

    Cells were plated in 24-well plates for 24 h and then treated with compound 35. After aspirating drug-containing medium, the cells were washed with 1 mL of Hank’s balanced salt solution (HBSS) and incubated with 500 μL of HBSS containing 0.5% bovine serum albumin (bHBSS), 3.7 kBq carrier-free 125I (PerkinElmer Life and Analytical Sciences), and 10 μmol/L solution of sodium iodide (specific activity of 740 MBq/mmol) at 37 °C for 30 min. The cells were then washed twice with ice-cold bHBSS and lysed with 500 μL of 2% sodium dodecyl sulfate. The radioactivity was measured using a γ counter (Cobra II; Canberra Packard, Packard Bioscience). The radioactivity of the cells was normalized using total protein concentrations determined by a bicinchoninic acid kit (Pierce Protein Biology, CA). To inhibit the iodide uptake, cells were pre-incubated with 300 μM KClO4 (as a specific inhibitor for NIS) for 30 min and then treated with 125I as described above.

    Clonogenic Assay with 131I

    The cells were seeded into six-well plates and left for 24 h to attach. At 24 h after treatment with 6 μM compound 35, the drug-containing medium was discarded and cells were washed twice in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). The medium was then replaced with DMEM in the presence or absence of 50 μCi 131I (KIRAMS, Korea) for 6 h. Cells were washed and left in regular culture medium for the time corresponding to six doublings. Finally, cells were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde solution and stained with 0.05% crystal violet. Control and 131I-treated colonies with more than 50 cells were counted.

    Analysis of mRNA Expression

    The cells were seeded in 100 π dishes for 24 h. Cells were then treated with various concentrations of compound 35. Total RNA was isolated using the TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen Co.) according to the manufacture’s protocol. Real-time quantitative PCR (Q-PCR) was performed with cDNA template (2 μL) and 2× Power SYBR Green (6 μL; Toyobo Co.) containing specific primers. Primer sequences for 36B4 (control gene), NIS, TSHR, TG, TPO, PAX-8, and TTF-1 are shown in Table 1. Q-PCR was carried out for 40 cycles using the following parameters: denaturation (95 °C for 15 s), annealing (60 °C for 1 min), and extension (60 °C for 1 min). Fluorescence intensity was measured at the end of the extension phase of each cycle. The threshold value for the fluorescence intensity of all samples was set manually. The reaction cycle at which PCR products exceeded this fluorescence intensity threshold during the exponential phase of PCR amplification was considered to be the threshold cycle (CT). Real-time PCR system instrument (Applied Biosystems) was used with the following primer sets:
    ERRγ (forward, 5′-AGGACAATTCAAGGCAATATAGA-3′; reverse, 5′-TCAAGACGCACCCCTTCTTT-3′), NIS (forward, 5′-CTG CCC CAG ACC AGT ACA TGC C-3′; reverse, 5′-TGA CGG TGA AGG AGC CCT GAA G-3′), TSHR (forward, 5′-ACC CTG ATG CCC TCA AAG AGC′; reverse, 5′-GCT TCA GTG TCA AGG TTT CAT TGC′), TPO (forward, 5′-CCT CTG CAA AGA TGT GAA CGA′; reverse, 5′-TCC CGG AGT CTA CGC AGG TT′), TG (forward, 5′-TCT AAC CGA TGC TCA CCT CTT CTG′; reverse, 5′-AGA TGA TGG CAC CTC CTT GAA CC′), and acidic ribosomal protein 36B4 (forward, 5′-CCA CGC TGC TGA ACA TGC T-3′; reverse, 5′-TCG AAC ACC TGC TGG ATG AC-3′). The target genes were normalized to the endogenous reference gene 36B4, and relative mRNA expression levels were calculated in the test and control samples.

    Western Blot

    The cells were treated with either vehicle or compound 35 for 24 h, and protein samples were prepared with radioimmunoprecipitation assay buffer containing complete protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche). Western blotting analysis was accomplished according to the same methods as those used in our previous work.(40)

    PET/CT Imaging with Radioactive 124I

    Mice were challenged with CAL62 cells (5 × 106 cells per mouse) subcutaneously. When tumor formation was detected with inspection and palpation, tumor-bearing mice were orally given either vehicle (poly(ethylene glycol)) or compound 35 (200 mg/kg) dissolved in solution (10% ethanol, 10% Cremphol, 80% PEG) once a day for 6 days. CAL62-tumor-bearing mice received radioactive 124I (50 μCi per mouse for PET imaging) via tail vein. After 2 h of circulation time, PET/CT imaging was performed.
    For PET/CT study, 20 min scan was performed with the Triumph II PET-CT system (LabPET8, γ Medica-Ideas, Waukesha, WI). CT scans were performed with an X-ray detector immediately following the acquisition of PET images. PET images were reconstructed by 3D-OSEM iterative image reconstruction, and the CT images were reconstructed using filtered back-projections. All mice were anesthetized using 1–2% isoflurane gas during imaging. PET images were coregistered with anatomical CT images using 3D image visualization and analysis software VIVID (γ Medica-Ideas, Northridge, CA). To measure the uptake for the volumes of interest (VOIs), the VOIs from each image were manually segmented from coregistered CT images by the use of VIVID.

    Statistical Analysis

    All data are expressed as the means ± SD, and statistical significance was determined using an unpaired Student’s t-test using GraphPad Prism 5 (La Jolla, CA). P values less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant.

    Protein Expression and Purification

    Human recombinant ERRγ-LBD (residues 222–458) was cloned in pGEX-6P1 vector plasmids and overexpressed in Escherichia coli BL21 DE3 cells. Single colonies of transformants were inoculated into Luria–Bertani medium at 37 °C. Once the OD600 reached 0.4–0.6, 1 mM isopropyl β-d-1-thiogalactopyranosid was added to the culture, and the culture was incubated at 18 °C for 16 h. The harvested cells were resuspended with PBS containing 1 mM phenylmethanesulfonyl fluoride, 10 μg/mL DNase I, and a protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche). The cells were sonicated and centrifuged at 20 000g for 20 min at 4 °C, and the supernatant was filtered with 0.20 μm syringe filters. The lysates were purified via a glutathione sepharose 4B column (GE Healthcare), followed by tag-cleavage with PreScission protease. The tag-free proteins were applied to the Hitrap Q HP column and the Superdex 75 column to increase the homogeneity. The purified proteins were concentrated to 5.2 mg/mL in 20 mM sodium acetate (pH 4.5) and 100 mM sodium chloride for crystallization.

    Protein Crystallization

    The purified ERRγ-LBD recombinant proteins were mixed with 3-fold molar excess of compound 35 and incubated for 1 h on ice, followed by rt incubation for 30 min. The precipitates were removed by centrifugation. The mosquito liquid handler was used for the initial screening of crystallization, and the crystal screening plates were maintained on the Rock Maker at 18 °C until crystals appeared. The cocrystals of proteins in complex with compound 35 appeared within 5 days under the condition of 0.1 M bis-Tris (pH 6.5), 16% PEG, 10 K, and 3% methanol.

    Data Collection, Structure Determination, and Refinement

    Diffraction data were collected at 2.90 Å resolution of beamline BL-5C in the Pohang Accelerator Laboratory, South Korea. HKL2000 (HKL Research, Inc., Charlottesville, VA) was used for integration and scaling. Molecular replacement was used to determine the structure of human ERRγ-LBD in complex with compound 35 using a previously solved human ERRγ-LBD structure (PDB entry 5YSO) as a search model without water and ligand. Rigid body refinement, followed by simulated annealing at 5000 K, was conducted using Python-based hierarchical environment for integrated xtallography (PHENIX). Subsequently, refinement was conducted in alternating cycles for manual model building in (COOT), followed by refinement in PHENIX until the R factors converged. Data collection and refinement statistics are shown in Table S1. The coordinate and structural factors of the complex structure have been deposited in the Protein Data Bank (PDB code 6A6K). The complex structure figures were generated using Chimera.
