    Abstract

    KRAS, the most common oncogenic driver in human cancers, is controlled and signals primarily through protein–protein interactions (PPIs). The interaction between KRAS and SOS1, crucial for the activation of KRAS, is a typical, challenging PPI with a large contact surface area and high affinity. Here, we report that the addition of only one atom placed between Y884SOS1 and A73KRAS is sufficient to convert SOS1 activators into SOS1 inhibitors. We also disclose the discovery of BI-3406. Combination with the upstream EGFR inhibitor afatinib shows in vivo efficacy against KRASG13D mutant colorectal tumor cells, demonstrating the utility of BI-3406 to probe SOS1 biology. These findings challenge the dogma that large molecules are required to disrupt challenging PPIs. Instead, a “foot in the door” approach, whereby single atoms or small functional groups placed between key PPI interactions, can lead to potent inhibitors even for challenging PPIs such as SOS1-KRAS.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    With more than 500,000 estimated interactions in humans,(1) protein–protein interactions (PPIs) are important for essentially all biological processes. PPI networks contain indispensable proteins, which are often targeted by disease-causing mutations, viruses, and drugs.(2−6) With mutation rates of 86–96% in pancreatic cancers, 40–54% in colorectal cancers, and 27–39% in lung adenocarcinomas,(7,8) one such cancer driver is the KRAS oncogene. Now, after decades of research, the first inhibitors against the KRASG12C mutant are showing promise in clinical trials,(9,10) but no clinical agents against the most prevalent mutants KRASG12D and KRASG12V have been discovered to date.
    Cycling between active (GTP-bound) and inactive (GDP-bound) forms, KRAS is best understood as a molecular switch (Figure 1). If mutated, intrinsic or GAP-induced GTPase activity of KRAS is reduced and the amount of GTP-bound KRAS increases. Key regulators of KRAS are the guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs) son of sevenless homolog 1 and son of sevenless homolog 2 (SOS1 and SOS2). Both SOS1 and SOS2 are involved in distinct PPIs with RAS.(11−13) The catalytic sites of SOS1/2 bind GDP-bound KRAS, promote GDP exchange to GTP, and activate downstream signaling of the MAP kinase pathway. Interestingly, only SOS1 seems to be subject to allosteric activation.(14) Binding of GTP-bound KRAS to the allosteric site leads to upregulation of the activity of the catalytic site of SOS1. In contrast, no evidence for SOS2 allosteric activation exists beyond the high similarity of the allosteric site region to that of SOS1.(15)
    A feedback release mechanism mediated from signaling molecules downstream of KRAS only leads to ERK-mediated phosphorylation of SOS1 but not its paralog SOS2, thereby attenuating the SOS1 GEF activity.(16,17) While SOS1 is only rarely mutated in most cancers,(18) SOS1-activating mutations are common in RASopathies,(19,20) with SOS1 being the second most frequently mutated gene (around 11%) in Noonan Syndrome patients(21,22) and hereditary gingival fibromatosis.(23)
    Efforts to identify small molecules binding to SOS1 have led to two different classes of SOS1 binders: (1) SOS1 activators, which bind to SOS1 but do not inhibit the PPI between KRAS and SOS1, and (2) SOS1 inhibitors, which bind to SOS1 and sterically prevent KRAS from binding. Fesik’s group has published a series of papers elucidating the discovery and optimization of SOS1 activators,(24–31) demonstrating that aminopiperidine indoles (exemplified by compound 3 in Table 1) stabilize the interaction of SOS1 and KRAS and increase guanine nucleotide exchange. Shortly thereafter, fragments with a similar binding mode but no measurable effect on the nucleotide exchange were published.(32) Recently, we and others highlighted the discovery of SOS1 inhibitors,(33,34) which occupy the same binding site as the aforementioned SOS1 activators with BI 1701963 currently undergoing phase 1 clinical trials.(35)

    In this paper, we describe the structure-based design of inhibitors of the PPI between SOS1 and KRAS. We hypothesized that inhibiting the catalytic activity of SOS1 by blocking its PPI with KRAS will reduce the levels of active KRAS, counteract cancer-associated activation of the MAPK pathway, and therefore have a selective effect on cancers driven by KRAS-activating mutations. To generate starting points for SOS1 inhibitors, we conducted a high-throughput screen using two complementary biochemical assays and surface plasmon resonance for biophysical validation. A robust system for crystallizing small molecules bound to SOS1 allowed for fast and efficient optimization using structure-based design. The stepwise evolution of our SOS1 inhibitor program culminated in BI-3406, an orally bioavailable, nanomolar potent and selective inhibitor of the SOS1::KRAS PPI, which binds to the catalytic site of SOS1 at the cusp of the RAS binding interface. BI-3406 reduces SOS1-catalyzed formation of GTP-KRAS, attenuates downstream signaling (pERK), and impairs cell proliferation of KRAS-driven cancers. Intriguingly, we show that positioning one single atom between key amino acid pairs (R73 on KRAS and Y884 on SOS1) is sufficient to efficiently inhibit a PPI despite its large surface (buried surface area: 3490 A2) and micromolar affinity between SOS1 and RAS.(36) These findings demonstrate a new paradigm to design inhibitors of protein–protein interactions, which might be generally applicable to GEFs and beyond.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS

    In our efforts to identify binders to SOS1, we designed a high-throughput screen (HTS) to search for inhibitors of the SOS1::KRASG12D PPI (Figure 2). We implemented rigorous biochemical and biophysical triaging for the fast identification of structurally enabled starting points. The Boehringer Ingelheim and FORMA Therapeutics proprietary libraries consisting of 1.7 million compounds in total were screened using a miniaturized KRASG12D-biotin GST-SOS1 protein interaction assay utilizing AlphaScreen(37) and fluorescence resonance energy transfer(38) (TR-FRET) readouts as orthogonal technologies. Due to its superior signal to noise and the better S/N ratio (Z′) compared to the TR-FRET format, the AlphaScreen technology was used in the primary screen. The primary hits were filtered in silico by removing non-drug-like molecules and pan assay interference (PAINS)(39) compounds and subsequently confirmed in the AlphaScreen assay at the screening concentration. From the remaining hits, false-positives were removed using an AlphaScreen shortcut assay utilizing a biotinylated GST protein. Hits that also inhibited the chemoluminescence in the absence of a PPI were considered as singlet oxygen quenchers and removed from further analysis. The resulting 2500 confirmed actives were submitted to dose–response testing in the AlphaScreen and TR-FRET assay format in parallel. Interestingly, only 47 hits (<2%) inhibited the SOS1::KRASG12D interaction with an IC50 value of less than 10 μM. Despite the shortcut assay in the screening cascade, only 18% of the <10 μM AlphaScreen hits could be confirmed in the TR-FRET assay. This highlights the power of the orthogonal biochemical screening paradigm. To distinguish between SOS1 and KRAS binders, we subsequently measured binding to SOS1 by SPR and to KRASG12D by using HSQC NMR. While none of the hits showed affinity to KRASG12D, 18 hits with SOS1 SPR KD’s of less than 15 μM were identified. Seventeen of these biophysical confirmed hits belonged to a cluster of structurally closely related quinazolines, exemplified by compound 1. In addition, a single compound (2) was obtained from a different structural series (Table 1).
    X-ray cocrystal structures of initial HTS hits and computational chemistry methods guided selectivity and potency optimization in the next steps. Although structurally unrelated to SOS1 activators, the obtained screening hits bind to the same, previously described pocket, albeit pointing directly toward the SOS1::KRAS interaction site.(24)
    As illustrated in Figure 3A, both SOS1 activators and inhibitors address a large lipophilic pocket defined by F890, L901, and M878. However, also new interactions between the inhibitors and SOS1 are formed. A hydrogen bond between the anilinic NH and N879 serves as a polar anchor between the inhibitor and the protein. Importantly, π-stacking of the quinazoline core with His905 significantly contributes to the affinity. Finally, the 6-methoxy substituent forms an important CH−π interaction with Y884. Compared to SOS1 activators, the different binding mode of inhibitors leads to a very distinct response. Whereas both activators and inhibitors have comparable affinity to SOS1 (measured by SPR), only 1 effectively disrupts the SOS1–KRAS interaction and causes inhibition in an AlphaScreen assay, displaying dose–response curves quantified by the half-maximal inhibitory value (IC50). In contrast to 1, activator 3 stabilizes the interaction of SOS1 and KRAS. The resulting activation of SOS1 is reflected by an increase in the signal in the AlphaScreen assay and can be quantified by a half-maximal effective concentration value (EC50), indicating an increasing signal. For comparison of both modes of action, see Figure 3B.
    Initial activities to optimize our starting points were focused on the hit series of quinazolines. All members of this series had already shown good potency in the AlphaScreen; however, the compounds originated from the historical EGFR inhibitor program and, as such, displayed strong EGFR inhibition. Profiling against a selected set of 31 kinases confirmed the high affinity of compound 1 toward EGFR kinase (IC50, 1 nM) and other kinases. Docking of 1 into the protein structure derived from the known cocrystal X-ray structure of EGFR kinase domain and afatinib(40) illustrates that the quinazoline core (especially via positions 1 and 2) forms strong interactions with the hinge motif of EGFR (Figure 4). Consequently, a methyl substituent was introduced to the 2-position of the quinazoline core to disrupt kinase hinge binding. The resulting compound 4 displayed a significantly decreased kinase activity toward EGFR as well as a panel of 31 kinases (Table 2).

    To optimize our SOS1 inhibitors further, we turned our attention toward the second HTS hit, compound 2. Although lacking the quinazoline core, its potency was comparable to 1. The surprising high affinity of compound 2 toward SOS1 was assumed to originate from an additional interaction between the sulfonamide substituent and the backbone carbonyl of Lys898 (Figure 5). Combining the superior π-stacking ability of compound 4 with the sulfonamide substituent of 2 resulted in ligand 5, which showed improved potency and no residual kinase activity (see also Table 2). Unfortunately, compound 5 suffered from a pronounced efflux ratio of 50, so alternative, less polar meta-substituents were explored. Among the most interesting were the trifluoromethyl and amino groups. The obtained IC50 and KD values show that the lipophilic van der Waals interaction of the CF3 substituent of compound 6 with Leu901 of SOS1 compensated for the hydrophilic interaction of the sulfonamide with Lys898 while eliminating the unfavorable efflux ratio (Figure 6A). Interestingly, introduction of a NH2 group resulted in a flip of the benzene ring and formation of a new H-bond with the backbone carbonyl of Met878 (compound 7 in Figure 6B). Although the potency of inhibitor 7 was only marginally improved compared to compound 4, we decided to combine both meta-substituents into a single molecule (compound 8) as the overlay of the two crystal structures highlighted the nearly identical binding mode to SOS1 (see also Figure 6C). The sevenfold increase in potency of inhibitor 8 compared to compound 7 might be surprising at first but can be rationalized by acidification of the amino substituent by the electron-withdrawing CF3 group. This highlights the need to consider the overall properties of molecules in drug design rather than rely on the additivity of structure–activity relationships.
    We next turned our attention to the actual protein–protein interface between SOS1 and KRAS. PPIs usually cover large areas, the interface between SOS1 and KRAS being particularly large at 1750 A2 on the SOS1 surface (buried surface area of 3490 A2 for the whole interface area on both proteins; calculated with the PDBe PISA (Proteins, Interfaces, Surfaces, and Assemblies) service at the European Bioinformatics Institute(41)). We speculated that the cation−π interaction between R73 (KRAS) and Y884 (SOS1) observed in the SOS1 KRAS cocrystal structure (Figure 3) plays a pivotal role in the interaction of both proteins.(11) To test this hypothesis, the affinity of SOS1 Y884A to KRAS was compared to the affinity of native SOS1 to KRAS. When adding untagged native SOS1, tagged SOS1 Y884A was far easier displaced from KRAS than tagged wild-type SOS1 (factor 30), indicating that the R73-Y884 interaction between SOS1 and KRAS is indeed important (Figure 7).
    In the binding event of compound 8 to SOS1, R73 of KRAS is displaced by the 6-methoxy substituent. This leads to disruption of the PPI and consequently prevents the nucleotide exchange of KRAS. Intrigued by this simple but effective mode of inhibition of SOS1, we decided to test compounds that do not feature a substituent that is able to address Y884. Strikingly, when a derivative without methoxy substituents (9) was tested, activation instead of inhibition was observed in the AlphaScreen (Table 3). This minimal structural change of two heavy atoms is sufficient to turn an SOS1 activator into an SOS1 inhibitor. It is worth mentioning that the binding affinity measured by SPR of activator 9 toward SOS1 (KD = 350 nM) is 20-fold reduced compared to inhibitor 8 (KD = 16 nM) and its binding mode to SOS1 is conserved except for the missing interaction with Y884 (Table 3 and Figure 8A).

    To elucidate the exact requirements for inhibition versus activation, a series of compounds with varied substituents at the 6-position and containing hydrogen at position 7 were prepared and measured. Exchanging hydrogen in compound 9 for fluorine gave compound 10, which remained an activator, albeit with a 2–3-fold loss of binding affinity and activator activity. Exchanging the 6-substituent for the larger chlorine atom (compound 11) was sufficient to switch the molecule from an activator to an inhibitor despite the 4-fold loss of activity versus the hydrogen-containing compound 9. The loss of activity for compound 11 is likely due to a repulsive interaction between the electronegative chlorine atom and π-electrons of Y884. This clearly shows that PPI inhibition is caused by sterics and is independent of affinity. The addition of a methoxy group capable of forming attractive CH−π interactions with Y884 results in the inhibitor compound 12, which displayed a significantly improved affinity (KD = 24 nM) and activity in the AlphaScreen (IC50 = 33 nM). Remarkably, the size of a single atom was the decisive factor for the mode of action and exchange of fluorine for a chlorine substituent turned an activator into an inhibitor.
    To find the optimal analogue to compound 8, further derivatives at the 6-postion were explored. The tetrahydrofuryl substituent lead to BI-3406, a 4-fold more potent compound that otherwise displayed sufficient permeability, solubility, and the pharmacokinetic profile for oral daily dosing in rodents (Table 4). BI-3406 represents a useful probe compound to investigate SOS1 biology, downstream signaling, and effects on proliferation.

    We have recently published that inhibition of SOS1 by BI-3406 reduces the formation of GTP-loaded KRAS, inhibits MAPK pathway signaling, and exhibits activity in a broad spectrum of KRAS alleles, including all major G12D/V/C and G13D oncoproteins.(33) Cell lines expressing mutant KRAS, such as DLD1 carrying a G13D mutation, are sensitive in 3D proliferation assays, whereas NCI-H520, a KRAS wild-type cell line, is resistant to BI-3406 (Table 4).
    However, due to the role of SOS1 as an important feedback node in the MAP kinase pathway,(16,17) we expect the biggest impact of SOS1 inhibitors in combination therapy settings. We previously showed that BI-3406 blocks the negative feedback relief induced by downstream MEK inhibition.(33,42) Accordingly, the combination of BI-3406 with the MEK inhibitor trametinib elicited strong antitumor efficacy in KRAS-mutant xenograft models. The concept of dual SOS1 and MEK inhibition is currently being tested in clinical trials.(35) To demonstrate the utility of BI-3406 as an SOS1 probe molecule and further elaborate the potential of SOS1 inhibition in cancer combination therapies, we tested the effects of vertical pathway co-inhibition by combining BI-3406 with the EGFR inhibitor afatinib. In DLD-1 KRASG13D mutant colorectal tumor cells, dual pathway inhibition of SOS1i and afatinib leads to enhanced PARP cleavage, a marker for apoptosis induction (Figure 9A). The effect of this combination on PARP cleavage is of similar magnitude as the effect of the BI-3406 plus trametinib combination. In agreement with the in vitro data, combination of BI-3406 with afatinib resulted in significantly enhanced efficacy compared to both monotherapies in a DLD-1 xenograft model (Figure 9B). This combination concept is in line with recent reports describing marked synergy of the anti-EGFR antibody cetuximab with KRASG12C inhibitors in KRASG12C mutant cancer cell lines(43) and of the EGFR inhibitor osimertinib with SOS1 inhibitors in EGFR mutant lung cancer cell lines.(44) Our results highlight the potential of SOS1 inhibitors as a combination backbone to augment the antitumor effect of MAPK pathway inhibitors, such as KRAS, EGFR, and MEK inhibitors through vertical pathway inhibition (Figure 9C).

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    With the emergence of KRASG12C-selective approaches, the need for additional therapies targeting other prevalent oncogenes of KRAS is increasingly coming into focus. One of the key regulators of KRAS is SOS1. To come up with a tool compound for dissecting cellular functions of SOS1 in the tumorigenesis of a broad range of KRAS-driven cancers, a high-throughput screen based on biochemical and biophysical triaging was used to identify suitable starting points. Structure-based drug discovery allowed for efficient rational design and guided selectivity and potency optimization of the corresponding hits early on. Fine-tuning of the resulting SOS1 inhibitors with respect to DMPK properties eventually led to the discovery of BI-3406. The compound binds to the catalytic domain of SOS1 at the periphery of the KRAS binding interface. Positioning of a comparably small substituent between a key amino acid pair formed by KRAS and SOS1 allowed for potent and selective PPI inhibition. Variation of this substituent led to the surprising observation that a single atom determines whether our compounds activate or inhibit SOS1. This is one of the rare examples where reversing the mode of action (activator–inhibitor) by a marginal structural modification has been described.(45) These findings demonstrate a new paradigm to design inhibitors of PPIs, which might be generally applicable to small GTPase–guanine nucleotide exchange factor PPIs and beyond. Finally, to tap into synergistic effects of vertical MAPK pathway inhibition, the dual combination of BI-3406 with an EGFR inhibitor was examined. This combination concept leads to stronger antitumor efficacy and is consistent with the importance of SOS1 as an important feedback node in cancers driven by a broad range of KRAS-mutant oncoproteins.
