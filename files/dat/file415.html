    Abstract

    Peptidase inhibitors (PIs) have been broadly studied due to their wide therapeutic potential for human diseases. A potent trypsin inhibitor from Tityus obscurus scorpion venom was characterized and named ToPI1, with 33 amino acid residues and three disulfide bonds. The X-ray structure of the ToPI1:trypsin complex, in association with the mass spectrometry data, indicate a sequential set of events: the complex formation with the inhibitor Lys32 in the trypsin S1 pocket, the inhibitor C-terminal residue Ser33 cleavage, and the cyclization of ToPI1 via a peptide bond between residues Ile1 and Lys32. Kinetic and thermodynamic characterization of the complex was obtained. ToPI1 shares no sequence similarity with other PIs characterized to date and is the first PI with CS-α/β motif described from animal venoms. In its cyclic form, it shares structural similarities with plant cyclotides that also inhibit trypsin. These results bring new insights for studies with venom compounds, PIs, and drug design.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Peptidases (proteases) are involved in a wide range of biological processes and in many human diseases. Therefore, peptidase inhibitors (PIs) have been studied due to their wide therapeutic potential as regulators of these enzymes.(1−3) Serine PIs are the largest superfamily of PIs and widely distributed in nature, and they can be subdivided into many classes based on their conserved functional motifs. The Kunitz-type inhibitors are the best characterized of them and, currently, the most described from venomous/poisonous animals.(4) Among the other structural motifs found in these animals are the Ascaris-type, Kazal-type, and Bowman–Birk-type motifs. Moreover, some of these PIs are also K+ channel blockers; however, the site of interaction with K+ channels differs from the site of interaction with peptidases.(4)
    Besides these, naturally occurring cyclic peptides rich in disulfide bonds have been characterized from plants and are known as cyclotides, which have approximately 30 amino acid residues and a cyclic cystine knot (CCK) motif. A minor cyclotide subfamily is known as the trypsin inhibitors, which currently contains the peptides MCoTI-I to MCoTI-VIII, from the bitter gourd Momordica cochinchinensis.(5) Head-to-tail cyclization increases the peptide’s resistance to exopeptidase digestion, which leads to a higher stability and half-life. Together with the CCK motif, these features are of great interest for drug design.(5)
    To date, three types of PIs have been described from scorpion venoms: (i) Kunitz-type,(6−12) with 58–70 amino acid residues, reticulated by three or four disulfide bonds; (ii) Ascaris-type,(12−16) with about 65 amino acid residues and five disulfide bonds; and (iii) serpin-type,(11,12) only with putative compounds to date. Here, we present the characterization of an initially acyclic peptide with 33 amino acid residues, isolated from Tityus obscurus scorpion venom, found in the Amazonian region, which evolves into a head-to-tail cyclic peptide in the presence of trypsin, acquiring structural characteristics like those of plant cyclotides. ToPI1 features make it unique, with no similarity with any PI previously described from venomous animals.

    Chemical Characterization of Native ToPI1

    The fractionation of T. obscurus venom by RP-HPLC yielded about 60 chromatographic fractions (Figure 1A), which were all tested against trypsin by means of a chromogenic assay. The fraction eluting at 26.3% acetonitrile was the only active fraction. Then, it was purified (Figure 1B), resulting in the peptide named ToPI1, from Tityus obscurus peptidase inhibitor 1. With a molecular mass of [M + H]+ = 3806.89 Da (Figure S1A,B), native ToPI1 was active against trypsin in a non-quantitative assay. Its amino acid sequence was partially determined by MALDI in-source decay (ISD) method, resulting in residues 9–29 (Figure S1C), and by the cDNA library. ToPI1 precursor has 177 nucleotides that encode a 58-amino acid residue peptide, including a signal peptide of 23 residues at the N-terminus and two C-terminal residues (-GK) that, when cleaved, result in the mature toxin with 33 residues. These two residues are enzymatically cleaved in the formation of the mature toxin, which is amidated at the C-terminus (Figure 1C), a common post-translational modification in scorpion toxins.(17) As expected, the theoretical molecular mass of the mature toxin ([M + H]+ = 3806.8930), including three disulfide bonds and the C-terminal amidation, matches with its experimental molecular mass ([M + H]+ = 3806.8975) (Figure S1B), with an accuracy of 1.2 ppm.
    ToPI1 presents low sequence similarity with other sequences from public databases, and all of them are scorpion venom K+ channel toxins (KTxs) or putative toxins (Figure 1C). Sequence similarity with KTxs was only detected when considering the precursor sequences since the signal peptides, besides the cysteine residues, were the main responsible for sequence similarity. Searches for similarity with ToPI1 mature peptide with other non-redundant sequences available in databases did not result in significant similarities.

    Chemical Synthesis of ToPI1s and ToPI1s-K21A

    Due to the insufficient amount of ToPI1 in the venom, it was chemically synthesized by means of the Merrifield technique, oxidized to form the three disulfide bonds and purified, being named ToPI1s (Figure S2). This nomenclature was used only to clarify whether native or synthetic peptide was used, but it is worth saying that no difference was detected between them during this study. The yield of the pure folded peptide (>97% purity) with [M + H] + = 3806.89 Da was about 8%. Since, in many KTxs, Lys21 is predicted to be one of the key amino acid residues for blockage of K+ channel currents, the analogue ToPI1s-K21A was also synthesized, replacing Lys21 by Ala21.

    Chromogenic Assays for Serine Peptidase Inhibition

    Such as native ToPI1, ToPI1s also inhibits trypsin, with a 1:1 stoichiometric ratio (Figure 2A). ToPI1s exhibits no inhibitory effect against chymotrypsin, and it almost completely loses its trypsin inhibitory activity in its reduced form, with no disulfide bonds (maximum of 20% inhibition at 3.5 mM) (Figure 2C). The mutation Lys21 to Ala21 did not have major effects in trypsin inhibition since ToPI1s-K21A and ToPI1s had similar inhibition profiles, which were also shared by BPTI, one of the most active and studied PIs.(4,18) The extra sum-of-squares F test showed no difference between the IC50 values of ToPI1s, ToPI1s-K21A, and BPTI, which varies from 214.0 to 232.3 nM (R2 = 0.9758) [F(2, 130) = 1745, p = 0.18] (Figure 2B). The concentration–response profiles for trypsin inhibition of these three inhibitors were well fit by the Morrison’s quadratic equation (Figure 2A), which is in agreement with tight binding inhibitors.(19)

    Surface Plasmon Resonance

    The ToPI1s interaction kinetics with trypsin was evaluated by surface plasmon resonance (SPR) at nanomolar concentrations from 15 to 35 °C (Figure S3). Surface control experiments (Figure S3A,B) were performed, and they indicated that the ToPI1s:trypsin interaction is bimolecular, with no obvious intermediate states, and that mass transport effects are negligible in these experimental conditions. Hence, further experiments were performed at a flow rate of 40 μL/min. As shown in Figure 2D, at 25 °C, ToPI1s (25–0.78 nM) strongly binds to trypsin and has a slow dissociation rate. Sensorgrams recorded at other temperatures are shown in Figure S3. The dissociation equilibrium constant (KD) of the ToPI1s:trypsin complex at 25 °C was 530 ± 76 pM, which was similar in the other tested temperatures (Table 1). Both the sensorgrams and the kinetic parameters show that, as temperature increases, association (ka) and dissociation (kd) rates become faster.

    Electrophysiological Tests

    ToPI1s, at 5 μM concentration, was electrophysiologically tested on six K+ channel subtypes (hKv1.1, hKv1.4, EAG1, hERG1, hERG2, and hERG3). Considering that this is a high toxin concentration compared to that of other K+ channel scorpion toxins (KTxs), the reduction in the current caused by the ToPI1s at 5 μM was minimal or null in the tested subtypes (Figure S4A). There was no statistical difference between the groups treated with 5 μM of ToPI1s by Kruskal–Wallis non-parametric test (KW = 5.463, p = 0.3621) (Figure S4B). However, since we observed a slightly higher maximum current reduction in hKv1.1 channels, we chose this subtype to test ToPI1s and ToPI1s-K21A at 20 μM (Figure S4C). While the addition of 5 μM ToPI1s caused a current reduction by 5.1% (I/Imax = 0.9486 ± 0.006), the addition of 20 μM reduced it by 18.3% (I/Imax = 0.8173 ± 0.022) (Figure S4D). In turn, the mutant analogue ToPI1s-K21A reduced the hKv1.1 current by 3.9% (I/Imax = 0.9606 ± 0.014), which is 4.7 times lower than ToPI1s at the same concentration, suggesting that Lys21 may be relevant in the inhibitor interaction with the hKv1.1 channel. However, there was no significant statistical difference between groups (KW = 5.982, p = 0.0502) (Figure S4D). All effects were reversible after washing the cell with external solution.

    Circular Dichroism (CD) Spectroscopy

    Native ToPI1 and the synthetic peptides ToPI1s and ToPI1s-K21A were analyzed by CD spectroscopy, with spectra recorded from 260 to 190 nm at 25 °C. All purified peptides dissolved well in water at 0.04 mg/mL. As expected, ToPI1s and ToPI1 had superimposable CD spectra (Figure S5A), confirming the success of synthesis and peptide folding. The mutation Lys21 to Ala21 does not seem to affect the secondary structure of ToPI1s-K21A since its CD spectra was also similar to native ToPI1. Little variation in ToPI1s CD spectra was observed with increasing temperature, from 25 to 95 °C. At more extreme pH values, such as 3.0 and 9.0, this variation was slightly higher than at pH 5.0 and 7.0 (Figure S5B–F). The thermal stability of ToPI1s may also be observed by the linearity of the thermal denaturation curve, without reaching molar ellipticity values close to zero. The results show that, regardless of pH, in the range of 3.0–9.0, the peptide did not denature up to 95 °C. In addition, there was no evidence of protein precipitation at elevated temperatures.

    X-ray Crystallography

    It was observed that linear ToPI1s undergoes cleavage and cyclization after its interaction with trypsin. Cyclization was obtained via the formation of the ToPI1s:trypsin complex, which was verified both by MALDI-TOF MS (Figure S6) and crystallographic analyses (Figure 3). It is worth mentioning that the peptide cyclization was only observed in the presence of trypsin during this study. The crystallographic structure of the complex between ToPI1s and trypsin was solved to high resolution, 1.29 Å, by molecular replacement. Data collection, processing, and refinement statistics are listed in Table 3. The structure was solved in a P212121 space group with just one molecule of ToPI1s and one of trypsin in the asymmetric unit. As expected from sequence similarity results, the structure of ToPI1s is similar to the structure of scorpion toxins that act on K+ channels, presenting the cysteine-stabilized α/β (CS-α/β) motif.(23) The structure of the ToPI1s:trypsin complex is deposited in the RCSB Protein Data Bank under accession code 6MRQ.

    Confirmation that ToPI1s is bound to bovine trypsin forming a complex (Figure 3A) can be checked by the omit map electron density of ToPI1s (Figure 3B). The structure shows the P1 residue, Lys32, at the S1 site of trypsin, in an interaction similar to canonical inhibitors (S1–P1 interaction). This unambiguously identifies the Lys32 residue as P1 in detriment of the other seven lysine and two arginine residues present in the inhibitor sequence that could be in this position. This lysine is flanked by two isoleucine residues, Ile31 and Ile1. The covalent bond between Lys32 and Ile1 binds the N- and C-terminus, establishing a cyclic peptide formed upon loss of a serine residue at its C-terminus (Figure 3B and Figure 4). This bond respects the peptide bond planarity and distance, being intact while bound to the active site of trypsin as other PIs reactive loops. The formation of a cyclic peptidase inhibitor (cToPI1s) from a linear peptide (ToPI1s) seems to be unique among the PIs characterized from the venom of animals available from public databases. In addition, the structure also shows which cysteines are involved in the three disulfide bonds, which are disposed between the cysteines at positions 5 and 22, 11 and 27, 15 and 29. Several interactions, mainly hydrogen bonds, are present in the interface between trypsin and inhibitor (Figure 3C), most of them are conserved in similar complexes. In its cyclic form, acquired after interacting with trypsin, ToPI1s shares some structural similarities with some plant cyclotides, as shown by the superposition of ToPI1s and MCoTI-II (Figure 3D).

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Amongst the animal’s PIs characterized so far, ToPI1 presents unique structural features. Based on sequence similarity and crystallographic structure, ToPI1 is the first PI with CS-α/β motif described from animal venoms so far, with high trypsin inhibitory activity but no affinity for chymotrypsin. As previously reviewed,(4,24) the difference between these enzymes’ catalysis and specificity are due to the catalytic triad of residues (Ser, Asp, and His) and the residues forming the enzymes’ specificity pockets, respectively. Chymotrypsin has greater affinity for large hydrophobic residues (Phe, Trp, Tyr, Leu, and Val, although Met, Asn, and His are also reported) at the P1 position of the substrate/inhibitor since the residues Ser189, Gly216, and Gly226 create a deep hydrophobic pocket in chymotrypsin. Otherwise, in trypsin, the enzyme residues Asp189, Gly216, and Gly226 create a negatively charged S1 site, which results in a higher affinity for substrates/inhibitors containing positively charged residues (Lys/Arg) at P1, such as Lys32 at the P1 position of ToPI1.
    Although ToPI1 shares no sequence similarity with any PI described to date, after its cleavage and cyclization it shares some structural similarities with plant cyclotides, such as MCoTI-I and II (Figure 4), and other acyclic squash trypsin inhibitors. The loop formed by residues -30MIKILKR4- of ToPI1 is similar to loop 1 of MCoTI-I and II, represented by the residues -5PKILKK10- and -5PKILQR10-, respectively (Figure 3D and Figure 4). This reactive site loop forms the primary contact with trypsin and the first lysine from this loop engages the active site.(25,26) Besides this expected conserved region, the first and second disulfide bonds are quite superimposable while the third one, between residues 15 and 29, is in a very different position (Cys15 Cα and its sequentially equivalent atom in Cys21 of MCoTI-II are separated by 6.7 Å after superposition). The difference is also observed in the sequences where six residues are accommodated between Cys15 and Cys22 in ToPI1 while only one is present between Cys21 and Cys23 in MCoTI-II, forming a β-sheet. This size difference is reversed in the region between Cys27 and Cys29 of ToPI1, with only one residue, while between Cys29 and Cys4 of MCoTI-II there are eight residues (Figure 3D and Figure 4). The differences in these two cysteine-delimited regions are directly related to the difference found in the position of the third disulfide bond. Thus, while there are similarities, differences in the structure are big enough to prevent conveying that all three disulfides are equivalent.
    We propose that the rearrangement of ToPI1 is caused by peptidase-catalyzed transpeptidation through an acyl-enzyme intermediate (Figure 4C).(27) The acyl-enzyme intermediate formed upon proteolysis of the precursor peptide, with the release of Ser33 as the product, would be directly used for the nucleophilic attack of the Ile1 amino group forming a cross-link between Ile1 and Lys32 via a peptide bond. Unlike ToPI1, where cleavage/cyclization has occurred with the mature peptide in the presence of trypsin, in cyclotides the cyclization occurs simultaneously to the cleavage of the C-terminal propeptide from the cyclotide precursor protein. This happens through a transpeptidation reaction mediated by an asparaginyl endopeptidase (AEP), a cysteine protease commonly found in plants, which recognizes a conserved Asn or Asp residue at the C-terminal processing site.(28,29) The use of peptidases to form peptide bonds in synthetic polypeptides, known as chemoenzymatic approach, has been used as an alternative for conventional chemical synthesis.(30) In this sense, ToPI1 could be used as a template for the synthesis of cyclic peptides. It is noteworthy that, although this reaction involves a significant molecular rearrangement, with ToPI1 cyclization, there was no kinetically distinguishable intermediate in the reaction, which appears to occur in a single step, as observed in the linked reaction test by SPR.
    A remarkable feature of ToPI1s is its high stability in its acyclic form (Figure S5), which is probably enhanced after its head-to-tail cyclization.(5) Regarding high structural stability under harsh conditions, it is worth mentioning the similarity with plant cyclotides, which were traditionally extracted via boiling water.(31) Additionally, the reduced activities of ToPI1s and mainly ToPI1s-K21A against the tested channels suggest fewer adverse effects from their possible therapeutic application.(32) Due to these characteristics and to their specific mechanism of interaction with trypsin, these compounds could be used such as activity based-probes to image trypsin activity in live animals and tissues. For this, the C-terminal residue Ser33 should be bound to a tag, such as a fluorophore, that is detectable once the peptidase cleaves Ser33 with the tag from the polypeptide.(33)
    As molecular tools, venom-derived peptides have not only helped to elucidate several critical physiological processes but also led to the discovery of new drugs and therapeutics for the treatment of medical conditions including hypertension, diabetes and chronic pain, as well as potential candidates for the treatment of cancer, autoimmune diseases, and neurodegenerative disorders.(34,35) The discovery of a new family of peptidase inhibitors from scorpion venoms, which will be better established as other compounds similar to ToPI1 are described, is quite encouraging since it confirms that venoms are still a valuable source of new classes of compounds. Thus, we remain very optimistic regarding the prospecting of venom-derived peptides for therapeutic application. In order to better understand the specificity of ToPI1, its activity upon other peptidases is already under study.

    General Procedures

    Starting materials, unless otherwise specified, were used as high-grade commercial products. Solvents were of analytical grade. All solvents and chemicals were used as purchased without further purification. Chromatographic separations were performed by reversed-phase (RP) high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). The instrument was a Shimadzu Co. (Kyoto, Japan) LC10A system equipped with a diode array detector (SPD-M10A). Three different columns were used: an analytical C18 column (Phenomenex Synergi C18 Fusion-RP 250 × 4.6 mm, 4 μm, 80 Å), a semipreparative C18 column (Phenomenex C18 Jupiter 250 × 10 mm, 5 μm, 300 Å), and a preparative C18 column (Vydac 218TP-1022 C18, 250 × 22 mm, 10 μm, 300 Å). For HPLC, solution A was 0.12% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) in water and solution B was 0.1% TFA in acetonitrile. The purity was determined by HPLC, and the purity of all final compounds was 95% or higher. Molecular mass analyses of compounds were performed on an Autoflex Speed MALDI TOF/TOF mass spectrometer (Bruker Daltonics, Germany) controlled by FlexControl 3.4 software and, in the case of the native peptide, also by a micrOTOF-Q II MS (Bruker Daltonics, Germany) equipped with an orthogonal electrospray ionization source (ESI) operated in the positive mode.

    Venom Source

    Adult specimens of Tityus obscurus were collected in the state of Amapá, Brazil, under the Instituto Brasileiro do Meio Ambiente e dos Recursos Renováveis (IBAMA) license number 048/2007-CGFAU. They were kept alive in the Arthropod vivarium at the University of Brası́lia, and the venom was extracted monthly, treated, and quantified as previously reported.(17)

    Purification Procedures of Native ToPI1

    Aliquots of 1.0 mg of T. obscurus dried venom were solubilized in 200 μL of deionized water, centrifuged at 14,000g for 15 min at room temperature, and the supernatant was submitted to HPLC using the analytical C18 RP column. The venom molecules were separated using a linear gradient applied from 100% solution A to 60% solution B during 60 min and eluted at a flow rate of 1 mL/min, with detection at 216 nm. Fractions were individually and manually collected, vacuum dried and stored at −20 °C until use. All fractions were submitted to a trypsin inhibition assay (n = 3 per fraction), and a pool of the active fraction, eluted at 26.3 min (26.3% solution B), was further fractionated by two additional chromatographic steps with the same HPLC system and column, at a flow rate of 1 mL/min, but with the column system at 45 °C. To the first chromatographic step, the following binary gradient was applied: 10% solution B from 0 to 5 min and 10–25% solution B from 5 to 50 min. Then, all fractions were analyzed by MALDI-TOF/TOF MS and an aliquot (20%) of them was tested against trypsin. The active fraction was submitted to another purification step, with the following gradient: 5–12% solution B from 0 to 7 min and 12–22% solution B from 7 to 57 min. The purified peptide, correspondent to the main chromatographic fraction, was also active against trypsin after another trypsin chromogenic assay, where 20% of the fully purified peptide was used. Thus, it was named ToPI1 (Tityus obscurus peptidase inhibitor 1). Fractions were individually and manually collected, vacuum dried, quantified, vacuum dried again, and stored at −20 °C until use.     Mass Spectrometry and Purity Analysis

    Molecular mass analyses of ToPI1 peptide and the synthetic peptides were performed on an Autoflex Speed MALDI TOF/TOF MS. Compounds were reconstituted at 50–300 μL of deionized water and mixed to an α-cyano-4-hydroxycinnamic acid matrix solution (1:3, v:v), spotted in triplicate onto a MALDI target plate and dried at room temperature for 15 min. The spectra were acquired at positive reflector mode. Calibration of the system was performed using the Peptide Calibration Standard for Mass Spectrometry calibration mixture (up to 4000 Da mass range, Bruker Daltonics). Spectra were processed with FlexAnalysis 3.4. In addition to MALDI-TOF/TOF MS, native ToPI1 was also analyzed in a micrOTOF-Q II MS (Bruker Daltonics). The sample was diluted in variable concentrations of 1% formic acid in a water/acetonitrile mixture (1:1, v:v) and applied to the mass spectrometer source by direct infusion.

    Amino Acid Sequence Determination of ToPI1

    The amino acid sequence of ToPI1 native peptide was partially determined by MALDI in-source decay (ISD) using a 1,5-diaminonaphthalene matrix solution on an Autoflex Speed MALDI TOF/TOF MS.(39) Calibration was performed with bovine serum albumin (BSA; FRESENIUS KABI, Brazil). The sequencing was guided by ToPI1 putative sequence obtained from the cDNA library of T. obscurus venom gland, performed as previously described.(17) Similarity searches were performed using BLASTp and BLASTn (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/blast) with an e-value cutoff set to <10–5 to identify putative functions and structures. ClustalO (http://www.ebi.ac.uk/Tools/msa/clustalo/) was used for multiple sequence alignments and calculation of the identity percentage between paired sequences. The theoretical molecular mass of mature ToPI1 peptide was calculated with the Isotopic Pattern tool, available from Bruker Daltonics analysis platform. The nucleotide sequence encoding ToPI1 is available at the European Nucleotide Archive (ENA) under the accession code LR594010.

    Chemical Synthesis of ToPI1s and ToPI1s-K21A

    ToPI1 was chemically synthesized by a solid-phase method using the Fmoc/t-butyl strategy(40) on a Prelude automated peptide synthesizer (Protein Technologies, Inc., USA). A Rink amide MBHA resin (100–200 mesh) (Novabiochem) was used to provide the amino group at the C-terminus of synthetic ToPI1 (named ToPI1s), identical to the native peptide. Chemical synthesis was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions, with a 100 μmol scale and a 4-fold molar excess of Fmoc amino acid derivatives. Peptide was cleaved from the resin on the Tribute UV-IR automated peptide synthesizer (Protein Technologies, Inc., USA) with simultaneous removal of side-chain protecting groups by treatment with 10 mL of cleavage cocktail (81.5% trifluoroacetic acid, 5% water, 5% phenol, 5% thioanisole, 2.5% ethanedithiol, and 1% triisopropylsilane) for 2 h under stirring at room temperature. Then, the cleaved polypeptide was transferred to a 50 mL tube and washed and precipitated with 10 mL aliquots of ice-cold diisopropyl ether followed by centrifugation at 4000g for 20 min. The pellet containing the synthetic polypeptide was resuspended in 15–25 mL of water followed by 15–25 mL of a water:acetonitrile solution (2:1; v/v). Then, it was transferred to a 250 mL round-bottom glass flask, frozen in liquid nitrogen, and finally lyophilized. After lyophilization, the material was quantified by dry weight and kept at −20 °C. The peptide was synthesized in its reduced form and later oxidized to form the three disulfide bonds. To induce the disulfide formation, the crude reduced peptide was dissolved in deionized water (5 mg/mL) and a folding buffer (25:1; v/v) in reducing conditions (20 mM Na2HPO4; 0.1 mM NaCl; 5 mM reduced glutathione; 0.5 mM oxidized glutathione) was added dropwise under constant stirring.(41) Then, the pH was adjusted to 7.9 with NaOH and the solution remained at room temperature for 6 h when the pH was adjusted to 3.0 with formic acid. An aliquot of 1.0 μL of the folded peptide was analyzed in an Autoflex Speed MALDI TOF/TOF mass spectrometer, as previously described, to verify if the process occurred according to expected. The oxidized sample was lyophilized, quantified by dry weight, and maintained at −20 °C until peptide purification.
    The analog peptide ToPI1s-K21A was also synthesized, and its amino acid sequence was suggested from the comparison of ToPI1 with potassium channel blocker scorpion toxins. The steps of chemical synthesis, cleavage, and oxidation of ToPI1s-K21A were identical to the steps described above for ToPI1s, replacing the Lys residue at position 21 for Ala residue.

    Purification Procedures of ToPI1s

    The synthetic folded peptide ToPI1s was purified by three successive steps of RP-HPLC fractionation using C18 columns and the previously described solutions. In the first step, aliquots of approximately 25 mg of the synthetic and oxidized sample in deionized water were filtered (Millex-LCR hydrophilic PFTE membrane filter, 0.45 μm pore size, Millipore) and chromatographed on the preparative C18 column with detection at 216 and 280 nm. The following binary gradient was used, at 10 mL/min flow rate: 5% B from 0 to 5 min; 5–50% B from 5 to 30 min; 50–95% B from 30 to 35 min; 95% B from 35 to 45 min. Then, the main fractions were analyzed by MALDI-TOF MS, as described above, and the fraction containing the peptide with 3806.9 Da (corresponding to the folded peptide ToPI1s) was subsequently chromatographed on the semipreparative C18 column at 45 °C, at 1.5 mL/min flow rate, as follows: 5–16% B from 0 to 10 min; 16–23% B from 10 to 37 min; 23–95% B from 37 to 39 min; 95% B from 39 to 44 min. After that, the major fraction was analyzed by MALDI-TOF MS, and aliquots of about 1.0 mg of folded ToPI1s were purified using the analytical C18 RP column maintained at 45 °C, at 1.0 mL/min flow rate, as follows: 7–16% B from 0 to 5 min; 16–22% B from 5 to 33 min; 22–95% B from 33 to 35 min; 95% B from 35 to 40 min. After purification, the main peptide had its molecular mass verified by MALDI-TOF MS and was subsequently quantified (in the same way as described for the native peptide), lyophilized, and stored at −20 °C. The identity between synthetic and native peptides was verified by percentage of acetonitrile in RP-HPLC elution, mass spectrometry, circular dichroism, and trypsin inhibition.
    A small amount of synthetic ToPI1s in its reduced form was also purified for some comparison assays. Aliquots of approximately 1.5 mg of the material obtained directly from the synthesis in deionized water were filtered (Millex-LCR hydrophilic PFTE membrane filter, 0.45 μm pore size, Millipore) and chromatographed using the C18 analytical column at 45 °C and 1.0 mL/min flow rate, with detection at 216 nm. To the first chromatographic step, the following binary gradient was used: 5–16% B from 0 to 5 min; 16–22% B from 5 to 45 min; 22–95% B from 45 to 50 min; 95% B from 50 to 55 min. After that, the main fractions were analyzed by MALDI-TOF MS, and aliquots of about 1.0 mg of the fraction containing the peptide with 3812.9 Da (corresponding to the reduced ToPI1s) were purified using the same column and system, with the following gradient: 7–17% B from 0 to 5 min; 17–19% B from 5 to 15 min; 19–22% from 15 to 35 min; 22–95% B from 35 to 40 min; 95% B from 40 to 45 min. After purification, the molecular mass of the peptide was checked and the peptide was quantified, lyophilized, and stored at −20 °C until use.

    Purification Procedures of ToPI1s-K21A

    The synthetic peptide ToPI1s-K21A was purified similarly to ToPI1s, being the first purification step, on a preparative C18 column, equal to the previously performed. Then, the main fractions were analyzed by MALDI-TOF MS, as described, and the fraction containing the peptide with 3850.35 Da (corresponding to the folded peptide ToPI1s-K21A) was subsequently chromatographed on the analytical C18 column, maintained at 45 °C, at 1.0 mL/min flow rate. We used the same method described for ToPI1s (7–16% B from 0 to 5 min; 16–22% B from 5 to 33 min; 22–95% B from 33 to 35 min; 95% B from 35 to 40 min) but with two identical chromatographic steps. After purification, the peptide molecular mass was checked and the compound was quantified, lyophilized, and stored at −20 °C until use.

    Chromogenic Assays for Serine Peptidase Inhibition

    While native ToPI1 was only subjected to the trypsin inhibition assay at a single non-quantified concentration (20% of the chromatographic fraction), due to its low availability in the venom, the synthetic peptide ToPI1s was tested against both trypsin and chymotrypsin by means of chromogenic assays. The synthetic peptide ToPI1s-K21A as well as ToPI1s in its reduced form were also tested on trypsin inhibition chromogenic assays. As control for trypsin inhibition, BPTI/aprotinin (Sigma-Aldrich) was used at the same concentration of tested compounds. As control for chymotrypsin inhibition, phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF) (Roche Applied Science, USA) was used at 1.0 and 2.0 mM final concentrations (Cf).
    The peptidase inhibition was obtained by measuring the hydrolysis of synthetic chromogenic substrates in the presence of serine peptidases: trypsin (bovine pancreatic trypsin; EC 3.4.21.4) and chymotrypsin (bovine pancreatic α-chymotrypsin; EC 3.4.21.1), and their respective chromogenic substrates Nα-benzoyl-dl-arginine 4-nitroanilide hydrochloride (BAPNA) and N-glutaryl-l-phenylalanine p-nitroanilide (GAPNA) (Sigma-Aldrich, USA).(42) Protein concentrations were determined by spectrophotometry as previously described.(43) Trypsin and chymotrypsin stock solutions were initially prepared in a 1.0 mM HCl solution (initial concentration of 2.8 μM). Their respective substrates, BAPNA and GAPNA, were solubilized in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) at 50 mg/mL, being subsequently dissolved in trypsin assay buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, 20 mM CaCl2, pH 8.2) or chymotrypsin assay buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, 20 mM CaCl2, pH 7.6), respectively. All tested compounds were dissolved in the respective assay buffer.
    The experiment was performed on flat bottom 96-well polystyrene plates in a total volume of 280 μL per well. Plate assembly (serial dilutions of the peptide and pipetting of buffer, enzyme, and substrate) was done by using the Microlab STARlet robotic platform (Hamilton Company, USA).
    Forty microliters of 2.8 μM enzyme (Cf = 400 nM) were incubated with 40 μL of peptide at different concentrations (Cf = 1–800 nM for trypsin or 1–3500 nM for chymotrypsin) at 25 °C for 20 min. Each peptide concentration was applied in triplicate. Then, 200 μL of BAPNA (Cf = 0.4 mM) or GAPNA (Cf = 1.0 mM) substrate was added to the previous solution, and the product release (p-nitroaniline) was monitored at the FlexStation 3 Benchtop Multi-Mode Microplate Reader (Molecular Devices, USA) spectrophotometer at λ = 410 nm for 10–30 min at 25 ° C, with records at every 30 s.
    The residual enzyme activity at different peptide concentrations was calculated using the absorbance (A410) given at 10 min for trypsin or 30 min for chymotrypsin. The positive control of the reaction (100% enzyme activity or 0% inhibition) was performed in the presence of enzyme and the absence of inhibitor (40 μL of enzyme, 40 μL of buffer, and 200 μL of substrate). The negative control for baseline subtraction was performed in the absence of enzyme (80 μL of buffer and 200 μL of substrate). The experiments were performed on three independent replicates for trypsin and two independent replicates for chymotrypsin, and the values are presented as the mean ± S.E.M. (n ≥ 3).
    By using the software GraphPad Prism version 6.0, the trypsin inhibition profiles were fit by the Morrison’s quadratic equation,(19,44) considering that Km equals to 0.8 mM. The Km value was experimentally obtained by keeping trypsin concentration at 400 nM while increasing BAPNA concentration (Cf = 0.025–2.4 mM); data was fit by the Michaelis–Menten equation by nonlinear regression (data not shown) and was in accordance to previously calculated values.(42,45) The peptide concentration that inhibits 50% of the enzymatic activity, IC50, was fit by the four-parameter logistic model (variable slope). Then, the extra sum-of-squares F test was performed to statistically compare the IC50 values obtained with different peptides.
    In order to check the molecular mass of ToPI1s after interacting with trypsin, an aliquot of the solution from the chromogenic assay, with ToPI1s (Cf = 400 nM) and trypsin (Cf = 400 nM) incubated for 1 h, without BAPNA addition, was analyzed by MALDI-TOF/TOF MS, as previously detailed. As control, an aliquot of ToPI1s and trypsin assay buffer was also analyzed.

    Surface Plasmon Resonance

    The kinetics and thermodynamics of the interaction between ToPI1s and the surface immobilized trypsin at physiological pH were evaluated by surface plasmon resonance (SPR) in a BIAcore 3000 biosensor (GE Healthcare, UK), similar to the methodology previously described.(22) HBS-EP buffer, CM5 sensor chips, amine coupling kits, and immobilization and regeneration buffers were purchased from GE Healthcare.

    Trypsin Immobilization

    CM5 sensor chips were docked into the instrument and hydrated for about 24 h before trypsin immobilization. Sequencing grade modified trypsin from Promega (Madison, WI, USA) was immobilized by standard amine coupling chemistry (probably in its free N-terminus) using HBS-EP (pH 7.4) as running buffer, flowed at a rate of 5 μL/min. The enzyme was resuspended in 10 mM sodium acetate buffer (pH 5.5) at a concentration of 5 μg/mL and injected in the flow cells previously activated by injecting a 35 μL mixture of 50 mM NHS:200 mM EDC, until immobilization levels of approximately 2000 resonance units (RU) were achieved. Then, the remaining surface-activated groups of experimental and control cells were deactivated with 35 μL of 1.0 M ethanolamine (pH 8.5). All experiments were conducted using two CM5 sensor chips.

    Mass Transport and Linked Reaction Test

    In order to identify any possible mass transfer limitations, the ToPI1s:trypsin interaction kinetics was evaluated at different flow rates (5, 15, and 75 μL/min). In addition, to investigate if there is a detectable reaction intermediate in the ToPI1s:trypsin interaction, the dissociation rate was monitored while varying the analyte injection time (1, 3, and 20 min). In both control experiments, a solution of 500 nM ToPI1s (from a stock solution in water) diluted in HPS-EP buffer (pH 7.4) was used.

    ToPI1s Interaction with Trypsin

    SPR analyses were performed at 40 μL/min flow rate over surface immobilized trypsin. In all experiments, a blank surface consisting of an activated and deactivated flow cell was used as a reference for data subtraction. ToPI1s (from a stock solution in water) was diluted in HPS-EP buffer (pH 7.4) in twofold dilution series, from 25 to 0.78 nM. At each evaluated temperature (15, 20, 25, 30, and 35 °C), each peptide concentration and double blanks (reference buffer) were randomly injected in triplicate. The entire experiment was replicated at the different temperatures to ensure reproducibility. After each temperature change of the system, and before a new concentration series, the system was normalized with 70% glycerol. Data corresponding to the association phase of interaction were acquired for 4 min, and the dissociation phase was monitored for 25 min due to the slow dissociation kinetics. Before the next cycle started, the surfaces were regenerated with one 30 s injection of 10 mM glycine-HCl buffer (pH 3.0).

    Data Analysis for SPR

    The response from an average of the blank injections was subtracted from all experimental sensorgrams to remove possible systematic artifacts between experimental and reference flow cells.(46) Then, the corrected response data of the sensorgrams were fit to a simple bimolecular model (A + B ↔ AB) using BIAevaluation 3.1. Both association (ka) and dissociation (kd) rate constants were extrapolated from the data in the sensorgrams of each concentration series by nonlinear least-squares global data fitting. The equilibrium dissociation constant KD was determined by the ratio kd/ka.

    Thermodynamic and Transition State Analyses

    To obtain the thermodynamic parameters, equilibrium constants were calculated for the ToPI1s:trypsin interaction at each evaluated temperature and data was fit to the linear form of the Gibbs–Helmholtz equation as previously described(22) using OriginPro 8.0 (Origin Lab, MA, USA). Transition state analysis was carried out using the Eyring equation, which gives the specific reaction rate for a chemical reaction in terms of the enthalpy and entropy of activation (ΔH†a and ΔS†a, respectively) and the temperature.(46) This equation assumes an active state in equilibrium with reactants (A + B ↔ AB* ↔ AB). It was also calculated considering an average of the kinetic and equilibrium constants at 25 °C. In the current context, this should be considered as a semi-empirical model.

    Electrophysiological Tests

    Cell Culture

    Cell lines were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and maintained at 37 °C in a 5% CO2 atmosphere. For transient expression of hKv1.1, hKv1.4, EAG1, hERG1, hERG2, and hERG3, Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells were cotransfected with plasmids of interest (∼2.0 μg of DNA) along with plasmid for green fluorescent protein (GFP) (∼0.2 μg of DNA) by using Lipofectamine 3000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Cells were cultured under standard conditions. Currents were measured 1 to 3 days after transfection.

    Solutions

    For recording hKv1.1, hKv1.4, and EAG1 currents, the standard extracellular solution (bath) contained (mM): 130 NaCl, 5 KCl, 2 CaCl2, 2 MgCl2, 10 HEPES, and 5 d-glucose (pH 7.29). The hERG1, hERG2, and hERG3 currents were recorded in a high K+ extracellular solution, which contained (in mM): 95 NaCl, 40 KCl, 2 CaCl2, 2 MgCl2, 10 HEPES, and 5 d-glucose (pH 7.29). This experimental condition provided the best signal-to-noise relation for these channels. For all recordings, the internal solution pipette contained (in mM): 130 K-aspartate, 10 NaCl, 2 MgCl2, 10 EGTA, and 10 HEPES (pH 7.29). Tested peptides were diluted in the extracellular solution from a concentrated stock in deionized water to the final concentrations of 5.0 or 20.0 μM.

    Patch-Clamp Recordings and Data Analysis

    Electrophysiological experiments were performed with patch-clamp technique in a whole-cell configuration using MultiClamp 700B amplifiers and Digidata 1440A digitizer. For data analysis, the pClamp10 software package (Molecular Devices, USA) was used. For recording of hKv1.1 and hKv1.4 currents, the cells were held at −90 mV holding potential, hyperpolarized to −100 mV for 500 ms, and depolarized to +40 mV for 500 ms followed by a repolarizing step at −50 mV (500 ms). The time between pulses was 10 s. For recording of hERG tail currents, the cells were held at −80 mV holding potential, depolarized at +60 mV for 500 ms to activate the current, and the membrane potential was stepped back at −120 mV to record the inward tail current, pulses were delivered every 5 s. The EAG1 currents were evoked by voltage steps to +60 mV for 1.0 s from a holding potential of −80 mV. Prior to performing experiments with ToPI1s on each K+ channel subtype, specific blockers were tested for proper current functioning (data not shown).
    Glass micropipettes were pulled from borosilicate capillaries, and after filling with internal solution, pipette resistance was about 2.0–3.0 MΩ in the bath solution. When necessary, 80–90% of cell capacitance and series resistance errors were compensated for prior to each voltage clamp protocol to decrease the voltage errors to less than 5% of the protocol pulse. The effect of peptides in a given concentration is displayed as remaining current fraction (RF = I/I0, where I is the current amplitude measured in the presence of the peptide upon reaching block equilibrium and I0 is the current amplitude measured in the peptide-free control bath solution). ToPI1s was tested on hKv1.1 (n = 3), hKv1.4 (n = 5), EAG1 (n = 3), hERG1 (n = 3), hERG2 (n = 2), and hERG3 (n = 3) subtypes at 5.0 μM and on hKv1.1 (n = 3) also at 20.0 μM. The analogue ToPI1s-K21A was tested on hKv1.1 (n = 3) at 20.0 μM concentration, and its current effect was compared to the results elicited by ToPI1s. Data points of the concentration–response curves are averages of independent measurements where the error bars represent the S.E.M. Data were analyzed by Kruskal–Wallis non-parametric test followed by Dunn’s post-test (α = 0.05). All statistical analyses were performed with the GraphPad Prism version 6.0 software.

    Circular Dichroism (CD) Spectroscopy

    In order to compare the secondary structure of native ToPI1, ToPI1s, and ToPI1s-K21A, CD measurements were carried out on a JASCO J-815 spectropolarimeter (JASCO International Co. Ltd., Tokyo, Japan), equipped with a Peltier temperature controller (JASCO PTC-4238715, Japan). Far-UV spectra were recorded at 25 °C between 190 and 260 nm at a scan speed of 50 nm/min and a resolution of 0.1 nm, using 0.1 cm path length quartz cells. The peptides were analyzed at a concentration of 0.04 mg/mL in deionized water. Three consecutive scans were accumulated for each experiment and the average spectrum was stored and baseline corrected. Data was collected from a single experiment.
    Thermal denaturation assays were performed with ToPI1s (0.05 mg/mL) in 2 mM of the buffers: Na-acetate pH 3.0, Na-acetate pH 5.0, Tris-HCl pH 7.0, and Tris-HCl pH 9.0. Temperature was increased from 25 to 95 °C, allowing the temperature to equilibrate before recording each spectrum. To avoid sample evaporation, 200 μL of mineral oil was added to the cuvette. Spectra scans were recorded at increments of 0.1 °C. Thermal denaturation was monitored at 208 nm, and at each 10 °C, three consecutive scans were recorded between 190 and 260 nm, with a resolution of 0.1 nm. Data was collected from a single experiment.
    Ellipticity values ([Θ]obs) were baseline corrected by subtracting each water or buffer spectrum, and they were converted to the mean residue ellipticity ([Θ]MRW) in deg·cm2·dmol–1, as previously described,(47) considering the mean molecular mass per residue of 115 Da. The software used to record and process the data were Spectra Manager (JASCO) and OriginPro 8.0, respectively. The obtained spectra were analyzed for the intensity and position of dichroic bands.

    X-ray Crystallography

    The co-crystallization of ToPI1s with bovine trypsin was undertaken by using a 2:1 molar ratio mixture of ToPI1s and 60 mg/mL bovine trypsin, 1 mM HCl, and 20 mM CaCl2 pH 7.6. The search for proper conditions was carried out using hanging drop vapor diffusion and Crystal Screen and Crystal Screen 2 (Hampton Research) in 96-well plates with the Mosquito robot (TTP LabTech). Optimization of the initial conditions leads to a final crystallization solution of 0.1 M HEPES sodium pH 7.5, 1.5 M lithium sulfate monohydrate in a vapor diffusion sitting drop procedure. Diffraction data collection took place at the MX-2 beamline of the Laboratório Nacional de Luz Sı́ncrotron (LNLS, Campinas, Brazil) at 100 K temperature with a wavelength of 1.20 Å. Data processing was performed with the XDS program.(48) The PHASER program(49) was used for structure determination by molecular replacement with the bovine trypsin (PDB 4I8H) as the search model. The initial phases were good enough to build the peptide using COOT throughout the modeling process.(50) The PHENIX program was used for refinement,(51) showing 98.01% of Ramachandran favored, 1.99% of Ramachandran allowed, and 0% of Ramachandran outliers.
