    Abstract

    Fifteen novel derivatives of glycyrrhetinic acid (GA) were synthesized and evaluated for anti-inflammatory activities. It was found that the introduction of 1-en-3-one and 9(11),12-diene and 2,20-dinitrile functionalities into the scaffold of GA led to the discovery of potent compound 19 for inhibition of LPS-induced NO production. Furthermore, 19 effectively inhibited the protein and mRNA expression of inducible NO synthase (iNOS) and the mRNA expression of TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β in LPS-stimulated RAW 264.7 macrophages. Mechanistically, 19 exerted inhibitory effects on the activation of the three main MAPKs and phosphorylation and degradation of IκB-α, as well as the ratio of nuclear/cytosolic content of p65. Importantly, 19 significantly decreased the mortality rate in the mouse model of LPS-induced sepsis shock. It is noteworthy that inhibitory effect of 19 on NO production was not blocked by the glucocorticoid receptor antagonist mifepristone, indicating that it does not act through the glucocorticoid receptor.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Inflammatory response plays an essential role in combating infection and tissue injury; however, its duration and magnitude must be tightly controlled because chronic and unchecked inflammation is a risk factor for many chronic diseases such as cardiovascular diseases, rheumatoid arthritis, bronchitis, diabetes, neurodegenerative disorders, and cancer.(1, 2)
    Activated macrophages play crucial roles in the initiation and maintenance of inflammation. Following activation by inflammatory stimuli such as lipopolysaccharide (LPS), macrophages secrete a number of potent bioactive inflammatory mediators, including nitric oxide (NO), prostaglandins (PGs), leukotrienes (LTs), and cytokines such as interleukin-1β (IL-1β), IL-6, and tumor necrosis factor α (TNF-α), distributing to the activation of mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) and nuclear factor κB (NF-κB).(3, 4)
    Of note, as an important inflammatory mediator, the paradoxical role of NO in the pathogenesis of inflammation generally depends upon its concentration.(5) Appropriate levels of NO produced by inducible NO synthase (iNOS) in response to inflammatory stimuli such as interferon-γ (IFN-γ), IL-1β, and LPS assist in mounting an effective defense against pathogens.(5) However, sustained overproduction of NO by iNOS is believed to be detrimental to the host and is associated with the pathogenesis of disorders mentioned above.(6) Therefore, pharmacological interference with the NO production cascade is appreciated as a promising strategy of many therapeutic intervention in inflammatory diseases.(7)
    Triterpenoids, one of the largest families of natural products ubiquitously distributed throughout the plant kingdom, are of interest both in their natural forms and as templates for synthetic modification.(8, 9) Glycyrrhetinic acid (GA, Scheme 1), the aglycone of glycyrrhizin, is a well-known naturally occurring oleanane-type triterpenoid. It is the major active ingredient of the roots and rhizomes of licorice (Glycyrrhiza species) with diverse pharmacological properties such as anti-inflammation, antitumor, and antihepatotoxic activity.(10) Recent studies have shown that GA inhibits the LPS-induced production of inflammatory mediators such as NO, TNF-α, and IL-1β, suppressing the expression of iNOS, which are involved in blocking activation of NF-κB and phosphoinositide-3-kinase (PI3K).(11, 12) Over the past decade, GA has also been serving as an effective structural template for the search of more potent anticancer agents.(13, 14) However, only few GA derivatives have been designed and synthesized to enhance the anti-inflammatory activities.(15) In continuing our effort to develop triterpene derivatives as potential therapeutic agents,(16-20) we herein employed GA as a lead compound for further investigation aiming to improve its anti-inflammatory activity.
    Natural products with an α,β-unsaturated carbonyl substructure are ubiquitous in nature, exhibiting numerous biological effects such as anti-inflammatory, antibiotic, or anticancer activity.(21-23) It was found that the introduction of a 1-en-3-one functionality in several synthetic triterpenoids greatly improved their anti-inflammatory activities.(24) Additionally, the 11-keto-Δ12(13) system of ring C is a significant factor for GA-induced pseudoaldosteronism.(25, 26) On the basis of these observations, we proceeded with modification of the GA structure using following strategies: (a) building up a 1-en-3-one moiety with various substituents at C-2 in ring A of GA; (b) converting the 11-oxo-12-ene of GA in ring C to 9(11),12-diene moiety; (c) changing C-20 carboxyl group to an ester, amide, or nitrile functionality. Utilizing the LPS-induced RAW 264.7 macrophage cells, a well-established model of macrophage activation at sites of inflammation,(27) we found that among 15 GA derivatives, 3-oxo-29-noroleana-1,9(11),12-trien-2,20-dicarbonitrile (19) exhibited the most potent activity against LPS-induced NO release. Noteworthy, its activity is approximately 1000-fold more potent than the lead compound GA and is also stronger than dexamethasone, although it does not act through the glucocorticoid receptor. In addition, 19 effectively suppressed expression of iNOS, TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β in RAW 264.7 macrophages, partially resulting from the blockage of the NF-κB and MAPK pathways. Furthermore, 19 exhibited therapeutic effects on the mouse model of LPS-induced sepsis shock. These results suggest that 19 may be a promising candidate of anti-inflammatory agent.

    Inhibition of NO Production in LPS-Stimulated Macrophages

    NO generation induced by bacterial LPS or cytokines plays an important role in inflammatory conditions. Hence, 15 target compounds (4–7, 9–19) and two intermediates (3 and 8), together with dexamethasone as a positive control, were evaluated for inhibition against NO production in LPS-stimulated RAW 264.7 macrophages (Table 1). To exclude the possibility that the inhibitions of inflammatory response were due to cytotoxicity caused by these compounds’ treatment, MTT assays were performed in RAW 264.7 macrophages treated with individual compounds at 80 μM with or without LPS for 24 h, and no significant inhibitory effect was observed (data not shown).

    The preliminary screening showed that most of the new derivatives of GA exhibited stronger inhibitory activities against the LPS-induced NO production in RAW 264.7 macrophages relative to the parent compound GA. Seven compounds displayed 50–99% inhibition rate at a dose of 50 μM. Among them, the IC50 values of 13 and 15 (6.22 and 3.08 μM, respectively) were the same order of magnitude as that of positive control dexamethasone (IC50 = 1.43 μM). Particularly, compound 19 (IC50 = 0.20 μM) showed higher activity relative to parent compound GA (IC50 = 187.88 μM) and positive control dexamethasone. In this regard, 19 was selected as the candidate compound for further studies of the mechanism of action at the transcriptional level and the in vivo anti-inflammatory activity.

    19 Inhibits the Induction of iNOS at the Transcriptional Level

    Since the overproduction of NO is the result of iNOS high expression,(6) we studied the ability of 19 and GA to modulate the LPS-induced iNOS expression. RAW 264.7 cells were incubated with indicated concentrations of 19 or GA for 2 h and then treated with LPS (100 ng/mL) for 24 h. Cell lysates were electrophoresed, and the expression levels of iNOS were detected with specific antibodies. As shown in Figure 1, in the control group (no 19 or GA pretreatment) LPS increased significantly the expression of iNOS (p < 0.001). However, this expression was markedly attenuated in RAW 264.7 macrophages by pretreatment with 19 in a dose-dependent manner (p < 0.001, Figure 1A). Correlating with the NO measurement, GA also exhibited significant inhibition on the expression of iNOS, but it was not as potent as 19.
    Similar patterns were observed for the effect of 19 on LPS-induced iNOS mRNA expression (p < 0.001, Figure 1B). After treatment with the indicated concentrations of 19 or GA for 2 h and thereafter treatment with LPS for 8 h, total RNA was prepared from RAW 264.7 cells and qPCR was preformed for the iNOS genes. These findings indicate that treatment with 19 significantly suppressed the LPS-stimulated induction of iNOS expression through transcriptional inhibition.

    19 Inhibits Inflammatory Cytokines Release

    Since IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α are early secreted proinflammatory cytokines and their elevated levels are observed in a variety of acute and chronic inflammatory diseases,(29) we determined the effects of 19 and GA on the mRNA expression levels of IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α in LPS-stimulated cells by qPCR. After plating and 24 h of growth, RAW 264.7 macrophages were treated with different concentrations of 19 or GA in the presence or absence of 100 ng/mL LPS for 8 h for real-time PCR. As shown in Figure 2, the mRNA levels of IL-6 and IL-1β were significantly down-regulated in the presence of 19 after stimulation with LPS (100 ng/mL), the mRNA levels of IL-6 had no significant difference with the vehicle group, while TNF-α was decreased only under relatively high dose (0.2 and 0.4 μM) treatment of 19. GA mildly inhibited the mRNA expression of IL-6 and IL-1β but exhibited no inhibitory effect on TNF-α mRNA expression.

    Anti-Inflammatory Effects of 19 are Mediated by NF-κB Inhibition

    NF-κB is one of the crucial regulators for the induction of iNOS genes and cytokines.(30) Thus, we next examined the activation of NF-κB in RAW 264.7 cells in response to LPS. The lysates were separated into cytosolic fractions for IκB-α, p-IκB-α, and p65 and nuclear fractions for NF-κB p65 analyses. The levels of these transcription factors were determined by Western blot analysis. Since the release of activated NF-κB is mediated by the phosphorylation and degradation of IκB,(30) we found that 19 or GA pretreatment for 2 h before 30 min LPS stimulation significantly alleviated LPS-induced IκB phosphorylation (Figure 3A) and the increased cytosolic IκB-α. This effect was accompanied by the reduced accumulation of the NF-κB p65 subunit in the nucleus after LPS stimulation (p < 0.001, Figure 3B). These results indicate that phosphorylation and degradation of IκB-α and the subsequent translocation of NF-κB p65 are inhibited by 19 and GA despite the differences in potency.

    19 Inhibits LPS-Induced Phosphorylation of MAPKs in RAW 264.7 Cells

    In addition to NF-κB activation, the stimulation of macrophage by LPS rapidly leads to the activation of p38, extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK), and c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK), the three well-defined MAPKs implicated in the regulation of expression of the iNOS and various cytokines.(31) Therefore, we investigated the phosphorylations of the distinct MAPK pathways to assess their potential involvement in the anti-inflammatory activities of 19 by Western blot analyses. As shown in Figure 4, 19 significantly suppressed the LPS-induced phosphorylations of p38, JNK, and ERK MAPKs, especially p38 and JNK, without affecting their nonphosphorylated forms. GA (75 μM) also suppressed the phosphorylations of these three MAPKs. These results suggested that phosphorylation of ERK, p38, and JNK is involved in the inhibitory effect of 19 and GA on LPS-induced inflammatory responses in RAW 264.7 cells.

    Pretreatment with 19 Protects Mice from Lethal-Dose LPS Challenge

    Sepsis is the systemic inflammatory response to severe microbial infection, triggered mainly by LPS; therefore, the mouse model of LPS-injection-induced sepsis shock is one of the most extensively used experimental animal model replicating sepsis shock.(32) Hence, we employed this model to assess the in vivo anti-inflammatory activity of 19. Mice were daily treated with 19 or GA or dexamethasone or vehicles from day 1 to day 5. On day 5, LPS (20 mg/kg) was intravenously injected 2 h after the last administration. Survival rates are shown in Figure 5. LPS (20 mg/kg) administration resulted in the death of all the mice in the model group and nine mice died in the GA group within 1 day, indicating that GA (50 mg/kg) provided no protection. In contrast, administration of a middle dose of 19 (50 mg/kg, M group) and dexamethasone (20 mg/kg) significantly improved the survival rate of the LPS-injected animals (p < 0.05 and p < 0.001, respectively), while low dose or high does groups (L group or H group) failed to exhibit significant improvement in mortality in the 6-day observation. Meanwhile, the body weight of each living mouse was monitored from day 1 to day 11 (Figure 5B). Before the LPS injection on day 5, the body weight progressively increased. The LPS injection induced significant body weight decrease from day 5 to day 8, but the mice treated with 19 or dexamethasone regained weight since day 9. These results indicated that 19 had much more profound anti-inflammatory effect in vivo than GA did.

    19 Does Not Act through a Glucocorticoid Receptor-Mediated Mechanism

    One of the major limitations of glucocorticoids in their usefulness for therapy of chronic disease is the side effects of aldosteronism resulting from activation of the glucocorticoid receptor.(33) To exclude the potential side effects, we used mifepristone, a glucocorticoid receptor antagonist, to determine whether the anti-inflammatory effects of 19 were mediated through the interaction with the glucocorticoid receptor. Figure 6 showed that, as expected, the inhibitory effect of dexamethasone on NO production was reversed by mifepristone (l μM), whereas the inhibitory activity of 19 on NO production was not affected by mifepristone. These data suggest that the actions of 19 on the NO production are not mediated by its interaction with the glucocorticoid receptor.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    To improve the anti-inflammatory activity of GA, 15 novel derivatives of GA were synthesized by introducing a 1-en-3-one moiety in ring A, converting the 11-oxo-12-ene in ring C to 9(11),12-diene moiety, and/or changing C-20 carboxylic acid to an ester, amide, or nitrile functionality in the present study. First, these derivatives were evaluated for their inhibitory activities against LPS-induced NO release in macrophages, since NO plays a pivotal role in many inflammatory-related physiological functions.(7) The results revealed some interesting structure–activity relationships: (1) In ring A, 1-en-3-one functionality and C-2 substituent are important for inhibitory activity. For example, the 1-en-3-one 4 (inhibition percentage is 25.05% at 50 μM) is a more potent inhibitor than alcohol 3 and ketone 8 (inhibition percentages are 15.62% and 7.36%, respectively, at 50 μM). Furthermore, introduction of a substitutent into the C-2 position of 1-en-3-one moiety could significantly enhance the activities. For instance, compound 15 (with a CN group) showed stronger inhibitory activities than peer compounds 14 (I) and 4 (H) (the inhibition percentages of 15, 14, and 4 at 50 μM are 98.44%, 60.32%, and 25.05%, respectively). Notably, the combination of 1-en-3-one functionality with a hydroxyl group at C-2 also enhances the potency. For example, the C-2 hydroxyl derivatives 9–11, especially 13, are more potent than the C-2 hydrogen compounds 4, 5, and 16. (2) In ring C, 9(11),12-diene compound 3 (inhibition percentage 15.62% at 50 μM) exhibits relatively higher activity compared to its parent compound GA (5.12%, at 50 μM), suggesting that in ring C converting 11-oxo-12-ene to 9(11),12-diene is beneficial for activity. (3) At C-20, the compounds with a free acid group exert better activity than corresponding methyl ester compounds (e.g., 4 vs 5, 9 vs 10, 14 vs 6, and 15 vs 7). As for amide compounds, they show similar activities compared to free acid compounds (e.g., 16 vs 4, 11 vs 9) except imidazolylcarbonyl compound 13, which shows stronger activity. Interestingly, the compounds with a nitrile group are generally more potent than the corresponding free acid and methyl ester compounds (e.g., 19 vs 7 and 15). (4) Notably, compound 19, which contains a 1-en-3-one functionality and a 9(11),12-diene moiety and a nitrile group at C-2 and C-20, respectively, exhibits strongest inhibitory activity against LPS-induced NO production among these derivatives and is about 1000 times stronger than the lead compound GA and superior to the positive control dexamethasone. Hence, 19 was selected for further investigations of the in vitro and in vivo anti-inflammatory activities and the modulating effect in NF-κB and MAPK signaling pathways.
    It was previously reported that LPS could up-regulate the expression of iNOS in macrophages and consequently cause overproduction of NO during inflammation.(7) Here, we found that 19 down-regulated both the protein and mRNA expression of iNOS in parallel in a concentration-dependent manner (Figure 1), suggesting that inhibitions of NO release may be attributed to the inhibition of iNOS gene transcription.
    Besides overproducing NO, LPS-stimulated macrophages also release several proinflammatory cytokines, including TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β, to modulate the pathogenesis of immune reactions and inflammation.(34) Since TNF-α,(35) IL-6,(36) and IL-1β(37) have profound effects on the regulation of inflammation, we investigated the inhibitory effects of 19 and GA on the mRNA expression of these three cytokines. We found that 19 significantly down-regulated mRNA expression of TNF-α at 0.2 and 0.4 μM, while GA exhibited no activity even at 75 μM. Furthermore, the inhibitory effects of 19 on IL-6 and IL-1β were concentration-dependent and more significant than those of GA (Figure 2). The inhibition of TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β expression by 19 further proved that 19 was capable of inhibiting the inflammatory mediators besides NO.
    Next, we investigated the effect of 19 on inflammation-related signaling pathways. Stimulation of macrophages by LPS spontaneously triggers the activations of the NF-κB and MAPKs pathways, which are well-known to be involved in the pathogenesis of inflammatory response.(38) NF-κB plays a central role in the regulation of many immune and inflammatory processes including sepsis.(39) It has been shown that adding LPS stimulation results in phosphorylation and degradation of IκB-α, as well as nuclear translocation of p65 subunits in RAW 264.7 macrophages.(4) Hence, we examined the effect of 19 or GA on LPS-mediated NF-κB activation in the RAW 264.7 cells. Western blot assay revealed that both 19 and GA inhibited LPS-induced phosphorylation and degradation of IκB-α and reduced the amount of p65 in nuclear fractions (Figure 3). These findings suggest that the inhibition of NF-κB activation by 19 and GA is possibly due to the inhibition of IκB-α phosphorylation and degradation.
    The MAPKs play a critical role in the regulation of cell growth and differentiation, as well as in the control of cellular responses to cytokines and stressors.(40) LPS activates all three MAPK pathways in RAW 264.7 macrophages, and these pathways are involved in the regulation of gene expression in LPS-activated macrophages.(41) More specifically, p38 MAPK can be predominantly activated by LPS, playing an important role in the expression of a number of proinflammatory molecules.(42) Activation of ERK1 and ERK2 by LPS is required for the correct induction of NO(43) and various cytokines including IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α.(44) Pharmacological studies with MAPK-specific inhibitors have demonstrated that the JNK pathway is required for LPS-induced NO production and iNOS expression in RAW 264.7 macrophages.(41) We found that incubation of RAW 264.7 cells with LPS led to the activation of p38, JNK, and ERK 1/2 and treatment with 19 inhibited the LPS-induced phosphorylation of these three MAPKs (Figure 4). These results suggest that p38, ERK-1/2, and JNK/SAPK are involved in the inhibitory effect of LPS-induced inflammatory responses.
    Sepsis is a dysregulated response of hosts to microbial infection. Lethal doses of LPS elicited a systemic inflammatory process of the immune system to release overwhelming amounts of proinflammatory mediators, resulting in hepatic and renal failure, myocardial dysfunction, acute respiratory distress syndrome, and disseminated intravascular coagulation that may lead to death.(45) Since 19 significantly inhibited LPS-induced inflammatory response in vitro, we studied the mortality rate in the mouse model with a lethal dose of LPS (20 mg/kg) to evaluate the in vivo anti-inflammatory activity of 19. Our data demonstrated that 19 significantly decreased LPS-induced mortality, whereas GA treatment failed to show a preventive effect (Figure 5), indicating that the in vivo anti-inflammatory activity of 19 was significantly stronger than that of GA.
    Although GA is a noteworthy drug among phytothereapy for inflammatory diseases, some side effects such as pseudoaldosteronism have been observed in patients treated with GA, which may be due to its inhibitory effect on renal 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 (11β-HSD2)(25) and its binding to both mineralocorticoid and glucocorticoid receptors.(33) It was reported that the cleavage or masking of the 11-carbonyl group in ring C of GA not only markedly reduced the inhibitory activity against 11β-HSD2(26) but also retained or even increased the anti-inflammatory activity.(15) Accordingly, we have designed and synthesized these GA derivatives by converting 11-oxo-12-ene to 9(11),12-diene. Our results demonstrated that glucocorlicoid antagonist mifepristone failed to block the inhibitory effect of 19 on NO production, suggesting that the anti-inflammatory activity is independent of the activation of glucocorticoid receptor. Evaluation for the 11β-HSD-inhibitory activity of 19 is needed to confirm that the application of 19 would not inhibit steroid metabolism.
    In conclusion, 19 inhibited LPS-induced expression of inflammatory mediators in RAW 264.7 macrophages at least partially through the blockade of NF-κB and MAPK pathways, suggesting that 19 could be a potential therapeutic agent for the treatment of inflammation-associated disorders. Further studies of 19 regarding its anti-inflammatory properties in other animal models and its effects on the upstream of MAPKs and NF-κB are under investigation and will be reported in due course.

    Unless otherwise noted, all commercially available solvents and reagents were used without further purification.     (18β,20β)-3-Oxooleana-1,9(11),12-trien-29-oic Acid (4)

    A mixture of 3 (4.5 g, 10 mmol) and IBX (11.2 g, 40 mmol) in anhydrous DMSO (100 mL) was stirred with heating at 85 °C for 4 h.     Cell Viability Assay

    Cell viability was assessed using the MTT assay. To summarize, RAW 264.7 cells were seeded into a 96-well plate at a density of 1.0 × 104 cells per well and incubated at 37 °C for 24 h. The cells were then treated with various concentrations of the compounds and glycyrrhizic acid in the presence or absence of LPS (100 ng/mL). After an additional 24 h incubation at 37 °C, 20 μL of MTT (5 mg/mL in PBS) was added to the wells, and the incubation continued for another 2 h. The resulting color was assayed at 540 nm using a microplate spectrophotometer (Tecan, Switzerland).

    Nitrite Measurement

    RAW 264.7 mouse macrophages were plated in 96-well plates at 30 000 cells/well in triplicate. On the next day cells were pretreated with vehicle or tested compounds (0–80 μM dose range) for 2 h. In the glucocorticoid receptor competitive experiment, glucocorticoid receptor antagonist mifepristone (1 μM) was added with or without the presence of dixamethasone or 19. After a 2 h treatment, the cells were treated with LPS (100 ng/mL) for 24 h. NO concentration in the medium was determined using the Griess reagent kit (Beyotime, China).

    Preparation of Cytosolic and Nuclear Extracts

    RAW 264.7 macrophages were cultured in 60 mm culture dishes (4 × 106 cells/dish) with or without LPS (100 ng/mL) and in the presence or absence of various concentrations of 19 (0.1, 0.2, 0.4 μM) and GA (75 μM). Protein extraction of cytosolic and nuclear fractions was performed using a cytosolic/nuclei isolation kit (KeyGEN, Nanjing, China), according to a multiple centrifugation method. Protein concentration was determined using an enhanced BCA protein assay (Beyotime, China).

    Western Blot

    Equal quantities of cytosolic/nuclear proteins from each treatment (80 μg of total protein/lane) were resolved on 10% SDS–PAGE and then transferred to polyvinylidene fluoride membranes (Millipore, MA, U.S.). The membranes were blocked with 5% nonfat dry milk–TBS–0.1% Tween 20 for 2 h and then incubated with the primary antibodies with gentle shaking at 4 °C for 8–12 h followed by incubation with horseradish peroxidase conjugated secondary anti-rabbit antibody (1:5,000; Bioworld, MN, U.S.) for 2 h. Immunoreactivity was detected by using the enhanced chemiluminescence detection reagents (KeyGEN, Nanjing, China) by a gel imaging system (ChemiScope 2850, Clinx Science, Shanghai, China).

    LPS-Induced Endotoxic Shock

    The mice were randomly divided into seven groups (n = 10 mice/group) and injected intraperitoneally with a low dose (12.5 mg kg–1 day–1), middle dose (50 mg kg–1 day–1), or a high dose (200 mg kg–1 day–1) of 19 or with a dose of GA (50 mg kg–1 day–1). It was previously reported that GA at a dose of 50 mg/kg exerted a protective effect on the mice with Staphylococcus aureus pneumonia,(47) and the middle dose of 19 consisted of nearly equimolar amounts of the dose of GA. As a positive control, another group received dexamethasone (20 mg kg–1 day–1). The control group and model group received vehicle, namely, 30% propylene glycol dissolved in saline, and the rest of the mice received the respective compounds daily for 5 days. During the fifth day, 2 h after the last administration, mice other than the ones of the control group were injected iv with 20 mg/kg LPS.(48) Survival of both groups was monitored for 6 days.
