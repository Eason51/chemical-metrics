    Abstract

    With the objective of developing efficient sensitizers for therapeutic applications, we synthesized a water-soluble 5,10,15,20-tetrakis(3,4-dihydroxyphenyl)chlorin (TDC) and investigated its in vitro and in vivo biological efficacy, comparing it with the commercially available sensitizers. TDC showed high water solubility (6-fold) when compared with that of Foscan and exhibited excellent triplet-excited-state (84%) and singlet-oxygen (80%) yields. In vitro photobiological investigations in human-ovarian-cancer cell lines SKOV-3 showed high photocytotoxicity, negligible dark toxicity, rapid cellular uptake, and specific localization of TDC in neoplastic cells as assessed by flow-cytometric cell-cycle and propidium iodide staining analysis. The photodynamic effects of TDC include confirmed reactive-oxygen-species-induced mitochondrial damage leading to necrosis in SKOV-3 cell lines. The in vivo photodynamic activity in nude-mouse models demonstrated abrogation of tumor growth without any detectable pathology in the skin, liver, spleen, or kidney, thereby demonstrating TDC application as an efficient and safe photosensitizer.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Photodynamic therapy (PDT) is a new approach for the safe and effective eradication of different types of cancerous tissues without affecting the normal cells. This emerging technique is based on the light activation of the sensitizer and the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in the presence of molecular oxygen.(1â5) PDT induces oxidative damage and can trigger either necrosis- or apoptosis-mediated cell death, depending on the intensity of the insult.(6â9) The reactive oxygen species can also prevent tumor growth indirectly through vascular shutdown and activation of an immune response.(4,5,10â17) In this modality, the sensitizer plays a key role in determining the efficacy of the photodynamic therapy. A series of sensitizers such as porphyrins, porphycenes, phthalocyanines, chlorins, bacteriochlorins, squaraines, rose bengal, methylene blue, and their derivatives, which exhibit good singlet-oxygen-generation efficiencies, have been evaluated for their photodynamic treatments.(18â24) Among these, the first-generation sensitizer, hematoporphyrin derivative (HpD), which is commercially known as Photofrin, is in clinical use for the treatment of several types of cancers.(25) However, HpD has disadvantages: it is a mixture of nine components, has relatively weak absorption at 630 nm, and is known to cause skin photosensitivity.(26,27)
    In the last few decades, the major challenge in the field of photodynamic therapy has been to overcome the drawbacks of HpD and to discover second-generation photosensitizers by red shifting the absorption of the Q bands together with producing high extinction coefficients in the red region. In this effort, several compounds have been investigated for anticancer PDT activity.(28) Among these, 5,10,15,20-tetra(m-hydroxyphenyl)chlorin, commercially known as Foscan, is one promising sensitizer and is a reduced form of the porphyrin.(29) The main disadvantages associated with Foscan are its poor solubility in aqueous media and the high photosensitivity it causes in patients even up to 20 days after PDT treatment.(30) The porphyrin-based photosensitizers developed so far either (a) are heterogeneous in their chemical composition and water insoluble and have lower photodynamic efficiencies or (b) are associated with extended cutaneous sensitivity.(31â33) The second-generation chlorin-derived photosensitizers are chemically pure but are minimally soluble in aqueous media.(33,34) Several attempts are being made to overcome these drawbacks by functionalizing the photosensitizers by folic acid attachment, lipid encapsulation, and other methods.(35) However, these additional methodologies compromise the targeting ability, toxicity, and shelf life. Both Photofrin and Foscan, which are approved for clinical use, have extended photosensitivity or require prolonged light exposure treatments for periods ranging for up to 1 week. Herein, we report a novel water-soluble sensitizer, 5,10,15,20-tetrakis(3,4-dihydroxyphenyl)chlorin (TDC), and the investigation of its photophysical and in vitro and in vivo photobiological aspects as compared with those of Photofrin and Foscan. Uniquely, TDC exhibited high absorption and photodynamic activity in SKOV-3 human-ovarian-cancer cells and acted as an antitumor agent in vivo in nude-mouse models, thereby demonstrating its use as an effective sensitizer in PDT applications.

TDC showed the characteristic chlorin absorption with a Soret band at 421 nm and four Q-bands at 521, 550, 596, and 650 nm.     To have a better understanding of the PDT efficacy of TDC, we have determined its singlet-oxygen-generation efficiency using a chemical-trapping method employing 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran (DPBF) as the singlet-oxygen scavenger(38) as well as by quantification of its emission intensity at 1270 nm.(39) To determine the singlet-oxygen quantum yields (Î¦1O2), the irradiation of a solution containing the chlorin system, TDC, along with DPBF was carried out using a 200 W mercury lamp as light source with a 515 nm long-pass filter at 0â60 s time intervals (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Interestingly, a comparative study showed a higher singlet-oxygen-generation efficiency for TDC in comparison with those of the commercially available sensitizers, Photofrin and Foscan (Table 1). Moreover, the hydrophilicity of TDC, due to the presence of hydroxyl groups, imparts better solubility for the compound in biological media (Table S1, Supporting Information). For example, the compound TDC exhibited ca. 6-fold enhancement (3.64 mM) in solubility in a 25% aqueous ethanolic medium, when compared with that of the commercially available sensitizer Foscan (0.6 mM). In addition to that, the increased solubility decreases aggregation in aqueous media, enhances its cellular uptake and bioavailability, which aids in faster plasma clearance and thereby contributes to the higher photodynamic efficacy of TDC, when compared with those of Photofrin and Foscan under similar conditions.(40)

    Investigation of In Vitro Photocytotoxicity

    To investigate the photobiological effects of TDC, we selected the human-ovarian-cancer cell line SKOV-3 and a Waldman PDT 1200L tunable light source, with an emission spectrum in the range of 600â720 nm. We used a dose of 200 J/cm2 at 50 mW/cm2 for the irradiation of the cells in culture and 100 J/cm2 (100 mW/cm2) for the irradiation in the animal-model experiments. To understand the cellular uptake and localization of TDC, we analyzed fluorescence- and confocal-microscopic images of TDC in the SKOV-3 cell line using the blue stain Hoechst 3342 in different conditions. It was interesting to observe that when the SKOV-3 cells were incubated with 20 Î¼M TDC for 1 h, the cells readily took up the sensitizer. Moreover, intracellularly, TDC showed high, punctate fluorescence, indicating its probable vesicular and mitochondrial association, and also diffused fluorescence along the membrane periphery, as shown in Figure 2. The dye localization to F-actin and to the mitochondria were subsequently confirmed by colocalization experiments using specific probes (Figures 6 and 7). It has been reported that even low levels of singlet oxygen produced in close proximity to the target organelle, such as a mitochondrion, are far more efficient in their phototoxic effects than large quantities generated at distant cellular locations.(41) The proximity of the localization of TDC to vital intracellular organelles could therefore yield more efficient phototoxicity.
    The cell viability, as measured by a 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay, showed clear dose-dependent phototoxicity (Figure 3A). We observed a significant loss of viability in the SKOV-3 cells at concentrations beyond 10 Î¼M and followed typical biological protocols. Under these experimental conditions, the IC50 value of TDC was calculated to be 11.16 Â± 2.08 Î¼M for the SKOV-3 cells. TDC exhibited excellent phototoxic effects with the IC50 values varying from 4 to 20 Î¼M depending on the cancer cell line used (IMR32, 3.5 Â± 0.84 Î¼M; MCF7, 4.4 Â± 1.62 Î¼M; and MDA MB 231, 6.1 Â± 1.16 Î¼M). Phase-contrast-microscopic images of TDC-treated SKOV-3 cells in the absence of light exposure displayed minimal alterations in the cell morphology. However, when exposed to light, the dye-treated cells displayed extensive changes in cell morphology, indicating cell death.
    Further, to understand the dark cytotoxicity and the cellular mechanism behind the photodynamic efficacy of TDC on SKOV-3 cells, we analyzed SKOV-3 cells by propidium iodide (PI) staining and flow-cytometric cell-cycle analysis (Figure 4).(42) In a typical distribution, the sub-G1 population represents the hypo-diploid, dead cells. The experimental controls displayed ca. 1â7% cells in the sub-G1 stage, whereas the cells treated with TDC and exposed to light showed a sub-G1 population of ca. 60% after 12 h and ca. 77% after 24 h. A gradual shift of cells to the sub-G1 population indicates the persistence of the photoinduced effects of TDC over a period of time. This gradual shift of cells to the sub-G1 phase represents the immediate and late events connected to the photodynamic damage of cellular DNA. Twenty-four hours after irradiation, we observed the shift of almost the entire population of cells toward the sub-G1 phase, indicating an excellent phototoxic effect of TDC.

    Mode of Cell Death

    To understand the mode of the phototoxic activity, flow-cytometric analysis was performed on live SKOV-3 cells (both control and TDC-treated cells) stained with propidium iodide (PI). The control cells (the untreated and dark controls) showed negligible PI fluorescence (the population of cells in the M2 window was â¼7â9%), whereas the cells treated with TDC and exposed to light displayed very intense fluorescence and showed about ca. 80% of the cell population. These observations clearly demonstrated the loss of cell membrane integrity (Figure 5) and indicated necrosis to be the main mode of the phototoxic killing effect of TDC. To have a better understanding of the generation of intracellular reactive oxygen species, we used a well-known fluorescent probe, 5-(and 6)-chloromethyl-2â²,7â²-dichloro-dihydrofluorescein diacetate acetyl ester (CM-H2DCFDA). To perform the CM-H2DCFDA assay, the SKOV-3 human-ovarian-cancer cells were treated with TDC and irradiated; this was followed by the addition of CM-H2DCFDA (10 Î¼M), and then the cells were examined via confocal microscopy. We observed the appearance of green fluorescence only in the TDC-treated, light-exposed cells, indicating the generation of ROS, whereas the untreated- and dark-control cells showed no fluorescence. A parallel flow-cytometric analysis under the same conditions also confirmed that ca. 73% of the TDC-treated, light-exposed cell population displayed ROS fluorescence, whereas the control cells exhibited negligible ROS levels (ca. < 1%, Figure S2, Supporting Information).
    In SKOV-3 cells, actin appears as circularly organized filaments located near the plasma membrane. Motivated by the live-cell propidium iodide (PI)-staining experiments, which indicated the loss of cell membrane integrity, we analyzed the actin-cytoskeletal integrity of the SKOV-3 cells before and after the photodynamic treatment using tetramethylrhodamine (TRITC)-labeled phalloidin, an F-actin-specific fluorescent probe.(43) The cells treated with TDC followed by irradiation showed a complete collapse in the F-actin filament network, whereas those of the control cells remained intact (Figure 6). These observations demonstrated the disruption of the filamentous-actin-cytoskeleton organization in cells. Loss of cytoskeletal integrity could potentially disrupt several physiological functions in cells, ultimately triggering cell death.(44)
    To understand their mitochondrial integrity, TDC-treated, light-exposed SKOV-3 cells were stained with MitoTracker Red (CMXRos, Molecular Probes), a fluorophore sensitive to the mitochondrial membrane potential (ÎÎ¨m). The mitochondria of the untreated SKOV-3 cells had a typical fibrillar appearance with bright fluorescence, denoting intact mitochondrial membranes (Figure 7A). TDC-treated, light-exposed SKOV-3 cells showed a loss of the mitochondrial network, which can be clearly evidenced by Figure 7B. Damage to mitochondrial integrity can lead to cellular events that ultimately trigger cell death, such as the cessation of adenosine 5â²-triphosphate (ATP) production and the release of toxic proteins into cytoplasmic milieu.(45,46) These results clearly demonstrate that TDC photodynamically damages the cell membrane, disrupts the actin cytoskeletal network, and results in the loss of the mitochondrial membrane potential (indicating mitochondrial damage). The phototoxic killing of SKOV-3 cells by TDC is the net-destruction of critical cellular organelles, and cell death is predominantly considered to be through necrosis or necroptosis. Moreover, TDC showed good photostability, and its photocytotoxic effects persisted over a period of time, thus probably minimizing the requirement of multiple light exposures.

    Demonstration of In Vivo Photodynamic Activity

    The excellent photodynamic killing by TDC on SKOV-3 cells in culture encouraged us to establish its tumoricidal efficacy in vivo using a nude-mouse model. To analyze this, SKOV-3 cells (â¼3â5 Ã 106 cells, in a final volume of 200 Î¼L) were injected subcutaneously in the dorsal flanks of the nude mice, which were kept for 1 week in order to develop the tumors. After the injections of the SKOV-3 cells, the nude mice were divided into two groups. The first group of six animals served as the tumor-bearing controls, whereas the second group of animals bearing tumors were treated with TDC followed by irradiation. The sensitizer, TDC (in physiological saline), was administered intravenously (20 mg/kg body weight) to the mouse models, which were anesthetized and exposed to light (100 J/cm2 and 100 mW/cm2) for about 12â18 h post-TDC administration. The tumor volumes (TV, mm3) were monitored over a period of 2 weeks and are shown in Figure 8. As per ethical animal practices, animals with tumor volumes exceeding 10% of their body weights were sacrificed.
    The tumor volumes, measured on the 1st day (before the administration of TDC) and on the 14th day, were monitored and are shown in Figure 8A. Interestingly, the tumor volumes were not measurable after 14 days in the TDC-treated, irradiation-exposed animal models. The in vivo biological experiments clearly indicate that the control animals, those shown in top panels, exhibited enormous tumor growth, whereas the TDC-treated, light-exposed animals showed a dramatic reduction in tumor volumes to below measurable levels, sometimes in a short span of <14 days, without any observable scar at the site of the tumor. After the experiment, a small bit of the tissue at the tumor site was biopsied, processed for histology and stained with hematoxylin and eosin. These tissue sections clearly showed the complete replacement of the neoplastic cells with fibrous cells and other cell types (Figure 8A, H&E stained panels on the right side). It can be observed that there is a minimal scarring at the site of the tumor. Uniquely, the skin exposed to the photodynamic treatment in peritumoral areas of the animals did not exhibit any sensitivity throughout the experimental period, thereby indicating that TDC administration is safer than the commercially available photosensitizers.
    To determine the in vivo cellular localization, tumor-bearing animals were injected with TDC intratumorally, and the tumors were biopsied 12 h postadministration, sectioned, and analyzed by fluorescence microscopy. We observed that TDC fluorescence (red) was mainly localized in the neoplastic cells (Figure 9), whereas the fibrous-tissue cells and stromal cells (blue-stained nuclei) did not show red fluorescence. The selective localization of TDC and other photosensitizers in cancer cells is a subject of immense interest. It is hypothesized that p-glycoproteins, which are responsible for drug efflux, play an important role in the selective cellular retention of the photosensitizers.
    To understand the effect of TDC on nontarget organs, we examined the systemic effects of PDT in biopsied and sectioned tissues of various body parts, such as the livers, kidneys, spleens, hearts, and lungs of the animal models. Post-PDT, the tissues, including the untreated, dark control and the TDC-treated group, were stained with standard hematoxylin and eosin, as shown in Figure 10. These studies clearly showed that the cellular morphology of these organs remained intact without any detectable tissue pathology, implying that TDC does not affect the normal tissue but uniquely localizes only in the neoplastic tissue and damages it selectively.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, the dihydroxychlorin described herein, TDC, exhibited high solubility in biological media and excellent quantum yields for its triplet excited state (0.84 Â± 0.02) and singlet-oxygen generation (0.80 Â± 0.03). Our in vitro photodynamic investigations have indicated that TDC localizes selectively in neoplastic cells, and the biological activity involved predominantly ROS as the reactive intermediates. Interestingly, TDC induces severe intracellular, oxidative, photodynamic damage in ovarian-cancer cells through the destruction of cellular organelles such as the cell membrane, the actin cytoskeleton, and mitochondria. Moreover, the absence of any detectable changes in apoptotic markers such as caspase-3 clearly indicates that the mechanism of cell death is predominantly through a necrosis pathway. Uniquely, TDC exhibited negligible dark cytotoxicity and excellent photodynamic activity both in cell culture and in nude-mouse animal models. Its ease of synthesis; chemical purity; aqueous solubility; high optical absorption in the photodynamic window; efficient generation of triplet- and singlet-oxygen species; excellent in vitro and in vivo photodynamic activity; and nontoxic effects on nontarget tissues such as the liver, kidney, spleen, heart, and others makes TDC a promising sensitizer for the photodynamic therapy of cancer.
