    Abstract

    Floxuridine (FUdR, 5-fluoro-2-deoxyuridine) was widely used in patients with tumor. But the poor activity and severe side effects have been observed in the clinic, which resulted from increased degradation cleavage of FUdR to 5-FU by thymidine phosphorylase and reduced transporter-mediated entry into cells. In this study, we have synthesized a series of l-aspartic acid β-esters and l-glutamic acid γ-esters of FUdR to improve the metabolic stability of FUdR and target FUdR to cancer cells via amino acid transporter ATB0,+ which was exclusively up-regulated in some cancerous tissue. The uptake mechanism, stability, in vitro/in vivo antiproliferation action, pharmacokinetics, and tissue distribution were studied. The combined results showed the unusual 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR possessed a better tumor inhibition rate and a better metabolic stability than FUdR through a ATB0,+-mediated prodrug approach. The present study provided the first proof-of-concept of exploiting ATB0,+ for tumor-selective delivery of nucleoside analogues in the form of prodrug.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Nucleoside analogues continue to make a major contribution to the current chemotherapy of cancer.(1)In vivo intracellular delivery was the initial and key step for nucleoside analogues to exert their pharmacological activity.(1,2) Specialized nucleoside transporters, such as equilibrative nucleoside transporter (ENT) and concentrative nucleoside transporter (CNT), were essential for the cytotoxicity of nucleoside analogues in cancer cells.(3) However, the deficiency and the low capacity of ENT vitiated the cellular uptake of nucleoside analogues, thus producing drug resistance and side effects.(4) Floxuridine (FUdR, 5-fluoro-2-deoxyuridine) was a typical fluorinated pyrimidine analogue and used for the treatment of various solid tumors. Similar to other nucleoside analogues, it was very inefficient for FUdR to transverse cancer cell membranes. Furthermore, FUdR could be rapidly metabolized to 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) in vivo by thymidine phosphorylase (TP). This degradative cleavage of FUdR to 5-FU represented another acquired drug resistance in the clinic because FUdR inhibited the proliferation of cancer cells 10- to 100-fold higher than 5-FU.(5) As a consequence, FUdR was often administered to patients via painful hepatic artery infusion (HAI).(6) Therefore, targeting the delivery of FUdR to cancer cells efficiently and improving the metabolic stability of glycosidic bond were very meaningful and valuable not only for enhancing the therapeutic effect and obviating the systemic toxicity but also for the patient compliance. Numerous prodrug technologies have been attempted to circumvent the limitations associated with FUdR, such as conjugates of chenodeoxycholic acid (CDCA) targeted to Na+/taurocholate cotransporting polypeptide (NTCP),(7) peptide transporter 1 (PEPT1)-targeted peptidomimetics,(8−10) phosphoramidate protides,(11−13) and so on. However, no further information on these prodrugs is reported at present.
    Rapid proliferation of cancer cell required enhanced supply of nutrients to serve as the building blocks for the synthesis of macromolecules (DNA, RNA, proteins, and lipids) and as the carbon source for generation of metabolic energy inside cancer cells.(14,15) To meet these requirements, some membrane transporters were specifically up-regulated in cancer cells to transport more nutrients, especially neutral and cationic amino acid transporter B0,+ (ATB0,+, gene code SLC6A14).(16−18) ATB0,+ can transport all neutral and cationic amino acids as well as many drugs, such as valacyclovir, valganciclovir, and nitric oxide synthase inhibitor using the inward-directed Na+/Cl– gradient and the inside negative membrane potential as the driving force.(16,19,20) The full understanding of cancer proliferation has prompted a novel transporter-mediated approach to achieve cancer-tissue-targeted drug delivery.(21,22) Except for broad substrates specificity and high transport capacity, ATB0,+ was exclusively up-regulated in some maligant tissues, for example, estrogen receptor (ER)-positive breast cancer, pancreatic cancer, and colon cancer. It has been reported that patients with colorectal cancer showed approximately 20-fold increase in SLC6A14 mRNA levels in tumor tissue over normal tissue.(20) These characteristics have conferred ATB0,+ a very promising and attractive target for anticancer drug design and can avoid undesirable off-target effects.(17) The ATB0,+-targeted drug development strategy is effective for tissue specific delivery using amino acids as the targeting or recognition promoieties.(21,23) For example, treatment of ATB0,+-positive tumors cells with α-methyl-dl-tryptophan (α-MT), a blocker of ATB0,+, led to a significant suppression of the colony-forming ability, whereas ATB0,+-negative cell lines were not affected.(24) Aspartic acid or lysine-decorated docetaxel-loading liposomes developed by Sun et al. could be targeted to ATB0,+ transporter, and they displayed higher antitumor efficacy and less systemic toxicity.(25)
    Inspired by the above findings, we hypothesized that amino acid-based prodrugs, especially some unusual amino acid β- or γ-ester prodrugs, could increase the binding affinity of nucleoside analogues to ATB0,+.(23,26,27) Our assumption is stated as follows: first, because the substrates of ATB0,+ were very broad, amino acid β- or γ-ester prodrugs could be accommodated by the binding pocket of ATB0,+;(17) second, β- or γ-amino acids introduced into the prodrugs would increase the resistance of prodrugs to metabolic degradation in the systemic circulation because the compounds composed of β- or γ-amino acids are completely resistant toward proteolytic and metabolic hydrolysis,(26) ensuring that more intact prodrugs can be recognized by ATB0,+.
    Herein, we put forward an unusual amino acid β- or γ-ester prodrugs strategy to increase the affinity of FUdR to ATB0,+. A series of FUdR l-aspartic acid β-esters and l-glutamic acid γ-esters have been synthesized in the present study. As a comparison, l-valine α-esters of FUdR were also synthesized. These prodrugs were screened regarding the affinity to ATB0,+ and the chemical/metabolic stability. The homology mode of ATB0,+ was constructed and applied to reveal the interaction between the prodrugs and ATB0,+ at the molecular level.(28) All results demonstrated that the candidate prodrug l-aspartic acid β-carboxyl ester (5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR) was stable in the systemic circulation and can markedly increase the metabolic stability of FUdR to TP. 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR exhibited a significantly higher inhibition rate for ATB0,+-positive tumor in a xenograft mouse model compared with FUdR after intravenous injection. The combined results corroborated the idea that exploiting ATB0,+ for tumor-selective delivery of nucleoside analogues was feasible in the form of a prodrug.

    2.1. Synthesis of Prodrugs

    Boc-l-Val, Boc-l-Asp-OtBu, and Boc-l-Glu-OtBu were used as the starting materials to synthesize α-, β-, and γ-carboxyl esters of FUdR, respectively. The protecting group Boc and tBu were removed with 95% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) in water (v/v) within an hour to prevent the formation of the amide product (Figure 1), and then the target compounds were precipitated and purified with ice-cold ether. Nine prodrugs were confirmed by 1H NMR, 13C NMR, and mass spectrometry. The purities were determined by HPLC.

    2.2. Interaction between Prodrugs and ATB0,+

    The inhibition of ATB0,+-mediated uptake of glycine, a typical substrate of ATB0,+, could be applied to assess the interaction between the substrates and ATB0,+. It can be seen from Table 1 that all nine prodrugs could inhibit the uptake of glycine by ATB0,+, but the parent drug failed. IC50 for 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR was 0.82 ± 0.14 mM, and it possessed the highest affinity to ATB0,+ followed by 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR. Furthermore, enhanced ATB0,+-mediated uptake in X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ was observed with all prodrugs when compared with mock X. laevis oocytes cells. The enhancement extent in uptake ranged from 2.44- to 9.28-fold, and 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR exhibited the highest improvement. So the process of nine prodrugs into X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ cells was a ATB0,+-mediated process, and they were all substrates of ATB0,+. As a comparison, the ability of these prodrugs to inhibit PEPT1-mediated uptake of gly-sar was investigated. It could be seen from Table 2 that only three l-valine α-esters displayed significant inhibition of gly-sar uptake; l-aspartic acid and l-glutamic acid monosubstituted ester of FUdR showed very weak inhibition as FUdR, and l-aspartic acid and l-glutamic acid disubstituted ester could not interact with PEPT1. Furthermore, the enhanced PEPT1-mediated uptake was observed only for 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 3′-α-l-Val-FUdR, and 3′, 5′-di-α-l-Val-FUdR, and there was no significant difference for the l-aspartic acid β-ester and l-glutamic acid γ-esters of FUdR between MDCK-PEPT1 cell and MDCK cell. Therefore, l-aspartic acid β-esters and l-glutamic acid γ-esters of FUdR were not the substrates of PEPT1. 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR displayed the highest affinity to ATB0,+ when l-valine, l-aspartic acid, and l-glutamic acid were coupled to the hydroxyl groups of FUdR at the different sites, respectively. Therefore, we focused our further development efforts on these three prodrugs. When the uptake of these three prodrugs in the presence and absence of glycine was studied in X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ cells, the inhibition of 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR uptake by glycine was from 50% to 57% (Figure 2), which further indicated that these compounds were substrates of ATB0,+.

    Concentration-dependent uptake of 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR determined in X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ cells is shown in Figure 3. The uptake process could be fitted by the Michaelis–Menten equations. The kinetics constants (Km) for 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR were 54.6 ± 9.56 pmol/μL, 35.6 ± 7.58 pmol/μL, and 84.0 ± 15.2 pmol/μL. The Vmax values for 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR were 278.4 ± 23.1, 201.4 ± 34.2, and 234.55 ± 19.7 pmol/(mg protein·min), respectively. The Km of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR was the lowest, and it showed the highest affinity to ATB0,+.

    2.3. Na+ and Cl– Activation Kinetics

    ATB0,+ was a member of the Na+/Cl–-dependent transporter family that required both Na+ and Cl– for transport to occur. Figure 4 showed the increasing concentrations of Na+ and Cl– (from 0 to 120 mM) on the uptake of 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR in X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+. Dose–response curves were fitted to the Hill equations. For Na+, the Hill coefficients were 2.06 ± 0.13, 2.14 ± 0.11, and 1.90 ± 0.10 for 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR, respectively. The Cl– data yielded a Hill coefficient of 0.97 ± 0.07, 1.13 ± 0.11, and 0.99 ± 0.07 for 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR, respectively. A Hill slope close to 2 for Na+ suggested the transport cycle involves the binding of about two Na+ ions, and a Hill slope for Cl– ion close to 1 suggested the binding of one Cl– ion to one substrate. Therefore, during the process of 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR into the X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+, a transport stoichiometry of two Na+, one Cl–, and one substrate was proposed, which was in agreement with the reported energetics of ATB0,+.

    2.4. Molecular Docking

    The refined homology mode of ATB0,+ was constructed using DAT and PFKA1 as the templates. The docking results of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR, and 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR revealed the interaction with ATB0,+ at the molecular level. As shown in Figure 5A, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR formed seven hydrogen bonds with amino acid residues. The carboxyl group formed hydrogen bonds with Asp478 and Tyr131. The amino group formed a hydrogen bond with Asp478. Additionally, the carbonyl groups on the C-2 and C-6 positions of pyrimidinedione formed hydrogen bonds with Trp60, Ser324, and Ser427. The hydroxyl group at the sugar formed an H-bond with Asn58.
    5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR formed six hydrogen bonds with residues (Figure 5B). The carboxyl group formed hydrogen bonds with Asp478. The amino group formed hydrogen bonds with Tyr321 and Asp478. As 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, the carbonyl groups on the C-2 and C-6 position of pyrimidinedione also formed two hydrogen bonds with Ser324 and Ser427. The hydroxyl group formed hydrogen bonds with Asn58.
    5′-α-l-Val-FUdR formed only two hydrogen bonds with residues (Figure 5C). The carbonyl group on the C-2 position of pyrimidinedione formed a hydrogen bond with Tyr131. The hydroxyl group formed a H-bond with Tyr132. The skeleton of pyrimidinedione in 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR formed a π–π conjugation with Tyr321.

    2.5. Stability Study

    The half-lives (t1/2) calculated from linear regression of pseudo-first-order plots of prodrug concentration vs time are listed in Table 3. It can been seen that the prodrugs in pH 7.4 phosphate buffer were more stable than in plasma, which indicated that the enzyme in plasma may participate in the hydrolysis of the prodrugs in vivo. The t1/2 values of all prodrugs in plasma were 30 min or higher, which indicated a greater probability of the prodrugs being intact for targeted tissue. It can be observed that β- and γ-esters prodrugs possessed a better stability than α-esters in plasma. The hydrolysis of prodrugs in cancer cell homogenates was very rapid, implying a possibly rapid bioactivation of prodrugs in cancer tissue. When FUdR and the prodrugs were incubated in TP buffers, the t1/2 of all prodrugs were more than 230 min, but the t1/2 of FUdR was only 10.2 min. Therefore, the amino acid-based prodrug strategy could render the glycosidic bond more resistant to TP and improve the metabolic stability of FUdR.

    2.6. Antiproliferation Study in Vitro

    GI50 values for FUdR and all prodrugs were tested in two ATB0,+-positive cancer cells ZR-75-1 and PaCa-2 and one ATB0,+-negative breast cancer MB231 cells (Table 4). GI50 values for FUdR in ZR-75-1, PaCa-2, and MB231 cell were 30.1 mM, 27.3 mM, and 35.1 mM, respectively. In ATB0,+-positive ZR-75-1 and PaCa-2 cells, GI50 for FUdR prodrugs was from 1.6 to 4.2 mM, which was about 7- to 8-fold lower than FUdR. However, the GI50 values of these prodrugs in ZR-75-1 and PaCa-2 cell were not significantly different between each other, and they did not exhibit any discernible correlations with their affinity to ATB0,+ (IC50 in Table 1). In the ER-negative breast cancer MB231 cells which did not express ATB0,+, GI50 for all prodrugs was from 29.3 to 38.3 mM, which was not significantly different from GI50 of FUdR (35.1 mM).

    2.8. Pharmacokinetics and Tissue Distribution

    The pharmacokinetics and tissue distribution of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR were studied in a nude mouse xenograft model with ZR-75-1 cells to delineate the disposition of the prodrug in vivo (Figure 8). The AUC0–t of FUdR was 1877.1 ± 100.2 and 4600.1 ± 502.8 ng/g·h in the tumor after FUdR (90 mg/kg) and 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR (90 mg/kg, calculated as FUdR) were intravenously injected into the mouse, respectively. Conversely, the AUC0–t of FUdR in plasma was 3419.0 ± 309.2 ng/mL·h and 1172.9 ± 132.1 ng/mL·h after FUdR and 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR were dosed to the mouse, respectively. These results translated to a tumor-targeting index (AUC(0–t) FUdR(tumor)/AUC(0–t) FUdR (plasma)) of 3.9 for 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR and 0.55 for FUdR, and the increase in the targeting index for 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR over FUdR was about 7.1 (Table 6). Therefore, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR could be effectively targeted to the ATB0,+-positive ZR-75-1 cells and then converted to the parent drug FUdR, thus increasing the exposure of the parent drug in target tissue, which can explain why 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR had a higher inhibition rate for ZR-75-1-derived tumor in mouse than FUdR. In addition, the lower levels of the metabolite 5-FU in plasma were observed after dosing of 5′-β-Asp-FUdR than after FUdR (588.2 ± 67.9 (ng/mL)·h vs 4107.2 ± 453.9 (ng/mL)·h). So the amino acid prodrug can increase the metabolic stability of FUdR in vivo and decrease the formation of 5-FU.

    3. Discussion and Conclusion

    Recently, the emphasis in cancer drug development has been shifting from cytotoxic and nonspecific chemotherapies to molecularly targeted and rationally designed drugs, promising greater effectiveness and fewer side effects.(22,29−31) A variety of biological targets, especially some endogenous transporters that are expressed on the membrane of cancer cell, could be utilized to exploit the differences between normal and cancerous tissues.(22) The common strategy was to develop a conjugated drug that incorporated a tumor targeting group and a cytotoxic drug. Due to the very high expression in cancerous tissue, the broad substrates specificity, high transport capacity, and the cotransport of Na+ as well as Cl– in the same direction,(16,18) the ATB0,+-targeted delivery strategy allowed exposure of SLC6A14-positve tumor cells to chemotherapy with minimal off-target effects.(17,31) So ATB0,+ was a better drug target than other amino acid transporters, such as SLC1A5, SLC6A19, PEPT1, and so on.(17)
    Like many other nucleoside analogues, poor activity and severe side effects of FUdR have been observed in the clinic, and this innate or acquired drug resistance has been ascribed to several parameters, such as increased degradation cleavage of FUdR to 5-FU by TP and reduced transporter-mediated entry of FUdR into cells.(32,33) Therefore, there is a need for an efficient delivery system for FUdR that bypasses these resistance phenomena, improves the therapeutic index, and decreases adverse effects.(34) It has been reported that some peptidomimetics consisiting of unusual β- or γ-amino acid demonstrate improved stability and are completely resistant to metabolic enzyme-mediated cleavage when compared to the α-amino acid counterpart.(26) Additionally, β- or γ-amino acid introduced in the prodrugs can decrease the affinity of prodrugs to PEPT1, a membrane transporter widely expressed in the healthy organ.(10,27) Hence, amino acid β- or γ-ester prodrugs of FUdR targeted to ATB0,+ would not be recognized by PEPT1, and the potential toxicity of prodrugs to the PEPT1-overexpressing normal tissues can be avoided.(27) Therefore, in the present study, the β-carboxyl group of l-aspartic acid and the γ-carboxyl group of l-glutamic acid were coupled to the 3′ or 5′-position hydroxyl of FUdR. Meanwhile, l-valine α-ester of FUdR was also synthesized as a comparison.
    The uptake inhibition of glycine (a typical substrate of ATB0,+) by the prodrugs was routinely carried out to investigate the prodrugs’ ability to interact with ATB0,+. From Table 1, it can be seen that IC50 for prodrugs was from 0.82 to 2.98 mM. Additionally, all prodrugs showed several-fold higher direct uptake in X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ than mock X. laevis oocytes. But the parent drug FUdR did not interact with ATB0,+. Hence, α-, β-, and γ-ester derivatives of FUdR were all substrates of ATB0,+. The experiments that followed, such as inhibition of uptake by glycine, concentration-dependent uptake, and Na+ and Cl– activation kinetics, further suggested that 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR were the substrates of ATB0,+. Their transport into cancer cells was an ATB0,+-mediated active process and could be seriously inhibited by glycine (Figure 2). The transport process could be fitted by the Michaelis–Menten equations (Figure 3) and complied with the energetics of ATB0,+ (Figure 4).(16,18) Interestingly, only three l-valine α-esters of FUdR were the substrates of PEPT1; β- and γ-esters did not interact with PEPT1 (Table 2). Therefore, all of the above results proved our hypothesis that ATB0,+ can accept amino acid β- and γ-esters of FUdR as the substrates. In line with these above observation, Ganapathy and his colleagues’ work have reported in 2004 that l-aspartate β-benzyl ester and l-aspartate γ-benzyl ester were the substrates of ATB0,+.(23) Like valacyclovir and valganciclovir, l-valine α-ester of FUdR can be recognized by ATB0,+ as well as PEPT1 due to the overlap of substrates between ATB0,+ and PEPT1.(35) According to Ganapathy’s opinion,(17) to avoid the potential side effects of PEPT1-overexpressing normal tissue, l-valine α-ester of FUdR should be excluded from the candidate drugs.
    In the present study, the 3D structure of ATB0,+ was constructed through a homology modeling approach with DAT and PFKA1 as the template.(28) The molecular docking indicated 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR formed seven and six hydrogen bonds with ATB0,+, respectively, and 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR formed only two hydrogen bonds. So 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR displayed the strongest interaction with ATB0,+, which was consistent with the lowest IC50 of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR in Table 1. The refined homology modeling can provide a deeper insight into the interaction between ATB0,+ and the substrates.
    Except for targeting to a specific transporter or receptor on cancer cell membrane, delivery and stability were other aspects of cancer drug development that have been receiving much attention.(36−38) The prodrugs should keep stable and be intact in the systemic circulation before they are delivered to cancer cells to ensure they could be recognized by the biological targets expressed in the surface of cancer cells.(39) The t1/2 of all prodrugs in plasma was more than 30 min, which implied a greater probability of being intact for targeted tissue (Table 3).(8,9) The prodrugs containing β- and γ-ester bond indicated a better stability than α-ester prodrug in plasma. When FUdR and the prodrugs were incubated in TP buffers, the t1/2 values of all prodrugs were more than 230 min, but the t1/2 of FUdR was only 10.2 min. So the amino acid derivative approach rendered the glycosidic bond more resistant to TP and improved the metabolic stability of FUdR.
    The antiproliferation study of FUdR and its derivatives in vitro was investigated in two ATB0,+-positive cancer cells ZR-75-1 and PaCa-2 and one ATB0,+-negative breast cancer MB231 cells (Table 4). In ATB0,+-positive cancer cells, all prodrugs displayed a significantly higher inhibition rate than FUdR, but the GI50 was not different between FUdR and the prodrugs in ATB0,+-negative breast cancer MB231 cells. Therefore, the ATB0,+-mediated transport may be responsible for the better cytotoxicity of the prodrugs in ATB0,+-positive cancer cells. However, the GI50 for prodrugs in ZR-75-1 and PaCa-2 cells did not exhibit any discernible correlations with their affinity to ATB0,+(IC50 in Table 1), and they were not significantly different from each other. It might be difficult to discern a meaningful correlation between GI50 and prodrug permeability.(9) The possible explanation may be that ATB0,+ had enough time to concentrate all intact prodrugs inside the cancer cells for its high transport capacity when cancer cells were incubated with the prodrugs for 3 h. To test the explanation above, we will determine the concentration of FUdR and prodrugs in cancer cells and clarify this phenomenon in a future study.
    Because of the highest affinity to ATB0,+, the appropriate stability in plasma and in TP buffers, and the rapid bioactivation in cancer cell homogenates, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR was selected as the optimal compound for antiproliferation study in vivo. Like α-MT,(21,24) 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR possessed a higher antitumor activity against ATB0,+-positive ZR-75-1 and PaCa-2 tumor in a xenograft mouse model than FUdR (58.6% vs 36.1% for ZR-75–1, 62.4% vs 41.3% for PaCa-2, Table 5, Figure 7). On the contrary, the inhibition rate for MB-231 tumor was not significantly different between 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR and FUdR (33.8% vs 36.9%). The pharmacokinetics and tissue distribution of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR in mouse xenograft can explain the pharmacological result above (Figure 8). The tumor to plasma distribution (tumor-targeting index) for FUdR was 7.1-fold higher after 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR dosing than after dosing of FUdR, which meant that 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR could achieve tumor-targeted delivery and less systemic exposure. Therefore, combined with the former transport results, the prodrug can alter the transport pattern of FUdR across the cell membrane and accumulate more parent drug in tumor cells with the aid of ATB0,+, thus producing a better pharmacological activity. In a future experiment, we will silence SLC6A14 in ZR-75-1 cells and construct a ZR-75-1/SLC6A14-shRNA bearing tumor mouse xenograft to clarify the role of ATB0,+ in the uptake of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR into tumor cells further. In addition, the lower levels of the metabolite 5-FU in plasma were observed after dosing of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR than after FUdR. Therefore, the amino acid prodrug conferred protection from metabolic degradation in vivo and decreased the formation of 5-FU (Table 6), which was another factor that contributed to the improved pharmacological activity of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR in the xenograft model.
    In conclusion, in the present study we have developed amino acid β- and γ-esters of FUdR to increase the binding affinity toward ATB0,+, leading to enhanced ATB0,+-targeted delivery and improved antiproliferation activity in vivo accompanied by better metabolic stability of FUdR in systemic circulation. The amino acid β- and γ-esters of FUdR displayed more advantages than their α-counterparts. Our findings disclosed that this bifunctional and unusual amino acid β- or γ-ester prodrugs strategy could be adopted to improve the tumor-targeted delivery of nucleoside analogues with severe side effects and metabolic deactivation.

    4.2. Prodrug Synthesis

    A solution of BOC-protected amino acid (22 mmol), carbonyldiimidazole (CDI, 3.57 g, 22 mmol), and anhydrous tetrahydrofuran (THF, 150 mL) was stirred under argon at 25 °C for 1.5 h and then 40–50 °C for 20 min. This mixture was slowly added to a stirred solution of FUdR (4.98 g, 20 mmol), DMAP (0.25 g, 2 mmol), triethylamine (TEA, 50 mL), and anhydrous N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF, 100 mL) at 80 °C. The reaction mixture continued to stir at this temperature for 1 h (Figure 1). The reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature, and TEA and THF were removed under vacuum. The remaining DMF solution was then neutralized to pH 7.5 with acetic acid, and DMF was evaporated under reduced pressure. The residue was diluted with ethyl acetate and successively washed with water and brine. The organic layer was condensed and the residue was purified by silica column chromatography (EtOAc/PE = 1:8 to 1:1) to give BOC-protected 3′-monoester, 5′-monoester, and 3′,5′-diesters of FUdR. The protecting groups Boc and tBu were removed with 95% trifluoroacetic acid in water (v/v) within an hour to prevent the formation of the amide product, and then the target compounds were precipitated and purified with ice-cold ether. Nine prodrugs were confirmed by 1H NMR, 13C NMR, and mass spectrometry. The purity of all prodrugs was >95% as determined by HPLC.

    4.3. Glycine Uptake Inhibition by Prodrugs

    X. laevis oocytes cell and MDCK were obtained from American Type Culture Collection (ATTC, Rockville, MD). Cells were grown routinely in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM, 4500 mg/L glucose) containing 10% fetal bovine serum, 1% nonessential amino acids, 1 mmol/L sodium pyruvate, and 1% l-glutamine as previously described.(23)
    To assess the interaction of prodrugs with ATB0,+, the inhibition of prodrugs on glycine uptake by ATB0,+ was studied. For uptake experiments, X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ samples were seeded onto 24-well plastic cluster trays at about 2.0 × 105 cells/cm2 for 6 days after seeding. The uptake buffer was 25 mM Hepes/Tris containing 140 mM NaCl, 5.4 mM KCl, 1.8 mM CaCl2, 0.8 mM MgSO4, and 5 mM d-glucose. After washing twice with uptake buffer, X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ samples were incubated with 10 μM glycine along with various concentrations of FUdR prodrugs (0.1–10 mM) at 37 °C for 10 min. After 10 min, the medium was removed, and the cells were rapidly rinsed twice with 1 mL of ice-cold uptake buffer (pH 6.0). The cells were collected and homogenized in 0.25 mL of water. The homogenates were centrifuged at 1500g for 6 min, and glycine in the supernatant was analyzed by HPLC/MS/MS. The protein concentration of each sample was determined by BCA protein assay. As a comparison, the inhibition of FUdR prodrugs on gly-sar (10 μM, a typical substrate of PEPT1) uptake by MDCK-PEPT1 was also performed as previously reported. IC50 was determined using nonlinear data fitting.

    4.4. Uptake of Prodrugs by ATB0,+

    The uptake of FUdR prodrugs in X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ and mock X. laevis oocytes cells was evaluated to study the role of ATB0,+. Two kind of cells were grown as described above. X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ and X. laevis oocytes cells were incubated with FUdR and nine derivatives (all 0.5 mM, pH 6.0) at 37 °C for 10 min. As a comparison, the uptake of FUdR prodrugs by MDCK-PEPT1 and mock MDCK was also performed. The samples were analyzed by HPLC.
    The inhibition of uptake of 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR (all 0.5 mM, pH 6.0) by X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ was assessed in the presence of glycine (10 mM). The concentration of 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR inside the cells was analyzed by HPLC.

    4.5. Concentration-Dependent Uptake of Prodrugs by ATB0,+

    To further study the interaction between the derivatives of FUdR and ATB0,+, the saturation kinetics for the ATB0,+-mediated uptake was performed for 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR. X. laevis oocytes-ATB0,+ cells at about 2.0 × 105 cells/cm2 were seeded for 6 days. The derivatives (0–0.4 mM) were added at 37 °C for 10 min, respectively. The concentration inside the cells was analyzed by HPLC.

    4.6. Na+ and Cl– Activation Kinetics

    To investigate Na+ or Cl– on the ATB0,+-mediated uptake process, the free Na+ or Cl– medium was applied to assess their inhibitory potential. The Na+ free medium was prepared by replacing NaCl with N-methyl-d-glucamine chloride (NMGCl) in uptake buffers. The Cl– free medium was prepared by replacing NaCl, KCl, and CaCl2 with sodium gluconate, potassium gluconate, and calcium gluconate, respectively, at the same concentration. The uptake of 5′-α-l-Val-FUdR, 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, and 5′-γ-l-Glu-FUdR (1 mM) at the different concentrations of Na+ or Cl– (from 0 to 120 mM) was investigated. The process was fitted to the Hill equations, and the Hill coefficient was calculated. The concentration inside the cells was analyzed by HPLC.

    4.7. Homology Modeling and Molecular Docking(28)

    The protein sequences of ATB0,+ (Uniprot ID: Q9UN76) was obtained from the Universal Protein Resource (https://www.uniprot.org/). After a protein–protein BLAST search against the NCBI Protein Data Bank (PDB) (https://www.rcsb.org/), the crystal structure of the Drosophila melanogaster sodiun-dependent DAT (PDB code 4M48) was set as the main template. Since the crystal structure of DAT lacks the EL2, a second local blast search was set on residues S205-W270 of ATB0,+, identifying the crystallographic structure of phosphofructokinase (PFKA1) from Saccharomyces cerevisiae (PDB code 3O8O) as an adequate template to fix the EL2 gap. The sequences of potential templates and ATB0,+ were aligned using ClustalW to compare the amino acid sequences. Finally, on the basis of the alignment results, the model was generated by using the SWISS-MODEL online server (https://www.swissmodel.expasy.org), which could build energy minimized protein model automatically with the ProMod3 engine.
    The molecular docking procedure was carried out with Schrödinger Glide docking in the “extra precision” (XP) mode in order to evaluate the ability of the tested compounds to bind the ATB0,+ binding channel, keeping the five top-scoring poses and setting the rigid docking protocol.

    4.8. Stability Study

    4.8.1. Chemical Stability

    The stability of the prodrugs (200 μM) in phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) was determined to obtain the contribution of the nonenzymatic hydrolysis. Rate constants of hydrolysis were determined with pseudo-first-order kinetic models. The samples was analyzed by HPLC.

    4.8.2. Stability in Plasma

    The solution of prodrugs was added to the glass tube, and the solvent was evaporated by N2. The mouse plasma was added to the tube and mixed, and the resulting concentration was 200 μM. Aliquot samples were collected at various time points (0, 30, 60, 90, 120 min). The plasma was pretreated with solid-phase extraction, and the concentration was determined by HPLC–MS/MS.

    4.8.3. Stability in Thymidine Phosphorylase

    FUdR and the prodrugs were added to the solution of thymidine phosphorylase (2 ng/mL) in phosphate buffer (pH 6.4) at 37 °C. The resulting concentration of FUdR and prodrugs was 300 μM. The samples were periodically collected at the predetermined time, quenched with cold TFA, and analyzed for prodrug, FUdR, and 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) with HPLC.

    4.9. Antiproliferation of Cancer Cell in Vitro(9)

    The stock solutions of FUdR and FUdR prodrugs were prepared with water and then were 2-fold serially diluted with buffer solutions (25 mM HEPES/Tris buffer, pH 6.4, 140 mM NaCl, 5.4 mM KCl, 1.8 mM CaCl, 0.8 mM MgSO4, and 5 mM glucose).
    The cancer cell proliferation studies were performed with ER-positive breast cancer ZR-75-1 cells, the pancreatic cancer PaCa-2 cells, and ER-negative breast cancer MB231 cells with no expression of ATB0,+. ZR-75-1 cells and PaCa-2 cells have a high expression of ATB0,+. These three cells lines were cultured in RPMI-1640 medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), 1% penicillin–streptomycin at 37 °C, 5% CO2, and 5% humidity. The cells were seeded onto 96-well plates at 1.0 × 105 cells per well. After incubation for 24 h, the medium was removed and washed with buffer solutions. The drug solutions were added to the well, and these cells were incubated for 3 h. After this period, the drug solutions were removed and the cells were washed gently with buffer solutions. Fresh culture medium was then added to the wells, and the cells were allowed to recover and grow for 24 h before evaluating cell viability with addition of MTT. The absorbance was measured with the microplate reader at a wavelength of 570 nm. GI50 was calculated using nonlinear data fitting.

    4.10. Inhibition of Tumor Growth in Mouse Xenografts

    All animal experiments in the present study have been approved by the University Committee on Use and Care of Animals, Jiangxi University of Traditional Chinese Medicines. Female NOD SCID nude mice were purchased from Beijing Vital River Laboratory Animal Technology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). The age was about 8 weeks, and the weight was about from 19 to 21 g. The nude mice were allowed to acclimate to the environment for 1 week prior to the experiment. To study the in vivo inhibition of tumor growth, the tumor-bearing nude mice were established. ZR-75-1 cells and MB-231 cells were injected subcutaneously close to the lower mammary gland on the right side of nude mice. PaCa-2 cells were injected subcutaneously into the right flank of the mice.
    The tumors were allowed to grow to about 100 mm3. Then the animals were divided into the control and the treatment groups (about 10 mice per group) wherein the control groups were free to the sucrose–water for drinking and the treatment group was injected with FUdR or 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR (90 mg/kg calculated as FUdR) via tail vein for 20 successive days. Tumor size in control and treatment groups was measured every 2 day by caliper, and the volume of the tumor was calculated using the formula (width2 × length)/2. On day 20, the tumor was removed from the mice, and the tumor weight and the body weight were calculated.

    4.11. Pharmacokinetics and Tissue Distribution

    The pharmacokinetics and tissue distribution were also approved by the University Committee on Use and Care of Animals, Jiangxi University of Traditional Chinese Medicines. The pharmacokinetics and tissue distribution of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR and FUdR were studied in NOD SCID nude mouse xenograft model established with ZR-75-1 cells to delineate the disposition of the prodrug in vivo. The tumors were allowed to grow to about 100 mm3. The mouse was injected with FUdR or 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR (90 mg/kg calculated as FUdR) via tail vein. Plasma and tumor were collected at 0, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12, and 24 h. At every sampling time point, 5 to 6 nude mice were anesthetized with ether and blood was collected via cardiac puncture from every mouse. Then, these mice were sacrificed, and all tumors were also collected. After centrifugation, about 100 μL of plasma from every mouse was collected and frozen. The concentrations of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, 5-FU, and FUdR in plasma and tumor were determined by HPLC–MS/MS.
    The pharmacokinetic parameters were processed by noncompartmental analysis using the DAS 2.0 software package (Chinese Pharmacological Society). The plasma or tumor concentration at different times was expressed as the mean ± standard deviation (SD), and the mean concentration–time curves were plotted. The maximum plasma or tumor concentration (Cmax) and the time to reach it were observed directly from the concentration–time curves. The area under the plasma concentration–time curve from zero to the time of the final measurable sample (AUC0–t) was calculated using the linear–trapezoidal rule up to the last sampling point with the detectable level (C).

    4.12. Analytical Method

    4.12.1. HPLC Analysis

    HPLC for the analysis of FUdR and nine prodrugs was performed on a Shimadzu LC-20ATHPLC system consisting of a SPD-M20A DAD, a SIL-20A autosampler, and a LabSolution workstation. The analytes were separated on ODS C18 (4.6 mm × 250 mm, 5 μm) with a wavelength of 254 nm, and the analytes were eluted by acetonitrile/water/glacial acetic acid (30:70:0.1%, v/v/v) with a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min. The column temperature was set at 25 °C.

    4.12.2. HPLC–MS/MS Method

    Shimadzu LCMS-8050 liquid chromatograph mass spectrometer system (HPLC–MS/MS) was used for the determination of the analyte. A Shimadzu LC-VP HPLC system (Kyoto, Japan) consisting of an LC-30AD pump and a SIL-30AC autosampler was used to separate the analyte.
    For determination of glycine and gly sar in cell homogenates, glycine, gly sar, and the internal standard isoniazid were extracted from the homogenates using a protein precipitation with methanol. A HILIC column (ACQUITY UPLC BEH HILIC, 50 mm × 2.1 mm, 1.7 μm) was used to separate the analytes from the endogenous components. The elution was carried out using a gradient of acetonitrile and water containing 0.1% formic acid. The ESI source was set in positive ionization mode. The MS/MS transitions of 76.1 → 76.1 for glycine, 147.2 → 90.2 for gly sar, and m/z 138.1 → 121.2 for isoniazid were applied for the determination.
    For determination of 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, 5-FU, and FUdR in plasma and tumor, three analytes and the internal standard lamivudine were extracted from rat plasma or tumor homogenates by Waters Oasis HLB cartridges (Waters Corporation; Milford, MA). The retention and separation were performed on a Shim-pack XR-ODSIII column (75 mm × 2.0 mm, 1.6 μm; Kyoto, Japan). The mobile phase was a mixture of 0.1% formic acid in water (v/v) (mobile phase A) and methanol (mobile phase B). The gradient elution program was as follows: 12% B, 0–0.5 min; 12–95% B, 0.5–3.0 min; 95% B, 4.0 min; 12% B, 4.01–5.5 min. The total analysis time per injection was 5.5 min, and the flow rate was set at 0.2 mL/min. The column oven was maintained at 30 °C. The electrospray ionization (ESI) source was performed in positive mode, and the mass spectrometric parameters were optimized at interface temperature 300 °C, desolvation line temperature 250 °C, and heat block temperature 400 °C; spraying gas, nitrogen, 3.0 L/min; nebulizing gas, 10 L/min; heating gas, 10 L/min. The transitions of 362.3 → 131.1 for 5′-β-l-Asp-FUdR, 247.2 → 131.1 for FUdR, 131.1→ 131.1 for 5-FU, and 230.1 → 112.3 for lamivudine were applied for the determination.
    All methods were validated according to the Guidance for Industry, Bioanalytical, Method Validation, U.S. Food and Drug Administration.

    4.13. Statistical Analysis

    The statistical differences were tested using a one-tailed Student t test at the p < 0.01 or at the p < 0.05 level.

        Redox-sensitive micelles for targeted intracellular delivery and combination chemotherapy of paclitaxel and all-trans-retinoid acid
        Han Lingfei; Wang Xin; Liu Wenyuan; Hu Lejian; Liu Fulei; Huang Xiaoxian; Liu Bowen; Feng Feng; Qu Wei; Feng Feng; Liu Wenyuan
        Asian journal of pharmaceutical sciences (2019), 14 (5), 531-542 ISSN:.
        The application of paclitaxel (PTX) in clinic has been restricted due to its poor solubility. Several traditional nano-medicines have been developed to improve this defect, while they are still lack of tumor targeting ability and rapid drug release. In this work, an amphiphilic polymeric micelle of hyaluronic acid (HA) - all-trans-retinoid acid (ATRA) with a disulfide bond, was developed successfully for the co-delivery of PTX and ATRA. The combination chemotherapy of PTX and ATRA can strengthen the anti-tumor activity. Due to these properties, cytotoxicity assay verified that PTX-loaded HA-SS-ATRA micelles showed concentration-dependent cytotoxicity and displayed obvious combination therapy of PTX and ATRA. Importantly, HA-SS-ATRA micelles could remarkably prolong plasma circulation time after intravenously administration. Therefore, redox-sensitive HA-SS-ATRA micelles could be utilized and explored as a promising drug delivery system for cancer combination chemotherapy.

        Recent advances in drug delivery strategies for improved therapeutic efficacy of gemcitabine

        Gemcitabine (2',2'-difluoro-2'-deoxycytidine; dFdC) is an efficacious anticancer agent acting against a wide range of solid tumors, including pancreatic, non-small cell lung, bladder, breast, ovarian, thyroid and multiple myelomas. However, short plasma half-life due to metab. by cytidine deaminase necessitates administration of high dose, which limits its medical applicability. Further, due to its hydrophilic nature, it cannot traverse cell membranes by passive diffusion and, therefore, enters via nucleoside transporters that may lead to drug resistance. To circumvent these limitations, macromol. prodrugs and nanocarrier-based formulations of Gemcitabine are gaining wide recognition. The nanoformulations based approaches by virtue of their controlled release and targeted delivery have proved to improve bioavailability, increase therapeutic efficacy and reduce adverse effects of the drug. Furthermore, the combination of Gemcitabine with other anticancer agents as well as siRNAs using nanocarriers has also been investigated in order to enhance its therapeutic potential. This review deals with challenges and recent advances in the delivery of Gemcitabine with particular emphasis on macromol. prodrugs and nanomedicines.
