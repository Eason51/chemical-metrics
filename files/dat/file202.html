    Abstract

    In the present article, the M1 mAChR bitopic agonist 1-[3-(4-butylpiperidin-1-yl)propyl]-1,2,3,4-tetrahydroquinolin-2-one (77-LH-28-1, 1) has been demonstrated to show unexpected D4R selectivity over D2R and D3R and to behave as a D4R antagonist. To better understand the structural features required for the selective interaction with the D4R and to obtain compounds unable to activate mAChRs, the aliphatic butyl chain and the piperidine nucleus of 1 were modified, affording compounds 2–14. The 4-benzylpiperidine 9 and the 4-phenylpiperazine 12 showed high D4R affinity and selectivity not only over the other D2-like subtypes, but also over M1–M5 mAChRs. Derivative 12 was also highly selective over some selected off-targets. This compound showed biased behavior, potently and partially activating Gi protein and inhibiting β-arrestin2 recruitment in functional studies. Pharmacokinetic studies demonstrated that it was characterized by a relevant brain penetration. Therefore, 12 might be a useful tool to better clarify the role played by D4R in disorders in which this subtype is involved.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Muscarinic acetylcholine receptors (mAChRs) are G protein-coupled receptors and include five subtypes, namely, M1–M5.(1) Among the five mAChRs, the M1 subtype is the most abundantly expressed in the brain, including cortex, hippocampus, amygdala, and striatum.(2) The M1 subtype is believed to be important for cognition and attention, with potential benefit for the treatment of cognitive symptoms exhibited by those suffering from Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and schizophrenia.(3) The search for a fully selective M1 agonist has been a pharmaceutical quest for many decades.(4) Among the several compounds published so far, one of the first was 77-LH-28-1 (1-[3-(4-butyl-1-piperidinyl)propyl]-3,4-dihydro-2(1H)-quinolinone, 1) (Figure 1), which selectively activates the M1 subtype over the other four M2–M5 mAChR subtypes.(5) Initially classified as an allosteric agonist, 1 is now considered as a bitopic agonist, interacting concomitantly with the orthosteric site and part of an allosteric site.(6) The muscarinic agonist 1 extends down into the orthosteric site as well as up toward extracellular receptor regions associated with an allosteric site. The piperidine basic center of 1 interacts with the Asp105 of the third transmembrane (TM) segment in the orthosteric binding site. Mutational data support the requirement of such a residue for the receptor activation by this muscarinic agonist.(7) However, the 3,4-dihydroquinolin-2(1H)-one aromatic group of 1 has been predicted to occupy space between TM2, TM3, and TM7 domains, near the extracellular loops. In preclinical models of schizophrenia and Alzheimer’s disease, 1 displayed antipsychotic and cognition-enhancing efficacy.(5) Unfortunately, its efficacy was limited by nonselective effects on other receptors, including dopamine D2 receptor subtype (D2R).(8) D2R belongs to the D2-like receptor family that also includes D3 (D3R) and D4 (D4R) subtypes, which activate Gαi/o to inhibit adenylate cyclase activity and cAMP production.(9) The Gαi/o class of G proteins is inactivated by pertussis toxin-catalyzed ADP-ribosylation. The DR signaling is now widely appreciated to be more complex than simple changes in cAMP levels. Studies have shown that D2-like receptors modulate numerous other signaling pathways, including phospholipases, ion channels, mitogen activated protein (MAP) kinases, and the Na+/H+ exchanger.(10)
    Several psychiatric and neurological disorders, in particular those involving the D2-like subtypes, including Parkinson’s disease, schizophrenia, attention deficit disorder/hyperactivity disorder, Tourette’s disorder, neuroleptic-induced akathisia, post-traumatic stress disorder, drug abuse, bipolar disorder, and restless legs syndrome, have been associated with hypoactivation or hyperactivation of dopamine-releasing neurons.(11) Furthermore, unmet medical needs involve side effects (motor, metabolic, cardiovascular, and emotional negative potential) associated with medications targeting D2-like receptors. This may in part be due to the lack of drugs able to selectively discriminate among the D2-like receptor subtypes. D2R shares 75% homology in the transmembrane regions with D3R and 53% identity with D4R.(12) Moreover, D2-like receptors share high homology with other receptor families (muscarinic, adrenergic, serotoninergic receptors), which mediate different physiological processes in distinct brain regions.(13) Thus, the need for the identification of new ligands with increased D2-like subtype selectivity and potentially reduced side effects remains an important challenge in the design of therapeutically useful drugs.
    Among the D2-like receptor subtypes, D4R aroused great interest in the early 1990s, when the known atypical antipsychotic agent clozapine demonstrated to bind it with high affinity and showed good selectivity over the other dopamine receptor subtypes.(14) This observation led to the development of novel D4R antagonists potentially useful for the treatment of schizophrenia. However, due to the failure of such antagonists in the clinic, D4R has lost its interest as a therapeutic target for decades. Recently, D4R has garnered a renewed attraction, emerging as a potential therapeutic target for diseases such as cancer, drug addiction, and Parkinson’s disease in which D4R antagonists can attenuate l-DOPA-induced dyskinesias.(15)
    Compounds belonging to different chemotypes, including aryl-linked piperazines, amide-linked piperazines, piperidines, morpholines, and imidazolines, have been reported as selective D4R ligands (15–19, Figure 2).(15) Concerning piperidines and piperazines, a general formula deduced from classical D3R and D4R ligands known in the literature has been proposed. It consists of a lipophilic moiety linked by a spacer to a basic function and an aromatic terminal, which seems to control intrinsic activity (Figure 3A).(16,17) The aromatic terminal can also be fused with the piperidine ring, affording 1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline derivatives, acting as potent and selective D3R ligands.(18) Another feature frequently observed in D2-like ligands is the presence of an amide function in the lipophilic moiety or in the spacer. Most of the ligands are characterized by the carbonyl function linked to the lipophilic moiety and the NH connected through a spacer to the basic function. However, some D2-like ligands bear an inverse amide function.(16,17) While in some benzolactam derivatives the effect of regioisomerism at the amide function on D2R and D3R affinity has been evaluated,(19) concerning D4R ligands, the optimal substitution pattern has not been clarified so far. The structure of 1 (Figure 3B) fits the features of the pharmacophore reported in Figure 3A, except for the presence of a butyl chain instead of the aromatic terminal. This structurally intriguing difference prompted us to know more about the pharmacological dopaminergic properties of 1, which was therefore evaluated for its affinity at D2R, D3R, and D4R subtypes by radioligand binding assays. Encouraged by its interesting affinity profile, according to which 1 showed high affinity for D4R and selectivity over D2R and D3R subtypes (Table 1), to better understand the structural features required for the selective interaction with D4R and to obtain compounds unable to activate mAChRs, the aliphatic butyl chain of 1 was variously modified, and the novel compounds 2–4 and 8–10 (Figure 1) were prepared and evaluated by radioligand binding studies at dopamine D2R, D3R, and D4R and at M1–M5 mAChRs. Compounds 5–7, already synthesized and evaluated for their affinity at mAChRs,(20) were included in this study. Furthermore, since it is well-known that not only piperidines but also piperazines are able to bind DRs, the novel compounds 11, 13, and 14, as well as the known compound 12, already evaluated for its analgesic activity,(21) were prepared and studied (Figure 1). Compounds 1, 9, and 12 were also evaluated by receptor binding assays at some off-targets (α1a-, α1b-, and α1d-adrenoceptor (AR), β1- and β2-AR, σ1 receptor, and dopamine and serotonin transporter (DAT and SERT, respectively)). The most relevant D4R selective ligands were tested for their functional activities by bioluminescence resonance energy transfer (BRET) assays that detect G protein activation and β-arrestin recruitment with D4R, M1, and M4 mAChRs.

    Affinity for Dopamine D2-like Receptor Subtypes

    The data reported in Table 1 show that the M1 bitopic compound 1 binds with high affinity to D4R (pKi = 9.01), and with significantly lower affinity to the other two D2-like receptor subtypes (pKi D2R = 6.17 and pKi D3R = 6.21). Consequently, 1 is highly selective for D4R over D2R (selectivity ratio D4/D2 = 691) and D3R (selectivity ratio D4/D2 = 631). This selectivity is comparable to those of already known D4R selective ligands belonging to different chemotypes (Figure 2).(15) Interestingly, for the first time, it has been demonstrated that the aromatic terminal present in all the known piperidine and piperazine ligands selective for D4R can be replaced by a butyl chain (Figure 3). The D4/D3 selectivity ratio shown by 1 is about 3.7 times higher than that of the known selective D4R antagonist 15, tested under the same experimental conditions used for the compounds of the present article.
    Similarly to 1, all its analogues 2–14 show affinities for D4R higher than those for D2R and D3R, a novel class of D4R selective derivatives being obtained. Within this series, 1 is the most selective compound for D4R over D2R and D3R, together with compound 9, which even shows increased selectivity ratios (D4/D2 = 724; D4/D3 = 1445).
    The butyl chain of 1 plays an important role in the affinity for all the dopamine D2-like receptor subtypes. Its shortening or lengthening, affording 3 and 4, respectively, slightly reduces both affinity and selectivity for D4R, while its replacement with a methyl group (compound 2) is strongly detrimental. Instead, the introduction of a phenyl ring directly linked to the 4-position of the piperidine nucleus (compound 8) or spaced by one or two methylene groups (compounds 9 and 10, respectively) is compatible with high affinity and selectivity for D4R. Indeed, compounds 8–10 show D4R affinity values similar to that of 1. The benzyl group confers to the ligand the best affinity for D4R and selectivity over the other D2-like receptor subtypes, as demonstrated by 9, which shows selectivity values even higher than those of 15.
    The introduction of a polar hydroxy group in the chain of compounds 3 and 4, affording 5 and 6, respectively, causes a decrease in both affinity and selectivity for D4R. However, while the affinity of compound 5 for D4R subtype decreases 14 times compared to 3, that of 6 decreases only three times compared to 4. The further elongation of the hydroxyalkyl chain, affording 7, is detrimental for D4R affinity and selectivity.
    The replacement of the piperidine ring of 1, 9, and 10 with a piperazine ring, affording 11, 13, and 14, respectively, causes a strong decrease in affinity, especially for D4R, suggesting that the introduction of a second basic function is detrimental for D4R affinity. However, when the terminal substituent is a phenyl ring, such a modification does not affect D4R affinity. Indeed, the piperazine derivative 12 shows a pKi value for D4R and selectivity over D2R and D3R comparable to those of its piperidine analog 8. The observation that the basicity of N4 of 12 is lower than that of N1 allows us to hypothesize that probably the N4 does not interfere with the interaction with the corresponding receptor site.

    Affinity for hM1–hM5 mAChR Subtypes

    From the results reported in Table 2, all the tested compounds show micromolar affinities for hM1–hM5 mAChR subtypes. Among the piperidine derivatives, the lowest affinities are those of compound 2, bearing a methyl group in the 4-position of the piperidine nucleus, while the highest affinities are shown by compound 10, bearing a phenethyl group in the same position. These results suggest that the reduction in the length of the alkyl chain of 1 (2 and 3) causes a decrease in affinity, while the presence of a pentyl chain (4) or an aromatic ring (9 and 10), unless it is directly linked to the piperidine ring (8), results in affinity values for all the hM1–hM5 mAChR subtypes similar to those of 1. Compounds 4–6 and 9 show selectivity profiles similar to that of 1, with higher affinity values for hM1, hM2, and hM4 with respect to the other two subtypes. The piperazine derivatives 11–14 exhibit affinity values for the mAChR subtypes lower than those of the corresponding piperidine analogs 1 and 8–10.

    Off-Target Binding Profiles

    Due to their interesting affinity and selectivity profiles for D4R, compounds 1, 9, and 12 were selected to be evaluated for their binding affinities at some off-targets, namely, α1a-, α1b-, and α1d-AR, β1- and β2-AR, σ1 receptor, DAT, and SERT. Compared to D4R, all the compounds show significantly lower affinities for all the investigated off-targets (Table 3).
    The observation that the piperidine analogues 1 and 9 bind σ1 receptor with high affinity values (pKi = 8.14 and 8.00, respectively) is not surprising, considering that the piperidine scaffold characterizes several potent σ1 receptor ligands(37) and numerous dopamine D4 receptor compounds have been reported to bind σ1 receptor with high affinity.(38) Interestingly, compared to 1 and 9, the piperazine derivative 12 shows significantly lower affinity for σ1 receptor (pKi = 6.57), and consequently, its selectivity binding profile for D4R is the best among the compounds of this series.

    Functional Activities at D4R and M1 and M4 mAChRs

    Due to their higher selectivity for D4R over D2R and D3R subtypes, compounds 1, 9, and 12 were selected to be evaluated for their functional activity at D4R. Compound 6 was included in this study because, unlike 1, it had previously been demonstrated to behave as a weak M1 and M4 mAChR antagonist.(20)
    Gi protein activation BRET shows that, compounds 1, 6, and 9 do not possess a partial agonist property for D4R (Table 4). When tested for an antagonist activity in D4R-Gi protein activation with a fixed amount (1 μM) of dopamine, compounds 1, 6, and 9 exhibited pIC50 and Imax values comparable to those of the selective D4R antagonist 15. On the contrary, compound 12 behaves as a D4R partial agonist (Emax = 48%) with a potency value higher than that of dopamine (pEC50 = 9.80) and as a partial antagonist (Imax = 41%) when tested in the presence of dopamine (pIC50 = 8.01).
    D4R β-arrestin2 recruitment result shows that compounds 1, 6, 9, and 12 have no agonist activity in agreement with the Gi protein activation. While they all show antagonist activity as expected, 1 and 12 have a higher pIC50 compared to 15. Of note, compounds 1, 6, 9, and 12 show Imax lower than 15, indicating good efficacies as antagonists in D4R mediated β-arrestin2 recruitment. In summary, for D4R activities, compounds 1, 6, and 9 behave as antagonists in both Gi activation and β-arrestin2 recruitment assays, while, interestingly, compound 12 shows a biased behavior, partially activating Gi protein and inhibiting β-arrestin2 recruitment.
    Compounds 1 and 6 had previously been reported to show functionally different behaviors at M1 and M4 mAChRs.(20) Therefore, the activity profiles of 9 and 12 were assessed at the same receptors for useful comparison. For G protein activation, analogously to 6,(20) compounds 9 and 12 show weak partial agonist activities. In the presence of carbachol (1 μM), they behave as partial antagonists. On the contrary, as previously reported,(20)1 behaves as a positive allosteric modulator in M1 mAChR Gq activation, while it exhibits partial agonism for M4 mAChR Gi activation. In β-arrestin2 recruitment assay, compounds 6, 9, and 12 show no agonist activity. In the presence of carbachol, they all show a nearly full to full antagonist activity, albeit a significantly lower potency compared to scopolamine. Therefore, in M1 and M4 mAChR activity, 6, 9, and 12 are all weak partial agonists with a low potency in G protein activation and antagonists with a low potency in β-arrestin2 recruitment.

    In Vitro ADME and in Vivo Pharmacokinetic Studies

    Compounds 9 and 12, selected for an in vitro ADME characterization, showed similar ADME profiles (Table 5). Both compounds are characterized by high kinetic solubility at pH 7.4 (477 and 520 μM for 9 and 12, respectively), moderate protein binding either in mouse plasma or in mouse brain tissue homogenates, and high apparent permeability without relevant efflux ratios (1.92 and 1.22 for 9 and 12, respectively) when tested in MDCKII-Mdr1 cell lines, therefore resulting in no P-gp substrates.
    Based on the overall results from the ADME in vitro assays and due to its interesting D4 selectivity profile, compound 12 was selected to investigate its brain penetration and systemic exposure following s.c. administration at 3 mg/kg in male CD-1 mice. From the results reported in Table 6, it emerges that compound 12 shows a relevant brain penetration (Kp = 4.35) and that, based on a Kpuu,brain of 4.05, there is no impact of efflux transporters but an even potential for active brain influx.(39,40) After 0.25 h from the treatment, exposures reach the mean maximum concentration either in plasma or brain (481 ng/mL and 2046 ng/g, respectively) (Figure 4). The exposures (AUClast) obtained were 518 and 2384 h·ng/mL in plasma and brain, respectively. Free concentration exposures (AUCulast), calculated by correcting total concentration (AUClast) for the free fraction in plasma or brain, resulted in 50.5 and 204 h·ng/mL, respectively.
    To rationalize the high clearance of the tested compound, a similarity-based predictive analysis was performed as recently published.(41) Briefly, the method is based on the MetaQSAR resource, which is a curated database of xenobiotic metabolism reactions collected by a meta-analysis of the recent specialized literature. The possible metabolites for 12 were then derived by considering the reported metabolites for the three most similar compounds included in the database: prilocaine (Tanimoto distance = 0.7), imipramine (0.67), and lidocaine (0.67). From their metabolic profile, three putative metabolic reactions can be figured out for 12: (a) hydroxylation of a Csp3 atom carrying a heteroatom; (b) oxidation of aryl moieties; and (c) N-glucuronidation of cyclic amines. Even though prilocaine and lidocaine include the metabolites deriving from the hydrolysis of their amide function, the quinolinone ring should be stable since lactams larger than four-membered rings are resistant to metabolic hydrolysis.(42)

    Molecular Modeling

    To rationalize the observed results, docking studies were performed using a D4R homology model based on the structure of the human dopamine D3R in complex with eticlopride as the template. Though a novel D2 structure in complex with risperidone has been recently released (PDB ID: 6c38),(43) the here-used D3 structure in complex with eticlopride still remains the more suitable template to generate the D4 homology model both for the markedly greater identity (78.39% vs 55.22% for D3/D4 and D2/D4 alignments, respectively) and for the more extended coverage (100% vs 91% for D3/D4 and D2/D4 alignments, respectively).
    Figure 5A shows the key interactions stabilizing the putative complex between D4R and 1, which is vastly dominated by the pivotal ion-pair that the charged ammonium head elicits with Asp115 reinforced by the H-bond with Tyr438. Again, the quinolinone ring elicits H-bonds with Cys119, Ser196, and Ser200 plus π–π stacking with Phe410, Phe411,and His414 as well as apolar contacts with Val116, Leu187, and Val193, which also contact the linker between the piperazine and quinolinone rings. The carbon skeleton of the piperazine ring and the alkyl tail stabilize an extended set of hydrophobic contacts with surrounding apolar residues such as Leu90, Phe91, Leu111, and Met112. The reported interactions can clearly rationalize the observed affinity values. In detail, the beneficial role of the alkyl chain is easily understood when considering the surrounding apolar residues, which are also well-suited to accommodate aromatic moieties as exemplified by 9, the benzyl group of which elicits a clear π–π stacking with Phe91 (see Figure 5B). The crucial role of these hydrophobic interactions is further confirmed by the negative effect exerted by the introduction of a hydroxyl function. Even though the binding site reveals some accessible H-bonding residues that might contact the hydroxyl function, such a polar contact would hamper the proper approach of the tail to the mentioned apolar residues with an overall negative impact. For example, Figure 5C shows that the hydroxyl function of 5 approaches Thr434 while inducing the complete loss of the mentioned apolar contacts. This finding emphasizes that the addition of a polar group can be deleterious, regardless of the elicited contacts, if it destabilizes a set of hydrophobic contacts involving extended lipophilic regions of the ligands.
    With a view to explaining the different affinity of the piperazine derivatives, the mentioned PM7-based semiempirical calculations were utilized to calculate the pKa values of the two nitrogen atoms by using a specific script included in VEGA. The obtained results reveal that in the alkyl derivative 11 the N4 atom is clearly more basic than the N1 atom [9.22 (N1) vs 10.22 (N4)], while the two nitrogen atoms possess comparable basicity in benzyl and phenethyl analogues [10.38 (N1) vs 10.55(N4) and 10.17 (N1) vs 10.11 (N4) for 13 and 14, respectively]. These results may indicate that the N4 protonated species is the one only involved in receptor recognition for 11, while both protonated forms can be involved for the benzyl and phenethyl analogues 13 and 14. What is more, docking results reveal that the shift of the protonated center on the N4 atom is detrimental for binding since it worsens the interactions involving the protonated N4 atom and impacts the pose of the quinolinone ring, thus destabilizing the key contacts involving it (results not shown). Thus, these results can rationalize the marked drop in affinity of 11 and the less pronounced decrease in affinity of 13 and 14 due to the possible simultaneous involvement of both protonated forms. Similar semiempirical calculations confirmed the vastly greater basicity of the N1 atom of 12 [10.19 (N1) vs 7.35 (N4)], while docking simulations reveal that the N-phenyl tail of 12 approaches some key residues in ECL2 such as Cys185 and Arg186 (Figure 5D). Even though other simulated ligands can also contact such residues, one may argue that the greater rigidity of the 4-phenylpiperazine moiety of 12 has a greater impact on the local folding of ECL2 explaining its biased behavior due to the role of the extracellular loop ECL2 in influencing the coupling with the signaling proteins as already reviewed.(44)
    To explain the observed selectivity of these derivatives toward D4R, Figure 6 reports the complex between D4R and 1 (as seen in Figure 5) in which the interacting residues are colored by conservation degree as derived from multiple alignments of D2R, D3R, and D4R computed by Clustal X.(45) The greatest differences among the three subtypes are detected in the region of TM3 surrounding the key and conserved aspartate residues. In detail, a key role should be played by the Leu111 and Met112 residues, which are replaced by Phe and Val residues in D2R and D3R. (i.e., Phe110 and Val111 as well as Phe106 and Val107 in D2R and D3R, respectively). Another relevant difference is seen in Leu90, which is replaced by a tryptophan residue in both D2R (Trp90) and D3R (Trp85). Collectively, Figure 6 underlines that the subpockets contacting the charged moieties and the quinolinone ring are highly conserved, while that harboring the apolar tail shows relevant differences among the monitored subtypes. In detail, these different residues should render the subpocket accommodating the alkyl tail in D2R and D3R narrower and more rigid compared to the corresponding D4R cavity and so unable to conveniently harbor bulky apolar groups, which, instead, beneficially contribute to the D4R affinity as before discussed. These findings can explain why the selectivity is roughly proportional to the steric hindrance of this hydrophobic tail and why the D2R vs D3R selectivity is almost always close to 1.0 since the relevant residues are conserved in these two subtypes.
    With a view to corroborating the obtained docking results, a correlative study was performed by using the docking scores computed by ReScore+ as well as a small set of representative physicochemical descriptors. For each ligand, the score averages as computed by considering all the 10 generated poses were utilized in order to account for the possible role of multiple binding modes as recently proposed.(46) The so-obtained best equation (eq 1) emphasizes the role of an optimal balance between lipophilic and polar moieties as encoded by lipole and the relevance of ionic contacts exerted by the protonated group as parametrized by the electrostatic scores (elect_mean).(1)
    Notably, the score averages perform better than the corresponding best values, thus suggesting that for rather flexible ligands such as those involved in the study the possibility of assuming slightly different binding modes beneficially contributes to the observed affinity reasonably due to positive entropic effects.

    Conclusion

    In summary, the bitopic M1 mAChR agonist 1 shows unexpectedly high D4R affinity and selectivity over D2R and D3R and behaves as an antagonist in both Gi activation and β-arrestin2 recruitment assays for D4R. Interestingly, the structure of 1 differs from those of other known selective D4R ligands, which are characterized by the presence of a properly substituted aromatic group at the 4-position of the piperidine ring (Figure 3). On the contrary, 1 bears a butyl aliphatic chain, indicating that not only π–π interactions but also hydrophobic interactions might be favorable for D4R affinity. The aliphatic chain of 1 can be successfully replaced by an aryl or alkylaryl chain (8–10) without affecting high D4R affinity and selectivity over D2R and D3R subtypes. From functional studies, it emerges that 9 behaves as a D4R antagonist and, unlike 1, is a weak partial antagonist at M1 mAChR. Compound 9 shows high D4R affinity and selectivity not only over the other D2-like subtypes but also over M1–M5 mAChRs. The substitution of the piperidine nucleus with a piperazine ring causes a sharp decrease in affinity, except for compound 12, which shows affinity for D4R and selectivity over D2R and D3R comparable to its piperidine analog 8. Compound 12 also displays high selectivity over M1–M5 mAChRs and other selected off-targets, namely, α1a-, α1b-, and α1d-AR, β1- and β2-AR, σ1 receptor, DAT, and SERT. Interestingly, in in vivo pharmacokinetic studies, a relevant brain penetration characterizes this compound. From functional studies, it shows a biased behavior, potently and partially activating Gi protein and inhibiting β-arrestin2 recruitment. Future studies with 12 might reveal mechanistically related behaviors and the interplay between G-protein- and β-arrestin-mediated signaling in D4R-related physiological effects.
    Docking simulations clearly confirm the key role of hydrophobic interactions, elicited by the alkyl chain, which are also responsible for the observed D4R selectivity. Moreover, they suggest that an affinitive ligand should possess an optimal balance between hydrophobic and hydrophilic portions as also confirmed by correlative studies. Finally, the biased behavior of 12, which contacts Cys185 and Arg186, emphasizes the role of the extracellular loop ECL2 in modulating the receptor activation.(44)
    Considering the renewed interest garnered by D4R, recently emerged as a potential therapeutic target for diseases such as cancer, drug addiction, and Parkinson’s disease in which D4R antagonists can attenuate l-DOPA-induced dyskinesias,(15) the selective D4R compounds 9 and especially 12 might help to better clarify the role played by this subtype in the above disorders and are good candidates for further evaluation in in vivo animal models for D4R-mediated pathologies.

    D2-like Radioligand Binding Assays

    Membranes were prepared from HEK293 cells stably expressing human D2, D3, or D4, grown in a 50:50 mix of DMEM and Ham’s F12 culture media, supplemented with 20 mM HEPES, 2 mM l-glutamine, 0.1 mM nonessential amino acids, 1× antibiotic/antimycotic, 10% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum, and 200 μg/mL hygromycin (Life Technologies, Grand Island, NY), and kept in an incubator at 37 °C and 5% CO2. Upon reaching 80–90% confluence, cells were harvested using premixed Earle’s Balanced Salt Solution (EBSS) with 5 mM EDTA (Life Technologies) and centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 10 min at 21 °C. The supernatant was removed, and the pellet was resuspended in 10 mL of hypotonic lysis buffer (5 mM MgCl2, 5 mM Tris, pH 7.4 at 4 °C) and centrifuged at 20,000 rpm for 30 min at 4 °C. The pellet was then resuspended in fresh EBSS buffer made from 8.7 g/L Earle’s Balanced Salts without phenol red (US Biological, Salem, MA) and 2.2 g/L sodium bicarbonate, pH to 7.4. A Bradford protein assay (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA) was used to determine the protein concentration, and membranes were diluted to 500 μg/mL and stored at −80 °C for later use.
    Radioligand competition binding experiments were conducted using thawed membranes. Test compounds were freshly dissolved in 30% DMSO and 70% H2O to a stock concentration of 10 mM or 100 μM. Each test compound was then diluted into 10 half-log serial dilutions using 30% DMSO vehicle. Previously frozen membranes were diluted in fresh EBSS to a 200 μg/mL (for D2 or D3) or 300 μg/mL (D4) stock for binding. Radioligand competition experiments were conducted in 96-well plates containing 300 μL of fresh EBSS buffer, 50 μL of diluted test compound, 100 μL of membranes (20 μg total protein for D2 or D3, 30 μg total protein for D4), and 50 μL of [3H]N-methylspiperone (0.4 nM final concentration; PerkinElmer). Nonspecific binding was determined using 10 μM (+)-butaclamol (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO), and total binding was determined with 30% DMSO vehicle. All compound dilutions were tested in duplicate, and the reaction was incubated for 1 h at room temperature. The reaction was terminated by filtration through a PerkinElmer Uni-Filter-96 GF/B, presoaked for 60 min in 0.5% polyethylenimine, using a Brandel 96-well plate Harvester manifold (Brandel Instruments, Gaithersburg, MD). The filters were washed three times with 3 mL (3 × 1 mL/well) of ice-cold binding buffer. Then 65 μL of PerkinElmer MicroScint 20 scintillation cocktail was added to each well, and filters were counted using a PerkinElmer MicroBeta microplate counter (calculated efficiency: 56%). IC50 values for each compound were determined from dose–response curves, and Ki values were calculated using the Cheng–Prusoff equation; Kd values for [3H]N-methylspiperone were determined via separate homologous competitive binding experiments. These analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism version 6.00 for Macintosh (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA). Ki values were determined from at least three independent experiments and are reported as mean ± SEM.

    Muscarinic Radioligand Binding Assays

    CHO cells stably transfected with the human muscarinic receptors (hM1–5) were grown in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum and 100 units/mL each of penicillin G and streptomycin (Sigma–Aldrich, Milano, Italy), 4 mM glutamine (Sigma–Aldrich, Milano, Italy), nonessential amino acid solution 100× (Sigma–Aldrich, Milano, Italy), and 50 μg/mL of G-418 (Gibco, Grand Iland, NY) at 37 °C under 5% CO2.(28)
    Cells were grown to confluence, trypsinized, and harvested with PBS buffer (25 mM sodium phosphate, containing 5 mM MgCl2 at pH 7.4). Cells were suspended in ice-cold PBS buffer and homogenized for 30 s using an Ultra-Turrax (setting 5) (IKA-Werke GmbH & Co. KG, Staufen, Germany). The homogenate was then pelleted by centrifugation (17,000g for 15 min at 4 °C), and the membranes were resuspended in the same buffer, rehomogenized in PBS with Ultra-Turrax, and stored at 80 °C. The protein content was measured according to the method of Bradford(47) using the Bio-Rad protein assay reagent (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Munchen, Germany).
    All binding assays were conducted in a 250 μL mixture of binding buffer (25 mM sodium phosphate pH 7.4 containing 5 mM MgCl2) in a 96-well plate (Sarstedt, Verona, Italy) and thawed membranes (25–70 μg/mL).(27) In ligand inhibition binding assays, 0.2 nM [3H]N-methylscopolamine chloride ([3H]NMS, PerkinElmer Life and Analytical Science, Monza, Italy) and the test ligands (concentrations ranging from 0.1 nM to 1 mM) were used. The samples were incubated for 2 h at room temperature. Nonspecific binding was determined in the presence of 10 μM atropine. In all assays, the total binding was always less than 10% of the total counts added. Incubation was terminated by rapid filtration through UniFilter GF/B plates (PerkinElmer Life and Analytical Science, Monza, Italy) using a FilterMate Cell Harvester (PerkinElmer Life and Analytical Science, Monza, Italy). Filters were than washed with ice-cold water and dried before the addition of 25 μL of scintillation liquid Microscint 20 (PerkinElmer Life and Analytical Science, Monza, Italy). The quantity of membrane-bound radioligand was measured by TopCount NXT Microplate Scintillation Counter (PerkinElmer Life and Analytical Science, Monza, Italy). Stock solutions of tested compounds were made in DMSO, and dilutions were usually made in the incubation buffer. DMSO at the highest concentration used (10%) had no effect on binding. Data (specific counts per minute) were analyzed using a four-parameter logistic equation in GraphPad Prism 5.02 (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA). Kd values for [3H]NMS were determined via separate saturation binding experiments, and the affinity estimates for test compounds (pKI) were calculated from the IC50 values using the Cheng and Prusoff correction. Data are presented as mean ± SEM of at least three experiments, each one performed in duplicate.

    BRET Assays

    BRET assays were performed in transiently transfected HEK293T cells with the constructs described later. For G protein activation assays, a pair between RLuc8 (renilla luciferase variant, donor) fused Gα subunit–mVenus (yellow fluorescent protein variant, acceptor) fused Gγ subunit was used as described.(20) For β-arrestin engagement assays, a pair between RLuc8 fused receptor and mVenus fused effector was used as described.(20) Briefly, a constant amount of plasmid cDNA (15 μg total) was transfected into HEK293T cells using polyethylenimine (PEI) (Sigma-Aldrich) in a 1:2 ratio (30 μg PEI) in 10 cm dishes. Cells were maintained in culture with DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS. Experiments were performed ∼48 h post-transfection. Cells were harvested, washed, and resuspended in PBS. Approximately 200,000 cells/well were distributed in 96-well plates, and 5 μM coelenterazine H was added to each well. Two min after the addition of luciferase, substrate ligands were added to each well. Typically, in antagonist mode, a ligand was added 15 min before the addition of agonist. The fluorescence (excitation at 500 nm and emission at 540 nm for 1 s recording in BRET) and luminescence (no filters, 1 s recording) were quantified 2 min after a ligand was added (Mithras LB 940, Berthold). In parallel, the BRET signal from the same batch of cells was determined by quantifying and calculating the ratio of the light emitted by mVenus (510–540 nm) over that emitted by RLuc8 (485 nm). The net BRET values were obtained by subtracting the background determined in cells expressing RLuc8 alone. Results are expressed as the BRET change produced by the corresponding drug. Data and statistical analyses were performed with Prism 5.01 (GraphPad Software). For Gi activation assays, receptor, Gαi1–RLuc8 (RLuc8 inserted at 91st amino acid), β1, and γ2–mVenus (mVenus fused at Nterminus) constructs were transfected. For Gq activation assays, receptor, Gαq–Rluc8 (RLuc8 inserted at 97th amino acid), β1, γ2–mVenus, and Ric8A constructs were transfected. For β-arrestin engagement assays, receptor and β-arrestin2–mVenus (mVenus fused at N-terminus) constructs were transfected. The cDNAs for human D4R, M1, and M4 mAChRs were obtained from www.cdna.org and tagged with signal peptide (SP),(48) followed by a Myc epitope tag (SM hereafter) for surface receptor expression detection. SM receptor constructs were used for the engagement assays. For the receptor–Rluc8 fusion constructs, RLuc8 cDNA sequence was fused to frame the C-terminus of D4R, M1, and M4 mAChRs.

    Computational Details

    The primary sequence of the human D4R was retrieved from Uniprot (Id: P21917, DRD4_HUMAN). The corresponding homology model was built by using Modeller9.12(49) based on the resolved structure of the human dopamine D3 receptor in complex with eticlopride as the template (PDB ID: 3PBL). Among the 10 generated structures, the best models were selected accordingly to both the scores computed by Modeller9.12 (i.e., DOPE and GA341 scores) and the percentages of residues falling in the allowed regions of the Ramachandran plot. The so-selected model was then minimized by keeping fixed the backbone atoms to preserve the predicted folding and underwent a set of structural checks to avoid the occurrence of wrong configurations, unpredicted gaps, cis-peptide bonds, and colliding residues. The so-refined model reveals a percentage of residues falling in the allowed regions of the Ramachandran plot equal to 98.8% (90.3% within the core regions), while the residues within the allowed regions of the chi space correspond to 95.1%. The ligands were simulated in the protonated state and their conformational space was explored by MonteCarlo simulations, which generated 1000 minimized conformers for each ligand. The so-obtained lowest energy structure was then optimized by PM7 semiempirical calculations(50) and finally underwent docking simulations. Docking simulations were performed by PLANTS(51) focusing the search on a 13 Å radius sphere around Asp115, which is involved in a key ion-pair with the protonated ligand. For each simulated molecule, 10 poses were generated and ranked by the ChemPLP scoring function with a speed equal to 1. All computed results were minimized and rescored by using ReScore+ as previously described.(52)

        Structural Effects of Some Relevant Missense Mutations on the MECP2-DNA Binding: A MD Study Analyzed by Rescore+, a Versatile Rescoring Tool of the VEGA ZZ Program
        Pedretti, Alessandro; Granito, Cinzia; Mazzolari, Angelica; Vistoli, Giulio
        Molecular Informatics (2016), 35 (8-9), 424-433CODEN: MIONBS; ISSN:1868-1743. (Wiley-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA)
        DNA methylation plays key roles in mammalian cells and is modulated by a set of proteins which recognize sym. methylated nucleotides. Among them, the protein MECP2 shows multifunctional roles repressing and/or activating genes by binding to both methylated and unmethylated regions of the genome. The interest for this protein markedly increased from the observation that its mutations are the primary cause of Rett syndrome, a neurodevelopmental disorder which causes mental retardation in young females. Thus, the present study is aimed to investigate the effects of some of these known pathogenic missense mutations (i.e. R106Q, R106W, R111G, R133C and R133H) on the MECP2 folding and DNA binding by mol. dynamics simulations. The effects of the simulated mutations are also parameterized by using a here proposed new tool, named Rescore+, implemented in the VEGA ZZ suite of programs, which calcs. a set of scoring functions on all frames of a trajectory or on all complexes contained in a database thus allowing an easy rescoring of results coming from MD or docking simulations. The obtained results revealed that the reported loss of the MECP2 function induced by the simulated mutations can be ascribed to both stabilizing and destabilizing effect on DNA binding. The study confirms that MD simulations are particularly useful to rationalize and predict the mutation effects offering insightful information for diagnostics and drug design.
