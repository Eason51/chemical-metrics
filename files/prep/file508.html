    Abstract

    The paracaspase MALT1 has gained increasing interest as a target for the treatment of subsets of lymphomas as well as autoimmune diseases, and there is a need for suitable compounds to explore the therapeutic potential of this target. Here, we report the optimization of the in vivo potency of pyrazolopyrimidines, a class of highly selective allosteric MALT1 inhibitors. High doses of the initial lead compound led to tumor stasis in an activated B-cell-like (ABC) diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) xenograft model, but this compound suffered from a short in vivo half-life and suboptimal potency in whole blood. Guided by metabolism studies, we identified compounds with reduced metabolic clearance and increased in vivo half-life. In the second optimization step, masking one of the hydrogen-bond donors of the central urea moiety through an intramolecular interaction led to improved potency in whole blood. This was associated with improved in vivo potency in a mechanistic model of B cell activation. The optimized compound led to tumor regression in a CARD11 mutant ABC-DLBCL lymphoma xenograft model.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Targeting the proteolytic activity of MALT1 (mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue lymphoma translocated gene 1, paracaspase-1, HsPCA-Ia(1)) has emerged as an attractive strategy for the treatment of lymphomas with oncogenic activation of the MALT1-dependent signaling pathway as well as autoimmune diseases.(2−5) However, there are also potential concerns associated with this strategy. On the one hand, protease-inactivated MALT1 knock-in mice display reduced regulatory T cell (Treg) numbers, which is thought to drive inflammation in multiple organs and autoimmunity seen in these animals.(6−10) The effect of MALT1 protease inhibition on reducing Treg numbers has, on the other hand, recently been suggested to potentiate efficacy of check-point immunotherapy in cancer.(11,12) Overall, the therapeutic potential of MALT1 protease inhibitors remains an open question and there is a need for suitable compounds to explore the in vivo efficacy and safety profile of MALT1 protease inhibition. Several classes of covalent(13−17) and noncovalent allosteric(17−23) MALT1 protease inhibitors have been used to probe the effects of pharmacological MALT1 inhibition. In particular, the covalent inhibitor MI-2 led to decreased tumor growth of ABC-DLBCL xenographs(14)in vivo. Mepazine was the first allosteric inhibitor to be reported and demonstrated in vivo effects in ABC-DLBCL lymphomas,(18) primary effusion lymphomas,(24) and in a model of multiple sclerosis.(25) Recently, we reported the highly potent and selective allosteric inhibitor MLT-827 which has been used in several in vitro studies, contributing to validate the key role of MALT1 in T cells,(22) myeloid cells,(26,27) and endothelial cells.(28) The low solubility of this compound motivated further optimization, as described in the accompanying paper by Pissot-Soldermann et al.(29) This led to the advanced derivative 1 (MLT-231, Figure 1), which induced tumor stasis in a CD79 mutant ABC-DLBCL lymphoma xenograft model. However, the required high dose of 100 mg kg–1 BID and the moderate efficacy illustrated the limitations to achieve sustained MALT1 inhibition in vivo thus preventing progression of this series to clinical development. Here, we report on further optimization of this scaffold, to achieve high in vivo potency and efficacy at low doses.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The high dose of MLT-231 needed for efficacy might result from two features, namely, a short in vivo half-life (2 h in mouse) and a suboptimal potency exemplified by the high circulating levels needed to achieve efficacy.
    A common strategy to prolong the in vivo half-life is to reduce the clearance. While the in vivo clearance of MLT-231 is only moderate in mice and rats (15 and 25 mL min–1 kg–1, respectively), further reduction may increase the residence time of the compound in circulation. The significant metabolic turnover of MLT-231 in liver microsomes in vitro (42 and 82 μL min–1 mg–1 for rat and mouse, respectively) suggested a contribution of metabolism to the clearance mechanism. In addition, the higher metabolic turnover of MLT-231 in human microsomes (114 μL min–1 mg–1) suggested that reducing metabolic clearance would be needed to identify compounds with potential for clinical development. Metabolism of MLT-231 was examined following in vitro incubation with either rat or human liver microsomes (see the Supporting Information for detailed results). Three major metabolites m1, m2, and m4 could be identified from rat liver microsomes (Figure 2). Metabolite m1 is formed by aromatic C-hydroxylation at the 5-position of the pyrazolopyrimidine moiety and subsequent O-glucuronidation. Oxidation and intramolecular cyclization lead to metabolite m2 while N-oxidation at the morpholine moiety leads to metabolite m4. The metabolism profile identified from human liver microsomes was very similar. Two notable differences were the lower production of m1 and m2 in human liver microsomes and the increased level of m3, which is formed by O-glucuronidation of m2. For both rat and human, intramolecular cyclization and N-oxidation at the morpholine moiety appeared to be major pathways.
    To determine the relevance of these transformations in vivo, the metabolism of MLT-231 was analyzed in blood, urine, bile, kidney, and liver after i.v. administration to bile duct cannulated rats(30,31) (see the Supporting Information for full details). Metabolite m1 was the major component in bile consisting of more than 80% of the total peak area. Metabolite m2 was the most abundant metabolite in blood while m4 could be observed at significant levels in urine. These data confirmed that the major metabolic soft spots identified by in vitro incubations with microsomes were predictive for the in vivo situation. A tentative calculation of the mass balance for MLT-231 suggested low levels of unchanged parent in body fluids and tissues indicating that MLT-231 is predominantly metabolized prior to excretion.
    Based on this information, we embarked on modifying the compound to attenuate the major metabolic pathways. We envisioned that replacing the nitrogen atom of the morpholine by a carbon atom would not only prevent N-oxidation but also reduce the electron density of the pyrazolopyrimidine moiety, which could slow down the oxidative cyclization reaction. To quickly test this hypothesis, we made the C7-methyl analog 2 (Table 1). Although 2 was significantly less potent than MLT-231 on target, the in vitro metabolic turn-over of 2 in rat and human microsomes was significantly reduced compared to MLT-231. To recover the lost potency, we introduced the bulkier isopropyl group, which led to compound 3 with comparable biochemical and cellular potency as MLT-231 while the low microsomal turn-over was maintained. We could further improve biochemical and cellular potency by introducing an additional triazole ring on the aminopyridine group, leading to compound 4. We then compared the in vivo clearance and disposition properties of 4 with MLT-231 in a rat PK cassette study (Table 1). In agreement with the reduced in vitro metabolic turnover, the in vivo clearance of 4 was reduced compared to MLT-231 which in turns led to a significantly longer half-life of 5.4 h.

Thus, the metabolic pathways observed for compound 4 are consistent with the metabolism of MLT-231. Low levels of these metabolites were observed with 91% parent compound 4 remaining in both species versus 58 and 39% for MLT-231 in human and rat microsomes, respectively. The lower metabolic turn-over of the biotransformation study observed for 4 is in line with Clint values depicted in Tables 1 and 2. With the good in vitro/in vivo correlation for MLT-231 and consistency of major metabolic pathways of MLT-231 and 4, the Clint values were used for further compound optimization.

    Next, we addressed the potency of the compound, which likely also contributed to the high dose needed for efficacy. Despite a high biochemical (IC50 = 9 nM) and cellular (IC50 = 0.18 μM) on target potency, high total blood exposure was required for efficacy of MLT-231in vivo (total exposure of 23.6 μM at peak and 0.7 μM at trough for T cell-dependent B cell responses in mice, see accompanying paper).(29) While this may be in part accounted for by the high plasma protein binding (∼99%) of the compound, we envisioned that an assay measuring potency in a more physiologically relevant system would better predict in vivo potency. Because target cells, such as T and B lymphocytes, are present in blood, we established an assay measuring pathway inhibition in whole blood. In fact, human whole blood assays have long been used to optimize compounds for auto-immune diseases and proved to be excellent predictors for clinical efficacy.(33−35) Using a similar paradigm to the cellular assay, we could measure IL-2 production in 90% human whole blood after stimulation with a combination of PMA with an anti-CD28 antibody, which leads to full-dependency on MALT1 proteolytic function for IL-2 up-regulation.(22) In this assay, MLT-231 displayed potency of only 9 μM, hence a >800-fold loss from the biochemical potency and ∼50-fold loss from the cellular potency. While 4 was slightly more potent in whole blood (2.5 μM), the shift from the biochemical assay remained in the same range (>800).
    Compound 4 displayed a high log P value of 4. Reducing binding to blood components including plasma proteins by lowering lipophilicity is often a strategy to improve translation of biochemical potency to whole blood.(34,36) We therefore prepared a set of derivatives with less lipophilic side chains in the 7-position of the pyrazolopyrimidine moiety (Table 2). The potency shift from biochemical to whole blood potency (ratio WB/MALT1 IC50) was calculated to monitor the effects of these changes.
    The slightly less lipophilic C7-cyclopropyl derivative 5 was in the same biochemical and cellular potency range as 4 but did not lead to significant improvement of potency in whole blood. To further reduce lipophilicity, we introduced a tetrahydropyran ring leading to compound 6, which showed a modest 3-fold loss of biochemical potency compared to 5. The reduction of log P from 4 for compound 4 to 3 for compound 6 was accompanied by a reduction of plasma protein binding from greater than 99% for compound 4 to 98.8 and 97.7% for compound 6 in human and rat plasma, respectively. However, compound 6 showed a similar or higher potency shift leading to very weak activity in whole blood. These data indicated that reducing lipophilicity is not sufficient to lower the potency shift and improve performance of the compounds in whole blood. In addition, compounds 5–8 display higher in vitro metabolic turn over than compound 4, in particular in rat liver microsomes, suggesting that the more polar C7 substituents may represent new metabolic weak spots or influence other metabolism pathways.
    Another strategy to reduce binding to blood components is to remove hydrogen-bond donors (HBD) and/or acceptors (HBA) which can interact with polar groups found in plasma proteins and other biomolecules.(37) The central urea of our scaffold constitutes a HBD–HBA–HBD motif which could contribute to binding to blood components. On one hand, previous SAR described in the accompanying paper(29) showed that modification of the NH linked to the pyridine led to complete loss of potency. On the other hand, the NH linked to the pyrazolopyrimidine could be replaced by a carbon with a moderate 4-fold loss of potency indicating that it may not contribute strongly to binding to MALT1. This potency loss may be due to the increased flexibility of the amide compared to the urea, which is fixed in the bioactive conformation. With this in mind, we envisioned that engaging the NH linked to the pyrazolopyrimidine in an intramolecular H-bond could maintain the bioactive conformation while shielding the HBD character. This was attempted by introducing a methoxymethyl-group in the C-7 position of the pyrazolopyrimidine moiety. Despite a 6-fold loss of biochemical potency, compound 9 showed a similar whole blood potency as compound 4. The potency shift was thus reduced to 168-fold. The loss of biochemical potency may be due to the lack of branched substituent in C-7 but the reduced potency shift suggested that this methoxymethyl-group reduced unspecific binding of the compound. Introducing an additional methyl group on the side chain led to isomers 10 and 11 (MLT-985), which retained this moderate potency shift and led, for the most active isomer MLT-985, to a high whole blood potency of 500 nM. Importantly, this methoxymethyl substituent does not impact the in vitro clearance of the compound which remained below 25 μL min–1 mg–1 in human, mouse and rat liver microsomes suggesting that metabolism is not significantly increased. When the position of the methoxy group was shifted by one carbon atom as in compounds 7 and 8, the reduced potency shift was not observed. This may be attributed to a less favorable internal hydrogen bond that would lead to the formation of a pseudo seven-membered ring versus a pseudo six-membered ring for MLT-985.(38) Because 7, 8, and MLT-985 have similar lipophilicity, the internal H-bond may be the major contributing factor to the reduced potency shift.
    To confirm this experimentally, we used NMR spectroscopy to obtain a quantitative value for hydrogen-bond strength for compounds 7 and MLT-985 (Figure 3). This can be achieved by comparing the 1H NMR chemical shift of individual protic hydrogens in DMSO and CDCl3. This chemical shift change can be empirically correlated to hydrogen-bond acidity.(39) The acidity (A) can be calculated using the equation A = 0.0066 + 0.133 Δδ where Δδ is the chemical shift change (Δδ = δ(DMSO) – δ(CDCl3)). A can be used as a quantitative assessment of intramolecular hydrogen bonding and for NH groups, A < 0.05 indicates that the NH is part of a intramolecular H-bond.(40) Indeed, both intramolecular hydrogen bonding and hydrogen bonding between solute and polar solvent have a strong electron deshielding effect on its resonance. Therefore, if a strong intramolecular hydrogen bond is formed, the chemical shift of the corresponding proton will not change significantly between polar (DMSO) and nonpolar (CDCl3) solvents. The small shift (Figure 3) and measured acidity of 0.037 for NH11 in MLT-985 indicated a strongly hydrogen-bonded proton, whereas a larger shift was observed for NH11 in compound 7 corresponding to an acidity of 0.141. For both compounds, NH16 displayed a large shift. This confirmed that only NH11 of MLT-985 is involved in a strong intramolecular hydrogen bond.
    Having identified this driver of whole blood potency, we prepared compound 12, named MLT-943,(41) which contains the 2-(trifluoromethyl)pyridin-4-amine substituent of MLT-231 in R2 (Table 2). MLT-943 displayed similar, albeit slightly reduced potency, than MLT-985 in the biochemical and cellular assays. Importantly, the potency shift remains similar which leads to a reasonable whole blood potency of 0.8 μM and in vitro metabolism is not affected.

    DMPK Properties of MLT-985 and MLT-943

    Based on their promising profiles, the DMPK properties of MLT-985 and MLT-943 were further characterized. MLT-985 and MLT-943 displayed high permeability as measured in the Caco-2 assay (Papp A-B [10–6 cm/s] = 12.8 and 45.2, respectively)(42) and showed no inhibition of the cytochrome P450 enzymes CYP3A4, CYP2C9 and CYP2D6 (IC50 > 20 μM). Their moderate thermodynamic solubility (0.004 g L–1 at pH 6.8) is comparable to that of MLT-231 and an intrinsic property of this pyrazolopyrimidine scaffold.(29)MLT-985 showed a promising in vivo pharmacokinetic profile in both rats and mice (dosed at 1 mg kg–1 intravenously (IV) and at 3 mg kg–1 orally (PO)), characterized by low clearance, a terminal half-life (t1/2) of 3.7 h in mice and 8.5 h in rats, sustained micromolar exposure levels in plasma after oral dosing, and high oral bioavailability (Table 3). In rats, MLT-943 displayed a similar profile. In mice, the compound showed a moderate clearance, a shorter half-life of 1.2 h and a moderate oral bioavailability of 50%. As our objective was to characterize the effects of these optimized compounds in mice xenograft models, we selected MLT-985 for further profiling because of its superior PK profile in this species.

    Cellular and Selectivity Profiling of MLT-985

    With its promising profile, MLT-985 was selected for further characterization. We sought to assess the activity of MLT-985 in cellular models of DLBCL.(14,18) Aberrant activation of the NF-κB signaling pathway by mutations in the B-cell-receptor or the CARD11/BCL10/MALT1 (CBM) complex is a hallmark of the ABC-subtype of DLBCL.(43,44) We therefore profiled MLT-985 in ABC-DLBCL cell lines harboring either activating mutations in CARD11 (OCI-Ly3), CD79A (OCI-Ly10) or CD79B (TMD8) and used BJAB germinal-center (GCB) DLBCL cells as a counter-screening model. Increasing concentrations of MLT-985 inhibited the growth of CARD11 mutant OCI-Ly3 cells grown in soft agar, but not of the BJAB cells (Figure 4A). Importantly, in this assay MLT-985 showed an approximately 10-fold lower EC50 than MLT-231(29) which is consistent with the improved potency in whole blood. Treating ABC-DLBCL cells for 24 h with MLT-985 inhibited MALT1 protease activity in a concentration dependent manner, as evidenced by blockade of cleavage of MALT1 substrates BCL10, CYLD and RELB (Figure 4B,C). The inhibition of the CBM complex by MLT-985 also led to decreased IRF4 levels. Potent cellular activity of MLT-985 was further corroborated in a NF-κB reporter gene assay, a Meso Scale Discovery platform-based assay to assess inhibition of BCL10 cleavage, and a flow-cytometry assay for the detection of reactive oxygen species (ROS) (Figure 4D). The impact of MLT-985 on cellular ROS was assessed based on previous findings of higher baseline ROS levels in BCR-DLBLC models such as OCI-Ly3, as compared with models of the OxPhos-DLBCL subset.(45) Taken together, MLT-985 demonstrated activity in cellular ABC-DLBCL models that is consistent with potent and selective inhibition of MALT1.
    MLT-985 showed excellent selectivity against a panel of 23 human proteases (all IC50 values > 100 μM, Table S1) including cysteine proteases such as Caspase 3 and Cathepsin B, C, K, L and S which can be attributed to its allosteric mode of action.(21) In addition, it showed high selectivity when tested against a standard safety panel of 25 enzymes, receptors, ion channels and transporters (IC50 values > 10 μM, see Supporting Information). MLT-985 did not inhibit the hERG channel (IC50 > 30 μM; dofetilide binding assay).

    In Vivo Pharmacology

    To verify that the whole blood in vitro potency was predictive of in vivo potency, we established a short-term efficacy model that would allow for testing several compounds. We used a well-established rat model of T-dependent B cell response monitoring IgM-producing cells.(41) Four days after immunization with sheep red blood cells (SRBC), IgM producing B cells are assessed by an ex vivo plaque forming assay (PFA) allowing the counting of cells able to lyse sheep erythrocytes through their release of SRBC-selective IgM.(46,47) We used this model to compare the in vivo efficacy and potency of compounds 4 and MLT-985, which have similar biochemical potency (IC50 = 0.003 μM) but differ in their whole blood potency (IC50 = 2.5 vs 0.5 μM). Because 4 has a shorter in vivo half-life than MLT-985 in rats (5.4 h vs 8.5 h), we used twice daily (BID) dosing for 4 and once daily (QD) dosing for MLT-985. As shown in Figure 5, both compounds significantly reduced SRBC-induced IgM producing cells in a dose-dependent manner. For 4, full inhibition was achieved at 10 mg kg–1 BID and 3 mg kg–1 BID could be considered as the dose required for about 50% inhibition (ED50). These doses corresponded to peak blood levels of about 7.6 and 1.5 μM, respectively, and trough blood levels of about 3.3 and 0.4 μM, respectively. A ten-fold higher potency was observed for MLT-985 that achieved full inhibition at 3 mg kg–1 QD (with peak and trough blood levels of about 2.3 and 1 μM, respectively) and ED50 at 0.3 mg kg–1 QD (with peak and trough blood levels of about 0.13 and 0.05 μM, respectively). MLT-943 performed similarly in this model suggesting that this compound is suited for rat in vivo studies.(41)
    Next, we studied the pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamics (PK/PD) relationship of MLT-985 in the CD79B-mutant ABC-DLBCL xenograft model TMD8. After a single-dose at 30 mg kg–1 p.o., MLT-985 exhibited rapid absorption, which was corroborated by a rapid decrease of IL-10 production in the tumor (Figure 6A). MLT-985 achieved a stronger effect on tumor IL-10 at a lower dose and exposure in this model compared to MLT-231,(29) confirming the increased in vivo potency of MLT-985. We then tested the efficacy of MLT-985 in the CARD11-mutant ABC-DLBCL xenograft model OCI-Ly3. We treated tumor-bearing mice with MLT-985 in an oral QD or BID schedule at 30 mg kg–1. At day 49, MLT-985 given at 30 mg kg–1 QD led to a nonsignificant tumor growth inhibition (T/C = 32%), while BID dosing resulted in a statistically significant tumor regression of −20% (Figure 6B). The tumors continued to respond to treatment from day 49 to day 53 with the MLT-985 QD schedule leading to approximately stasis, and the BID schedule leading to a −50% regression. Both regimens were well tolerated based on body weight (data not shown). Notably, the antitumor effect correlated with compound exposure and tumor PD modulation (Figure 6C,D).

    Chemistry

    An efficient synthetic route to access compound 2–6 and 9 is shown in Scheme 1. First, ethyl 2-(ethoxymethylene)-3-oxoate intermediates A were obtained from the corresponding commercially available β-ketoesters by reaction with triethyl orthoformiate in acetic anhydride at 135 °C. These were then cyclo-condensed with 3-chloro-1H-pyrazol-5-amine(48) in EtOH at reflux temperature to give the ethyl pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyrimidine-6-carboxylates B. After saponification of the ester, the corresponding carboxylic acids C were converted to an intermediate isocyanate by thermal curtius rearrangement in the presence of DPPA and Et3N in dioxane, which was trapped in situ with either 2-(trifluoromethyl)pyridin-4-amine to give compounds 2 and 3, or with aminopyridine 13 to give compounds 4-6 and 9. Aminopyridine 13 was prepared in two steps from commercially available 2,3-dichloro-5-nitropyridine by aromatic nucleophilic substitution with 2H-1,2,3-triazole followed by reduction of the nitro-group by SnCl2 in HCl/MeOH.
    Examples 7, 8, 10, and MLT-985 were prepared in an analogous manner as described in Scheme 2. First, β-ketoesters A were prepared by condensation of 3-(tert-butoxy)-3-oxopropanoic acid dianion with the imidazolides of corresponding commercially available carboxylic acids. In a one pot reaction, A were then converted to tert-butyl pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyrimidine-6-carboxylates B by forming the enamine intermediate with dimethylformamide-dimethylacetal at 120 °C followed by cyclo-condensation with 3-chloro-1H-pyrazol-5-amine in EtOH at reflux temperature. After acidic cleavage of the tert-butyl ester, carboxylic acids C were converted to an intermediate isocyanate by thermal Curtius rearrangement in the presence of DPPA and Et3N in dioxane, which was trapped in situ with aminopyridine 13 to give compounds 7, 8,10, and MLT-985. As 3-methoxy-2-methylpropanoic acid was used as racemate, compounds 7 and 8 were obtained by chiral separation at the final step.

    Conclusions

    Jump To
    The identification of highly potent and selective MALT1 inhibitors with high in vivo efficacy is critical to explore the therapeutic potential of this target. Starting from an advanced lead MLT-231, we designed compounds with improved in vivo efficacy through two essential optimization steps. First, metabolism studies identified the 7-aminosubstituent as the main driver of metabolism prompting us to replace it with alkyl substituents. The resulting compound 4 displayed reduced in vitro metabolism as well as reduced clearance and increased half-life in rats. In the second step of the optimization, the potency in a whole blood assay measuring MALT1 pathway activation was optimized. This was achieved by introducing an intramolecular H-bond via a pseudo six-membered ring between the pyrazolopyrimidine NH and a newly introduced methoxy-group. The resulting MLT-985 showed improved potency in whole blood as well as increased in vivo efficacy in a rat model of B-cell activation. It also showed improved potency in blocking proliferation of MALT1 dependent ABC-DLBCL cell lines while control GCB-DLBCL were not affected. As a result, increased PD modulation in a CD79 mutant ABC-DLBCL at lower exposures were observed. This optimized compound is the first MALT1 inhibitor to demonstrate tumor regression in a xenograft model of ABC-DLBCL lymphoma. It may therefore be useful to further explore the therapeutic potential of MALT1 inhibitors. Recently, we reported long term toxicology studies on MLT-943.(41) These data suggest that prolonged MALT1 protease inhibition can lead to a progressive IPEX-like pathology and highlights the risks and challenges associated with the chronic administration of MALT1 inhibitors in clinical settings.

    Human Whole Blood IL-2 Assay

    Human whole blood was obtained from healthy volunteers by venipuncture at the Novartis Basel Health care unit (around 75 mL in 10 Monovetten Na-Heparin 7.5 mL-tubes). Age of donors ranged between 22 and 45 years. Both genders were included. Blood was kept at room temperature, protected from light, with gentle rotation for up to 5 h before use. All experimental steps performed according to “BL2” Novartis HSE guidelines. Blood from the collecting tubes was pooled into two 50 ml Falcon tubes and filtered using a 100 μm (or 70 μm)-cell strainer before transfer to new 50 mL tubes. In 2 mL-deep well plates, 180 μL of X–VIVO medium was added in column (one column for one compound to profile). Compound dilution was performed in a U-bottom plate, 90 μL of DMSO was added to the first row and 40 μL in all other wells. Then, 10 μL of compound was added to the first well of each column (10× predilution). Compounds were diluted by transferring 20 μL from the first row into the second row and repeating the process 4 times (3×-serial dilutions). 20 μL of prediluted compounds/DMSO (from U-bottom plate) were transferred to the 180 μL X–VIVO in the deep-well plate. The two filtrated blood tubes were pooled into a 100 mL reservoir and homogenized , prior to dispension into the plate. Then, 500 μL of whole blood was added to a new deep well plate and 55 μL of the X–VIVO/compound mixes were added to the blood. The deep well plates were sealed and preincubated for 1 h at 37 °C with shaking (∼600 rpm). The co-stimulation in X–VIVO medium was prepared (11×-concentrated): PMA (1100 ng/mL) /anti-CD28 (3300 ng/mL). After the preincubation time each blood sample was mixed 10-fold and 200 μL of the blood/compound mixes in duplicates were transferred to a new flat bottom plate. 20 μL of diluted stimulation was added to the blood to obtain final concentrations of PMA (100 ng/mL) /anti-CD28 (300 ng/mL). Two wells in duplicates remained unstimulated. Incubation was performed overnight at 37 °C + 5% CO2. Then, the blood plates were spun for 10 min at 470 g and 100 μL of the resulting serum was transferred, well by well, to new U-bottom plates. The plate was measured for IL-2 levels using MSD, according to the manufacturer’s protocol. For delayed measurement, a lid was sealed and the plate was frozen at −80 °C.

    Rat Sheep Red Blood Cell Model

    Female albino OFA rats (IFFA CREDO, France) weighing 150–160 g were housed and used for experimentation under strict adherence to the Swiss law for animal protection (ad libitum standard diet [NAFAG890; Provimi Kliba SA]; IVC cages at 21 ± 2 °C and 55 ± 10% relative humidity; light from 6h00 to 18h00). The rats were randomized in treatment-groups (n = 5 each) and oral treatments started 2 days prior to immunization with 2 × 108/mL SRBCs (0.5 mL via tail vein injection). Daily treatments continued for another 4 days and, on day 4 post-immunization, all rats were euthanized by CO2 inhalation and the spleens were collected together with blood samples. The impact of treatments on the ability of B cells to produce SRBC-specific IgM was assessed using a classical ex vivo plaque forming assay (PFA) (Jerne and Nordin, 1963). For that, single spleen cell suspension (30 × 106/mL) of each immunized animal was prepared and plated with fresh antigen (SRBC, 40 × 108/mL) onto the soft agar (50 μL splenocytes + 50 μL SRBC). After an incubation for 60 min at 37 °C, 1.4 mL guinea pig complement 10% (Harlan) was added and incubation continued for another 60 min at 37 °C. During this time, B cells produce IgM antibodies specific for the SRBC, which then bind to SRBC membrane antigens and produce complement mediated lysis of the plated SRBC and subsequent formation of plaques (i.e., clear areas of hemolysis around each antibody forming cell). The numbers of hemolytic plaques, corresponding to the number of IgM producing cells in each spleen, were then counted manually. A mean number of hemolytic plaques was calculated for each treatment group (n = 5 each) and a percent inhibition versus vehicle controls was calculated for each treatment group. Cyclosporin A (CsA) is used as a reference compound at 6 mg kg–1 p.o. QD and classically demonstrates 76–98% inhibition (mean 85.1%, ± SD 5.7) of plaque forming cells compared to the vehicle treated control group.

    Xenograft Studies

    Subcutaneous tumors were induced by injecting TMD8 cells (5 × 106, 50% Matrigel) or OCI-Ly3 cells (1 × 107, 50% Matrigel) into 6–8 weeks old female SCID-bg mice (Taconic). For OCI-Ly3, developing xenografts were propagated by fragment transplantation. For PK/PD studies, treatment was started after randomizing mice into groups of n = 2–3 for vehicle and dose groups when the average tumor size had reached approximately 500 mm3. For efficacy studies, treatment was initiated when tumors reached a mean tumor volume of around 350 mm3. At this point, animals were randomized into different treatment groups based on similar tumour size and body weight. Tumor size was measured twice a week with a caliper. Tumor volume was calculated using the formula (length × width) 3 π/6 and expressed in mm3. Data are presented as mean ± SEM. Statistical analysis was performed on ΔTVol by comparing the treatment groups to the vehicle control group (d49) by Kruskal–Wallis followed by Dunn’s post hoc test. At indicated time points post last dose blood samples were taken and tumors were excised and snap frozen in liquid nitrogen. Concentrations of MLT-985 in plasma and tumor were determined using ultra-high-pressure liquid chromatography/tandem MS.

Subsequent optimization for potency and PK properties lead to the discovery of ((S)-5-(methoxymethyl)-7-(1-methyl-1H-indol-2-yl)-2-(trifluoromethyl)-4,7-dihydropyrazolo[1,5-a]pyrimidin-6-yl)((S)-2-(3-methylisoxazol-5-yl)pyrrolidin-1-yl)methanone I, with an acceptable PK profile in preclin. species and potent efficacy in the preclin. rabbit atrial effective refractory period (AERP) model.
