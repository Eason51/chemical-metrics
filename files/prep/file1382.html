    Abstract

    Advancements in recanalization therapies have rendered reperfusion injury an important challenge for stroke management. It is essential to work toward effective therapeutics that protect the ischemic brain from reperfusion injury. Here, we report a new concept of neuroprognostic agents, which combine molecular diagnostic imaging and targeted neuroprotection for treatment of reperfusion injury after stroke. These neuroprognostic agents are inflammation-targeted gadolinium compounds conjugated with nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). Our results demonstrated that gadolinium-based MRI contrast agents conjugated with NSAIDs suppressed the increase in cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) levels, ameliorated glial activation, and neuron damage that are phenotypic for stroke by mitigating neuroinflammation, which prevented reperfusion injury. In addition, this study showed that the neuroprognostic agents are promising T1 molecular MRI contrast agents for detecting precise reperfusion injury locations at the molecular level. Our results build on this new concept of neuroprognostics as a novel management strategy for ischemia-reperfusion injury, combining neuroprotection and molecular diagnostics.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Cerebral ischemic stroke accounts for almost 80% of stroke cases and is a leading cause of death and disability worldwide.(1−3) Reperfusion, or restoration of cerebral blood flow, is the desired goal of acute stroke treatment. Currently, intravenous thrombolytic therapy via a tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) and endovascular mechanical thrombectomy (MT) are the two main reperfusion strategies after acute ischemic stroke.(4,5) These treatments are associated with complications after recanalization of the involved cerebral vessels, of which reperfusion injury is among the most serious. No therapies for reperfusion injury are currently available, and there remains a need to develop neuroprotective therapeutics to prevent reperfusion injury after recanalization for acute stroke.
    Reperfusion injury occurs via complex biochemical mechanisms, including oxidative stress, inflammation, free radical damage, and apoptosis.(6−8) Neuroinflammation is predominantly implicated in ischemia-reperfusion injury after recanalization for acute stroke.(9−11) Damage-inflammatory responses are accompanied by the overexpression of several inflammatory factors, such as cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α, inducible nitric oxide (iNOS), and interleukin (IL)-1β.(12,13) In particular, COX-2 is critically involved in the loss of cortical and striatal neurons and injury to the blood vessels.(13,14) Therefore, the capacity of specific and nonspecific COX-2 inhibitors, such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), has been studied for possible neuroprotective effects against reperfusion injury.(15−17)
    Reperfusion injury after stroke remains an exigent clinical issue in both diagnostic imaging and prevention of injury. Although brain imaging techniques in stroke have advanced substantially, imaging of reperfusion injuries is still challenging.(18,19) While techniques such as diffusion and perfusion MRI can visualize ischemic brain regions, the accuracy of these imaging protocols in identifying reperfusion injury after the stroke remains inconclusive. Despite the ongoing efforts to develop molecular MRI contrast agents to detect reperfusion injury after an acute stroke, a molecular MRI contrast agent that can detect the inflammatory responses associated with reperfusion injury at the molecular or cellular levels remains elusive.(20−22) Concerning protection or prevention, several treatments to prevent ischemia-induced inflammation have been studied, but with little success.(23,24)
    This study was aimed to design, synthesize, and test inflammation-targeted gadolinium compounds conjugated with NSAIDs as potential neuroprognostic agents against reperfusion injury after stroke. These new agents combine a molecular imaging agent and a protective drug into a single integrated platform, wherein MR imaging reveals inflamed brain areas at the molecular level as possible perfusion injury sites. Furthermore, subsequent MR imaging monitors the neuroprotective efficacy of the integrated agent by revealing changes in the infarction area. We also explored possible molecular mechanisms of the neuroprotective characteristics of the agent to clarify how the efficacy of anti-inflammatories might be affected by being combined with the imaging agent. To the best of our knowledge, the potential for the synergic interaction between imaging agents and therapeutic drugs has yet to be fully investigated.

    Synthesis

    A series of neuroprognostic agents were designed and synthesized using Gd-DO3A and one of two NSAIDs, diflunisal or sulindac, which are used as COX-2 inhibitors in inflammatory disease (Figure 1A). As depicted in Scheme 1. N,N′-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide (DCC)/N-hydroxysuccinamide (NHS) coupling reaction resulted in the formation of 2a and 2b. NSAID-conjugated DO3A, 3a and 3b can be readily prepared by acid hydrolysis with trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) of the corresponding tert-butyl ester. The reaction of Gd chelation using GdCl3 resulted in a white and yellow solid, Gd-DO3A-Dif (4a), and Gd-DO3A-Sul (4b), respectively. The formation of 3a-b and its Gd-complexes, 4a-b was confirmed by microanalysis and spectroscopic techniques, such as 1H NMR, 13NMR, HR-FABMS, and HPLC.

    MR and Pharmacological Characteristics of Neuroprognostic Agents

    The longitudinal (r1) and transverse (r2) relaxivities of Gd-DO3A-diflunisal (Gd-DO3A-Dif) and Gd-DO3A-sulindac (Gd-DO3A-Sul) in PBS were slightly higher than those of the gadolinium-based contrast agent (GBCA) in current clinical use, Gd-BT-DO3A (Gadovist) (Table 1). Relaxivities were measured in 0.67 mM human serum albumin (HSA) solution to replicate the physiological conditions, as 0.67 mM (4.5%) is the concentration at which HSA, the most abundant protein in human blood plasma, is protected from leakage into the interstitial space.(25,26) The increased relaxivities of the neuroprognostic agents in the HSA solution indicate the importance of aromatic rings in lipophilic–HSA interactions (Table 1). It is however worthy to note a limitation in relaxivity measuremnts. Because of a technical reason, it was difficult to measure the relaxivity at 37 °C instead of at room temperature. Therefore, the relaxivities reported in this study at room temperature likely underestimate the relaxivity at 37 °C.

    The factor used to estimate the lipophilicity of a compound, log Poct/wat, was obtained by calculating partition coefficients. The values of these coefficients for the neuroprognostic agents (log Poct/wat: Gd-DO3A-Dif = −0.50 and Gd-DO3A-Sul = −1.46) indicated that their lipophilicities were higher than those of clinically used GBCAs (Table S1, Supporting Information). In particular, the binding percentage of Gd-DO3A-Dif in the HSA solution were a little higher than those of Gd-DO3A-Sul and Gd-BT-DO3A (Table 1), providing evidence of a longer elimination half-life for Gd-DO3A-Dif compared with Gd-DO3A-Sul, likely because of a noncovalent interaction with HSA (Figure 1B).
    Kinetic stabilities of neuroprognostic agents can be compared according to the evolution of their normalized paramagnetic longitudinal relaxation rates R1P(t)/R1P(0) as a function of time against those of Gd-DOTA, Gd-DTPA-BOPTA (Multihance), Gd-DTPA-EOB (Primovist), and Gd-BT-DO3A (Figure 1C). Here, the value of R1P at time t is a good estimator of the extent of transmetalation between Gd and zinc. Gd3+ can be displaced by Zn2+, which is known to be present at higher concentrations (55–125 mM) in the blood than any other ion.(27) GBCAs used in this study can be classified into two groups depending on their graph pattern, macrocyclic chelates, or linear chelates. Neuroprognostic agents exhibited high values of kinetic inertness, comparable with those of Gd-BT-DO3A and Gd-DOTA, which contain an identically structured macrocyclic chelate. No significant changes in relaxivity were observed over 3 days. Conversely, other GBCAs containing linear DTPA analogues showed a significant decrease in R1 over the same period. In addition, minimal change was observed in R1 across pH values ranging from 1 to 11, demonstrating that neuroprognostic agents are appropriately stable under highly acidic and basic conditions compared with the commonly used Gd-DOTA and Gd-BT-DO3A (Figure S6, Supporting Information).

    Inflammation Targeting Efficacy of Neuroprognostic Agents as a New Type of COX-2 Inhibitor

    In vitro experiments targeting microglia cells were conducted to investigate the potential of MR neuroprognostic agents to target inflammation in stroke. Upon brain ischemia, microglia are activated and lead to an inflammatory response.(28) In this study, microglial cells were activated by lipopolysaccharide (LPS), and COX-2 expression was evaluated as evidence of an inflammatory response by LPS-induced microglia cells in vitro (Figure 2A). The full gel is shown in Figure S7, Supporting Information. Inflammation-induced microglial cells were incubated with neuroprognostic agents or Gd-BT-DO3A (positive control). T1-weighted MR images were obtained from the supernatant after a 24 h incubation period. The degree of signal enhancement in the supernatant incubated with Gd-DO3A-Dif or Gd-DO3A-Sul was higher than that seen with Gd-BT-DO3A, suggesting that Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul were capable of targeting the inflamed microglial cells (Figure 2B,C). To confirm the linear relationship between the COX-2 expression levels and the concentration of neuroprognostic agents, three different concentrations of the neuroprognostic agent were treated in various concentrations of LPS-induced microglia cells and incubated for 24 h. After that, gadolinium concentrations were measured by ICP with harvested cells implying the quantity of the bound neuroprognostic agent. Gadolinium concentrations of bound neuroprognostic agents were increased with the levels of COX-expressions, and the degree of bound neuroprognostic agents also depends on the increase of treated their concentrations in the COX-2 expressed cells (Figure 2D,E).
    We performed a protein–ligand docking simulation and binding energy calculation to evaluate the interaction between COX-2 and the neuroprognostic agents(29) to elucidate the underlying mechanisms of the inflammation targeting effect based on in vitro results with microglia cells (Figure 3). In this simulation, Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul were used as COX-2 target molecules. COX-2 contains three important active sites: a hydrophobic pocket, an active site lined with hydrophilic residues, and a secondary pocket.(30,31) The secondary pocket is more accessible in COX-2 because the I523 residue in COX-1 was replaced with V523, which has a smaller side chain residue.(32) In the case of traditional NSAIDs, diflunisal and sulindac can bind to hydrophobic residues in the more accessible secondary pocket of COX-2, such as H90, R513, and Y355 (Figures 3A,B and S8A,B). For example, diflunisal is involved in π–π stacking between an aromatic ring and W387 as well as hydrogen bonding with H90 and R513 in the hydrophobic pocket. In the case of sulindac, it forms hydrogen bonds with the R120 and F518 backbones, a π-cation interaction with R513, and π–π stacking with Y355. Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul appeared to be deeply inserted into the hydrophobic pocket of COX-2 similar to traditional NSAIDs, diflunisal, and sulindac (Figures 3A,B and S8C,D). In addition, unlike the conventional diaryl heterocycle-based compounds, which possess a pharmacophore that selectively binds to the secondary pocket of COX-2,(29) each Gd-chelate possesses a specific binding sites. First, Gd-DO3A-Dif shows that the hydroxyl proton of the phenol group and the proton of the amide bond in Gd-DO3A-Dif form hydrogen bonds with the positively charged residue S353, and the solvent-exposed Gd-chelate also interacts with a relatively polar region near the surface of COX-2 (Figure 3A).
    Second, Gd-DO3A-Sul has additional hydrogen bonds with L352, H90, and P514 compared with sulindac. In particular, the salt bridge interaction between the Gd ion of Gd-DO3A-Sul and D515 of COX-2 contributes to additional binding alongside interactions with R120, R513, H90, and L352 (Figure 3B). Moreover, the solvent-exposed Gd chelates on these neuroprognostic agents also interact with Q192, a relatively polar region near the surface of COX-2, which greatly enhanced binding strength between the neuroprognostic agents and COX-2 (Figure S8, Supporting Information).
    Binding energies approximate the ligand-binding free energy and are indicated by GlideScores and docking scores. The binding energies of Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul (−10.690 and −10.739 kcal/mol, respectively) are more potent than those of diflunisal and sulindac (−8.342 and −8.467 kcal/mol, respectively) and show greater fitness values, as shown in Table S2, Supporting Information. Importantly, these results indicate that both neuroprognostic agents (Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul) have higher binding energies with COX-2 than either NSAID, suggesting an increase in COX-2 selectivity. Based on the docking simulation and GlideScore results, the neuroprognostic agents Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul can be considered a new class of COX-2 selective inhibitors because they have binding sites that are distinct from the NSAIDs compounds diflunisal and sulindac.
    UV–vis spectrophotometry was performed to confirm the direct COX-2 binding properties of the neuroprognostic agents. Incremental additions of COX-2 to neuroprognostic agent solutions result in concomitant decreases in absorbance, indicating an interaction between the neuroprognostic agent and COX-2 because of π–π and hydrophobic interactions.(33) Binding constants are determined using the Benesi–Hildebrand equation (eq 3).(34) COX-2 binding affinities (Ka) of Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul are 16.76 × 106 M and 13.66 × 106 M, respectively, and are higher than those of traditional NSAIDs (Figure 3C,D). These data are in agreement with the in silico ligand–protein docking simulation results described above.

    Longitudinal Evaluation of Biomarkers in Reperfusion Injury after Ischemic Stroke

    Previous studies report changes in various biomarkers, including an increase in COX-2 expression levels after stroke,(35,36) but longitudinal changes in the expression levels of these biomarkers in reperfusion injury after stroke remains poorly understood. Longitudinal evaluation of expression patterns of biomarkers is important for neuroprognostic agents because the neuroprognostic agents must be administrated to protect the injury before the injury cause serious brain damage. Hence, before evaluating the neuroprotective effects of Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul, we assessed the longitudinal expression patterns of COX-2 as a neuroinflammatory marker; the presence of glial fibrillary acid protein (GFAP), an astrocyte marker;(37) βIII-tubulin; and neuronal nuclear antigen (NeuN), a neuron marker.(38) We also directly assessed expression levels in the brain of the inflammatory factors TNF-α, interferon γ (IFNγ), IL-6, IL-1β, and iNOS using immunofluorescence staining and real-time PCR (Figure S9, Supporting Information).
    Reperfusion injury after an ischemic stroke causes a progressive increase in the infarction size (Figure S9A,B, Supporting Information) that continues for as long as 48 h after reperfusion. COX-2 expression data reveal a tendency for it to increase from 3 to 24 h after reperfusion, with the highest expression level at 24 h (Figure S9C,D, Supporting Information). Following reperfusion, there is an increase in the GFAP expression and a decrease in βIII-tubulin and NeuN expression, a pattern that is indicative of glial cell activation and neuronal loss (Figure S9C,D, Supporting Information). Immunofluorescence results reveal that the core and penumbra of the cerebral infarction region also present with an increase in COX-2 expression up to 24 h after reperfusion; neuronal loss is also evident from the NeuN expression and glial cell activation is evident by the presence of GFAP and ionized calcium-binding adaptor molecule 1 (Iba-1), a microglial marker(37) (Figure S9E,F, Supporting Information). These neuroinflammatory factor expression patterns are accompanied by an increase in inflammatory cytokines associated with cerebral infarction, although these increases occur at different times (Figure S9G, Supporting Information). Specifically, after reperfusion, the peak time for IL-6, IL-1β, and iNOS was 12 h, and for COX-2, TNFα, and IFNγ, it was 24 h.

    Neuroprotective Effects Against Reperfusion Injury after Ischemic Stroke

    Based on the results of longitudinal evaluation shown in Figure S9, Supporting Information, including expression patterns of COX-2 and other factors that indicate the activation of glial cells (GFAP) and neuronal loss during reperfusion injury (βIII-tubulin and NeuN),(38) the protective effects of the neuroprognostic agents against reperfusion injury were confirmed.
    To evaluate the neuroprotective effects of Gd-DO3A-Sul and Gd-DO3A-Dif, these agents were administered via intravenous injection at 3 h after reperfusion. As a control experiment, sulindac and diflunisal were also administered orally at 3 h after reperfusion. At 24 h after reperfusion, sulindac and Gd-DO3A-Sul significantly suppress the COX-2 expression by at least 50% compared with the levels induced in the negative control (NC) group. Conversely, suppression of βIII-tubulin, NeuN, and GFAP expressions are not significant, suggesting a lack of effect on neuronal loss and glial cell activation (Figure 4A,B). For diflunisal, smaller decreases in the COX-2 expression and smaller increases in βIII-tubulin and NeuN expressions are observed, none of which are statistically significant. Importantly, Gd-DO3A-Dif not only suppresses 49.3% of the COX-2 expression but also increases βIII-tubulin and NeuN expressions by 68.9% compared to the induced levels in the NC group, indicating a protective effect (Figure 4C,D).
    The protective efficacy of Gd-DO3A-Dif is at least 20% more than that of diflunisal against neuronal loss caused by reperfusion injury (Figure 4C,D). Inflammatory cytokines, including COX-2, TNF-α, IFNγ, IL-6, IL-1β, and iNOS, are suppressed after treatment with sulindac, Gd-DO3A-Sul, diflunisal, or Gd-DO3A-Dif (Figure 4E). To directly identify protein expression levels in the cerebral tissue, we conducted immunofluorescence staining experiments. The results revealed that sulindac, diflunisal, Gd-DO3A-Sul, and Gd-DO3A-Dif have greater inhibitory and protective effects than suggested by immunoblot results (Figure 5). For the COX-2 expression, inhibition is 39.2% for sulindac, 39.9% for Gd-DO3A-Sul, 40.5% for diflunisal, and 42.5 for Gd-DO3A-Dif. For the GFAP expression, inhibition is 54.6% for sulindac, 54.0% for Gd-DO3A-Sul, 53.3% for diflunisal, and 68.3 for Gd-DO3A-Dif. For the Iba-1 expression, inhibition is 60.7% for sulindac, 53.0% for Gd-DO3A-Sul, 55.2% for diflunisal, and 63.1% for Gd-DO3A-Dif. For the NeuN expression, a positive neuronal cell marker, increases are 32.4% for sulindac, 16.7% for Gd-DO3A-Sul, 39.7% for diflunisal, and 50.8% for Gd-DO3A-Dif. Among all treatments tested, Gd-DO3A-Dif shows the strongest protective effects, recapitulating results from the protein-level analyses.
    We then measured infarction volume at 24 h after reperfusion with T2-weighted MRI and triphenyltetrazolium chloride (TTC) staining. The infarction volumes (red volume) were visualized in 3D-rendered whole brains (Figure 6A), and the infarction ratio was measured as the ratio of the infarction volume relative to the whole brain volume (see infarction volume/whole brain volume in Figure 6B). The average infarction ratio of MCAO-R whole brain after administration of sulindac and Gd-DO3A-Sul are 1.75 and 1.60 times smaller, respectively, than those in the NC group. The results with diflunisal and Gd-DO3A-Dif also show infarction volumes that are 2.19 and 2.12 times smaller, respectively, than those in the NC group.
    In addition, estimations of infarction volume using TTC staining results corresponding to the T2-weighted MR images show good agreement with the 3D-rendered infarction volume results (Figure 6C). In contrast to the infarction volume of the NC group, the infarction volumes of all treated groups are observed to have 2-fold decreases in infarction volumes, which reflect their neuroprotective effects in the MCAO-R model (Figure 6D).

    Neuroprognostic Agents as Diagnostic Molecular MR Imaging Agents in Reperfusion Injury after Acute Stroke

    Two neuroprognostic agents (Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul) were tested for diagnostic molecular imaging capability, and Gd-BT-DO3A, an extracellular fluid (ECF) that is used clinically as an MR contrast agent, was used as a control agent. Reperfusion was performed after 45 min of middle cerebral artery occlusion; the agents were administered intravenously 3 h after reperfusion (Figure 7A). Pre-T1, T2-weighted MR images and diffusion-weighted images (DWI) were obtained before administration, and T1-weighted images were acquired up to 3 h after injection to visualize the signal enhancements of the neuroprognostic and control agents (Figure 7A). On the pre-T1, T2-weighted images, the infarction location could not be identified even several hours after reperfusion (Figure 7C). Although DWI showed more potential to identify developing reperfusion injuries, no significant signal intensity changes were observed for DWI in this study.
    The lack of significance was likely due to transient recovery during the early phase of reperfusion (Figure 7C).(39) In the case of T1-weighted images, a strong and prolonged signal enhancement at the infarction site is seen up to 3 h after administration of Gd-DO3A-Dif. Gd-DO3A-Sul also shows increased signal enhancement up to 3 h, but the degree of enhancement is small compared to that of Gd-DO3A-Dif. Conversely, Gd-BT-DO3A shows almost no signal enhancement at the infarction site after administration (Figure 7C).
    We speculate that the strong and prolonged signal enhancement by Gd-DO3A-Dif at the infarction site might be due to its targeting effect with COX-2 and its relatively long circulation time, providing more opportunities for targeting COX-2. We also evaluated Gd-DO3A-Dif as a COX-2 targeting agent by administering Gd-DO3A-Dif at a different time point after reperfusion, using T1-weighted images acquired at 6 h after reperfusion. The post-T1-weighted images show strong signal enhancement of the infarction site even though the Gd-DO3A-Dif was administered 6 h after reperfusion (Figure S14, Supporting Information).
    The CNR was calculated for T1-weighted images with eq 1 for quantitation of signal enhancement.(40) One representative slice was measured as a region of interest (ROI) for each time point (Figure 7B). The listed parameters represent the signal intensities of the infarction area (Sinfarction), normal tissue on the contralateral side (Snormal), and background noise (Snoise).(1)
    In the cortex and striatum, CNRs using Gd-DO3A-Dif showed a 2-fold increase in signal enhancement over Gd-DO3A-Sul and Gd-BT-DO3A at 3 h after injection (Figure 7D,E). This pronounced and prolonged enhancement by Gd-DO3A-Dif in the infarct areas strongly supports the capability for in vivo inflammation targeting efficacy. Immunofluorescence images, showing increased COX-2 at the enhanced cortex at 3 h after injection, also support the use of Gd-DO3A-Dif for MR molecular imaging because of its COX-2 targeting capacity (Figure 7F).

    Reperfusion injury after stroke remains a challenge for diagnosis and protection after acute stroke. This study evaluates two new neuroprognostic agents for simultaneous MRI molecular diagnosis and protection from reperfusion injury after stroke. After conjugation with NSAIDs, both neuroprognostic agents, Gd-DO3A-Dif and Gd-DO3A-Sul, performed well as T1 MRI molecular contrast agents compared to the commercially used Gd-BT-DO3A in MR relaxivities, kinetic inertness, and inflammation targeting efficacy. These neuroprognostic agents, especially Gd-DO3A-Dif, had clinically relevant effects on reperfusion sequelae with the suppression of the COX-2 expression, the reduction of phenotypic glial activation, and the protection of neuronal damage. To conclude, these gadolinium-based MR neuroprognostic agents showed significant neuroprotective and diagnostic capacity regarding neuroinflammation of reperfusion injury after stroke. Our data suggest that these neuroprognostic agents are also viable T1 MR contrast agents that can enhance the infarction site while preventing its enlargement by suppressing neuroinflammation. In this respect, this new concept of neuroprognostic agents is proposed as a promising management strategy for reperfusion injury after stroke.

    Relaxivity(42)

    Relaxivity samples were prepared as water solutions of the gadolinium contrast agent at various concentrations (0.0625–1 mM). T1 measurements were carried out using an inversion recovery method with a variable inversion time (TI) at 1.5 T (64 MHz). The magnetic resonance (MR) images were acquired at 35 different TI values ranging from 50 to 1750 ms. and T1 relaxation times were obtained from nonlinear least squares fit analyses of the signal intensity measured at each TI value. For T2, the CPMG (Carr-Purcell-Meiboon-Gill) pulse sequence was adapted for multiple spin-echo measurements. Twenty eight images were acquired with 28 different echo time (TE) values ranging from 10 to 1300 ms. T2 relaxation times were obtained from nonlinear least squares fit analyses of mean pixel values for multiple spin-echo measurements at each echo time. Relaxivities (R1 and R2) were then calculated as an inverse of relaxation time per mM. The determined relaxation times (T1 and T2) and relaxivities (R1 and R2) were then image processed to provide a relaxation time map and relaxivity map. Values are expressed as mean ± SD (n = 3).
    The relaxivities in 0.67 mM solution of HSA were performed on a 3 T Whole Body study (Siemens Magnetom Tim Trio scanner, Germany). T1 relaxivities were obtained with fast spin-echo (FSE) pulse sequence as follows: repetition time (TR)/TE = 2000/28 ms, TI = 50–1700 (interval 50), echo train length (ETL) = 17, slice thickness = 2 mm, average = 1, matrix = 215 × 250, and field of view (FOV) = 215 × 250. T2 relaxivities were measured with the following spin-echo pulse sequence: TR/TE = 2000/33.8–1081.6 (interval: 33.8), slice thickness = 2 mm, average = 1, matrix = 320 × 262, and FOV = 215 × 250. Values are expressed as mean ± SD (n = 3).

    Pharmacokinetic Study

    Blood plasma samples from six week old male SD (Sprague-Dawley) rats (180–210 g) were assessed after intravenous injection with Gd-DO3A-Dif or Gd-DO3A-Sul (0.1 mmol/kg). Blood (100 μL) was collected from the tail vein at ten time points between 5 min and 24 h. Collected samples were immediately mixed with a heparin solution and centrifuged at 5000 rpm at 4 °C for 10 min to obtain the plasma. The plasma samples were then treated with 70% nitric acid (HNO3) and 30% hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) at 180 °C for 3 h. The gadolinium concentration of the treated sample was measured by an inductively coupled plasma spectrophotometer (NexION 300X, PerkinElmer, USA). The recognition limit of this process is 1 ppb. Blood plasma pharmacokinetics were estimated by plotting the gadolinium concentration as a function of time and fitting the decay to a biexponential function using the MATLAB (R2018b, Natick, MA, USA) curve fitting tool, and elimination half-lives (t1/2) were estimated. Concentrations are expressed as mean ± SD (n = 7).

    Transmetalation Kinetics

    This experiment was conducted according to methods described in the literature.(27) It was based upon the experimental protocol for the evolution of the water proton relaxation rate (R1P), where a phosphate buffered solution (pH 7.4) contains equimolar amounts of the gadolinium complex and zinc chloride. ZnCl2 solution (10 μL, 100 mM) was added to 1 mL of the paramagnetic complex solution (1 mM). The mixture was vigorously stirred and immediately measured for 72 h. Control studies were run on Gd-BT-DO3A, Gd-DTPA-EOB, Gd-DTPA-BOPTA, and Gd-DOTA with ZnCl2 for comparison. The R1 relaxation rate is the reciprocal of the observed relaxation time, T1. The relative value of R1P at any time t, R1P(t)/R1P(0) was represented as an estimate of the extent of transmetalation. The measurements were performed on a 3 T Whole Body study (Magnetom Tim Trio, siemens, Germany) with the same T1 sequence from the HSA relaxivitiy study, at room temperature. Values are expressed as mean ± SD (n = 3).

    Brain sections (5 μm) were blocked with TBS including a 0.1% Triton X-100 and 3% goat serum (Gibco Co., Grand Island, NY, USA) and incubated with primary antibodies (anti-NeuN, anti-βIII-tubulin, anti-Iba1, or anti-GFAP) for 2 h at 25 °C. They were then washed with TBS and incubated for 1 h in the presence of IgG-labeled secondary antibodies with Alexa Fluor (Cell Signaling Technology Inc., Beverly, MA, USA).     3D MR Image Processing

    Image data were resampled to isovoxel 0.117 mm using a ResampleImage tool in Advanced Normalization Tools (ANTs) (http://picsl.upenn.edu/ANTS) to facilitate more accurate brain extraction. Subsequently, the 3dSkullstrip tool of AFNI was used for rat brain extraction. Extracted rat brain data in AFNI were converted to a NIFTI file to perform normalization in SPM12. The N4 bias field correction was applied to the data to correct for low-frequency intensity nonuniformity signal.(49) The extracted and bias-corrected brain data were normalized to isovoxel 1.25 mm resolution of the rat brain template(50) using the normalization toolbox of SPM12. Stroke region segmentation was performed by researchers trained in segmentation methods on the same data. A segmentation wizard toolbox of a 3d slicer (http://www.slicer.org)(51) was used to segment stroke regions from the normalized brain data. Segmented stroke regions were overlaid on the rat brain template to be visualized in the 3D space using the Multi-Image Analysis GUI (Mango) (http://ric.uthscsa.edu/mango/). Finally, volumes of whole brain and stroke regions were calculated by fslstats tools as implemented in FSL (https://fsl.fmrib.ox.ac.uk/fsl/fslwiki/).(52)
