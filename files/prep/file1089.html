    Abstract

    Peptide stapling chemistry represents an attractive strategy to promote the clinical translation of protein epitope mimetics, but its use has not been applied to natural cytotoxic peptides (NCPs) to produce new oncolytic peptides. Based on a wasp venom peptide, a series of stapled anoplin peptides (StAnos) were prepared. The optimized stapled Ano-3/3s were shown to be protease-resistant and exerted superior tumor cell-selective cytotoxicity by rapid membrane disruption. In addition, Ano-3/3s induced tumor ablation in mice through the direct oncolytic effect and subsequent stimulation of immunogenic cell death. This synergistic oncolytic-immunotherapy effect is more remarkable on melanoma than on triple-negative breast cancer in vivo. The efficacies exerted by Ano-3/3s on melanoma were further characterized by CD8+ T cell infiltration, and the addition of anti-CD8 antibodies diminished the long-term antitumor effects. In summary, these results support stapled peptide chemistry as an advantageous method to enhance the NCP potency for oncolytic therapy.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Cancer is a major cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide and an important economic burden on health care systems.(1) To address this global threat, unrelenting efforts have been committed to cancer therapeutic modality development. Modern cancer treatment options have shifted from their reliance on surgery and radiation therapy to an increased emphasis on systemic therapies, such as hormonal therapy, chemotherapy, and immunotherapy. Among these treatment options, chemotherapy remains the primary systemic treatment for advanced and metastatic stages of common cancers.(2) However, conventional chemotherapeutics are often subjected to a considerable loss of efficacy due to drug resistance, lack of tumor-specific selectivity, and potential toxicity.(3,4) In this context, the application of natural cytotoxic peptides (NCPs) represents a promising alternative approach for cancer prevention and treatments.(5−7)
    In nature, NCPs are expressed to capture prey, fend off predators, and boost innate immunity.(8) Cationic NCPs exhibit strong oncolytic activity by lysing cancer cell membranes, a mechanism of action with a decreased tendency to induce resistance.(9−11) Along with the structural characterization of NCPs, certain NCPs and their derivates are the focus of drug development as novel antitumor agents.(11−13) As the shortest (10-amino acids), amphipathic, linear α-helical NCP from the venom sac of the spider wasp, Anoplius samariensis, anoplin has attracted much attention on the account of its simple structure, broad spectrum of activities, and lack of hemolytic toxicity.(14) However, further development of the linear form of anoplin has been hindered by its conformational instability, susceptibility to proteolytic degradation, and suboptimal therapeutic activity.(15−17) All-hydrocarbon stapling has been considered one of the most promising strategies for constraining peptides in an α-helical conformation to form protein epitope mimetics with improved bioactivity and increased proteolytic stability.(18−20) However, all-hydrocarbon stapling has never been applied to oncolytic NCP design. The tumor membrane lytic capacity of anoplin exhibits high dependency on its α-helical structure,(21,22) making anoplin an ideal template for modification by peptide stapling.
    Synthetic StAnos peptides were prepared (Ano-1 to 6), and the structure–activity relationships were examined. The side-chain-retention stapling methodology was used to produce the lead StAno structure (Ano-3s). Mechanistic studies revealed that Ano-3s cause rapid tumor cell membrane lysis in 4T1 and B16F10 cells with significantly improved water solubility and hemolytic safety. In addition, Ano-3/3s induced immunogenic cell death (ICD) of different tumor cell lines, which suggests the potential synergy between oncolytic therapy and the immunotherapeutic function. Ano-3/3s displayed pronounced antitumor activity in an immunologically “hot” tumor model (melanoma) following a rapid tumor cell killing effect and protective immune response but not in the “cold” tumor model [triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC)]. The significant difference in the therapeutic outcome exhibited high correlation with the significantly increased CD8+ T cell infiltration in melanoma, evidenced by the depletion effect of anti-CD8 antibodies (Figure 1). Therefore, this work not only broadens the application of the stapled chemistry in the development of natural oncolytic peptides but also provides an interesting insight into the subsequent long-term immune response.

    Staple Scan Design and Functional Screening

    When designing bioactive stapled peptides, one of the challenges is where to install the staple.(23) The ultrashort structure of anoplin, ten amino acids in total, is favorable for the design and production of a library of StAnos by performing a “staple scan” that sequentially places the staple along the sequence of anoplin, yielding 6 i, i + 4, and 3 i, i + 7 stapled peptides, termed Ano (i + 4) and Ano (i + 7) (Figures 2A and S1A). For programmatic expediency, we prepared entire staple-scanning anoplin libraries using standard Fmoc solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) procedures and ring-closing metathesis on the resin (Figure 2B). To verify the conformational changes induced by the all-hydrocarbon crosslinking strategy, we calculated the percent helicity of all the StAnos through analysis of their circular dichroism (CD) spectra (Figures 2A, S1 and S2). The Ano-libraries demonstrated α-helicity with a 1.3- to 15.8-fold increase compared to the parent anoplin, suggesting that the structural constraint is a feasible method for α-helical stabilization of anoplin. These stapled peptides were evaluated for in vitro cytotoxicity in two highly malignant tumor cell lines with multiple mechanisms of drug resistance: mouse TNBC 4T1 and mouse melanoma B16F10 (Figures 2A and S1).(24,25) The antiproliferative activity of the whole StAnos panel showed remarkable enhancement against both cancer cell lines. The half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of the Ano-libraries ranges from 4 to 66 μM, corresponding to a 5.8- to 47.8-fold enhanced potency. These results also suggest that conformational constraint is preferable to increase the bioactivity of these oncolytic peptides.
    To identify determinants for the variable trends in structure–activity relationships of StAnos, we analyzed several biophysical parameters that include charge, C18-HPLC retention time, staple placement, and α-helicity (Figures 2A and S1). No single determinant uniformly correlates with the IC50 values of the Ano (i + 7) library. To make matters more complicated, Ano-8 and Ano-9 included two (E/Z)-steric configurations in a hydrocarbon staple. For example, Ano-8-1 and Ano-8-2 that derived from the same amino acid sequence showed nearly identical HPLC retention times, yet their helical contents and antitumor selectivity were significantly different, and it is technically difficult to confirm the absolute configuration of diastereoisomeric stapled peptides. In contrast, the IC50 values for the Ano (i + 4) library revealed a traceable correlation among the examined variables. On the whole, the stable α-helix topology effectively increases the interaction with cancer cells, and the hydrophobicity of the hydrophobic face determines the level of oncolytic efficacy. To be more specific, the hydrophobic ring-closing amino acids S5 sacrifice peptide cationic charges (Arg and Lys) in some stapling positions and increase their HPLC retention time. To visualize the consequence of staple placement along the anoplin helix, we plotted and compared a variety of StAnos surfaces, generated in PyMol according to the NOE-based NMR structure of anoplin in dodecylphosphocholine micelles (protein data bank ID: 2MJQ) (Figure 2C).(26) The secondary structure model of anoplin is an idealized amphipathic conformation with nonpolar residues (Leu2, Leu3, Ile6, Leu9, and Leu10; in yellow) on the one side and basic residues (Lys4, Arg5, and Lys7; in blue) on the opposite side that is joined by a polar, uncharged Thr8 (green). When the staple is inserted at the amphipathic boundary, as in Ano-1, -3, and -5 (Figure 2D), there was a maximal increase in antitumor activity compared to anoplin. Placing the staple within the highly hydrophobicity regions, such as in Ano-2 and Ano-6, or the hydrophilic face (e.g., Ano-4) of the anoplin helix surrounded by positively charged residues, the antitumor effect of the StAnos has a reduced activity relative to Ano-1, -3, and -5. Further, staple placements that expand the hydrophobic network on the hydrophobic face improved the strength of the interaction with cancer cells, whereas inspection of the Ano-2, -4, and -6 structures revealed that the localization of the staple only increased the overall hydrophobicity without changing the landscape of the hydrophobic surface, thus diminishing the increase in bioactivity.

    StAno Peptides Rapidly Kill Cancer Cells by Membrane Lysis

    Despite the remarkable promise of StAnos as a novel class of antitumor compounds, stapled natural peptides can lead to nonspecific hemolysis.(27,28) To detect stapled peptide structure–toxicity relationships, Walensky and coworkers(29) devised a predictive algorithm to generate “hydrophobicity network maps (HNMs)” and lyticity indices of stapled antimicrobial peptides. Inspired by this algorithm, we developed HNMs of the better-performing Ano-1, -3, and -5 to systematically visualize and quantitate the hydrophobic interactions on the three-dimensional α-helical surface (Figure 3A). The HNMs of StAnos showed a greater number and thickness of linkages within the networks compared to anoplin, which reflected an increased surface area of the hydrophobic interactions. The nonspecific predicted lyticity index (LI), depending on a computational algorithm, demonstrated that Ano-3 has the lowest calculated value (LI 304.4) among the three StAnos with optimum antitumor activity, an LI value far below the threshold of 600 set by authors at the supratherapeutic dosing level in vivo. This is probably because the Ano-3 HNM is not that structurally dissimilar from the anoplin parent structure. To confirm the predicted LI relationships, red blood cell (RBC) hemolysis assays were performed to determine the actual hemolytic activity of the three StAnos (Figure 3B). Consistent with the predictions, StAnos with higher LI values exhibited a greater percentage of RBC hemolysis, and Ano-3 had the best therapeutic index, even the most hemolytic Ano-1 provided a relatively wide therapeutic window of selectivity for cancer cells versus nontumor mammalian cell membranes. These results indicate that anoplin can serve as an idealized template for stapling modification. To investigate the oncolytic mechanism of StAnos, Ano-3 was used as a representative and examined in StAnos-sensitive 4T1 cells. The 9-mer amphipathic helical peptide LTX-315 (Ruxotemitide, Oncopore), a potential first in class oncolytic peptide in the phase II clinical trial (clinical trials. gov identifier: NCT03725605),(13) served as a standard positive control for cytotoxic activity in the two cancer cell lines (Figure S3); it can kill cancer cells through Bax/Bak-regulated mitochondrial membrane permeabilization.(11) In the lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) release assay that monitors the release of intracellular contents, treatment of cells with Ano-3, LTX-315, and anoplin each leads to a loss of membrane integrity in a concentration-dependent manner (Figures 3C and S4). The ability to disrupt cell membranes was in line with cell viability assay results. A time-course study on the release of ATP revealed that ATP was detected in the supernatant as early as 5 min after the cells were exposed to the peptides (Figure 3D). Live cell imaging [three-dimensional (3D) cell explorer] was used to observe the morphology of Ano-3-treated 4T1 cells in the bright field; the time-lapse movie displayed a rapid change from a completely adherent morphology to a total collapse. Broken disrupted cell particles were clearly visible, and these changes occurred within less than 5 min (Figure 3E). In addition, peptide-lysed cell membranes were observed by confocal imaging. As shown in Figure 3F, a significant difference in membrane profiles was observed between the treated and control cells. Peptides FITC-Ano-3 and FITC-LTX-315 caused disruption of the complete membrane and cytoplasmic staining, whereas the plasma membrane of untreated and FITC-Anoplin-treated cells remained intact. A similar outcome was obtained in the calcein-AM and propidium iodide (PI) dual staining assay. Treatment with Ano-3 and LTX-315 produced an increase in cells displaying red fluorescence that became the dominant cell type over time, suggesting that a large number of cells were disrupted (Figure 3G). In contrast, the subtherapeutic anoplin did not incur such a change (Figure S5). Taken together, these results indicate that StAnos-mediated membrane lysis of cancer cells is a rapid process that is both concentration- and time-dependent and that Ano-3 exerts superior oncolytic activity relative to LTX-315.

    Increasing StAno Hydrophilicity and Tumor Cell Selectivity with Side Chains

    Despite previous success, a potential drawback of StAnos is the lack of the natural side chains in the stapling position. For example, a staple-induced hydrophobic network could improve the ability of Ano-3 to interact with cancer cells but at the cost of original cationic Lys side chains. We envisioned that the retention of a parental side chain would further increase the water solubility and tumor cell selectivity of Ano-3 (Figure 4A). In an earlier study, we synthesized a series of amino acids which contain both native side chains and stapling groups and used them to expand the scope of the all-hydrocarbon stapled peptide strategy (Figure 4B).(30) Thus, with these novel stapling amino acids (Fmoc-Leu*-OH and Fmoc-Lys*(Boc)-OH) in hand, Ano-3s, the side-chain-retention stapled anoplin peptide based on Ano-3, was rapidly assembled via SPPS (Figure 4C). As anticipated, the introduction of a hydrophilic side chain shortened the HPLC retention time of Ano-3s when compared to Ano-3 (Figure 4D). Therefore, the side-chain-retention stapling strategy produced a more water soluble Ano-3s peptide. The Ano-3s maintained IC50 values for cancer cells comparable to Ano-3 (Figure 4C). At a relatively high concentration of 80 μM, there was less than 10% hemolysis, reflecting a potentially broad membrane-specificity window of Ano-3s (Figure 4E). However, Ano-3s cannot keep the same degree of helicity as Ano-3 (Figure 4F). A logical explanation is that the steric hindrance of the α,α-disubstituted amino acids results in the helix-kinked structure. Overall, the side-chain-retention stapling method bestowed Ano-3s with increased hydrophilicity and tumor cell selectivity.
    In terms of oncolytic potency, FITC-Ano-3s also disrupted and permeated cancer cell membranes (Figure 4G). Additionally, the PI influx was detected immediately after Ano-3s was added to calcein-AM-loaded 4T1 cells, suggesting that Ano-3s permeabilized cell membranes in a time-dependent manner (Figure 4H). The proteolytic sensitivity of the lead StAnos toward α-chymotrypsin-mediated degradation [pH 7.4, phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) buffer, room temperature] was monitored by HPLC (Figure S6A). As shown in Figure S6B, linear anoplin and LTX-315 displayed a weak protease stability with a half-life (t1/2) of 12 min and 34 min, respectively. In contrast, Ano-3 was stable with no degradation observed after 12 h, and over 60% of Ano-3s remained intact after 2 h protease exposure likely because of a probable kinked helix of Ano-3s that altered the peptide exposure to enzyme cleavage sites. These properties indicated that the structurally reinforced, cancer cell membrane-selective and protease-resistant StAnos have significant advantages over the linear peptides.

    StAnos Induces Tumor Ablation In Vivo

    Upon confirming the pronounced selectivity and antitumor effect of the lead StAnos in vitro, these peptides were further evaluated for their therapeutic potential in vivo using the 4T1 cell-based mouse cancer model (Figure 5A). Approximately, two days after inoculation (when the 4T1 tumor volumes reached ∼30 mm3), the tumor-bearing female BALB/c mice were divided into groups and administered a vehicle, LTX-315, anoplin, Ano-3, or Ano-3s via intratumorally injection once every day, for a total of three consecutive injections. The equivalent dose of peptide was 1 mg/50 μL solution. During treatment, the body weight from all groups had a similar increase (Figure 5D), indicating no overt signs of toxicity. Unexpectedly, initial tumor growth was only slightly inhibited after treatment with LTX-315, Ano-3, and Ano-3s. The tumors relapsed shortly thereafter, while LTX-315 and StAnos failed to stall tumor progression. Analysis of the dissected tumor weight identified a statistically significant difference between the control and Ano-3 groups (Figure 5B,C). Hematoxylin–eosin (HE) staining of the excised tumor tissue showed notable levels of necrosis in the LTX-315, Ano-3 and Ano-3s groups on day 7 (Figure 6A,C), but there was no extensive necrosis of the tumor tissue on day 18 (Figure 6A,D). Collectively, LTX-315, Ano-3, and Ano-3s did not affect the 4T1 tumor growth in vivo significantly.
    In a separate study, the same administration schedule was used to treat subcutaneous B16F10 tumor-bearing C57 mice when the tumor volume reached 20–30 mm3 (Figure 5A), As shown in Figure 5E,F, the anoplin monotherapy had no significant impact on tumor growth due to the subtherapeutic treatment. In contrast, all other treated groups exhibited reduced tumor growth. The HE-stained tumor tissue samples revealed that LTX-315 and StAnos treatment induced cellular necrosis and tumor cell killing, indicated by a significantly lower percentage of live cells in comparison to the control and anoplin treatment by day 10 or 14 (Figure 6B,E,F). In addition, none of the group caused any significant change in body weight during treatment (Figure 5G). These results indicated that StAnos could be a promising antitumor agent in the B16F10 model.

    StAnos Boosts Innate Immunity by Oncolytic Efficacy and Resulting ICD

    The intriguing observation was that the StAnos displayed higher antiproliferative activity on 4T1 cells than that on B16F10 cells in vitro, while the situation was reversed in the animal models. These peptides did not produce an antiproliferative effect in 4T1 tumor-bearing mice equivalent to that observed at the cellular level. Our results are consistent with previous work on LTX-315, although the authors did not provide an explanation for this difference.(31,32) Because intratumoral treatment with LTX-315 can provide local tumor control, followed by ICD in B16 melanoma cells,(31) StAnos was evaluated in 4T1 and B16F10 cells for the ability to induce ICD. The expression of calreticulin (CRT) and high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB-1) was used as markers for ICD. Similar to those observed in the LTX-315 study, Ano-3 triggered surface translocation of CRT (Figure 7A) and the release of HMGB-1 (Figure 7B) in both cell lines, thereby facilitating the maturation of antigen presenting cells and intratumoral recruitment of cytotoxic lymphocytes (CTLs).(33) These results in combination with the ATP release data (Figure 3E) suggested that StAnos induces ICD which can mediate a subsequent immune response.
    Melanomas are considered to be immunologically “hot” tumors and are characterized by a high level of CTL infiltration within the tumor matrix,(34,35) rendering them capable of inducing a synergistic immunological therapeutic response following the oncolytic effect, resulting in tumor ablation. Meanwhile, TNBC belongs to the infiltration-excluded “cold” class of tumors that are relatively void of CTLs within the tumor mass.(36) To test this hypothesis, immunohistochemical staining was performed to determine immune cell infiltration. Among the samples examined, both CD4+ and CD8+ T lymphocytes were extremely scarce among all groups of 4T1 tumors (Figure S7) and B16F10-untreated control (Figure 8). Compared with TNBC tumors (4T1), melanoma tumors (B16F10) exhibited enhanced CD4+ and CD8+ T cell infiltration as an immunologically “hot” tumor. In B16F10-inoculated mice, both LTX-315 and StAnos induced a significant infiltration of CD8+ T cells into tumors (Figure 8). Increased CD8+ T cell infiltration has also been observed in LTX-315-treated melanoma patients.(37)
    The effects of StAnos on tumor growth and CD8+ T cell infiltration suggested that the oncolytic peptide may also promote antitumor immunity. To test this hypothesis in melanoma models, mice carrying B16F10 tumors treated with StAnos also received anti-CD8 antibodies to deplete the CD8+ T cells (Figure 9). As expected, CD8+ T cell depletion in Ano-3s treated mice did not prevent initial B16F10 tumor progression within the first 20 days after StAno administration (first response phase). However, CD8+ T cell depletion triggered faster tumor reoccurrence (second response phase). After 40 days of B16F10 inoculation, only StAno monotherapy groups still had fifty percent of tumor-free mice (3 in 6 mice); these findings support the notion that Ano-3s treatment-induced CD8+ T cell infiltration in B16F10 tumors had antitumor functions and was involved in long-term suppression of malignant progression.
    These findings shed lights on the cause for tumor model-dependent therapeutic outcome incurred by the same treatment. Furthermore, cancer treatments that employ StAnos or other oncolytic peptides should afford additional clinical benefit due to the combination of a powerful oncolytic effect and antitumor immune response.(38−40)

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    There is an urgent need for highly effective and relatively nontoxic chemotherapeutics that can eradicate multidrug-resistance (MDR) tumors.(41) Because specific drug target-associated gene mutations(42) and drug efflux pump expression (e.g. P-glycoprotein and MDR proteins)(43) can decrease tumor sensitivity to traditional chemotherapy, the tumor membrane-lytic NCPs offer a highly desirable chemotherapeutic solution.(44) Meanwhile, in the cell membrane of many cancers, phosphatidylserine and O-glycosylated mucins are usually overexpressed, thereby causing these membranes to carry higher net negative charge than those of normal eukaryotic cells.(7,45) Therefore, the cationic oncolytic peptides or NCPs usually display the membrane potential-mediated selectivity. However, clinical development of oncolytic NCPs has been hampered by intrinsic peptide instability and subtherapeutic efficacy.(46) Based on the 10-amino acid wasp venom peptide, anoplin, an all-hydrocarbon stapling strategy was applied to yield potent, stable, and protease-resistant peptides with a relatively high degree of tumor cell selectivity.
    Prior efforts to develop stapled peptides involved a potential limitation with the sacrifice of side chain-bearing amino acids at the stapled positions, leading to reduction in solubility and selectivity.(47,48) By applying a side-chain-retention stapling strategy, we identified Ano-3s as an enhanced leading StAno with improved hydrophilicity, decreased hemolytic activity, stability to proteolytic degradation, and excellent oncolytic activity. Moreover, the therapeutic potential of selected StAnos was determined in two cancer models. Heterogeneity between these two tumor models led to different treatment outcomes, depending on peptide-induced immune responses that rely on CD8+ T cell infiltration in vivo. This is the first systematic report of the peptide stapling strategy applied in the improvement of oncolytic natural peptides and in-depth evaluation of their antitumor activities.
    Owing to intrinsic drawbacks of current oncolytic peptides, including short circulation half-life in vivo, poor bioavailability, and unfavorable systemic toxicity profiles, intratumoral injection is the reasonable choice for clinical administration. However, this limits the further clinical indications. In this case, our pioneering efforts using stapled chemical strategy may serve as the potential ideas to accelerate oncolytic peptides development as novel systematic chemotherapeutics with improved therapeutic applications. Furthermore, truncated modification(49) and targeted drug delivery systems(50) should also be further evaluated and studied as the potential strategies to overcome previously mentioned limitation in the future.

    Analytical Methods

    1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra were obtained on a Bruker Avance 600 MHz NMR Spectrometer (Germany). The chemical shifts of protons are given on the δ scale, ppm, with tetramethylsilane as the internal standard. All NMR experiments were conducted at room temperature unless otherwise stated. The 1H NMR spectra are reported as follows: δ/ppm (multiplicity, coupling constant J/Hz, and number of protons). Multiplicity is abbreviated as follows: s = singlet, d = doublet, t = triplet, m = multiplet, and dd = doublet of doublet. Coupling constants (J) are quoted in hertz and recorded to the nearest 0.1 Hz. The 13C NMR spectra are reported in δ/ppm. HR-Q-TOF-MS was measured on an Agilent 6538 UHD Accurate Mass Q-TOF LC/MS mass spectrometer (America). Analytical HPLC was run on an Agilent 1260 Infinity LC instrument using an analytical column (ZORBAX Eclipse XDB 80 Å C18, 4.6 × 150 mm, 3.5 μm particle size, flow rate 1.0 mL/min, r.t.). Analytical injections were monitored at 214 and 254 nm. All separations used a mobile phase of 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) (v/v) in water (solvent A) and 0.1% TFA (v/v) in MeCN (solvent B), with a linear gradient of 5–65% solvent B in 30 min at room temperature. Semipreparative HPLC was run on a Waters Auto Purification System (America) instrument using a semipreparative column (XBridge Prep C18, 19 × 250 mm, 10 μm particle size, flow rate 20 mL/min).     Synthesis of StAnos

    All peptides were prepared using standard Fmoc-based SPPS on a Rink Amide MBHA resin. Briefly, 400 mg resin was swelled with DCM (5 mL) for 20 min. Then, the resin was treated with 20% piperidine/dimethylformamide (DMF)/0.1 mol/L Oxyma pure twice (5 min × 2), followed by washing with DMF (5 times), DCM (5 times), and DMF (5 times) at 35 °C. For coupling of the natural amino acid, Fmoc-AA-OH (1 mmol), DIC (1 mmol), Oxyma pure (1 mmol), and NMP (6 mL) were mixed for 1 min and then added to the resin at 60 °C. After 15 min, the resin was washed with DMF (5 times), DCM (5 times), and DMF (5 times). The peptide couplings of S5, R8, Leu*, and Lys* were carried out over a single 2 h coupling cycle using 2 equiv of the Fmoc-protected amino acids at 60 °C. The deprotection, washing, coupling, and washing steps were repeated until all the amino acid residues were assembled. The peptide-bound resin was treated with 20% piperidine/DMF/0.1 mol/L Oxyma pure to remove the Fmoc group from the N-terminus. After the resin was washed, the resin was treated with 10 mM solution of Grubbs’ first-generation catalyst in DCE. After the first round of the 2 h metathesis, we repeated the same procedure for a second round of catalyst treatment with fresh catalyst solution, the peptide-resin was washed with DMF (5 times) and DCM (5 times). The peptide was cleaved from resin by treatment with cocktail TFA [TFA/triisopropylsilane (TIPS)/H2O = 95/2.5/2.5, v/v/v] for 2 h at room temperature. After completion of the cleavage reaction, TFA was evaporated by blowing with Ar. The crude peptides were obtained by precipitation with 40 mL of cold diethyl ether and centrifugation at 3500 rpm for 3 min (3 times). The supernatant diethyl ether was decanted from the centrifuge tube, and the crude peptide was allowed to air dry and purified by RP-HPLC to give the final product. All peptides possess a purity of at least 95%.

    Fluorescent Labeling of StAnos

    Cell Cultures

    Cell Viability Assay

    Cancer cells were plated in triplicate wells into a 96-well plate at a density of 5000 cells/well. After 24 h culture, the medium in the wells was replaced with fresh medium containing peptides at different dose; cells without peptides were treated as control. After 24 h incubation, cells viabilities were determined using Cell Counting Kit-8 (Dojindo, CK-04) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The cells were incubated with CCK-8 solution and cultured at 37 °C for another 1 h. Absorbance of the dissolved solutions was detected at 450 nm on a Cytation 5 (BioTek, America) microplate reader. The cell viability rate was calculated as follows: [(As – Ab)/(Ac – Ab)] × 100%, where As was the absorbance of the treatments of each group with different concentrations, Ac was the absorbance of the control treatments with medium, and Ab was the absorbance of the blank with only medium.

    Hemolysis Assay

    The hemolytic activity of the peptides was evaluated by determining hemoglobin release from erythrocyte suspensions of fresh mice blood. The cells were centrifuged, washed three times with physiological saline (NS), dispensed into 96-well plates as 200 μL of 4% (v/v) red blood cells in NS, and different peptide solutions were added to each well. Plates were incubated for 1 h at 37 °C and then centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 10 min. Samples (100 μL) of the supernatant were transferred to 96-well plates, and hemoglobin release was monitored by measuring absorbance at 570 nm (BioTek Cytation 5, America). Zero hemolysis was determined in NS (ANS), and 100% hemolysis was determined in 0.1% (v/v) Triton X-100 (ATriton). Hemolysis percentage hemolysis was calculated as (Apeptide – ANS)/(ATriton – ANS) × 100%.

    LDH Release Assay

    Lactate dehydrogenase release was performed by using cytotoxicity lactate dehydrogenase release assay Kit-WST (DOJINDO, CK-12). Briefly, 4T1 cells were plated in triplicate wells into a 96-well plate at a density of 5000 cells/well. After 24 h culture, the medium in the wells was replaced with fresh medium containing peptides at different dose; cells without peptides were treated as control. After 24 h incubation, lysis buffer was added as a positive control for 30 min. The working solution was then added to each well for 30 min at room temperature, followed by adding the stop solution. The absorbance at 490 nm was measured by a microplate reader (BioTek Cytation 5, America).

    ATP Release Assay

    The supernatant of peptide-treated 4T1 cells was analyzed using an enhanced ATP assay kit (Beyotime, S0027). Cells were seeded (20,000 cells/well) in a 96-well plate and treated with peptides (50 μM) for varying time points (5, 15, 30, and 60 min). All experiments were performed in duplicate at a total of 3 times. Positive controls were untreated cells exposed to a serum-free medium containing lysis buffer. Negative controls were untreated cells exposed to a serum-free medium alone. Samples were analyzed using a luminometer (Tecan Spark, Switzerland) according to the manufacturer’s protocol.

    Observation of the Peptide Effect Using a 3D Cell Explorer

    4T1 cells were seeded at 10,000 cells/well in a complete medium in a 20 mm dish and were allowed to adhere overnight. Cells were washed twice with a serum-free RPMI-1640, treated with peptides dissolved in RPMI, and investigated using Bright Field on a 3D cell explorer (Nanolive SA, Switzerland). The microscope was equipped with an incubation chamber with CO2 and temperature control.

    Observation of FITC-Peptide Translocation Using Confocal Microscopy

    4T1 cells (30,000 cells/well) in a 20 mm dish were treated with 16 μM FITC-labeled peptides for 1 h, and the cells without peptide treatment served as controls. After washing three times with PBS, the cell membranes were labeled with DiD perchlorate (5 μM), and the cell nuclei were stained with Hoechst 33342 (50 μM). Peptide translocation into the cells was monitored using a Leica TCS SP8 confocal microscope (Leica Microsystems, Germany). The excitation wavelength was set at 488 nm, and the emission wavelengths were set at 505–530 nm for the FITC-labeled peptides. The excitation and emission wavelengths were set at 644 and 663 nm, respectively, for the DiD perchlorate-stained cell membranes. For cell nuclei, the excitation and emission wavelengths were set at 350 and 461 nm, respectively.

    LIVE/DEAD Cell Viability/Cytotoxicity

    4T1 cells were plated in triplicate wells into a 96-well plate at a density of 10,000 cells/well. After 24 h culture, the live cells were labeled with 2 μM calcien-AM (Ex: 488 nm and Em: 515 nm). Cells were washed twice with a serum-free RPMI-1640 and treated with 10 μM peptide and 8 μM PI (Ex: 535 nm and Em: 617 nm) dissolved in RPMI-1640. The cells were photographed for varying time points by SparkCyto (Tecan, Switzerland). The microscope was equipped with an incubation chamber with CO2 and temperature control.

    Protease Stability

    The peptides were dissolved in PBS buffer solutions (50 mM, pH = 7.4) to a final concentration of 1 mM, respectively. α-Chymotrypsin was dissolved in PBS buffer (50 mM, containing 2 mM of CaCl2, pH = 7.4) to a final concentration of 0.5 ng/μL. Then, the peptide solutions (100 μL) were incubated with α-chymotrypsin solution (1 mL) at room temperature. 100 μL of digestion mixture was taken at the different time and then quenched with 20 μL of hydrochloric acid (1 M). The solution of the α-chymotrypsin peptide fragments was monitored by HPLC at different times to determine the fraction of protease degradation at 214 nm. Each experiment was performed in triplicate.
