    Abstract

    Retinoic acid receptor-related orphan receptor gamma-t (RORγt) is considered to be the master transcription factor for the development of Th17 cells that produce proinflammatory cytokines such as IL-17A. Overproportionate Th17 cell abundance is associated with the pathogenesis of many inflammatory conditions including psoriasis. In a high-throughput fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) screen, we identified compound 1 as a hit with promising lipophilic efficiency (LipE). Using structure-based drug design based on a number of X-ray cocrystal structures, we morphed this hit class into potent imidazoles, exemplified by compound 3. To improve the poor absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) properties of neutral imidazoles, we extended our ligands with carboxylic acid substituents toward a polar, water-rich area of the protein. This highly lipophilicity-efficient modification ultimately led to the discovery of compound 14, a potent and selective inhibitor of RORγt with good ADME properties and excellent in vivo pharmacokinetics. This compound showed good efficacy in an in vivo delayed-type hypersensitivity pharmacology model in rats.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Retinoic acid receptor-related orphan receptor gamma-t (RORγt) is the key transcription factor that is required for the differentiation, effector function, and cytokine production of IL-17-producing T-helper 17 (Th17) cells and innate immune cells.(1,2) Although these cells are important for host immunity against various bacteria and against Candida, they also produce pro-inflammatory cytokines that are drivers of various autoimmune and inflammatory diseases including psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, and rheumatoid arthritis.(3,4) Based on the importance of this transcription factor to direct pro-inflammatory Th17 cytokine production and Th17 effector function, RORγt is considered to be a highly attractive drug target for modulating the pathological Th17/IL-17 immune axis. Two isoforms of this nuclear hormone receptor are known, the widely expressed isoform RORγ and the immune cell-specific isoform RORγt.(5) As the ligand-binding domain (LBD) is identical, low molecular weight binders will most likely modulate the transcriptional activity of the RORγ and the RORγt isoform. Various low molecular weight ligands targeting the LBD of RORγt were recently identified and some of these were shown to attenuate pro-inflammatory diseases in various in vivo rodent models.(6−11) Our goal was to identify a novel, structurally different compound class and to advance a compound toward a clinical candidate. Here, we report how we discovered N-aryl imidazoles as potent inhibitors of RORγt after a scaffold-morph of a screening hit. We disclose how we improved the series properties and ultimately identified a compound that showed in vivo efficacy after a low oral dose.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    Medicinal Chemistry

    Our first hit-finding effort toward inhibitors of the RORγt function involved a high-throughput screen using a TR-FRET format using the RORγt LBD and fluorescently labeled RIP140 as a co-activator peptide. The TR-FRET screen was run in order to identify compounds that disrupt the interaction of the RORγt LBD and RIP140 (“displacers of co-activator binding”)(12) in the anticipation that this mechanism of action (MOA) will result in an inhibition of transcription on a cellular level (“RORγt inhibitors”). This screen identified a number of hits, and many of them were highly lipophilic. Amongst these hits, ester 1 caught our attention as an attractive starting point because of its more favorable lipophilic efficiency (LipE), see Table 1. Originating from a previous Ciba-Geigy fungicide program, this hit is a mixture of four components: racemic with respect to the stereocenter next to the ester and in addition atropisomeric because of hindered rotation around the N-aryl bond. When we determined the X-ray structure of ester 1 bound to RORγt (see Figures S1 and S2), we found that only one isomer cocrystallized as a density fit, for any of the other isomers was not possible. In order to reduce the complexity caused by atropisomers, we aimed for a symmetrical substitution on the aromatic ring and found the ortho and ortho-dichloro substitution pattern to be optimal. In addition, we found that the thiophene ring can be replaced by a furan ring that showed a slightly better potency in most cases. Intriguingly, the ester substitution pattern showed a steep structure–activity relationship (SAR). Although FRET activity for methyl esters was barely detectable, the comparatively small change to an ethyl ester resulted in a large activity increase. Adding additional ester bulk such as in isopropyl ester 2 increased the activity even further. A cocrystal structure of isopropyl ester 2 with the RORγt LBD helped us rationalize these SAR observations and generate ideas for further optimization (Figure 1). Only the (S)-enantiomer of ester 2 was observed in the electron density. The compound binds close to the helix 11–helix 12 interface, which is critical for the transcriptionally active conformation, and was therefore termed the “business end”.(13) The H-bond between H479 (on helix 11) and Y502 (on helix 12), shielded from the bulk solvent in a highly lipophilic environment, is critical for this assembly. When this interaction is stabilized, such as with cholesterol sulfate,(13) helix 12 is part of a groove (together with helix 3 and helix 4/5), and the helical LXXLL co-activator motif can bind to the LBD (Figure S4A). However, when the helix 11–helix 12 interaction is destabilized and helix 12 is displaced, co-activators can no longer bind, and transcription is unlikely to occur. Compound 2 destabilizes this interface by the following distinct changes within the binding site: (1) the amide bond carbonyl forms a strong H-bond acceptor with H479 (bond length 2.7 Å), depriving Y502 of its binding partner, (2) the isopropyl ester sterically clashes with W317 (on helix 11), and as a consequence, (3) W317 is pushed away and binds into the area that used to be occupied by Y502. As a result, helix 12 is destabilized, does not form the co-activator-binding groove, and co-activator binding is prevented overall (Figure S4B). We termed this “push–pull” mechanism (“push” on W317 and “pull” on H479), and both elements in the ligand molecule are necessary in order to destabilize co-activator binding. In addition, compound 2 complements the largely hydrophobic surface of this part of the binding site via undirected van der Waals contacts. This binding mode model explains the steep ester SAR mentioned earlier: a methyl ester is likely too small to cause W317 movement; thus, co-activator displacement is not observed in the FRET assay. Compared to ligands that extend all the way to the “sulfate pocket”,(13) we felt that this binding mode close to the “business end” was advantageous as we should be able to keep the overall size of the molecule small because of the proximity to the crucial helix 11–helix 12 interface.

    In line with sufficient potency in the FRET assay, ester 2 also displayed cellular assay activity and reduced PMA + CD3 mAb-induced IL-17A production (IC50 = 452 nM) in a T-cell line assay stably expressing RORγt. In order to further improve potency, we probed limiting the conformational flexibility and designed imidazoles 3 and 4. In addition to an overall shape overlap, we expected that a large position-4 substituent would lead to W317 displacement and that the position-3 nitrogen would act as a strong H-bond acceptor for H479.(14) This scaffold morph was successful and led to the first very potent compounds. We successfully solved the cocrystal structure for imidazole 4, and Figure 2 shows this structure superimposed with the structure obtained for ester 2. As intended, the binding mode is similar, and both ligands overlay well: the 4-benzyl substituent of ligand 4 dislodges W317, and the imidazole position-3 nitrogen accepts the H-bond of H479 as anticipated. We concluded that the affinity increase for imidazoles compared to esters such as 2 can largely be attributed to the conformational constraint. Cellularly highly active imidazoles 3 and 4 were crucial tool compounds to help establish important project assays, in particular, our Th17 assay (inhibition of IL-17A production in either CD4 positive T-cells polarized with a Th17 cytokine mix) and whole blood assays [concanavalin A (ConA)-stimulated rat/human whole blood], see Table 1.
    Although these first imidazoles were highly potent, we realized that this activity gain was mainly lipophilicity driven. A log P above the limit of quantification (>6.9) implied a LipE drop to <1.5, and in vitro ADME parameters were poor as expected (Table 2): low solubility and very high microsomal clearance (human clearance correlated well with rat clearance for this series, data not shown). In order to progress candidate molecules to in vivo experiments, we needed to identify more lipophilic efficient analogues, and the permissive SAR space is now described in more detail. R1 substituents needed to be lipophilic in order to maintain potency. Although larger alkyl or substituted alkyl R1 substituents were tolerated, we found that additional molecular weight was not needed and that a trifluoromethyl group was sufficient to maintain activity for most combinations. At position-5, only small substituents were accepted: a methyl group generally led to more potent derivatives than leaving this position unsubstituted. Being aware of the risk of furan rings to form reactive metabolites,(17) we discovered that an additional 2-chloro or 2-methyl substituent on the furan was able to further increase the activity. Substituting thiophenes in this way has been shown to reduce the amount of glutathione (GSH) adducts,(18) and we hypothesized that in analogy, 2-chloro or 2-methyl furans may also bear a reduced potential for reactive metabolite formation. We tested representative 2-chlorofuran derivatives in metabolite identification studies and did not find any GSH adducts. Table 2 contains key derivatives that illustrate our LipE-driven optimization efforts toward a more favorable ADME space. Compounds 3 and 5 are representatives of a highly potent, yet highly lipophilic chemical space. In order to significantly lower the lipophilicity, we replaced the two aromatic chlorines with nitriles, switched from the furan to an oxazole and omitted the 5-methyl group on the imidazole. Compound 6 is indeed much less lipophilic (log P = 2.0), and while solubility markedly increased, microsomal clearance remained high (RLM Clint = 153 μL·min–1·mg–1). In contrast, compound 7 had a significantly reduced metabolic turnover (RLM Clint = 33 μL·min–1·mg–1), presumably because of a favorable combination of lipophilicity space (log P = 1.1) and a complete lack of metabolically vulnerable terminal methyl groups. However, compound 7 only showed residual FRET activity. We had to accept that although we were making lipophilicity efficient improvements (LipE = 5.0 and 4.6 for compounds 6 and 7, respectively), this progress was futile as FRET activity was poor in absolute terms and insufficient for cellular activity. We concluded that our initial strategy to stay close to the “business end” was successful in terms of identifying comparatively small ligands with the desired functional activity to disturb the helix 11–helix 12 interface. At the same time, we learned that this part of the binding pocket is largely coated by lipophilic amino acid residues which severely restricted our efforts to identify ligands with good ADME properties. As a consequence, we directed our efforts to find polar areas of the protein that would be within reach with an extension on our ligands. To this end, we took advantage of the extensive protein cavity knowledge based on a number of X-ray cocrystal structures. Figure 3 shows an overlay of the ligand 4 cocrystal structure with the crystallographic water molecules identified in 18 other RORγt LBD crystal structures (most of them internally generated). This overlay suggested the presence of a water-rich area that is part of a channel leading to solvent. Examination of the ligand 4 cocrystal structure using Water Analysis Tools for Molecular Dynamics (WATMD)(19) revealed a high oxygen and hydrogen voxel occupancy in this channel (Figure S3). A SiteMap(20) analysis of this structure also indicated the presence of H-bond donor and acceptor sites in this area of the protein. Taken together, we were confident that reaching out into this part of the protein with polar extensions on our ligands would be tolerated and lead to either direct or water-mediated interactions with the protein. In order to substantially lower lipophilicity, we designed molecules containing a terminal carboxylic acid via the exit vector next to the aromatic chlorosubstituent, see Figure 3 for an example supported by docking studies.(21) As the phenyl ring is unsymmetrically substituted in this design, atropisomers around the aromatic C–N bond were generated as a consequence. For analogues with an o,o-dichlorophenyl substituent, atropisomers can be separated by chiral chromatography, and they do not interconvert to any detectable degree even at elevated temperatures. Analogues with a 5-methyl group on the imidazole are particularly stable. For one intermediate, we experimentally determined the rotation barrier which was also in reasonable agreement with quantum-chemical calculations (see Synthetic Chemistry section and Supporting Information). Compounds 8–14 are single atropisomers, and only the data for the active enantiomer are reported in the tables. When both atropisomers were tested in the FRET assay, there was always one clear active isomer and the other enantiomer was typically >100-fold less active. These observations are in good agreement with the binding mode where the additional substituent on the distomer severely clashes with the protein structure. From our first set of compounds designed this way, azetidine-linked carboxylic acids 8 and 9 were amongst the most potent. The X-ray cocrystal structure of compound 9 (Figure 4) shows that the carboxylic acid group is embedded in a water-mediated H-bonding network between the backbone carbonyl groups of F377 and H323 explaining the good activity. Compared to neutral imidazoles 3 and 5, lipophilicity could be reduced by more than three log units, and a favorable lipophilicity space combined with no significant loss in the FRET activity (LipE = 5.4 for compound 8 and 5.3 for compound 9) was a breakthrough in the series as we could place compounds in a favorable ADME space for the first time. Similar to their neutral counterparts, these carboxylic acid derivatives were permeable and their FRET activity translated into cells. As for the neutral matched pair 6 and 7, there was also a noticeable difference in the microsomal turnover between carboxylic acids 8 and 9. In both cases, metabolic stability was achieved by omitting terminal methyl groups on the imidazole position-4 substituent. Amide 10 and tetrazole 11 confirm that it is the charge at neutral pH that is important and not the substitution pattern by itself. Amide 10 is neutral because of its increased lipophilicity prone to metabolism (log D@pH7.4 = 4.9, RLM Clint = 169 μL·min–1·mg–1), whereas acidic tetrazole 11 (log D@pH7.4 = 2.5, RLM Clint = 49 μL·min–1·mg–1) is metabolically turned over to a similar degree as carboxylic acid 9. In summary, to keep excessive metabolism under control and to avoid metabolic switching, compounds need to satisfy two conditions: (1) placement in a favorable lipophilicity space (log P/D < 3.0) and (2) primary metabolic weak spots need to be blocked (furan substitution, no terminal methyl groups). Our next goal was to confirm that our optimization of in vitro ADME parameters translated into favorable in vivo pharmacokinetics. The rat PK results for compounds 8 and 9 are summarized in Table 3. As anticipated by its in vitro profile, compound 8 exhibited high in vivo clearance (CL = 228 mL·min–1·kg–1), and as a result, the overall exposure was poor (AUC d.n. = 156 nM·h). In contrast, compound 9 showed a markedly reduced clearance (CL = 30 mL·min–1·kg–1) and exposure increase (AUC d.n. = 1179 nM·h). In addition, bioavailability (BAV = 80%) also improved significantly.

    With the confirmation that our in vitro ADME-based optimization led to the desired in vivo profile, we turned our attention toward generating compounds with improved cellular potency to assess the effect of RORγt inhibition in an in vivo model. We discovered that compound 12, a homologue of compound 9, was 2–7 times more potent, and its single-dose pharmacokinetic parameters were similar to those of compound 9. However, when we dosed 50 mg/kg per day over 3 days, we were surprised to find that the AUC on day 3 dropped ca. 10-fold (compared to day 1), leading to insufficient target coverage over time. In addition, we identified a severe time-dependent CYP3A4 inhibition (TDI) flag for compound 12 that we needed to address. We decided to focus our optimization on reducing the TDI signal in the hope that major structural modifications would also lead to a mitigation of the exposure-drop after multiple administrations. To identify structural motifs that are associated with a high kobs value, we profiled a number of orthogonally substituted imidazoles in the TDI assay. Figure 5A illustrates that although the presence of a 2-furyl substituent undoubtedly leads to compounds with a higher TDI kobs value, still approximately half of the derivatives were in the low risk bin (kobs < 0.010) or in the medium risk bin (kobs < 0.025).(23) However, the simultaneous presence of both a 2-furyl and a 4-trifluoromethyl group on the imidazole markedly increases the chances for a high risk kobs value (kobs > 0.025, Figure 5B) and not a single compound containing these two structural motifs displayed a kobs in the low/medium risk bins. As the 4-CF3 substituent on the imidazole had been a crucial structural feature to maintain low clearance, we needed to identify a suitable replacement. In search for a bulky, yet metabolically stable motif, we came across the trifluoromethylcyclopropyl group that has been suggested as a tert-butyl group replacement.(24) Although we knew that any replacement of the furan would result in a loss of affinity, we tried again to avoid this functionality at this point. Based on earlier SAR observations, we knew that a cyclopropyl replacement for the furan was associated with the least activity loss. We anticipated that we could counterbalance the expected loss in affinity by additional interactions of a well-designed substituent within the polar channel. To this end, we designed a large virtual library of carboxylic acids with different linkers to the aromatic ring and docked them into the protein using flexible water positioning.(25) We prioritized docking poses that extended into the polar channel rather over those that stretched toward the sulfate pocket. Next, we synthesized a selection of library members with the best docking score, among them is the 2-azaspiro[3.3]heptane-containing carboxylic acid 14 (Figure 6). Table 4 shows that these changes indeed reduced the risk for reactive metabolite formation; both trifluoromethylcyclopropyl-containing analogues 13 and 14 displayed a very low kobs. We tested both compounds in multiple-dose PK studies (3 days) and found that while furan 13 still showed a considerable exposure drop after 3 days, cyclopropyl analogue 14 maintained exposure after multiple administrations. A clear rationale for these differences is lacking.

    In Vitro and In Vivo Characterization of Compound 14

    Carboxylic acid 14 displayed all the desired on-target characteristics, and it is also selective over other isoforms, other nuclear receptors, and safety panel targets (LBD-Gal4 luciferase reporter gene assays: RORγt IC50 = 151 nM; RORα, RORβ, FXR, RXRα, ERα, PPARγ, PR, PXR, GR, and vitD3 IC50 > 10 μM, safety target panel assays see the Supporting Information). The low lipophilicity (log D @pH7.4 = 2.0) and simultaneous blocking of all metabolic weak spots resulted in excellent in vitro and in vivo clearance (RLM = 14 μL·min–1·mg–1, in vivo CL < 3 mL·min–1·kg–1, Table 3), good exposure, and supportive half-life in rats. Aqueous solubility was sufficient (HT-sol = 0.146 mM), and a high BAV (90%) was observed after p.o. dosing as a suspension. Consequently, compound 14 was qualified to be tested in our rat delayed-type hypersensitivity (DTH) model. Figure 7A shows that while a dose of 1 mg/kg of compound 14 did not lead to a reduction of the ear swelling ratio after an immunization/challenge protocol using methylated BSA as the antigen, doses of 5 and 15 mg/kg inhibited ear swelling to a similar degree as an anti-IL-17A mAb which served as the positive control. In addition, compound 14 reduced the frequency of IL-17-producing cells isolated from draining lymph nodes in a dose-dependent manner with a maximum reduction of ca. 50% (Figure 7B).

    Synthetic Chemistry

    The synthesis of isopropyl ester 2 is shown in Scheme 1. When 2,6-dichloroaniline was acylated with furan-2-carbonyl chloride, we exclusively observed bis N-acylation. Mild basic saponification of this bisacylated intermediate led to the formation of N-(2,6-dichlorophenyl)furan-2-carboxamide. N-alkylation with ethyl 2-bromopropanoate, followed by saponification and re-esterification, led to isopropyl ester 2. The synthetic approach toward N-aryl imidazoles that were used early in the project is shown in Scheme 2, exemplified by the synthesis of imidazole 4. 2,6-Dichloroaniline was N-alkylated with 3-bromo-1-phenylbutan-2-one, and then acylated with furan-2-carbonyl chloride. The resulting ketoamide was cyclized to imidazole 4 using ammonium acetate in acetic acid. This condensation was typically low-yielding, and we found that using amidines as intermediates led to a much improved imidazole formation. Schemes 3 and 4 illustrate this synthetic approach which was used for all remaining N-aryl imidazoles in this article. Amidine A1 was formed via Lewis acid-promoted addition of the aniline to an appropriately functionalized nitrile. Amidine N-alkylation followed by cyclocondensation led to N-aryl imidazole intermediates A2 and A3. Neutral analogues 3 and 5 were also prepared accordingly. 2,6-Dicyanophenyl imidazoles 6 and 7 were prepared via palladium-catalyzed cyanation of dichloro intermediate A2. For electron deficient R1 substituents, the direct incorporation of a methyl group into imidazole position-5 via the bromoketone building block (A1 → A3) was often sluggish. In these cases, intermediate A2 was prepared, and the methyl group was introduced after Buchwald–Hartwig amination of the aminoester side chain (Scheme 4) via bromination/iodination of the imidazole followed by palladium-catalyzed cross-coupling using trimethylboroxine (A4 → A5). Most atropisomer separations were performed at the stage of A5 intermediates using chiral chromatography. Final ester saponification liberated acids 8, 9, 12, and 13. Further functionalization of carboxylic acid 9 via ammonolysis of the acyl-imidazole gave carboxamide 10. Tetrazole 11 was obtained from amide 10 via dehydration to the nitrile and subsequent [3 + 2] cycloaddition with trimethyltin azide (Scheme 5).
    Scheme 6 shows the synthesis of compound 14, which follows the strategy outlined in Schemes 3 and 4. Lewis acid-catalyzed reaction of aniline 15 with cyclopropanenitrile gave amidine 16. Condensation with 2-bromo-1-(1-(trifluoromethyl)cyclopropyl)ethan-1-one under basic conditions then led to the formation of racemic imidazole 17. The enantiomers were separated by chromatography using a chiral stationary phase. In order to assess the racemization risk for subsequent reactions run at higher temperatures, we experimentally determined the rotation barrier by solving the Eyring equation using four different epimerization rate constants (deduced from monitoring the racemization of 17b in DMA at 170, 180, 190, and 200 °C). Using this protocol, the rotation barrier of 17b was calculated to be ΔH (rot.) = 31 kcal/mol, inferring a racemization half-life of 2.7 × 106 years at 25 °C, 98 days at 110 °C or 2.5 min at 250 °C. Using this knowledge, the undesired atropisomer 17b was recycled by re-equilibration to the racemate at 250 °C within minutes. Next, the desired atropisomer 17a was converted to ester 18 via a Buchwald–Hartwig amination at 110 °C, a temperature where negligible racemization occurs within the timeframe of the experiment. The 5-methyl group on the imidazole was then introduced in two steps via bromination, followed by Suzuki coupling with trimethylboroxine. Final saponification of the ester led to carboxylic acid 14. The absolute (S) configuration of the atropisomer was confirmed by X-ray crystallography (Figure 8).

    Conclusions

    Jump To
    A detailed understanding of the binding mode of high-throughput screening hit 1 as revealed by the X-ray cocrystal structure enabled us to morph this scaffold into o,o-disubstituted N-aryl imidazoles as a novel class of RORγt inhibitors. The prototypical imidazoles 3 and 4 displayed ADME liabilities that are commonly observed for such highly lipophilic compounds, among them low solubility and low metabolic stability. Once polar substituents extending ligand binding into a water-rich area of the protein were introduced, our LipE-guided optimization approach led to compounds in a good ADME space. Again, knowledge gained by a number of cocrystal structures was instrumental for the success of this approach. We effectively replaced two substituents on the imidazole that had caused a strong TDI potential and ultimately identified compound 14 as a candidate for in vivo profiling. Compound 14 showed excellent pharmacokinetic properties in rats and dose-dependently reduced ear swelling in an mBSA-induced DTH model in rats. The discovery of the promising profile of compound 14 coincided with the finding that prolonged RORγt inhibitor treatment in rats causes progressive thymic alterations similar to those in RORγ-deficient mice (constitutive knockout and induced knockout in adult mice) prior to T cell lymphoma development.(27) There is a risk that RORγt inhibitors ultimately phenocopy the observations with RORγ-deficient mice, and long-term studies are needed in order to assess the safety of oral RORγt inhibitors for chronic indications.
