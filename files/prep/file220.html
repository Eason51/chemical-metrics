    Abstract

    Despite major advances in the β-lactamase inhibitor field, certain enzymes remain refractory to inhibition by agents recently introduced. Most important among these are the class B (metallo) enzyme NDM-1 of Enterobacteriaceae and the class D (OXA) enzymes of Acinetobacter baumannii. Continuing the boronic acid program that led to vaborbactam, efforts were directed toward expanding the spectrum to allow treatment of a wider range of organisms. Through key structural modifications of a bicyclic lead, stepwise gains in spectrum of inhibition were achieved, ultimately resulting in QPX7728 (35). This compound displays a remarkably broad spectrum of inhibition, including class B and class D enzymes, and is little affected by porin modifications and efflux. Compound 35 is a promising agent for use in combination with a β-lactam antibiotic for the treatment of a wide range of multidrug resistant Gram-negative bacterial infections, by both intravenous and oral administration.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    It has now been several years since the scourge of carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae was recognized and the call was sounded for new agents by WHO and other public health authorities.(1) β-Lactamase inhibitor (BLI) combination products have played a major role in the response, with FDA approvals awarded for avibactam (with ceftazidime, 2015),(2) vaborbactam (with meropenem, 2017),(3) and relebactam (with imipenem–cilastatin, 2019)(4) (Figure 1). While these agents address the problem of class A serine carbapenemases such as KPC, they have no activity against metallo-β-lactamases such as NDM-1 and also have no activity against the problematic class D enzymes of Acinetobacter baumannii, such as OXA-23. Hence, there is an ongoing need for inhibitors showing a broader spectrum that captures these other types of β-lactamases. Current work in this area has been reviewed recently.(5,6)
    When our team first began synthesizing boronic acid inhibitors in 2009, many had doubts about whether it would be possible to achieve potent inhibition of both serine and metalloenzymes with a single compound. However, recent publications have described boronates with activity against some enzymes from both classes.(7,8) Herein we describe our own efforts to achieve broader spectrum, higher potency, and reduced susceptibility to uptake resistance mechanisms (porin modifications and efflux), resulting in discovery of QPX7728 (35), a remarkably potent and broad-spectrum inhibitor with potential for both intravenous and oral application.

    Early Evolution of Bicyclic Boronate BLIs

    The benzoxaborinine 1 (Figure 2), first reported by researchers at the University of Toronto,(9) has proven to be an extremely important lead structure in the β-lactamase inhibitor arena because of its broad spectrum of inhibition of serine β-lactamases. Early efforts to develop compounds included those by Protez Pharmaceuticals(10−13) and Rempex Pharmaceuticals,(14,15) with RPX7009 (vaborbactam)(16,17) being the first inhibitor to enter the clinic and achieve regulatory approvals in the U.S. and EU. Other groups have recently entered other boronate BLIs into clinical development.(18,19)

    Effect of Substitution Ortho to the Carboxylate

    Soon after vaborbactam was advanced into development, members of the team that discovered this agent began investigations into compounds related to benzoxaborinine 1. Work by this team(14,20) and others(21−24) explored a wide variety of acyl groups on the amine α to boron. As was observed in the vaborbactam discovery effort,(17) this region is largely insensitive to modification, as potent, broad-spectrum activity is obtained with a wide variety of substituents. Conversely, little had been described with respect to substitution on the aromatic ring. Thus, our initial efforts were directed toward determining the effect of such substituents.

    One of the issues that we had encountered with earlier unsubstituted analogs in this series was a propensity to form oligomers in solution. This phenomenon was observed by HPLC as longer retention time peaks whose mass corresponded to dimers, trimers, etc. The extent of formation was pH- and concentration-dependent. Such species were not observed with monocyclic BLIs such as vaborbactam. Although the structures were never formally characterized, we believed these to be of the type shown in Figure 3, as boronic acids are known to complex with salicylates.(25) Representative chromatograms, LC–MS data, and tentative structural assignments are provided in the Supporting Information. While we believed these issues were manageable, we envisioned that the formation of such species in an intravenous dosage form would complicate the toxicological qualification of impurities to enable human administration.
    Upon synthesis of ortho-substituted analogs, we quickly learned that the presence of an ortho-substituent markedly reduces the extent of oligomer formation. The extent of oligomerization was evaluated by HPLC–UV at various concentrations at pH 7.8 and is expressed as area % of monomer (the desired form).     Another benefit of substitution ortho to the carboxylate concerned the extent to which the MIC in P. aeruginosa was affected by the MexAB-OprM efflux pump. To evaluate the impact of substitution in this region on efflux, the plasmid expressing the gene encoding the KPC-2 carbapenemase was introduced into two isogenic strains of P. aeruginosa, PAM1032 and PAM1154, that overexpressed or lacked MexAB-OprM, respectively. Biapenem is not affected by efflux in P. aeruginosa, and both strains had the same biapenem MIC of 32 μg/mL due to the expression of KPC-2. The efflux index (EI) for each BLI was defined as the ratio of MPC64 values in PAM1032/KPC-2 (efflux proficient) and PAM1154/KPC-2 (efflux deficient).

    α-Thio Analogs with Much Improved Metallo Activity

    While in this compound series the potent inhibition of a broad spectrum of serine β-lactamases was well-established, activity against metalloenzymes (class B) had not been reported at that time. Our modeling studies suggested that members of this series would be capable of binding in the active site of metallo-β-lactamases such as NDM-1. Compounds were therefore evaluated in biochemical and microbiological assays to determine the extent of inhibition of this enzyme. Indeed, compound 4s, in addition to being a potent inhibitor of the serine carbapenemases KPC-2 (class A) and OXA-48 (class D), demonstrated modest inhibition of NDM-1 (class B) with a Ki of 7.4 μM (Table 4). Whole-cell activity against NDM-1 producing strains, however, was lacking.

    In parallel with pursuit of the above series, one of our chemists prepared sulfur-linked compound 6a, in part because it was easily accessible from the α-chloroboronate intermediate being used to make amide-linked analogs. These efforts resulted in a 300-fold improvement in potency against NDM-1 (Ki = 0.024 μM). Importantly, in whole cells this compound at a concentration of 0.6 μg/mL restored susceptibility to biapenem of an NDM-1 producing strain of K. pneumoniae (Table 4). The potency of compound 6a was not affected by increasing concentrations of zinc (up to 0.6 mM), suggesting a mechanism other than zinc chelation or competition with zinc for binding to the protein. Importantly, this compound did not inhibit any members of a panel of human metalloproteases. Recently, other reports have appeared on observations of metallo activity with certain acylamido analogs.(7,8)

Initial efforts in this series were conducted in the series with no substituent ortho to the carboxylate, with the intention of optimizing that substituent later. Compounds 6a–r were obtained by reaction of chloroboronate 2 with the appropriate nucleophile, followed by deprotection (Scheme 2). Displacement by sulfur nucleophiles was straightforward, whereas displacement by oxygen nucleophiles failed in most cases; thus, only two examples are shown.

    Removal of the Substituent Next to Boron

    To further broaden the scope of our findings, we sought to replace the methoxy-bearing carbon atom of 7a with a nitrogen atom. As the synthesis of this target (9) was expected to be challenging, we began with a model system lacking the substituent next to boron. The synthesis of this compound (16) is shown in Scheme 3. Saponification of methyl 3-benzyloxy-4-bromo-2-pyridinecarboxylate (10) followed by re-esterification affords tert-butyl ester 12. Palladium-catalyzed installation of the vinyl group using potassium vinyltrifluoroborate affords 13, which is subjected to iridium-catalyzed hydroboration to give boronate 14. Hydrogenolysis of the benzyl group gives ester 15; the tert-butyl ester was then hydrolyzed using TFA in triethylsilane. The crude product 16 was purified first as the free acid and then was converted to the sodium salt for repurification.
    Upon successful synthesis of 16, we observed far better activity than was expected, given the lack of a substituent thought to be required for potent inhibition. We immediately sought to capitalize on our previous observations that the fluoro and methoxy substituents generally afforded the best activity in the benzo series.     Compound 17b was advanced to further evaluation and displayed an excellent PK profile in the rat, including 50% oral bioavailability. Further evaluation in the dog and monkey, however, revealed significant oxidative deboronation to afford the long-lived metabolite 18 (Figure 4). The extremely slow clearance of 18 was attributed to high serum protein binding (approaching 100%). The generation of a substantial level of a long-lived metabolite was viewed as a development risk, and further efforts to advance 17b into development were suspended.

    Optimization of Cyclopropyl Analogs

    Buoyed by these findings, we sought to prepare several close-in cyclopropyl analogs; the results are shown in Table 10.     Structural Biology of Compound 35

    The complex crystal structures of 35 with KPC-2, NDM-1, OXA-48, and VIM-2 (Figure 5) were obtained through soaking and cocrystallization experiments (see Experimental Section). In the serine enzymes KPC-2 and OXA-48, the catalytic serine residue is covalently bound to the boron atom of the inhibitor. In the metalloenzymes NDM-1 and VIM-2, the catalytic water molecule is covalently bound to the boron atom. The conformation of 35 is nearly identical in the various structures, with the exception of the carboxylate moiety which is nearly coplanar in the metalloenzymes and closer to orthogonal in the serine enzymes. While the polar interactions vary from one structure to another, in all cases the hydrophobic region of 35 is packed against lipophilic (predominantly aromatic) side chains.

    Microbiology

    Compound 35 (at a fixed concentration of 8 μg/mL) was evaluated in combination with cefepime, ceftolozane, and meropenem against panels of Enterobacterales, including Klebsiella pneumoniae (n = 511), E. coli (n = 297), Enterobacter cloacae complex (n = 88), and other organisms (n = 119); of these, 507 isolates expressed ESBL enzymes and 508 were carbapenem-resistant isolates. Compound 35 was also tested against carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii (n = 503) and Pseudomonas aeruginosa (n = 500). The MIC50/MIC90 values are shown in Table 12. Comparative data with the marketed agents ceftazidime–avibactam and meropenem–vaborbactam are also shown. All three combinations with 35 were highly effective against Enterobacterales producing ESBLs as well as strains expressing the KPC and OXA-48 carbapenemases. In strains expressing metalloenzymes (NDM and VIM), cefepime and meropenem potencies were enhanced to MIC90 values of 1 and 2 μg/mL, respectively, whereas ceftolozane achieved little benefit. This is attributed to a high ceftolozane hydrolyzing activity of MBLs combined with a lower (compared to serine enzymes) inhibition of MBLs by 35. Against carbapenem-resistant A. baumannii, only meropenem potency was adequately enhanced, whereas against P. aeruginosa, all three combinations achieved an MIC90 ≤ 8 μg/mL. Studies in strains overexpressing and/or deficient in efflux pumps and porin modifications showed that 35 is largely unaffected by these common resistance mechanisms (data not shown). Interestingly, 35 displays weak antibacterial activity of its own, with a modal MIC of 16 μg/mL against Enterobacterales (range of <1 to ≥32 μg/mL); in general this activity is at much higher concentration than the potentiated MICs and therefore is unlikely to be a major contributor to the activity of the combinations. Overall, the results of these studies demonstrate the broad utility of 35 and that there may be benefit to more than one combination being available to the clinician.

    Efficacy in Infection Models

    Compound 35 was evaluated in a 24 h neutropenic mouse thigh infection model against Klebsiella pneumoniae strain KP1244 (Figure 6). This strain expresses KPC-3, SHV-11, and SHV-12 and is resistant to carbapenems, with an MIC to meropenem of >64 μg/mL; in the presence of 8 μg/mL of 35, the MIC to meropenem is 0.25 μg/mL. The doses shown of both meropenem and QPX7728 were administered every 2 h for 24 h (12 doses of the designated amount per day). In this study, meropenem at a dose of 300 mg/kg given every 2 h fails to control the infection, whereas the coadministration of 0.5–1 mg/kg of 35 affords a static effect, and 10 mg/kg achieves a >1 log reduction in CFU/thigh (relative to the start of treatment).

    Safety

    Compound 35 was studied in a 7-day pilot toxicology study at daily doses of 30, 100, and 300 mg/kg in rats (five males, five females per dose level) administered by iv infusion. No changes were observed (tissue histology and clinical chemistry).

    This Drug Annotation describes the path from the narrow-spectrum first-generation boronic acid BLI vaborbactam to a remarkably potent and broad-spectrum agent 35 (Figure 7). The amide series represented by 4b displays potent inhibition of serine enzymes of classes A and C but has only weak activity against class B (metallo) enzymes. Replacement of the amide linkage by a thio linkage, as exemplified in 7a, achieves much-improved potency against class B enzymes. As a model system for introduction of a heteroatom into the aryl ring, compounds unsubstituted next to the boron atom were prepared. Compound 17b represented a breakthrough in terms of potency and spectrum. Susceptibility of 17b to metabolism precipitated work to identify substituents that would block metabolism while retaining all other desirable properties, culminating in 35 (QPX7728).
    Compound 35 displays potent activity against the vast majority of β-lactamase enzymes, including not only the serine enzymes of classes A and C but also metalloenzymes such as NDM-1 and the difficult class D (OXA) enzymes of Acinetobacter baumannii. In vivo, 35 displays pharmacokinetics in the rat similar to β-lactam antibiotics and has good oral bioavailability without the need for a prodrug. Efficacy in a mouse infection model in combination with meropenem against a strain of Klebsiella pneumoniae expressing multiple β-lactamase enzymes was established. Selectivity for the β-lactamase target was established by screening against a panel of mammalian proteases. Preliminary toxicology studies in rats showed no adverse findings. Compound 35 has been advanced to late-stage preclinical development to support IND filing.

    Pharmacokinetic Studies in Rats

    After acclimation, rats (n = 3/dose level) were administered either single intravenous infusions (in 0.9% saline) of 35 at 30, 100, or 300 mg/kg or at 30, 100, 300, or 1000 mg/kg via the oral route (in water). Intravenous doses were infused over 0.5 h via an indwelling femoral vein cannula, while oral doses were administered via a bead tipped oral gavage. Plasma (∼0.3 mL) samples were collected from each rat at designated time points up to 24 h. Blood samples were centrifuged within 5 min of collection at 12 000g for 5 min to obtain plasma. The plasma samples were analyzed using an HPLC–MS method. PK analysis was performed using WinNonlin.

    Mouse Efficacy Studies

    Swiss-Webster mice were rendered neutropenic by the administration of cyclophosphamide and were infected under isoflurane anesthesia by intramuscular injection of K. pneumoniae KP1244 (inoculum 1 × 106) in both thighs (meropenem MIC of >64 μg/mL; meropenem MIC in the presence of 8 μg/mL 35 was 0.25 μg/mL). Treatments (formulated in water) were administered every 2 h by the intraperitoneal route, starting 2 h postinfection. Animals were sacrificed 24 h after the start of treatment, and the thighs were removed, homogenized, and plated to determine bacterial counts.

        MolProbity is a structure-validation web service that provides broad-spectrum solidly based evaluation of model quality at both the global and local levels for both proteins and nucleic acids. It relies heavily on the power and sensitivity provided by optimized hydrogen placement and all-atom contact anal., complemented by updated versions of covalent-geometry and torsion-angle criteria. Some of the local corrections can be performed automatically in MolProbity and all of the diagnostics are presented in chart and graphical forms that help guide manual rebuilding. X-ray crystallog. provides a wealth of biol. important mol. data in the form of at. three-dimensional structures of proteins, nucleic acids and increasingly large complexes in multiple forms and states. Advances in automation, in everything from crystn. to data collection to phasing to model building to refinement, have made solving a structure using crystallog. easier than ever. However, despite these improvements, local errors that can affect biol. interpretation are widespread at low resoln. and even high-resoln. structures nearly all contain at least a few local errors such as Ramachandran outliers, flipped branched protein side chains and incorrect sugar puckers. It is crit. both for the crystallographer and for the end user that there are easy and reliable methods to diagnose and correct these sorts of errors in structures. MolProbity is the authors' contribution to helping solve this problem and this article reviews its general capabilities, reports on recent enhancements and usage, and presents evidence that the resulting improvements are now beneficially affecting the global database.
