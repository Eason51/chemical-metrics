    Abstract

    Tankyrases 1 and 2 (TNKS1/2) are promising pharmacological targets that recently gained interest for anticancer therapy in Wnt pathway dependent tumors. 2-Aryl-quinazolinones were identified and optimized into potent tankyrase inhibitors through SAR exploration around the quinazolinone core and the 4′-position of the phenyl residue. These efforts were supported by analysis of TNKS X-ray and WaterMap structures and resulted in compound 5k, a potent, selective tankyrase inhibitor with favorable pharmacokinetic properties. The X-ray structure of 5k in complex with TNKS1 was solved and confirmed the design hypothesis. Modulation of Wnt pathway activity was demonstrated with this compound in a colorectal xenograft model in vivo.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The two highly homologous human tankyrase isoforms, TNKS1 and TNKS2, are members of the poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) family comprising 17 proteins that share a common catalytic PARP domain.(1−5) These PARP proteins cleave NAD+ into ADP-ribose and nicotinamide and transfer the ADP-ribose units onto their substrates, resulting in a post-translational modification referred to as PARsylation.(6) Recently, tankyrases were proposed as potential drug targets in cancer therapy.(7) Mechanistically, tankyrases mark axin proteins, the concentration-limiting components of the β-catenin destruction complex, for degradation by PARsylation and subsequent ubiquitination through the E3 ligase RNF146.(8,9) Inhibition of tankyrase antagonizes the Wnt signal transduction pathway by stabilizing axin proteins and promoting β-catenin degradation.(10,11) Therefore, inhibition of tankyrase activity appears to be a promising strategy for the treatment of cancer subtypes that depend on high Wnt pathway activity, like colorectal cancer.(12,13)
    A diverse set of tankyrase inhibitors with a variety of different scaffolds has previously been reported, which can be classified into three different subgroups based on their binding mode.(7) Among these, the 2-phenyl-3,4-dihydroquinazolin-4-one scaffold was early in focus. The TNKS2 crystal structure of XAV939, which was a milestone in the discovery of tankyrase inhibitors, served as a basis for structure-based design efforts.(14,15) Obvious structural similarities with flavones were exploited by others for the development of a series of para-substituted 2-phenyl-3,4-dihydroquinazolin-4-ones, and comprehensive structural studies revealed several key interactions with the protein.(16) This scaffold forms a hydrogen bond from the lactam NH of the pyrimidine ring to the carbonyl group of Gly1032, while the lactam carbonyl forms hydrogen bonds with the main chain amide of Gly1032 as well as with the hydroxy group of Ser1068. Additionally, the quinazolinone forms a π–π stacking interaction with Tyr1071, while the phenyl substituent fits well into a hydrophobic pocket lined together with Tyr1050, Tyr1060, His1031, and Pro1034. Interestingly, substituents at the para position of the distal phenyl ring yield a smaller hydrophobic tunnel, flanked by Phe1035 and Ile1075. While this interaction was intensively explored with a variety of residues, structure–activity relationships for the substitution pattern at the quinazolinone core have only been studied sparingly. For instance, it was described that derivatives with a methyl group at either the 6- or 7-position showed weak inhibitory activity against TNKS2.(15) However, investigation of the impact of core substitution on tankyrase inhibition predominantly focused on the 8-position, because the applied model suggested that substitution would not be tolerated at the 5-, 6-, and 7-positions of the quinazolin-4-one and that only small groups at the 8-position may be acceptable. These studies revealed that methyl at this position appears to be optimum for potency.(14,15) 2-Phenyl-3,4-dihydroquinazolin-4-one derivatives are the most studied classes of compounds as PARP inhibitors. Hence, for studies on the SAR of this chemotype on tankyrase inhibition determination of the selectivity over PARP1 was essential in order to identify structural features able to drive TNKS selectivity.(14−16)
    Herein, the identification of the 2-phenyl-quinazolin-4-one scaffold via biochemical HTS approach and the subsequent optimization of this chemotype into a potent, selective tankyrase inhibitor with favorable physicochemical profile and pharmacokinetic properties modulating Wnt pathway activity in a colorectal xenograft model is reported.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS

    Identification and in Vitro Characterization of HTS Hits

    The initial quinazoline hits were identified as active compounds against TNKS1 among other nicotinamide pocket binders in a high-throughput biochemical screen. The homogeneous TR-FRET assay monitored the inhibition of the physiological auto-PARsylation activity of tankyrase. In this HTS assay, 250 nM tankyrase was applied, thereby limiting the bottom IC50 determination level to 100 nM. Thus, for further compound optimization, confirmation, and IC50 determination, orthogonal more sensitive activity ELISA assays (TNKS1 and TNKS2) based on the same auto-PARsylation activity were developed. Selectivity of the compounds was determined in a PARP-1 assay, which was selected as a representative member of the PARP family. Cellular potency of tankyrase inhibitors was assessed by detection of axin2 accumulation using a quantitative antibody-based ELISA-like read-out. Since axin2 is marked for degradation upon PARsylation by tankyrase, the increase of axin2 protein upon inhibition of tankyrase can be used as surrogate for tankyrase activity in DLD1 cells.(10) This cell line expresses a truncated version of the tumor suppressor APC, thereby triggering constitutive activation of the canonical Wnt pathway, and has already been used previously as valuable model by others to study the effects of tankyrase inhibitors on activity of canonical Wnt signaling.(10,17,18)
    Initial quinazolines were selected from the hit set of the original HTS due to their fragment-like structures and favorable efficiency indices (e.g., 1b, LE = 0.52; LLE = 4.51 based on clogD). Properties of representative examples (1a–c) of the hit series are depicted in Table 1.

    Compounds 1a–c showed significant TNKS1 and TNKS2 inhibition but were less potent (∼10-fold) in our hands compared to reported data.(16,19) These hits were further evaluated and revealed to be inactive in the cellular mechanistic assay, poorly soluble, and metabolically unstable shown by in vitro microsomal assays. Moreover, the most potent tankyrase inhibitor 1b was modestly selective (4-fold selective over PARP1). As such, efforts were concentrated on improving TNKS potency and PARP selectivity, while in parallel optimizing drug-like properties in order to deliver a compound for mechanistic evaluation by in vivo pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) studies.

    Chemistry: Part 1

    The target quinazolin-4-ones were assembled by a classical method, which allowed the quick evaluation of SAR patterns and rapid optimization of the initial hits. This key condensation reaction of anthranilamides with substituted benzaldehydes in the presence of sodium disulfite at elevated temperature was widely tolerant of various functionality and provided quinazoline derivative 3b intermediates 4a–m in moderate to excellent yields (Scheme 1). Only reaction of 2-amino-5-fluorobenzamide with methyl 6-formylpyridine-3-carboxylate leading to 6 with a pyridyl substituent instead of the phenyl ring at position 2 afforded this intermediate in low yield. Subsequent functional group manipulation allowed the facile evaluation of SAR at the para position of the distal phenyl ring. For instance, the methyl ester function of 4a–m and 6 could be transformed into a 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl moiety via a Grignard reaction leading to the final compounds 5a–m and 7. We observed a high variability of the yield of this reaction, which, however, did not correlate with the substitution pattern at the quinazolinone core. Preparation of two close analogs (5n, 5p) of compound 5d was accomplished by functionalization of the hydroxy group with excess ethane-1,2-diol or ethylene glycol monomethyl ether, respectively, under acidic conditions. Compound 5m was derivatized with ethane-1,2-diol under the same conditions resulting in derivative 5o.

    Structure–Activity Relationship: Part 1

    The aim of the SAR study was to optimize tankyrase potency and selectivity over PARP1 and to improve physicochemical and pharmacokinetic properties in order to deliver a suitable compound for in vivo studies. Initial structural investigations concentrated on the 4′-position of the phenyl residue. The focus was predominantly directed toward determining the importance of the substitution pattern at the quinazolin-4-one core (Table 2). Exploration of the SAR commenced with derivative 3a, which was demonstrated to be a potent tankyrase inhibitor with good selectivity over PARP-1 by others.(16) According to the published crystal structure, the lipophilic tert-butyl group in the para position of the phenyl residue does not make additional interactions with the binding site but fits very well into the small hydrophobic tunnel, driving potency. The reported activity and selectivity (PARP1 35% inhibition at 10 μM) of this compound was confirmed in our biochemical assays, but more importantly, 3a was the first compound of this series with micromolar activity in the cellular axin2 assay (EC50 = 1.5 μM). Introduction of a fluoro substituent at the 6-position of the quinazolinone (3b) significantly improved biochemical and cellular activity, and the excellent selectivity over PARP1 is retained (>1000-fold). However, the poor metabolic stability of 3b and the low solubility of both tert-butyl derivatives limited further evaluation in in vivo experiments. Further efforts focused on the identification of similarly potent TNKS inhibitors with an improved physicochemical profile. To this end, a few substituents at the 4-position of the distal phenyl residue were explored. Various substituents in this position were already known, and besides the tert-butyl and other lipophilic groups also more hydrophilic moieties were evaluated. Crystal structures of these hydrophilic substituted compounds revealed water molecules in this pocket.(16) To analyze the thermodynamic properties of water molecules in the TNKS1 binding site, WaterMap calculations(20,21) were performed. Analysis of TNKS structures in complex with known nicotinamide pocket binders like XAV939 (TNKS1, PDB code 3UH4) or compound 3a (TNKS2, PDB code 5AKU) revealed that the terminal group at the phenyl ring (e.g., CF3 or tert-butyl) replaces up to three unstable water sites. In both cases, these unstable water sites were replaced by hydrophobic groups and thereby contributed positively to binding affinity by liberating water molecules into the solvent. A closer analysis of the hydrogen bond network of the WaterMap water clusters surrounding the tert-butyl group suggested that introduction of a hydroxyl group could potentially be beneficial by making additional contacts with water sites in this region (Figure 1).

    This hypothesis was evaluated by exchanging one of the methyl groups from the tert-butyl residue with a hydroxy group. The resulting 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl residue retains the size of a tert-butyl group but has a significantly decreased lipophilicity (decrease of clogD from 3.4 (3a) to 2.6 (5a)). This minor structural change was the key design feature and sparked further optimization of this series. Matched pair analysis of 3a/5a and 3b/5d, respectively, revealed that biochemical and cellular potency of the more hydrophilic derivatives (5a, 5d) was slightly reduced or even retained. More importantly, solubility and metabolic stability liabilities of previous molecules were overcome. These hydroxy derivatives possessed acceptable solubility and metabolic stability profiles. Consequently, compound 5d represented a suitable compound for further characterization. Interestingly, the introduction of this hydrophilic moiety in 5a and 5d significantly enhanced binding to PARP1 (IC50 < 1 μM), leading to lower overall selectivity as compared to the tert-butyl substituted derivatives. Subsequent evaluation of quinazolinone scaffold SAR was conducted with the terminal phenyl ring fixed with a 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-1-ethyl moiety. WaterMap analysis indicated that adding small residues in the 5-, 6-, and 8-position might enhance activity by displacing three unstable water molecules (Figure 1). Initial SAR revealed that biochemical potency of the 5-fluoro derivative 5b was similar to the unsubstituted derivative 5a, but cellular activity was improved (∼2-fold) into the sub-micromolar range. The 5-chloro derivative 5c was still a potent inhibitor of both enzymes with double-digit nanomolar IC50 values, but the cellular potency was diminished. The impact of substitution of this position on selectivity was ambiguous because it was increased in the order F< H < Cl. The gain of cellular potency by introduction of a fluoro substituent at position 6 of the quinazolinone core was comparable to position 5. Compound 5d, which was already discussed before, had a cellular EC50 of 614 nM and was slightly less potent than the tert-butyl derivative 3b and 2-fold more potent than the unsubstituted derivative 5a. Introduction of a chlorine at this position (5e) caused a 20-fold loss of cellular potency. Comparable to the effect at position 5, the 6-fluoro substituted derivative (5d) was the least selective (28-fold over PARP1) within the series of investigated substituents. The in vitro profile of compound 5f bearing a fluoro substituent at the 8-position was largely identical to the 5-fluoro derivative 5b. At this position, a small decrease of potency by replacement of fluorine with chlorine (5g) was observed. However, the impact of halogen type on selectivity was reversed compared to position 5 with the best selectivity observed for the fluoro compound 5f (∼80-fold). Thus, it can be summarized that a chloro substituent was tolerated at all three investigated sites resulting in still potent tankryase inhibitors, but a fluoro substituent was always superior especially with respect to cellular activity. The introduction of electron-donating groups like methyl or methoxy in the 8-position resulted in converse effects. Potency of the methoxy substituted derivative 5h decreased compared to the unsubstituted derivative 5a, while the methyl derivative 5i was one of the most potent tankyrase inhibitors within this structural class. Although larger substituents can be accommodated in the 8-position, WaterMap analysis could not rationalize different activity for methyl and methoxy derivatives. Compound 5i inhibited TNKS1 and TNKS2 in the single-digit nanomolar range (IC50 = 1.2 and 1.4 nM, respectively) and showed a cellular EC50 of 31 nM. Unfortunately, increase of lipophilicity (clogD 2.8) by this methyl group deteriorated solubility and stability in the mouse microsomal assay and made this compound overall less attractive. Monosubstitution at position 8 had a minor impact on isoform selectivity. Compounds with larger residues like chloro (5g) or methoxy (5h) at this position were 2–3-fold less selective than derivatives with smaller substituents like methyl or fluoro. Investigation of the impact of different substitution patterns on activity and properties by combination of the most interesting residues at each investigated position was the target of the preparation of the next set of compounds. 5,6-Difluoro derivative 5j was more potent biochemically than the corresponding monofluoro derivatives 5b and 5d. The EC50 of the induction of cellular axin2 by compound 5j was determined to be 456 nM, which was in the same range as the determined value for derivative 5d (EC50 = 614 nM). Furthermore, 5j was reasonably soluble and metabolically stable and was therefore another inhibitor with an attractive in vitro profile. The same was true for derivative 5k with a 6,8-difluoro substitution pattern at the quinazolinone core. This compound was a single-digit nanomolar inhibitor of both tankyrases, was potent in the cellular assay (EC50 = 319 nM), was metabolically stable, and was even slightly better soluble than 5j. The third variant, the 5,8-difluoro substitution pattern, was less attractive because compound 5l was inferior in all respects compared to the previous derivatives 5j and 5k. A tankyrase inhibitor with outstanding biochemical potency was the 6-fluoro-8-methyl derivative 5m. According to our knowledge, this compound with even sub-nanomolar biochemical potency (TNKS1 IC50 = 0.8 and TNKS2 IC50 = 0.5 nM) is one of the most potent tankyrase inhibitors reported to date that solely binds to the nicotinamide pocket. Cellular potency (EC50 = 37 nM) is also remarkable, but like 5i, this compound was not further pursued due to its unfavorable physicochemical profile. The same was true for compound 5o, a close analog of 5m with a hydroxy-ethoxy residue, which was prepared as described for 5n via acid-catalyzed condensation. Although 5o was a potent inhibitor (TNKS1 IC50 = 1.6; TNKS2 IC50 = 1.5 nM; cellular EC50 = 79 nM), increased lipophilicity resulted in decreased metabolic stability in microsomal assays. Substitution of the core at all three positions at once was also tolerated, and the resulting activities and properties resembled the profiles of the disubstituted derivatives 5j and 5k (data not shown). Additional evaluation of the WaterMap suggested that a larger hydrophilic moiety could replace additional water molecules in the binding cavity, which might increase potency. Moreover, the in silico prediction of a similar logD value for compound 5d and, for example, 5n, a close analogue functionalized with a hydroxy-ethyl group, sparked our interest in these derivatives. As such, two derivatives of compound 5d were prepared by introduction of a hydroxy-ethoxy residue (5n) or a methoxy-ethoxy residue (5p) via acid-catalyzed condensation reaction. Both derivatizations delivered very potent tankyrase inhibitors with low double-digit nanomolar IC50 values for both enzymes, but potency was not increased compared to 5d. The above-mentioned water sites are relatively solvent exposed and apparently did not contribute as much as predicted to the overall binding affinity. It is interesting to note that the affinity to PARP1 decreased resulting in higher selectivity (∼230-fold). While the overall profile of 5p bearing a methoxy-ethyl residue was inferior compared to 5d, activity (TNKS1 IC50 = 10 nM, TNKS2 IC50 = 13 nM; EC50 = 360 nM) and compound properties of 5n were also very attractive. Biochemical and cellular data of derivative 7 were in the micromolar range and selectivity over PARP1 was least within the discussed set of compounds, which clearly demonstrated that the distal phenyl substituent could not be replaced by a 2-pyridyl residue.

    Chemistry and SAR: Part 2

    As the SAR of the quinazolinone scaffold has been largely mapped out, subsequent efforts were focused on improving the solubility of the current lead molecules through the introduction of basic side chains.
    Fortunately, the condensation reaction was also applicable to benzaldehydes bearing cyclic amines. The methyl-substituted piperazine derivatives 8a and 8b were accessible in moderate yields in this manner (Scheme 2). In the case of the piperidine derivatives the Boc-protected building block was used. After acidic deprotection of the condensation products compounds 8c and 8d were obtained, which in turn were methylated to afford compounds 8e and 8f (Scheme 2). The potency of these derivatives demonstrated that large cyclic amines could be accommodated at the 4′-position (Table 3), but all matched pairs with 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl derivatives were less potent in biochemical and cellular assays. In addition, intrinsic solubility of the piperazine derivatives 8a and 8b and the methylpiperidine derivative 8f was limited, which reduced their attractiveness. Slight solubility improvements compared to the 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl derivatives were only observed when the target compounds were isolated as salts, for example, 8d as hydrochloride. Not surprisingly, however, introduction of basic motifs triggered inhibition of hERG. This unwanted activity was observed for 8d (Ki = 6.8 μM), and therefore this compound was not considered for further investigation.

From this set of optimized compounds with rather equivalent profiles, 5k was selected and further characterized.

    Selectivity of Compound 5k

    To assess the target specificity of 5k, biochemical inhibition of a broad panel of human PARP enzymes covering 70% of the entire family was determined. IC50 values for TNKS1 (10 nM) and TNKS2 (7 nM), as well as for PARP1 (710 nM), were highly consistent with results from internal ELISA assays. Overall, only single-digit micromolar potency was observed for PARP2 and PARP3, but none of the other tested PARP enzymes were inhibited by 5k (Table 4), which indicated excellent selectivity of 5k for TNKS1 and TNKS2 within the PARP family.

    Crystal Structure of TNKS1 with Compound 5k

    We were able to solve the X-ray structure of 5k in complex with TNKS1 to a resolution of 1.2 Å (PDB code 6QXU), and indeed, the binding mode of 5k was as expected (Figure 2). The quinazolinone scaffold of this disubstituted derivative superimposes quite nicely with the unsubstituted core of 3a (5AKU structure) and makes two hydrogen bonds with Gly1185 amide backbone and the side chain of Ser1221. Both fluoro substituents are well accommodated in the pocket and contribute to the increased potency by making additional van der Waals interactions. The fluoro atom in the 6-position addresses Lys1220 and Glu1291 and causes a shift of both residues enabling the observed contacts. The second fluoro substituent at position 8 interacts with Tyr1203 and Tyr1213 accompanied by a slight movement of both amino acids. Interestingly, the terminal 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl residue is rotated by approximately 60° compared to the tert-butyl group of 3a, which allows the hydroxyl group to interact with the predicted water sites (Figure 1). Indeed, these hydrogen bonds are observed in the X-ray structure of 5k (Figure 2). Thus, the determined binding mode confirmed the WaterMap design hypothesis that introduction of a polar hydroxyl group is able to address these water molecules and to maintain binding affinity.

    In Vitro and in Vivo Characterization of Compound 5k

    To further confirm the mode of action of 5k, cellular levels of tankyrase were analyzed, since it is well described that inhibition of tankyrase-mediated auto-PARsylation results in an accumulation of tankyrase protein.(10,22) To this end, DLD1 cells were treated with different concentrations of 5k for 24 h, which led to a dose-dependent increase of tankyrase protein abundance with an EC50 of 320 nM. This is in the same potency range as the value for axin2 increase (EC50 = 319 nM) although the absolute increase was higher for tankyrase protein (∼8-fold) compared to axin2 (∼3-fold) (Figure 3). The favorable in vitro metabolic stability of 5k observed in mouse as well as in human liver microsomes was further confirmed by stability testing in hepatocytes demonstrating high stability against phase I and II metabolic reactions (Clint values of 15.2 and 1.25 μL/min/106 cells in mouse and human hepatocytes, respectively). Caco-2 permeability of 5k was high (Papp a → b 40 × 10–6 cm/s), and no efflux (efflux ratio of 0.9) was observed in this assay. Subsequently, 5k was subjected to an in vivo PK study in mice and showed a favorable PK profile, that is, a low clearance of 0.41 L/h/kg and a moderate half-life of 1.4 h. Oral administration of 30 mg/kg of 5k in mice resulted in a high bioavailability and suggested that free plasma level (fraction unbound, 8.6% free) exceeded the EC50 value (319 nM) in the mechanistic cellular assay for a period of ≥20 h (data not shown), which emphasized the suitability of 5k as a chemical tool for pharmacological in vivo evaluation.

    PK/PD Analysis of Compound 5k

    To address the relationship between the pharmacokinetic (PK, concentration vs time) and pharmacodynamic (PD, effect vs time) effects of 5k, we aimed to investigate modulation of Wnt pathway activity in the human colorectal cancer xenograft model DLD1. To this end, DLD1 tumor bearing mice were treated once with three different doses (10, 30, and 90 mg/kg) of 5k. After 2, 4, 8, and 12 h, drug concentrations were determined in plasma and pharmacodynamic biomarkers were assessed in tumor samples.
    Exposure of 5k increased in direct proportion to the dose (10, 30, and 90 mg/kg), and free plasma levels were above the cellular EC50 of 319 nM for at least 12 h for the three tested doses (Figure 4a). Inhibition of tankyrase activity was confirmed ex vivo by analyses of tankyrase and axin2 protein abundance in tumor lysates. A time dependent increase of both tankyrase (Figure 4b) and axin2 (Figure 4c) protein levels with maximum effects after 12 h was already observed for the lowest dose of 10 mg/kg. Furthermore, we could show that in vivo inhibition of tankyrase translated into reduced canonical Wnt pathway activity of APC deficient colorectal cancer cells. Compared to control tumors, abundance of active nuclear β-catenin was significantly decreased in a time- and dose-dependent manner in tumors treated with 5k (Figure 4d). While in vivo PD effects have been reported for tankyrase inhibitors with different binding modes,(23−26) this is the first study that demonstrated concentration dependent in vivo target modulation of tankyrase activity with a nicotinamide pocket binder of the quinazolinone class in a human xenograft tumor model by quantitative assessment of pharmacodynamic biomarkers of the Wnt signaling pathway on protein level.

    Conclusion

    Jump To
    The identification and optimization of 2-aryl-quinazolin-4-ones into potent tankyrase inhibitors was achieved by exploration of the SAR around the quinazolinone core and 4′-position of the distal phenyl residue. While introduction of a 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl residue in the 4′-position was a key modification for the improvement of solubility and metabolic stability profiles of the compounds, biochemical as well as cellular potency was significantly increased by variation of core substitution. The lead compound 5k identified through these efforts exhibited enhanced activity in biochemical and cellular assays, favorable selectivity over related PARP proteins, and acceptable pharmacokinetics in mice. Further, 5k was evaluated in vivo in assays showing target modulation of tankyrase and subsequent reduction of Wnt pathway activity in a human xenograft tumor model. Overall, these findings provide the basis for the rationale design of further tankyrase inhibitors from different structural classes toward preclinical drug-like candidates for the treatment of patients with Wnt pathway driven tumors.(23)

    TR-FRET Assay of TNKS1 and TNKS2 (Auto-PARsylation Assay)

    The auto-PARsylation assay was performed as homogeneous HTRF (Cisbio, France) assay in Greiner low volume nb 384-well microtiter plates and was used for high throughput screen.
    Truncated human recombinant GST-TEV-TNKS1 (1023–1327 aa) and GST-TEV-TNKS2 (873–1166 aa) were expressed in Sf9 insect cells with the baculovirus expression system. TNKS2 was purified by affinity purification. TNKS1 was purified by affinity purification, concentration, and gel filtration. GST-tagged TNKS1 (1023–1327 aa, 250 nM) or GST-tagged TNKS2 (873–1166 aa, 250 nM) and 5 μM bio-NAD (Biolog, Germany) as cosubstrate were incubated in a total volume of 5 μL (50 mM HEPES, 4 mM MgCl2, 0.05% Pluronic F-68, 1.4 mM DTT, 0.5% DMSO, pH 7.7) in the absence or presence of the test compound (10 dilution concentrations) for 90 min at 30 °C. The reaction was stopped by the addition of 1 μL of 50 mM EDTA solution. Two microliters of the detection solution (1.6 μM SA-Xlent, 7.4 nM Anti-GST-K (Cisbio, France) in 50 mM HEPES, 800 mM KF, 0.1% BSA, 20 mM EDTA, 0.1% CHAPS, pH 7.0) was added. After 1 h incubation at room temperature, the TR-FRET was measured with an Envision reader (PerkinElmer LAS Germany GmbH) (exc 340 nm; em 615 nm/665 nm). The auto-PARsylation activity was detectable directly via the increase in TR-FRET signal. As full value, the inhibitor-free reaction was used. The pharmacological zero value used was XAV-939 (Tocris) 5 μM fc. The inhibitory values (IC50) were determined using Condosseo from GeneData.

    Activity ELISA of TNKS1 and TNKS2 (Auto-PARsylation Assay)(23,25,26)

    DLD-1 Xenograft Model

    Animal procedures were performed in accordance with the German Laws for Animal Welfare and were reviewed and approved by the Hessian Government Body (Regierungspräsidium) in Darmstadt (License\395). Animals were acclimatized a week prior to use, and general health status was monitored during the experiments. Female immunodeficient athymic nude mice (Envigo) 5–7 weeks old received subcutaneously implanted tumor cell suspensions of DLD-1 (5 million cells in 100 μL of PBS per animal) in the flank. Tumors were grew until the tumor volume ranged from 300 to 800 mm3 (calculated L × W × W/2 from digital calliper measurements). Mice with appropriate tumor volume were stratified into control and treatment groups (n = 5/group). Administrations of vehicle or compound (formulated in 0.5% Methocel/0.25% Tween20/water) were orally given by gavage, and plasma and tumors were collected at the respective time points (2 h for vehicle-treated and 2, 4, 8, or 12 h for compound-treated) for subsequent PD analysis. Mice were euthanized, blood was collected via armpit bleeding and processed for plasma, and tumors were excised and frozen at −80 °C.

    PD Analyses

    Tumor samples were homogenized using a Precellys tissue homogenizer by applying 1000 μL of ice-cold lysis buffer to 100 mg of tumor tissue. Protein concentrations were determined using the BCA method. Abundance of tankyrase and axin2 were determined in 50 μg of total protein per sample as technical triplicates applying the Luminex-based bead assays as described above.
