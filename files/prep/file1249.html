    Abstract

    The single bromodomain of the closely related transcriptional regulators CBP/EP300 is a target of much recent interest in cancer and immune system regulation. A co-crystal structure of a ligand-efficient screening hit and the CBP bromodomain guided initial design targeting the LPF shelf, ZA loop, and acetylated lysine binding regions. Structure–activity relationship studies allowed us to identify a more potent analogue. Optimization of permeability and microsomal stability and subsequent improvement of mouse hepatocyte stability afforded 59 (GNE-272, TR-FRET IC50 = 0.02 μM, BRET IC50 = 0.41 μM, BRD4(1) IC50 = 13 μM) that retained the best balance of cell potency, selectivity, and in vivo PK. Compound 59 showed a marked antiproliferative effect in hematologic cancer cell lines and modulates MYC expression in vivo that corresponds with antitumor activity in an AML tumor model.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    There has been significant progress recently in the development of non-BET (bromodomain and extra terminal domain family) bromodomain probes that will facilitate the understanding of the physiological function of the targeted bromodomain and its relevance in cellular models of disease states.(1, 2) As part of a discovery program aimed at identifying potent and selective non-BET bromodomain inhibitors(3, 4) we were interested in discovering such an inhibitor for the bromodomain of cyclic-AMP response element binding protein (CBP or CREBBP). CBP and a highly similar paralogue, adenoviral E1A binding protein (EP300 or p300), are large, multidomain proteins(5) that each contain a single bromodomain shown to recognize acetylated p53 at K382 following DNA damage.(6) CBP and EP300 have been implicated as potential oncology targets.(7-9) We have reported that the bromodomain of CBP/EP300 (going forward in the paper “CBP” will be used as a surrogate for CBP/EP300) regulates MYC,(10) a transcription factor and oncogene widely expressed in human cancer, which suggests a potential therapeutic strategy for targeting multiple myeloma and other lymphoid malignancies. In addition, we have described the ability of CBP bromodomain inhibitors to impair Treg differentiation and suppressive function, and this activity could constitute a novel small molecule approach for cancer immunotherapy.(11) CBP has also been implicated in neurological disorders where a CBP bromodomain inhibitor has been shown to reduce expression of a regulator of G-protein signaling (RGS4).(12) Given these combined activities, the CBP/EP300 bromodomain has emerged as an especially interesting new therapeutic target.
    Because of the potential therapeutic relevance, there has been increasing interest in drug discovery targeting the bromodomain of CBP.(3, 13-18)Figure 1 shows selected CBP bromodomain inhibitors (1,(13)2,(14)3,(17)4,(15)5,(16) and 6(3)) reported in the literature. A key issue has been to identify CBP inhibitors that are not only potent and selective with excellent cellular activity but that are also devoid of BET activity. Inhibition of the BET family bromodomains produces profound phenotypes that are, in some cases, related to effects produced by CBP bromodomain inhibitors, and there is little shift in cellular potency from the measured biochemical IC50s against bromodomain-containing protein 4 (BRD4; representative BET bromodomain).(19, 20) It is therefore imperative to use compounds with a high degree of selectivity over BET in studies to understand CBP and that in vitro and in vivo studies that are performed do not significantly use concentrations above the BRD4 biochemical/cellular potency. We have recently reported on the fragment-based lead discovery of 6 (CPI-637), a highly selective in vitro probe with good cellular potency (CBP BRET IC50 = 0.29 μM, MYC EC50 = 0.13 μM).(3) Unfortunately, an in vivo probe remained elusive from further modification of 6 and we hoped a new chemical series may prove more suitable for the development of an in vivo tool compound. While we and others have made significant progress in identifying selective in vitro probes, there remains the challenge of identifying a probe suitable for in vivo studies. Here we describe the structure-based design of 59 (GNE-272), a potent and selective CBP inhibitor that is active in a MOLM-16 pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) model measuring MYC expression and is efficacious in a MOLM-16 acute myeloid leukemia (AML) xenograft model.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Screening of CBP against a lead-like property-restricted diversity set(21) in a thermal shift assay resulted in the identification of several ligand-efficient hits. Compounds that increased the CBP bromodomain melting temperature by at least one degree Celsius were further characterized by dose response in a time-resolved fluorescence resonance energy transfer (TR-FRET) assay. In addition to ligand efficiency, selectivity against the first bromodomain of BRD4 [BRD4(1)] (our surrogate for BET bromodomains) as well as other bromodomains was of central importance in choosing a starting point. Compound 7 (ΔTm = 2.3, Figure 2A) emerged as a submicromolar CBP inhibitor that met our criteria (LE = 0.47; see Supporting Information for selected for more extensive bromodomain selectivity data). Importantly, this hit was selective against BRD4(1), displaying approximately 100-fold greater affinity for CBP. We obtained a co-crystal structure of 7 with the bromodomain of CBP (Figure 2B) and found that the amide of 7 binds in the KAc (acetylated lysine) binding site, with the amide carbonyl making a canonical hydrogen bond to Asn1168 and additional water-mediated hydrogen bond interactions to Pro1110 and Tyr1125. The pyrazolopiperidine core is sandwiched between the gatekeeper (Val1174) and Leu1120, while the phenyl ring makes van der Waals interactions with the LPF shelf (Leu1109, Pro1110, Phe1111). To understand the lower affinity of 7 for BRD4(1), we examined several published structures. Modeling of 7 into BRD4(1) (PDB 3MXF) shows BRD4 Trp81 clashing with 7 (this conformation of Trp81 is representative of that seen in the majority of published BRD4(1) structures). To further probe the preferred binding mode, we carried out ab initio torsion scans (Supporting Information) of the pyrazolopiperidine core with respect to the aniline. The results illustrate that the out-of-plane conformation of 7, as it is bound to CBP, is a low-energy conformation, whereas forcing the aniline to be planar with respect to the pyrazolopiperidine to avoid steric clash with BRD4 Trp81 would raise the conformational energy of the ligand and help to explain the selectivity of 7 for CBP over BRD4(1).
    From the co-crystal structure of 7, there were several areas in the binding site for which we could explore to improve potency. We initially examined three different areas in parallel for SAR optimization: interactions with the LPF shelf, the ZA loop, and the KAc binding regions.(1) A series of benzene derivatives bearing small electron-withdrawing substituents and lipophilic substituents at the 2-, 3-, or 4-positions was designed to explore the LPF region (Table 1) to observe the effect on van der Waals interactions with Pro1110 and Val1109. Substitution at the 2-position reduced inhibitory potency relative to the unsubstituted 7, whereas substitution at the 3- or 4-positions enhanced inhibitory potency (up to 7-fold for 18). Our hypothesis for the loss of potency for substituents placed at the 2-position was that substituents at the 2-position provided a disfavorable interaction with Pro1110. Because this initial data with the limited number of substituents at the 2-position was not productive, we chose to focus on substituents at the 3- and 4-positions. Generally, substitution at the 3- or 4-positions increased inhibitory potency with substitution at the 3-position being marginally (up to 2-fold for 12 compared to 13) more potent. Building on compound 9, we looked at a small set of substituents at the 3-position. Extension of the methyl substituent of 9 to an ethyl (14) resulted in a ∼2-fold increase in potency. Most likely, the increase in potency is due to the increased van der Waals interaction of the ethyl substituent with Pro1110 and Val1109. Further examination of various substituents (methyl ester 16, trifluoromethyl 18, methoxy 19, and difluoromethoxy 20) revealed that all were tolerated matching or exceeding the potency of 9. Although we were unable to obtain any co-crystal structures for this small set of compounds, we hypothesized that substitution at the 3-position created a better interaction with Arg1173 (e.g., dipolar interactions, hydrogen bond interactions, van der Waals interactions with the carbon side chain of Arg1173) with the methyl ester of 16 and trifluoromethyl group of 18, exhibiting a ∼7-fold increase in potency from compound 7. Consistent with previous reports,(13) substituents in this same area improve potency. Importantly, the compounds shown in Table 1 failed to bind significantly to BRD4(1). However, we continued to monitor BRD4(1) potency to ensure that we were not affecting selectivity.

    In the second area suggested by the co-crystal structure with 7, effects of changes to the KAc-mimetic group were examined (Table 2). Extending the acyl chain length of 9 by one (21) or two (22) carbons dramatically decreased inhibitory potency. Replacing the terminal acetyl carbon with a cyclopropyl (23) or trifluoromethyl (24) substituent was also not productive. Lastly, replacing the acetyl with an alternative Asn binder, methyl sulfone (25), was not tolerated. The strong sensitivity to the length of the acetyl moiety is consistent with previous work,(4) where we have found in another series that while certain alkyl chains are accommodated in the conserved water region of BRD9 and TAF1(2), they are not tolerated by CBP. Taken together, these findings suggest that the conserved water molecules in CBP may be especially difficult to displace. Alternatively, neither the pyrazolopiperidine of 9 nor the pyrrolopyridone scaffold(4) may present the optimal vector to grow toward the analogous pockets of CBP. On the basis of results from our small SAR study (Table 2), we chose the acetyl group as the preferred KAc binding region moiety for future analogues.

    The third area of the binding site that we explored is the ZA loop. In particular, we explored substitution on the N1 nitrogen of the pyrazole of 9 to improve contacts with this region of the protein. A solvent accessible surface mapped with a hydrophobic potential indicated that hydrophobic substituents might increase potency (Figure 2D). Addition of alkyl substituents from methyl (26) to propyl (28) incrementally increased potency 5-fold over parent 9. Further derivitization of 28 with polar functionality (29–31) resulted in decreased potency. Cyclopropyl derivatives (32–34) were prepared to increase hydrophobic interactions within the ZA loop region (Table 3). While 32 and 34 lost ∼2.5-fold in potency compared to 28, compound 33 had a marginal increase in potency compared to 28. The subtle improvement in potency of 33 over the straight-chain alkyl analogues (26–28) indicated that larger groups may improve potency from van der Waals interactions and filling more volume between the ZA and BC loop regions. With this in mind, the cyclopropyl of 33 was replaced with a phenyl and various pyridine regioisomers. Unfortunately, all of these aryl replacements (35–38) lost inhibitory potency (only 2-fold in the case of pyridine derivative 37) compared to 33 and this area of the active site may not be able to accommodate this larger substitution. At this point, we proceeded to continue with holding the cyclopropyl substituent constant because this modification appeared to be the most productive in this set of analogues in regards to potency, albeit marginal in some instances (ranging from 1.2- to 5.7-fold increase potency over parent 9).

    Out of these explorations, one of the more productive substitutions on the phenyl ring of 7 was a methyl ester at the 3-position (16, Table 1). With this in mind, we explored the LPF shelf further with a small set of 3-amido derivatives (Table 4) using 33 as the core. Methyl amide 39 matched the potency of 33. At this point, we began monitoring MLM (mouse liver microsome) stability to make note of SAR that not only improved potency but metabolic stability as well. Compound 39 (MLM Clhep = 45 mL/min/kg) showed encouraging MLM stability compared to 33 (MLM Clhep = 78 mL/min/kg). We proceeded to explore other amides that might improve potency and MLM stability. Tertiary amide 40 was evaluated, however, this modification gave a 5-fold loss in potency compared to 39. Extending the alkyl chain of 39 (ethyl amide 41 and propyl amide 42) led to increased potency but no improvement of MLM stability. Ether 43 resulted in a dramatic loss in potency. To help increase van der Waals interactions in the LPF shelf, we explored branched analogue 44. Compound 44 increased potency ∼3-fold over 39 and maintained moderate selectivity (123-fold) over BRD4(1). In addition, this compound displayed submicromolar potency in our bioluminescence resonance energy transfer (BRET) cellular assay and maintained moderate MLM stability. Compound 44 garnered our attention as it was potent and highly selective. In addition, we were encouraged by the molecule’s moderate MLM stability and, thus, evaluated 44 in mouse PK. Although 44 had moderate in vivo clearance (43 mL/min/kg) with a good in vitro/in vivo correlation, poor oral bioavailability (F = 5%) precluded use of this compound for in vivo studies. Examination of the in vitro permeability of 44 revealed relatively low permeability (Papp A to B = 2.4 × 10–6 cm/s) and suggested this as a property to optimize. We obtained a co-crystal structure of 44 with CBP to understand the contributions of both the cyclopropyl methyl and isopropyl amide moieties (Figure 3). The cyclopropyl group appears to make van der Waals contacts with Ile1122 in the ZA loop region. The isopropyl group makes van der Waals contacts with Leu1109 in the LPF shelf and with Pro1106. Arg1173 swings in to make a favorable hydrogen-bond contact with the amide carbonyl.

    We surmised that the amide (h-bond donor) of 44 contributed to the low permeability of this compound. To eliminate the H-bond donor, we replaced the amide group of 44 with various isosteric aryl analogues (Table 5). We initially explored phenyl analogue 45 and were pleased to observe a 2-fold increase in biochemical potency and, more importantly, improved permeability (∼7-fold increase). However, the addition of the phenyl ring also resulted in an increase in cLogP(22) potentially contributing to the observed decrease in MLM stability of this compound compared to 44. With this in mind, we evaluated heterocycles that would have a desired lipophilic profile. Generally, the various heterocycles were only ∼2-fold less potent than parent 45 and most of the heterocycles evaluated imparted comparable permeability. At this point, we focused our attention on pyrazole 52, which had good cellular potency and physical chemical properties. We acquired metabolite identification data (see Supporting Information) from liver microsomes, and the two major metabolites observed were oxidation on the phenyl ring and cleavage of the cyclopropylmethyl moiety. With this data in hand, we focused our strategy on addressing these metabolic soft spots as a way to improve MLM stability.

    To address the oxidation of the phenyl ring, we moved the N-methyl pyrazole to the 4-position (54, Table 6) to observe if blocking oxidation here would affect MLM. On the basis of the SAR discussed earlier (Table 1), we expected that moving the pyrazole from the 3-position to the 4-position would result in only a slight decrease in inhibitory potency. However, we hoped that blocking the metabolic soft spot at the 4-position would improve microsomal stability and offset any potency loss. Indeed, pyrazole 54 had comparable potency to regioisomer 52, and generally this trend was observed with other aryl rings as well. Gratifyingly, we observed a 2-fold improvement in MLM stability for 54 over 52. We evaluated 54 in mouse PK experiments and observed high CL (118 mL/min/kg), which prompted us to turn our attention to the cyclopropylmethyl group of 54 to see if variations would lead to further improvement of MLM stability. Paring back the cyclopropylmethyl (54) to a methyl (55) resulted in a slight decrease in biochemical potency but a substantial increase in MLM stability. We designed cyclic ethers (56–58) that could fill the ZA loop region and provide superior physicochemical properties compared to the cyclopropylmethyl group of 54. Previous SAR (compound 43) suggested that the oxygen might not be tolerated, but modeling of the THF showed that there was a possibility of interacting with Arg1173. Experimentally, these compounds had increased MLM stability compared to 54 and we were pleased to observe that 57 retained potency. The flexible methoxy ethyl chain of 43 may reside in a different area in ZA loop region making unfavorable contacts compared to the rigidified THF of 54. At this point, we obtained metabolite identification data for 54 in mouse hepatocytes and, interestingly, found that the sole metabolite was glucuronidation of the parent. This prompted us to collect mouse hepatocyte (MH) stability data for the analogues shown in Table 6 and not surprisingly revealed a disconnect between MLM stability and MH stability for these compounds as glucuronidation/phase II metabolism is likely the primary route of biotransformation for these compounds. Under the conditions used for mouse liver microsome incubations, only phase I metabolism is captured while hepatocyte incubations captures both phase I and II metabolism. We hypothesized that the aniline nitrogen was the likely culprit for glucuronidation and thus focused our efforts on modification of 57. This tetrahydrofuran-containing analogue was equipotent to 54 but lacked the cyclopropyl methyl group that was a known metabolic liability. To decrease the propensity of 57 (calculated pKa 2.34) to undergo glucoronidation, we attempted to reduce the pKa of the aniline by placing a fluorine atom at the 2-position (59, calculated pKa 2.1, Figure 4). This simple modification substantially improved MH stability while maintaining CBP potency. Previous SAR studies (Table 1) suggested the addition of a fluorine atom to the 2-position would lead to a slight reduction in inhibitory potency, but we were surprised when we observed that the potency for 57 versus 59 was indistinguishable. We obtained a co-crystal structure of 59 and the CBP bromodomain (Figure 5A). The compound binds as expected based on similar compounds in the series, with the pyrazolopiperidine core occupying the KAc binding site. The fluorine atom appears to provide little interaction with the binding site. The N-methylpyrazole packs along the surface defined by the LPF shelf, while the tetrahydrofuran sits under the ZA loop. The flexible side chain of Arg1173 points toward the oxygen of the tetrahydrofuran, making a hydrogen-bond contact. While we were unable to get a crystal structure for 11, we were able to compare the CBP co-crystal structure of 7 with 59. An overlay of these compounds (see Supporting Information) shows that the 2-fluorophenyl pyrazole arm of 59 shifts in the binding site relative to parent 7. This suggests that the SAR on the simpler molecules in Table 1 would not necessarily follow the SAR of 59 and provides an explanation why there is a disconnect in SAR going from 7 to 11 and 57 to 59. The MH stability of 59 was improved while maintaining similar MLM clearance, thus supporting the hypothesis that phase 2 metabolism was at play. Furthermore, 59 demonstrated low clearance following a 1 mg/kg intravenous dose in a mouse PK experiment and good oral bioavailability when dosed at 100 mg/kg, reaching an unbound Cmax of 26 μM. Examination of a subset of bromodomains revealed that this compound is exquisitely selective for CBP/EP300 (full bromodomain selectivity profile provided in Supporting Information) and remarkably selective (650-fold) over BRD4(1). When tested at 10 μM in an Invitrogen kinase panel (35 kinases; full list of kinases in Supporting Information) and Cerep off-target screening panel (42 receptors; full list of receptors in Supporting Information), 59 did not inhibit any target at >30%. In addition, 59 did not inhibit (>10 μM, top concentration) several cytochrome P450s (3A4, 1A2, 2C9, 2C19, 2D6). The compound had good potency in the BRET cellular assay. In an orthogonal measure of the target engagement, 59 was shown to inhibit the expression of MYC(10) (MV4–11 cell line) with an EC50 of 0.91 μM and good correlation between the BRET and MYC cellular assays was observed. We also observed that the enantiomer of 59 (see Supporting Information) is 2-fold less potent in the biochemical assay (CBP IC50 = 0.4 μM) and MYC assay (EC50 = 1.9 μM), providing an additional orthogonal measure of target engagement.

    To further understand the selectivity for 59 over BRD4(1), we obtained a co-crystal structure of 59 with BRD4(1). As shown in Figure 5B, the bulky side chain of Trp81 from the WPF shelf of BRD4(1) causes the aniline and the 4-N-methylpyrazole to become almost coplanar to the pyrazolopiperidine core. This planar arrangement has a higher ligand energy as calculated by ab initio torsion scans (Supporting Information) when compared to the 59–CBP structure, as we had postulated from modeling the interaction of BRD4(1) with 7 from the 7–CBP structure (Figure 2), and provides an explanation for the observed selectivity of 59 for CBP over BRD4(1). In addition, the N-methylpyrazole of 59 resides completely in bulk solvent when in complex with BRD4(1) rather than making the favorable van der Waals interactions it does with CBP.
    Given that compound 59 afforded a good balance of cell potency, selectivity, favorable in vivo PK, and good in vitro/in vivo correlation with MH, it was selected for additional profiling. We initially investigated the in vitro activity of 59 in a panel of hematologic cancer cell lines (Figure 6A). Compound 59, dosed at 5 μM (which is below its BRD4 (1) and BRD4(2) IC50 values), caused inhibition of MYC expression in a variety of genetic contexts. In a dose response study, using the AML MOLM-16 cell line, 59 repressed MYC expression at both the RNA (EC50 = 69 nM) and protein level (Figure 6B). The impact of 59 treatment on expression of other oncogenes was also assessed by reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR). After 4 h of treatment, significant repression of a number of key oncogenes was observed in MOLM-16 AML cells (Figure 6C). Consistent with the ability of 59 to inhibit oncogene expression, treatment of a broad panel of blood cancer cell lines caused a marked antiproliferative effect after 6 days (Figure 6D).
    After demonstrating the ability of 59 to modulate MYC in vitro, we determined the effect of 59 in vivo in a PK/PD experiment in a MOLM-16 xenograft mouse model. Single doses of 59 were given at dose levels between 3 and 100 mg/kg in MOLM-16 tumor-bearing animals, and samples were collected at time points covering 2–24 h. Tumor RNA was generated and used to assess MYC transcript by quantitative RT-PCR relative to vehicle treated animals. Suppression of MYC was observed at doses as low as 10 mg/kg at 2–8 h with maximal suppression observed at 30 and 100 mg/kg at 2 h (78% and 86% inhibition, respectively) (Figure 7A). While the tumor-unbound drug levels at the 3 mg/kg dose were right at the MYC EC90 for 59 (621 nM; calculated from the MYC EC50 determined in Figure 6B), at the 10 mg/kg dose unbound drug levels were above MYC EC90 at all time points save for the 24 h time point. It should be noted that suppression of MYC at the 10, 30, and 100 mg/kg doses was seen without reaching the BRD4(1) IC50 (13 μM) of 59. Inhibition of MYC-transcript was transient over the 24 h window, and an apparent rebound effect was observed at all doses, rising to about 150–200% of vehicle control. Increasing suppression of MYC-transcript level corresponded with increasing plasma and tumor drug levels (Figure 7A).
    To evaluate the in vivo antitumor efficacy of 59, MOLM-16 AML xenografts were established in SCID beige mice. Upon tumor establishment, dosing of compound 59 was initiated with po doses of 12.5–50 mg/kg, twice daily (BID). Single-agent efficacy was observed at all doses, as evidenced by dose-dependent inhibition of MOLM-16 tumor growth (Figure 7B). Tumor growth inhibition (%TGI) was 46%, 65%, and 102% at 12.5, 25, and 50 mg/kg, respectively. At the highest dose, treatment resulted in one complete response out of eight mice tested. All doses of 59 were well tolerated over the 21-day dosing window, with a maximal body weight loss of −7.4%. Taken together, these results demonstrate that 59 is active in vivo to modulate CBP-dependent MYC transcription, which corresponds with antitumor activity in a MYC-dependent AML tumor model.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Herein we have outlined the identification of an inhibitor of the CBP/EP300 bromodomains that is potent biochemically and in cells and displays suitable metabolic stability for application as an in vivo probe. A ligand efficient screening hit, compound 7, displayed submicromolar potency toward CBP while maintaining good selectivity toward a small panel of bromodomains. A co-crystal structure of 7 in CBP was obtained and guided initial design targeting the LPF shelf, ZA loop, and KAc binding regions. While modification of the acetyl moiety of 7 was not productive, structure–activity relationship studies in the ZA loop and LPF regions of the active site led to the identification of 44 (TR-FRET IC50 = 0.03 μM, BRET IC50 = 0.96 μM, BRD4(1) IC50 = > 20 μM) as a viable in vitro probe, but the poor PK properties of this compound precluded further use in in vivo experiments. Further optimization to improve permeability and MLM stability led to the identification of 57 (CBP TR-FRET IC50 = 0.02 μM, CBP BRET IC50 = 0.73 μM, BRD4(1) IC50 = 8.5 μM, but the poor MH stability (Clhep = 59 mL/min/kg) of this compound again limited further evaluation. Addition of a fluorine ortho to the aniline of 57 to reduce the pKa and thus glucoronidation of the aniline afforded 59 (CBP TR-FRET IC50 = 0.02 μM, CBP BRET IC50 = 0.41 μM, BRD4(1) IC50 = 13 μM). Compound 59 afforded improved mouse hepatocyte stability (Clhep = 6.2 mL/min/kg) while retaining the best balance of cell potency, selectivity, and favorable in vivo PK. Further evaluation of 59 in vitro showed a marked antiproliferative effect in hematologic cancer cell lines. In vivo, 59 is able to modulate CBP transcriptional dependent MYC expression that corresponds with antitumor activity in a MYC-dependent AML tumor model. Furthermore, the selectivity of this compound, particularly against BET bromodomains, underscores the use of 59 as a tool compound to further delineate phenotypes of CBP/EP300 bromodomains over that of BET bromodomains..

    Thermal Shift Assay Protocol

    His-Flag tagged human CBP bromodomain (K1082-G1197) was expressed in Escherichia coli and purified to >95% purity in-house. In a conical tube CBP (4 μM final) was combined with SYPROOrange (Invitrogen) to a final dye concentration of 10× in 25 mM HEPES, 100 mM NaCl, pH 6.8. The tube was centrifuged briefly to remove precipitate, and 80 μL of the protein:dye solution was then added to each well of a black clear bottom 384-well plate (Greiner), and spun briefly (1 min, 900g). From this stock plate, 23 μL was transferred (three separate transfers per stock plate well) to either DMSO controls or compounds plated from 10 mM DMSO stocks into clear bottom Fluotrac200 plates (Greiner) to a final compound concentration of 50 μM (1.0% v/v DMSO). Subsequently samples (15 μL) were transferred to LightCycler 480 plates (Roche Diagnostics), spun (2 min, 900g), and analyzed on a Roche Lightcycler 480 II using a temperature gradient of 25–65 °C and a scanning rate of 4.2 °C/min. The midpoint of the melting transitions (Tm) were assessed using an application developed in-house measuring the first derivative of the rate of fluorescence change as a function of temperature. Compound induced changes in the melting temperature, ΔTm, were calculated relative to DMSO controls within the same plate.

    Time-Resolved Fluorescence Resonance Energy Transfer Assays

    Compound potencies were evaluated in a panel of biochemical bromodomain binding assays. Binding of biotinylated small-molecule ligands to recombinant His-tagged bromodomains was assessed by time-resolved fluorescence resonance energy transfer (TR-FRET). Test compounds that compete with the biotinylated ligand for bromodomain binding reduce the TR-FRET signal. All biochemical assay protocols were carried out as previously described.(3, 4, 24)

    In Vitro Metabolic Stability Experiments

    Metabolic stability experiments were performed with 1 μM of compound and were evaluated in pooled liver microsomes from female CD-1 mice (Corning, Tewsbury, MA) and pooled cryopreserved hepatocytes from female CD-1 mice (BioreclamationIVT, Baltimore MD) using methods previously reported.(25) Liver microsomal incubations consisted of 0.5 mg/mL microsomal protein, 1 mM NADPH in 100 mM Kpi buffer. Reaction was initiated with the addition of compound (1μM), 50 μL aliquots were sampled at 0, 20, 40, and 60 min, and reactions were quenched with 2× volume of acetonitrile containing an in-house internal standard. Cryopreserved hepatocytes were thawed and resuspended in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM, pH 7.4). Incubation mixture containing 0.5 × 106 cells/mL and 1 μM compound were incubated in a 37 °C incubator with 95% relative humidity and 5% CO2 environment, 50 μL aliquots were sampled at 0, 60, 120, and 180 min, and reactions were quenched with 2× volume acetonitrile containing an in-house internal standard. Quenched samples from microsomes and hepatocytes were centrifuged for 10 min at 2000g. Supernatant were removed and diluted with water (2×) and analyzed by LC-MS/MS using t = 0 peak area ratio set to 100%. The in vitro intrinsic clearance and scaled hepatic clearance were determined as described by Obach et al.(26)

    MDCK Permeability Experiments

    MDCK cells were plated in 24-well plates at 2.5 × 105 cells/mL and allowed to grow for 4 days at 37 °C in 95% humidity and 5% CO2 environment. Media containing Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) and Earle’s Balanced Salt Solution supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum was changed 2 days after seeding and the day prior to the experiment. Compound was added to either the apical or the basolateral side of the monolayer at an initial concentration of 10 μM and was incubated at 37 °C for 180 min. Samples were taken from the receiver chambers at 60, 120, and 180 min and analyzed by LC-MS/MS. The apparent permeability (Papp), in the apical-to-basolateral (A–B) and basolateral-to-apical (B–A) directions, was calculated as Papp = (dQ/dt)(1/AC0), where dQ/dt = rate of compound appearance in the receiver compartment, A = surface area of the inset, and C0 = initial substrate concentration at time = 0. The efflux ratio (ER) was calculated as (Papp,B–A/Papp,B–A).

    In Vitro Protein Binding Experiments

    Mouse plasma (100%) was thawed and was adjusted to pH 7.4 with either sodium hydroxide or phosphoric acid if needed. Plasma (300 μL), spiked with compound (final concentrations 5 μM), was placed into the sample chamber of the single-use RED plate on the donor side, and aliquots (500 μL) of PBS were placed into the adjacent chamber on the receiver side, both in duplicate. The plate was covered with a clear plate cover top and incubated on a Liconic shaker at 37 °C for 4 h. After 4 h, 40 μL aliquots from the receiver and 4 μL from the donor were withdrawn and acetonitrile (150 μL) containing 2.5 nM propranolol (internal standard) was added. To create analytically identical sample matrices to minimize matrix effect, 40 μL of blank plasma was added to receiver wells, whereas 36 μL of blank plasma and 40 μL of PBS were added to donor wells. Samples were centrifuged at 1000g for 10 min, and supernatants (100 μL) were transferred to a 96-well analysis plate. Water (100 μL) was added to the samples prior to LC-MS/MS analysis. The percent of protein binding was calculated from the area ratio of the analyte detected (normalized to internal standard) from the receiver and donor side multiplied by 100.
