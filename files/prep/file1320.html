    Abstract

    The aim of the present study is the evaluation of the 99mTc complexes of two bombesin-like peptides: Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′-Aca-BN[2−14] (BN-1.1) and Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′-Aca-BN[7−14] (BN-1.1p). The BN derivatives were synthesized according to the solid phase peptide synthesis method, and characterized by ESI-MS and NMR. 185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p were also identified by ESI-MS and NMR. The 99mTc complexes were stable over time in human plasma, while they degraded rapidly in kidney−liver homogenates. The peptides and their 99mTc complexes showed high affinity for the human GRP receptors expressed in PC-3 cells. The rate of internalization of these radiolabeled biomolecules was found to be time-dependent. Also, it was found that there was no long-term retention of the radioactive metabolites into the cells. Tissue distribution of the radiopeptides was evaluated in normal mice and in prostate cancer experimental models. Significant uptake of radioactivity was observed in the pancreas of PC-3 tumor-bearing SCID mice. Dynamic studies of both radiopeptides showed satisfactory tumor images.

    The amphibian 14-amino acid peptide BN(16) (pGlu1-Gln2-Arg3-Leu4-Gly5-Asn6-Gln7-Trp8-Ala9-Val10-Gly11-His12-Leu13-Met14-NH2) is a member of the superfamily of BN-like peptides that also includes the mammalian homologue gastrin releasing peptide (GRP)(17, 18) and the related peptide neuromedin B (NMB).(19) BN-like peptides have a wide range of physiological activities, including stimulation of the release of gastrointestinal hormones, stimulation of pancreatic enzyme secretion, thermoregulation, and inhibition of thyroid-stimulating hormone.(20, 21) They exert their effects on target cells by binding to surface GRP-Rs that belong to the superfamily of G-protein-coupled receptors characterized by the typical configuration of seven transmembrane domains.(20) There are four known receptor subtypes for BN-like peptides, three of which are found in humans, the BB2 receptor (BB2-R or GRP-R),(22) the NMB-R,(19) and the orphan BB3-R(23) for which a native ligand has not yet been identified.
    A great number of radiolabeled BN analogues have been synthesized for nuclear oncology applications. Radiolabeling requires covalent coupling of the peptide sequence to a suitable chelating agent for stable binding of the radionuclide, which is usually a metal.(24) For example, BN-like peptides conjugated to DTPA (diethylenetriaminepentaacetic acid) have been synthesized in order to be labeled with 111In3+.(25, 26) DOTA (1,4,7,10-tetraazacyclododecane-1,4,7,10-tetraacetic acid) has been also employed for labeling with 111In3+ as well as 64Cu2+and 68Ga3+ for PET imaging and 177Lu3+ for radiotherapy and SPECT imaging.(27-29)18F-labeled BN analogues are also reported in the literature for PET imaging.(30) However, 99mTc is still the most widely applied radionuclide for diagnostic purposes, mainly because of its favorable γ-ray emission characteristics, low cost and availability.(24, 31) Therefore, a major goal of researchers is the development of 99mTc-labeled BN-like peptides involving a wide range of chelators. The cores of the most commonly employed chelator groups for 99mTc labeling in the literature are N2S2, N3S, P2S2, and N4, resulting in very stable complexes. The oxidation state of 99mTc in labeled BN derivatives is usually (V),(14, 32-40) even though in the past 10 years an increasing number of 99mTc-BN-like peptides with the radionuclide in oxidation state (I) have been referred to in the literature.(41-44)
    One of the cancers targeted with BN-labeled derivatives is prostate carcinoma, which overexpresses the BB2 subtype of GRP-Rs.(10, 45) Prostate cancer is the most common solid tumor found in men and the second leading cause of cancer death in the U.S. Even though prostate cancer is one of the few slowly growing cancers that when organ-confined may never threaten the lives of some patients, it becomes potentially lethal if it metastasizes. Thus, its detection at early stages and the investigation of the extent of the disease are particularly important for effective treatment.(46) Functional imaging methods to detect prostate cancer are MRI, SPECT, and PET. MRI imaging is able to discriminate cancer from surrounding healthy tissue and necrosis; however, in tumors less than 1 cm, the accuracy and positive predictive value are 24.2 and 75.9%, respectively.(47) The most widely used PET radiotracer, 18F-FDG, cannot be applied for the detection of prostate cancer probably because of the low metabolic rate of this type of cancer and the proximity of the prostate to the bladder.(48) Other PET tracers (11C-choline, 18F-fluoroethylcholine) are reported in the literature for imaging prostate cancer,(49, 50) but their availability is currently limited. SPECT imaging on the other hand is widely available and has significant potential for imaging with tumor-specific radiopharmaceuticals, especially with those having physical half-lives well matching the time-course of tumor uptake and background clearance of the radiolabeled molecules.(46)
    The aim of the present work is the synthesis and evaluation of the 99mTc complexes of two BN derivatives for prostate cancer detection with SPECT. The first derivative is Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′-Aca-BN[2−14] (BN-1.1), and the second derivative is the truncated peptide Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′-Aca-BN[7−14] (BN-1.1p, Chart A), where Aca is 6-aminohexanoic acid. Both peptides include the BN C-terminal amino acid sequence Trp8-Ala9-Val10-Gly11-His12-Leu13-Met14-CONH2, known to be critical for receptor binding affinity and biological activity of BN-like peptides. The tripeptide group Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′ is an amino acid sequence capable of complexing a variety of radiometals.(51) The Aca moiety serves as a spacer between the ligating Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′ sequence and the BN part, in order to minimize any possible steric hindrance introduced by the oxometal core on the interaction of the biologically active sequence with the receptor. Preliminary results on the synthesis and labeling of both peptides have already been reported.(36, 52)
    The complexes of BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p were initially synthesized with the stable 185/187ReO(V)3+ core (185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p, respectively; Chart B) and were fully characterized by NMR and ESI-MS analysis. Rhenium has similar physical and chemical properties as technetium, being its third row congener, and commonly replaces technetium for structural characterization.(53) The synthesis of the complexes was subsequently successfully transferred at tracer 99mTc level. In vitro studies followed to determine the efficiency of internalization of the 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p complexes and the degree of residualization of radioactivity into the cells as a function of time. The in vivo behavior of the 99mTc complexes was assessed in normal mice as well as in mice bearing PC-3 prostate cancer xenografts. Dynamic imaging studies were also performed for the tumor bearing mice.

    Synthesis

    The BN derivatives BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p (Chart A) were synthesized according to the solid phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) method. The purification of the crude peptides was performed by semipreparative RP-HPLC with a yield of 34% for BN-1.1 and 48% for BN-1.1p. The purity of the peptides was >95%. The respective 185/187Re complexes 185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p were synthesized via the 185/187Re-gluconate precursor. The purification of the 185/187Re complexes was performed as above, by semipreparative RP-HPLC with a yield of 30% for 185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 35% for 185/187Re-BN-1.1p. HPLC analysis showed the formation of a single complex in each case. The HPLC retention times of the two peptides and their corresponding complexes are presented in Table 1.

    NMR Spectroscopy

    NMR spectra for the peptides and their complexes were recorded in 9:1 H2O/D2O at 25 °C. The pH of all samples was in the 3.3−3.8 range, and it remained uncorrected. Complete assignments of 1H and 13C resonances for BN-1.1, BN-1.1p, 185/187Re-BN-1.1, and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p are reported in Tables S1, S2, S3, and S4, respectively, of the Supporting Information. 1H assignments were based on the combined analysis of well resolved TOCSY and ROESY spectra according to the sequential assignment method,(54) while the assignment of the 13C resonances was based on 1H−13C single and multiple bond correlation experiments (HSQC and HMBC experiments, respectively).
    The Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′ ligand moiety of the sequence was first identified for the BN-1.1 peptide. The Gly1′ amine protons are not observed in the 1H NMR spectrum because of fast exchange with the solvent. The αCH2 protons of both Gly1′ and Gly2′ are equivalent, appearing at 3.92 and 4.08 ppm, respectively (Figure 1A). The αCH2 protons of Gly1′ overlap with the αCH2 protons of Gly5 and Gly11, giving rise to a high intensity peak; they were identified by their αCH-NH(i,i+1) cross-peak with the Gly2′ amide proton in the ROESY spectrum. The 3JNH-αH coupling constant for Gly2′ was the only one directly measurable in the 1D spectrum and was determined to be 5.9 Hz. The βCH2 protons of Cys3′ appear as two very closely spaced doublets centered at 2.92 ppm. The αCH of Cys3′ at 4.47 ppm is not visible in the 1D spectrum, being affected by the water peak suppression procedure; however, it is clearly observed in the 2D spectra.
    Upon complexation of peptide BN-1.1 to the 185/187ReO(V)3+ core, the amide protons of Gly2′ and Cys3′ disappear from the 1H spectrum. The αCH2 protons of Gly1′ and Gly2′ are shifted downfield (Figure 1B) and appear as magnetically nonequivalent doublets with a geminal coupling of 17.6 and 18.7 Hz, respectively. The average downfield shift of the αCH2 protons is 0.30 ppm for Gly1′ and 0.52 ppm for Gly2′. Intense changes are also noted upon complexation for the Cys3′ residue. Specifically, the αCH proton is shifted downfield by 0.78 ppm appearing as a doublet of doublets (3J = 8.3 and 3.9 Hz), while the βCH2 protons appear as two well separated peaks (2J = 12.5 Hz) shifted downfield by an average of 0.58 ppm. Downfield shifts are expected when a ligand binds to a metal; therefore, these data are consistent with the coordination of the amine nitrogen of Gly1′, the amide nitrogen of Gly2′, and the amide nitrogen and thiol sulfur of Cys3′ to the 185/187ReO(V)3+ core to form a N3S complex with three chelate rings, as designed (Chart B). In agreement with the participation of the Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′ amino acids in the coordination sphere, downfield shifts of 8.6, 14.5, and 12.7 ppm, respectively, are noted upon complexation for their αC carbons, while the βC of Cys3′ displays a downfield shift of 20.7 ppm.
    The truncated BN-1.1p derivative displays, analogous to BN-1.1, spectroscopic behavior in both its free and coordinated form.
    One interesting feature in both 185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p complexes is the approximately 0.5 ppm upfield shift of the amide proton of the Aca moiety upon complexation. This upfield shift is unique among all the protons of the complexes and can be ascribed to the magnetic anisotropy of the oxometal center and the coordination sphere that mainly affects protons in the vicinity of the complexing site.(55-57)
    The complexes remained stable in solution for a period of several months, and no decomposition or conversion into isomeric forms was observed.

    Radiolabeling

    Radiolabeling of the BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p peptides was achieved by ligand exchange reactions employing 99mTc-gluconate as precursor. HPLC analysis of the reaction mixture revealed a single radioactive species for both peptides, at a yield of more than 98%, which remained stable for 6 h after labeling.(36, 52) The retention times of the 99mTc complexes are similar to those of the analogous 185/187Re complexes (Table 1), indicating that the complexes formed at tracer level have the same chemical structure as those prepared at the macroscopic level. Pertechnetate had a retention time of 3.5 min under the specific HPLC conditions. The maximum specific radioactivity was 1.84 Ci/μmol for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 1.30 Ci/μmol for 99mTc-BN-1.1p.

    Competition Binding Assays

    The affinity of BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p, as well as their respective 185/187Re complexes for the GRP-Rs, was determined by measuring their ability to compete with [125I-Tyr4]-BN for binding to PC-3 prostate cancer cells, known to predominately express the BB2-R subtype. All compounds were able to displace [125I-Tyr4]-BN, except the T-epitope of cholera, which was used as negative control. The displacement curves of all the above derivatives are compiled in Figure 2. Binding data were analyzed and IC50 values were calculated by nonlinear regression according to a one-site model, and the results are summarized in Figure 2. The IC50 values determined for 185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p were slightly higher than those of the BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p, respectively. All IC50 values were found to be in the low nanomolar range, indicating that the presence of the chelate group, the spacer, and the metal core do not affect the affinity of the active part of the BN derivatives for the BB2-Rs.

    Internalization/Efflux

    Internalization curves of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p in PC-3 cells during incubation at 37 °C are presented in Figure 3. Internalization was rapid with 50% and 30% of the cell-associated radioactivity being internalized at 5 min after incubation for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p, respectively. Maximum internalization was reached at 30 min with 75% and 65% internalized radioactivity for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p, respectively, and it remained at these levels for 120 min. Nonspecific binding was found to be approximately 1% after incubation of the cancer cells with 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p in the presence of 1 μM [Tyr4]-BN, indicating that the complexes display high affinity and specificity for the BB2-Rs. The externalization curves of the 99mTc complexes are shown in Figure 4. It can be seen that for both complexes approximately 50% of the internalized activity was released between 1 and 2 h and that after 5 h approximately 60% of the internalized radioactivity was released.

    Metabolic Stability

    The enzymatic degradation of the labeled peptides 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p was assessed after incubation in plasma and liver−kidney homogenates at 37 °C, followed by HPLC analysis of the metabolic mixture. The percentage of intact complexes versus time is presented in Figure 5. 99mTc-BN-1.1 is stable with more than 80% remaining intact after 2 h of incubation in human plasma, while 99mTc-BN-1.1p seems to degrade more rapidly with approximately 35% of the complex remaining intact at the same time point. Apparently, the shorter sequence of BN-1.1p is more accessible to plasma peptidases.
    A completely different picture was observed after incubation of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p with liver and kidney homogenates. As shown in Figure 5 rapid degradation of the radiolabeled peptides takes place. In the case of 99mTc-BN-1.1p two major metabolites were detected by HPLC analysis, while for 99mTc-BN-1.1 more than two metabolites were observed.
    It is interesting to note that no peak in the HPLC chromatogram corresponds to free 99mTcO4− that would be present in the case of degradation of the 99mTc complex and release of the metal.

    Biodistribution Studies in Normal Swiss Mouse Models

    Table 3 summarizes the results of biodistribution studies of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p conjugates in normal Swiss mice. The mice were sacrificed at 0.5, 1, 2, 4, and 24 h postinjection (pi). Rapid clearance from blood was observed for both radiolabeled peptides. Statistical evaluation (t test) of the data sets of the two radiolabeled species at 0.5, 1, 2, 4, and 24 h demonstrated P < 0.02. The uptake in stomach was low, providing evidence for the conjugates’ stability in vivo. Fast intestinal uptake for 99mTc-BN-1.1p at 0.5 h (15.3 ± 2.2% ID/g) was observed, which remains relatively high at 4 h for both products (10.91 ± 1.6% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 11.4 ± 3.1% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1p). As far as the excretion pathway of the radiolabeled products is concerned, 99mTc-BN-1.1 shows slightly pronounced kidney elimination. Liver uptake was similar and nonsignificant for all the time points of the study for both of the derivatives. Competition binding assays displayed high affinity of BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p, as well as their 185/187Re complexes for GRP-Rs. These results were also confirmed from the biodistribution studies, since the two radiolabeled analogues exhibit high affinity for pancreas, an organ that overexpresses the GRP-Rs (P < 0.05 for all the tested time points). It is worth noting that even at 2 h pi pancreatic uptake remains relatively high (3.6 ± 1.5% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 5.2 ± 2.1% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1p).

These findings are in agreement with literature data on the in vivo behavior of related 99mTcO(V)[N3S] BN complexes.(32, 36, 39, 52) In the case of 99mTc-BN-1.1 lower liver uptake was observed, which however remains higher at the later time points, as opposed to 99mTc-BN-1.1p (P < 0.04), where more effective clearance was observed. Apparently, the faster retention of 99mTc-BN-1.1p to the liver and the intestines compared to 99mTc-BN-1.1 is probably due to the absence of the amino acid sequence [2−6] which affects the excretion pathway of the truncated radiolabeled peptide, a fact that has been reported in the literature.(24, 39, 42)
    In order to investigate whether 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p exhibit in vivo selectivity, the radioactivity in pancreas, an organ overexpressing BB2-Rs, was also counted. High pancreatic uptake for both derivatives with maximum uptake of 11.5 ± 1.7% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 14.2 ± 1.3% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1p was observed at 0.5 h pi, while at 2 h the most pronounced difference was noted, with 10.0 ± 3.1% ID/g for the 99mTc-BN-1.1p. Statistical analysis of the data of the two radiolabeled species at 0.5, 1, 2, 4, and 24 h showed P < 0.03. Uptake in the pancreas was shown to be specific, as demonstrated by the approximately 70% reduction (Table 4) of the radioactivity uptake in animals co-injected with a high dose of native BN (100 μg/mouse), indicating the effective in vivo blockade of this organ (P < 0.04).
    The two complexes show relatively comparable tumor uptake with 4.9 ± 0.5% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 3.8 ± 0.8% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1p, 0.5 h p.i., with slightly higher values for 99mTc-BN-1.1 (P < 0.02). Co-injection of a big excess of native BN (100 μg/mouse) resulted in both cases in the reduction of radioactivity in the tumor site, in agreement with the in vitro blocking of cancerous cells by [Tyr4]-BN (Figure 3). For both 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p blocking led to higher kidney uptake because the BB2-R rich organs have been saturated with the native BN, resulting in higher circulation of the radiolabeled products and consequently higher kidney accumulation. In the case of 99mTc-BN-1.1 blocking resulted in a great drop in the radioactivity observed in the intestines as expected by the presence of BB2-Rs in this organ. On the contrary, no significant difference between blocked and unblocked intestine percentages is observed for 99mTc-BN-1.1p, a fact that provides further evidence for the fast elimination of this derivative through this pathway.

    Dynamic Studies

    In addition to biodistribution measurements, dynamic imaging studies were performed. In these studies, data were continuously recorded and images were stored every 3 min providing fast and low-cost dynamic planar view of the biodistribution of the labeled compounds in the animal. It is possible to record data in list mode format and construct images at shorter intervals. Dynamic studies of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p up to 120 min are presented in Figures 6A and 7A. The experimental tumors were clearly delineated from the first 15 min pi in both cases. Regions of interest (ROIs) were drawn in the tumor region (right shoulder) and on background regions (left foot, right foot and up left hand). Total counts were corrected for 99mTc decay and were plotted as a function of time (Figures 6B and 7B). It can be clearly seen that the total counts recorded in the tumor region are much higher compared to the background muscle regions.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this work, N3S tripeptide 99mTc complexes of two BN derivatives are synthesized and evaluated for imaging of GRP-R positive tumors. Tripeptide chelators of the N3S type are frequently employed for the synthesis of MO(V)3+ (M = 99mTc, 185/187Re) complexes of receptor-binding peptides.(58, 59) AcuTect ([99mTcO]apcitide)(60) employed for the imaging of acute deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and NeoTect ([99mTcO]depreotide)(61) for lung tumor imaging, both in clinical use today, are based on peptide targeting vectors linked to tripeptide N3S ligands. In our case, the peptide chelate sequence Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′ is expected(62, 63) to lead to the formation of a neutral N3S MO(V)3+ (M = 99mTc, 185/187Re) complex with the loss of three protons of the donor groups (the amide proton of Gly2′ and the amide and thiol protons of Cys3′), while the less readily ionizable primary amine of Gly1′ is expected to coordinate to the MO(V)3+ core through its lone pair of electrons and form a dative bond with the metal.
    All the NMR and MS analysis data are consistent with this type of coordination and formation of the complexes 185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p shown in Chart B. According to the literature,(64-68) these complexes are expected to have a distorted square pyramidal structure with the 185/187Re═O bond perpendicular to the plane of the N3S coordinating atoms.
    The presence of the chiral αC of Cys3′ in the coordination plane may lead to the formation of two diastereomeric complexes depending on the syn or anti orientation of the peptide chain on αC with respect to the oxygen of the 185/187Re═O bond.(64-68) In our case, HPLC analysis of the reaction mixture showed the preferential formation (>95%) of one isomer for both 185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p, a fact that is considered advantageous since syn and anti diastereomers often exhibit different biological behavior and separate evaluation of their properties is necessary. Comparisons of our NMR data to those reported in the literature(64, 67-70) for diastereomers of 99TcO(V)/185/187ReO(V) tripeptide N3S complexes of related, but not identical, structure are not conclusive enough to allow for unambiguous assignment of the syn or anti configuration in our complexes. Furthermore, no conclusive comparisons of the circular dichroism (CD) data of our complexes (Figure S1) can be made to those reported in the literature(68, 70) to be diagnostic for the syn and anti configuration in tripeptide N3S complexes, because of structural differences in the chelating part (e.g., the presence of one asymmetric center in the Gly1′-Gly2′-Cys3′ sequence in our case, compared to at least two asymmetric centers in the literature). However, it is noted that the upfield shift of approximately 0.5 ppm of the NH proton of the Aca moiety observed in both complexes is suggestive of the syn configuration because similar upfield shifts are noted in related N3S tripeptide complexes(70) when the peptide chain on αC of Cys3′ has the syn direction.
    Comparison of the chemical shifts of 185/187Re-BN-1.1 and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p to those of the free peptides and to those of the parent BN(71, 72) shows that the presence of the metal core does not affect the chemical environment and conformation of the BN moiety significantly, a fact that is also reflected in the conservation of the biological action of the peptides after their complexation. Apparently the presence of the Aca chain serves its purpose as a spacer between the metal core and the biologically active part of the BN derivatives.
    The biological affinity and specificity for BB2-Rs of BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p and of their 185/187Re complexes was determined through in vitro competitive cell binding assays on PC-3 cells against [125I-Tyr4]-BN (Figure 2). Results from these cell-binding studies demonstrated that BN-1.1, BN-1.1p, 185/187Re-BN-1.1, and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p bind to the BB2-Rs on PC-3 cells with high affinity and specificity, with IC50 < 1.88 nM. The affinity of BN-1.1 for BB2-Rs (IC50 = 0.59 ± 0.04 nM) is higher than that of BN-1.1p (IC50 = 1.45 ± 0.03 nM) which is nonetheless comparable to the IC50 value of the control peptide [Tyr4]-BN. The IC50 value of the 185/187Re complexes towards PC-3 cancer cells was only slightly higher than that of the BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p peptides, indicating that the presence of the chelated metal part does not appreciably affect biological activity. On the basis of the satisfactory IC50 values of the 185/187Re complexes, it was expected that the corresponding 99mTc complexes will demonstrate high affinity for the BB2-Rs.(39, 41, 73)
    The stability studies indicated that 99mTc complexes were practically stable in human plasma; however, they degraded quickly in the presence of kidney−liver homogenates (Figure 5), giving rise to more than one radioactive species, as evidenced by HPLC. It is known from the literature that two enzymes, especially endopeptidase EC 3.4.24.11 and angiotensin-converting enzyme, are the major metabolizing enzymes of BN.(42, 74) Apparently, the presence of the chelated metal core and the spacer Aca moiety does not inhibit the proteolytic action of kidney and liver enzymes and the rate of degradation of the complexes is comparable to that of native BN.(75)
    In agreement with the BB2-Rs agonist behavior displayed (Figure 2) by BN-1.1, BN-1.1p, and their 185/187Re complexes, 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p internalized rapidly in PC-3 cells, a finding also reported in the literature for other 99mTc-radiolabeled agonist-BN derivatives.(14, 35, 40-43) Despite the rapid uptake and high total amount of internalized radioactivity (Figure 3), there was no long-term retention in the cells, with approximately 50% of the internalized radioactivity being released between 1.0 and 2.0 h for both radiolabeled peptides (Figure 4). The rapid externalization of radioactivity does not preclude the potential application of these radiolabeled derivatives for diagnostic purposes because the remaining amount of internalized radioactivity is enough to give satisfactory images. However, their possible use as therapeutic agents, complexed to β-emitting isotopes, may be prohibitory.
    Biodistribution studies in normal and PC-3 tumor-bearing SCID mice showed fast clearance of both radiolabeled peptides from the blood. 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p are eliminated from both the renal−urinary and hepatobiliary systems with 99mTc-BN-1.1 showing a slightly pronounced kidney excretion. This difference is probably due to the lipophilic character of the truncated peptide. The high accumulation of radioactivity in the abdominal region such as liver and intestines as well as the urinary bladder may prevent the detection of prostate tumors. Thus, some strategies must be applied in order to minimize the amount of nondesirable radioactivity in these regions and especially in the bladder. Highlighting the prostate gland before the filling of the urinary bladder may be one option, while voiding the bladder can also be an applicable solution to this issue.(76, 77)
    In biodistribution studies in SCID mice bearing a solid PC-3 tumor,(78) high and specific uptake of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p was observed by both the tumor and the pancreas. The pancreas is the in vivo indicator of the affinity of radiolabeled BN derivatives for BN receptors because it is an organ overexpressing BB2-Rs.(78) The pancreatic uptake (11.3 ± 2.1% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 10.1 ± 1.5% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1p at 30 min pi in normal mice and 11.5 ± 1.7 ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 14.2 ± 1.3% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1p at 30 min pi in PC-3 tumor-bearing SCIC mice) was comparable to that of other assessed BN derivatives.(28, 31, 36, 39, 40, 72) Uptake in the pancreas was higher than the tumor uptake which was 4.9 ± 0.5% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 3.8 ± 0.8% ID/g for 99mTc-BN-1.1p at 30 min pi. High pancreatic uptake does not always mean that good tumor binding will occur.(41, 42) Low accumulation of radioactivity in the tumor tissue was presumably due to poor vascularization of the tumor.(77, 78) For both 99mTc-BN complexes, tumor uptake is considered satisfactory, since uptake values reported elsewhere for a variety of BN derivatives labeled with 99mTc range from 0.8 to 24.6% ID/g 1 h pi.(14, 40-44, 79-82)
    As can be seen from the biodistribution data (Table 4), the length of the BN moiety does not affect tumor uptake, since both 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p exhibit similar affinity for the experimental PC-3 tumors. The length of the BN sequence appears to affect the in vivo washout from pancreas that is slower for 99mTc-BN-1.1p than 99mTc-BN-1.1. This last finding is not in accordance with the in vitro results where the percentage of the externalized radioactivity from cancer cells was similar for both radiolabeled peptides. This difference in the in vitro and in vivo results may be due to the fact that the lipophilicity of the truncated compound does not affect the in vitro results. However, similar differences between in vitro and in vivo results frequently appear in the literature.(14, 32, 34, 40-44, 80)
    Blocking experiments were performed by co-injection of 100 μg/mouse of native BN. An approximately 45% reduction in tumor-specific accumulation of the radiolabeled products was observed. Pancreatic uptake was reduced by approximately 70%, indicating the ability of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p to target the GRP-Rs. Co-injection of a big excess of native BN resulted in reduction in the uptake of radiolabeled peptides by intestines, tumor, and pancreas, confirming the presence of GRP-Rs in these organs.(76) In other organs such as liver and kidneys the concentration of radioactivity is increased after blocking experiments, possibly caused by the saturation effect of native BN in distribution and transport mechanisms.
    Maina et al. states that BN-like peptides may have different structure−function relationships in different species.(83) In the present study some small conflicts are reported, as far as the biodistribution profile of some organs is concerned, when comparing healthy animals and PC-3 tumor models. These discrepancies should be ascribed to the different animal models used in the present study. Particularly, Swiss mice were used for biodistribution studies in normal mice, while athymic SCID mice were used for biodistribution studies in pathological models.
    The in vivo planar imaging studies show that after intravenous administration, 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p are rapidly cleared from blood circulation (Figures 6A and 7A). Most of the radioactivity is located in the abdominal region, a fact that is more noticeable in the case of 99mTc-BN-1.1p, in accordance with the faster uptake of this peptide mainly in the intestines. It is worth noting that radioactivity in the abdominal region is maintained at a high level even 2 h after the administration of the radiotracers. Despite the amount of radioactivity in the abdominal region, the experimental tumors are clearly delineated at 15 min pi and it is obvious that after a 2 h dynamic imaging the tumors are still clearly visible. These data are confirmed from the regions of interest (ROIs) graphs (Figures 6B and 7B) where the total counts in the tumor region are higher than the background and remain high throughout the experiment. The data from the biodistribution studies showed that the tumor uptake for both radiolabeled peptides was comparable, a point that is not in accordance with the images obtained from dynamic studies. It is obvious for Figures 6A and 7A that in the case of 99mTc-BN-1.1 better delineation of the experimental tumor was achieved because the background in the abdominal region was not so high, a fact that is probably due to the faster uptake of 99mTc-BN-1.1p to this region.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Two BN derivatives, namely BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p, containing the 2−14 and 7−14 sequence of BN, respectively, have been radiolabeled with 99mTc. The presence of the spacer successfully distanced the metal coordination sphere from the BN part, thus leading to 99mTc complexes that retain the biological activity of native BN. Both radiolabeled BN derivatives exhibit high affinity for the BB2-Rs expressed in PC-3 cancer cell cultures, as well as in experimental PC-3 tumor models. All experimental results indicate that the truncated BN sequence (7−14) can be used in place of the longer (2−14) sequence, probably with some modifications in order to improve its excretion pathway toward more favorable kidney elimination. Overall, the biological properties exhibited by 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p (high affinity for BB2-Rs, rapid internalization, fast delineation of PC-3 experimental tumors, good quality of images) compare favorably to those of other BN derivatives in the literature, indicating a successful design concept that merits further exploration.

    Synthesis of BN Derivatives

    Synthesis of the peptide amide was carried out manually by using α-fluorenylmethoxycarbonyl (Fmoc) amino acids on Rink amide resin following the method of Fmoc solid phase peptide synthesis. Couplings were performed by using the DIPC/HOBt methology in dimethylformamide (DMF). Coupling success was checked by the established Kaiser ninhydrin test. Deprotection of the Fmoc group was achieved by repetitive treatment with 20% piperidine in DMF. The resin-bound peptide was removed from the resin, and amino acid side chains were deprotected by treatment with a cocktail of TFA/thioanisol/ethanodiol/phenol/H2O/triisopropylsilane, 81.5:5:2.5:5:5:1 (v/v/v/v/v/v) for 1 g of resin. After removal of the organic solvents, the crude product was precipitated with cold diethyl ether.
    The crude peptide was dissolved in water and purified by RP-HPLC. Elution was performed by a gradient system consisting of two solvents: solvent A consisting of 0.05% TFA in H2O and solvent B consisting of 60% acetonitrile (ACN) in solvent A at an 8 mL/min flow rate. Detection of the peptide was achieved with a variable-multiwavelength detector set at 220 nm.(37)

    Synthesis of 185/187ReO(V) Complexes

    Synthesis was effected through ligand exchange reaction between the 185/187Re(V)-gluconate precursor and the BN derivatives according to the literature.(51, 52, 84)

    Analysis of BN-1.1, BN-1.1p, 185/187Re-BN-1.1, and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p. RP-HPLC Analysis

    The pure peptides BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p, as well as their complexes with 185/187Re, were analyzed by RP-HPLC. Flow rate: 1 mL/min. Eluents: A = 0.05% TFA in H2O and B = 90% ACN, 10% solvent A. Linear gradient: from 10% B to 40% B for a period of 19 min. The detection of BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p was performed via a UV detector at 220 nm.

    ESI-MS

    ESI-MS mass spectral analysis of BN-1.1, BN-1.1p, 185/187Re-BN-1.1, and 185/187Re-BN-1.1p was performed at the “Mass Spectrometry and Dioxin Analysis Lab”, NCSR “Demokritos”. Test solution in 50% aqueous ACN was infused into an electrospray interface mass spectrometer (AQA Navigator, Finnigan) at a flow rate of 0.1 mL/min, using a Harvant syringe pump. Negative or positive ion ESI-MS spectra were acquired by adjusting the needle and cone voltages accordingly. Hot nitrogen gas (Dominic-Hunter UHPLCMS-10) was used for desolvation at 170 °C.

    NMR Spectroscopy

    Aqueous solution studies of 3−4 mg of BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p peptides and the corresponding 185/187Re complexes were undertaken in 90% H2O, 10% D2O containing 60 μM NaN3 to prevent microbial growth. The concentration of the NMR samples ranged from 3.2 to 5.4 mM, and the pH was 3.1−3.8 without adjustment. No deterioration of the samples was detected for several months. The 1D spectra were acquired using 32K data points and zero filled to 64K before Fourier transformation. All 2D spectra were recorded in the phase-sensitive mode using time proportional phase increments (TPPI). ROESY spectra were acquired with a mixing time of 250 ms. TOCSY spectra were obtained with a mixing time of 80 ms using the MLEV-17 spin lock sequence. Typically, homonuclear 1H 2D spectra were recorded with sweep width of 12 ppm in both dimensions, 2048 data points, 400 t1 increments, and 32−64 transients. Heteronuclear (1H−13C) 2D spectra were recorded with sweep width of 250 ppm in F1 dimension, 2048 data points, 256 t1 increments, and 96 or 512 transients in the case of long-range couplings. The relaxation delay in all the experiments was fixed to 1.5 s. The excitation sculpting pulse sequence was used for water peak suppression. Proton chemical shifts were referenced to internal or external 3-(trimethylsilyl)propanesulfonic acid, sodium salt (DSS). All spectra were collected at 25 °C.

    Radiolabeling

    Radiolabeling of the BN derivatives was performed according to an already published method.(36, 52) Peptide radiolabeling with 99mTc was accomplished via the precursor 99mTc-gluconate. For the quality control of radiolabeling, 10 μL aliquots of the reaction solution were analyzed by RP-HPLC, applying a linear gradient system at a 1 mL/min flow rate from 0% B to 100% B in 20 min, where solvent A = 0.05% TFA in H2O and solvent B = 60% ACN in solvent A.

    Cell Culture

    The human PC-3 adenocarcinoma cell line was maintained in Dulbecco’s DMEM-high glucose supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% l-glutamine, 1% penicillin/streptomycin, 1% GlutaMAX. Cells were cultured at 37 °C in a humidified incubator under a 5% CO2 atmosphere and passaged weekly using a trypsin−EDTA solution.

    Competition Binding Assays

    Competition binding experiments were performed using the PC-3 cell line. [Tyr4]-BN served as the control peptide and [125I-Tyr4]-BN as the radioligand. In brief, cells were placed at confluence in 24-well plates with 35 000 cpm [125I-Tyr4]-BN in the presence of increasing concentrations (0−1000 nM) of the BN-1.1 and BN-1.1p as well as of their respective 185/187Re complexes. The T-epitope of cholera toxin served as the negative control (0−1000 nM). The total volume of the binding buffer in which the assay was performed was 500 μL (DMEM-high glucose supplemented with 1% FBS, 1% l-glutamine, 1% penicillin/streptomycin, 1% GlutaMAX, 50 mM HEPES, 1 μg/mL aprotinin, 0.25 mM PMSF, and 0.125% BSA, pH 7.4). After 1 h at 37 °C, cells were washed twice with ice-cold binding buffer in order to remove the unbound radioligand. The solubilization of the cells was achieved with 1 N NaOH. The radioactivity of the surface bound radioligand was determined in a NaI γ-counter (Packard). IC50 values were calculated by applying the PRISM 4 program (Graph Pad Software, San Diego, CA). All experiments were carried out two to three times in triplicate.

    Internalization of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p

    Internalization experiments were performed in six-well plates and the BB2-R positive PC-3 cell line was used. Briefly, 1 million cells per well were washed with internalization medium (binding buffer-DMEM-high glucose supplemented with 1% FBS, 1% l-glutamine, 1% penicillin/streptomycin, 1% GlutaMAX, 50 mM HEPES, 1 μg/mL aprotinin, 0.25 mM PMSF, and 0.125% BSA, pH 7.4). Subsequently, in each well was added 1.2 mL of binding buffer and 150 µL of a phosphate-buffered saline (PBS)/0.5% BSA solution containing 100000−300000 cpm of the radiolabeled peptide. This amount of radioactivity corresponds to 200 fmol of the total peptide (the concentration of the total peptide was kept the same for all internalization experiments). To determine nonspecific internalization, an excess of [Tyr4]-BN (150 μL of a solution PBS/0.5% BSA, final concentration of [Tyr4]-BN 1 μM) was added. The cells were incubated at 37 °C in 5% CO2. Internalization was stopped at appropriate time points (5, 15, 30, 60, and 120 min) by removal of the medium. The cells were washed twice with ice-cold PBS (pH 7.4) and then washed twice with 1 mL of glycine buffer (0.05 mM glycine solution, pH adjusted to 2.8 with 1 N HCl) for 5 min at 37 °C to distinguish between cell surface-bound (acid releasable) and internalized (acid resistant) radioligand. Finally, cells were solubilized with 1 N NaOH at 37 °C for 10 min to detach them from the plates. Surface-bound and internalized activities were measured in a γ-counter. Considering that the total activity comprises membrane-bound plus internalized activity, the percent internalized activity for the different time points was calculated with Microsoft Excel. All experiments were carried out two to three times in triplicate.

    Efflux

    Confluent PC-3 cells were seeded in six-well plates (approximately 1 000 000 cells per well) 24 h before conducting the efflux experiment. On the day of the experiment, cells were washed with internalization medium. Then 1.2 mL of fresh internalization medium was added. Subsequently, an amount of 100000−300000 cpm of the radiolabeled peptide (approximately 200 fmol of total peptide in 150 μL of PBS/0.5% BSA) was added and the cells were incubated for 30 min at 37 °C to allow for maximal internalization. The supernatant was discarded, and the cells were washed twice with PBS. Cells were then incubated again at 37 °C with 1.5 mL of culture medium for 0, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0, and 5.0 h. The activity in the supernatant was measured to determine released activity from the cells. After two washing steps with PBS, surface-bound activity was removed by two steps of 5 min acid wash (50 mM glycine-HCl, 100 mM NaCl, pH 2.8) at room temperature (1 mL of acid wash per well). Finally, the amount of the remaining internalized activity was recovered by solubilizing the cells at 37 °C with 1 mL of 1 N NaOH per well. Externalized, surface-bound and internalized activities were measured in a γ-counter. Experiments were performed two to three times in triplicate.

    Metabolism in Plasma

    Human blood (approximately 3 mL) was collected in heparinized polypropylene tubes and centrifuged at 5000 rpm at 4 °C for 5 min. The plasma was collected and incubated with the radiopeptides at 37 °C. Fractions withdrawn at 15 and 120 min were mixed with ethanol in a 2:1 EtOH/aliquot v/v and centrifuged at 14 000 rpm for 30 min. Supernatants were filtered through Millex GP filters (0.22 μm) and analyzed by RP-HPLC.

    Metabolism in Liver and Kidneys

    Liver and kidneys were excised from ether sacrificed female Swiss albino mice and rapidly rinsed and immersed in ice-cold 50 mM TRIS/0.2 M sucrose buffer, pH 7.4. They were subsequently homogenized with a manual homogenizer for 5 min. The radiopeptides were added in fresh homogenate at 37 °C and incubated for 5, 15, and 30 min. In order to determine the enzymatic degradation of the peptides, ethanol was added and the mixture was centrifuged at 14 000 rpm for 30 min. Supernatants were filtered through Millex GP filters (0.22 μm) and analyzed by RP-HPLC for detection of metabolites or released 99mTcO4−. All filtered supernatants were analyzed by RP-HPLC as above, applying the same gradient system as the one used for the identification of the radiolabeled species.

    Biodistribution Studies in Normal Swiss Mouse Models

    The biodistribution studies of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p were determined in normal Swiss mice. The mice were injected with 100 μL (200−300 μCi) of each radiolabeled peptide per animal via the tail vein. The animals were sacrificed 0.5, 1, 2, 4, and 24 h pi by ether anesthesia. The main organs were removed and together with samples of muscles and urine were weighed and counted in a NaI well counter, and the biodistribution data were calculated as the percent injected dose per gram (% ID/g), using an appropriate standard. Stomach and intestines were not emptied before the measurements. The % ID in whole blood was estimated assuming a whole-blood volume of 6.5% of the total body weight.

    Biodistribution Studies in PC-3 Tumor Bearing SCID Mice

    Athymic SCID mice (average weight of 20 g) were obtained from the breeding facilities of the Institute of Biology NCSR “Demokritos”. The animals were inoculated subcutaneously into the right front leg with PC-3 cells (107 cells/animal). Tumors were allowed to grow for 3 weeks. Tissue distribution studies of 99mTc-BN-1.1 and 99mTc-BN-1.1p were carried out after intravenous administration of 100 μL (200−300 μCi) of each radiolabeled peptide per animal via the tail vein. The animals were sacrificed 0.5, 1, 2, 4, and 24 h pi by ether anesthesia. Subsequently, the tissues and organs were weighed and counted in a NaI well counter, and the % ID/g of each organ or tissue was calculated. Stomach and intestines were not emptied before the measurements. The animals of an additional group were co-injected each with 100 μL (100 μg) of native BN (1 mg/mL) and 100 μL (200−300 μCi) of the labeled peptide. These animals were sacrificed 1 h pi.
    In vivo studies were performed in compliance with European legislation. The Hellenic authorities approved all animal protocols.

    Dynamic Studies in PC-3 Tumor Bearing Mice

    Mice were injected intravenously into the tail vein with 100 μL (200−300 μCi) of each radiolabeled peptide per animal. An initial stock solution of the anesthetic is prepared by dissolving 1.0 g of 2,2,2-tribromomethanol in 1.0 mL of 2-methyl-2-butanol. This solution is kept in the dark at 4 °C. On the day of the experiment, an amount of 50 μL of the above solution was dissolved in 1 mL of NaCl 0.9%. The animals were subsequently anesthetized by the subcutaneous injection of this solution at a dose of 300 μL per 30 g body weight. Mice were placed right on the camera at approximately 3 min after tracer injection. This is the position for planar studies because it minimizes the distance between the mouse and the collimator and maximum resolution and sensitivity are achieved.
    In both cases, tracer kinetics were not fast, so data were acquired for 3 min per frame in order to improve image statistics. The images were stored in raw format and processed with ImageJ software.(85)
    Regions of interest (ROIs) were drawn in the tumor region and on a background region (up left hand). The total counts were corrected for 99mTc decay, and they were plotted as a function of time (acquisition frame number). Normally ROI analysis was carried out in SPECT mode; however, in this case, the lack of other organs in the shoulder region and the symmetry of the two shoulders gave a satisfactory approximation of radiopharmaceuticals concentration as a function of time.

    Statistical Analysis

    All data are expressed as the mean of values ± standard deviation (mean ± SD). Data comparison was performed by using the Student’s t test. P < 0.05 was considered significantly different.

        99mTc-Labeled Bombesin(7-14)NH2 with Favorable Properties for SPECT Imaging of Colon Cancer

        In this report, we present the synthesis and evaluation of the 99mTc-labeled β-Ala-BN(7-14)NH2 (ABN = β-Ala-Gln-Trp-Ala-Val-Gly-His-Leu-Met-NH2) as a new radiotracer for tumor imaging in the BALB/c nude mice bearing HT-29 human colon cancer xenografts.
