    Abstract

    Monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL), a serine hydrolase extensively expressed throughout the brain, serves as a key gatekeeper regulating the tone of endocannabinoid signaling. Preclinically, inhibition of MAGL is known to provide therapeutic benefits for a number of neurological disorders. The availability of a MAGL-specific positron emission tomography (PET) ligand would considerably facilitate the development and clinical characterization of MAGL inhibitors via noninvasive and quantitative PET imaging. Herein, we report the identification of the potent and selective irreversible MAGL inhibitor 7 (PF-06809247) as a suitable radioligand lead, which upon radiolabeling was found to exhibit a high level of MAGL specificity; this enabled cross-species measurement of MAGL brain expression (Bmax), assessment of in vivo binding in the rat, and nonhuman primate PET imaging.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL) is a serine hydrolase target of growing interest due to its bifunctional nature of signaling, both through the endocannabinoid pathway via 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG) as well its influence on arachidonic acid (AA) production in the brain.(1) MAGL catalyzes the hydrolysis of 2-AG to liberate AA; approximately, 50% of AA produced in the brain derives from 2-AG. Peripherally, however, phospholipid hydrolysis by cytosolic phospholipase A2 acts as the predominant source of AA. The arachidonate pathway has a significant role in the inflammatory response, as AA is not only an inflammatory signal itself but moreover also can be converted downstream to pro-inflammatory eicosanoids. Thus, the dual impact of MAGL on brain inflammation occurs through the simultaneous modulation of both the endocannabinoid and arachidonate systems:(2) inhibition of MAGL decreases pro-inflammatory eicosanoids and cytokines in the brain while also increasing 2-AG signaling at CB1/2 receptors.(3) Mouse models of Alzheimer’s disease that invoke disruption of MAGL activity, either through genetic alteration or pharmacological inhibition, exhibit decreased neuroinflammation biomarkers, including cytokines and gliosis.(4) Currently, steroids are the primary intervention for pronounced brain inflammation but are limited in their duration and frequency of dosing due to a lack of precision in their mode of action. As such, there is a strong interest in the development of potent, selective MAGL inhibitors for the treatment of acute and chronic diseases associated with neuroinflammation;(5) this will simultaneously require the identification of quantifiable and translational biomarkers to facilitate clinical development of such inhibitors. Specifically, we are interested in developing a positron emission tomography (PET) radioligand(6) with high target specificity to enable preclinical and clinical characterizations of MAGL inhibitors.
    PET is a well-established, noninvasive imaging method that has proven to be particularly valuable for central nervous system (CNS) drug discovery, as it provides quantitative biomarker information, such as target expression and target occupancy measurements, that is otherwise unattainable in the clinical setting.(7) Our group has previously disclosed a set of physicochemical and in vitro absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) parameters to guide the rational design of novel CNS PET tracers.(8) The application of these CNS PET ligand design parameters has facilitated our research team’s discovery of several first-in-class PET ligands, targeting PDE2,(8) PDE4B,(9) and recently BACE1.(10) Specifically, we focus our PET ligand discovery effort within the favorable physicochemical property space defined by CNS PET multiparameter optimization score (MPO) >3. This optimization increases the likelihood of the desired alignment between passive permeability [Ralph–Russ canine kidney (RRCK) Papp AB > 5 × 10–6 cm/s]/low efflux (MDR1 BA/AB ≤ 2.5) and fraction unbound in the brain [(Fu_b) > 0.05], for the brain permeability and low risk of nonspecific binding (NSB), respectively. Herein, we describe the discovery, guided by the aforementioned CNS PET ligand design parameters, of the highly specific MAGL PET radioligand lead PF-06809247 (7). This PET ligand enabled the measurement of cross-species MAGL expression, a rat in vivo binding study and, subsequently, a key nonhuman primate (NHP) PET imaging study, which demonstrated rapid brain uptake and high MAGL specificity for 7.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Our PET ligand discovery effort started with a property survey on the available literature and Pfizer in-house MAGL inhibitor chemotypes, with a primary goal of defining an optimal starting point for PET-specific specific absorption rate (SAR). As shown in Figure 1A, previously disclosed MAGL PET radioligands bear two different types of leaving groups: a hexafluoroisopropanol (HFIP) moiety, such as [11C]KML29 (1)(11,12) and [11C]SAR127303 (3),(12,13) or a 1,2,4-triazole moiety, as found in [11C]JJKK-0048 (2)(14) and [11C]TZPU (4).(12) Ligands incorporating the polar 1,2,4-triazole clearly demonstrate better properties for PET considerations, including low Clog D, favorable CNS PET MPO, and high cFu_b (Figure 1B). The high reactivity of this moiety, however, leads to unfavorable selectivity versus other serine hydrolases,(15) and this hampers their usage as PET ligands. Unlike 1,2,4-triazole, HFIP exhibits sufficient reactivity toward Ser122 at the MAGL active site while retaining sufficient stability to minimize off-target serine hydrolase inhibitions, resulting in a desirable level of MAGL potency and selectivity. Unfortunately, the highly lipophilic nature of the HFIP moiety results in suboptimal CNS PET MPO scores (2.00 for compound 1 and 2.45 for compound 3) and exceptionally low fractions unbound in the brain (0.002–0.003 for these two ligands), suggesting a high risk of nonspecific binding (Figure 1B). In contrast, a new irreversible MAGL inhibitor 5 bearing a fine-tuned trifluoromethyl glycol leaving group, recently disclosed by our team,(16) exhibits a well-balanced profile that not only retains the potency and selectivity of HFIP but also lowers lipophilicity; this translates to both favorable physicochemical properties (CNS PET MPO = 3.81) and a much improved in silico-calculated fraction unbound in the brain (cFu_b = 0.02). Although compound 5 did not fully satisfy our PET design criteria, it served as an encouraging starting point relative to the other irreversible chemotypes. In an effort to mitigate PET-specific shortcomings, a design strategy was employed that included the following: (1) further reduction in lipophilicity while maintaining desirable MAGL potency/selectivity and (2) introduction of a suitable functional group amenable to late-stage [3H]- or [11C]-radiolabeling, to enable saturation binding and PET imaging studies (e.g., a methoxy group).
    Based on the SAR previously reported around 5,(16) our chemistry effort focused on the R1 vector due to its toleration of structural variations (Table 1). Significant prioritization was applied to potential analogs of interest by using in silico-calculated PET-specific properties. This provided a minimalistic approach that advanced only the top targets to synthesis, through application of the following criteria: (1) improved PET parameters compared to compound 5, particularly Clog D and cFu_b; (2) the presence of structural moieties amenable to facile [3H]- and [11C]-labeling; and (3) the ready availability of coupling reagents plus high synthetic doability. From this exercise, analogs 6 and 7 clearly emerged as the top targets, each bearing a methoxy pyridyl moiety suitable for radiolabeling. The calculated PET properties of 6 and 7 are given in Table 1, showing decreased lipophilicity (Clog D < 3), favorable predicted passive permeability (cRRCK), low efflux (cMDR1 BA/AB), and improved cFu_b. Compounds 6 and 7 were synthesized and evaluated in our primary human MAGL pharmacology assay. Encouragingly, both compounds retained suitable MAGL potency, with an approximately 4-fold drop from the reference compound 5. Although the pharmacology profiles of these two compounds were indistinguishable, compound 7 (PF-06809247) was selected for further advancement, primarily based on structural considerations: this constitutional isomer is free of the potential competition between O- and N-methylation that could complicate radiolabeling of 6.

The results reveal minimal cross-species potency differences between human, rat, and mouse MAGL and a high level of selectivity versus fatty acid amide hydrolase (FAAH) (>1000-fold). At a concentration of 10 μM, 7 demonstrated modest inhibition of carboxyesterase enzyme CES1 (76%) and α/β hydrolase domain 6 enzyme ABHD6 (67%), with minimal or no inhibition observed for the remainder of the panel, in line with the selectivity expected for this leaving group.(16) Furthermore, compound 7 exhibited an exquisite selectivity profile in the CEREP panel (Table 2),(17) showing no significant activities at the test concentration of 10 μM, except for the CB1 receptor (EC50 = 1.5 μM).     Chemistry

    The synthesis of compounds 6 and 7 was achieved using the general strategy outlined in Scheme 1. Beginning with tert-butyl (1R,5S,6r)-6-(1H-pyrazol-3-yl)-3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane-3-carboxylate (8),(16) the pyrazole was regioselectively arylated with the requisite methoxypyridine moiety via either a Chan–Lam coupling with a 3-pyridyl boronic acid or a palladium-mediated coupling with a 2-chloropyridine, to afford compounds 9 and 10, respectively. Removal of the tert-butyloxycarbonyl protecting group resulted in the corresponding secondary amines 11 and 12, which were coupled with a mixed carbonate possessing a monoprotected glycol moiety to provide carbamates 13 and 14. Removal of the p-methoxybenzyl (PMB) protecting group was achieved with trifluoroacetyl (TFA), affording the desired targets (6 and 7) in good yields. The synthesis of 7 was later modified to enable a late-stage introduction of a radiolabeled methyl group. The alternate synthesis proceeded, as shown in Scheme 2. In this case, intermediate 8 was arylated with 5-bromo-2-fluoropyridine via an nucleophilic aromatic substitution reaction. The resulting bromopyridine 15 was converted to a pinacol boronate and, subsequently, oxidized to hydroxypyridine intermediate 17. Deprotection and carbamate formation, as described above, afforded key intermediate 20.
    Compound 20 served as an attractive intermediate for the introduction of both [3H] and [11C] radiolabels, via methylation of the phenol moiety. To demonstrate the feasibility of this process, compound 20 was treated with methyl iodide and potassium carbonate in DMF. Methylation of the phenolic group proceeded cleanly, affording compound 14 in 96% yield (see the Experimental Section). This procedure was later translated into [3H]- and [11C]-radiolabeling, as illustrated in Scheme 3. Methylation of compound 20 with tritiated methyl iodide (CT3I) in DMF, followed by PMB deprotection at room temperature, yielded [3H]7 in high radiochemical purity (99.2%) and specific activity (77.43 Ci/mmol). The C-11 radiolabeling was accomplished by employing a slightly modified procedure: methylation with [11C]methyl triflate, followed by a rapid PMB deprotection using trifluoroacetic acid at 100 °C to afford [11C]7 in 25% yield. The radiochemical purity was >98% (n = 2), and the molar activity at the time of injection averaged 1640 GBq/μmol. The resultant [11C]7 was formulated in sterile saline containing <10% (v/v) ethanol and demonstrated stability sufficient for subsequent in vivo studies.

    MAGL Cross-Species Brain Expression (Bmax) Determinations

    Bmax is a critical parameter for PET ligand discovery, as it helps to determine the potential of PET application for a given target, with higher expression corresponding to higher PET feasibility. It also defines the required potency range for novel PET ligand development: most successful PET ligands exhibit a Bmax/Kd ratio >10.(18) With [3H]7 in hand, we performed a preliminary MAGL specificity assessment in rat brain homogenates, to determine whether there was sufficient specific binding (SB) to allow MAGL Bmax measurement. Two rat brain regions were used in this study: frontal cortex and hippocampus. The total binding (TB) was measured using samples pretreated with the vehicle [50 mM Tris/10% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)], while the nonspecific binding (NSB) was assessed using samples pretreated with the selective MAGL inhibitor CPD-4645(19) (10 μM). As shown in Figure 3A, high levels of SB for [3H]7 were observed for both brain regions: 93% for the rat frontal cortex and 89% for the rat hippocampus. Encouraged by this result, we next conducted a comprehensive saturation binding study of [3H]7 using rat frontal cortex, rat hippocampus, and NHP frontal cortex brain tissues. Significant levels of MAGL expression were observed across all three brain tissues (>1500 fmol/mg protein), with no significant cross-species differences observed (Figure 3B). The NHP frontal cortex Bmax was measured to be 1661 fmol/mg protein, which translates into approximately 83–166 nM based on the assumption of 50(18)–100 mg(20) protein/g wet tissue. The high level of brain MAGL expression suggested good PET imaging feasibility, and that the MAGL potency of 7 was likely sufficient based on high SB achieved.

    In Vivo Binding in Rats

    With high specific binding demonstrated in the tissue, we next conducted a rat in vivo binding study, to examine whether high MAGL-specific binding could be achieved in vivo. In this experiment, Sprague-Dawley rats were administrated [3H]7 [50 μCi/kg, intravenous (iv) injection] after 25 min pretreatment with either vehicle or escalating doses of CPD-4645 (0.1–10 mg/kg, sc). Total binding was defined by the vehicle arm, and nonspecific binding was assessed using the group pretreated with CPD-4645 (10 mg/kg). We were gratified to find that the specific binding observed in vitro indeed translated in vivo, with [3H]7 showing high specific binding in all three brain regions examined (frontal cortex, hippocampus, and cerebellum) as well as in the kidney, a representative peripheral tissue (Figure 4A). Importantly, the binding of [3H]7 in the three brain regions was shown to be attenuated by CPD-4645 in a dose-dependent manner, providing a robust proof-of-concept for target occupancy measurement in an in vivo setting using 7 (Figure 4B).

    NHP PET Imaging

    With high MAGL-specific binding clearly demonstrated in both brain tissue saturation binding and in vivo rodent binding studies, compound 7 was radiolabeled with C-11 and advanced to NHP PET imaging for further assessment. Brain PET images were dynamically acquired for 123 min under baseline condition and pretreatment condition (on the same day), after intravenous administration of [11C]7 to a cynomolgus monkey. PET measurements were conducted using high-resolution research tomography (HRRT) (Siemens Molecular Imaging). The injected radioactivities (n = 2) of [11C]7 were 162 and 155 MBq, with molar activities at the time of injection of 1599 and 1680 GBq/μmol, and injected masses of 0.042 and 0.038 μg, respectively, under baseline and pretreatment conditions. The radiochemical purity was higher than 98%. The summed whole-brain PET images were co-registered to the magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) image, as shown in Figure 5. The baseline scan demonstrated rapid, high brain uptake with a wide distribution pattern, as expected for the MAGL target. The time–activity curves (TACs) of the whole brain are shown in Figure 6. The baseline TAC indicated that [11C]7 reached the peak concentration, ∼3.5 standardized uptake value (SUV) and 3% injected dose (ID), approximately 40 min post-injection and remained relatively plateaued throughout the 2 h time course, consistent with the irreversible nature of the radioligand. After 1 h pretreatment with a blocking compound A(21) at a dose of 1.27 mg/kg, the whole-brain uptake of [11C]7 decreased clearly to a value of <0.5%ID after approximately 100 min. A robust level of blocking (approximately, 90% based on the average SUV over the 123 min time course) was seen in the NHP images of the pretreatment arm, demonstrating the high in vivo specificity of [11C]7 in the monkey brain. It is noted that during the course of our work, several MAGL PET radioligands based on reversible chemotypes were recently reported.(22) The NHP brain uptake of [11C]7, which exceeds a maximum SUV of 3, compares favorably to [11C]MA-PB-1,(23) the best of the published MAGL PET tracers to date, and the in vivo blocking by [11C]7 is on par with the complete inhibition of the signal reported recently reported for [18F]T-401.(22a)

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Prioritization of existing chemical matter through the utilization of the CNS PET radioligand selection criteria enabled a rapid, focused effort to optimize these parameters, along with MAGL potency, and allowed us to identify 7 as a promising radioligand PET lead. Compound 7 exhibits high MAGL potency, good passive permeability, low measures of in vitro efflux, and reasonable unbound fraction in the brain. Additionally, 7 offers radiolabeling flexibility for its utilization in preclinical and clinical studies, as it is amenable to either [3H]- or [11C]-labeling. Saturation binding of [3H]7 in rat and NHP brain homogenates showed extensive MAGL expression in the brain and minimal Bmax differences across species. In rats, the specific binding of [3H]7 across multiple brain regions (>90%) was blocked in a dose-responsive manner by a selective MAGL inhibitor. Further evaluation, in an in vivo NHP PET study, revealed a high brain uptake of [11C]7 at the baseline and clear blocking effects after pretreatment with a MAGL inhibitor, demonstrating high specific binding of 7 to MAGL sites within the brain. Together, these data support [11C]7 as a promising radioligand for clinical MAGL PET imaging. Additional studies, including NHP target occupancy, binding kinetics, and radiation dosimetry in NHPs, will be provided in future publications.

    Experimental Section

    Biology

    Saturation Binding

    Saturation studies with [3H]7 were conducted in 96-well microtiter plates in a total volume of 200 μL containing 0.5–70 nM [3H]7 (77.4 Ci/mmol; 1 mCi/mL). Total binding was defined with 50 mM Tris/10% DMSO. Nonspecific binding was defined with 10 μM CPD-4645. The binding assay was initiated by the addition of 300 μg of brain homogenates in binding buffer [50 mM Tris, 1 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), 10 mM 3-[(3-cholamidopropyl)dimethylammonio]-1-propanesulfonate (CHAPS), and 1% gelatin; pH 7], followed by incubation at room temperature for 120 min. The reaction was terminated by rapid vacuum filtration (Brandel Harvester) through presoaked (0.3% polyethyleneimine) Whatman GF/B filter sheets using the Brandel ML 96-well harvester, followed by washing with 50 mM Tris. Filters were transferred to scintillation vials containing Ultima Gold MV scintillation cocktail and allowed to sit overnight. Filter-bound radioactivity was measured using the PerkinElmer TriCarb liquid scintillation counter.

    In Vivo Target Occupancy

    Sprague-Dawley rats were given a single subcutaneous dose (2 mL/kg) of the vehicle (5% DMSO; 5% Cremophor; 90% saline) or CPD-4645 (0.1–10 mg/kg) 25 min prior to an intravenous administration of [3H]7 (50 μCi/kg). Animals were sacrificed by rapid decapitation 5 min following the iv treatment, and the cerebellum, frontal cortex, and hippocampus were immediately dissected and homogenized (100 mg/mL) in the assay buffer (50 mM Tris, 1 mM EDTA, 10 mM CHAPS, and 1% gelatin; pH 7) with a polytron for 10 s. The 5 min time point post-iv treatment was chosen as it provided maximum radiotracer binding in a time-course study (5, 15, 30, and 60 min post-iv treatment). Brain homogenates (400 μL) were filtered through presoaked (0.3% polyethyleneimine) Whatman GF/B filter circles, followed by 2 × 5 mL of 50 mM Tris. Filters were transferred to scintillation vials containing Ultima Gold MV scintillation cocktail and allowed to sit overnight. Filter-bound radioactivity was measured using the PerkinElmer TriCarb liquid scintillation counter. Total binding was defined by the vehicle, and nonspecific binding was determined by 10 mg/kg of CPD-4645.

    Chemistry

    General Methods

    All solvents and reagents were of reagent grade and were used as supplied by the manufacturer. All reactions were run under a N2 atmosphere. Organic extracts were routinely dried over anhydrous Na2SO4 or MgSO4. Concentration in vacuo refers to rotary evaporation under reduced pressure. Chromatography refers to flash chromatography using disposable RediSep Rf 4–120 g silica columns or Biotage disposable columns on a CombiFlash Companion or Biotage Horizon automatic purification system. Purification by mass-triggered high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) was carried out using Waters XTerra PrepMS C18 columns, 5 μm, 30 mm × 100 mm. Compounds were presalted as TFA salts and diluted with 1 mL of DMSO. Samples were purified by mass-triggered collection using a mobile phase of acetonitrile and 0.1% TFA in the water, with a gradient of 100% aqueous (aq) to 100% acetonitrile over 10 min at a flow rate of 20 mL/min. Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC–MS) analyses were performed on a Waters Acquity ultra-performance liquid chromatography (UPLC)–MS system with a Waters Aquity HSS T3, 1.7 μm C18 column (50 mm × 2.1 mm).     Synthesis of [11C]7

    [11C]Methane ([11C]CH4) was produced in-target via the 14N(p,α)11C reaction on nitrogen mixed with 10% of hydrogen, with 16.4 MeV protons using a GEMS PET trace cyclotron (GE, Uppsala, Sweden). Typically, the target gas was irradiated for 15–20 min with a beam current of 35 μA. 11C-Labeled methyl iodide, [11C]CH3I, was produced following the previously published method.(24) In short, the produced [11C]CH4 was released from the target and collected in a Porapak Q trap cooled in liquid nitrogen. After collection, the [11C]CH4 was released from the trap by heating with the pressurized air, and, subsequently, [11C]CH4 was mixed with vapors from iodine crystals at 60 °C followed by a radical reaction at 720 °C. The formed [11C]CH3I was collected in a Porapak Q trap at room temperature, and the unreacted [11C]CH4 was recirculated for 3 min. [11C]CH3I was released from the Porapak Q trap by heating the trap using a custom-made oven at 180 °C. [11C]Methyl triflate ([11C]CH3OTf) was produced by the inline transfer of [11C]CH3I through a glass column packed with silver triflate at 165 °C. [11C]CH3OTf was transferred into a solution of 20 (0.5–1 mg) and Cs2CO3 in DMF by a helium stream. Deprotection was performed by adding TFA with heating at 100 °C for 5 min. The crude reaction mixture was diluted with sterile water (500 μL) before purification via HPLC to obtain the desired radiolabeled product. The HPLC system consisted of a semipreparative reversed-phase (RP) ACE column (C18, 10 mm × 250 mm, 5 μm particle size) and a Merck Hitachi UV detector (λ = 254 nm) (VWR, International, Stockholm, Sweden) in series with a GM-tube (Carroll-Ramsey, Berkeley, CA) used for radioactivity detection. Acetonitrile/TFA was used as the mobile phase. The radioactive fraction corresponding to pure [11C]7 was collected from the HPLC, and the solvents from the HPLC mobile phase were removed by solid-phase extraction purification. Subsequently, [11C]7 was eluted with ethanol and formulated in sterile saline containing less than 10% (v/v) ethanol. The product yield was ∼1000 MBq of [11C]7. Radiochemical purity (>98%) was analyzed by HPLC (XBridge C18 column, 150 mm × 4.6 mm; eluted with acetonitrile/TFA aqueous solution (0.1%); flow rate: 2 mL/min). [11C]7 was stable in the formulated solution up to 90 min after the end of synthesis. The total synthesis time including purification was 40 min.

    NHP PET Imaging

    The study was approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of the Swedish Animal Welfare Agency and was performed according to “Guidelines for planning, conducting, and documenting experimental research” (Dnr 4820/06-600) of Karolinska Institutet. One cynomolgus monkey (male, body weight 7400 g) was used. The NHP was housed in the Astrid Fagraeus Laboratory (AFL) of the Swedish Institute for Infectious Disease Control, Solna, Sweden. Anesthesia was induced by intramuscular injection of ketamine hydrochloride (approximately 10 mg/kg) at AFL and maintained by the administration of a mixture of isoflurane (1.5–2.0%), oxygen, and the medical air with endotracheal intubation in the KI PET center. The head was immobilized with a fixation device. Body temperature was maintained by a Bair Hugger model 505 (Arizant Healthcare, MN) and monitored by an esophageal thermometer. Electrocardiogram, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, and oxygen saturation were continuously monitored throughout the experiments. Fluid balance was maintained by a continuous infusion of saline. NHP was scanned twice, one baseline scan and one pretreatment scan on the same day. At pretreatment scan, compound A was administrated intravenously with bolus infusion (15 s, volume; 0.5 mL/kg) of 1.27 mg/kg approximately 1 h before PET scanning. Compound A was formulated by dissolving in phosphate-buffered saline. The pH of the final dose was approximately 7.5.
    PET measurements were conducted using high-resolution research tomography (HRRT) (Siemens Molecular Imaging). A transmission scan of 6 min using a single 137Cs source was performed before the [11C]7 injection. List-mode data were acquired continuously for 123 min immediately after iv injection of the radioligand. List-mode data were reconstructed with a series of 34 frames (20 s × 9, 1 min × 3, 3 min × 5, and 6 min × 17) using the ordinary Poisson-three-dimensional-ordered subset expectation maximization (OP-3D-OSEM) algorithm, with 10 iterations and 16 subsets including modeling of the point spread function (PSF). The corresponding in-plane resolution with OP-3D-OSEM PSF was 1.5 mm full width at half-maximum in the center of the field of view and 2.4 mm at 10 cm off-center directions. The summed PET images of the whole scanning were co-registered to the MRI image of the individual NHP. By applying the co-registration parameters to the dynamic PET data, the time–activity curves of the whole brain were generated for each PET measurement.
