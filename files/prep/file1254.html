    Abstract

    Four novel fluorinated cyclic analogues of biphalin with excellent to modest binding affinity for μ-, δ-, and κ-receptors were synthesized. The cyclic peptides have a combination of piperazine or hydrazine linker with or without a xylene bridge. Among the ligands, MACE3 demonstrated a better activity than biphalin after intravenous administration, and its corresponding analogue incorporating the hydrazine linker (MACE2) was able to induce longer lasting analgesia following subcutaneous administration. An analogue of MACE2 containing 2,6-dimethyl-l-tyrosine (MACE4) showed the best potency and in vivo antinociceptive activity of this series.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The treatment of intense pain is usually based on μ-opioid receptor (MOR) agonists; however, the use of opiates is often limited by significant severe side effects. Chronic pain is a predominant pain type among patients but tends to be refractory to conventional analgesics including opioids.(1) The palindromic peptide biphalin is a dimer of two d-Ala containing enkephalin-like portions joined by a hydrazide moiety.(2) It possesses a similar affinity for MOR and DOR; however, the antinociceptive effects measured in animal models revealed higher efficacy after intracerebroventricular administration (icv) than after peripheral administration, suggesting a poor penetration of the BBB and/or a low metabolic stability.(3) Biphalin was recently found to produce profound analgesia in a murine skin cancer model and, unlike morphine, has less tolerance when chronically administered to the spinal cord.(4)
    For years our laboratories have developed new biphalin analogues by introducing stepwise modifications (Figure 1). In order to improve BBB penetration, different non-hydrazine linkers have been incorporated together with the pF-Phe in place of the natural Phe, resulting in a novel linear peptide named AM94.(5) This analogue is characterized by high binding affinity at MOR and DOR and a greater and longer lasting efficacy than the parent compound after different administration routes such as intrathecal and subcutaneous.(6) The Phe residue has also been replaced with βh3-Phe resulting in a novel linear peptide with good binding properties at MOR and DOR and improved stability in human plasma.(7) However, among the linear analogues, AM94 was the most potent, efficacious, and long lasting in vivo.
    We have also developed several cyclic biphalin analogues. Among the cyclic peptides, the introduction of a disulfide bridge was a step forward in revealing the active conformation at the binding pocket, leading to more potent compounds than the parent linear peptide.(8) Recently different cyclization strategies involved in the rational design of peptidomimetics have been applied by our research group to DPDPE and biphalin.(9−11) Another milestone in this process was the synthesis of the cyclic analogue series recently published, where the disulfide bridge was successfully substituted by a xylene bridge using CLIPS technology.(9,10)
    Among these compounds, the o-bis(methyl)benzene bridge containing peptide exhibits good stability in human plasma and an antinociceptive effect higher than that of biphalin, after intracerebroventricular, intrathecal, and subcutaneous administrations.(10) These results prompted us to retain this feature in the analogues described in this paper, namely, MACE1–4 (Figure 1), together with pF-Phe, which seemed to be related to the increase of the activity found for of AM94.(4−6) Constriction in the χ space is also an interesting tool to modulate the potency/affinity of opioid peptides with a modification in the conformational freedom of the aromatic side chain residues such as tyrosine, by using dimethyl tyrosine (Dmt);(12) thus, we exploited this structural feature in the novel compound MACE4.
    The choices of pharmacophore type and the correct spacer type and length are key features that lead to an optimized interaction between the bivalent analogues and the receptor binding site.(3,12) The nature of the hydrazine moiety in biphalin is not critical for the properties of the compound; thus this moiety may be replaced by a piperazine, and the replacement of the Phe residues with a pF-phenylalanine may also be applied. These substitutions increase activity at MOR and DOR; their effects are additive and synergistic, leading to one of the best linear biphalin analogue described to date (AM94).(5,6) Affinity and bioactivity of mixed μ/δ-opioid receptor ligands are also potentiated by the incorporation of the Dmt structure. In a previous study the Tyr residue has been replaced by Dmt to give a novel cyclic biphalin analogue joining a -S-S- bridge with some flexible linkers at the C-terminus, with improvement in opioid agonist potency and efficacy.(12) The Dmt moiety promotes MOR/DOR recognition, playing the “address” portion in the “message–address” model for opioid peptides.(13,14)
    In this work we report a novel series of peptides described above (MACE1–4) designed as molecular hybrids of AM94 and cyclic biphalins obtained by CLIPS technology. These approaches are expected to impart improvements in terms of BBB penetration and plasma stability, thus ameliorating their in vivo performance vs biphalin.(13,14) Our results show that novel peptides bind to both MOR and DOR to produce strong antinociception, validating our design. Furthermore, it was found that some of the novel cyclic products possess high affinity for KOR, providing additional SAR information on our approach.

    Chemistry

    In this work, we adopted a closely related synthetic route to that previously described by Mollica et al.(15) The amino acid residues were added step by step to the piperazine/hydrazine cores in a symmetric manner (see Scheme 1). Topographical control of the tyrosine side chain was applied incorporating Dmt in place of the native tyrosine in MACE4. Cyclic intermediates 3a,b were coupled with Boc-Tyr-OH to give compound 6 or subjected to a one-pot-reduction/cyclization reaction to create the cyclic compounds 4a,b. ACM side chain protection of d-Cys facilitates the cyclization reaction/o-bis(bromomethyl)benzene incorporation, resulting in a fast and straightforward procedure for the preparation of the novel compounds, as previously described.(10) The cyclic peptides MACE1–4 were obtained as trifluoracetic salts. The overall yields were good, and the purity was assessed to be ≥95% by RP-HPLC (see Supporting Information).

    Opioid Receptor Binding Affinity

    The affinity of MACE1–4 for all three opioid receptors was evaluated using competition binding assays against the human MOR, DOR, or KOR expressed in CHO cells. The results show that all four compounds bind to the MOR and DOR with high to modest affinity (4.94–83.7 nM, Figure 2). While these affinities are relatively high, they are still lower than biphalin and its linear fluorinated analogue containing the piperazine linker, AM94.(5)
    Interestingly the binding affinity of these compounds to the MOR is higher than that of the previously published analogue characterized by an o-bis(bromomethyl)benzene/hydrazine combination (6a),(10) which is probably related to the presence of the pF substitution on phenylalanine. Also, all four MACE compounds bind to the KOR with lower but still modest affinity (76.7–293.3 nM, Figure 2). Interestingly, the KOR curves are all saturated but do not fully displace the radioligand, which is consistent with mixed allosteric binding. Taken together, the data suggest high affinity and no apparent selectivity between MOR and DOR, similar to biphalin, with an acquired moderate affinity for the KOR, unlike biphalin. This led to weak to moderate selectivity for MOR/DOR over KOR.

    Opioid Receptor Functional Activity

    The ability of the novel analogues to activate the G-protein-coupled opioid receptors was measured using 35S-GTPγS coupling with the human MOR, DOR, or KOR expressed in CHO cells as for the binding above (Figure 3). Interestingly, all four MACE compounds displayed high potency (<0.51–7.5 nM) and high efficacy (83.6–104%) at the MOR (Figure 3A,B). This is in contrast to the binding affinities measured in Figure 2, particularly for MACE1, and implies that the novel MACE analogues have high intrinsic efficacy for the MOR. MACE3 did show a highly elevated baseline in the MOR experiments, which is suggestive of ligand aggregation; including Tween80 in the reaction buffer resolved this baseline issue without significantly altering the DAMGO control (Figure 3B). At the DOR, MACE1, -3, and -4 displayed high potencies (∼4–8 nM); however, the potency of MACE2 dropped to 142 nM. As MACE2 had a high DOR affinity, this suggests a poor intrinsic efficacy at the DOR for MACE2. MACE4 shows a subnanomolar potency at MOR as full agonist, and only a partial agonist activity at DOR and KOR. All four compounds displayed weak efficacy at the DOR (11.0–27.7%), suggesting they are weak partial agonists at the DOR, unlike biphalin and AM94. These results also suggest that the MACE compounds, as weak partial agonists, could block full agonist effects at the DOR. This was tested in antagonist mode vs 100 nM SNC80, which showed that MACE1 had low potency (404 nM) but high efficacy (90%) antagonism, while MACE2 had high potency (8.9 nM) but low efficacy (42%) antagonism; both MACE3 and MACE4 showed no detectable antagonism (Figure 3). It is not clear at this point why MACE3,4 did not show antagonist activity but did show binding affinity. The explanation could encompass differences in the binding pose of diprenorphine (binding) vs SNC80 (GTPγS) or other factors. Overall though DOR antagonism should be considered in the antinociceptive mechanism of these compounds, particularly MACE1,2.
    Lastly, all four compounds had high efficacy (69.3–76.0%) at the KOR, with variable potency (Figure 3). MACE1 and MACE3 had high potencies (9.7 and 25.9 nM, respectively), improving on their KOR binding affinity, suggesting high intrinsic efficacy. MACE2 had a poor potency (806 nM), suggesting a poor intrinsic efficacy, although this compound also had the weakest KOR binding affinity. Overall, these results indicate that MACE1 is nonselective between the receptors and MACE3 has a slight selectivity for MOR/DOR over KOR (∼5-fold), suggesting both ligands will activate MOR and KOR with high efficacy and DOR with low efficacy at therapeutic doses. MACE2 on the other hand is moderately selective for the MOR, with a ∼20-fold selectivity against the DOR and ∼100-fold selectivity against the KOR; MACE4 is the most potent and selective for MOR. On the basis of these results, MACE4 could selectively activate the MOR at therapeutic doses.

    Antinociceptive Tests

    In the hot plate test, ANOVA with Tukey’s multiple comparisons test revealed that compounds MACE2, MACE3, and MACE4 had an antinociceptive efficacy better than the other cyclic analogue MACE1 as well as biphalin (Figure 4A). Statistical analysis also revealed that compound MACE4 showed high antinociceptive efficacy after iv administration in comparison to the AM94 analogue (Figure 4B). MACE1 showed lower antinociceptive efficacy in comparison to the cyclic o-xylene analogue with hydrazine linker (6a) previously described,(10) whereas MACE2, MACE3, and MACE4 antinociceptive efficacy was similar to that observed after 6a administration (Figure 4C). These differences could be due to the MACE compounds’ selectivity profiles for the opioid receptors described in Figures 2 and 3; alternatively, they could be due to discrepancy in BBB penetration or metabolic stability.
    In order to investigate the site of MACE4 action, we performed some experiments using this compound, which showed strong antinociceptive activity after iv administration in the hot plate test, in the presence of the peripherally restricted opioid antagonist naloxone methiodide. As reported in Figure 4D, the intraperitoneal (ip) administration of naloxone methiodide performed 20 min before MACE4 iv injection was able to significantly inhibit the efficacy of MACE4 by reducing its effect below the 25% of the MPE in the hot plate test. In further experiments, naloxone methiodide was administered in the left cerebral ventricle (icv) 20 min before MACE4 was administered iv (Figure 4D). The icv pretreatment with the quaternary antagonist slightly reduced MACE4 antinociceptive effects in the hot plate, since it was able to exert a quite comparable antinociceptive activity to that of MACE4 administered alone for the first 30 min and from 45 to 60 min. These data may lead to the conclusion that the analgesic effect of MACE4 is due to the stimulation of the peripheral opioid receptors rather than the central opioid receptors; this finding is also in agreement with the pharmacokinetic study performed.
    We also tested the antinociceptive efficacy of our compounds in the formalin test (FT) after sc administration. ANOVA followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons test revealed that MACE2 and MACE4 had an antinociceptive effect better than the other cyclic analogues MACE1, 6a, and AM94 in comparison to biphalin both in the early (Figure 5A) and in the late phase (Figure 5B) of the FT. As observed in the hot plate test, MACE1 showed lower antinociceptive efficacy in comparison to 6a in both phases of the FT (Figure 5A,B) and also had lower efficacy than AM94 in the late phase of the FT (Figure 5B).
    These results are somewhat unexpected since MACE1 and MACE3 are both cyclic peptides incorporating a piperazine linker; MACE1 is less potent then the linear analogue AM94 in the HP test after intravenous administration and in the FT after subcutaneous administration, while MACE3 containing an o-xylene moiety is more effective than AM94 and MACE1 in producing analgesia after iv administration, but it is less efficacious than AM94 in the formalin test after sc route. Our findings indicate that the influence of the o-xylene linker on the in vivo pharmacological profile of these compounds is more pronounced than that of the piperazine linker commonly shared by MACE1, MACE3, and AM94. This hypothesis is also supported by the in vivo performance of MACE2 and MACE4; these novel compounds exert strong antinociception following both sc and iv administration, higher than that of the previous described products, prompting also the possible preference of this class of compounds for the more flexible hydrazine linker present in 6a(10) over the piperazine one contained in MACE1,3 and AM94.
    These modifications were intended to improve the overall druggability of opioid peptides derived from biphalin, a goal successfully achieved as suggested by the increased efficacy of MACE4 with sc administration (Figure 5), while iv administration of 6a and MACE2,3 demonstrates similar efficacy to MACE4 (Figure 4C). Alternatively, the improved efficacy of MACE4 in sc formalin pain could be due to the enhanced selectivity of MACE4 for the MOR (Figure 2). To investigate this effect, the BBB-PAMPA penetration and plasma stability assays for MACE2 and MACE4 were also carried out.

    Blood–Brain Barrier PAMPA

    The BBB permeability of AM94, MACE2, and MACE4 was examined using the PAMPA (parallel artificial membrane permeability assay) assay (see Supporting Information). The capability to cross the artificial membranes was interpreted following the literature,(16) as Pe > 4.0 = high permeation, Pe < 2.0 = low permeation. MACE2 exhibited the best permeability value among the biphalin analogues tested; the Pe value of MACE4 was lower than that evaluated in the PAMPA assay of the linear fluorinated biphalin AM94 and very close to that of the cyclic peptide Dmt-c[d-Lys-Phe-p-CF3-Phe-Asp]NH2 (F18) (Table 1).(17)

    The human plasma half-life (t1/2) of MACE2 and MACE4 was calculated by least-squares linear regression analysis of compound amount (area) vs time and was very close to the AM94 value (>240 min) (see Supporting Information).     In Vivo Pharmacokinetics

    We determined the concentrations of MACE4 in plasma and brain of mice (Figure 6) to directly assess the impact of novel drug features on drug distribution in vivo. Following sc administration at 2.1 mg/kg, the same as for the formalin test above, we quantified plasma (total and free) as well as brain concentrations over a 2 h time course. Pharmacokinetic analysis indicated that maximal plasma concentrations were achieved rapidly (CMAX = 548 nM; TMAX = 0.083 h) with a predicted half-life of approximately 30 min (T1/2 = 0.513 h; AUCINF = 433 h·nM; Figure 6A). We also determined a plasma protein binding value of 98.0% based in part on the free plasma concentration curve, which paralleled the total plasma curve with a peak value of ∼10 nM (Figure 6B). Evaluation of the concentration of total drug in the brain revealed low brain exposure (∼5 ng/g) in a subset of samples, with most samples falling below the level of detection, which precluded calculation of pharmacokinetic parameters for the brain. Overall our data show a time course that roughly parallels the iv hot plate time course data (above) with low brain penetration. These observations are consistent with the PAMPA assays and with the naloxone methiodide experiments in Figure 4D, which suggest that MACE4 acts at peripheral sites to produce antinociception. These data also suggest that the structural modifications made in the MACE series have improved in vivo stability vs the earlier biphalin and AM94 compounds.

    Molecular Docking

    An in silico analysis was conducted to examine and compare the possible interactions of MACE1 and MACE4 on MOR, DOR, and KOR vs crystallographic reference ligands. The docking of the novel molecules was done on the crystallized receptor–ligand complexes of MOR (5C1M), DOR (4RWD), and KOR (4DJH) obtained from the RCSB Protein Data Bank and submitted to the Preparation Wizard module present in Maestro 10.2.(18−20) In this preparation step of the crystallographic receptors, several errors in the raw crystal structures have been amended such as the addition of the missing residues, and all the molecules belonging to the crystallization buffer were eliminated from the files, with the only exception for the crystallographic ligand. Also, the water 1303 and water 101 for DOR, 526 and 538 for MOR, and 1303 and 1307 for KOR were kept. The included water molecules have been demonstrated to be essential to form a key water network connection between the crystallographic ligand and the histidine residue also present in the opioid receptors downloaded. Several hydrogen bonds stabilized this network by forming a web of interactions between the hydroxylic phenolic portion of the opioid ligands, the two water molecules, and His297 for MOR, His278 for DOR, and His291 for KOR, respectively. Each water network originally present in the crystal structures was first optimized for the crystallographic ligand by the Interactive H-bond Optimizer interface, featured in the Preparation Wizard application embedded in Maestro 10.2,(20) and then the PDB file of each crystal structure containing this network was saved and used for the docking of the new ligands MACE1 and MACE4. The Interactive H-bond Optimizer interface successfully improved the water molecule orientation by finding the best possible interactions with the histidine and the ligand. This method more precisely defined the presence of the water network in the crystallized structures, since only the heavy atoms are present in the raw PDB files; the final results of the optimization are reported in Figure 7. It seems to involve also other surrounding residues like Lys233, Lys214, and Lys227 for MOR, DOR, and KOR, respectively.
    After this key preparation step, we proceeded with the docking experiments.(21) Following a previously established protocol on these targets reported by Schuster et al.(22) and by our research team,(23) the software Gold 6.0 was employed to perform the docking in order to study the new ligands MACE1 and MACE4. As a first step, the self-docking experiment was carried out to further validate the docking procedure with GOLD 6.0, using the prepared crystallographic complex MOR/DOR/KOR. Thus, two water molecules bound to the OH phenolic moiety of the crystallographic molecules involving His297 in MOR, His278 in DOR, and His291 in KOR were also considered in the docking parameters configuration instructions of the software. GoldScore was employed as a fitness function to perform the self-docking validation process. The self-docking was set up following the procedure previously reported in the literature.(22,23) The water molecules were fixed to “toggle and spin”; these parameters allow the software to rotate and move the water molecules within 1 Å from the original positions. Then, the docking cavity was defined as a space of 10 Å radius around the crystallographic ligand. Using these settings, the returned acceptable RMSD values are reported in Figure 8.
    Following this validation study, Gold 6.0 set with the GoldScore fitness function was employed in the in silico experiments on MACE1 and MACE4 at the receptors 4RWD (DOR), 5C1M (MOR), and 4DJH (KOR) (Figure 9 for MACE4 and Figure S9 for MACE1 reported in the Supporting Information).

Further interactions have been found with Lys233 (also present for MACE4), Trp133, and His197 which on the contrary are absent in the crystallographic and MACE4 poses. Of some relevance is the direct π–π stack to His297 which has been recognized to be involved in the water network discussed above. Interestingly, the 2,6-dimethyl substitution present on the Dmt residue is able to orient the plane of the aromatic ring (the one interacting with the water network) of the side chain parallel to the o-xylene bridge, thus forming a π–π stack between the two aromatic moieties. This intramolecular stabilization could be advantageous to stabilize the correct overall three-dimensional structure of the molecule. It is worth noting that only in the MOR docking pose has this interaction been found.

MACE1 was able to form further interactions with Arg202, Glu297, and Phe293, which are not present in the crystallographic and MACE4 poses. On the contrary the two π–π stacks to Trp287 and Tyr139 present in the MACE4 pose are missing.

Both MACE1 and MACE4 conserve the ability to form the water network and the other typical interaction found in the crystallographic models. In order to better compare the best poses of compounds docked on the three receptors, the method MM-GBSA embedded in Maestro 10.2 was employed to calculate the ΔG free binding energy.(20) The calculation returned with the free binding energy for MACE1 docked to DOR (−93.244 kcal/mol) > MOR (−80.619 kcal/mol) > KOR (−66.732 kcal/mol) and on the other hand the ΔG binding energy for MACE4 docked to DOR (−77.360 kcal/mol) > MOR (−74.313 kcal/mol) > KOR (−47.803 kcal/mol). In the in silico experiments, the docking pose on KOR was comparable to the binding results, being less stabilized than those found for DOR and MOR. Also further interactions to Lys233 and Lys214 are present in the best poses of MOR and DOR respectively, and may have a pivotal role in the stimulation of the opioid receptors. However, the relative maximal efficacy followed the order MOR > KOR > DOR; thus this compound should be considered a full agonist at MOR and a partial agonist of DOR and KOR, with some MOR/DOR selectivity vs KOR.

    Conclusion

    Chronic pain treatment still represents an unmet goal for modern medicinal chemistry, as it is often untreated or undermanaged due to a lack of long-term tolerable and efficacious medications. Opiate therapy is one of the most commonly adopted treatments for the management of chronic pain; however, opiates can have limited efficacy in certain pain types and are also accompanied by tolerance, addiction, and other serious drawbacks.(24) The prolonged use of opioid ligands with peripherally restricted activity for the management of inflammatory pain could be devoid of peripheral tolerance,(25) which is an important aspect to be considered in the management of chronic arthritis, neuropathic, postoperative pain, and tumor related pain.
    In this context it is worthwhile to mention the small molecule DiPOA, a selective μ-opioid receptor agonist administered systemically, that is restricted to the periphery; this small molecule is nonsedating and antihyperalgesic in inflammatory and postsurgical pain states.(26) In addition, the μ-opioid receptor agonist HS-731 described by Bileviciute-Ljungar et al. is able to induce a long lasting antinociceptive effect after sc and oral administration in a rat model of inflammation, which permits the determination of the primary interaction of this molecule with MOR located at the periphery.(27)
    The scope of our research encompasses the development and discovery of new opioid peptides with strong antinociception after peripheral administration. Our findings suggest that the novel molecule MACE4 presented in this work may exert its antinociceptive activity via peripheral sites of action. The attachment of an o-xylene moiety was reported to yield compounds with improved bioavailability, stability, and plasma half-life,(9,10) whereas the use of piperazine linkers together with the pF-phenylalanine in position 4,4′ has been demonstrated to confer κ affinity without loss of affinity toward δ- and μ-receptors. We have optimized the structure of MACE2 by using the Dmt residue in place of the native Tyr with the aim to control the tyrosine side chain’s rotation. The interaction of MACE4 with MOR was improved by dimethylation of the phenol ring, probably through a proper orientation of the tyrosine aromatic ring driven by the methyl groups,(28) as demonstrated by molecular modeling studies in which, as discussed above, the aromatic ring of Dmt is able to form an intramolecular π–π stack to the o-xylene bridge in the MOR best pose.(29)
    The new analogue MACE4 possesses an improved KOR affinity in respect to MACE2 and subnanomolar activity at MOR. Its ability to bind to multiple opioid receptors with good affinity could be a desirable feature, since the selective μ-opioid receptor agonist DAMGO produces respiratory depression and constipation. Highly selective KOR agonists reported in literature induce more side effects than some mixed KOR/MOR agonists,(30) and the mixed MOR-agonist/DOR-antagonist MDAN-21 is 50 times more potent than morphine.(31) Thus, the strategy to develop nonselective analogues as new analgesics may lead to compounds that maintain a more favorable profile.
    The novel designed entities MACE2, MACE3, and MACE4 show enhanced antinociceptive effects in hot plate and formalin pain states after peripheral administration in comparison to biphalin and the established analogues like AM94. This enhanced antinociception could be due to the selectivity profiles of these compounds vs the nonselective MACE1; alternatively, compounds MACE2, MACE4, and AM94 showed scarce BBB penetration properties, measured by a BBB-PAMPA model with an excellent plasma stability which may provide for a better, longer lasting analgesic effect. These results were confirmed and extended for MACE4 in a pharmacokinetic study in mice, suggesting that MACE4 preferentially stimulates peripheral opioid receptors. Indeed, MACE4 injected by the iv route was tested in the hot plate test, following pretreatment with naloxone methiodide (NM), which cannot cross the blood–brain barrier, administered by ip or by icv. The test clearly showed that NM was able to substantially revert the antinociceptive effect of MACE4 only if administered peripherally, thus demonstrating that this compound is able to act by stimulating the peripheral opioid receptors. This is also consistent with the low BBB passage found in the PAMPA study and supported by the low concentration of the peptide detected in the brain after ip administration in the PK study. Overall the combined approaches used in the design of these novel compounds conferred high efficacy and antinociceptive properties after peripheral administration.

        A novel energy model (VSGB 2.0) for high resoln. protein structure modeling is described, which features an optimized implicit solvent model as well as physics-based corrections for hydrogen bonding, π-π interactions, self-contact interactions, and hydrophobic interactions.
