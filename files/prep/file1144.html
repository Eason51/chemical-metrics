    Abstract

    Increased usage of daptomycin to treat infections caused by Gram-positive bacterial pathogens has resulted in emergence of resistant mutants. In a search for more effective daptomycin analogues through medicinal chemistry studies, we found that methylation at the nonproteinogenic amino acid kynurenine in daptomycin could result in significant enhancement of antibacterial activity. Termed “kynomycin,” this new antibiotic exhibits higher antibacterial activity than daptomycin and is able to eradicate methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus (VRE) strains, including daptomycin-resistant strains. The improved antimicrobial activity of kynomycin was demonstrated in in vitro time-killing assay, in vivo wax worm model, and different mouse infection models. The increased antibacterial activity, improved pharmacokinetics, and lower cytotoxicity of kynomycin, compared to daptomycin, showed the promise of the future design and development of next-generation daptomycin-based antibiotics.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The increasing prevalence of multidrug-resistant bacterial pathogens has severely undermined the effectiveness of public health management and caused a dramatic increase in patient morbidity and mortality.(1,2) Among bacterial infections caused by Gram-positive bacteria, vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus (VRE) and methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) pose a particularly serious threat to human health.(3−7) According to a CDC report in 2013, an estimated 66 000 healthcare-associated Enterococcus infections occur in the United States each year, of which 20 000 infections are due to VRE, resulting in approximately 1300 deaths.(8) Likewise, an estimated 72 444 invasive MRSA infections and 9,194 related deaths occurred in 2014 in the United States.(9) In 2017, more than 119 000 cases of bloodstream infections were caused by Staphylococcus aureus in the United States and resulted in almost 20 000 deaths.(10)
    Daptomycin is one of the few antibacterial agents that have been developed within the past two decades, being used to treat skin infections and bacteremia caused by Gram-positive bacterial pathogens. Approved by the FDA for clinical use in 2003,(11) the drug exhibits significant bactericidal effects on highly resistant Gram-positive bacterial strains including MRSA, vancomycin-resistant S. aureus (VRSA), and VRE.(12) Daptomycin is a cyclic lipodepsipeptide consisting of 13 amino acids, 10 of which make up a 31-membered ring and three are anchored as an exocyclic tail with the N-terminal n-decanoyl lipid. Within this structure are two unnatural amino acids, kynurenine (Kyn) and 3-methyl-glutamic acid (3-mGlu), as well as three d-amino acids (i.e., d-Asn, d-Ala, and d-Ser). Functionally, this compound exhibits a unique mode of action, which is significantly different from that of other currently used antibiotics. Its actual mechanism of action is still controversial.(13) Generally, daptomycin undergoes conformational change upon binding to Ca2+ ions that will facilitate the insertion and oligomerization into bacterial membranes that contain phosphatidylglycerol.(14) The subsequent events after membrane binding leading to the cell death of bacteria are still debatable. Two prevailing models of the mechanism of action have been proposed: (1) pore formation on the membrane accompanied by leakage of ions and membrane depolarization;(15) and (2) membrane curvature alternation or reduction of membrane microdomain fluidity inducing aberrant recruitment of peripheral membrane proteins, which eventually leads to cell envelope defects.(16−18) Following increased clinical usage, bacterial pathogens begin to develop resistance to daptomycin. In particular, daptomycin-resistant VRE has emerged at an alarming rate. Surveillance from hospitals in Singapore showed that 50% of VRE exhibited daptomycin nonsusceptible phenotype,(19) suggesting an urgent need for development of next-generation daptomycin-based antibiotics.
    Efforts have previously been made by various research groups to establish the structure–activity relationship (SAR) of daptomycin and produce daptomycin analogues. Mainly, analogues were generated either via genetic engineering of the nonribosomal peptide synthetases in the daptomycin biosynthetic pathway or via the chemoenzymatic and semisynthesis approaches.(20−25) These approaches were constrained by the limited modification sites. To date, only one daptomycin analogue with improved bactericidal activities had been put forward for clinical trials, namely, surotomycin. Surotomycin was produced by substitution of a lipid tail containing alkene and an aromatic ring via enzymatic deacylation of the lipid, followed by reacylation. Surotomycin exhibited potential therapeutic effect on Clostridium difficile-associated diarrhea (CDAD),(25) but it failed the phase 3 clinical trials as it did not show superiority to vancomycin for clinical response over time in treatment of C. difficile infections.(26,27) On the other hand, we expect that chemical synthesis of daptomycin would facilitate the generation of structurally diverse analogues with flexibility and precision. Total syntheses of daptomycin developed by our research team in 2013(28) and Taylor’s group in 2015(29) have made it possible to conduct comprehensive medicinal chemistry studies. Herein, we report the discovery of methylated daptomycin as potential next-generation daptomycin-based antibiotics obtained by chemical synthesis. We anticipate that our findings from this study will enable researchers to develop daptomycin-like calcium-dependent antibiotics.

    Methylated Daptomycin Analogues with Comparable or Improved Antibacterial Activities as Compared to Daptomycin

    More than 80 daptomycin analogues have been obtained via chemical synthesis approaches including alanine scanning, site-specific mutation, functional amino acid swapping, and nonproteinogenic amino acid incorporation, and their antibacterial activities were initially accessed by testing the minimal inhibitory concentrations (MICs) against strains of S. aureus and Enterococcus.(30) Among all of these analogues, we surprisingly discovered that methylation on daptomycin, including N-methylations of Gly-5 (into sarcosine), Trp-1, and Kyn-13 (Figure 1), could maintain or enhance the in vitro antibacterial activities as compared to daptomycin (Table 1). These methylated analogues were synthesized based on total synthesis we developed with adaptations. The methylations were introduced by incorporating the methylated building blocks during the Fmoc-SPPS process. In particular, we identified that the daptomycin analogue with methylated Kyn-13, which we named kynomycin, exhibited a promising antibacterial profile 2–4 times better than that of daptomycin in terms of the ability to eradicate both daptomycin-susceptible and daptomycin-resistant MRSA and VRE. As kynomycin showed the best activity among all of the three methylated analogues against all strains tested, it was chosen as the target for further studies.

    In Vitro Antibacterial Profile of Kynomycin

    The MIC of kynomycin was further tested in 72 clinical Enterococcus spp. strains and 101 clinical S. aureus strains (Table S1 and 2). Among the 72 Enterococcus spp. strains, 35 were resistant (MIC ≥ 8 μg/mL) to daptomycin and 34 exhibited intermediate resistance (MIC = 4 μg/mL). Kynomycin was found to exhibit bactericidal effect at a lower concentration for these bacterial strains compared to daptomycin (Figure 2). The MICs for daptomycin against the 72 Enterococcus spp. strains and the 101 S. aureus strains were 2.68 ± 0.21- and 1.76 ± 0.07-fold (mean ± standard error of mean (SEM)) higher than that of kynomycin on average, respectively (Figure S1). MICs of various antibiotics against these clinical Enterococcus spp. and S. aureus strains are shown in Tables S1 and S2. Most of these tested bacterial strains exhibited different profiles of MICs, suggesting that the tested strains were genetically distinct. Consistently, minimum bactericidal concentration (MBCs) of kynomycin and daptomycin showed the same trend as the MIC. The MBCs of kynomycin and daptomycin were determined to be 0.06 and 0.25 μg/mL, respectively, for S. aureus ATCC29213 and 4 and 16 μg/mL, respectively, for Enterococcus faecalis ATCC29212. Time-killing assays were also performed on the S. aureus strain ATCC29213 and the E. faecalis strain ATCC29212 to compare these two antibiotics on E. faecalis and S. aureus. At concentrations of 4 and 8 μg/mL, both daptomycin and kynomycin exhibited similar efficacy in killing the S. aureus strain ATCC29213 (Figure S2a,b). However, at 16 μg/mL, kynomycin was slightly more effective in eradicating the S. aureus strain ATCC29213 after 24 h of incubation (Figure S2c). In contrast, kynomycin exhibited a higher killing efficacy on the E. faecalis strain ATCC29212 at 8, 16, and 32 μg/mL at all time points of incubation, with the best killing effect identified compared with daptomycin at a concentration of 16 μg/mL (Figure S2d–f).

    In Vivo Antibacterial Activities of Kynomycin

    The in vivo antibacterial activities of kynomycin were tested with different animal models including the wax worm model, the neutropenic mouse thigh infection model, the neutropenic mouse kidney survival model, and the neutropenic mouse sepsis models. Kynomycin was found to exhibit higher antibacterial activities when compared to daptomycin in both S. aureus and E. faecalis infection models. In S. aureus infection models, treatment of wax worms infected by MRSA ATCC43300 or laboratory-induced daptomycin-resistant MRSA strain SA11 (Dap MIC = 2 μg/mL) with kynomycin led to higher survival rates than daptomycin, with much better effect being seen in treating infections caused by the MRSA ATCC43300 strain (Figure 3a,b). In a mouse thigh infection model, treatment of mice infected by the MRSA strain SA11 with kynomycin resulted in a significantly higher rate of eradication of bacteria than daptomycin (P = 0.049) (Figure 3c). A more dramatic difference was observed in mice infected with the MRSA USA300 strain. In the sepsis model, 100% of mice challenged with a lethal dosage of S. aureus USA300 survived upon treatment with 0.925 mg/kg kynomycin, whereas treatment with daptomycin could only rescue half of the mice (Figure 3d). Treatment with 1.25 mg/kg kynomycin, but not daptomycin, was able to clear most of the bacteria from the kidney (P = 0.024) (Figure 3e). In addition, mouse intra-abdominal infection models using strains of MRSA ATCC33591 and MRSA 0114 were also studied. The results showed that ED50 values in the kynomycin treatment groups were 1.38 and 1.65 mg/kg, respectively, which is significantly lower (P = 0.018 and 0.023, respectively) than those in the daptomycin treatment groups (2.14 and 2.19 mg/kg, respectively) (Tables S3 and S4).
    As resistance to daptomycin in VRE is a more urgent clinical issue, the efficacy of daptomycin and kynomycin in treating E. faecalis infection was also tested and compared in both wax worm and mouse infection models. When infected with 1.8×106 CFU of the clinical daptomycin-susceptible E. faecalis strain ZS129 (daptomycin MIC = 0.5 μg/mL), wax worm larvae died within 36 h without treatment. Treatment with 50 mg/kg body weight of daptomycin delayed the death of larvae to 72 h, whereas treatment with 50 mg/kg body weight of kynomycin further delayed killing, with 40% of the treated animals remaining alive at 96 h, suggesting a higher efficacy of kynomycin than daptomycin in treating E. faecalis infection (Figure 3f).
    When infected with the E. faecalis strain T1027, which exhibited daptomycin MIC of 16 μg/mL, daptomycin treatment only slightly reduced the death rate, whereas treatment with kynomycin resulted in 60% survival of the tested animals at 96 h, suggesting that kynomycin is active against daptomycin-resistant E. faecalis (Figure 3g). In the mouse thigh infection model, treatment with daptomycin reduced the CFU of the E. faecalis strain T1027 in mice when compared to the no-treatment group, while treatment with kynomycin caused a significantly (P = 0.011) higher efficacy to kill E. faecalis when compared to the daptomycin group (Figure 3h). These data, which suggested that kynomycin exhibited higher potency than daptomycin in eradicating daptomycin-resistant E. faecalis, are consistent with data obtained from the wax worm experiments. Based on the animal models, kynomycin showed improved antibacterial activity against both S. aureus and E. faecalis infection when compared to daptomycin.

    Evaluations of Kynomycin as a Potential Drug Candidate

    The toxicities of daptomycin and kynomycin were tested and compared to ensure that the increased antibacterial activity of kynomycin was not accompanied with a higher toxicity. A rat cell hemolysis assay was performed, with results showing that both kynomycin and daptomycin did not exhibit hemolytic activity at 320 μg/mL, the highest concentration that we have tested (Figure S3a). The cytotoxicities of daptomycin and kynomycin were also determined and compared in human embryonic kidney 293 (HEK293) cells, with results showing that kynomycin exhibited a lower toxicity than daptomycin. At a concentration of 20 mg/mL or higher, daptomycin exhibited slight cytotoxicity, while kynomycin did not produce any sign of cytotoxicity at a concentration of 40 mg/mL and only minor cytotoxicity at 80 mg/mL (Figure S3b). We also tested the solubility of kynomycin in aqueous solutions. We found that the solubilities of both daptomycin and kynomycin could reach at least 100 mg/mL in 0.9% NaCl (the highest concentration we have tested). While we cannot rule out the possibility that the methylation can affect the solubility, at the clinical administration concentration of daptomycin (i.e., 50 mg/mL), both compounds can be fully dissolved to give a clear yellow solution.
    To test the potential of kynomycin and daptomycin in inducing resistance in bacteria, mutation prevention concentration (MPC) was determined. The results showed that MPCs of kynomycin were 8 and 16 μg/mL, respectively, for S. aureus ATCC29213 and E. faecalis ATCC29212, while those of daptomycin were 16 and 32 μg/mL, respectively. The resistance development potential was also determined by the serial dilution method as described previously.(31) Our data showed that over the 7 day induction period, kynomycin did not cause an increase in its own MIC against S. aureus, while daptomycin led to a twofold increase in MIC on day 1 and an eightfold increase in MIC on day 7. For E. faecalis, although both kynomycin and daptomycin led to increase in MIC during the 7 day induction period, daptomycin induced a slightly higher MIC in a shorter time interval when compared to kynomycin (Figure S4).
    Next, we determined and compared the pharmacokinetics of kynomycin and daptomycin. Our data showed that the plasma concentration of kynomycin upon injection of 20 mg/kg of kynomycin through iv or ip in mice was higher than that of daptomycin at the same dose, suggesting that kynomycin exhibited a better pharmacokinetic profile than daptomycin (Figure S5).
    In addition, we used the amino(4-(6-(amino(iminio)methyl)-1H-indol-2-yl)phenyl)methaniminium chloride assay(32) to compare cell membrane permeability and damage upon daptomycin and kynomycin treatment with S. aureus ATCC29213 and E. faecalis ATCC29212 as model organisms. Amino(4-(6-(amino(iminio)methyl)-1H-indol-2-yl)phenyl)methaniminium chloride is a nucleic acid stain that is impermeant to live cells but can penetrate and stain the nuclei if the membrane integrity was compromised. The effects on cell membrane permeability and damage upon treatment with different concentrations of daptomycin and kynomycin at different time points were recorded by measuring the fluorescence intensity of the bacterial cells. As shown in Figure 4, treatment with kynomycin produced a slightly higher fluorescence signal than daptomycin when S. aureus cells were incubated with 8 μg/mL of the respective antibiotics in the presence of amino(4-(6-(amino(iminio)methyl)-1H-indol-2-yl)phenyl)methaniminium chloride for 0.5 and 2 h. Treatment with various concentrations of kynomycin was also shown to cause increased cell membrane permeability and more severe membrane damage in E. faecalis, with the most dramatic effect being observed after incubation with 16 μg/mL kynomycin, when compared to the effect produced by the same amount of daptomycin (Figure 4a). Consistently, microscopic examination confirmed that treatment with kynomycin resulted in observation of high-density fluorescence in almost every bacterial cell, whereas treatment with daptomycin only induced a weak fluorescence signal, with much fewer cells exhibiting high-density signals. Quantification of the average fluorescence signal indicated that treatment with kynomycin led to significantly higher (P < 0.0001) average fluorescence signals in bacteria cells than daptomycin treatment (Figure 4b–e). The results indicated that both daptomycin and kynomcyin have the same mechanism of action that they cause membrane damage to bacteria. The nucleic acid stain assay results suggested that the enhanced activity of kynomycin against Gram-positive bacteria is most likely due to its ability to induce a greater increase in membrane permeability and bigger damage to the bacterial membrane, probably through its stronger interaction with cell membranes of Gram-positive bacteria containing phosphatidylglycerol. It is also likely that daptomycin and kynomycin affect the de-energized state of the membrane differently.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we found that methylation at certain sites of daptomycin resulted in daptomycin analogues with enhanced antibacterial activities. Among the methylated analogues we synthesized, kynomycin with N-methylation at Kyn-13 is the most active analogue based on the preliminary screening by MIC determination. Further in vitro and in vivo experiments showed that kynomycin is more effective in treating MRSA and VRE infection. Moreover, kynomycin is active against daptomycin-resistant strains and has a lower tendency to induce resistance in S. aureus. Furthermore, kynomycin was shown to exhibit slightly lower toxicity than daptomycin in red blood cell hemolysis assays and the HEK293 cytotoxicity assay.
    Our results showed that methylation of Kyn in daptomycin improves the pharmacokinetic profile of kynomycin compared to daptomycin. In addition, the results of the nucleic acid stain assay suggested that the increase in the antibacterial activities of kynomycin may also correlate with the increased membrane permeability and damage.
    Our efforts in structural modifications of daptomycin, using total chemical synthesis, led to the discovery of kynomycin. The in vitro and in vivo evaluation showed that kynomycin has the potential to be developed as a new antibiotic against multidrug-resistant pathogens. It is anticipated that our studies herein will help guide the future design and development of cyclic peptide-based calcium-dependent antibiotics.

    General Experimental Details

    All commercially available materials were used without further purification. Amino acids, coupling reagents, and resins were obtained from GL Biochem, CSBio, and Chem-Impex unless otherwise specified. Fmoc-1-Me-Trp-OH was purchased from Bachem. Daptomycin was purchased from Carbosynth Limited and Melone Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd. All noncommercially available reagents were synthesized according to the corresponding referenced literature. Anhydrous tetrahydrofuran (THF) was freshly distilled from sodium and benzophenone. All analytical reversed-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC) separations involving a mobile phase of 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) (v/v) in acetonitrile (Solvent A) and 0.1% TFA (v/v) in water (Solvent B) were performed on a Waters HPLC system equipped with a binary pump (Waters 1525), a photodiode array detector (Waters 2996), and a mass spectrometer (Waters 3100) using a Grace Vydac 218TP C18 column (5 μm, 300 Å, 4.6 × 250 mm2) at a flow rate of 0.6 mL/min. Preparative HPLC separations involving a mobile phase of 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) (v/v) in acetonitrile (Solvent A) and 0.1% TFA (v/v) in water (Solvent B) were performed on a Waters HPLC system equipped with a quaternary gradient module (Waters 2545) and a photodiode array detector (Waters 2996), using either a Grace Vydac 218TP C18 column (10 μm, 300 Å, 22 × 250 mm2) at a flow rate of 10 mL/min or a Waters XBridge BEH C18 OBD prep column (10 μm, 130 Å, 30 × 250 mm2) at a flow rate of 15 mL/min. Analytical TLC was performed on E. Merck silica gel 60-F254 plates and visualized under UV light (254 nm) or by staining with ninhydrin. Flash column chromatography was performed on E. Merck 230–400-mesh silica gel 60. The 1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on Advance DRX Bruker 400, 500, and 600 MHz spectrometers or a Bruker ASCEND 500 MHz spectrometer. The 2D NMR spectra were recorded on Advance DRX Bruker 600 MHz and Bruker ASCEND 500 MHz spectrometers. High-resolution mass spectrometry was performed on a Waters Micromass Q-TOF Premier Mass spectrometer. All tested compounds were determined to be >95% purity by RP-HPLCMS.

    General Procedures for Daptomycin Analogue Synthesis

    Fmoc Deprotection

    The Fmoc deprotection was carried out using 3 mL of 20% piperidine in DMF at room temperature for 20 min. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL).

    Coupling of Commercially Available Fmoc Amino Acids and Decanoic Acid

    Fmoc amino acid or building block (4 equiv), HATU (4 equiv), and DIEA (8 equiv) were dissolved in DMF, and the solution was added to the resin and gently agitated on vortex with the resin at room temperature for 1 h. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL).

    Synthesis of Kynomycin (1)

    First, 100 mg of 2-chlorotrityl chloride resin was swollen in dry DCM for 20 min in a 5 mL disposable vessel (TORIVQ) equipped with a porous polypropylene disc at the bottom. A solution of Fmoc-Gly-OH (2.0 equiv) and DIEA (4.0 equiv) in DCM was added, and the reaction vessel was shaken on the vortex at room temperature for 1 h. The resin was washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL) and then was shaken with a solution of DCM/MeOH/DIEA (17:2:1, v/v/v, 3 mL) for 20 min and washed again with DMF (5 × 3 mL). Fmoc deprotection was carried out using 3 mL of 20% piperidine in DMF at room temperature for 20 min. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL). Fmoc-Asp(OtBu)-OH (4 equiv), HATU (4 equiv), and DIEA (8 equiv) were dissolved in DMF, and the solution was added to the resin and gently agitated on a vortex with the resin at room temperature for 1 h. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL). The resin was subsequently submitted to an iterative Fmoc-SPPS coupling/deprotection cycle for the coupling of d-Ala, Asp, and ornithine (Orn). After the coupling of Fmoc-Orn(Boc)-OH, the tetrapeptide building block S4 (2 equiv), HATU (2 equiv), and DIEA (4 equiv) were dissolved in DMF. The solution was added to the resin and gently agitated on a vortex with the resin at room temperature for 2 h. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL), followed by Fmoc deprotection using 3 mL of 20% piperidine in DMF for 10 min. A solution of Fmoc-mGlu(OtBu)-OH(33) (2 equiv), HATU (2 equiv), and DIEA (4 equiv) in DMF was gently agitated on a vortex with the resin at room temperature for 2 h. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL). Standard Fmoc deprotection then followed. Then, Alloc-d-Ser(tBu)-OH (4 equiv), HATU (4 equiv), and DIEA (8 equiv) were dissolved in DMF, and the solution was added to the resin and gently agitated on the vortex with the resin at room temperature for 1 h. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL). Then, to reduce the azido group, a solution of 1 M dithiothreitol (DTT) with 0.5 M DIEA in DMF was gently agitated on the vortex with the resin at room temperature for 3 h. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL) followed by an Fmoc-SPPS coupling/deprotection cycle for the coupling of d-Asn, Trp, and decanoic acid. The resin was then washed with DMF (5 × 3 mL) and DCM (5 × 3 mL). The reaction vessel was stoppered by a septum and charged with argon. A solution of 0.16 mL of PhSiH3 in 1 mL of dry DCM was added to the resin by a syringe, followed by addition of a solution of 10 mg of Pd(PPh3)4 in 1 mL of dry DCM. After 1.5 h, the resin was washed with DCM (5 × 3 mL) and then subjected to 5 mL of mild acidic cleavage cocktail of DCM/AcOH/trifluoroethanol (8/1/1, v/v/v) for 1 h; this step was repeated for another 30 min. Following filtration, the resulting cleavage solutions were combined and concentrated in vacuo to give the crude side-chain protected peptide. The crude peptide was further dried by vacuum prior to macrocyclization. To a solution of the crude peptide (1 equiv) in DCM:DMF = 3:1 (5 mM) were added PyBOP (5 equiv) and DIEA (5 equiv). The solution was stirred at room temperature for 16 h. After the solvent was removed in vacuo, 10 mL of cleavage cocktail (TFA/PhOH/H2O = 95:2.5:2.5, v/v/v) was then added and the solution was stirred at room temperature for 2 h. The solution was concentrated under a stream of nitrogen, and the crude product was precipitated by cold diethyl ether. Diethyl ether was discarded after centrifugation, and the crude product was washed with diethyl ether three times. The washed crude product was dried under vacuum to remove residual diethyl ether. The crude product was dissolved in 0.1N HCl (5 mM). The solution was heated to 60 °C for 15 h to remove the CHO group at the side chain of kynurenine (Kyn). The solution was diluted with 20% acetonitrile and subjected to HPLC purification using a gradient of 30–55% solvent A/solvent B over 35 min followed by lyophilization, affording kynomycin as a yellowish powder (5.7 mg, 10%).     Antimicrobial Susceptibility Tests

    A total of 72 clinical Enterococcus spp. strains and 101 clinical S. aureus strains with various MICs of daptomycin used to screen for the activity of kynomycin were isolated from patients in the Second Affiliated Hospital of Zhejiang University. S. aureus and Enterococcus spp. strains were assayed for their susceptibilities to both daptomycin and analogues, respectively, by the broth microdilution method as recommended by the Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI)(34) in the presence of 50 μg/mL Ca2+ ions. Minimal test concentration (MIC) was recorded at which no bacterial growth was observed.

    Mutation Prevention Concentration (MPC) and Minimum Bactericidal Concentration (MBC)

    MPC was measured on MH agar containing various concentrations of daptomycin or kynomycin. The minimal daptomycin/kynomycin concentration that inhibited growth of S. aureus ATCC29213 after 72 h of incubation at 37°C was taken as MPC.(35,36) MBC was determined by inoculating loopful of medium from the MIC inhibitory that showed no growth onto new LB agars and incubated overnight at 37 °C as previously described.(37) MBC was the concentration at which no growth was observed.

    Time-Killing Assay

    An overnight culture was diluted 1:100 in MH broth and incubated at 37 °C with aeration until OD600 reached 0.3. Bacteria were then treated with antibiotics at different concentrations (4, 8, and 16 μg/mL for S. aureus; 8, 16, and 32 μg/mL for E. faecalis) at 37 °C and 250 rpm. At indicated time intervals, 100 μL of culture was removed, followed by 10-fold dilutions. Then, 100 μL from various dilutions was spread onto LB agar and incubated at 37 °C until colony-forming units (CFUs) were evident. CFU was counted in the various dilutions in which separate colonies are obtained (25–250 CFUs). The final CFUs in the culture were determined by multiplying to the dilution factors.

    Galleria mellonella (Wax Worm) Killing Assays

    Wax moth larvae (G. mellonella) weighing around 300 mg (around 5 weeks old) were purchased from Tianjin Kaide Ruixin International Trade Co., Ltd. (Tianjing, China) and were maintained on woodchips in the dark at 15 °C until being used. S. aureus ATCC43300 and E. faecalis strains ZS129 and T1027 were cultured in LB broth overnight and diluted into fresh broth at 1:100 dilution for 5 h, followed by washing and resuspension of the pellet in sterile 0.9% NaCl solution (50 mL). Bacterial infection of G. mellonella was conducted as previously described,(38,39) and the survival rate of G. mellonella was recorded. Briefly, larvae were divided into four groups, with 10 in each group. Three groups of larvae were subjected to injection of 10 μL (S. aureus ATCC43300, 7.4 ×106 CFU; E. faecalis ZS129, 2.8 ×107 CFU; and E. faecalis T1027, 3.7 ×107 CFU) of bacterial suspension into the last right proleg and one group was injected with PBS. Infected larvae were treated with saline, 50 mg/kg daptomycin or kynomycin in 5 μL volume at the last right proleg. The death of larvae (no response to physical stimuli) was recorded at 12, 18, 24, 30, 36, 48, 60, and 72 h postinfection. All experiments were carried out in triplicate.

    Murine Thigh Infection Model

    NIH mice were purchased from the Guangdong provincial experimental animal center. Male NIH mice (average 12 g, 4 weeks) were allowed food and water throughout the study. Mice (n = 5) were rendered neutropenic by a series of intraperitoneal cyclophosphamide injections for 4 days at a dose of 150 mg/kg before infection and treatment experiments. E. faecalis strain T1027 and MRSA SA11 strain were cultured in LB broth overnight, diluted into fresh broth at 1:100 dilution for 5 h, followed by washing and resuspension of the pellet in sterile 0.9% NaCl solution (50 mL). Mice were then infected by single injection of 7.4 × 107 CFU of the E. faecalis strain T1027 (200 μL) or 7.5 × 107 CFU of the MRSA SA11 strain (200 μL) at their thigh muscle. The mice were then treated subcutaneously with 50 mg/kg daptomycin or kynomycin in 0.1 mL/mice at 2 and 12 h postinfection. The control group was treated with saline. Mice were sacrificed at 48 or 60 h postinfection, and the thigh muscles were homogenized in a sterile homogeneous bag (for 1 g added 10 mL sterile 0.9% NaCl solution). Various dilutions were spread on an agar plate to determine the CFU of the survived E. faecalis strain. Comparison between kynomycin and daptomycin groups was analyzed using the unpaired t-test by Graph Pad Prism. All experimental protocols followed the standard operating procedures of the approved biosafety level 2 animal facilities and were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of The Hong Kong Polytechnic University.

    Abdominal Infection Mouse Model

    Cytotoxicity Assay on Human Embryonic Kidney 293 Cells (HEK293)

    HEK293 was cultured in a medium containing 90% Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM, high glucose) and 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (HyClone, USA). HEK293 cells were seeded in a sterile 96-well plate (Cellter, Chile) and incubated for 24 h before experiments. Various concentrations of daptomycin and kynomycin were added to cells. Cells without treatment were included as the control. Each experiment was repeated four times. After treatment at 37 °C for 24 h, 20 μL of MTT (BioFroxx, Germany) solution (5 mg/mL) was added to a 96-well plate. A mixture of the cell culture medium and MTT was removed 4 h later, and 150 μL of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was added to each well to dissolve the purple sediment at the bottom. The plate was shaken gently for 10 min. Four wells with only 150 μL of DMSO were regarded as the blank control. The absorbance of each sample at 570 nm was measured using a microplate reader (Clariostar, BMG). ABSt, ABSc, and ABSb were defined as the absorbance values of the test group, the control group, and the blank group, respectively. The cell survival rate was calculated by %Survival ratio = 1 – [(ABSc – ABSt)/(ABSc – ABSb) × 100%].

    Hemolytic Assay

    Fresh rat blood was extracted to test the hemolysis of daptomycin and kynomycin. In brief, fresh blood was centrifuged for 5 min at 3000 rpm to collect red blood cells, which were subjected to a washing procedure using PBS buffer (HyClone, USA) three times. Resultant red blood cells were diluted to 8% solution with PBS. Various concentrations of daptomycin and kynomycin (test group) were added into the red blood cells solution and incubated for 1 h at 37 °C. Negative control (red blood cells without any compounds) and positive control (red blood cells with 0.1% Triton X-100) were set up for reference. Cells were then centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 5 min. The supernatant was carefully transferred to a 96-well plate to obtain the absorbance at 540 nm. ABSt, ABSpc, and ABSnc were defined as absorbance values of the test group, positive control, and negative control, respectively. The rate of hemolysis is calculated by %hemolysis = [(ABSt – ABSnc)/(ABSpc – ABSnc)] × 100%.

    Solubility Test

    An accurately weighed amount of daptomycin and kynomycin was added to a 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tube. Then, a calculated amount of 0.9% NaCl for reaching the target concentration of 100 mg/mL was added to the tubes. The tubes were then inverted a few times to dissolve the powder and were set aside for 15 mins. Afterward, the solution was inspected after centrifugation. Both daptomycin and kynomycin were fully dissolved to give clear yellow solutions.

    Resistance Development Assays

    Resistance development was performed by the sequential passaging method as described previously.(31) Briefly, S. aureus ATCC29213 and E. faecalis ATCC29212 were incubated at 37°C until the exponential phase. It was then diluted to OD600 of 0.01 in 1 mL of MHB supplemented with 50 μg/mL calcium ion, which also contained daptomycin or kynomycin (0.5× MIC, 1× MIC, 2× MIC, 4× MIC). Samples were incubated at 37 °C with 250 rpm agitation. At 24 h intervals, the cultures were checked for growth. Cultures from the highest concentrations that showed sign of growth were diluted 1:100 into fresh media containing 0.5× MIC, 1× MIC, 2× MIC, and 4× MIC of daptomycin or kynomycin. The serial passaging was repeated daily for 7 days. When determining if resistance is acquired by the tested strains during serial passaging in the presence of sub-MIC levels of antimicrobials, for daptomycin 16 and 512 μg/mL were the highest concentrations tested on S. aureus ATCC29213 and E. faecalis ATCC29212, respectively. For kynomycin, 4 and 64 μg/mL were the highest concentrations tested on S. aureus ATCC29213 and E. faecalis ATCC29212, respectively. The cultures that grew at the highest concentration at each passage were transferred on drug-free MHA plates, and the MIC was then determined by broth microdilution.

    Pharmacokinetics Study

    Male Sprague–Dawley rats (8–9 weeks) were randomly grouped into four groups, with four rats per group. Rats in group 1 were injected with 20 mg/kg kynomycin in the tail vein; rats in group 2 were intraperitoneally injected with the same dose of kynomycin; rats in group 3 were injected with 20 mg/kg daptomycin in the tail vein; and rats in group 4 were intraperitoneally injected with the same dose of daptomycin. Plasma samples were collected at 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, and 24 h postinjection and stored at −20 °C for determining the drug concentration in the plasma later. All collected samples were brought to room temperature. A mixture of 100 μL of plasma, 50 μL of reserpine solution, and 350 μL of methanol (MeOH) was vortexed for 1.0 min and centrifuged at 13 000g for 10 min. The supernatant was transferred to a 1.5 mL tube and dried using a gentle stream of nitrogen. The residue was then reconstituted in 100 μL of MeOH/water (50/50, v/v), vortexed for 1 min, and centrifuged again at 13 000g for 10 min. Then, 5 μL of the sample was injected into the UPLC-ESI-MS/MS system for analysis. All experimental protocols followed the standard operating procedures of the approved biosafety level 2 animal facilities and were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of The Hong Kong Polytechnic University.

    Bacterial Cell Membrane Permeability and Damage Assays

    The bacterial cell membrane permeability and damage assay was performed using the SYTOX green staining approach. SYTOX green exhibited green-fluorescence color upon binding to the nucleus and chromosome of bacteria. It is impermeant to living cells, making it nonfluorescent in a living bacteria culture. However, bacterial cells with enhanced cell permeability due to membrane damage or cell death would be stained as green fluorescence.(32,40,41) Briefly, an overnight culture of S. aureus strain ATCC29213 or E. faecalis strain ATCC29212 was diluted 1:100 in MH broth and incubated at 37 °C with aeration until OD600 reached 0.3. Bacteria were then treated with antibiotics at different concentrations (8 μg/mL for S. aureus; 8, 16, and 32 μg/mL for E. faecalis) and incubated at 37 °C with 250 rpm. At intervals of 0.5 and 2 h, aliquots were removed and washed three times with sterile saline. Then, 200 μL of bacteria suspension at OD600 of 0.2–0.3 was incubated with 1 μM SYTOX Green for 10 minutes at room temperature. Fluorescence was measured by a microplate reader (CLARIOstar, BMG) at a wavelength of 523 ± 5 nm upon excitation at 504 ± 5 nm. Each experiment was repeated three times, and the average of three experiments was recorded. Microscopic measurements of membrane permeability were performed similarly as described above using confocal laser scanning microscopy (Leica TCS SP8 MP), with the exception that the OD600 of bacteria suspension used was about 1. After incubation with SYTOX Green, 2 μL of aliquots of the bacteria suspension was added on a glass slide and covered by a coverslip. Fluorescence images were taken at a detection wavelength of 523 ± 20 nm upon excitation at 504 ± 20 nm. The signals were quantified by LAS X software.         The effects of 405 nm light on bacterial membrane integrity determined by salt and bile tolerance assays, leakage of UV-absorbing material and SYTOX green labelling
        McKenzie, Karen; MacLean, Michelle; Helen, Grant M.; Ramakrishnan, Praveen; MacGregor, Scott J.; Anderson, John G.
        Microbiology (London, United Kingdom) (2016), 162 (9), 1680-1688CODEN: MROBEO; ISSN:1465-2080. (Microbiology Society)
        Bacterial inactivation by 405 nm light is accredited to the photoexcitation of intracellular porphyrin mols. resulting in energy transfer and the generation of reactive oxygen species that impart cellular oxidative damage. The specific mechanism of cellular damage, however, is not fully understood. Previous work has suggested that destruction of nucleic acids may be responsible for inactivation; however, microscopic imaging has suggested membrane damage as a major constituent of cellular inactivation. This study investigates the membrane integrity of Escherichia coli and Staphylococcus aureus exposed to 405 nm light. Results indicated membrane damage to both species, with loss of salt and bile tolerance by S. aureus and E. coli, resp., consistent with reduced membrane integrity. Increased nucleic acid release was also demonstrated in 405 nm light-exposed cells, with up to 50% increase in DNA concn. into the extracellular media in the case of both organisms. SYTOX green fluorometric anal., however, demonstrated contradictory results between the two test species. With E. coli, increasing permeation of SYTOX green was obsd. following increased exposure, with >500% increase in fluorescence, whereas no increase was obsd. with S. aureus. Overall, this study has provided good evidence that 405 nm light exposure causes loss of bacterial membrane integrity in E. coli, but the results with S. aureus are more difficult to explain. Further work is required to gain greater understanding of the inactivation mechanism in different bacterial species, as there are likely to be other targets within the cell that are also impaired by the oxidative damage from photo-generated reactive oxygen species.
