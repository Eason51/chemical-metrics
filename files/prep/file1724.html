    Abstract

    Various racemic and enantioenriched (trans)-X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthoyl analogues as well as X-hydroxy-naphthoyl analogues of CRTh2 antagonist 2-(2-(1-naphthoyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic acid (1, Setipiprant/ACT-129968) were synthesized in order to gain insight into regio- and enantioselectivity of the metabolic oxidation of 1 and to verify the structures of four metabolites that were proposed earlier in a clinical ADME study. Analytical data of the synthetic standards were compared with data from samples of biological origin. The two major metabolites M7 and M9 were unambiguously verified as 2-(2-((trans)-3,4-dihydroxy-3,4-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)– and 2-(2-((trans)-5,6-dihydroxy-5,6-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic acid, respectively, each composed of two enantiomers with 68% and 44% ee in favor of (+)-(3S,4S)-M7 and (+)-(5S,6S)-M9, respectively. Likewise, minor metabolites M3 and M13 were identified as 2-(8-fluoro-2-(5-hydroxy-1-naphthoyl)– and 2-(8-fluoro-2-(4-hydroxy-1-naphthoyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5-yl)acetic acid, respectively.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Prostaglandin D2 (PGD2), a major metabolite of arachidonic acid, is predominantly involved in the physiological response to allergy. It is released at high concentrations by immunoglobulin E activated mast cells upon allergen challenge and was demonstrated to activate two G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs), the DP1 receptor and the chemoattractant receptor-homologous molecule expressed on T-helper 2 cells (CRTh2),(1, 2) more recently titled DP2 receptor.(3) CRTh2 activation leads to the recruitment of granulocytes and Th2 cells by chemotaxis to the inflammation site and has been demonstrated to be pro-inflammatory.(2, 4, 5) Furthermore, it is now well established that antagonizing selectively the CRTh2 receptor could be useful in the treatment of asthma and other inflammatory diseases of the airways such as allergic rhinitis.(4, 6, 7) More recently, mouse and human studies suggested that inhibition of PGD2 signaling has the potential for the prevention and treatment of hair loss in man, often presenting as male pattern baldness, or androgenetic alopecia.(8)
    CRTh2 antagonist 2-(2-(1-naphthoyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic acid (1, Chart 1), originally discovered and developed for the treatment of asthma and seasonal allergic rhinitis,(9) now under development to treat androgenic alopecia in male subjects (KYTH-105),(10) was reported a low clearance compound with a Clint value of 5 μL min–1 (mg protein)−1 in both human and mouse liver microsomes, Clint of 3.1 μL min–1 (106 cells)−1 in rat hepatocytes, and CLiv 1.3 mL min–1 kg–1 after iv application to rats.(11) Recently, data were published from a study investigating the absorption, disposition, metabolism, and elimination (ADME) of 1 in healthy male subjects. A single oral dose of 1000 mg of 1, spiked with [14C]1 as radiotracer (mixture of [14C]1/nonlabeled 1 in a ratio of approximately 1:2,200), was administered with a total radioactivity of 2.60–2.62 MBq (approximately 71 μCi).(12) In this study, 1 appeared to be metabolized to only a moderate extent. Two metabolites M7 and M9 (Chart 1) accounted for 9.3% and 20% of the administered radioactive dose in the excreta (urine and feces). With respect to the administered dose, the recovered mean radioactivity of M7 and M9 in urine accounted for 4.78% and 1.04%, respectively. Moreover, M7 was the only metabolite quantifiable in plasma but at concentrations consistently below 10% of those of the parent drug. Structurally, M7 and M9 were proposed as two distinct dihydroxy-dihydronaphthoyl isomers. In addition, various metabolites W1, W2, W3, and V (Chart 1), most likely monohydroxylated at the naphthoyl moiety, were identified in feces and urine, however in much lower concentrations.
    Herein, the syntheses of various (trans)-X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthoyl regioisomers in their racemic and enantioenriched form as well as regioisomeric X-hydroxy-naphthoyl analogues of 1 are described, and their use as synthetic standards is described to assign and verify the structures of metabolites M3, M7, M9, and M13 (Chart 1). The two major metabolites M7 and M9 were isolated from human urine, and the structures of the proposed (trans)-X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthoyl moieties were unequivocally assigned. Additionally, the structures of the two minor metabolites M3 and M13, both containing a X-hydroxynaphthoyl group, were elucidated. With the data provided, insight was gained into the regio- and enantioselective process of the metabolic naphthyl ring oxidation in a more complex molecule.
    Metabolites identified in in vitro metabolism assays were termed M1, M2, M3, etc., a letter code (C, D, E, etc.) was used in the above-quoted clinical study. Therein, the two most dominant metabolites were correlated with M7 and M9 of the original in in vitro studies, and hence the terms were adopted.

    Metabolite Identification in Human Urine

    Recently pooled human urine samples became available that were collected in a phase I in vivo human ADME study of 1.(12) A total volume of 500 mL of human urine containing excreted 1 and metabolic products (14C-labeled, Figure 3c in ref 12), was lyophilized and the lyophilizate was extracted with EtOAc. After preparative HPLC separation, the two metabolites M7 and M9 were isolated in mg amounts (7.3 and 2.3 mg, respectively) as powder and were characterized by means of LC/MS, LC-MS/MS, and 1H NMR spectroscopy. Despite the high dilution with unlabeled product (approx.1:2,200), the respective 14C-labeled M7 ([14C]M7) and M9 ([14C]M9) were detectable with high resolution MS, however, because 14C-labeled products only differ in +2 amu (Table S1, Supporting Information) and otherwise display identical fragmentation pattern, only data of the unlabeled portion will be presented and discussed within the following paragraph.
    Presented in Figure 1 are the positive ion ESI LC-MS of M7 and M9 with high resolution accurate mass measurements and with the respective MS/MS fragmentation pattern obtained upon collision induced dissociation (CID) and high-energy collision dissociation (HCD). Eluting at tR = 32.98 and 34.59 min, protonated molecules were detected at m/z 437.1513 [M + H]+, corresponding to a theoretical formula with the elemental composition of C24H22FN2O5 (ΔM +1.4 ppm). Compared with 1, the observed molecular mass of both, M7 and M9, differed by +34 amu and likely corresponded to the addition of two hydrogen and two oxygen atoms.
    For both metabolites, fragment ions of m/z 419 [M + H-H2O]+ were detected upon CID of the [M + H]+ ion at m/z 437.1 in the ion trap (CID 30% relative collision energy), indicating loss of one molecule of H2O. A key fragment for both, M7 and M9, was found at m/z 232, which is typical for the tetrahydropyridoindole-acetic acid core of 1. Due to the fact that the core remained unchanged, it was concluded that the two putative hydroxyl groups are located at the naphthalene ring, and that both M7 and M9 are representatives of generic structure 2 (Chart 1). The observed fragment ions m/z 171 of M7 and M9 and m/z 189 of M9 were attributed to the naphthoyl part and are in agreement with proposed X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl cationic fragments, m/z 171 is indicative for a characteristic loss of another H2O from fragment m/z 189.

    Metabolite Identification in Human Microsomes, Hepatocytes, and Bactosomes

    Radiolabeled [14C]1 (61 mCi/mmol) was incubated at single substrate concentration of 10 μM with freshly prepared or cryopreserved human hepatocytes for up to 24 h. The viabilities of cryopreserved and freshly prepared hepatocytes were 77% and 93%, respectively, the radioactivity recovery exceeded 98% in both cases. There was a moderate difference in the rates of metabolism between the cryopreserved batch and the freshly prepared cells, e.g., after 24 h incubation time, 14 metabolites, denominated M2, M3, M5, M7, M9, M10, M11, M12, M13, M15, M18, M21, M32, and M33, were detected and were accounting for 61% and 89% of the total initial radioactivity, whereas remaining [14C]1 accounted for 37% and 11%, respectively. After 24 h incubation time, both major metabolites, M7 and M9, were found at retention time (tR) = 33.8 min ([14C]M7) and tR = 32.2 min ([14C]M9), accounting together for 38% and 43% of the initial radioactivity with the cryopreserved and freshly prepared cells, respectively (Figure 2a).
    Six metabolites were observed after incubating [14C]1 with human liver microsomes for 60 min, namely M2, M3, M7, M9, M13, and M15, altogether totalizing 16% of the initial radioactivity. Again, [14C]M7 and [14C]M9 were identified as the most prominent metabolites, which together were accountable for more than 60% of all metabolite radioactivity (Figure 2b).
    As expected, retention times (radiochromatogram and LC-MS) were observed for [14C]M7 and [14C]M9 after incubating [14C]1 with human hepatocytes and microsomes that were comparable with M7 and M9 in human urine. Considering the presence of 14C in the molecule, exact molecular mass, theoretical formula as well as MS/MS fragmentation pattern were identical and corresponded to structure 2 (Supporting Information, Table S1, Figure S1, Figure S2).
    Two metabolites, marked as M3 and M13, were identified as two X-hydroxy-1-naphthoyl regioisomers in a radiochromatogram after incubating [14C]1 with hepatocytes and microsomes (Figure 2a,b). To further investigate mechanistically the formation of M7, M9, M3, and M13 and to deduce their structures, incubations of [14C]1 (61 mCi/mmol) with bacterial membranes containing human cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes and coexpressed human NADPH-cytochrome P450 reductase (human bactosomes) were performed in the presence and absence of microsomal human epoxide hydrolase (hEH). Among the studied human bactosomes with enzymes 1A1, 1A2, 2A6, 2B6, 2C8, 2C9, 2C18, 2C19, 2D6, 2E1, 3A4, and 3A5, only human bactosome 2C8, 2C9, and 3A4 were found to produce M3 (2.2%, 13%, 3.1%) and M13 (8.4%, 4.4%, 1.4%) after 60 min incubation time. A representative radiochromatogram with bactosome 2C9 (Figure 2c) revealed that two metabolites were corresponding to the [14C]M3 and [14C]M13 peak in the incubations with human hepatocytes and human microsomes (Figure 2a,b). Accurate mass measurement by LC-MS/MS provided at tR = 42.3 min and at tR = 43.5 min an m/z of 421.1439 [M + H]+ for both ions, corresponding to a theoretical formula with the elemental composition of C2314CH20FN2O4 (ΔM −0.2 ppm). After incubating human bactosomes 2C8, 2C9, and 3A4 with 10 μM of [14C]1 in the presence of microsomal hEH, [14C]M3 (1.2%, 9.8%, 3.1%) and [14C]M13 (3.5%, 3.9%, 1.4%) as well as [14C]M7 (4.3%, 0.97, 0.46%) and [14C]M9 (1.4%, 3.0%, 2.0%) were identified in the assay cocktail and were verified by LC-MS, as demonstrated with a representative radiochromatogram from a bactosome 2C9 incubation (Figure 2d). These data support the formation of a transient oxirene (IIa–g, Scheme 1) as the first intermediate in the metabolic naphthyl ring oxidation.
    The metabolism of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) in general and of naphthalene derivatives in particular has been investigated for decades. For example, as early as in 1947, Young was able to isolate and characterize (−)-(trans)-1,2-dihydro-1,2-dihydroxy-naphthalene from rat urine after subcutaneous administration of naphthalene.(13) Later, van Bladeren et al.(14) and Yang(15) reported on the enantiomeric composition of (trans)-1,2-dihydrodiol metabolites formed from PAH. Two consecutive enzymatic steps were proposed for this transformation: an initial regio- and stereoselective epoxidation occurring at a formal double bond of a PAH by microsomal CYP enzymes, followed by a regio- and enantioselective hydration of the formed epoxide by microsomal epoxide hydrolase. Naphthalene I, R substituted at position C(1), offers seven formal double bonds for epoxidation that would lead to isomeric dihydronaphthalene epoxide (IIa–g, Scheme 1). The two sterically more hindered sites, the C(1)–C(2) and the C(7)–C(8) double bonds were thought to be less prone to the enzyme catalyzed oxidation leading to IIa and IIf, respectively. Epoxides IIb and IIe as well as dihydrodiols IIIb and IIIe were expected to be unstable, mainly due to the nonaromatic character of the apparent tetraene structures. Once formed, epoxides IIb and IIe were anticipated to rearrange to the respective benzo[d]oxepine rather than to undergo hydration to form the ring-opened 2,3-dihydroxy-2,3-dihydronaphthalene IIIb and 6,7-dihydroxy-6,7-dihydro-naphthalene IIIe.(16-18) To date, no precedent has been reported on nonaromatic naphthalene oxide IIg and 4a,8a-dihydronaphthalene-4a,8a-diol IIIg, neither as putative naphthalene metabolites nor on their synthetic access and properties. Because of the vast steric hindrance, enzyme catalyzed epoxidation and hydrolase-catalyzed hydration of the epoxide was expected to occur rather unlikely at this position.
    On the basis of both, the aforementioned mass spectroscopy results and the above-discussed literature, metabolism of 1 to M7 and M9 was hypothesized to occur in two consecutive enzymatic steps, with the initial CYP catalyzed epoxidation at the naphthyl ring, followed by the EH catalyzed hydration of the epoxide, thus opening the oxirane ring to form two out of seven hypothetical vicinal substituted (trans)-X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthoyl regioisomers.
    In concordance with the results of Jerina et al., who demonstrated 1,2-naphthalene oxide as the sole intermediate in the microsomal metabolism of naphthalene, epoxides IIa, IIc, IId, and IIf were considered potential intermediates (Scheme 1) in the oxidation process of I.(19) Taking into consideration that the CYP catalyzed epoxidation might take place preferentially at the two least hindered and more alkene-like C(3)–C(4) and C(5)–C(6) double bonds, formation of dihydronaphtho[1,2-b]oxirene IIc and IId, respectively, was anticipated. Therefore, in the particular case of the metabolic oxidation of 1, (trans)-3,4-dihydroxy-3,4-dihydronaphthoyl 2b and (trans)-5,6-dihydroxy-5,6-dihydronaphthoyl 2c were proposed to be formed as major metabolites (Chart 2). However, in the absence of EH, oxirenes IIc and IId were expected to rearrange with a subsequent hydride migration (NIH shift), leading to the rearomatized 4-hydroxy- and 5-hydroxynaphthyl regioisomers IVa and IVc, respectively (Scheme 1). Therefore, structures 3a and 3b were anticipated to represent monohydroxylated metabolites of 1 (Chart 2).
    Two studies were supportive regarding the preferred sites of epoxidation. Data of an in vitro biotransformation study with antifungal 5 (Chart 3) using human liver microsomes and recombinant human CYPs,(20) and an in vivo metabolism study where calcimimetic agent 6 (Chart 3) was administered to animals and human volunteers(21) led to the conclusion that the respective metabolites comprised a (trans)-3,4-dihydroxy-3,4-dihydronaphthalene and a (trans)-5,6-dihydroxy-5,6-dihydronaphthalene modification. However, as indicated with arrows in Chart 3, inconsistent conclusions concerning the site of oxidative attack were provided by three other reports on the metabolism of drugs or drug candidates containing naphthyl as a structural feature. For example, isomeric (trans)-3,4-dihydroxy-3,4-dihydro– and (trans)-7,8-dihydroxy-7,8-dihydro-naphthalene derivatives were proposed as main metabolites in human urine samples from pharmacokinetic studies with 4, suggesting that enzymatic attack occurred at one of the preferred and at one of the sterically more hindered double bond.(22) In two in vitro microsomal metabolism studies, the authors claimed that both cannabinoid receptor CB1 agonist 7,(23) and peripheral cannabinoid receptor CB2 agonist 8,(24) were leading to the respective nonaromatic 2,3-dihydroxy-2,3-dihydronaphthalene metabolites. However, in both cases, a second dihydroxy-dihydronaphthalene isomer was observed and characterized. A favorable (trans)-5,6-dihydro-5,6-dihydroxy-naphthalene structure was assigned for 7 and again a sterically less favored (trans)-7,8-dihydro-7,8-diol for 8.
    Consequently, the syntheses of the four regioisomers 2a–d (Chart 2), in racemic and in enantioenriched form, were envisaged. These synthetic standards were expected to be useful tools for assigning the correct structure to the (trans)-X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthoyl group in M7 and M9 by comparing the analytical data with those obtained from biological samples. Hence, further insight into the regio- and enantioselective course of the metabolic oxidation of 1 was anticipated. Herein, no attempts have been undertaken toward the syntheses of unprecedented and unlikely to be generated regioisomers of structural type 2,3-dihydroxy-2,3-dihydronaphthalene (IIIb), 6,7-dihydroxy-6,7-dihydro-naphthalene (IIIe), and 4a,8a-dihydronaphthalene-4a,8a-diol (IIIg).
    Generally, dihydronaphtho-oxirenes like IIc and IId (Scheme 1) are difficult to detect by means of mass spectrometric analysis, especially in biological samples. This is due to the short lifetime of IIc and IId and the propensity to immediately rearrange with a subsequent hydride migration (NIH shift) in aqueous media or under the ionization conditions in the mass spectrometer, preferentially leading to respective rearomatized X-hydroxy-1-naphtyl regioisomers IVa and IVc.(25) An effort was directed toward the synthesis of 3a and 3b (Chart 2) in order to verify the structures of M3 and M13 and to further prove the preferred site of oxidative attack.

    Retrosynthetic Analysis

    Key to the synthetic access of (trans)-X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthalene isomers 2a–d was the preparation of the respective isomeric (trans)-X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthalene-1-carboxylic acids 9a–d (Scheme 2), modified with an appropriate protecting group (PG) for the vicinal (trans)-diol. Epoxides 10a–d should be obtained in multiple steps starting from α-tetralone (11a), 5-bromo-1-tetralone (11b), and 8-bromo-1-tetralone (11c), respectively. Diol protection was expected to be important for the successful final amide bond forming step between ethyl 2-(8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetate (12) and building block 9a–d. In any case, pure 13a–d was provided in reasonable yields and without any hydroxy-naphthoyl contaminants (Scheme 3).

    Synthesis of (trans)-1,2-Dihydroxy-1,2-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic Acid (2a)

    As depicted in Scheme 4, the synthesis of building block 9a started with the conversion of α-tetralone 11a to vinyl bromide 14 using bromotriphenoxyphosphonium bromide (TPPBr2).(26) Lithiation of 14 with 1.3 equiv of n-BuLi at −78 °C followed by addition of solid CO2 (dry ice) as the electrophile gave 3,4-dihydronaphthalene-1-carboxylic acid (15) in 68% yield.(27) Esterification of acid 15 was performed under acidic condition to provide the corresponding ethyl ester 16 in 79% yield. Epoxide 10a was obtained by m-CPBA epoxidation of olefin 16 in 99% yield. A highly regio- and stereoselective epoxide ring-opening process was reported by Harvey and Tang.(28) Under acid-catalyzed hydrolytic conditions, using catalytic amounts of 10-camphorsulfonic acid (CSA) in aqueous acetone at room temperature, (trans)-diol was formed with inverted stereochemistry at the benzylic carbon atom of the epoxide ring. However, no epoxide hydrolysis was observed for 10a under those conditions until the reaction temperature was raised to 60 °C for 48 h. Hence, two epimers of 1,2-dihydro-1,2-diol 17 were formed in 70% yield, and a mixture of (cis)-/(trans)-diol in a 18:82 ratio was identified by LC-MS, inseparable in a preparative scale. The presence of the two epimers indicated that the presumed diastereoselective hydrolysis of epoxide 10a was compromised. As concluded in an earlier study by Chini et al.,(29) the tertiary benzylic oxirane carbon atom facilitates syn addition of the nucleophile (H2O) due to the stabilizing effect of the carbocationic center at C(1). This carbocationic center is additionally stabilized by the adjacent carboxylate group in the present case of epoxide 10a (Scheme 2). The neighboring group effect of the carboxylate was supposedly responsible for the increased resistance to hydrolysis of the oxirane ring. As a consequence, enhanced reaction temperature and prolonged reaction time were necessary to drive the reaction to completion. In contrast to the reported 1-methyl substituted 1,2-epoxide,(29) no rearrangement to the ethyl 2-oxo-1,2,3,4-tetrahydronaphthalene-1-carboxylate 18 was observed in the reaction mixture.
    Conditions that were successfully applied earlier to form the bis-silyl ether of related vicinal diols involved two equivalents of tert-butyldimethylsilyl chloride (TBSCl) and imidazole in DCM at room temperature for 8 h.(30) However, attempts to protect both hydroxyl groups in 17 as bis-silyl ether failed, even after heating at reflux for 24 h, presumably due to steric hindrance caused by the adjacent carboxylate group. Selective TBS protection at C(2)–OH provided 19 in 92% yield as a mixture of epimeric (cis)- and (trans)-19. These epimers could be separated by flash chromatography on silica gel. The structure was verified for each epimer by NMR studies including selective gradient 1H–1H 2D ROESY experiments. Two significant ROE correlations were observed between Hx (δ = 5.58 ppm) and H2 (δ = 4.00 ppm), H2 (δ = 4.00 ppm), and H3 (δ = 1.87 ppm). Together with the observed coupling constants JH2,H3 = 4.3 Hz and JH2,H3′ = 9.8 Hz a (trans)-1,2-diol configuration could be established for the second eluting epimer at tR = 1.08 min ((trans)-19, Figure S3, Supporting Information).
    For the first eluting epimer at tR = 1.05 min, two ROE correlations were observed between Hx (δ = 4.79 ppm) and H3′ (δ = 2.01 ppm), and H2 (δ = 4.32 ppm) and H3 (δ = 1.78 ppm), but no ROE correlation was observed between Hx (δ = 4.79 ppm) and H2 (δ = 4.32 ppm). Together with the measured coupling constant JH2,H3 = 3.5 Hz and JH2,H3′ = 11.9 Hz, a (cis)-1,2-diol configuration could be demonstrated ((cis)-19, Figure S3, Supporting Information).
    Benzylic oxidation at C(4) in (trans)-19 was achieved according to the mild method developed by Suresh et al.(30) by applying a mixture of NaClO2 and t-BuOOH in MeCN in H2O at reflux to give tetralone 20 in 43% yield. Treatment with p-TsNHNH2 furnished tosylhydrazone 21. Olefin 22 was obtained after treatment of tosylhydrazone 21 with sodium hydride in low 18% yield as a Bamford–Stevens degradation product.(31, 32) Saponification of ethyl 1,2-dihydro-naphthalene-1-carboxylate 22 gave the envisaged building block 9a in excellent yield. The final steps toward 2a are depicted in Scheme 3. Ethyl 2-(8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetate 12 and carboxylic acid 9a were coupled under peptide bond forming condition using coupling reagent HATU and diisopropylethylamine (DIPEA) in THF to provide 13a in low 20% yield. The ethyl ester in 13a was saponified with 2 N aqueous NaOH in EtOH and the TBS group was cleaved under acidic workup conditions, providing 2a in 52% yield.

    Synthesis of (trans)-X,Y-Dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic Acid (2b–d)

    The syntheses of building blocks 9b–d started with commercially available α-tetralone (11a), 5-bromo-1-tetralone (11b), and 8-bromo-1-tetralone (11c) via the key intermediate epoxides 10b–d, respectively (Scheme 5). Olefins 23a–c were obtained in two steps by means of a high yielding (94–98%) sodium borohydride reduction of the ketones 11a–c and subsequent p-toluenesulfonic acid (PTSA) catalyzed dehydration of the respective benzylic alcohols 23a–c.(30) To obtain 23d, olefin 23c was first lithiated with n-BuLi in THF at −78 °C and then reacted with CO2, added as dry ice to the reaction mixture. The intermediate carboxylic acid was isolated and subsequently esterified to the ethyl ester 23d in good 93% yield for the two steps. The desired respective oxiranes 10b–d were produced by applying m-CPBA epoxidation reaction conditions to olefins 23a–d. Crude oxirene products 10b–d were submitted without further purification to a regio- and stereoselective acid catalyzed epoxide hydrolysis in the presence of H2O and CSA. Solely (trans)-1,2-diols 24a–c were generated in 51–83% yield, as expected based on published data.(28)1H NMR spectroscopic evidence confirmed a (trans)-1,2-diol arrangement for all three cases. For example, coupling constants at δ 4.27, 4.28, and 4.48 ppm (J = 5.85, 5.7, and 5.5 Hz) for H–C(1) in 24a–c, respectively, were found to correlate well with published data for 24a.(33) The two hydroxy groups in 24a–c were protected using TBSCl and imidazole at room temperature to provide 25a–c in 59–83% yield. The benzylic methylene position at C(4) was oxidized to the respective α-tetralones 26a–c in 31–83% yield, again by means of the aforementioned mixture of oxidizing reagents NaClO2 and t-BuOOH. Subsequent treatment with either hydrazine hydrate in refluxing EtOH or p-TsNHNH2 in refluxing toluene furnished hydrazone 27a and tosylhydrazones 27b and 27c, respectively.(34, 35) Reacting 27a with CuBr2 in the presence of t-BuOLi gave vinyl bromide 28a in 73% yield.(36) Olefins 28b and 28c were obtained as the Bamford–Stevens degradation products in 69% and 49% yield after exposure of tosylhydrazones 27b and 27c, respectively, to a large excess of NaH in toluene at reflux.(31, 32) Halogen–lithium exchange in 28a and 28b with 1.3 equiv of n-BuLi at −78 °C in THF was followed by the addition of solid CO2 (dry ice) as the electrophile, affording envisaged dihydronaphthalene-1-carboxylic acid 9b in 73% and 9c as a crude product in 86% yield. Final saponification of the ethyl ester 28c afforded building block 9d albeit in a low 24% yield. The syntheses of 2b–d were completed by condensing 12 with carboxylic acids 9b–d using HATU as peptide coupling reagent in the presence of DIPEA in DMF to give ethyl esters 13b–d in 51–73% yield (Scheme 3). Saponification with aqueous 2 N NaOH in EtOH and subsequent cleavage of the two TBS groups during acidic workup provided the final products 2b–d in 17–73% yield.

    Metabolite Structure Verification

    The analytical data of metabolites M7, M9, M3, and M13 were compared with the data obtained from the synthetic standards 2a–d, enantioenriched (3R,4R)-2b and (5R,6R)-2c, and 3a and 3b.

    Human Urine

    Metabolite M7, eluting at tR = 35.9 min with m/z 437.1506 for formula C24H22FN2O5, matched the synthetic standard 2b eluting at tR = 36.0 min. On the basis of the identical LC-MS tandem mass spectra (trace M7 and 2b, Figure 3) and coelution of M7 and 2b after coinjection of equal concentrations (Figure S4a, Supporting Information), the structure of M7 was unambiguously verified as 2-(2-((trans)-3,4-dihydroxy-3,4-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5-yl)acetic acid. Likewise, M9 was demonstrated to coelute with 2c during LC-MS/MS (Figure S4b, Supporting Information), and the structure of M9 (tR = 34.3 min, m/z of 437.1506) was verified as 2-(2-((trans)-5,6-dihydroxy-5,6-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic acid by comparing the retention time (tR = 34.3 min for 2c) and fragmentation pattern of the metabolite with the synthetic standard (trace M9 and 2c, Figure 3). Although accurate mass measurement of 2a and 2d gave identical m/z of 437.1506, confirming the same elemental composition as C24H22FN2O5, neither retention time nor fragmentation pattern matched with M7 and M9 (Figure 3).
    NMR spectra were recorded under identical experimental conditions with similar sample concentrations for 2a–d, M7 and M9. A clear mismatch was observed if the 1H NMR spectra of M7 and M9 were compared with those of synthetic standard 2a and 2d. However, the identity of 2b and M7 and 2c and M9 could be confirmed by assessing their respective 1H NMR spectra (Figure 4).

    Microsomes, Hepatocytes, Bactosomes 2C9

    The structures of the two major metabolites formed after incubating [14C]1 with human liver microsomes and human hepatocytes were verified by LC-MS with the synthetic standards 2b and 2c. Positive-ion electrospray LC-MS detected [14C]M7 and [14C]M9 as protonated molecules. In a first experiment, data of [14C]M7 (tR = 33.44 min, m/z 439.1545, C2314CH22FN2O5) perfectly matched unlabeled standard 2b (tR = 33.44 min, m/z 437.1514, C24H22FN2O5), e.g., the extracted ion chromatogram of [14C]M7 was overlaid with the one of 2b produced an increase in intensity at tR = 33.44 min, whereas the intensity of the peak at tR = 31.73 min remained unchanged (green trace, Figure S5a, Supporting Information). Vice versa, a perfect match was observed in a second experiment for [14C]M9 (tR = 32.40 min, m/z 439.1545, C2314CH22FN2O5) with standard 2c (tR = 32.40 min, m/z 437.1514, C24H22FN2O5). A combined ECI mass spectrum of [14C]M9 with 2c was doubling the peak intensity at tR = 32.40 min (green trace, Figure S5b, Supporting Information). Furthermore, the fragmentation pattern of [14C]M7 and 2b, and [14C]M9 and 2c, respectively, were found to be identical and correlated with the data presented so far. Again, the structure of [14C]M7 was unequivocally assigned and verified as 14C-labeled 2-(2-((trans)-3,4-dihydroxy-3,4-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5-yl)acetic acid and of [14C]M9 as 14C-labeled 2-(2-((trans)-5,6-dihydroxy-5,6-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic acid, confirming that the main metabolic oxidation products obtained in the in vitro experiments were identical to the one in the urine extract.
    As described earlier herein, the two minor hydroxy-naphthalene metabolites [14C]M3 and [14C]M13 could be identified after incubation of [14C]1 with human bactosomes 2C8, 2C9, and 3A4. A bactosome 2C9 incubation with unlabeled 1 was used to verify the structure of M3 and M13 by LC-MS/MS (Figure 5). By comparing EIC, protonated M3 was detected eluting at tR = 12.2 min (m/z 419.1407 [M + H]+, C24H20FN2O4) and was found to exactly match with synthetic standard 3b. Both M3 and 3b were fragmenting with the same pattern (Figure S6a, Supporting Information). Thus, the structure of M3 was verified as 2-(8-fluoro-2-(5-hydroxy-1-naphthoyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5-yl)acetic acid. Likewise, protonated M13 was detected at tR = 12.6 min (m/z 419.1407 [M + H]+, C24H20FN2O4) and was assigned to standard 3a, again based on identical retention time (Figure 5) and fragmentation pattern (Figure S6b, Supporting Information), confirming the structure of M13 as 2-(8-fluoro-2-(4-hydroxy-1-naphthoyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5-yl)acetic acid. The apparent extra peak in the EIC at tR = 12.4 min (m/z 419.1407 [M + H]+, C24H20FN2O4) was characterized as an additional X-hydroxynaphthoyl isomer and was found to correspond with M2 in the hepatocyte radiochromatogram (Figure 2a).

    Enantiomeric Composition of the Metabolites M7 and M9

    To gain insight into the stereochemical course of the metabolic conversion of the naphthoyl ring in 1 producing M7 and M9 and hence to verify the respective enantiomeric composition of the products, samples of both metabolites, M7 and M9, isolated from urine were separated by HPLC on a chiral stationary phase. The spectra were compared with the respective enantioenriched and enantiopure synthetic standards 2b and 2c (Figure 6). Metabolite M7 was separating into two peaks. The first eluting enantiomer (ent1-M7) at tR = 6.28 min (16%) and the second eluting ent2-M7 at tR = 8.19 min (84%) revealed that M7 is composed of a mixture of ent1-M7/ent2-M7 with 16:84 er or with 68% ee in favor of ent2-M7. A comparable analysis showed that M9 separated into two peaks as well. Minor ent1-M9 (28%) was eluting at tR = 9.92 min prior to ent2-M9 (72%) at tR = 14.38 min, accounting a 28:72 er for ent1-M9/ent2-M9 or 44% ee in favor of ent2-M9. A comparison with the synthetic standards disclosed that minor ent1-M7 and major ent2-M7 were identical to (−)-(3R,4R)-2b and (+)-(3S,4S)-2b, respectively. Similarly, minor ent1-M9 and major ent2-M9 were matching with (−)-(5R,6R)-2c and (+)-(5S5)-2c, respectively (Figure 6), concluding that (+)-(3S,4S)-M7 and (+)-(5S,5S)-M9 were formed with 68% and 44% ee, respectively.
    The stereochemical outcome of the metabolically formed (trans)-dihydro-diol from naphthalene and other PAHs during incubation with rat liver microsomes, reconstituted monoxygenase system containing rat cytochrome P450c (CYP1A1), and a reconstituted system containing P450c and epoxide hydrolase was studied and discussed in the past.(14, 15) It was shown that the (+)-(1R,2S)-enantiomer of naphthalene-1,2-oxide was predominantly formed with 73%, and its enzymatic hydrolysis to the (trans)-dihydro-diol by EH occurred predominantly via attack at the allylic position, whereas (−)-(1S,2R)-naphthalene-1,2-oxide was shown to be hydrated at both the allylic and benzylic carbon, resulting in a 40:60 er for (−)-(1R,2R)-/(+)-(1S,2S)-1,2-dihyroxy-1,2-dihydro-naphthalene. In contrast, the oxidation of naphthalene with CYP450b (CYP2B1) enzyme produced predominantly the (−)-(1S,2R)-naphthalene-1,2-oxide with 74%.(41) The data presented for 1 were considered in accordance with the earlier findings discussed herein, namely the observed 68% and 44% ee of the respective (+)-(3S,4S)-M7 and (+)-(5S,5S)-M9 enantiomers in the human urine sample results from an overall metabolic hydroxylation process of 1, with so far unknown individual enantioselective contributions of each of the involved CYP2C8, 2C9, and 3A4 enzymes to the epoxidation step. Moreover, the epoxide hydrolase catalyzed ring opening is additionally impacting the stereochemical outcome of the (trans)-diol formation depending on the chirality of the naphthalene oxide.(41)

    In summary, the oxidative metabolism of clinical candidate 2-(2-(1-naphthoyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic acid (1, Setipiprant/ACT-129968), a CRTh2 antagonist, is presented. Four main metabolites, M3, M7, M9, and M13, were identified during incubation of 1 with human hepatocytes, microsomes, and bactosomes expressing the human CYP2C8, 2C9, and 3A4. M7 and M9 were isolated from human urine of healthy male volunteers participating in a phase I clinical study. The metabolites were analyzed, and four (trans)-X,Y-dihydroxy-X,Y-dihydronaphthoyl regioisomers (2a–d) were proposed for M7 and M9 and X-hydroxynaphthoyl regioisomer (3a and 3b) were suggested for M3 and M13. Moreover, the syntheses of the potential metabolites as racemates and in enantioenriched form were disclosed. The comparison of the analytical data (LC-MS/MS, 1H NMR spectra) with those of the synthetic standards allowed to unambiguously assign and verify the structures for M7 and M9. M7 was found to correlate with 2b and was verified as 2-(2-((trans)-3,4-dihydroxy-3,4-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5-yl)acetic acid, whereas M9 was identical with 2c and hence was verified as 2-(2-((trans)-5,6-dihydroxy-5,6-dihydronaphthalene-1-carbonyl)-8-fluoro-3,4-dihydro-1H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5(2H)-yl)acetic acid, respectively. Renally excreted M7 and M9 were demonstrated to be composed of two enantiomers with an er of 16:84 for ent1-/ent2-M7, and 28:72 for ent1-/ent2-M9. Data of an LC/MS separation on a chiral stationary phase were compared with the data of the synthetic enantioenriched and enantiopure standards, allowing assignment and verification of ent1-M7 as (−)-(3R,4R)-M7, ent2-M7 as (+)-(3S,4S)-M7, ent1-M9 as (−)-(5R,6R)-M9, and ent2-M9 as (+)-(5S,6S)-M9, corresponding with respective enantiomers of 2b and 2c. The observed 68% and 44% ee in favor of the particular (+)-(3S,4S)-M7 and (+)-(5S,6S)-M9 enantiomers is the result of an overall metabolic oxidation of 1 and can be attributed most likely to the apparent involvement of the three CYP enzymes 2C8, 2C9, and 3A4, converting the substrate with variable stereospecificity in the epoxidation step. Moreover, depending on the chirality of the naphthalene oxide, the epoxide hydrolase catalyzed ring opening is impacting the stereochemical outcome of the (trans)-diol formation as well. As for M3 and M13, the analytical data were matching the synthetic standards 3b and 3a, respectively. The structure of M3 was unambiguously proved to be 2-(8-fluoro-2-(5-hydroxy-1-naphthoyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5-yl)acetic acid. Likewise, the structure of M13 was confirmed as 2-(8-fluoro-2-(4-hydroxy-1-naphthoyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indol-5-yl)acetic acid.
    The metabolic oxidation pathway of 1 leading to verified structures of the four phase I metabolites M3, M7, M9, and M13 is summarized in Scheme 8. In a first step, CYP enzymes 2C8, 2C9, and 3A4 epoxidize the naphthoyl ring in 1 at the C(3)–C(4) and the C(5)–C(6) bond, respectively, to afford the two regioisomeric epoxides 32a and 32b. Hydration of epoxide 32a by the human epoxide hydrolase (hEH) produces M7, whereas M9 is derived from epoxide 32b. Also, a minor part of produced epoxide 32a can be converted in a nonenzymatic decomposition via a hydride shift (NIH shift) to the 4-hydroxy-naphthoyl metabolite M13, whereas 5-hydroxy-naphthoyl metabolite M3 originates from 32b.
