    Discovery of Potent 17β-Hydroxywithanolides for Castration-Resistant Prostate Cancer by High-Throughput Screening of a Natural Products Library for Androgen-Induced Gene Expression Inhibitors

    Abstract

    Prostate cancer (PC) is the second most prevalent cancer among men in Western societies, and those who develop metastatic castration-resistant PC (CRPC) invariably succumb to the disease. The need for effective treatments for CRPC is a pressing concern, especially due to limited durable responses with currently employed therapies. Here, we demonstrate the successful application of a high-throughput gene-expression profiling assay directly targeting genes of the androgen receptor pathway to screen a natural products library leading to the identification of 17β-hydroxywithanolides 1–5, of which physachenolide D (5) exhibited potent and selective in vitro activity against two PC cell lines, LNCaP and PC-3. Epoxidation of 5 afforded physachenolide C (6) with higher potency and stability. Structure–activity relationships for withanolides as potential anti-PC agents are presented together with in vivo efficacy studies on compound 6, suggesting that 17β-hydroxywithanolides are promising candidates for further development as CRPC therapeutics.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Due to earlier detection and increased life expectancy, prostate cancer (PC) is rapidly becoming the most frequent solid tumor in older men and the second leading cancer killer of all men in North America.(1) PC is a hormone-sensitive cancer that is influenced by androgens such as testosterone produced from cholesterol.(2) Dihydrotestosterone (DHT), the main metabolite of the male hormone testosterone, is the principal ligand for the androgen receptor (AR), which plays a central role in PC development and progression.(3) Androgen deprivation is still the standard therapy used to treat the metastatic disease,(4) but patients invariably relapse with more aggressive androgen-insensitive or castration-resistant PC (CRPC). Available treatments for CRPC employ radiopharmaceutical, monoclonal antibody, immunotherapeutic, and chemotherapeutic approaches.(5) Current chemotherapeutic agents for CRPC include abiraterone, cabazitaxel, enzalutamide, and ixabepilone (Supporting Information, Figure S1). Despite the success of recently approved therapies targeting AR signaling, such as abiraterone(6) and second-generation anti-androgens including enzalutamide,(7) durable responses appear to be limited, presumably due to acquired resistance. Significantly, AR is expressed at high levels in most cases of CRPC, with the AR gene being amplified in about one-third of the cases.(8) Moreover, CRPC resumes expression of multiple AR regulated genes (such as prostate-specific antigen (PSA)), indicating that AR transcriptional activity becomes reactivated at this stage of the disease.(9) Thus, a relevant issue for PC drug discovery is to target both androgen-dependent and androgen-independent cells in order to avoid recurrent phenomena.
    Natural products (NPs) represent a vast structural diversity not matched by any other sources of small molecules, and they remain the best source and inspiration for the discovery and development of new drugs.(10) This is exemplified by three of the currently employed drugs for CRPC, abiraterone, cabazitaxel, and ixabepilone, all of which are semisynthetic derivatives of NPs (Figure S1). In our continuing search for NP-based anticancer agents,(11) we have used a high-throughput screening (HTS) approach employing the ArrayPlate gene expression profiling (gene signature) assay(12) that directly targets several genes of the AR pathway to evaluate a library of NP extracts and fractions derived from Sonoran desert plants and their associated microorganisms. Our goal was to identify small-molecule NPs capable of reversing the AR signature to its inactive state, thus providing a novel therapeutic entry point for PC. Of the 18 000 NP samples tested, the most active was an extract derived from the plant Physalis crassifolia Benth (family: Solanaceae), which blocked androgen-induced expression of the genes KLK2, KLK3 (gene encoding for PSA), and SPDEF. Bioactivity-guided fractionation of this extract led to the identification of five withanolides, all bearing a 17β-hydroxy substituent. Four of these 17β-hydroxywithanolides were new, and their structures were determined as 15α-acetoxyphysachenolide D (1),(13) 15α-acetoxy-28-hydroxyphysachenolide D (2), 18-acetoxy-17-epi-withanolide K (3), and 15α,18-diacetoxy-17-epi-withanolide K (4). The fifth was identified as physachenolide D (5), which has previously been encountered in Physalis chenopodifolia.(14)
    Withanolides constitute a class of steroidal lactones structurally based on an ergostane skeleton and are abundant in plants of the family Solanaceae.(15) Plants of this family belonging to genera Acnistus, Physalis, and Withania have been extensively investigated for their constituent withanolides, in large part because they are employed in some traditional systems of medicine practiced in Asia and South America.(15) Of over 600 natural withanolides known to date, only about 40 contain an α-oriented side chain at C-17 which is not a part of a cyclic system, and all these bear a β-hydroxy group at this carbon.(16) Among all withanolides, the most extensively studied is withaferin A (WA, 8), with a β-oriented side chain at C-17, that has been evaluated for its anticancer activity in vitro(17) and in vivo against human brain,(11) breast,(18) pancreatic,(19) and prostate(20) cancer xenografts in mice. Although some 17β-hydroxywithanolides have been reported to have in vitro cytotoxic activity against breast(21) and prostate(22) cancers, and inhibition of TNF-α-induced NF-κB,(23) none of them have been evaluated for their in vivo activity. Herein we disclose the discovery of potent and selective in vitro activity of 17β-hydroxywithanolides against the PC cell lines, LNCaP (androgen-dependent) and PC-3 (androgen-independent), and in vivo pharmacokinetic (PK) and efficacy studies of the most promising analogue 6 in mice implanted with xenografts of these PC cell lines. Our results demonstrate for the first time that 17β-hydroxywithanolides are efficient inhibitors of androgen-induced genes, KLK2, KLK3, and SPDEF, which sheds light on a possible mechanism of their selective and potent anti-PC activity and suggests that the use of gene expression inhibition assays provides an efficient approach for NPs-based anticancer drug discovery.

    High-Throughput Screening of Natural Products Library

    Our goal was to identify small-molecule NP inhibitors that interfere with the DNA binding of ERG, an ETS family of genes known to regulate cell proliferation and differentiation, angiogenesis, inflammation, and apoptosis.(24) We started by screening an 18 000 member library consisting of 4500 extracts of Sonoran desert plants and associated microorganisms and 13 500 fractions derived from these extracts by employing a high-throughput ArrayPlate assay(12) to search for small-molecule NPs that affected the expression pattern of a subset of genes that are known to be involved in PC. The assay we developed allowed us to quantitatively analyze the mRNA levels for genes of the AR gene signature along with control genes for each test sample. During validation of the assay, 30 relevant genes were tested, and 13 of these genes that showed optimal signals for their expression in LNCaP cells with or without stimulation by DHT were selected for use in our screening array (see Supporting Information, Additional Experimental Methods and Figure S2). Included were the genes KLK2, KLK3 (the gene encoding for the PSA), FKBP5, SPDEF, HSPA5, NDRG1, TPD52, STK39, SGK, NSMAF, and SIM2 up-regulated by DHT, and the genes MYRIP and MIDI1 down-regulated by DHT. It is noteworthy that, during assay validation, only those samples that are specific for these 13 genes and have no effect on housekeeping control genes were detected as hits, suggesting the suitability of this assay for the purpose of identifying samples capable of inhibiting only target genes. Of the 18 000 samples tested, an extract derived from the plant P. crassifolia showed significant inhibition of expression of the genes KLK2, KLK3, and SPDEF up-regulated by DHT (with IC50 = 4.11, 3.90, and 3.80 μM, respectively). These three genes were used for evaluating compounds during screening because of their good screening Z scores (see Additional Experimental Methods). Therefore, this extract was selected for further investigation.

    Isolation and Identification of 17β-Hydroxywithanolides 1–5 with Gene Expression Inhibitory Activity

    The most promising extract derived from the aerial parts of P. crassifolia was subjected to gene expression assay-guided fractionation, affording 15α-acetoxyphysachenolide D (1),(13) 15α-acetoxy-28-hydroxyphysachenolide D (2), 18-acetoxy-17-epi-withanolide K (3), 15α,18-diacetoxy-17-epi-withanolide K (4), and physachenolide D (5)(14) (Figure 1). Although the 17β-hydroxywithanolide, physachenolide D (5), has previously been encountered in a related plant, P. chenopodifolia, and its structure determined by NMR, MS, and X-ray crystallographic data,(14) thus far no reports have appeared on its biological activity. The identity of 5 was confirmed by comparison of its 1H and 13C NMR data with those reported for physachenolide D.(14) The 1H and 13C NMR spectra of 1–4 (Supporting Information, Tables S1 and S2, respectively), assigned with the help of the HMQC and heteronuclear multi-bond correlation (HMBC) data, suggested that these are 17β-hydroxywithanolides structurally related to 5. The molecular formula of 1 (C32H42O10) and its NMR data indicated that the only difference between 1 and 5 is that one of the carbocyclic ring CH2 groups of 5 has undergone acetoxylation, leading to a CH–OAc moiety [δH 5.16 (1H, dd, J = 8.8, 8.8 Hz) and 2.12 (3H, s); δC 171.1 and 21.3] present in 1. The HMBC data of 1 (Figure S12) suggested that this OAc group was attached to C-15, and the ROESY data (Figure S14) helped to determine its orientation as α. Thus, the structure of 1 was established as 15α-acetoxyphysachenolide D(13) [(20S,22R)-15α,18-diacetoxy-14α,17β,20-trihydroxy-1-oxowitha-2,5,24-trienolide] (Figure 1). The molecular formula of 2 (C32H42O11) together with its 1H and 13C NMR data (Tables S1 and S2) suggested that it is a mono-oxygenated analogue of 1 in which one of the allylic CH3 groups has undergone oxidation to a CH2OH. The position of this oxygenated methylene was determined to be C-28 on the basis of strong cross-peaks observed between CH2-28 and C-23 in its HMBC spectrum (Figure S18). The ROESY data for 2 (Figure S20) suggested that the stereochemistry of the OAc group at C-15 was the same as that in 1. 17β-Hydroxywithanolide 2 was thus identified as 15α-acetoxy-28-hydroxyphysachenolide D [(20S,22R)-15α,18-diacetoxy-14α,17β,20,28-tetrahydroxy-1-oxowitha-2,5,24-trienolide] (Figure 1). The HRESIMS and 13C NMR data of 3 were consistent with the molecular formula C30H40O8, indicating that 3 is an isomer of 5. The 1H and 13C NMR spectra of 3 (Tables S1 and S2), assigned with the help of HSQC and HMBC data (Figures S23 and S25, respectively), were almost identical with those of 5, except for the absence of signals due to the ring A 2(3)-en-1-one moiety in ring A of 3. Instead, the 1H NMR spectrum exhibited signals due to a tetra-substituted 3(4),5(6)-conjugated diene moiety [δ 6.01 (1H, br d, J = 10.0 Hz), 5.64 (1H, br d, J = 2.8 Hz), and 5.56 (1H, m)]. The presence of cross-peaks for H-3 (δ 5.56)/H-4 (δ 6.01), H-3/H-2 (δ 3.23, 2.70), H-4/H-2, and H-6 (δ 5.64)/H-7 (δ 1.97, 2.33) in its 1H–1H COSY spectrum (Figure S24) further supported the presence of this diene moiety in 3. Based on foregoing evidence, the structure of 3 was determined as 18-acetoxy-17-epi-withanolide K [(20S,22R)-18-acetoxy-14α,17β,20-trihydroxy-1-oxowitha-3,5,24-trienolide] (Figure 1). The HRESIMS and 13C NMR data of 4 gave the same molecular formula (C32H42O10) as for 1. The 1H and 13C NMR spectra of 4 (Tables S1 and S2) suggested the presence of 3(4),5(6)-diene moiety [δH 6.00 (1H, br d, J = 10.0 Hz), 5.59 (1H, m), and 5.56 (1H, m)] as in 3, but had additional signals at [δH 4.86 (1H, br m) and 2.13 (3H, s); δC 171.1 (C), 75.7 and 21.2 (CH3)], suggesting that 4 is an acetoxylated analogue of 3. The HMBC data of 4 (Figure S30) suggested that this OAc group was attached to C-15 as in 1, and the ROESY data (Figure S31) helped to determine its orientation as α. Thus, the structure of 4 was established as 15α,18-diacetoxy-17-epi-withanolide K [(20S,22R)-15α,18-diacetoxy-14α,17β,20-trihydroxy-1-oxowitha-3,5,24-trienolide] (Figure 1).

    In Vitro Activity

    As an initial screen to identify promising lead compounds, the 17β-hydroxywithanolides 1, 3, 4, and 5 encountered in this work were evaluated for the inhibition of expression of three of the androgen-induced genes, KLK2, KLK3, and SPDEF, in DHT-activated LNCaP cells using our ArrayPlate assay. As depicted in Table 1, all compounds tested were found to be active, and among these physachenolide D (5) exhibited the strongest activity, with IC50 = 0.58, 0.75, and 0.77 μM for KLK2, KLK3, and SPDEF, respectively. The antiproliferative activities of 1–5 were then determined in two PC cell lines, one that can be activated by androgen (LNCaP) and the other that cannot be activated by androgen (PC-3). In order to determine selectivity, also included in this panel were three sentinel human cancer cell lines, MCF-7 (estrogen receptor positive breast adenocarcinoma), NCI-H460 (non-small-cell lung), and SF-268 (human CNS glioma), and normal HFF (human foreskin fibroblast) cells. Cells were incubated for 72 h with compounds, and relative viable cell number was measured by a standard dye reduction assay.(25) Doxorubicin and DMSO were used as positive and negative controls, respectively, and WA (8) was included for comparison purposes. To determine the concentrations that could inhibit cell viability and/or proliferation by 50% (IC50 value), cells were treated with increasing amounts of compounds ranging over 3–4 logs of concentrations. The results (Table 2) suggested that, among the compounds tested, physachenolide D (5), which showed the strongest inhibition of expression of androgen-induced genes (mean IC50 = 0.70 μM), exhibited the most potent and selective antiproliferative activity against PC cell lines LNCaP and PC-3, with IC50 = 70 and 130 nM, respectively.

    Preliminary Structure–Activity Relationships

    Several structure–activity relationship (SAR) studies of WA (8) and its analogues have suggested that, in addition to the ring A 2(3)-en-1-one moiety, the presence of the 5β,6β-epoxide moiety in ring B enhanced their cytotoxic activity.(26) Therefore, it was of interest to see if epoxidation of the 5(6)-double bond of the most active 17β-hydroxywithanolide, physachenolide D (5), had any effect on its gene-expression inhibitory and cytotoxic activities. Epoxidation of 5 with m-CPBA afforded its 5β,6β-epoxide (6) and 5α,6α-epoxide (7). Interestingly, 6 was found to be identical with physachenolide C occurring in the same plant, P. chenopodifolia, in which 5 was encountered,(14) but there are no reports on its biological activity. Thus, 6 and 7 were evaluated for their antiproliferative activity. As expected, β-epoxidation of 5 improved its activity against the two PC cell lines while maintaining selectivity, whereas α-epoxidation caused significant loss of cytotoxic activity. These data for antiproliferative activity presented in Table 2 also suggested the existence of some SARs for withanolides 1–8. Comparison of the data for WA (8) bearing a 17β side chain with data for 1–7 containing a 17α side chain and a 17β-hydroxyl group suggested that the orientation of the side chain and/or the presence of the OH group at C-17 may be partly responsible for their significant selectivity to the PC cell lines, especially LNCaP, compared to other cancer cell lines. In addition, the IC50 data of physachenolide C (6) and its 18-deacetoxy analogue, withanolide E (9),(27) suggested that the presence of the OAc group at C-18 and/or the absence of the OH group at C-4 in 6 caused ca. 15-fold enhancement in cytotoxic activity for LNCaP cell line (personal communication, Dr. Thomas Sayers, Molecular Immunotherapy Section, Cancer and Inflammation Program, Frederick National Laboratory for Cancer Research, National Cancer Institute, Frederick, MD 21702). All 17β-hydroxywithanolides evaluated in this study also contained hydroxy groups at C-14 and C-20. As 17β-hydroxywithanolides lacking these hydroxy groups are not known or rare, the contribution of these groups to the selective activity of 17β-hydroxywithanolides to PC cell lines could not be evaluated and remains to be determined.

    In Vivo Activity of Physachenolide D (5)

    Among the natural androgen-induced gene expression inhibitory 17β-hydroxywithanolides encountered in this study, the selective PC cell proliferation inhibitory activity combined with its ready availability made physachenolide D (5) the best candidate for initial in vivo studies. However, considering the steroidal nature of the molecule, it was essential to show that these biological activities associated with 5 were not due to its possible AR agonist activity. This was confirmed by subjecting it to Invitrogen PolarScreen assay.(28) In this assay 5 had no effect on androgen binding to the AR, while DHT inhibited binding with high affinity (IC50 = 13.1 nM) (see Figure S3). In order to determine the maximum tolerated dose (MTD), 5 was administered to mice at doses ranging from 1.3 to 50.0 mg/kg, and their body weights were measured every 3–5 days starting 5 days before the treatment and up to 18 days. As shown in Figure 2A, animals were able to tolerate a maximum dose of 50 mg/kg of 5 with no apparent toxicity. In a preliminary in vivo study, we next examined therapeutic efficacy of 5 in PC-3 xenograft model of CRPC. In order to determine the optimum dose and dosing schedule, a minimal number of male SCID mice (4–5 weeks old) were used. Cells were inoculated subcutaneously at 1 × 107 cells/mouse in sterile saline. After 6 days mice were pair-matched based on tumor size into four groups of three mice each and treated by intraperitoneal administration of solutions of 5 in DMSO at doses of 15 and 50 mg/kg at 5 day intervals (Figure 2B, blue boxes). The preliminary data indicated that 5 was capable of reducing tumor mass after 30 day treatment by ca. 66% at a dose of 50 mg/kg. However, the third dosing schedule was abandoned after 2 days because of the inability to measure the concentration of 5 in serum and this was suspected to be due to the reactivity of the ring A enone toward Michael-type addition of nucleophiles present in the serum and/or its instability possibly as a result of oxidation. On prolonged storage, 5 was found to undergo double bond rearrangement with concomitant oxidation leading to 6α-hydroperoxy-4(5)-dehydro-5(6)-dihydrophysachenolide D (5a) and 6β-hydroperoxy-4(5)-dehydro-5(6)-dihydrophysachenolide D (5b) (see Additional Experimental Methods).

    In Vivo Studies of Semisynthetic Physachenolide C (6) in Mice

    Prior to in vivo efficacy evaluation, the stable analogue 6 prepared by the epoxidation of 5 was subjected to PK studies and the determination of the MTD. PK studies were conducted using a solution of 6 in ethanol/polyethylene glycol (EtOH/PEG) and as a dispersion in lecithin. Semisynthetic 6 was administered as a single dose (50 mg/kg) in both vehicles to eight mice by both intraperitoneal (IP) and gastric gavage (GG) routes and monitored by time-dependent plasma levels (Figure S4). The mice were bled alternately in groups of four so that blood was drawn from each mouse only four times. Initial plasma uptake of 6 was much greater with IP administration in EtOH/PEG. Compound 6 was not found in the plasma after GG. Since 6 was cleared between 3 and 8 h, the EtOH/PEG IP bolus dosing was repeated using earlier time points to accurately measure the peak plasma concentration which was determined to be at 15 min (Figure S4). Trappsol, a vehicle that has been recommended for administering hydrophobic compounds and has been approved by the U.S. FDA for injection into humans(29) was next tested. Subcutaneous (SC) administration was used since this would be a potential method of administration for human therapy. The outcome was encouraging as it showed a good serum level of 6 after 1 h (see Figure S5). Consequently, MTD and efficacy studies on 6 were performed using the SC route and Trappsol as the vehicle.
    For the MTD determination, 12 mice were dosed with 6 in 20% Trappsol SC in four different groups: 25 mg/kg administered once per day (25 mg/kg/day), 25 mg/kg administered twice daily (50 mg/kg/day), 50 mg/kg administered twice daily (100 mg/kg/day), and 50 mg/kg administered four times daily (200 mg/kg/day) (see Figure S6). In all dosing schedules the same concentration of 6 in Trappsol was maintained. Efficacy studies of 6 were performed as a single agent and in combination with Taxotere using two human PC xenograft models: one model mimicking an androgen-dependent disease using LNCaP cells and the other an androgen-independent model using PC-3 cells. Cells were inoculated subcutaneously at 1 × 107 cells/mouse in 50% RPMI/50% Matrigel. After tumors reached a mean size of 100–150 mm3, mice were pair-matched into cohorts of 10 animals each and drug regimens begun. Taxotere (12 mg/kg IV) was used in combination with physachenolide C (6), as the former is the standard treatment for CRPC(30) and would very likely be used in combination therapy for any new target-based therapeutic for CRPC. The mice were treated as described above with drugs five times per week for two weeks, and the mice were sacrificed when mean tumor volume was 2000 mm3. The mice were observed daily for distress and weighed, and tumors were measured twice per week with calipers.
    The results from the LNCaP experiment showed that 6 was able to reduce tumor growth in a dose-dependent manner in the mice that did not die from toxicity (Figure 3A). However, there was no increased reduction in tumor growth from treatments with 6 together with Taxotere. The results from the PC-3 xenograft experiment showed that 6 was able to reduce mean tumor growth in a dose-dependent manner in mice that did not die from toxicity (Figure 3B). There was no difference between animals receiving Taxotere or 6 and Taxotere. Although no synergistic effect with Taxotere was observed in this experiment, compound 6 was shown to be active in reducing tumor growth, and it is possible that other methods of administration or dosage-timing and/or administration of compound 6 following the treatment with Taxotere may improve the outcome.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Natural products (NPs) have a long history of safety and efficacy as anticancer agents.(31) High-throughput screening of a NP-derived library consisting of 18 000 extracts and fractions using a gene-expression assay targeting several genes of the androgen receptor (AR) pathway led to the discovery of 17β-hydroxywithanolides as a new class of anti-androgen small molecules capable of inhibiting the expression of PSA and having nanomolar potency and selectivity to androgen-sensitive LNCaP and androgen-insensitive PC-3 cell lines. Preliminary structure–activity relationships identified physachenolide C (6) as a lead 17β-hydroxywithanolide that exhibits promising in vivo efficacy in mice implanted with LNCaP and PC-3 xenografts.
    As highlighted earlier, prostate tumors initially regress in response to androgen-ablation therapy, but most cancers eventually relapse with an androgen-depletion-independent phenotype that is often more aggressive than the original androgen-dependent tumor. Importantly, most relapsed tumors still rely upon AR activity for proliferation and survival. It is known that the cellular Fas-associated death domain (FADD)-like inhibitory protein (cFLIP), a key regulator of tumor necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL)-promoted apoptosis involving caspase-8, is important in modulating the response of PC cells to AR-targeted therapy and that androgenic protection from TRAIL-induced apoptosis predominantly occurred via enhanced transcription of cFLIP in PC cells.(32) It has recently been reported that the 17β-hydroxywithanolide, withanolide E (9, Figure 1), was capable of sensitizing TRAIL and enhancing the death-receptor-mediated apoptotic signaling by a rapid decline in the level of cFLIP proteins.(27) Interestingly, physachenolide C (6) was also shown to have the ability to sensitize TRAIL-induced apoptosis of renal carcinoma cells.(33) Thus, it is possible that our lead 17β-hydroxywithanolide, physachenolide C (6), exhibiting 15-fold higher cytotoxicity to LNCaP cells compared to 9 (see above), is capable of inducing death-receptor-mediated apoptosis by declining cFLIP proteins, in addition to having selective toxicity to PC cells. Further investigation of this class of withanolides to clearly define their mechanisms of potent and selective anti-PC activity is in progress and will be reported in due course.

    Androgen-Induced Gene Expression Inhibitory Assay

    Prior to evaluating the above NP samples for the inhibition of androgen-induced gene expression, the ArrayPlate assay used was validated as described in Additional Experimental Methods. LNCaP cells for the ArrayPlate assay were obtained from the ATCC collection and were grown in RPMI medium with 10% fetal calf serum. After trypsinization and centrifugation to remove the medium with fetal calf serum, the cells were re-suspended in RPMI medium at a concentration of 2 × 105 cells per m of RPMI medium with 10% charcoal-filtered serum. Cells (20 000) were plated on PDL-coated 384-well plates and were starved for 3 days. Extracts stored in 384-well plates (columns 1–22) (352 extracts per plate) were used for transfer to the test plate. A 384 plastic pin tool (V&P Scientific. Inc.) was used to transfer 220 nL of extracts to 100 μL of medium in the 384 well plates. A MicroFlo dispenser (Biotek, Inc.) was used to add DHT to a final concentration of 100 nM in columns 1–23. Column 24 held unstimulated controls. The cells were incubated for 18 h. Following incubation, medium was removed from the wells using a 24-well wand, 15 μL of lysis buffer (HTG, Inc., Tucson, AZ) containing 30 pM protection fragments (50mers) was added to the wells. Next, 30 μL of oil (HTG, Inc.) was added on top of the lysis buffer, and the plates were heated at 95 °C for 10 min and incubated at 70 °C for 6 h. Nonhybridized DNA and RNA were removed by addition (10 μL/well) of S1 nuclease (HTG, Inc.). Incubation was continued at 50 °C for 30 min. Subsequently, 20 μL of the bottom liquid was transferred from the cell culture plate to a new 384-well plate containing 5 μL of Stop Solution (HTG, Inc.), followed by heating for 15 min at 95 °C and cooling to RT. This step also serves to degrade any residual RNA. Then 5 μL/well of neutralization solution was added and aliquots (35 μL) of the aqueous phases were transferred to the wells of the ArrayPlates for quantification of RNA levels. In ArrayPlates used 17 anchor sequences were spotted in each well of a 384 well microplates and stored at room temperature until needed. All reagents were supplied by HTG, Inc. and plates were washed using a SQUIRT microplate washer (Brooks Automation, Inc.) with ArrayPlate wash solution (HTG, Inc.). ArrayPlates were washed and linkers (5 nM) added and incubated at 50 °C for 1 h. The plates were then washed again using the SQUIRT, and 35 μL of the aqueous phase from the cell culture plate was added to the ArrayPlate with linkers attached and 40 μL of oil layered on top. The ArrayPlates were incubated at 50 °C overnight, washed with ArrayPlate wash buffer. Detection linkers (5 nM) were added and the plates incubated at 50 °C for 1 h. The plates were then washed, and HRP-detection probe (10 nM) was added. Following incubation at 37 °C for 30 min, the plates were washed, and HRP chemiluminescent peroxidase substrate (Atto-PS, Lumigen, Southfield, MI) was added. The luminescence was captured following transfer of the plates into an OMIX imager (HTG, Inc.). Typical imaging times were 6 min within 30 min of substrate addition. The results were analyzed by software sold by NuvoGen Research, Inc. Gene signatures and housekeeping genes were designated, and the level of each gene was normalized to housekeeping genes measured simultaneously within each well. This was carried out by calculating a normalization factor for each sample equal to 1000 divided by the intensity of the housekeeping gene measured for that sample. This normalization factor was then multiplied times the intensity of every gene in the array from that well. In the case two or more housekeeping genes were used for normalization, the normalization factor was equal to 1000 divided by the average intensity of the housekeeping genes. Standard deviation was calculated from replicate sample measurements using the STDEV formula from Excel. Control genes for normalization were ubiquitin and actin. A negative control gene, GST, from Arabidopsis (plant gene not found in mammalian cells) served as a S1 control. If S1 worked appropriately, there would not be a signal at spot 17 at which the oligos for GST were specific.

    Cytotoxicity Assay

    Relative cell growth and survival were measured in 96-well microplate format using the fluorescent detection of resazurin (alamarBlue) dye reduction as an end point.(25) Compounds were tested against human androgen-sensitive prostate adenocarcinoma (LNCaP), androgen-insensitive human prostate adenocarcinoma (PC-3), human breast cancer (MCF-7), human non-small-cell lung cancer (NCI-H460), human CNS cancer (SF-268), and human foreskin fibroblast (HFF) cells. Serial dilutions of test compounds or vehicle control (DMSO) were added to triplicate wells. After 72 h incubation, dye solution was added to each well (1:10 dilution). After brief agitation, incubation was continued for 4 h at 37 °C before data acquisition using a microplate fluorometer (Ex/Em = 560/590). Mean fluorescence intensity per well as a measure of relative viable cell number in compound-treated wells was compared to that of DMSO-treated wells. The conventional chemotherapeutic drug doxorubicin served as a positive control.

    In Vivo Studies in Mice

    All experimentation involving mice was performed in accordance with the protocol (No. 07-029) approved by the University of Arizona Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC). For preliminary efficacy study of physachenolide D (5) using PC-3 xenograft, male SCID mice (4–5 weeks of age) were used. Cells were inoculated subcutaneously at 1 × 107 cells/mouse in 50% RPMI/50% Matrigel. Six days after inoculation, mice were pair-matched based on tumor size into four groups of three mice each, and drug treatments (blue boxes) were begun on day 7. Compound 5 was dissolved in DMSO and given IP once per day for 5 days, followed by 2 days of rest, and repeated. The length (L) and width (W) of the tumor mass were measured, and the tumor volume (TV) was calculated as TV = W2(L/2). Tumor growth inhibition (TGI) was calculated using the following formula: TGI = [1 – (T – T0)/(C – C0)] × 100, where T and T0 are the mean tumor volumes on a specific experimental day and on the first day of treatment, respectively, for the experimental groups, and likewise where C and C0 are the mean tumor volumes for the control group. Evaluation of physachenolide C (6) as a single agent and in combination with Taxotere was done for two human PC xenografts models, one mimicking an androgen-dependent disease using LNCaP cells and the other an androgen-independent model using PC-3 cells. For both studies, 72 male SCID mice (4–5 weeks of age) per xenograft model were used. The method of administration of 6 was determined from results of the PK studies (Figures S3 and S4). The MTD was determined for physachenolide C (6) given to the mice with the same regimen as to be used in the efficacy experiments. The highest dose completely tolerated was 25 mg/kg given once per day for 5 days, followed by 2 days of rest, and then repeated (Figure S5). Cells were inoculated subcutaneously at 1 × 107 cells/mouse in 50% RPMI/50% Matrigel. After tumors reached a mean size of 100–150 mm3, mice were split into cohorts of 8 or 10 animals each, and drug regimens were initiated. For the LNCaP experiment, six groups of 10 mice per group were pair matched (by tumor size) and each group was treated IP with (i) vehicle (Trappsol) alone; (ii) 15 mg/kg of 6; (iii) 25 mg/kg of 6; (iv) Taxotere alone (12 mg/kg IV, given once); (v) 15 mg/kg of 6 and Taxotere (12 mg/kg); and (vi) 25 mg/kg of 6 and Taxotere (12 mg/kg). The mice were treated as described above with drugs five times per week for 2 weeks, observed daily for distress and the mice weighed and tumors measured twice per week with calipers and the mice were sacrificed when average tumor size was 2000 mm3. The tumor volume was calculated as described above for the experiment with 5. The same regimen described above for LNCaP xenograft model was used for the experiments involving PC-3 xenograft model. Compound 6 in Trappsol was administered SC b.i.d. for 5 days, the mice were rested for 2 days, and then a second series of injections was done. Survival of mice in the LNCaP xenograft experiment showed that deaths due to drug toxicity or unidentified causes occurred for only three conditions: (i) vehicle only (one death); (ii) 25 mg/kg of 6 (three deaths of the eight mice); and (iii) 25 mg/kg of 6 and Taxotere (all deaths), whereas deaths in PC-3 xenograft experiment occurred due to drug toxicity or unidentified causes only for the highest dose of 6 (40 mg/kg) (five deaths of the eight mice). All other deaths occurred due to euthanization because of tumor size (Figures S6 and S7).
