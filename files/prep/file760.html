    Abstract

    The protein FKBP52 is a steroid hormone receptor coactivator likely involved in neurodegenerative disease. A series of small, water-soluble, bioinspired, pseudopeptidic fluorescent ligands for the FK1 domain of this protein are described. The design is such that engulfing of the ligand in the pocket of this domain is accompanied by hydrogen-bonding of the dansyl chromophore which functions as both an integral part of the ligand and a fluorescent reporter. Binding is concomitant with a significant wavelength shift and an enhancement of the ligand fluorescence signal. Excitation of FK1 domain native tryptophan residues in the presence of bound ligand results in Förster resonance energy transfer. Variation of key ligand residues within the short sequence was undertaken, and the interaction of the resulting library with the protein was measured by techniques including isothermal calorimetry analysis, fluorescence, and FRET quenching, and a range of Kd values were determined. Cocrystallization of a protein ligand complex at 2.30 Å resolution provided detailed information at the atomic scale, while also providing insight into native substrate binding.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    FKBP52 is a 52 kDa protein belonging to the family of FK506-binding proteins (FKBPs). It is a large, multifunctional, multidomain immunophilin originally discovered as part of a steroid receptor complex.(1) It is most well-known for its involvement in steroid receptor signaling.(2) More recently, light has been shed upon its neurological role. New studies focus on its interaction with both the protein microtubule associated protein (MAP) Tau and microtubules and thus its part in Alzheimer’s disease and other tauopathies.(3−5) These studies describe its ability to bind to and destabilize microtubules as well as its activity on the fibrillation of a key fragment of Tau, the main component of the intracellular neurofibrillary tangles which are a hallmark of Alzheimer’s disease.(6) Complementary investigations have shown that FKBP52, normally abundant in the brain, was found to be not only dramatically reduced in Alzheimer’s patients but also exhibited an “anti-Tau” activity in opposing its accumulation in neurons.(7) In this context, fluorescent reporter ligands for this protein may shed further light on its neurological function as well as providing useful tools for intraneuronal imaging and in vitro competition assays for FKBP52 and related FKBP proteins.(8)
    Structurally, FKBP52 is composed of three distinct domains (Figure 1a). The first domain (FK1) is a well-characterized FK506-binding domain that harbors a prolyl cis/trans isomerase (PPIase) activity and whose presence is necessary for the potentiation of glucocorticoid receptor transactivation.(9,10) This domain binds strongly to macrolide immunosuppressors such as FK506 (tacrolimus) and rapamycin, the consequence of which knocks out its PPIase activity.(11) We recently showed that while FKBP52 does isomerize selected bonds in Tau mutants or Tau fragments, and that this activity resides solely on the FK1 domain, its capacity to oligomerize Tau forms is not linked to its PPIase activity in vitro, at least in the absence of a suitable chaperone.(12,13) A second “FKBP-like” domain (FK2) shows structural and sequence similarity to the FK1 domain but lacks a discrete pocket. This FK2 domain also harbors a putative consensus ATP/GTP-binding sequence.(14) The third domain encompasses three tetratricopeptide repeat (TPR) motifs and is responsible for binding to HSP90 via the acidic C-terminal “charge-clamp” EEVD region of the HSP90.(15,16) A fourth putative “post-TPR” domain is posited to contain a calmodulin-binding site, and although a TPR-based calmodulin-binding domain has previously been proposed, subsequent literature has tended to focus on the former as the potential binding domain.(17) The crystal structure of FKBP52 in its entirety has not yet been reported; however, a composite of two fragments with the superimposition of a common domain (FK2) has been generated, allowing for the simulation of a whole protein structure.(16,18) In this structural context, several different X-ray crystal images have been described around the FK1-FK2 “hinge” region, suggesting that this part of the protein has a large degree of flexibility.(19) Similar flexibility has also been observed for the structurally related but functionally opposing FKBP51 protein which has 70% sequence similarity with FKBP52.(20)
    We recently carried out a small bioinspired study on peptide-based ligands for the FK1 domain of FKBP52.(21) These ligands were derived from a conserved β-turn found in certain steroid hormone receptors, including the human estrogen receptor α. Our study showed the binding effects resulting from the selective chemical modification of a small series of these receptor-derived peptides. While the Kd values remained weak, this study also supports the notion of the FK1 domain/active site pocket as a PPIase-independent platform of recognition between nuclear receptors and FKBP52, and thus as a potential target for protein–protein inhibition.(20) A second structural study investigated the effects on the folding of these peptides into tight turns (type II and/or type VI), and a preference for certain turns by the FK1 domain based on their similarity to high affinity ligands.(22) The aim of the current study was to generate a small library of water-soluble, fluorescent reporter molecules capable of binding the FKBP52 FK1 domain. These compounds had substantial peptide character to emulate natural substrates.
    Our approach entailed the design of ligands displaying significant fluorescence change (wavelength and/or intensity) upon binding to the protein. These ligands also underwent Förster resonance energy transfer (FRET) because of the presence of two tryptophan residues (Figure 1b), one partially conserved at the base of the PPIase binding pocket (Trp90) and an unconserved Trp (Trp60) on the opposite side of the domain on the 50–60 β-sheet, and which has been shown to be responsible for over 95% of the Trp fluorescence of this domain.(23)

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Initial design of the ligand family included a fluorescent “reporter”, which would be an integral part of the molecule. We chose as the reporter component, a 5-Dimethylamino-Naphthalene-1-Sulfonyl (Dansyl) moiety, which was incorporated at the N-terminus of the peptides using standard SPPS (Scheme 1). Sulfonyl amides may be used as isosteric replacements for the dicarbonyl moiety found in macrolide immunomodulators such as rapamycin and other high affinity FKBP ligands, such as ElteN378 (Figure 2).(24,25) Replacement of the pipecolic acid (Pip) motif (Figure 2) with substituted prolines and other secondary amino acids was undertaken, as this part of the ligand is known to be involved in hydrophobic contacts within the pocket of FKBP domains as well as H-bonding of the carbonyl/sulfonyl moieties either side of this unit with the Ile backbone amide proton (Ile87) and the tyrosine hydroxyl proton (Tyr113).
    Expansion of this moiety to a polycyclic structure can also precisely orient the carbonyls/sulfonyls for “ideal” binding as well as allowing the addition of pendant atoms to enhance binding.(26)
    Substituents on the ring allowed us to easily explore conformational space and to enhance hydrophobic or steric contacts by the choice of hydrophobic, hydrophilic, or encumbered groups. In their determination of a minimal motif for FKBP12, the prototypic member of the FKBP family, Martina et al. have shown a preference for a trans, cis-Pip configuration which orientates the carbonyl oxygens on either side of the pipecolic acid in an “optimal” position for binding, by separating them by ∼4.5 Å.(27) While the affinities of earlier generation ligands for FKBP12 has tended to be greater than for the larger family members (FKBP51/FKBP52), the key interactions of the pipecolate clamp remains the same, as evidenced by the increasing number of FKBP/FK506 complexes in the PDB.
    The first ligand synthesized as a proof of concept was the pseudopeptide 1 (Scheme 1 and Figure 2). This molecule reproduces a simplified rapamycin-binding domain with a carbon skeleton that includes the pipecolic core; a sulfonamide, chosen to be isosteric with the dicarbonyl motif of high affinity ligands; and a homophenylalanine, chosen to mimic the methoxy/hydroxyl substituted cyclohexyl ring (Figure 2). The addition of an arginine as the C-terminal amino acid was primarily to enhance the water solubility of the ligands. The carbon skeleton of this ligand is also similar to the ligand Elten378, which was proposed as the minimal binding motif for FKBP12 (Figure 2). To our knowledge, there are currently no affinity constants available for this ligand with the larger FKBP FK1 domains.
    We first tested the fluorescence of compound 1 in PBS (Figure 3). Excitation at 331 nm was accompanied by a broad fluorescence emission peak with a λmax of around 560 nm and a Stokes shift of approximately 230 nm (red curve). Excitation of the FK1 domain alone under the same conditions gave undetectable fluorescence (orange curve). The addition of approximately 0.67 equiv of FK1 to compound 1 resulted in a 30 nm blue shift of the resultant fluorescence peak (green curve), indicative of the engulfing of the ligand in a hydrophobic environment. Strikingly, a roughly 4-fold enhancement in fluorescence was also observed. As expected, the addition of three equivalents of FK506 completely reversed the process, displacing 1, and returning fluorescence to its original wavelength emission and voltage (purple curve). The FK2 domain of FKBP52 shows little or no PPIase activity, but given the structural (or at least the backbone secondary structure) similarity between the FK1 and FK2 domains, we tested the interaction between 1 and the FK2 domain as a control. No interaction was observed by fluorescence (blue curve).
    Next, titration of compound 1 by FK1 (Figure 4a) was carried out to determine a Kd value. Fitting of the titration curve using the fluorescence increase gave a Kd of ∼4.6 μM. Much to our satisfaction, isothermal calorimetric (ITC) analysis of the same compound, this time titrating 1 against the FK1 domain, gave a similar Kd value (1.6 μM), with a stoichiometry of 1:1 (Figure 4b). We thus turned our attention to FRET between the ligand and the protein. Tryptophan and the dansyl chromophore make an excellent FRET pair with a Förster radius of around 22 Å.(28) There are two tryptophan residues present in the FK1 domain of FBKP52: (i) the partially conserved Trp90, which lines the base of the pocket of the active site and interacts hydrophobically with the pipecolic acid of FK506; and (ii) the poorly conserved Trp60, which is further away on the 50–60 β-sheet.(23) We had thus anticipated a FRET from one or both of the tryptophan residues upon excitation, with subsequent emission at the wavelength of the dansyl group.
    Excitation of the FK1 domain alone at 295 nm was accompanied by fluorescence with a λmax at around 342 nm (Figure 5a). Addition of one equivalent of 1 resulted in significant quenching (∼67%) of the Trp emission with the appearance of a new emission peak around 537 nm ,which corresponds to the blue-shifted ligand emission when bound by the protein (vs ∼560 nm unbound).
    To further illustrate the usefulness of 1, we carried out a more comprehensive competition assay against FK506. Equimolar concentrations (20 μM) of 1 and FK1 were incubated and titrated with increasing concentrations of FK506 (Figure 6a). Given the high affinity of FK506 for the FK1 domain of FKBP52, one equivalent of FK506 was sufficient to displace 1.(8)
    Having validated and quantified the interaction using multiple techniques, we generated two mini-libraries of ligands of the form Dansyl-Xaa-Yaa-Arg-OH. The first round of synthetic ligands held the Xaa Pipecolic acid (Pip) moiety constant, generating a Dansyl-Pip-Yaa-Arg-OH series (Table 1), where only the Yaa position was varied. Yaa was a hydrophobic, aromatic, or cyclic amino acid and included both natural and non-natural amino acids such as phenylalanine (Phe), homophenylalanine (hPhe), pipecolic acid (Pip), aminocyclopentanoic acid (Acpa), 2-pyridylalanine (2-pyrAla) 3-pyridylalanine (3-pyrAla), 4-pyridylalanine (4-pyrAla), 2S-1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline-3-carboxylic acid (Tic), Tryptophan (Trp), and tert-leucine (tLeu). The second series of ligands (Table 2) held the Yaa homophenylalanine residue (hPhe) constant, and the Xaa residue was modified. The Pip residue was varied with a selection of secondary amino-acids including 2S, 4S-fluoroproline (flp), 2S, 4R-fluoroproline (Flp), 2S,4R-hydroxyproline (Hyp), n-Butyl-glycine (nBu-Gly), d-proline (d-Pro), l-proline (Pro), Tic, and 5,5-dimethylproline (dmP).

    ITC titrations of the ligands showed dissociation constants ranging from approximately 1 μM to greater than 50 μM. In cases of low solubility (<100 μM), fluorescence was used to determine the presence of binding. In the first series of ligands, variation of the Yaa position did not seem to have a significant influence on the Kd for many of the compounds tested, as ligands containing hPhe (1), Phe (2), Pip (3), 2-pyridylalanine (7), 3-pyridylalanine (8), and tLeu (10) in the Yaa position all showed very similar affinities in the low-single-digit micromolar range. Trp (6), and acpa (4) showed slightly reduced affinities, perhaps for steric reasons. The Tic-containing ligand (5), which is in effect an aromatic more encumbered version of the Pip-Pip ligand 3, did not bind all again likely due to steric reasons. The 4-pyridylalanine pyrAla (9) peptide (>50 μM) seemed to be largely disfavored relative to the 2- and 3-substituted (3.5 and 3 μM, respectively) analogues, and we propose that this may be due to electrostatic repulsion, perhaps within the ligand itself (with the dimethylamino moiety of the dansyl group), from displacement of the nitrogen in the aromatic ring. In the second series of ligands where the Xaa position was varied, we show that a natural 2S conformation at the proline/pipecolic acid position is a necessary criterion for interaction (compounds 12 vs. 13). The Hyp analogue 11 did not bind, and this may be due to the size of the substituent at the 4 position or the polarity of the hydroxyl group. The second Tic-containing peptide 16 did not bind at all, most likely due to both the relatively small size of the pocket in which the pipecolic acid is buried and the position of the aromatic substituent and position (vide infra). The 2S, 4R-Flp ligand 18 bound with 10-fold less affinity than its 2S, 4S-flp analogue 15 though whether due to steric/puckering/conformational effects is unclear. Ligand 14, where the cyclic Pro/Pip motif was replaced by a noncyclic analogue (N-butylglycine), did not bind implying the importance of the rigidity imposed by the ring system. An arginine-deleted version of compound 1 (compound 19, see SI) showed reduced affinity (10 μM as determined by fluorescence) compared to compound 1 and also reduced solubility, which highlights the importance of the presence of the arginine backbone and consequently the number of residues involved in the interaction. In the case of 5,5-dimethylproline (dmP), which imposes a cis conformation, no binding was observed. The absence of binding could be due to steric hindrance of the dimethyl group at the 5- position within the small pocket.(29) A control molecule (Dansyl-l-Proline 20, see SI) showed no binding by fluorescence.

    Crystallization

    To decipher the interactions involved in the formation of the complex and in an attempt to explain the different observed affinities of these peptidomimetic ligands, cocrystallization experiments of the FK1 domain with 1 were undertaken. Binding of the pipecolic amide “core” of 1 was in line with that anticipated (Figure 7a,b). The sulfonamide and carboxamide oxygens, which flank the piperidine motif of the pipecolic acid, are engaged in hydrogen-bonds with the protein in a manner similar to that observed in the crystal structure of FK1/FK506 (PDB: 4LAX) (Figure 7, panel c vs panel d). This includes a short hydrogen bond (2.85 Å) between the pipecolic acid carboxamide oxygen and the backbone amide of Ile87. One of the sulfonyl oxygens forms a hydrogen-bond to Tyr57 (3.32 Å). The phenolic proton of Tyr113 also forms a hydrogen-bond with both the amide nitrogen (3.14 Å) and the carboxamide oxygen (2.79 Å) of the ligand homophenylalanine residue. Thus, the pseudopeptidic ligand is stabilized by multiple “native” backbone type electrostatic contacts at the rim of the shallow pocket, (in fact every amide/sulfonamide bond is engaged in at least one hydrogen-bond) while the hydrophobic pipecolic acid ring is engulfed within the hydrophobic pocket comprised of residues Tyr57, Trp60, Phe67, Phe77, Phe79, Val86, Ile87, Leu128, and Val130. Immersion of the pipecolic ring within the pocket results in the orientation of both the amide and sulfonamide bonds adjacent to or between pairs of hydrogen bond donor/acceptors and effectively wrapping the ligand backbone around the hydroxyl group of Tyr113. Thus, the sulfonamide is directly between the Tyr113 and Tyr57 hydroxyl protons, the Pip-hPhe amide bond is between Tyr113 and the Ile87 amide proton, and the hPhe-Arg amide bond is between the Tyr113 hydroxyl proton and the Glu85 carbonyl oxygen. In fact, the Tyr113 and Glu85 comprise two sides of a channel through which the hPhe-Arg bond is “threaded” and that sits atop the hydrophobic pocket. While the distance between the second non-hydrogen bonded sulfonamide oxygen and the Tyr113 hydroxyl group is relatively long (3.77 Å), it may well participate in binding in solution. The sulfonamide may in many respects be considered as a simultaneous mimic of both the cis and trans conformations of a peptide amide bond. The presence of a tyrosine in proximity to both the “cis” and “trans” mimicking sulfonyls could imply a role for stabilization of both of these states in a catalytic context. Both the aromatic dansyl and phenyl groups are projected out of the pocket. This may help explain the relative independence of the Kds (below a certain threshold) with ligands varied at the hPhe position.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We have thus generated a series of fluorescent reporter ligands for the FK1 domain of FKBP52 with a range of Kd values suitable for in vitro competition assays and FKBP binding studies. We have also solved the crystal structure of the domain with ligand 1. To our knowledge, this is the first reported crystal structure of the FKBP52-FK1 domain with a sulfonamide type compound. Our library gives superior binding information to simple Trp quenching assays and other labeled ligands due to simultaneous observation of binding of both the protein and ligand by fluorescence. While not all members have tryptophan residues (e.g., FKBP12.6), our ligands should be applicable to the majority of FKBPs. We make no presumptions toward any specificity/selectivity within the FKBP family of proteins other than the differences in Kd typically observed for other ligands such as FK506 when interacting with smaller (e.g., FKBP12) or larger (e.g., FKBP51/FKBP52) members of the family. The peptidic nature of our ligands donates multiple points of anchorage and mimicking native protein ligand character, while the sulfonamide reproduces the dicarbonyl binding of immunomacrolide molecules and provides insight into potential substrate binding while stabilization. The relatively high solubility (>1 mM for most) of these ligands makes them interesting for water-based assays and techniques that are less amenable to the presence of organic solvents. Finally, the intrinsic fluorescence of these ligands may indeed make them suitable for use in cellular assays, the study of their localization in cellulo, and their potential application as theranostics.(30)

    Synthesis

    Compounds were synthesized using standard SPPS (Scheme 1).
    Peptide Synthesis: Manual Fmoc solid phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) was carried out in a fritted syringe, typically on a 0.1 mmol scale, using preloaded Fmoc-Arg(Pbf)-O-2-chlorotrityl chloride resin (0.62 mmol g–1). Coupling was carried out by shaking of the requisite Fmoc protected amino acids (10 equiv), HBTU (9.5 equiv), and DIPEA (20 equiv) for 1 h. For precious amino acids, extended couplings (2–4 h) were carried out twice using two equivalents of amino acid and HATU. Fmoc deprotection was carried out by using 20% piperidine in DMF (1 × 1 min, followed by 1 × 10 min.). After each coupling and deprotection, the peptides were washed five times with DMF. The completed resin-bound pseudopeptides were washed successively in DMF (×3), DCM (×3) and MeOH (×3), and then dried in vacuo. Cleavage of pseudopeptides from the resin with concomitant deprotection was achieved using the cocktail TFA – iPr3SiH (TIPS) – H2O (95:2.5:2.5), approximately 10 mL g–1 of peptide resin, for 2 h. The suspension was filtered, and the filtrate was evaporated to dryness in vacuo. The residual material was triturated with ice-cold ether, centrifuged, and the supernatant discarded (x 3) to yield an off-white/yellow solid. Purification: The crude peptide product was dissolved in water:acetonitrile:TFA (90:10:0.1) and lyophilized before purification and analysis by semipreparative and analytic RP-HPLC, respectively. RP-HPLC was performed using a Waters setup, composed of a Waters 1525 binary pump system with a Waters 2487 dual wavelength absorbance detector and UV detection at 220 and 331 nm. Solvent A: 0.1% TFA in water and solvent B: 0.1% TFA in CH3CN. Analytical RP-HPLC was performed using a Higgins Analytical RP proto 200 C18 5 μM column (4.6 × 100 mm), at a flow rate of 1.0 mL min–1 with a typical gradient of 5% to 60% B over 10 min. Semipreparative RP-HPLC was performed using an Xbridge C18 5 μM (19 × 50 mm) column with a flow rate of 10 mL min–1 using the appropriate gradient. All compounds were >95% pure by analytical HPLC analysis. MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry analyses were performed in the ion positive reflector mode on an ABI Voyager DE-Pro MALDI-TOF mass spectrometer (Applied Biosystems) using as matrix a saturated solution of α-cyano-4-hydroxy-cinnamic acid (CHCA) in CH3CN:H2O:CF3COOH (50:50:0.1). 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra on selected compounds were recorded at room temperature with tetramethylsilane (TMS) as an internal standard on a Bruker Avance III 300 MHz, a Bruker Avance III 400 MHz, or a Bruker Avance III with cryoprobe 500 MHz spectrometers.

    Isothermal Titration Calorimetry

    ITC was performed on a Microcal ITC200 (Malvern) (calorimetry platform, PIM/I2BC). Titration of approximately 30–40 μM FKBP52-FK1 protein, while stirring at 300 rpm, was carried out by 20 injections of 2 μL of ligand at 300–600 μM in the same buffer as the protein (PBS). Experimental data were fitted to the theoretical titration curves using the Origin software (OriginLab, Northampton, MA) according to the relationship between the heat generated by each injection. The following values were calculated: ΔHcal, enthalpy change in kcal·mol–1; Ka, association-binding constant in M–1; n, number of binding sites.
