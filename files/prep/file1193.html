    Abstract

    A series of 3-acylindole-1-benzylcarboxylic acids were designed and synthesized while searching for a PPARγ modulator with additional moderate intrinsic PPARα agonistic activity. 2-[3-[[3-(4-Chlorobenzoyl)-2-methyl-6-(trifluoromethoxy)-1H-indol-1-yl]methyl]phenoxy]-(2R)-butanoic acid (12d) was identified as such an agent which demonstrated potent efficacy in lowering both glucose and lipids in multiple animal models with significantly attenuated side effects such as fluid retention and heart weight gain associated with PPARγ full agonists. The moderate PPARα activity of 12d not only contributed to the agent’s ability to manage lipid profiles but also appears to have potentiated its PPARγ efficacy in lowering glucose levels in preclinical diabetic animal models.

    Diabetes is a metabolic disorder afflicting about 5% of the population in the industrialized nations and is now rapidly expanding in the rest of the world, especially in those heavily populated developing countries such as China and India. Its growth rate is estimated to be 6% annually, reaching a potential total of 200−300 million cases in 2010.(1) Non-insulin dependent type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DMa)

The underlying causes for T2DM are complex. Besides genetic and other pathological factors, the onset of T2DM has been clearly linked to lifestyle changes such as diet, exercise, and age and is increasingly accompanied by other metabolic disorders including obesity, dyslipidemia, and atherosclerosis. The combination of these risk factors, termed metabolic syndrome, significantly increases morbidity and mortality and is a major public health issue in the U.S. Over the years, several therapeutic agents for treating these disorders individually have been developed with varied success. There remains an urgent need for more comprehensive approaches for managing such complex diseases. For a diabetic patient with severe dyslipidemia, the increased incidence of atherosclerotic cardiovascular attack(2) may pose more of a threat than hyperglycemia.(3) Clinical intervention in such patients will have to include glycemic control as well as cardiovascular risk management. A single agent for treating both conditions simultaneously will not only fill an unmet medical need but also open up a new frontier in fighting metabolic syndrome.
    Peroxisome proliferator activated receptor (PPAR) belongs to a subfamily of ligand-activated nuclear hormone receptors(4) that are involved in regulating nutrient storage and catabolism.(5) There are three PPAR subtypes, commonly designated as PPARα, PPARγ, and PPARδ(β).(6) Agonists of the α subtype have been shown to improve lipid profiles by lowering triglycerides (TG) and low density lipoprotein (LDL) and are therefore used to treat dyslipidemia.(7) The fibrates, which are weak PPARα agonists, have been used in the clinic for more than 20 years with a good safety profile.(8) The γ subtype has been studied extensively for its role in regulating glucose metabolism and insulin sensitivity.(9) PPARγ full agonists, such as rosiglitazone and pioglitazone, have been developed and marketed for the treatment of T2DM.(10) PPARα/γ dual agonists combining the features of both glucose and lipid lowering have been identified as potential therapeutic agents for diabetic hyperglycemia and dyslipidemia.(11) However, despite extensive efforts from industry and academia,(12) no such agents have advanced to the clinic. Mechanism-based side effects including weight gain, edema, and congestive heart failure, as well as recently reported possible bone fracture following rosiglitazone treatment, are among the major safety hurdles associated with PPARγ full agonists.(13, 14) Lately, several reports in the literature,(15) including work from our own laboratories,(16) have demonstrated that selective PPARγ modulators (SPPARγMs) could bind to the receptor in a distinct mode relative to full agonists, providing a physical basis for different biological effects. As a result, such ligands act as partial agonists in transcriptional activity assays and induce an attenuated adipocyte gene signature in the gene chip study. In preclinical species, SPPARγMs were shown to retain antidiabetic efficacy comparable to full PPARγ agonists while displaying reduced PPARγ mechanism-based adverse effects. Over the years, several reports from our laboratories have described a variety of PPAR ligands including γ selective,(17)α selective,(18) and α/γ dual agonists.(19) Recently, we reported the discovery of a new class of selective PPARγ modulators.(20) These indolecarboxylic acids showed potent PPARγ activity in binding assays, partial agonism in cell-based transactivation assays, and good efficacy in lowering hyperglycemia in rodent models of diabetes. In contrast, all previous α/γ dual agents evaluated in preclinical and clinical settings were full PPARγ agonists that carry the aforementioned PPARγ adverse effects. Many of these dual agonists are also highly potent against PPARα, which may pose additional liabilities associated with full and potent PPARα activation.(21) Considering that fenofibrates are relatively weak PPARα agonists that possess a good efficacy and tolerability profile clinically, we sought to combine both features of a SPPARγM and a relatively weak PPARα agonist to generate a new type of PPARγ/α dual agonist with improved efficacy and safety profiles. We envisioned that such an agent would ideally be a highly potent PPARγ receptor binder with a differentiated partial activation profile for optimal glycemic control and minimal adverse effects. The PPARα activity would preferably be several orders of magnitude less potent, comparable to fenofibrates after adjusting for dosing levels needed for lipid modulation. Here, we describe the discovery process of 12d and its in vitro and in vivo biological profiles as a PPARγ modulator or partial agonist with additional PPARα activity.

    Chemistry

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In the previous report,(20a) we described the SAR of 1-benzoyl-3-benzylindole carboxylic acid analogues (1) as SPPARγMs. Although potent, this class of compounds suffered a drawback of quick debenzoylation in vivo, resulting in lowered PK and high levels of circulating 3-benzylindole metabolites. To circumvent this issue, we transposed the 1- and 3-substituents to create the 3-acyl-1-benzylindolecarboxylic acid (2), which is superimposable to the original structure (Chart 1). A series of such 3-acyl-1-benzylindolecarboxylic acids (12) were prepared following procedures as depicted in Scheme 1. Starting from properly substituted phenylhydrazines (3) and thiosalicylic acid (4), classical Fisher indole cyclization produced two isomers of the indole-3-sulfides (5 and 6) which were separated by flash column chromatography. The desired isomer 6 was subjected to reductive cleavage of the sulfide bond to afford the parent indole core 7. Friedel−Crafts acylation at C-3 of the indole 7 followed by N-alkylation gave the acylindole structural platform (8). The carboxylic acid moiety was introduced by O-alkylation after demethylation of the OMe group of 9 to expose the phenol functionality. Different substitutions on the indole, the benzoyl, and benzyl groups were explored, and the SAR is essentially identical to that of the earlier 1-benzoylindole series (1). Major chemistry efforts were then directed toward the introduction of chirality and different substitution patterns at the α-position of the carboxylic acids (12), which proved to be a very productive site for fine-tuning selectivity ratio between the γ/α receptors.
    O-Alkylations on intermediate 10 were carried out by Mitsunobu coupling with corresponding 2-hydroxylcarboxylic esters or through simple displacement reactions with 2-bromocarboxylic esters. Chirality was introduced by either starting with optically pure carboxylic esters or by resolution using chiral HPLC. Several noncommercially available chiral 2-bromocarboxylic esters were prepared starting with the corresponding chiral 2-aminocarboxylic acids via Sandmeyer’s reaction, followed by esterification. For the α,α-dialkylcarboxylic acid analogues 12i and 12j (Table 1), direct α-alkylation on the indolecarboxylic ester 11d via lithium diisopropylamide (LDA) and iodomethane resulted in methylation of the 2-methyl group on the indole core. To avoid this side reaction, 3-methylphenol (13) was used as the starting material. Attachment reaction with ethyl 2-bromobutyrate (14) followed by α-alkylation with LDA and iodomethane gave the α,α-dialkylcarboxylic ester 16. This intermediate was converted to the benzyl bromide 17 by free radical bromination with N-bromosuccinamide (NBS) and then attached to the indole 8 to give the racemic carboxylic ester, which was resolved by chiral HPLC and hydrolyzed to afford acids 12i and 12j (Scheme 2). A separate manuscript describing an improved synthesis of 12d on a 3 kg scale has been accepted for publication.(22a)

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Earlier SAR efforts established that the 3-acylindole series exhibited slightly enhanced PPARα activity relative to the 1-benzoylindole series. Extensive SAR exploration followed, with variations of substitutions on the indole ring, the acyl group, and the aromatic ring of the 1-benzyl substitution. Several hundred analogues were prepared, and many of them are potent PPARγ selective modulators which will be the subject of a separate publication.(22b) Among all the permutations we explored with this chemotype, the only modification that significantly affects the selectivity and activity ratios between α and γ receptors is at the position α to the carboxylic acid. We herein focus the discussion only on a few selected analogues varying the α substituent with the rest of the structure optimized for PPARγ activity. The 3-acylindolecarboxylic acids described herein are summarized in Table 1. Their biological activities are evaluated on two criteria: their affinity to PPARγ and α receptors (IC50)(23) and their agonist activity in a PPAR-GAL4 chimeric transactivation assay in COS-1 cells in terms of potency (EC50) and maximum activation (Max%) relative to rosiglitazone for γ agonism.(17) All compounds with α agonism have similar activation levels as WY14643, a PPARα full agonist control.(18) Compound 1 is a typical 1-benzoyl-3-benzylindolecarboxylic acid type of PPARγ selective modulator with high binding affinity to the γ receptor and potent EC50 activity in the cell based transactivation assay with 30% of maximum activation. Its PPARα receptor activity is minimal in both the binding and transactivation assays. Compound 2 is the corresponding 3-acyl-1-benzylindolecarboxylic acid equivalent. There is little change in PPARγ potency and maximum activation. But a slight increase of potency on the mouse α receptor was detected in the transactivation assay. Introduction of a methyl substitution at α-position to the carboxylic acid significantly increases the PPARα activity, while the PPARγ potency remains unaffected. Interestingly, this increase in PPARα activity can be separated within the same molecule by separating the two stereoisomers generated by the substitution. While compound 12a, the (S)-enantiomer, remains a highly potent and highly selective PPARγ modulator, compound 12b, the (R)-enantiomer, carried all the activity toward the α receptor with only a slight decrease in potency for the γ receptor. This was a consistent pattern for the (R)-enantiomers of the series. Generally, increasing the size of the substituents increases the α potency. Compound 12c, the (S)-enantiomer of the ethyl-substituted analogue, is a selective PPARγ modulater, while its (R)-enantiomer, compound 12d, has significant PPARα activity in addition to its potent PPARγ activity. The propyl-substituted analogues 12e and 12f are very similar to their ethyl-substituted counterparts with small increases of α activity on the mouse receptor. With further increases in substituent size, PPARα activity starts to manifest in the (S)-enantiomer. As a result, compound 12g, the (S)-enantiomer of the isopropyl analogue, bears weak activity toward the PPARα receptor in the transactivation assay. The corresponding (R)-enantiomer, compound 12h, becomes a potent PPARα/γ dual agonist with EC50 = 2 nM on both receptors in the same assay. The α,α-disubstituted carboxylic acids exemplified by 12i−j are generally γ weighed γ/α dual agonists which have equal distribution of selectivity in their stereoisomers. The cyclobutyl substituted analogue 12k shows balanced α activities toward both mouse and human receptors with PPARγ activities slightly weaker compared to that of other analogues. It is important to note that all the compounds in this series demonstrated a high degree of selectivity over PPARδ subtype. No activity was observed for this receptor at the highest concentration tested (50 μM) in the binding assay.

    In vivo efficacy of selected analogues was evaluated using db/db mice by daily oral administration of the test compounds for 10 days.(24) On the 11th day, blood glucose (Glu) and triglyceride (TG) levels from test animals were measured vs those from the vehicle control. All compounds were dosed at 10 mpk with rosiglitazone (10 mpk) as positive control. As shown in Table 1, a clear relationship between PPARα agonism and its efficacy in glucose and TG lowering in db/db mice is established. The highly potent PPARγ selective agonists 12a,c,e exhibited modest efficacy (22−53%) in glucose lowering and essentially no effects on TG levels. Their modest glucose-lowering efficacy relative to the selective PPARγ full agonist, rosiglitazone (−76%), is likely due to partial activation of the γ receptor and lower blood exposures. However, 12b,d,f, the enantiomers of 12a,c,e, showed robust glucose (94−105%) and TG (80−90%) lowering in the db/db study. The only apparent difference between these enantiomers was their intrinsic PPARα activity. For compounds 12g and 12h, both (S)- and (R)-enantiomers carried a certain amount of PPARα agonist activity, and therefore, both showed good in vivo efficacy regarding Glu and TG lowering. However, the (R)-enantiomer (12h) was a much more potent PPARα agonist and was much more efficacious than its (S)-enantiomer (12g). The α,α-disubstituted analogues (12i−j) had moderate PPARα activity and thus moderate efficacy in Glu and TG lowering. Further comparison of PK profiles of these (R)-enantiomers with their (S)-enantiomer counterparts (Table 1) indicated that they have similar blood exposure levels, eliminating the possibility that elevated drug levels contributed to the increased efficacy of the former ligands. The apparent contribution of PPARα to the observed hypoglycemic effect is consistent with reports of insulin sensitization by PPARα selective agonists in rodents.(25) Recently, PPARα agonists have been reported to demonstrate insulin sensitizing activity in nonrodent models of insulin resistance.(26) The additional contribution of PPARα agonism to the hypoglycemic effect of PPARγ partial agonism may provide these unique PPARα/γ dual ligands with enhanced clinical efficacy, although this hypothesis remains to be proven in human trials.
    One important criterion for selecting a candidate for further preclinical development in this project was the proper balance between PPARγ and PPARα activity within one molecule. Considering the partial PPARγ activation of this class of compounds, we targeted a candidate to be a highly potent PPARγ agonist with maximum activation in the range of 50% relative to the full agonists. Most of the compounds in Table 1 have PPARγ potency that is 10- to 20-fold greater than that of rosiglitazone but also showed significantly lower drug exposures in rat PK, about 10−20% to that of rosiglitazone. After adjustment of potency and exposure, the estimated dosing levels of these compounds in humans are expected to be similar to that of rosiglitazone, which is 2−8 mg q.d.(27) In contrast, the clinical dose of fenofibrate, which is a weak PPARα agonist with EC50 = 2−4 μM in our transactivation assay, is in the range of 100−150 mg q.d.(28) Achieving the same PPARα agonist effects as fenofibrate at a dose level of 2−8 mg would require the selected compound to have an EC50 of around 200 nM in our PPARα transactivation assay (see later discussions for further correlation). Among the four most efficacious compounds (12b,d,f,h) in our in vivo efficacy studies, the functional activity of 12h on the PPARα receptor was deemed too potent (2 nM) in our selection criteria, while the α potency of 12f was insufficient.
    It has been demonstrated that PPARα agonists can exhibit differential species-dependent transactivation profiles.(4) It is desirable to identify a compound with relatively similar PPARα potency in the various species used in preclinical efficacy and safety studies as well as in man. Thus, transactivation assays were conducted using chimeric PPARα receptors derived from a variety of appropriate species. The results indicated that compound 12b had very different potency on human vs mouse α receptors, while 12d exhibited comparable agonist activity toward mouse, hamster, dog, and human PPARα receptors with EC50 values of 0.12, 0.46, 0.55, and 0.23 μM, respectively. In the screen of a panel of cytochrome P450 (CYP) isozymes for potential inhibition, 12d gave IC50 values of 91, 24, 40, 7, and 10 μM, respectively, for 2C19, 4A4, 1A2, 2D6, and 2C9, a significant improvement from some of the indole analogues published earlier.(29) Furthermore, compound 12d demonstrated a high degree of selectivity against other members of the nuclear receptor family. In nuclear receptor counterscreens, compound 12d was negative in binding and/or activation assays of LXRα, LXRβ, RXRα, PXR, GR, ERα, ERβ, TRα, and TRβ at concentrations up to 10 μM. On the basis of this attractive profile, compound 12d was selected for further evaluation.
    Compound 12d was assessed for its ability to lower plasma levels of glucose, TG, or insulin in db/db mouse,(24) ob/ob mouse,(30) and HFD/STZ mouse models.(31) In several independent 12-day db/db studies (Table 2), 12d consistently produced dose-dependent corrections of glucose (78−101%) and TG levels (75−85%) in a dosing range of 1−10 mpk. As a positive control in these experiments, rosiglitazone at 10 mpk lowered glucose levels by an average of 76% and TG by 71%. Measurements of systemic drug exposure for 12d showed Cmax and AUC values of 1.5/5.4/9.6 μM and 1.9/6.4/9.6 μM·h, respectively, at dosages of 1/3/10 mpk. In contrast, drug levels of rosiglitazone in this assay were measured to be much higher, with Cmax of 68 μM and AUC of 317 μM·h at a dosage of 10 mpk. Thus, 12d produced substantial efficacy at drug levels that were lower than those typically observed with rosiglitazone. In the same experiment, pioglitazone at 10 mpk lowered glucose level by 53% and TG by 65% with a systemic exposure of 180 μM·h. In two independent ob/ob studies, 12d lowered insulin levels by 67% at 3 mpk and 89% at 10 mpk while rosiglitazone treatment at 1 mpk led to a corresponding insulin reduction of 50% (Table 3). In the more stringent HFD/STZ mouse model, 12d produced glucose corrections of 75% and 103% at dosages of 5 and 30 mpk while rosiglitazone lowered glucose by 36% and 45%, respectively, at the same dosing levels (Table 4). These results clearly demonstrate the superior antihyperglycemic and antidyslipidemic efficacy of compound 12d in multiple animal models vs TZDs currently available in the clinic.

    A 7-day study was conducted in obese, insulin resistant Zucker fa/fa rats,(32) a preclinical model we use to directly compare the insulin sensitizing efficacy vs the mechanism-based side effects of PPARγ ligands. The plasma volume (PV) and heart weight of test animals were measured as well as their insulin levels and lipid profiles. In this study, 12d (0.03, 0.3, 3.0, 15 mpk) was compared to rosiglitazone (1.0, 10, and 100 mpk). Both compounds substantially lowered elevated levels of free fatty acids, insulin, and triglycerides, indicating significant insulin sensitizing activity. However, efficacy was achieved with lower exposure of 12d (insulin lowering ED50 = 10 μM·h) than rosiglitazone (insulin lowering ED50 = 125 μM·h). Importantly, while rosiglitazone produced clear dose responsive increases in plasma volume and heart weight at dosages of ≥10 mpk, no significant increase in heart weight was seen with 12d; and at 15 mpk of 12d (1060 μM·h), only a slight increase in plasma volume was observed (Figure 1).
    In additional studies, the ability of 12d to improve serum lipid profiles was assessed in both hamster(33) and dog(34) efficacy models. In a 9-day study conducted in Syrian hamsters, 12d produced substantial dose-proportional decreases in total cholesterol (22−51%) and triglycerides (10−57%) at dosages of 0.3−3 mpk. In the same study, fenofibrate (100 mpk) served as a positive control and lowered cholesterol (23%) and TG (34%). Systemic exposures from an independent 9-day dosing experiment in hamsters gave Cmax and AUC values of 0.02/0.07/0.6 μM and 0.13/0.55/2.11 μM·h, respectively, at dosages of 0.3/1/3 mpk. The hamster AUC for fenofibrate at 100 mpk averaged 1800 μM·h (Table 5). Thus, by exposure-based comparisons, 12d was substantially more potent (>1000-fold) than fenofibrate in this assay and also achieved a maximal efficacy that exceeded the fenofibrate effects. In a 14-day male Beagle dog study, low dosages of 12d were associated with significant lipid lowering. The observed total cholesterol lowering in dogs was 12.8% and 26% at 1 and 3 mpk. In this same study, fenofibrate at 50 mpk lowered total cholesterol by 17%. As shown in Figure 2, the net lipid lowering by 12d at 1−3 mpk was similar, or superior, to the efficacy of 50 mpk fenofibrate. If this result is translated to human, to achieve the same clinical efficacy of 150 mg fenofibrate would require 3−9 mg of 12d, which is right in the range of expected clinical dose for 12d based on its PPARγ instigated antidiabetic effect. Compound 12d was also compared with simvastatin in the dog lipid model. Simvastatin (4 mpk) lowered cholesterol to a lesser extent than 3 mpk 12d. Coadministration of 12d (3 mpk) with simvastatin (4 mpk) produced additive effects (cholesterol lowering of 36.4%) vs simvastatin alone (18.4%). Drug exposure measurements of 12d at 1 and 3 mpk showed Cmax of 1.03 and 18.58 μM and AUC of 5.41 and 21.4 μM·h, respectively. Exposure values for fenofibrate in this assay at 50 mkd were higher (Cmax = 15 μM, AUC = 117 μM·h). Drug levels of 12d, simvastatin, and simvastatin acid were unaffected by coadministration (Figure 2).

    To evaluate the typical PPARγ and PPARα agonist-mediated side effects, 12d was compared head-to-head with both the full PPARγ agonist rosiglitazone and the PPARα ligand fenofibrate in a 2-week normal Sprague−Dawley (SD) rat tolerability study. As shown in Table 6, typical effects associated with PPARγ activation such as brown adipose tissue (BAT) proliferation(35a) and heart weight increases(35b) were observed with rosiglitazone but not or in less extent with 12d. For example, rosiglitazone increased heart weight by 25% and BAT depot mass by 260% while 12d only increased these two parameters by 1% and 32%, respectively. In contrast, the PPARα driven liver signatures(36) such as increased liver weight and albumin/globulin and bilirubin levels were observed with both fenofibrate and 12d on a similar scale. These findings are consistent with the profile of 12d as a PPARγ partial agonist with additional PPARα full agonistic activity. Overall, 12d was well tolerated in this experiment and there were no significant findings other than those described above.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, compound 12d is a potent PPARγ partial agonist possessing additional modest PPARα activity. Its glucose and insulin lowering activity was similar or superior to that of rosiglitazone in rodent models of T2DM but at significantly lower plasma levels. Potent lipid-modulating efficacy was demonstrated in hamster and dog. An improved therapeutic window was noted with 12d in insulin resistant rat studies in which plasma volume expansion and cardiac enlargement were diminished vs that caused by less efficacious doses of rosiglitazone. Unlike the marketed thiazolidinediones, 12d is a PPARγ modulator (PPARγM). This designation is based on the partial agonism observed in cell-based functional assays and mutagenesis studies(37) plus the results obtained in our in vivo efficacy studies. The additional PPARα activity of 12d not only contributed to the agent’s ability to manage lipid profiles but also appear to have potentiated its PPARγ efficacy in lowering glucose levels in db/db mice. Its relative potencies on PPARγ and PPARα receptors are proportional for projected clinical applications. These unique biological properties of compound 12d as a PPARγM with additional modest PPARα activities make it an attractive candidate for a novel therapeutic agent for the treatment of T2DM and dyeslipidemia.

    In Vitro Assays

    Activities of compounds were evaluated for both binding affinity and functional activity. Binding affinities for the PPARs were measured in a scintillation proximity assay (SPA). Selected compounds from the binding assay were then evaluated for their potencies of PPAR gene activation in cell-based transcription assays using GAL4-PPAR chimeric receptors as previously described.(20a) All results were produced in triplicate, and mean values are reported.

    In Vivo db/db Mouse Studies

    Male db/db (C57BLKS/J-m +/+Leprdb) mice at 12−13 weeks of age and nondiabetic db/+ (lean) mice from Jackson Laboratories were housed seven mice per cage and fed a rodent chow with free access to water. Mice (seven per group) received a once daily oral dosing of test compounds with vehicle (0.25% methylcellulose) by oral gavage for 11 days. Blood was collected from the tail immediately prior to the next dosing at days 0, 4, 7, and 11 for measurement of the plasma glucose levels.

    In Vivo ob/ob Mouse Studies

    Female ob/ob mice from Jackson Laboratories (∼8 weeks old; body weight ∼50 g; food P5008) were fasted for 5 h after which time a baseline blood sample for determination of glucose and insulin was collected into a heparinized capillary tube. Animals were subsequently assigned to treatment groups (n = 5/group) such that baseline glucose values were equivalent between groups. Mice were administered compounds in a volume (0.5 mL/animal) of vehicle (0.25% methylcellulose) by oral gavage for 5 consecutive days. One hour prior to the last day of dosing food was removed from all animals’ cages. Four hours after the last dose of compound the animals were euthanized via carbon dioxide inhalation and a terminal blood sample was collected by cardiac puncture. Sera prepared from the blood samples were used for the determination of glucose (using a Sigma glucose (Tinder) 100 assay kit) and insulin (using a Linco rat insulin radioimmunoassay kit).

    In Vivo HFD-STZ Mice Studies

    Male mice were fed high fat diet (HFD) for 3 weeks and then injected with a single dose (90 mg/kg) of streptozotocin (STZ) and fed HFD for an additional 3 weeks. Animals were subsequently assigned to treatment groups (n = 5/group) and were administered compounds in a volume (0.5 mL/animal) of vehicle (0.25% methylcellulose) by oral gavage for 12 consecutive days. Animals were then euthanized via carbon dioxide inhalation and a terminal blood sample was collected by cardiac puncture. Sera prepared from the blood samples were used for the determination of glucose.

    Hamster Lipid Studies

    Golden Syrian hamsters weighing between 120 and 150 g were purchased from Charles River Laboratories and used for the experiments. Hamsters were housed in boxes (five per box) and fed a normal rodent chow with free access to water. Hamsters (10 for each group) were orally dosed with compounds (suspended in 0.5% methylcellulose) for 9 days. On the morning of the 10th day, hamsters were euthanized with carbon dioxide, and blood samples were obtained via heart puncture. Serum cholesterol and triglyceride levels were determined from the samples.

    Dog Lipid Studies

    Mature male beagle dogs weighing between 12 and 18 kg were purchased from Marshall Farm, PA. They were housed individually and fed a cholesterol-free chow diet with free access to water. Prior to starting experiments, the dogs (five for each group) were bled weekly from the jugular vein and their serum cholesterol levels were determined. Test compounds were suspended in 0.5% methylcellulose and administered orally to the dogs daily for 2 weeks. Blood samples were taken during and after the dosing periods, and serum cholesterol levels were determined.

Following 7 days of acclimation, rats were treated once daily for 7 days by oral gavage with test comopunds (0.25% methylcellulose, 10 mL/kg).

    Evaluation of Insulin Sensitization

    Bioelectrical Impedance Analysis for Determination of Extracellular Fluid Volume

    Twenty-four hours following the final dose, rats were anesthetized with ketamine (85 mg/kg, im) and xylazine (10 mg/kg, im). Animals were positioned on a nonconductive surface in dorso-lateral recumbency, and extracellular fluid volume was determined by bioelectrical impedance analysis, following procedures described by the manufacturer (Hydra ECF/ICF impedance analyzer model 4, Xitron, San Diego, CA). A tetrapolar impedance monitor was used to measure impedance, and hence the total body water, over a frequency range of 5 kHz to 1 MHz. Source electrodes (1 cm × 26G stainless steel needles) were inserted 5 mm subcutaneously. Detector electrodes were inserted along the midline at the anterior point of the sternum and the anterior point of the penis. The distance between electrodes was measured and included for data modeling.

    Plasma Volume Measurement

    After determination of extracellular fluid volume, plasma volume was measured in anesthetized animals using a dye dilution technique following methods described by Belcher and Harriss(38) with minor modifications. Twenty minutes after injection, a heparinized blood sample (2 mL) was withdrawn from the descending aorta. Plasma was separated by centrifugation of the blood at 1100g for 15 min; samples were kept at −80 °C until assayed. Absorbance of the thawed plasma was read at 620 nm, and plasma Evans blue dye concentrations were calculated according to a standard curve generated by a serial dilution of the 25 mg/mL Evans blue dye−saline solution.
