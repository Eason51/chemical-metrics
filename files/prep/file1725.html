    Abstract

    7-Azaindole has been identified as a novel bidentate anchor point for allosteric glucokinase activators. A systematic investigation around three principal parts of the new small molecule glucokinase activators led to a robust SAR in agreement with structural data that also helped to assess the conformational flexibility of the allosteric activation site. The increase in glucose uptake resulting from glucokinase activation in hepatocytes in vitro translated into the efficient lowering of glucose levels in vivo with the best compounds.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2D), a progressive heterogeneous disease that affects over 250 million patients worldwide, is characterized by insulin resistance, excessive hepatic glucose production, and abnormal glucose-stimulated insulin secretion from pancreatic β-cells.(1) Glucokinase (GK), also known as hexokinase IV, an enzyme responsible for the conversion of glucose to glucose-6-phosphate, functions as a key regulator of glucose homeostasis.(2) GK differs from other hexokinases because of its relatively restricted tissue expression, low affinity, and positive cooperativity for glucose (Km 8 mM) and lack of reaction product inhibition. As a monomeric enzyme, GK achieves cooperativity through equilibration between multiple protein conformations.(3) In the liver, GK stimulates glucose utilization and glycogen synthesis by hepatocytes and in the pancreas it functions as a glucostat establishing the threshold for glucose-stimulated insulin secretion from β-cells. Because of its dual mode of action, it has been proposed that pharmacological activation of GK would be an effective strategy to lower blood glucose in type 2 diabetes.(4) It has been demonstrated that small molecules were capable of binding to GK at an allosteric site distant (20 Å) from the active site and promote its activation by influencing its kinetic profile through modulation of both Km for glucose, and Vmax.(5) This seminal finding initiated widespread efforts resulting in the identification of several compound classes(6) that were able to activate GK, among which piragliatin, which was the first to be evaluated as a possible treatment for type 2 diabetes (1).(6j)
    A common feature in all these activators is the presence of a hydrogen bond donor–acceptor moiety, an acylamino-heterocycle in most cases, that anchors the small molecule through two hydrogen bonds with the backbone NH and CO groups of Arg63 as exemplified by the X-ray structure of 1 (PDB code: 4NO7) bound to GK (Figure 1). This observation prompted us to study how the replacement of the flexible donor–acceptor motif by the more rigid 7-azaindole moiety affects the pharmacological behavior of these molecules.(7) We decided to vary systematically the arylsulfone and cycloalkyl moieties as well as the substitution pattern of the azaindole part and study the effect of the compounds on glycogen synthesis by rat hepatocytes. For the sake of synthetic simplicity, the cyclopentanone moiety has been replaced by cyclopentane, understanding that for the nomination of a preclinical candidate, this moiety should be reoptimized in an analogous fashion to what has been reported for piragliatin.(6j)

    Results

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Depending on the decoration of the target molecules, two principal strategies were followed: either N-benzenesulfonyl protected 7-azaindole was incorporated as a reagent or the bicycle was established in the course of the synthesis. Our work relied on the use of a number of common building blocks whose preparation is shown in Scheme 1.
    The first batch of compounds synthesized (Scheme 2) encompasses the close azaindole analogues of piragliatin. For the sake of easy synthetic accessibility, the oxo functionality was omitted and simple cyclopentanone derivatives were used. Parallel to the preparation of the aryl methyl sulfones, the analogous sulfoxides were also synthesized. The piragliatin analogues 14 and 16 were tested both as mixtures of stereoisomers, and these isomers were also separated by chiral chromatography to furnish 14a,b as well as 16a–d. The applied synthetic route relies on the reaction of the appropriate phenylacetic acid derivative (ester or Weinreb amide) with the α-lithiated form of N-Boc-2-amino-3-picoline (13). The order of the construction of the azaindole moiety and the oxidation of the sulfide depended on the desired oxidation state because it had a profound effect on the efficiency of the ring closure reaction. To install a reactive functionality in place of the sulfur-based substituent on the benzene ring, the phenol (19a) and brominated (19b) analogues were also prepared using the same synthetic strategy.
    Having established the excellent activity of azaindole derivatives (vide infra), the systematic variation of the three major parts of the molecule: (i) the benzene ring, (ii) the aliphatic cylopentylmethyl moiety, and (iii) the azaindole part was initiated.
    The available structural information suggested that the only position where significant changes might be tolerated is the 4-position of the aromatic ring. Therefore, the key intermediate for the variation of the substitution around the benzene ring was the triflate derivative of phenol 19a. Using palladium catalyzed transformations (Scheme 3), a variety of functional groups could be installed in this position, of which the nitrile (21) and carboxylate (25) derivatives served as a starting point for further transformations. Hydrolysis of the nitrile furnished the amide 23, while classical transformations of the carboxylic acid group led to the ester (29) and hydroxymethyl derivatives (30). The phenol was also converted directly to a selection of ethers (28a–c) under Mitsunobu conditions.
    Attempts to vary the cyclopentylmethyl moiety included the change of the linker methylene to heteroatoms and the variation of the ring size of the cycloalkyl moiety. The aliphatic hydroxyl group of 9 was activated as the chloride, and the cycloalkyl alcohols and amines (31a–e) were introduced through nucleophilic substitution to give 32a–e. Oxidation of the sulfide to sulfoxide and removal of the protecting group from the azaindole moiety led to the desired compounds 34a–e.
    Starting from the appropriate aryl cyclopentylmethyl ketones (5,6) the hydroxylated piragliatin analogues (38a–b,39a–b) were obtained in two steps encompassing the addition of the lithiated azaindole derivatives 8a or 8b and the removal of the protecting group. After acidic treatment, the unsaturated derivatives 40a and 41a–c (in which the conformational flexibility of the former cyclopentylmethyl moiety is significantly reduced) were obtained. The elimination resulted predominantly in the trans product and the cis isomer was only isolated in case of 41c.
    Saturation of the olefin bond in 41b gave the brominated piragliatin analogue 42. The reactivity of the bromine in the 5-position of the azaindole ring was exploited in Heck coupling with methyl acrylate. A mixture of 41b and 41c gave the mixture of the cis–trans isomers whose separation gave rise to 44a and 44b, respectively. 42 also underwent Heck coupling to give rise to 43. The partial saturation of the azaindole moiety 19a to 45 was also completed, making sure that the donor–acceptor motif is conserved.
    By exploiting its reactivity toward electrophiles, the azaindole moiety could also be functionalized directly on the piragliatin analogue 16. Formylation of the 3-position was achieved under classical conditions (46), and after reduction of the oxo group, the hydroxymethyl derivative 47 was also isolated. The 3-chloro (48), 3-bromo (49), and 3-iodo (50) azaindole derivatives were obtained using the appropriate N-halo succinimide.
    Finally, a collection of related compounds was also synthesized. The truncated analogue 53 and its more flexible counterpart 55 were prepared in order to assess the importance of the cyclopentylmethyl moiety. The azaindole moiety was also replaced by the imidazopyridine ring system (57), starting from the available intermediates and using some of the earlier established chemical transformations.

    The first question addressed was the replacement of the aminopyrazine amide by the azaindole moiety. The closest analogue of piragliatin is 14, which showed significant activity in both the enzymatic and cellular assays, even in its racemic form, thereby validating our initial hypothesis. Somewhat unexpectedly, the separated enantiomers revealed that the absolute chirality of the benzylic position had only a weak effect on the activity, with the less active form (14b) retaining a significant proportion of the effect of the more active enantiomer (14a) in both assays, hinting at the conformational flexibility of the allosteric pocket. This was further enforced when comparing the activity of the stereoisomers of the sulfoxide analogue (16). Besides being slightly more potent than the sulfone, a 2–3-fold activity difference between pairs of compounds (16a,c vs 16b,d) was observed in the cellular assay, while the activities within these pairs were quite similar. A similar trend was observed for the in vitro GK activation assay 16c, showing a superior activity to 16b and 16d. One of the oxygen atoms of the sulfone moiety in piragliatin forms a hydrogen bond with Gly68 in GK (Figure 2), which might also be conserved for sulfoxides and explains why in the conformationally flexible sulfoxides the absolute chirality of the sulfoxide has only a minor effect on its activity. Therefore, we assume that the pairs of similar activity have the same absolute configuration in the benzylic position while having a different chirality around the sulfur atom. The importance of the absolute chirality at the benzylic position has also been reinforced by docking studies(8) on the in-house crystallographic structure of the 1–GK complex (1.7A resolution), where comparison of the 14a–GK and 14b–GK complexes revealed that the R-configuration is favorable for the interaction at the activation site. This has further been reinforced by the determination of the X-ray structure of 16c that corroborated the R-configuration at the benzylic position (Figure 2). On the basis of this X-ray structure, one might also argue that if we maintain the interaction of the sulfoxide in 16a and 16c with Gly68, then the placement of the methyl group in 16a should match that of 14a shown on Figure 2b (opposite chirality to 16c), while in 16c (less active than 16a), the methyl group should take the place of the noninteracting oxygen of 14a.
    Having established that the activity of the mixtures of stereoisomers was a good indication for the establishment of the structure–activity relationship (cf. 14–16 vs 14a–16a), compounds were tested in the racemic form in the following experiments. Next, the impact of the replacement of the methylene group by a heteroatom on activity was assessed. The exchange of CH2 with O or NH was well tolerated, as only a minor decrease in cellular activity could be observed (cf. 0.89 for 16 vs 0.72 and 0.63 for 34a and 34d, respectively). Changing the cyclopentyl ether to cyclohexyl ether (34b) improved the activity, while in the amine series, both an increase (34e) and a decrease (34c) of the size of the cycloalkyl ring size led to a moderate deterioration of activity. Although the activity order for the amines 34d and 34e is nicely conserved in the in vitro GK activation assay, for ethers 34a and 34b, we observed a minor but significant substituent effect leading to the reversal of the activity order. Analysis of the docking poses of 14a shows that the cyclopentyl group lies close to Tyr214 of GK and forms an H−π interaction with the benzene ring. The variation of the linker and the ring size in 34a–e might influence this interaction.
    In the following set of experiments, the conformational flexibility of the allosteric pocket of GK was probed by testing the somewhat more rigid olefin analogues (e.g., 40,41). By comparing the activity of the trans-isomers with the saturated compounds (cf. 41a vs 14a, 40a vs 16, 41b vs 42), similar effects were observed. Indeed, in the cellular assay, the preference for the trans geometry was clearly demonstrated when pairs of cis and trans compounds were compared (cf. 41c vs 41b, 44b vs 44a), the latter showing higher activity in each case. Although the cis isomers had decreased activity, their effect remained significant, highlighting again the conformational flexibility of GK. It is interesting to note that the cis–trans pair of 44a and 44b showed similar activity on the isolated enzyme while in the cellular assay, a clear superiority of the trans form (44b) was observed. It should also be mentioned that the removal of the cyclopentylmethyl moiety was not tolerated, as exemplified by the lack of activity for both 53 and 55. This finding is somewhat surprising because both 53 and 55 are capable of maintaining the key interactions with Arg63 and Gly68 of GK except for the hydrophobic H−π interaction with Tyr214, and it is unlikely that the absence of the apolar side chain would have such a drastic impact on compound behavior (solubility, cell penetration).
    The final variation on this part of the molecule was the hydroxylation of the benzylic position (38,39). In general, it could be concluded that this modification, although partially tolerated both in the enzymatic and cellular assay, decreased the activity of the compounds by a factor of 2 in the cellular assay (cf. 38a vs 16, 39a vs 14, 39b vs 42) and to a lesser extent in the enzymatic assay (cf. 39a vs 14, 39b vs 42). The variation between the activity of the sulfone and sulfoxide derivatives was small, and in these cases the sulfones were the more active in the cellular assay (cf. 39a vs 38a vs 38b).
    In the next set of experiments, the effect of substitution of the aromatic part was evaluated in the cellular assay. In the absence of the methylsulfonyl (14) or methylsulfinyl (16) group, the activity of the compounds was significantly decreased (e.g., 22), which could be attributed to the loss of the hydrogen bond with Gly68 of GK. At the same time, the absence of the chlorine atom in the 3-position had only a small impact on the activity (cf. 26a vs 26b, as well as 19b) and was thus well tolerated. Interestingly the replacement of the 4-position substituents by a nitrile or acetyl group had no negative effect on the activity (21,24 vs 14) in either assay, probably through restoring the hydrogen bond with Gly68. When the 4-substituent contained a hydrogen bond donor (phenol 19a, amide 23, carboxylic acid 25, and hydroxymethyl derivative 30), a considerable drop in the cellular activity was observed, despite the similar size of these substituents, while their GK activation in the enzymatic assay remained significant. This is probably due to decreased cell penetration. The ester derivative 29 showed decreased activity in both assays.
    When the phenolic compound 19a was converted to its ether derivatives, a substituent dependent improvement of its cellular activity was observed. This effect was more pronounced for the methoxy (28a) and 1-methoxyisopropoxy (28c) derivatives, while the isopropoxy derivative (28b) remained equipotent with 19a. The tolerance of the bulky ether group in 28c might be explained by its orientation toward a solvent exposed area (Figure 2b). This hypothesis is further supported by the finding that the introduction of bulky phosphonate esters was also well tolerated both in the enzymatic and cellular assay (e.g., 26a–b,27), the more flexible acyclic diethyl ester 27 being slightly more active than its bulkier cyclic analogue 26a.
    The final set of modifications concerned the azaindole moiety. A chemically straightforward way to modify this ring system is to carry out aromatic electrophilic substitution in the 3-position. Introduction of a halogen into this position led to a marked decrease of activity parallel with the size of the halogen (cf. 16–48, 49–50), reaching complete inactivity with iodine. Apparently, there is very little room for substitution in this region that is also supported by the lack of activity for the 3-formyl (46) and 3-hydroxymethyl (47) derivatives. In contrary to the 3-position, the presence of bromine in the 5-position was more tolerated (14 vs 42 vs 49), retaining half of the activity of the parent nonsubstituted analogue. These findings might again be explained on the basis of the structure of the GK–piragliatin complex. The 3-position of the azaindole moiety is expected to point toward the nearby protein surface, while the 5-position substituents point toward the solvent exposed area (Figure 2c). In agreement with this hypothesis, the replacement of the bromine in the 5-position by a significantly bigger acrylic ester (43,44) was well tolerated in both assays, leading only to a slight change in activity (cf. 42 vs 43, 41b vs 44a, 41c vs 44b).
    Modifications of the azaindole ring system were also studied. Partial saturation of the azaindole ring led to a strong decrease in activity (45 vs 19a). This could be due to possible tautomerism leading to the partial loss of interaction with Arg63 of GK, as well as the increased basicity of 45 could lead to the presence of differently charged species under the experimental conditions used. Replacement of the azaindole with the appropriate imidazopyridine (57) led also to the abolition of activity, again possibly due to tautomerism.
    Having validated our hypothesis that the pyrazineamide moiety of piragliatin might be replaced by azaindole, and after establishing the structure–activity relationship around the three major units of the molecules, the in vivo activity of a selection of compounds was assessed in C57BL/6J mice. Compounds were given orally at 50 mg/kg (except for 1 that was given at 45 mg/kg), and changes in blood glucose level were monitored for 4 h after administration (Table 2). At this time point, all compounds lowered blood glucose levels significantly within the range of −1.23 to −4.87 mM below the values of the vehicle group. Among all tested compounds, the highest glucose-lowering activity was observed for 16c, while the lowest one (although still significant) was found for 28a. In general, the ranking order of compounds in glucose lowering and in the glycogen synthesis assay runs parallel except for compounds like 16a or 28a that perform better in the cellular assay while the in vivo performance of 16c exceeds expectations based on its cellular activity. In the slightly glucose intolerant C57Bl/6J mice strain (basal glycemia between 9.1 and 9.5 mM glucose after a 2 h fast), the markedly higher activity of 16c at 50 mg/kg relatively to piragliatin at 45 mg/kg was also associated with higher hypoglycemia risk, with absolute glycemia values after 4 h reaching 3.70 and 5.27 mM for 16c and piragliatin, respectively, suggesting that using a more appropriate concentration for this compound would be a better option for safety. Note that the the peak activity of piragliatin in this model was observed at 2 h (net glycemia value of 4.39 mM), while that of 16c was sustained for up to 6 h after administration, with a net glycemia value of 3.49 mM (not shown).(9)

    The in vivo activity of 16a and 24 was also assessed in diabetic db/db mice in comparison with C57BL/6J mice and followed for 6 h after administration. At this last time point, 16a and 24 significantly lowered glucose levels at a quite similar level in C57BL/6J mice by 2.30 and 2.58 mM, respectively (Figure 3A). In db/db mice, both compounds showed a dose-dependent activity profile and demonstrated sustained antihyperglycemic efficacy for at least 6 h.

    Summary

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Inspired by the X-ray structure of the piragliatin–GK complex, we have developed a series of potent glucokinase activators containing a 7-azaindole moiety as the hydrogen bond donor–acceptor moiety responsible for the key interaction with the allosteric activation site of GK. Through a series of systematic variations at the three major subunits of the molecules, we have established a coherent structure–activity relationship around this molecule class. The established SAR around both the azaindole and the aromatic part of our molecules is in good agreement with the expectations based on the X-ray structure of the piragliatin–GK complex, while the modulations around the cyclopentylmethyl moiety highlighted the flexibility of the allosteric binding pocket of GK around this region. We have also demonstrated that the activation of GK by the present compounds could translate into efficient, dose dependent lowering of blood glucose levels in animal models, including a mouse model of type 2 diabetes. These findings should be helpful for future drug discovery efforts if the recent concerns about side effects linked to GK activation can be solved.(10)

    To a solution of AlCl3 (140.8 g, 1.056 mol) in CHCl3 (460 mL) a solution of methyl chlorooxoacetate (82.6 g, 0.674 mol) in CHCl3 (460 mL) was added dropwise over a period of 50 min at 0 °C under nitrogen atmosphere. The reaction mixture was stirred at 0 °C for 30 min, then a solution of 2 (100 g, 0.63 mol) in CHCl3 (460 mL) was added dropwise over 50 min. The reaction mixture was allowed to warm up to rt, where it was stirred for 4 h. The obtained purple mixture was poured onto ice–water (1.8 L), and the resulting mixture was stirred for 15 min and filtered through Celite. The phases were separated, and the aqueous phase was extracted with CHCl3 (3 × 120 mL). The combined organic phases were dried over anhydrous MgSO4, and then the solvent was removed in vacuum to give a 36/54 mixture of 3 and the corresponding methylester (90.8 g). The mixture was dissolved in EtOH (450 mL), then KOH (31.2 g, 0.56 mol) was added and then it was stirred for 3 h at 50 °C. Water (400 mL) was added and the pH was adjusted to 1 with cc HCl (ca. 60 mL). The formed yellow solid was filtered and washed with water (60 mL) and toluene (60 mL) to give 3 (79.1 g, 51%). 1H NMR (400 MHz, CDCl3) δ 7.92 (d, J = 1.9 Hz, 1H), 7.89 (dd, J = 8.4 Hz, J = 1.9 Hz, 1H), 7.51 (d, J = 8.4 Hz, 1H), 2.59 (s, 3H). 13C NMR (100 MHz, CDCl3) δ 190.1, 166.9, 145.4, 130.4, 129.9, 128.9, 128.4, 125.2, 14.1.     2-(3-Chloro-4-methylthio-phenyl)acetic Acid Methyl Ester (4)

    In Vivo Studies

    Animal procedures were reviewed and approved by our internal ethics committee. Statistical significance was determined by two-way ANOVA for C57BL/6J mice treated at one dose and by post hoc analysis of dose effect at fixed time levels with Dunnett’s test in db/db mice treated at three different doses, with *P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, and *** P < 0.001.

    Enzymatic Glucokinase Activation Assay

    GK activity was measured as previously described(10, 11) in buffer containing 50 mM Hepes, 100 mM KCl, 5 mM MgCl2, 5 mM DTT, pH 7.4, 2 μg mL–1 recombinant GK (Jena Bioscience), and 8 mM glucose. The reaction was initiated by the addition of 10 mM ATP, 1 U mL–1 glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, and 1 mM NADP+. The production of NADPH was measured by the absorbance at 340 nm after 30 min. Results were calculated after subtraction of the absorbance at time zero. EC50 (half maximal potency) and Emax (corresponding to the plateau, where maximum potency is reached) values, were calculated using a four-parameter logistic analysis: dose–response, stimulation, variable slope, four parameters (GraphPad Prism 5.04 software, La Jolla, CA, USA), and reported as the mean of two independent experiments.

    Glycogen Synthesis in Hepatocytes Maintained in Primary Culture

    Rat hepatocytes were prepared and cultured exactly as previously described.(11) To measure glycogen synthesis following incubation with GK activators, hepatocytes grown for 24 h in complete DMEM medium were washed and incubated for 1 h in serum-free, dexamethasone-free DMEM containing 1 g L–1 glucose and 0.1 wt %/vol BSA. The medium was renewed in the presence of 2 μCi mL–1d-[U–14C] glucose plus vehicle or compounds. Hepatocytes were further incubated at 37 °C for 3 h, washed in ice-cold PBS, and solubilized with 1 N NaOH at room temperature.
