    Abstract

    DGAT2 plays a critical role in hepatic triglyceride production, and data suggests that inhibition of DGAT2 could prove to be beneficial in treating a number of disease states. This article documents the discovery and optimization of a selective small molecule inhibitor of DGAT2 as well as pharmacological proof of biology in a mouse model of triglyceride production.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Diacylglycerol O-acyltransferase 2 (DGAT2) is an integral membrane protein of the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) that catalyzes the final step in triglyceride (TG) synthesis.(1) DGAT2 mRNA is expressed strongly in adipocytes and hepatocytes and is well-conserved across multiple species including mice, rats, dogs, monkeys, and humans. Published studies have demonstrated a key role for DGAT2 in hepatic VLDL production.(2-4) Specifically, DGAT2 synthesizes TG molecules for delivery to the ER lumen, where they are transferred to nascent apolipoprotein B (ApoB), the main structural protein of VLDL and LDL. If there is insufficient TG in the ER lumen to adequately lipidate ApoB, then the nascent protein is degraded and VLDL will not form. Therefore, a treatment that reduces TG pools available for lipidation is expected to lower intracellular ApoB levels and inhibit VLDL production from the liver.
    Preclinical studies support this hypothesis, as antisense oligonucleotide (ASO)-mediated knockdown of DGAT2 in mouse liver and adipose tissue over a â¥4 week period reduced hepatic TG levels and production rates of both VLDL-TG and ApoB by â¼50% in obese and leptin-deficient mice.(4) Such data suggests that a small molecule inhibitor of DGAT2 could be therapeutic in treating a number of disease states related to TG synthesis (i.e., atherosclerosis, diabetic dyslipidemia, hepatic steatosis).
    With this in mind, our research team focused on the identification of small molecule inhibitors of DGAT2. Herein, we describe the discovery of compound 16, a potent and selective small molecule inhibitor of DGAT2 that effectively inhibits triglyceride production in mice.

    Results

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A focused screen of our sample collection, performed using an LC-MS-based DGAT2 enzymatic assay,(5) revealed a number of structurally diverse molecules that were proficient inhibitors of the DGAT2-catalyzed production of triacylglycerol. Further biological and chemical validation of these hit classes revealed the isoquinoline-based chemotype, typified by compound 1, as a promising hit class worth additional profiling.
    Further studies of compound 1 focused on the selectivity and ADME properties. In this regard, compound 1 showed reduced activity against other acyl transferase enzymes including glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase (GPAT1) and diacylglyceride O-acyltransferase 1 (DGAT1); however, 1 demonstrated nearly equipotent inhibition of monoacylglycerol O-acyltransferase 2 (MGAT2). The ADME profile of compound 1 revealed the need for extensive optimization as 1 exhibited high clearance and no oral bioavailability in a mouse pharmacokinetic study (Table 4). With these observations in mind, our hit-to-lead effort focused on structural modifications to the isoquinoline series with the goal of optimizing the DGAT2 activity and the MGAT2 selectivity as well as improving the physicochemical and pharmacokinetic properties to support in vivo pharmacological proof of biology studies.
    Truncation studies (Table 1) suggested that both the C-5 and C-8 substituents on the isoquinoline ring are critical for the observed DGAT2 inhibition, given the diminished activity of compounds 2 and 3. In light of this data, a diverse array of substituents were explored at both the C-5 and C-8 positions. As illustrated in Table 2, a number of substituted amides were tolerated in the C-8 position of the isoquinoline. For example, the spiro-cyclopropyl derivative 4 showed good DGAT2 inhibition with modest selectivity over MGAT2, whereas the cyclopropyl compound 5 showed comparable inhibition of both DGAT2 and MGAT2. Despite the poor MGAT2 selectivity of compound 5, the pharmacokinetic profile was encouraging (Table 4). Most notably, the oral bioavailability of 5 was significantly improved over that of 1. Conversion of the phenyl moiety to the 1,3,5-oxadiazole, as in compound 6, provided a 4-fold improvement in MGAT2 selectivity and significantly improved the physicochemical properties of the series,(6) as reflected by the ligand lipophilicity efficency (LLE) and polar surface area (PSA), while maintaining the ligand binding efficiency (LBE) (Table 4).(7)

    In Vivo Pharmacology

    To assess the effect of DGAT2 inhibition on triglyceride production in vivo, mice were treated under standard conditions with a general lipase inhibitor, poloxamer-407 (LPL IC50: 4 Î¼M), and a DGAT2 inhibitor while measuring the accumulation of plasma TG.(11) In this regard, overnight-fasted male mice received an injection of P-407 (1000 mpk, ip) and either a single subcutaneous injection of compound 16 (30 mg/kg) or an oral dose of an MTP inhibitor (10 mg/kg)(10) where the accumulation of plasma TG was measured over 6 h. As illustrated in Figure 1, a significant reduction in plasma TG was observed in mice treated with compound 16 and the MTP inhibitor (Figure 1A) when compared to that of noncompound treated controls, with 40 and 70% reductions for the respective inhibitors (Figure 1B). This result agrees uniformly with previous work in the field.(4)
    In addition to investigating the effect of DGAT2 inhibition on total plasma triglycerides, understanding the effect of DGAT2 inhibiton on de novo triglyceride production and fatty acid flux in vivo was of interest. With this in mind, the administration of stable-isotope-labeled fatty acids was used to elucidate lipid assembly in vivo using a rapid LC-MS method to measure the labeling pattern of triglycerides, diglyceride, phospholipids, and cholesteryl esters (Figure 2).(12) In this regard, C57BL/6 mice were pseudofasted and treated with either a single dose of compound 15 (30 mg/kg, SC) or a single dose of an MTP inhibitor (50 mg/kg, PO) with an appropriate vehicle control. One hour following compound treatment, mice were given an intravenous dose of [13C18] linoleic acid (50 mg/kg in intralipid 20; 10 mL/kg via tail vein) and labeling of plasma triglycerides, diglycerides, cholesteryl esters, and phosphatidylcholines was quantified by LC-MS over a 2 h time period. As illustrated in Figure 2A, a decrease in the amount of newly synthesized [54:6] plasma triglyceride is observed when DGAT2 and MTP are inhibited. However, the effect of compound 15 and the MTP inhibitor on plasma diglyceride [36:4], phosphatidylcholine [34:2], and cholesterol ester [18:2] was quite different (Figure 2BâD). In this regard, despite having no effect on plasma diglycerides [36:4], DGAT2 inhibition caused an increase in both plasma phosphatidylcholine [34:2] and cholesteryl ester [18:2], whereas the MTP inhibitor reduced all three groups. Overall, the distribution of the [13C18]-labeled linoleic acid tracer in the absence and presence of a DGAT2 inhibitor reveals a metabolic fingerprint of DGAT2 inhibition characterized by a reduction of newly synthesized triglyceride and a redistribution of the linoleic acid into newly synthesized phosphatidylcholine and cholesteryl ester, a result that further highlights the role of DGAT2 inhibition in the regulation of triglyceride production.

    Chemistry

    Synthesis of the tetrahydroisoquinoline-derived DGAT2 inhibitor 16 is outlined in Scheme 1. As illustrated, the commercially available dihalide intermediate I was used as a common starting material. The regioselective Suzuki coupling of I to the commercially available boronic acid, at ambient temperature, yielded the desired C-5 substituent II. Cyanation of chloride II was accomplished by heating in the presence of zinc cyanide, and compound III was reduced to the aminomethyl derivative IV using Raney nickel in ammonia/methanol. Coupling of the commercially available carboxylic acid to the amino-methyl derivative IV using EDC/HOBT furnished the desired isoquinoline V.
    Further elaboration to the tetrahydroisoquinoline was accomplished from the corresponding isoquinoline derivative V. The C-5/C-8-substituted isoquinoline V was treated with sodium cyanoborohydride in acetic acid and methanol to yield the desired tetrahydroisoquinoline analogue, intermediate VI. Finally, functionalization of the nitrogen was accomplished with t-butyl isocyanate and Hunigâs base to furnish compound 16.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, compound 16, N-(tert-butyl)-8-(((1S,2R)-2-(3-methyl-1,2,4-oxadiazol-5-yl)cyclopropanecarboxamido)methyl)-5-(3(trifluoromethoxy)phenyl)-3,4-dihydroisoquinoline-2(1H)-carboxamide, was identified as a potent and selective inhibitor of DGAT2. Compound 16 features a clean off-target profile and DMPK properties suitable to support pharmacological studies in mice. In this regard, compound 16 effectively reduced the rate of triglyceride production in both primary mouse hepatocytes and in mouse models of triglyceride production, providing pharmacological proof of biology of DGAT2 inhibition. On the basis of these results, compound 16 has been selected as a candidate for further evaluation in nonhuman primate proof-of-biology studies.(13)
