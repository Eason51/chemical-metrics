    Abstract

    Genetic depletion of cyclin-dependent kinase 12 (CDK12) or selective inhibition of an analog-sensitive CDK12 reduces DNA damage repair gene expression, but selective inhibition of endogenous CDK12 is difficult. Here, we report the development of MFH290, a novel cysteine (Cys)-directed covalent inhibitor of CDK12/13. MFH290 forms a covalent bond with Cys-1039 of CDK12, exhibits excellent kinome selectivity, inhibits the phosphorylation of serine-2 in the C-terminal domain (CTD) of RNA-polymerase II (Pol II), and reduces the expression of key DNA damage repair genes. Importantly, these effects were demonstrated to be CDK12-dependent as mutation of Cys-1039 rendered the kinase refractory to MFH290 and restored Pol II CTD phosphorylation and DNA damage repair gene expression. Consistent with its effect on DNA damage repair gene expression, MFH290 augments the antiproliferative effect of the PARP inhibitor olaparib.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Cyclin dependent kinases, or CDKs, are an intensively investigated family of evolutionarily conserved protein kinases that orchestrate the cell cycle and gene transcription.(1,2) Human cells encode 20 distinct CDKs which possess highly similar ATP-binding pockets, thereby making the development of selective CDK inhibitors challenging.(3) Despite these difficulties, some CDKs can be targeted quite selectively, such as CDK4/6 by the FDA approved drug palbociclib, based on recognition of unique combinations of residues in the ATP pocket. A more recently explored strategy has been to exploit uniquely positioned cysteine residues with reactive, electrophile-modified inhibitors as exemplified by the development of the CDK7/12/13 inhibitor THZ1 (Figure 1) which targets an unusual cysteine 312 located on a loop that traverses the inhibitor binding pocket.(4) Further structure-guided optimization of THZ1 resulted in the development of the selective CDK12/13 inhibitor THZ531(5) (Figure 1), which targets a spatially distinct cysteine residue (Cys1039 for CDK12 and Cys1017 for CDK13).
    CDK12 and the highly homologous protein CDK13 are both activated upon binding to cyclin K to regulate transcription through phosphorylating Ser2 residues located on the C-terminal domain (CTD) of RNA-polymerase II (RNA-Pol2) which contains 52 repeats of the YSPTSPS motif.(6) Selective inhibition of CDK12 kinase activity preferentially downregulates a small set of genes that respond to and mediate the repair of DNA-damage including homologous recombination (HR) repair genes BRCA1 and BRCA2.(7,8) Inhibitors of CDK12 increase DNA damage and have been demonstrated to potentiate the antiproliferative effects of poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors.(9) Recently, an in vivo combination treatment of dinaciclib (Figure 1), a clinical-stage nonselective CDK12 inhibitor, with olaparib, a poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitor, has demonstrated a significant tumor growth inhibition in a PDX ovarian model,(10) spurring a wave of medicinal chemistry efforts to discover more potent and selective CDK12 inhibitors.
    Among the reported inhibitors of CDK12 and CDK13 are the acrylamide-modified covalent inhibitors: THZ1 and THZ531 (Figure 1) in cancer cells. THZ531 was shown to irreversibly inhibit phosphorylation of ser2 of RNA pol II CTD, represses transcription of DNA damage response (DDR) genes, and results in potent antiproliferative effects.(5) Although validated as a cellular probe, THZ531 is not suitable for in vivo murine efficacy studies due to its poor metabolic stability resulting in high clearance in mice.(5) In addition to the covalent inhibitors, dinaciclib,(11) a clinical-stage pan-CDK inhibitor, exhibits activity in numerous models but is not suitable as a selective pharmacological probe of CDK12. Recently, 2,6,9-trisubstituted purines (2 and 4) and a trisubstituted urea (3, Figure 1) have been reported as reversible CDK12 inhibitors that exhibit high kinase selectivity for CDK12.(12−14) Here we report our efforts that culminated in the development of MFH290, a highly selective covalent inhibitor of CDK12/13 using a structure-guided drug design strategy by combining structural features of THZ531 with a previously reported noncovalent, pan-CDK inhibitor SNS032(15) (Figure 2A).

    Development of MFH290 as Covalent CDK12 Inhibitor

    High-throughput biochemical screening of kinase-focused chemical libraries is a common method to find starting points for inhibitor optimization campaigns. However, our efforts to develop a kinase assay suitable for screening CDK12 reversible inhibitors were unsuccessful. Therefore, we changed our strategy to use a competition-based pulldown assay to screen for reversible CDK12 binders. Our aim was to identify compounds that bound CDK12 that could be optimized and converted to covalent inhibitors targeting the uniquely positioned cysteine 1039 (Cys1039) residue that lies in the C-terminal domain of CDK12. Competitive pulldown assays were performed by utilizing biotinylated (bio)THZ1, which has been shown to covalently bind and pull down both CDK12 and CDK13 (CDK12/13).(5) As bioTHZ1 can be pulled down using streptavidin-coated beads, we gauged the ability of bioTHZ1 to bind free CDK12/13 by monitoring the extent to which CDK12, CDK13, or their obligate binding partner, cyclin K, were pulled down. We screened a focused collection of reported CDK inhibitors for their ability to protect CDK12 and -13 from being pulled down by bioTHZ1 (Figure S1A in Supporting Information). Among all known CDK inhibitors we screened, pretreatment with SNS032, a known inhibitor of CDK2, -7, and -9 (Figure 2A), resulted in a significant reduction of cyclin K pulldown from Jurkat cells by bioTHZ1 (Figure S1B in Supporting Information), providing a starting point for medicinal chemistry efforts.
    The cocrystal structure of SNS032 with CDK2 (PDB code 5D1J) clearly illustrates the binding mode where the (1) 3-aminothiazole group forms two key hydrogen bonds with the hinge region, (2) 5-(tert-butyl)oxazole ring occupies the back hydrophobic pocket II, and (3) the piperidine faces the solvent exposed region. Structural overlay of CDK12 crystal structure (PDB code 5ACB) and SNS032/CDK2 cocrystal structure indicated that the solvent-exposed piperidine ring would lie in close proximity to Cys1039 (Figure 2B). Subsequent chemistry was performed to explore the ability to form a covalent bond with Cys1039 and to gain additional selectivity for CDK12 relative to other kinases. All newly synthesized compounds were assayed for CDK12 binding using the bioTHZ1 competition assay (Figure S2 in Supporting Information). To quickly assess the selectivity for CDK12 versus the other CDKs targeted by SNS032, we used commercially available biochemical assays from Life Technologies for CDK2/cyclin A, CDK7/cyclin H/MNAT1, and CDK9/cyclin T1. In addition, we engineered HAP1 cells by mutating Cys1039 of CDK12 to a nonreactive serine to generate a chemical–genetic system to allow dissection of covalent-dependent inhibitor pharmacology.
    To explore advantageous modifications to SNS032, we started by installing the requisite acrylamide warhead on the solvent exposed region facing the cysteine residue. For example, compounds 1a, 1b, and 1c were all designed with an acrylamide group attached to either phenyl or piperidine ring whose structural alignment suggested would position this warhead in close proximity to Cys1039. All warhead modifications conserved the ability to block cyclin K pulldown with bioTHZ1, indicating that CDK12/13 binding was preserved (Table 1). In regard to selectivity, compound 1b maintained inhibitory activity against all the CDKs tested, whereas 1a and 1c exhibited dramatically reduced inhibition of CDK7 (Table 1 and Figure S2 in Supporting Information). In contrast, 3-aminopiperidine, which based on our work on THZ531 emerged as a privileged scaffold for selective CDK12 inhibition, led to a dramatic reduction in inhibition of CDK2/7/9 activity while CDK12/13 binding was retained (1d, hereafter named MFH290, Table 1). Moreover, the S-enantiomer of MFH290 (1e) lost the ability to target CDK12, meaning that the R configuration of 3-aminopiperidine is required to provide the proper trajectory to target Cys1039. Removing the carbonyl and installing an amino group through 3-aminocyclohexane (1f) slightly reduced CDK12 binding while also significantly restoring activity on CDK2 and CDK9. Therefore, further modifications focused on retaining the 3-aminopiperidine moiety.

    Introduction of either a methyl group to the 2-position (1g) or two fluorines to the 5-postion of piperidine ring (1h) abrogated the inhibitory activity on all CDKs, whereas introduction of a methyl group to the 6-position (1i) resulted in comparable, but less selective, activity to MFH290. Moreover, a phenyl ring extending from the piperidine was found to be more favored than a pyridine ring (1j) and a small group such as a fluorine (1k) was better tolerated compared to a larger CF3 group at the 3-position (1l) (Table 1). Replacement of the tert-butyloxazole group with pyridine (1m), pyrimidine (1n), or tetrahydropyran (1o) rings resulted in compete loss of activity. Additionally, the thioether linkage to the core thiazole was necessary for CDK12/13 activity as all non-thioether linked compounds such as 1p, 1q and 1r were inactive (Table 2). Likewise, oxidation of the thiol to sulfoxide (1s) diminished inhibition and binding to CDK2/7/9 and CDK12. Finally, a nonsubstituted thiazole core was most favored as replacement with a pyrimidine (1t), thiadiazole (1u), or even methylated thiazole (1v) significantly reduced CDK12 binding activity (Table 3). Taken all together, MFH290 emerged as our best-in-class inhibitor from this series.

    To confirm the covalent bond formation of MFH290 with Cys1039 on CDK12, we analyzed purified CDK12 protein by LC–MS after incubation with a 10-fold molar excess of MFH290 for 2 h at 37 °C and observed a mass shift consistent with addition of a single molecule of MFH290 to CDK12 (Figure 3A and Figure 3B). To confirm the site of modification, labeled protein was digested with trypsin and peptides were analyzed by capillary electrophoresis-MS/MS. Database search revealed exclusive modification of CDK12 by MFH290 at Cys1039 (Figure 3C). Taken together, our mass-spectrometry-based analysis showed that MFH290 forms a covalent adduct with CDK12 by reacting with a single cysteine, Cys1039. We therefore carried MFH290 forward into all subsequent cellular experiments to characterize the inhibitor and evaluate CDK12/13 function.
    To characterize the kinome-wide selectivity of MFH290, we employed DiscoverX’s KinomeScan binding assays against a panel of 468 kinases at a concentration of 1 μM.(17,18) As shown in Figure 4A and Table S1 in Supporting Information, MFH290 exhibits exquisite selectivity with CDK13 as the only potently inhibited target (CDK12 is not included in the panel). We also tested MFH290 on an extensive enzymatic CDKs panel which included CDKs 1–9, 12–14, and 16. The measured IC50 values across all other CDKs are higher than 100 nM, indicating that MFH290 did not potently inhibit any of these CDKs (Figure 4B).
    To define the working concentration range of MFH290, we employed the same competition-based target engagement assay described above (Figure S1A in Supporting Information). Concentrations of MFH290 that block bioTHZ1-mediated pulldown of CDK12/13/cyclin K identify doses at which MFH290 covalently binds CDK12/13 in cells. Experiments in both Jurkat T-ALL and HAP1 (a near haploid) CML lines indicate that MFH290 saturates CDK12/13 binding at a concentration of approximately 40 nM (Figure 5A, Figure S3 in Supporting Information).
    To determine the cellular selectivity of MFH290, we used CRISPR/Cas9-mediated allelic replacement(8) to establish isogenic cell lines expressing covalent inhibitor refractory cysteine-to-serine mutant forms of CDK12 (C1039S), CDK13 (C1017S), and CDK12/CDK13 double mutants (Figure S4A in Supporting Information). These isogenic lines enable us to assess the relative contribution of CDK12, CDK13, and CDK12/13 inhibition to MFH290 pharmacology. Western blotting demonstrated that WT and mutant forms of CDK12 and CDK13 were all expressed at similar levels (Figure S4B in Supporting Information).
    We previously reported that THZ531, our first-generation CDK12/13 inhibitor, led to a pronounced and rapid decrease in cell proliferation;(5) therefore we tested MFH290 across its effective dose range for antiproliferative activity in the HAP1 isogenic lines. In WT HAP1 cells, MFH290 displayed low nanomolar antiproliferative activity. Mutation of the reactive cysteine within CDK12, but not CDK13, significantly blunted these effects, with CDK12 mutant cells exhibiting 11-fold resistance to MFH290 to WT CDK12 cells, which was further enhanced to 25-fold as compared to WT when both CDK12 and CDK13 were mutated (Figure 5B).
    CDK12 and CDK13 have been implicated in the regulation of RNA polymerase II (Pol II)-mediated gene transcription by phosphorylating Pol II at conserved residues within the CTD.(19) Therefore, we asked whether MFH290 affected Pol II CTD phosphorylation levels in our HAP1 isogenic lines. Indeed, MFH290 treatment reduced Pol II CTD phosphorylation at serine residues 2, 5, 7 (Ser2, Ser5, Ser7), as well as overall Pol II levels (Figure 5C). Mutation of CDK12 greatly restored levels of both total and phosphorylated Pol II to near WT levels in the presence of MFH290, while mutation of CDK13 exhibited a significantly reduced rescue. Complete rescue of Pol II CTD phosphorylation was achieved by expressing both mutant CDK12 and CDK13 in HAP1 cells (Figure 5C). The difference in rescue demonstrated by mutation of CDK12 compared to that of CDK13 likely stems from the fact that CDK12 is the more highly expressed and predominant homolog in HAP1 cells.
    As CDK12 has been shown to specifically regulate the expression of DNA damage response (DDR) genes,(20) we treated HAP1 cells with MFH290 at 40 and 200 nM and analyzed expression of DDR RNA and protein expression by RT qPCR and Western blot. We found that treatment with MFH290 reduced expression of RAD51 and RAD51C at both the RNA and protein level (Figure 6A and Figure 6B). These effects were almost completely rescued at the protein level and largely rescued at the RNA level by expression of mutant CDK12 alone or in combination with C1017S CDK13.
    Impaired expression of RAD51 and other homologous recombination (HR)-regulating proteins has been shown to sensitize cancer cells to PARP inhibitors.(21) To ask if MFH290 could do the same, we treated HAP1 cells with increasing concentrations of both olaparib, an FDA-approved PARP inhibitor, and MFH290. We examined the proliferation data for synergistic effects as evaluated by extrapolated excess over Bliss score (0–1).(22,23) Indeed MFH290 (25 nM) exhibited maximum synergy with olaparib (1.2 μM) with excess over Bliss scores of 0.75 (Figure 6C).
    These data indicate that CDK12 is the primary target of MFH290 in HAP1 cells, that covalent targeting of CDK12 is obligatory for MFH290 activity, and that CDK12 inhibition is largely responsible for the observed antiproliferative effects. Consistent with biochemical kinase profiling, these results indicate that MFH290 has exceptional cellular selectivity for CDK12/13 (Tables 1–3), supporting the notion that CDK12/13 inhibition is responsible for inhibitor-induced effects on DDR gene expression.

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We have converted a pan-CDK inhibitor SNS032 to a selective CDK12/13 inhibitor MFH290 by hybridizing it with structural elements of the first selective CDK12/13 covalent inhibitor THZ531. Extensive structure-guided medicinal chemistry modifications delivered a narrow SAR, with (R)-3-aminopiperidine emerging as a privileged scaffold, enabling high binding affinity for CDK12/13 to the exclusion of other CDKs. MFH290 formed a covalent bond with Cys1039 on CDK12 and thus demonstrated a sustained CDK12 inhibition-induced phenotype including strong antiproliferative effect on cancer cells, a transcriptional defect for DDR genes, and a combinatorial effect with PARP inhibition. Compared to first generation covalent inhibitor THZ531, a higher degree of rescue from MFH290-induced phenotypes in HAP1 cells engineered with cysteine-to-serine CDK12 and CDK13 mutations implies that MFH290 possesses a superior selectivity for CDK12/13. Further optimization of this scaffold to improve in vivo properties will enable in vivo investigation of drug efficacy.

    Synthesis of Compound 1a

    To a solution of 5-(((5-(tert-butyl)oxazol-2-yl)methyl)thio)thiazol-2-amine (54 mg, 0.2 mmol) in DMF (1 mL) were added HATU (160 mg, 0.42 mmol), 3-acrylamidobenzoic acid (60 mg, 0.31 mmol), and DIEA (52 mg, 0.4 mmol). The resulting mixture was stirred at room temperature for 1 h and then diluted with ethyl acetate (100 mL). The mixture was washed with water and brine, dried over Na2SO4, and concentrated.     Constructs

    For CRISPR studies, guide RNAs (sgRNA) targeting CDK12 and CDK13 were cloned into pX330 (Addgene, 42230). pUC57-AMP was used as storage vector for CDK12 and CDK13 genome reference sequence.

    Genome Editing

    The CRISPR/Cas9 system was used to mutate the endogenous CDK12 and CDK13 WT loci to encode for CDK12 C1039S and CDK13 C1017S, respectively. Target-specific oligonucleotides were cloned into pX330 (Addgene, 42230), which carries a codon-optimized version of Cas9 and was further modified to express GFP for identifying transfectants. Cells were co-transfected (X-tremeGENE 9 (Roche)) with (1) pX330 expressing Cas9 and CDK12 (or CDK13) targeting sgRNA and (2) a pUC57-AMP construct bearing 1500 bp of modified CDK12 (or CDK13) reference genome that is centered around the CRISPR targeting site in CDK12 (or CDK13). Two days after transfection, cells were sorted using GFP as a marker of transfected cells and cells were replated for 5 days. Cells were then replated at low density to facilitate the isolation of individual clones. Individual clones were isolated, expanded, and PCR genotyped using mutant specific PCR primers. Following initial PCR screening, individual clones were Sanger sequenced to confirm the presence of the desired mutation. To generate a CDK12 C1039S/CDK13 C1017S double mutant clone, mutations were serially performed, sequenced verified, and resubjected to further mutagenesis. Western blot confirmed the presence of intact CDK12 (or CDK13) kinase. Subsequent experiments were conducted using a CDK12 C1039S clone, a CDK13 C1017S clone, a CDK12 C1039S/CDK13 C1017S double mutant clone, and a WT control clone that was carried through the entirety of the CRISPR protocol but that was verified by Sanger sequencing to be WT CDK12 and CDK13.

    Pulldown/Immunoprecipitation (IP) Experiments

    Cells were treated with MFH290 or DMSO for 6 h. Following treatment cells were washed 2-fold with cold PBS and then lysed in the following lysis buffer: 50 mM Hepes, pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl, 1% Nonidet P40 substitute, 5 mM EDTA, 1 mM DTT, and protease/phosphatase cocktails. Following clearance, lysates were treated with bio-THZ1 for pulldown overnight at 4 °C. Lysates were further incubated at room temperature for 3 h to increase the efficiency of covalent bond formation. Agarose beads were washed 6 times with lysis buffer and then boiled in 2× SDS page loading buffer for 10 min at 95 °C. SDS–PAGE resolved precipitated proteins were probed for the indicated proteins.

    Mass Spectrometry Analysis

    CDK12/cyclin K complex (5 μg) was treated with DMSO or a 10-fold molar excess of MFH-2-90 for 1 h at room temperature and analyzed by LC–MS using an HPLC system (Shimadzu, Marlborough, MA) interfaced to an LTQ ion trap mass spectrometer (ThermoFisher Scientific, San Jose, CA). After injection, proteins were desalted for 4 min on column with 100% A and then eluted with an HPLC gradient (0–100% B in 1 min; A = 0.2 M acetic acid in water; B = 0.2 M acetic acid in acetonitrile). The mass spectrometer was programmed to acquire full scan mass spectra (m/z 300–2000) in profile mode (spray voltage = 4.5 kV). Mass spectra were deconvoluted using MagTran software version 1.03b2.(25)
    To identify the site of covalent modification, MFH290 treated CDK/cyclin K complex was reduced with 10 mM tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine for 10 min at room temperature, alkylated with 20 mM methyl methanethiosulfonate for 10 min at room temperature, and digested with Glu-C protease (Promega, Madison, WI) overnight at 37 °C. Peptides were desalted using C18 (SOLA, ThermoFisher Scientific, Madison, WI), dried by vacuum centrifugation, reconstituted in 50% acetonitrile, 1% formic acid, 100 mM ammonium acetate, and analyzed by CE-MS using a ZipChip CE-MS instrument and autosampler (908 devices, Boston, MA) interfaced to a QExactive HF mass spectrometer (ThermoFisher Scientific). Peptides were resolved at 500 V/cm using an HR chip with a background electrolyte consisting of 50% acetonitrile with 1% formic acid. The mass spectrometer was operated in data dependent mode and subjected the 5 most abundant ions in each MS scan (m/z 300–2000, 60K resolution, 3E6 target, 100 ms max fill time) to MS/MS (15K resolution, 1E5 target, 100 ms max fill time). Dynamic exclusion was enabled with a repeat count of 1 and an exclusion duration of 5 s. Raw mass spectrometry data files were converted to .mgf using Multiplierz software(26) and searched against a forward–reverse human refseq database using Mascot version 2.6.2. Search parameters specified fixed methylthio modification of cysteine, variable methionine oxidation, and variable MFH290 modification of cysteine. MFH290 modified spectra were examined and figures prepared using mzStudio software.(27) MFH290 specific product ions were assigned as described.(16)
