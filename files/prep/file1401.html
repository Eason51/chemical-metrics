    Abstract

    In our search for selective agonists for the α4β2 subtype of the nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs), we have synthesized and characterized a series of novel heterocyclic analogues of 3-(dimethylamino)butyl dimethylcarbamate (DMABC, 4). All new heterocyclic analogues, especially N,N-dimethyl-4-(1-methyl-1H-imidazol-2-yloxy)butan-2-amine (7), showed an improved binding selectivity profile in favor of α4β2 over other nAChR subtypes, primarily due to impaired binding at β4 containing receptors. This observation can be rationalized based on cocrystal structures of (R)-4 and (R)-7 bound to acetylcholine binding protein from Lymnaea stagnalis. Functional characterization at both (α4)2(β2)3 and (α4)3(β2)2 receptors using two-electrode voltage clamp techniques in Xenopus laevis oocytes indicates that the investigated compounds interact differently with the two receptor stoichiometries. Compound 7 is an efficacious agonist at both α4-β2 and α4-α4 binding sites, while the close analogue N,N-dimethyl-4-(1,4-dimethyl-1H-imidazol-2-yloxy)butan-2-amine (9) primarily activates via α4-β2 binding sites. The results suggest that it may be possible to rationally design compounds with specific stoichiometry preferences.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The α4β2 nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (nAChR) subtype is the most abundant nAChR in the central nervous system (CNS), and numerous studies have confirmed its significance in higher brain function and in relation to diseases and disorders of the nervous system.(1, 2) Animal models have indicated the potential of α4β2 receptors as drug targets in the treatment of schizophrenia(3) and depression,(4) and furthermore, the receptors are targeted by marketed smoking cessation aids including cytisine and varenicline.(5-7)
    One of the major challenges when targeting nAChRs is the existence of many receptor subtypes. To date, 16 mammalian nAChR subunits have been identified, and when assembled into functional pentamers, they result in a plethora of receptor subtypes. Considerable efforts have gone into the synthesis of subtype selective nAChR ligands by exploiting differences in binding affinity between subtypes, but the concept of functional selectivity has also been pursued.(8-10) The complexity of many subtypes is further complicated by subtypes assembling in different stoichiometric ratios. The α4β2 nAChR is known to assemble in two functional stoichiometries: (α4)2(β2)3 and (α4)3(β2)2.(11, 12) The (α4)2(β2)3 receptors contain two identical binding sites with α4 as the principal and β2 as the complementary subunit (α4-β2) and display monophasic concentration–response relationships when challenged with acetylcholine (1, Figure 1) and other agonists. The (α4)3(β2)2 receptors, on the other hand, display an inherent biphasic concentration–response relationship to 1 and many other agonists. As we recently demonstrated, this biphasic response can be decomposed into activation by α4-β2 and α4-α4 interface components with the latter being unique to the (α4)3(β2)2 receptor.(13) The presence of distinct receptor subpopulations represents yet another challenge complicating rational nAChR drug discovery. However, it may also serve as an intriguing opportunity to obtain functional selectivity by selectively targeting specific receptor stoichiometries. For instance, a positive allosteric modulator, only enhancing the agonist response at (α4)3(β2)2 receptors, has been reported recently.(14)
    In nAChR drug discovery, X-ray crystallographic studies of acetylcholine binding proteins (AChBPs) isolated from mollusks have played an important role in elucidating the molecular details of ligand binding and understanding SARs.(15-18) AChBPs are soluble structural homologues of the extracellular domain (ECD) of nAChRs. Despite a relatively low overall sequence identity of 20–24% between AChBPs and the ECDs of the nAChRs, the sequence identity in the orthosteric binding pocket is high and AChBP has been shown to bind a wide range of orthosteric nAChR ligands including 1, carbamoylcholine (2), and (S)-nicotine (3).(16, 19) In contrast to the binding event, the mechanism by which the receptor is activated by agonists is under debate. Structural studies have revealed a high degree of flexibility of the so-called loop C. The loop caps the ligand upon binding, and the capping motion has been suggested to couple the binding event with channel activation.(16, 17, 20) Some also suggest that the degree of loop closure correlates to efficacy.(20, 21) However, in a recent study using AChBP from the mollusk Lymnaea stagnalis (Ls-AChBP) as a surrogate for the ECD of the α4β2 receptor we found no correlation between loop C closure and efficacy.(18) In the study of a series of five α4β2 agonists with varying degrees of efficacy the data rather suggested that an intersubunit bridge formation governs efficacy.(18)
    Over the past decade we have explored the SARs of several homologues of 2 through a classical medicinal chemistry approach with the specific aim of obtaining agonists with high affinity toward the α4β2 subtype.(22-26) Of these, the ligand 3-(dimethylamino)butyl dimethylcarbamate (DMABC, 4) (Figure 1) is suitable as lead compound for further investigation. Compound 4 has low muscarinic receptor affinity(23) and captures the essence of previous SAR studies,(22-25) which is in brief an essential tertiary amine, a methyl group in the C-3 position of the carbon chain, and a carbamate moiety bearing only small substituents.
    In the present study we have explored scaffold 4 further by replacing the aminocarbonyl part of the essential carbamate moiety with five- and six-membered heteroaromatic rings (compounds 5–11). On the basis of the structure of Ls-AChBP with 2 bound,(16) we hypothesized that the tertiary amine of these compounds would form contacts to the principal subunit (α-subunit in α4-β2 interface), while the moiety corresponding to the carbamate would have contacts to the complementary subunit (β-subunit in α4-β2 interface). Hence, substitution of the carbamate moiety alters the β2 versus β4 preference and potentially leads to compounds with improved subtype selectivity and could help to clearly distinguish between α4β2 and α3β4 subtypes which are the more abundant and widespread subtypes in the CNS and PNS.(27) Here, we present the synthesis and pharmacological characterization of a series of novel heterocyclic analogues of 4, all of which show improved selectivity toward the α4β2 nAChRs in radioligand binding experiments. Further, we present X-ray crystallographic data of Ls-AChBP in complexes with the parent compound 4 and one of the new high affinity heterocyclic analogues (7). The combination of affinity data and X-ray structures gives a unique opportunity for building a comprehensive SAR of this class of compounds and provides a structural framework for future structure-based drug design projects. Finally, we have characterized three compounds (4, 7, and 9) by two-electrode voltage clamp techniques (TEVC) in Xenopus laevis oocytes at both (α4)3(β2)2 and (α4)2(β2)3 receptors. The results suggest that it may be possible to selectively target individual α4β2 stoichiometries to obtain functionally selective ligands targeting the orthosteric site of the nAChRs.

    Chemistry

    The synthesis of 4 has been previously reported,(24) and seven new heterocyclic analogues thereof were synthesized. The synthetic approaches to the target compounds 5–11 are outlined in Scheme 1. Compounds 5–9 were all synthesized using the sodium salt of the amino alcohol 12, synthesized as previously described,(28) and the appropriate halogenated heterocycle. In the synthesis of imidazole analogues 7–9, regioselective halogen–metal exchange was performed using 2,4,5-tribromo-1-methyl-1H-imidazole. The dibrominated intermediate 14 was subsequently treated with an excess of n-BuLi and protonated to afford 7 and 8. Compound 9 was obtained from 14 by introduction of a trimethylsilyl protecting group in the 4-position of the imidazole ring followed by addition of additional n-BuLi and methyl iodide. Desilylation of the C-4 protected imidazole analogue using sodium methoxide afforded 9. The pyrazole and pyridine analogues 10 and 11 were synthesized via the aminoalkyl bromide 13, in a nucleophilic substitution of the sodium salts of 1H-pyrazol-1-ol and pyridin-3-ol, respectively. All target compounds were isolated as the oxalate salts.

    Pharmacology

    The binding affinities of reference and target compounds (1, 3, and 5–11) were determined at recombinant heteromeric rat α4β2, α3β4, and α4β4 receptors as previously reported.(25) For selected compounds, binding affinities at homomeric rat α7 receptors and Ls-AChBP were estimated using chimeric constructs where the ECDs of α7 and Ls-AChBP were linked to the ion channel portion of the 5-HT3A receptor subunit. These constructs, in the following referred to as α7/5-HT3A and Ls-AChBP/5-HT3A, have previously been shown to reproduce binding affinities of the native receptor(24, 29) and soluble protein,(18) respectively. The results of binding affinity studies are summarized in Table 1, together with the selectivity ratios for α3β4 and α4β4 nAChR subtypes relative to α4β2. As previously reported, 4 binds with 20 nM affinity at the α4β2 receptor subtype, whereas affinities at the α3β4 and α4β4 receptor subtypes are approximately 10- to 20-fold lower and essentially no affinity is observed at α7/5-HT3A.(22) Like 4 and other previously tested homologues of 2,(22-25) all compounds tested in the present study are practically devoid of affinity at the α7/5-HT3A chimera. Relative to 4, the thiazole, imidazole, and pyrazole analogues (5, 7, and 10) have unchanged binding affinity at the α4β2 receptors and an approximately 10- to 70-fold reduction in affinities at β4-containing receptors leading to improved selectivity ratios for these compounds. Further substitution on the imidazole ring, as seen for compound 8 and 9, leads to impaired binding at all heteromeric receptors tested. Analogues bearing six-membered rings, 6 and 11, display a modest, approximately 1.5- to 10-fold reduction in affinities at α4β2 receptors compared to 4, whereas an approximately 10- to 100-fold reduction in affinities at β4-containing receptors is observed. Consequently, all heterocyclic analogues 5–11 show increased selectivity toward the α4β2 receptors compared to 1, 3, and 4. Of the compounds that retain low nanomolar affinity, the highest α3β4/α4β2 selectivity ratio is observed for 7, which binds with ∼500 times higher affinity to α4β2 than to α3β4.

    For selected compounds 4, 7, and 9 the binding affinity at the Ls-AChBP/5-HT3A chimera was determined. The affinities of these compounds at the Ls-AChBP/5-HT3A chimera reflect their affinities at the α4β2 receptors, as the same trend in affinity increase/decrease is observed. The affinity at the Ls-AChBP/5-HT3A is just lowered by a factor of about 3–5 compared to the affinity at the α4β2 receptors. Compound 4 and the imidazole analogue 7 have similar binding affinities, while the substituted imidazole analogue 9 displays impaired binding. Similar affinities at the α4β2 receptor and the Ls-AChBP/5-HT3A chimera are in accordance with previous observations by Rohde et al.(18) and support the use of Ls-AChBP as a structural surrogate for the α4β2 receptor ECD with these compounds.
    The selected compounds 4, 7, and 9 were characterized functionally at both human (α4)2(β2)3 and (α4)3(β2)2 receptors by TEVC in Xenopus laevis oocytes. Homogenous receptor populations in the TEVC experiments were ensured by injection of oocytes with α4 and β2 cRNA in 1:4 and 4:1 ratios, respectively, as previously described.(13) The concentration–response relationships obtained by TEVC electrophysiology are shown in Figure 2, and functional data are summarized in Table 2. At the (α4)2(β2)3 receptor, compounds 4 and 7 are equipotent with potencies matching that of 1 (1.6 μM),(13) whereas 9 displays a decrease in potency by a factor of ∼10. Interestingly, compounds 4, 7, and 9 are all partial agonists at (α4)2(β2)3 receptors, evoking similar efficacies in the ∼30–45% range relative to 1 (Table 2). A quite contrasting picture emerged at the (α4)3(β2)2 receptors, as compounds 4, 7, and 9 are almost equipotent. Although agonist data for (α4)3(β2)2 receptors should theoretically always be fitted to a two-component Hill equation, these data were fitted to a one-component Hill equation for reasons stated in the section Discussion and Conclusion. Despite the partial nature at (α4)2(β2)3 receptors, 4 and 7 are high partial to almost full agonists at the (α4)3(β2)2 receptors with efficacies of ∼65–90% of the 1 response. In contrast, 9 displays very low efficacy, evoking less than 10% of the control response. Compound 4 has previously been shown to be a full agonist at α3β4 receptors (EC50 = 11 μM, Rmax = 110%)(24) in the FLIPR membrane potential (FMP) Blue assay. Despite limitations in terms of control of subunit stoichiometries in receptors expressed in HEK293 cells and the lack of linearity of the response in the FMP assay, the overall functional profiles of compounds 7 and 9 at the α3β4 receptor were evaluated in the assay. Compound 7 was found to be an agonist (EC50 [pEC50 ± SEM] = 20 μM [4.7 ± 0.05], Rmax = 95% ± 7%), while 9 was devoid of agonist and antagonist activity up to 1000 μM.

    All orthosteric binding sites at the subunit interfaces are occupied by ligands and capped by loop C (Figure 3B). This pentameric arrangement and location of the ligands are also observed in other structures of AChBPs cocrystallized with small organic amines.(16-18, 20) Residues in the so-called loop F (Thr155-Glu163) generally gave rise to poor electron densities and were consequently omitted from the models.
    Despite being cocrystallized with the racemates of 4 and 7, the electron densities, as illustrated by the omit 2Fo – Fc electron density maps shown in Figure 4A and Figure 4B, allowed unequivocal fitting of the (R)-enantiomers of both ligands. This is in agreement with pharmacological data showing that the eutomer of 4 and other structurally related compounds are the (R)-enantiomers.(22, 25) Both ligands adopt essentially the same binding mode with a nonextended conformation which is unlike the previously suggested binding mode of 4.(23) The closure of loop C induced by the ligands is 7.1 Å for both structures as measured from the backbone carbonyl of Trp143 to Sγ of Cys187 in accordance with the definition suggested by Brams et al.(21) On the principal side (Figure 4A and Figure 4B) the amine moieties of 4 and 7, which are likely to be protonated at physiological pH, are within hydrogen bonding distance of the backbone carbonyl of Trp143 (2.8–2.9 Å; chains C and D were chosen as a representative interface). The amine is also in the vicinity (3.0 Å) of the internal ether-like oxygen common to both 4 and 7. In addition, there are close contacts (2.9–3.5 Å) from one of the N-methyl groups on the amine moiety to several amino acid residues on the principal subunit (Tyr89, Ser142, and Trp143). Contacts (3.7–4.1 Å) are observed to side chains of several of the residues within the aromatic box (Tyr89, Trp143, Tyr192) to the other N-methyl group (pointing out of the plane in Figure 4A and Figure 4B) and the methylene group adjacent to the amine. Furthermore, Tyr192 is within 3.0 Å of the methyl substituent on the imidazole moiety of 7 and the corresponding methyl group of 4 (3.2 Å), indicating electrostatic interactions to polarized N-methyl groups. Similar alkyl mediated contacts have been observed for several nAChR ligands, e.g., 3,(16, 17) and in other ligand–protein interactions as reviewed by Bissantz et al.(30)
    On the complementary side (Figure 4A and Figure 4B), the carbonyl group of 4 and N3 in the imidazole moiety of 7 form hydrogen bonds to a well-defined water molecule located in the binding site. The water molecule is linked to the complementary subunit through hydrogen bonds with the NH group of Met114 (2.9 Å) and the backbone carbonyl of Leu102 (2.9 Å) as also observed for other nAChR ligand–AChBP complexes.(16-18, 20) Furthermore, Cβ of Arg104 is in a van der Waals contact distance (3.3 Å) from C5 of the imidazole moiety and the corresponding methyl group in 4. The two cocrystallized ligands deviate sterically by the presence of an additional carbon (C4) in the imidazole moiety of 7 (Figure 4C). This extra carbon atom is within contact distance of the water molecule (3.5 Å), the carbonyl group of Leu102 (4.0 Å), the carbonyl group of Leu112 (3.7 Å), the Cβ of Arg104 (4.0 Å), and the backbone amine of the same residue (4.0 Å).

    Discussion and Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In the present study, we present a series of novel α4β2 nAChR agonists, using 4 as a scaffold. Initially, the heterocyclic analogues were designed to incorporate the hydrogen bond acceptor feature corresponding to the carbonyl oxygen of 4 into heteroaromatic rings, thereby bioisosterically replacing the aminocarbonyl part in 4 with a rigid heteroaromatic ring. Compounds 5–11 were prepared using conventional synthetic methods in a convergent synthesis where heterocycles could be introduced in place of the aminocarbonyl moiety in the last diversifying step of the synthesis. Among the heterocyclic analogues tested, four compounds, the thiazol-2-yl derivative (5), the 1-methylimidazol-2-yl derivative (7), the pyrazol-1-yl derivative (10), and the pyridin-3-yl derivative (11), show retained binding affinity at α4β2 receptors and decreased affinities at α3β4 and α4β4 receptors compared to 4 (Table 1). The α3β4/α4β2 selectivity ratios for compounds 7 and 10 were as high as 450- to 500-fold, which corresponds to a ∼25-fold increase in selectivity ratio relative to the parent compound 4. On the basis of its binding affinity profile and selectivity ratio, compound 7 was selected for further pharmacological and biostructural characterization along with the parent compound 4 and the substituted imidazole analogue 9. The binding affinity of 4, 7, and 9 at the Ls-AChBP/5HT3A chimera suggested a correlation with α4β2 affinities, which is in line with previous results.(18) Therefore, compounds 4 and 7 were cocrystallized with Ls-AChBP to investigate the binding modes and thereby to aid the understanding and interpretation of structure–activity/selectivity relationships. Attempts to cocrystallize 9 resulted in poorly diffracting crystals.

    Compounds 4 and 7 Bind in Nonextended Conformations

    Both 4 and 7 bind to Ls-AChBP in nonextended conformations, unlike the previously suggested nAChR binding mode for 4.(23) The nonextended conformations of the cocrystallized ligands seem to be stabilized partly by an internal hydrogen bond from the protonated amine of the N,N-dimethylamine moiety to the ether-like oxygen, common to both compounds 4 and 7, which is within hydrogen bonding distance. We have previously explored analogues of 4 where this ether-like oxygen was replaced with methylene, methine, sulfur, and nitrogen, which all lead to significantly impaired binding affinities at all heteromeric nAChRs tested.(22) Retrospectively, as an internal hydrogen bond is either not possible or expected to be significantly weaker in these analogues, this observation offers an explanation for the lower pharmacological activity of these compounds. Given the binding mode of 7, it seems reasonable to speculate that the other structurally similar analogues 5, 6, 10, and 11 (8 and 9 discussed below) bind in a similar binding mode with the nitrogen of the heterocycle forming a hydrogen bond to the water molecule located in the binding site. This is supported by similar α4β2 affinities of 4, 5, 7, 10, and 11 (13–30 nM).

    The Complementary β-Subunit Is the Main Determinant of Binding Selectivity

    The most notable difference in the binding studies between the new heterocyclic analogues and the parent compound 4 is the improved α4β2 selectivity, which is largely accomplished by a lowering of the binding affinity at β4-containing receptors. To rationalize this, we have compared the amino acid residues lining the binding sites of the subtypes investigated in binding experiments. Binding site residues were defined as those corresponding to amino acids within 8 Å of the carbamate and imidazolyloxy moieties in the Ls-AChBP cocrystal complexes. The alignment of amino acid sequences is shown in Figure 6, where residues differing between subtypes have been highlighted in gray. The corresponding residues in the Ls-AChBP cocrystal structures are shown in stick representation in Figure 4C. On the principal side the only residue that differs within 8 Å of the ligands corresponds to Thr144 of Ls-AChBP. In the α4 subunit the corresponding residue is Thr155, while it has been substituted by Ser150 in the α3 subunit. Comparing binding affinities of compounds 5–11 at α4β4 and α3β4, which both have the same β-subunit, suggests that this rather conservative substitution, Thr to Ser, has limited effect on the binding affinities of these compounds, as these tend to differ by no more than a factor of ∼5. The structures show that 4 and 7 are within van der Waals distance to Cβ and Cγ of Thr144 (3.7 Å) and that Thr144 shapes the binding cavity. Therefore, a Thr to Ser substitution could lead to a differently shaped cavity and may represent an important binding selectivity determinant for other compound classes.
    The X-ray structures confirmed our assumption based on the structure of Ls-AChBP with 2 bound(16) that the variable part of the compounds would primarily have contacts to the complementary subunit. From the binding experiments it is evident that all heterocyclic analogues 5–11 have decreased binding affinities toward β4-containing receptors compared to 1, 3, and 4 (Table 1). This suggests that the steric bulk in the variable part of the analogues hampers binding to β4-containing receptors. On the basis of the alignment in Figure 6, four residues differ on the complementary side, corresponding to Gln55, Gln73, Arg104, and Leu112 in Ls-AChBP. While indirect effects of the first two residues (Thr59 and Lys79 in β2 and Lys61 and Ile81 in β4) cannot be excluded, the residues seem unable to be in direct contact with the bound ligands in the X-ray structures. This is supported by the fact that a point mutation T59K in the β2 subunit has been shown only to reduce the binding of the small molecule agonist epibatidine by a factor of ∼2.(31) The remaining two residues, Arg104 and Leu112 in Ls-AChBP corresponding to Val111 and Phe119 in β2 and Ile113 and Leu121 in β4, are both in the vicinity of the ligands. As shown in Figure 4C, the carbamate and heterocyclic imidazolyloxy moieties of 4 and 7 superimpose almost perfectly and have contact distances to Cβ of Arg104 in Ls-AChBP and to Cδ2 of Leu112 in the 3.3–3.8 Å range. Therefore, it is reasonable to speculate that these two substitutions, Val to Ile and Phe to Leu (β2 vs β4), may discriminate ligands differently and be the determinants of the observed decrease in binding affinities of the bulkier heterocyclic analogues at the β4-containing receptors.

    Compounds 4 and 7 Bind under Fully Closed C-Loops Despite Being Partial Agonists at (α4)2(β2)3 Receptors

    In TEVC experiments compounds 4 and 7 were equipotent with 1, whereas 9 displayed ∼10-fold lower potency at (α4)2(β2)3 receptors, which contain two identical α4-β2 binding sites. Even though a crystal structure with 9 could not be obtained, the cocrystallized imidazole analogue 7 allows us to interpret the effects of imidazole C4-substitution. In this structure, shape complementarities are observed between 7 and the protein as illustrated in Figure 5. Therefore, substitution in the C4 position of the imidazole ring would lead to an unfavorable steric interaction with the protein, as reflected in the ∼10- to 20-fold decrease in binding affinity of 8 and 9 and a corresponding ∼10-fold decrease in potency for 9. With regard to efficacy, 4, 7, and 9 were all partial agonists at (α4)2(β2)3 receptors, evoking ∼30–45% efficacy relative to 1. Previous studies have attempted to correlate efficacies to the degree of closure of loop C.(21, 32) However, the observed degree of closure of loop C for complexes with 4 and 7 does not deviate from what has been observed for other agonists in complex with Ls-AChBP, e.g., 3 (PDB code 1UW6), 2 (PDB code 1UV6), and 1-(6-bromo-5-ethoxypyridin-3-yl)-1,4-diazepane (PDB code 3U8N)(16, 18) as illustrated in Supporting Information Figure S2. These compounds have all been shown to be agonists at (α4)2(β2)3 receptors spanning a broad range of efficacies. Thus, data for 4 and 7 add to the notion that closure of loop C is not directly correlated with agonist efficacy in line with our recent studies.(18, 33)

    Minor Substitutions Can Change the Functional Properties of Ligands at (α4)3(β2)2 Receptors

    Compounds 4, 7, and 9 were also characterized functionally at the (α4)3(β2)2 receptor that contains two identical α4-β2 binding sites but also an additional α4-α4 binding site. This additional binding site makes the interpretation of data less straightforward especially in a structural context. It has been shown that a biphasic concentration–response curve is an intrinsic property of the (α4)3(β2)2 receptors.(13) One component of this biphasic curve corresponds to the α4-β2 binding site (usually displaying highest sensitivity toward prototypical nicotinic agonists), while the second component reflects the α4-α4 binding site. However, a prerequisite for reliably observing biphasic concentration–response relationships in, for example, TEVC is a substantial difference in potencies at the two sites. This turned out not to be the case for 4, 7, and 9, and given that functional potencies at the α4-β2 binding site were already established from (α4)2(β2)3 data, we chose to fit the (α4)3(β2)2 receptor data to monophasic curves when obtaining potencies and efficacies at the α4-α4 binding site.
    Compounds 4 and 7 are agonists at (α4)3(β2)2 receptors with a ∼20-fold potency shift relative to the (α4)2(β2)3 receptors, which is less than the ∼50-fold reported for 1.(13) Surprisingly, this shift is accompanied with increased efficacy levels (∼65–90%, Figure 2 and Table 2), indicating that the presence of an α4-α4 site contributes heavily to the efficacy of these compounds; i.e., 4 and 7 are highly efficacious at the α4-α4 site. Compound 9, on the other hand, behaved very differently, as only a marginal shift is noticed in functional EC50 values between the two receptor stoichiometries and further the efficacy level at the (α4)3(β2)2 receptor was very low (∼9%). A straightforward interpretation of these findings is that currents evoked by 9 are dominated by the α4-β2 binding site component, suggesting that the compound is devoid of efficacy at the α4-α4 site. This interpretation is consistent with the low efficacy and the “missing” potency shift of compound 9. Structurally, these observations indicate that the C4 position methylation in 9 substantially reduces efficacy in the α4-α4 site relative to 7. Thus, minor substitutions, for example, addition of a methyl group, can change the functional properties of ligands and alter their efficacy and potency at specific orthosteric subunit interfaces. This opens an intriguing possibility of designing orthosteric ligands that not only display overall receptor subtype selectivity but further selectivity for a given receptor stoichiometry, thereby being able to fine-tune the receptor response. Such orthosteric ligands could represent an alternative to stoichiometry selective positive allosteric modulators, as the orthosteric ligands do not rely upon receptor activation by sufficient levels of endogenous ligand that might not be present in, for example, severe stages of Alzheimer’s disease.
    It is highly likely that it is achievable to design orthosteric ligands selective for the α4-α4 site, as this site is more hydrophilic in nature than the α4-β2 site as noticed previously by Harpsøe et al.(13) As seen in Figure 6, the hydrophobic Val111, Phe119, and Leu121 of the α4-β2 interface are substituted for the more hydrophilic His114, Gln122, and Thr124 in the α4-α4 interface. An indication of this is reflected in our functional results because 4, which is the most hydrophilic of the tested compounds, is also the most efficacious in the α4-α4 site. The reverse is observed for 9, which has negligible (if any) efficacy in the α4-α4 site and also represents the most hydrophobic compound tested.
    We have shown that the carbamate and the imidazolyloxy moieties are bioisosters at α4β2 nAChRs. Compounds 4 and 7 display similar binding affinities at α4β2 receptors and similar potencies and efficacy profiles at both (α4)2(β2)3 and (α4)3(β2)2 stoichiometries, and the bioisosteric properties are supported by similar binding modes as revealed by cocrystal structures with Ls-AChBP. Furthermore, the study has revealed that minor structural changes, such as addition of a methyl group (7 vs 9), can result in stoichiometry specific effects. Along with the structural studies, these data indicate that a rational and structure based approach toward stoichiometry selective orthosteric α4β2 agonists is possible.

    The [3H]epibatidine binding experiments using membranes from HEK293 cell lines stably expressing rat α4β2, α3β4, and α4β4 nAChRs and the [3H]MLA binding experiments with tsA-201 cells transiently expressing the α7/5-HT3A chimera were performed essentially as described before.(24)
    In the [3H]epibatidine binding experiments, the stable HEK293 cell lines were harvested at 80–90% confluency and scraped into assay buffer [140 mM NaCl, 1.5 mM KCl, 2 mM CaCl2, 1 mM Mg2SO4, 25 mM HEPES (pH 7.4)], homogenized using a Polytron for 10 s, and centrifuged for 20 min at 50000g. Cell pellets were resuspended in fresh assay buffer, homogenized, and centrifuged at 50000g for another 20 min. Then the cell pellets were resuspended in the assay buffer, and the cell membranes were incubated with 25 pM [3H]epibatidine in the presence of various concentrations of compounds in a total assay volume of 1500 μL. Nonspecific binding was determined in reactions with 5 mM 3. The mixtures were incubated for 4 h at room temperature while shaking. In the [3H]MLA binding experiments, tsA-201 cells were split into a 10 cm tissue culture plate and transfected the following day with 8 μg of α7/5-HT3A-pCDNA3.1 using Polyfect as a DNA carrier according to the protocol by the manufacturer (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). The day after the transfection the culture medium on the cells was changed, and the following day the [3H]MLA binding assay was performed. Briefly, the cells were scraped into 30 mL of assay buffer [50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.2)], homogenized using a Polytron for 10 s, and centrifuged for 20 min at 50000g. The resulting pellet was homogenized in 30 mL of assay buffer and centrifuged again. Then the cell pellet was resuspended in the assay buffer, and the membranes were incubated with 0.5 nM [3H]MLA and various concentrations of the test compounds. The total reaction volume was 1000 μL, and nonspecific binding was determined in reactions with 5 mM 3. The assay mixtures were incubated for 2.5 h at room temperature while shaking.
    In both binding experiments, Whatman GF/C filters were presoaked for 1 h in a 0.2% polyethyleneimine solution, and binding was terminated by filtration through these filters using a 48-well cell harvester and washing with 3 × 4 mL of ice-cold isotonic NaCl solution. Following this, the filters were dried, 3 mL of Opti-Fluor (Packard) was added, and the amount of bound radioactivity was determined in a scintillation counter. The fraction of specifically bound radioligand was always <10% of the total amount of radioligand. The binding experiments were performed in duplicate at least three times for each compound. Binding affinities at Ls-AChBP were estimated as reported by Rohde et al.(18) by displacement of [3H]epibatidine (PerkinElmer Life Sciences) at Ls-AChBP linked to the transmembrane domain of the 5-HT3A receptor and expressed in HEK293.

    Electrophysiology

    Xenopus laevis oocyte preparation and electrophysiological experiments were performed as described previously.(34) Briefly, isolated oocytes were obtained by defolliculating lobes from ovaries of female adult Xenopus laevis frogs using collagenase. Oocytes were injected with a total of ∼25 ng of cRNA encoding human wild-type α4 and β2 subunits in a 1:4 or 4:1 ratio and incubated for 2–7 days at 15–18 °C in modified Barth’s solution (90 mM NaCl, 1.0 mM KCl, 0.66 mM NaNO3, 2.4 mM NaHCO3, 10 mM HEPES, 2.5 mM sodium pyruvate, 0.74 mM CaCl2, 0.82 mM MgCl2, 100 μg/mL gentamycin and pH adjusted to 7.5). TEVC electrophysiological measurements were performed using a custom-built system. Ligands were dissolved in OR2 (90 mM NaCl, 2.5 mM KCl, 2.5 mM CaCl2, 1.0 mM MgCl2, 5.0 mM HEPES and pH adjusted to 7.5), and solutions were applied directly to the oocytes via a glass capillary tube placed in the vicinity of the cell to ensure rapid solution exchange (few seconds). Each application of ligand lasted ∼1 min. The protocol included dynamic adjustments of washing periods (3–6 min) to ensure full recovery between applications at high agonist concentrations.

    FLIPR Membrane Potential (FMP) Blue Assay

    Compounds 7 and 9 were characterized functionally at the α3β4-HEK293(35) cell lines in the FMP assay essentially as previously described.(24, 25) In brief, cells were split into poly-d-lysine-coated 96-well black Opti-plates (BD Biosciences, Bedford, MA) in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle medium supplemented with penicillin (100 U/mL), streptomycin (100 μg/mL), and 10% fetal calf serum, supplemented with 0.7 mg/mL G-418. At 16–24 h later the medium was aspirated, and the cells were washed with 100 μL of Krebs buffer [140 mM NaCl, 4.7 mM KCl, 2.5 mM CaCl2, 1.2 mM MgCl2, 11 mM HEPES, 10 mM d-glucose, pH 7.4]. Then 50 μL of Krebs buffer was added to the wells (in the antagonist experiments, various concentrations of the antagonist were dissolved in the buffer), and then an additional 50 μL of Krebs buffer supplemented with the FMP assay dye (1 mg/mL) was added to each well. Then the plate was incubated at 37 °C in a humidified 5% CO2 incubator for 30 min and assayed in a NOVOstar plate reader (BMG Labtechnologies, Offenburg, Germany) measuring emission (in fluorescence units, FU) at 560 nm caused by excitation at 530 nm before and up to 1 min after addition of 33 μL of agonist solution. The experiments were performed in duplicate at least three times for each compound at the receptor.

    X-ray Crystallography

    Ls-AChBP was expressed and purified as previously described.(16, 18)Ls-AChBP was cocrystallized with ligands 4 and 7 by the hanging drop vapor diffusion technique at 20 °C with a reservoir volume of 0.5 mL. The protein solution contained 6.24 mg/mL Ls-AChBP in 20 mM Tris, 20 mM NaCl, pH 8.0, and was mixed with the actual ligand dissolved in water to a final protein and ligand concentration of 2.86 mg/mL and 50 mM, respectively. Crystals were grown at room temperature in drops containing 1 μL of ligand–protein solution and 1 μL of reservoir solution. For 4, the reservoir solution contained 0.1 M Tris and 2.0 M (NH4)2SO4 at pH 8.5, and plate-like crystals grew within 1 month. For 7, the reservoir solution contained 0.1 M Tris, 2.0 M (NH4)2SO4, and 2% PEG400 at pH 8.5, and rodlike crystals appeared in the drop within 6 months. Crystals were flash-cooled in liquid nitrogen after transfer to cryoprotectant solution. For 4, the cryoprotectant solution was the same as the reservoir solution plus 20% of glycerol while using a standard cryoprotectant solution for the complex with 7 containing 0.1 M Tris, 2.0 M (NH4)2SO4, 1% PEG400 at pH 8.0, and 20% of glycerol.

Diffraction data of Ls-AChBP in complex with 7 were collected at beamline 14.1, Bessy II, Helmholtz Zentrum Berlin, Germany, equipped with a MX-225 detector and at a fixed wavelength of 0.9184 Å.
    Complete data sets were collected to 2.48 and 2.19 Å resolution for complexes with 4 and 7, respectively. Diffraction data of Ls-AChBP in complex with 4 were processed with MOSFLM and Scala in the CCP4 software package,(36) while XDS(37) was used for the complex with 7. For crystal data and data collection statistics, see Table 3.
    Both structures were solved by molecular replacement using Phaser.(38) The coordinates of one protein pentamer from Ls-AChBP in complex with 2 was used as search model (PBD code 1UV6) for obtaining initial phases of the complex with 4. The structure of Ls-AChBP in complex with 7 was solved in a similar manner but using the complex with 4 as search model (PBD code 3ZDG). The results clearly showed four and two pentamers in the asymmetric unit of the crystals of Ls-AChBP with 4 and 7, respectively.
    Initial model building was done using the autobuild function in PHENIX(39) and then inspected and manually corrected in COOT.(40) Subsequent refinement was done in PHENIX applying NCS restraints and individual isotropic B-factors. A manual inspection and correction round in COOT followed each refinement cycle. Low energy ligand conformations of 4 and 7 were generated in MAESTRO (Maestro, version 9.1, Schrödinger, LLC, New York, NY, 2010) with a positive charge on the tertiary amine and compared to the ligand densities by visual inspection. The best ligand conformations were chosen, and constraints for the ligands were then generated using eLBOW,(41) keeping the ligand geometry obtained in MAESTRO.
    Water molecules were added automatically in PHENIX and inspected in COOT. Additionally, sulfate ions, PEG monomers, PEG400, and N-acetylglucosamine molecules were added to the structure in COOT. Amino acid residues in disordered regions were excluded during refinement because of poor electron densities. This especially applies to a region in the flexible loop F (Thr155-Glu163) and the C-terminus (Lys204-Leu210) of Ls-AChBP. Hence, the residues in these regions have in most circumstances been omitted in the final model structures. As an exception, a well-defined loop F was observed in chain C of the complex with 4 where Thr156-Asn158 packs against the loop C residues (Ser182-Thr184) in chain G of a neighboring pentamer. Though no differences were observed in binding mode or loop closure, the involved interfaces BC and GF were omitted from the structural analysis.
    A final validation of the structures was carried out by using the programs MolProbity(42) and PROCHECK.(43) The atomic coordinates and structure factor amplitudes have been deposited with the RCSB Protein Data Bank (PBD codes 3ZDG and 3ZDH).
    Comparison of the relative binding modes of the ligands were done in PyMOL (The PyMOL Molecular Graphics System, version 1.4.1, Schrödinger, LLC.) by superimposing the β-sheets of one pentamer of each complex with 4 and 7.
