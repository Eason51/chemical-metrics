    Abstract

    Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is a highly invasive and aggressive malignant glioma. Current treatment modalities are unable to significantly prolong survival in patients diagnosed with glioblastoma, so more effective strategies of antitumor treatments are in urgent demand. Here, we found that lysosomal sulfatase expression was significantly correlated with poor prognosis of GBM. Hence, a new probe, MNG, was developed with a new protocol utilizing glucose groups to detect lysosomal sulfatase. It also exhibits potential for monitoring GBM cells, depending on the hyperactive lysosomal sulfatase expression of tumor cells. Meantime, we identified that sulbactam as the first reported lysosomal sulfatase inhibitor inhibits the tumor growth of GBM. Collectively, our work highlights that lysosomal sulfatase was detected using a new protocol and its potential as a therapeutic target in GBM and reveals a distinct mechanism that sulbactam inhibits cell proliferation related to lysosomal sulfatase, indicating that sulbactam could be a promising therapeutic agent against GBM.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is a malignant neoplasm of the central nervous system that is the most common primary adult gliomas, remaining universally fatal with a lower disease-free survival (DFS).(1−3) This is due to the current drugs failing to effectively inhibit malignant cells, which will eventually lead to disease relapse. At present, the antitumor therapy is temozolomide (TMZ) chemotherapy or exposing the tumor to multiple types of drugs, for example, temozolomide and procarbazine in the first-recurrence patients of GBM are used in clinic.(4) However, it does not significantly increase the survival time of patients due to enhanced proliferation, chemoresistance, and invasive potential. Clearly, choosing better treatment drugs and developing a novel therapeutic target make the cure of GBM more challenging. Current research studies focus on the molecular mechanisms involved in GBM development and progression, while studies on the fundamental roles of cellular metabolism in tumor development and progression are not given more emphasis. Here, we found that the enzyme associated with the survival rate of GBM was lysosomal sulfatase through data mining. Sulfatases catalyzing the hydrolysis of sulfate esters ubiquitously have been implicated in the onset of various pathophysiological conditions, including lysosomal storage disorders and developmental abnormalities. Unsurprisingly, the enzymes that catalyze these reactions are involved in human diseases, making them important drug targets. Hence, it may be a new strategy of inhibiting sulfatase activity in cancer cells that could restrict tumor progression, blocking cell growth on GBM cells, and would hold great therapeutic promise in improving GBM treatment efficacy.
    Achieving tumor cell-targeted detection and new drug therapy is of particular significance in GBM treatment, which is emerging as the next important direction for GBM treatment. First, it is critical to monitor the expression levels of lysosomal sulfatase during treatment of GBM. Recently, techniques such as polymerase chain reaction (PCR) have been used to assess lysosomal sulfatase activity.(5) However, the method fails to present enough activity detection due to genomic complexity, and fluorescence probes are mostly devoted to aryl sulfatase,(6) which may not be suitable metabolic sites, so a suitable tool is extremely urgent and so is a new drug therapy to treat GBM. In our study, a probe is required to monitor lysosomal sulfatase; fluorescent detection of lysosomal sulfatase was characterized using naphthalene anhydride dye modified with water-soluble glucose groups. It is a new protocol to abandon the traditional metabolic sites and replace them with glucose groups to detect sulfatase. The newly designed MNG showed the intense fluorescence in tumor cells depending on the overexpression of lysosomal sulfatase in tumors. Therefore, MNG is demonstrated to detect sulfatase, which holds significance in continuously researching the disease progression and treatment responses. Then, we have found an effective compound—sulbactam, a lysosomal sulfatase inhibitor. It is demonstrated to target the lysosomal sulfatase proteins and inhibit activities to suppress cell growth, resulting in decreased GBM progress. Sulbactam exerts its excellent efficiency in GBM by selectively inhibiting lysosomal sulfatase and prolonging the survival of GBM cells.

    Data Mining of Lysosomal Sulfatase

    Sulfatases were thought to be primarily confined to the degradation of organic sulfates in soil throughout the 20th century; then, it was discovered that sulfatase deficiencies also led to a number of physiological disorders in body. Indeed, six physiological disorders in humans are known to arise from the accumulation of toxic levels of non-degradable sulfated compounds, owing to deficiencies of single lysosomal sulfatase.(7) Sulfatases are a highly conserved family of enzymes that catalyze the hydrolysis of sulfate ester bonds from a wide variety of substrates, ranging from small cytosolic steroids, such as estrogen sulfate, to complex cell-surface carbohydrates, such as glycosaminoglycans. In the body, the secretory sites of sulfatases are targeted, such as transport to the extracellular matrix (ECM), endoplasmic reticulum (ER), Golgi complex, and lysosome. At present, 17 genes encoding human sulfatases have been reported in different subcellular locations, six of which are distributed in lysosomes.(8) The lysosomal sulfatases were the first to be associated with physiological functions, which may have dysregulation in tumors; we hypothesized that abnormal expression of lysosomal sulfatase is a factor affecting tumors. We found that the lysosomal sulfatase genes are abnormally expressed in GBM cells in The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) and The Genotype-Tissue Expression (GTEx) studies (Figure 1A). The differential expression of lysosomal sulfatase was proved by principal component analysis (Figure 1B). To further validate the clinical significance of lysosomal sulfatase in human tumors, suspecting that the low survival rate is due to the overall higher expression of lysosomal sulfatase in tumors, we explored the potential relevance of sulfatase diversity to survival in a GBM cohort composed of individuals (Figures 1C, S1, Supporting Information). Compared with non-lysosome sulfatase, Kaplan–Meier survival of lysosomal sulfatase analyses demonstrated a correlation between poor prognosis and the high lysosomal sulfatase expression (log-rank test p = 0.0013) within GBMs in TCGA databases. The result suggests that the different expression of lysosomal sulfatase is meaningful to GBM survival and treatment.

    Evaluation of Fluorescence Probe Properties

    At present, there is no suitable tool to detect lysosomal sulfatase activities; most were radioactive labeling, electrophoresis, and polymerase chain reaction of a single gene. The existing commercial probes, monitoring the activity of sulfatase, focus on the exploration of arylsulfatase and lack the subcellular organelle location.(9,10) To address this issue, we thus developed a highly stable and selective probe for lysosomal sulfatase, named as MNG, which was designed to monitor the sulfatase activity by means of fluorescence change. It breaks the traditional determination of sulfate esterase and replaces sulfate ester with a glucose group as the active site. Specifically, the morpholinyl-locating group binds to the dye,(11) and the glucose was further conjugated to the fluorophore to develop into a specific activity site (Figure 2A). It is a new protocol to detect sulfate using glycose groups, considering that the sulfatase may have a glycol metabolism possibility. To gain insights into possible modes of probe binding, we performed docking experiments with the probe. Through molecular dynamics, we modeled a ligand at the sulfatase active position with the greatest affinity for the structure and validated this structure of probe through composite omit electron density maps (Figure 2B). The glucose moiety of the probe is stabilized with hydrogen bonds with the side chains of N133, K135, H145, N147, D235, and R239 (Figure 2C). The details of the active site strongly support a substrate–enzyme model.
    To verify the feasibility of the probe, the probe was synthesized (Figure 2A, Schedule S1·Supporting Information). First, we investigated the reactivity of MNG toward sulfatase under physiological simulation conditions. As can be seen, changes in spectra were observed toward the reaction (Figures 2D,E, S2, Supporting Information), showing significant fluorescence enhancement (ex/em = 450/568) with a red-shifted absorption from 360 to 450 nm; this change was caused by the formation of new hydrolytic products. Meanwhile, metabolism was validated by HPLC-MS (Figure S3·Supporting Information), indicating a response of MNG under the sulfatase as we anticipated. Next, the selectivity of MNG for sulfatase in the presence of related enzymes was examined. The fluorescence was significantly enhanced only with sulfatase, indicating that the probe has excellent specificity for sulfatase over other interference species (Figure 2F). Meantime, the fluorescence response of the probe has no significant decrease toward various biological molecules over physiological concentration (Figure S4), indicating that other biological molecules have no obvious interference. The pH effect and stability were further validated (Figures S5 and 6), which are suitable for sulfatase detection in living systems. By spectral analysis, the response of MNG shows that the time dependence and fluorescence reached a plateau after 30 min (Figure S7·Supporting Information). It is worth noting that there was a good linear correlation between fluorescence intensity and sulfatase concentrations, with the low detection limit of 0.131 mU/mL (Figure S8·Supporting Information), illustrating that the probe can detect small fluctuations of sulfatase in vitro. In addition, compared to the reported data (Table S1·Supporting Information), the kinetic parameters Vmax and Km were determined as 70.93 nmol/min/mg and 21.60 μM, respectively, indicating that MNG can monitor sulfatase with strong affinity (Figure 2G). Collectively, these data validate the proof of concept for the MNG design as an efficient turn-on probe, which shows the potential outstanding performance under physiological conditions and can be applied to detect trace amounts of sulfatase in vitro.

    Inhibitor Screening and Validation

    Current research studies on the inhibitor of sulfatases have focused on the activity of estrone sulfatase (ARSC). The upregulation of ARSC has been noted in several forms of hormone-dependent cancer, in particular, small-molecule therapeutics aimed at ARSC inhibition.(12) Several potent inhibitors with various scaffolds have been developed, such as EMATE and STX64.(13) However, these representative inhibitors were individually tested using the designed fluorescence probe; the data have revealed that the compounds as the reported inhibitors have not produced a distinct decrease in the fluorescence signal (Figures 3A, S9·Supporting Information), suggesting that these compounds were not suitable for sulfatase as inhibitors toward the designed probe. Hence, the need for potent lysosomal sulfatase inhibitors for further biological research or tumor treatment is urgent.
    Toward this goal, we conducted an initial screening using a collection of about 500 drugs with MNG; the inhibitory activity of the drug toward sulfatase was examined with the fluorescent assay, and the percentage of drug inhibition calculated from the relative loss of fluorescence was observed. Only about 13% of the drugs inhibited over 50% of sulfatase activity (Figure 3B). Then, more in-depth kinetic studies can be executed on selected drugs using MNG after excluding the possible interference of ion-containing drugs. Further screening with the probe showed that sulbactam has significant potential to inhibit sulfatase with IC50 values of 3.5 μM (Figure 3C,D); kinetic measurements confirmed the selectivity and potency of this inhibitor. In addition, it is necessary to explore the applicability of probes in the human body on the detection of sulfatase because of the complexity of human metabolic enzymes, so we performed a detailed experiment on human complex-metabolizing enzymes using MNG (Figure 3E). The results confirmed the superior applicability of the probe for sulfatase. Meanwhile, kinetic parameters were further determined in human liver S9 (Figure 3F, Table S1·Supporting Information), which showed affinity strongly.

    Sulbactam Inhibits Lysosomal Sulfatase In Vitro

    Encouraged by the excellent properties of MNG in vitro, MNG was further used in living cells to investigate whether the sulbactam can inhibit the activity of sulfatase. In situ imaging of sulfatase was performed in living cells with MNG to evaluate the efficacy of MNG to visualize sulfatase in living cells. First, since the probe contains repellency and toxicity, the cytotoxicity of MNG against T98G cells was examined by standard MTT assays (Table S2·Supporting Information), exhibiting the lower cytotoxicity and the good biocompatibility of the probe. T98G cells displayed almost no emission signal in the absence of MNG. T98G cells displayed a remarkable cellular fluorescence enhancement in the presence of MNG, and significant fluorescence reduction was observed from sulbactam-treated cells clearly (Figure 4A), indicating that MNG could be easily loaded into T98G cells as a small-molecular probe, and the strong fluorescence of T98G cells is attributed to the metabolism of sulfatase with MNG.
    To further determine the intracellular localization of MNG, commercial Lyso-Tracker Red labeling was used. As shown in Figure 4B, MNG and Lyso-Tracker Red resulted in the localization of red and green fluorescent puncta, respectively, as indicated by puncta emitting yellow fluorescence. It indicates that the red signal from the Lyso-Tracker Red was greatly overlapped with the green signal from the probe (Pearson correlation coefficient = 0.86) in T98G cells. The explanation is that after the metabolism of MNG, the free MN was still retained in the lysosomes. The distribution tendency can be explained by the fact that a compound with a located group significantly increases the localization in lysosomes. We therefore conclude that MNG is an appropriate tool for sulfatase activity studies in living cell, and sulbactam is described as an effective inhibitor of sulfatase.

    Sulbactam Inhibits Cell Proliferation

    In recent years, more drugs were developed besides TMZ, such as entrectinib,(14) lomustine,(15) paxalisib,(16) and larotrectinib.(17) The sulfatase activity determination with the probe was not inhibited by the abovementioned drugs (Figure 4C), indicating that these drugs treating GBM are not through the sulfatase pathway, while sulbactam has an obvious inhibitory effect, declaring that sulbactam is a therapeutic candidate worthy of further development. Sulbactam has long been characterized as exhibiting remarkable antimicrobial effects and has not hitherto been applied in pathological GBM or other brain diseases. We link sulfatase with sulbactam-induced treatment for the first time. Although this has broken through our traditional cognition of sulbactam, it remains unknown whether sulbactam treatment is involved in the antitumor effect in GBM.
    To verify the feasibility and the effectiveness of therapeutic medicines, MNG was selected for assessing the relationship between sulfatase activity and sulbactam therapy. We cultured T98G cells in vitro to investigate the effects of sulbactam on cell growth and proliferation; the percentage of cell viability by MTT assay showed that sulbactam caused remarkable proliferation inhibition with a fraction from 34 to 41% approximately depending on its concentration from 3.5 to 350 μM after 48 h of incubation in T98G cells (Figure 4d). With regard to the action of drug, the result clarified the impact sulbactam induced on proliferation inhibition. Additionally, through in-depth in vitro experiments, we investigated in great detail the potential mechanisms underlying the antitumor effects of sulbactam. To investigate the changes in sulfatase activity on T98G cells, the probe was applied to monitor the activity of the sulfatase through the alteration of fluorescence of the cell. Sulbactam as the sulfatase inhibitor was used to inhibit sulfatase; the determination of the intensity of cellular fluorescence was done, as depicted in Figure 4e. As suspected, we observed that the fluorescence of cells decreased with sulbactam treatment in cell lines. Hence, our findings established that sulbactam inhibits cell proliferation related to lysosomal sulfatase, which could be a promising therapeutic agent against GBM.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Overall, our work may provide an entirely new mechanism based on the hyperactive sulfatase in GBM cells. We found that GBM cells may be dependent on the overexpression of lysosomal sulfatase for decreased survival. It will be an innovative opportunity to discover a potent drug through lysosomal sulfatase. However, as there is a lack of tools for lysosomal sulfatase monitoring in the body, our study developed a new protocol with glycose groups to detect sulfatase. The MNG as a convenient tool might investigate the pathology and therapeutic function of lysosomal sulfatase in GBM. With sulbactam as the first reported lysosomal sulfatase inhibitor, we found that sulbactam is able to inhibit sulfatase activity with the growth phenomenon of glioblastoma hindered. A probable strategy for GBM treatment with a lysosomal sulfatase inhibitor, sulbactam, indicates a new opportunity to improve GBM clinical treatment efficiency. Hopefully, this work gives us insights into the importance of lysosomal sulfatase and contributes to further research on the future clinical application of sulbactam.

    Data Mining

    Raw counts of RNA sequencing data and the corresponding clinical information were obtained from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) dataset (https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov/) in January 2020, in which the method of acquisition and application was complied with the guidelines and policies. The survival analysis with log-rank test was also used to compare the survival difference between the abovementioned two groups. For Kaplan–Meier curves, p-values and the hazard ratio (HR) with a 95% confidence interval (CI) were generated by log-rank tests and univariate Cox proportional hazard regression. All analytical methods mentioned above and R packages were performed using GEPIA2 analysis.(18)p < 0.05 was considered as statistically significant.

    Molecular Simulation

    The detail of the sulfatase crystal structure (ID: 5FQL) was obtained from the structure in the protein data bank (http://www.rcsb.org/pdb/home/home. do). The virtual screening was done using AutoDock Vina.(19) In this way, nine models were finally selected as docked conformations for the ligand, and the representative model from each group was determined by the relative binding energy (Table S3·Supporting Information). The model with the lowest binding energy was selected as the starting structures of the sulfatase–substrate complex. The ligand was optimized at the level of B3LYP theory with the 6-31+g(2d,p) basis set using the Gaussian09 package.(20) We employed the Amber16 packages with the casual force field ff14SB(21) to add the missing hydrogen atoms and optimize their positions.
