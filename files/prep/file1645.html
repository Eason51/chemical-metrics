    Abstract

    The overexpression of NIK plays a critical role in liver inflammatory diseases. Treatment of such diseases with small-molecule NIK inhibitors is a reasonable but underexplored approach. In this paper, we reported the discovery of a potent and selective NIK inhibitor 46 (XT2). 46 inhibited the NIK kinase with an IC50 value of 9.1 nM in vitro, and it also potently suppressed NIK activities in intact cells. In isogenic primary hepatocytes, treatment of 46 efficiently suppressed the expressions of NIK-induced genes. 46 was orally bioavailable in mice with moderate systemic exposure. In a NIK-associated mouse liver inflammation model, 46 suppressed CCl4-induced upregulation of ALT, a key biomarker of acute liver injury. 46 also decreased immune cell infiltration into the injured liver tissue. Overall, these studies provide examples that an NIK inhibitor is able to suppress toxin-induced liver inflammations, which indicates its therapeutic potentials for the treatment of liver inflammatory diseases.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    NF-κB-inducing kinase, also known as NIK or MAP3K14, is a key regulator of noncanonical NF-κB signaling pathway.(1−6) Upon ligand binding of the TNF family receptors such as BAFFR, CD40, RANKL, and TWEAK, the receptor-associated TRAF3 degrades and consequently releases NIK from the TRAF3-TRAF2-cIAP1/2 complex.(7,8) The released NIK is stabilized and activated, which in turn phorsphorates the Ser176 residue of IKKα.(9) Activation of NIK and IKKα results in phosphorylation of the Ser866 and Ser870 residues of p100, subsequent proteolytic cleavage of p100, and production of p52.(3,10,11) Eventually, p52 and RELB form a heterodimer and translocate into the nucleus, where they initiate target gene expression.(7) The aberrant stabilization and activation of NIK correlate with augmented activities of NF-κB2, which are often associated with autoimmune diseases,(12) inflammatory diseases,(13−15) and cancers.(16,17) Therefore, inhibition of NIK has been pursued as an attractive strategy for the treatment of systemic lupus erythematosus,(18) inflammatory disease,(13,19−21) multiple myeloma,(22) and pancreatic cancer,(23) among others.
    Previously, a few NIK inhibitors have been reported and several selected examples are shown in Figure 1. Compound 1 inhibited NIK with an IC50 value of 4.2 nM, and the cocrystal structure of NIK/1 complex has been reported, showing the details of ligand-protein interacting features.(24) In addition, 2 and 3 have also been shown to exhibit inhibitory activities for NIK in enzymatic and cellular assays.(25) Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that 4 suppressed NIK activities with an IC50 value in the pM range and was efficacious in animal models of systemic lupus erythematosus.(18,26) In addition, 5(27) and 6(28) have also been reported as good NIK inhibitors.
    Overexpression of NIK plays a critical role in liver inflammatory diseases(13,20) and B/T cell malfunction related diseases.(18,29−31) Treatment of such diseases with small-molecule NIK inhibitors is a reasonable but underexplored approach.(3,32) In this paper, we report our efforts to design and synthesize a very potent and highly selective small-molecule NIK inhibitor 46 (XT2), which has anti-inflammatory effects in vitro and in vivo.

    Results and Disscussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    On the basis of information showcased in the cocrystal structure of NIK/1,(24) we envisioned that a 7H-pyrrolo[2,3-d]pyrimidine moiety could possibly mimic the structural function of the pyrimidine moiety of 1; therefore the 7H-pyrrolo[2,3-d]pyrimidine-containing compounds could be a new class of NIK inhibitors (Table 1). Upon extensive optimizations, we eventually found a hit compound 7, which features a benzene ring as a replacement of the 2,3-dihydroindole core of 1. In a kinase ATP consumption assay, 7 inhibited NIK kinase activity with an IC50 value of 303 nM.

    Further optimization of 7 with various substitutions on the benzene ring yielded compounds 8–23 (Table 1). The fluorine substitutions (8–10) were tolerated in terms of NIK inhibition, but no significant improvement was observed compared with that of 7. 11 had an OMe group as R1, which was detrimental for its potency. 12 had an OMe group as R3 and had a similar IC50 value as that 7, suggesting that the R3 position tolerates substitution groups. A similar trend was observed for methyl-group-substituted 13 and 14 with the latter had comparable potency as that of 7. Regarding two CN-containing analogues, 15 was 10-fold more potent than 16, which suggests that the R1 position might tolerate a basic group. However, large basic groups or soluble groups were incompatible at the R1 position (17–20). Different substitutions on the 2-amine-5H-pyrrolo[3,2-d]-pyrimidin moiety (21, 22) were also examined, but neither yielded more potent compounds. 23 had an IC50 value of 3837 nM, 12-fold less potent than that of 7, highlighting that 1,3-disubstitution pattern of the benzene ring was critical for optimal inhibition of NIK activity. Overall, 8, 9, and 15 had slightly improved enzymatic activity, and the beneficial effect of the CN group of 15 suggests that a small basic group might be beneficial for the inhibition of NIK enzymatic activities.
    In order to know the NIK binding mode of 7, molecular docking studies have been carried out. The cocrystal structure of 1/NIK (PDB code 4G3E) was selected as a docking template, and a putative binding mode was generated by Glide program. The docking results show that 7 bound to the active site of NIK and was partially superimposed on 1 (Figure 2A). In addition, one hydrogen bond (H-bond) was observed between the 7 and the hinge region Glu472, and three additional H-bonds were observed between 7 and the DFG region Glu442, Asp536, and Phe537 (Figure 2B). Interestingly, the carboxylic moiety of Asp536 was close to the benzene ring of 7 (3.5 Å, Figure 2C), which partially explained why the CN group of 15 was beneficial for good potency.
    Given the insights provided by the molecular docking studies, we incorporated a pyridine ring into the molecules to make compounds 24–40, which were summarized in Table 2. Interestingly, 24 had an IC50 value of 22.4 ± 0.3 nM, which was comparable to that of the control 1. Replacement of the thiazole on 24 with an oxazole or a pyrazine group (25 and 26) did not improve the activity. When the thiazole group was replaced by a five-membered or six-membered lactam group, 27 and 28 exhibited potent inhibition of NIK activity as that of 1. Meanwhile, other cyclic aliphatic rings were less tolerated (29–34), highlighting the importance of the lactam structures.

    The incorporation of fluorine on the pyridine ring did not enhance the activity of 36, and an ethyl group was not tolerated at the propargylic position of 35 with 100-fold decrease compared to 24. Importantly, the N position in pyridine was critical for the high potency of 24 because a close analogue 37 was about 83-fold less potent than 24. Removal of one N from the 2-aminopyrimidine ring yielded compound 38, which is 95-fold less potent than 27. Addition of a methyl or ethyl substitution on the 2-aminopyrimidine ring was detrimental for the kinase inhibitory activity as both 39 and 40 had weaker IC50 values compared with that of 27. These data suggest that the 2-aminopyrimidine is critical for good NIK inhibitory activities. Overall, compounds 24, 27, and 28 showed good inhibitory activities of the NIK kinase because of the incorporation of a nitrogen atom.
    We next studied the pharmacokinetic properties of 27 in mice. Unfortunately, it had oral bioavailability less than 1%, probably because of instability of the triple bond caused by the pyridine moiety.(26) In line with these, compound 24 was unstable in pH = 1 aqueous solution and also had very poor pharmacokinetic properties in mice. We then introduced a similar pyrrole-fused-pyrimidine core structure 41 (Table 3), hoping that improved pharmacokinetic profiles could be obtained with a different core structure. Subsequently, molecular docking studies have been performed on 41 to evaluate how it could possibly bind NIK (Figure 3). The docking results show that the thiazole, alcohol, and pyrimidine moieties of 41 and 1 all had the same poses, whereas the benzene of 41 and the indoline of 1 overlapped. The 2-aminopyrimidine moiety of 41 had two H-bonds with the hinge region Glu472 and Leu474. Additional three H-bonds were observed between the tertiary alcohol moiety of 41 and the DFG region Asp536, Phe537, and Glu442. Overall, the molecular docking results show that 41 could be well accommodated by the active site of NIK.

    Therefore, 41 was synthesize and it inhibited NIK with an IC50 value of 218 nM. Further optimization of the benzene ring with a fluorine or OMe substitution provided compounds 42–44 and 47–49. Among these compounds, 44 was the most potent molecule (IC50 = 85.5 ± 26.1 nM). A methyl group at the R4 position (50) was found to be detrimental compared with 44. We have also determined that compound 46 with R absolute stereochemistry was the active enantiomer of 44, which inhibited NIK with an IC50 value of 9.1 ± 2.0 nM. In contrast, the S enantiomer 45 has an IC50 value of 2230 ± 220 nM, >240-fold less potent than its enantiomer 46.
    In addition to the modifications discussed above, it has been shown that proper substitutions of the propargyl alcohol moiety are critical for better NIK inhibitory activities.(25,26) We therefore set out to optimize the thiazole group of 41, and the results are summarized in Table 4. Replacement of the thiazole with an oxazole (51), a pyrazine (52), or an α-OMe-pyridine group (53) did not improve the NIK inhibitory activity. In addition, the tertiary alcohol conformation was found to be essential as a secondary alcohol-containing compound 54 was much less potent. Replacement of the thiazole with a cyclohexyl group (55) or a methyl group (56) was not tolerated. Compounds 57 and 58 tethering cyclic five-membered rings did not show improved activities, while compounds 59 and 60 tethering amides were inactive within assay range.

    Interestingly, fluorine-containing compound 61 inhibited NIK with an IC50 value of 5.9 ± 0.8 nM, which is more potent than that of 1. Overall, through the examination of compounds 41–61, we obtained compound 44, its R enantiomer 46, and 61, which were stable in acidic aqueous media and show potent inhibition of the NIK activity.
    We next verified the NIK inhibitory activity of 46 with the aid of a commercial kinase assay (KINOMEscan). 46 had an IC50 value of Kd = 4.9 ± 1.0 nM (Figure 4), which was consistent with our in-house kinase assay data.
    In order to profile the kinase selectivities of 46, we screened it in a panel of 98 kinases in a KINOMEscan assay, and the results are summarized in Figure 5 and Table S1 in the Supporting Information. The data showed that 46 was a very selective kinase inhibitor and it had no apparent affinity against other 97 kinases at 500 nM concentration.
    Previous biological studies(3,10,11,32) have proven that activation of NIK led to the phosphorylation of the Ser866 and Ser870 residues of p100, subsequent proteolytic cleavage of p100, and thus production of p52. In order to examine whether inhibition of NIK will halt the production of p52, the primary hepatocytes were infected with p100 and NIK adenovirus in the presence of 46 or a positive control compound 1 (B022).(19) As shown in Figure 6, the concentrations of two proteins, NIK and p100, were high in the double adenovirus-infected cells, whereas the level of p52 was also high as the consequence of the NIK mediated proteolysis of p100. In the presence of 46, the level of p52 dose-dependently decreased, indicating the blockade of p100 proteolysis caused by inhibition of the cellular NIK activity. The control compound B022 (5 μM) showed the same cellular effects as that of 46 at 10 μM. In addition, the protein levels of cellular NIK were not affected by treatment of either 46 or B022, suggesting the down-regulation of p52 was not caused by decreasing of NIK protein levels. Overall, these data suggest that 46 effectively inhibited cellular NIK kinase activities.
    Next, we examined whether 46 inhibits NIK-induced expression of inflammatory genes in primary hepatocytes (Figure 7). The primary hepatocytes were infected with NIK adenovirus in the presence of different concentrations of 46 (0, 1, or 10 μM). As shown in Figure 7, the expression levels of NIK-induced genes,(19) TNFα, CCL2, CCL5, CXCL5, and NOS2, decreased dose-dependently in the presence of 46 in the primary hepatocytes infected with the NIK adenovirus. These data indicate that 46 inhibited the kinase activities of cellular NIK and therefore suppressed the expression of NIK-induced genes.
    We further evaluated the pharmacokinetic properties of 46 in order to know the systemic exposure of 46 in mice (Table 5). Upon intravenous injection of a single dose of 46 at 5 mg/kg in mice, the values of Cmax and AUC achieved in plasma were 3040 ng/mL and 891 h·ng/mL, respectively, which indicate moderate systemic exposure but fast clearance rate of 46. Upon oral administration of a single dose of 46 at 15 mg/kg in mice, the values of Cmax and AUC achieved in plasma were 510 ng/mL and 348 h·ng/mL, respectively, and the oral bioavailability was relatively low at 13%. We have also examined the pharmacokinetic properties of 61; unfortunately, it had even lower oral systemic exposure with 7% oral bioavailability (data not shown).

    In view of the anti-inflammatory activities of 46 in the primary hepatocytes and its moderate systemic exposure, we proceeded to evaluate its in vivo anti-inflammatory effects in a CCl4-induced mouse liver injury model (Figure 8). Previous studies have demonstrated that the NIK mRNA level and p52 protein level were upregulated in mouse liver exposed to CCl4,(19) which indicates strong association between activation of NIK/p52/NF-kB2 and CCl4-induced liver injury. The same mouse model had been adopted in the present study and in C57BL/6 mice; 46 was intravenously administrated at 40 mg/kg dose every 3 h following the intraperitoneal injection of CCl4. The blood samples and liver tissues were harvested at 12 h for analysis, and the data are summarized in Figure 8 and Supporting Information Figure S2. The serum activity of alanine aminotransferase (ALT, a biomarker for acute liver injury) was measured, and the 46-treated group had lower ALT levels compared with that of the untreated vehicle group (Figure 8, panel A). In addition, the 46-treated group had lower expression levels of NIK-induced genes,(19) TNFα, NOS2, CCL5, CXCL5, and CCL2, in liver tissues compared with that of the untreated vehicle group (panel B). These data indicate that 46 inhibited the kinase activities of cellular NIK and therefore suppressed the expression of NIK-induced genes in vivo.
    In addition, immunofluorescent detection of the macrophage-specific antigen F4/80(34) in the mouse liver tissues showed that the 46-treated group had less immune cell infiltration compared with that of the untreated vehicle group (Figure 9). The B022-treated group had the same and slightly stronger effects compared with that of 46. Overall, these results indicate that small-molecule NIK inhibitor 46 has suppressive effects on the CCl4-induced liver inflammation.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we have discovered and optimized a series of 5H-pyrrolo[3,2-d]pyrimidin-containing compounds, which yielded a very potent and highly selective small-molecule NIK inhibitor 46. 46 inhibited NIK kinase with an IC50 value of 9.1 nM in vitro and also potently suppressed NIK activities in intact cells. In isogenic primary hepatocytes, treatment of 46 efficiently suppressed the expressions of NIK-induced genes, such as TNFα, CCL2, CCL5, CXCL5, and NOS2, which are all liver inflammation related. 46 was orally bioavailable in mice with moderate systemic exposure. In a NIK-associated mouse liver injury model, 46 suppressed CCl4-induced upregulation of ALT, a key biomarker of acute liver injury. 46 also decreased immune cell infiltration into the injured liver tissue. Overall, these studies provide examples that a NIK inhibitor is able to suppress toxin-induced liver inflammations, which indicates its therapeutic potentials for the treatment of liver inflammatory diseases.

    1. General Methods

    Unless otherwise indicated, all commercial reagents and anhydrous solvents were used without further purifications. 1H NMR spectra were recorded with a Bruker Advance NMR 400 spectrometer and calibrated with tetramethylsilane (δ = 0 ppm), DMSO-d6 (δ = 2.5 ppm), MeOH-d4 (δ = 3.31 ppm), or CDCl3 (δ = 7.26 ppm). 13C NMR spectra were obtained by a Bruker Advance NMR 500 spectrometer and calibrated with DMSO-d6 (δ = 39.5 ppm), MeOH-d4 (δ = 49.0 ppm), or CDCl3 (δ = 77.2 ppm). Multiplicities are given as follows: s (singlet), d (doublet), t (triplet), q (quartet), dd (doublet of doublets), dt (doublet of triplets), and m (multiplets). High-resolution mass spectral (HRMS) analysis was performed on UHPLC-QTOF (Agilent Corporation) and Thermo DFS (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Reverse phase HPLC purification used SunFire Prep C18 OBR 5 μm, 19 mm × 150 mm column with a 10 mL/min flow rate. Solvent A was H2O with 0.1% TFA, and solvent B was CH3CN with 0.1% TFA. The purity of the final compounds was analyzed on the SHIMADZU LC20-AR with a SunFire C18 column (5 μm, 4.6 mm × 150 mm) or the Waters UPLC H class with an ACQUITY UPLC BEH C18 column (1.7 μm, 2.1 mm × 50 mm). The purity of all final compounds was greater than 95%.

    2. Synthesis of Intermediates and Final Compounds

    General Procedure A for C–N Coupling

    7H-Pyrrolo[2,3-d]pyrimidin-2-amine (S1) or its analogues (1 equiv), aryl iodide (2 equiv), proline (0.2 equiv), K2CO3 (3 equiv) were added into DMSO (10 mL) at room temperature. The solution was degassed before addition of copper(I) iodide (0.1 equiv). The reaction was degassed and heated up at 90 °C under N2 for 16 h. The reaction was then quenched by addition of water. The aqueous layer was extracted with ethyl acetate, and combined organic layers were washed with brine and dried over anhydrous Na2SO4. The volatile components were removed, and the residues were purified by flash column chromatography to afford the target molecules.

    General Procedure B for Sonagashira Coupling

    Aryl halide (1 equiv), bis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(II) chloride (1 equiv), triphenylphosphine (2 equiv), and copper(I) iodide (1 equiv) were added into DMSO (10 mL) at room temperature. The solution was degassed before addition of alkyne (3 equiv) in one portion. The solution was degassed followed by addition of diisopropylamine (0.2 mL). The solution was degassed and heated up at 80 °C under N2 for overnight. The reaction was then quenched by addition of water. The aqueous layer was extracted with ethyl acetate, and combined organic layers were washed with brine and dried over anhydrous Na2SO4. The volatile components were removed, and the residues were purified by flash column chromatography and subsequent reverse phase HPLC purification. The desired products were obtained as salts of trifluoroacetic acid (TFA).

    General Procedure C for Fischer Indole Synthesis

    (2-Aminopyrimidin-5-yl)boronic acid (1.5 equiv), di-tert-butyl azodicarboxylate (1 equiv), and Cu(OAc)2 (0.05 equiv) were dissolved in MeOH. The solution was degassed and then heated up at 65 °C under N2 for 1 h. The reaction was cooled followed by addition of the corresponding 2-arylacetaldehyde (1.5 equiv in 1,4-dioxane).     3. Molecular Modeling Methods

    The cocrystal structure of NIK (PDB code 4G3E) was obtained from RCSB Protein Data Bank. The water molecules were removed, while an NIK structure in the asymmetric unit was extracted and optimized using the Protein Preparation Wizard module in Schrödinger software. A grid box of 20 Å × 20 Å × 20 Å centered on the active site was generated as the docking area using the Receptor Grid Generation module. Subsequently, proper conformations of 7 and 41 were prepared in the Ligprep module, while the ionized states of 7 and 41 (pH = 7.4 ± 1.0) were generated using the Epik method. The aforementioned NIK structure and conformations of 7 and 41 were allowed to dock in the Glide module. Finally, the best binding modes were chosen according to the glide docking scores and visualized in Pymol.

    4. NIK ATP Consumption Assay

    The recombinant human NIK protein (318–947aa) was purchased from BPS Bioscience. The NIK ATP consumption assay was performed with an ADP-Glo kinase assay kit (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Briefly, the assay components including 2 μL of enzyme solution (30 nM NIK), 2 μL of substrate solution (5 μM ultrapure ATP), and 1 μL inhibitor solution were added to the individual well of a 384-well microplate, diluted in HEPES buffer (50 mM HEPES, 10 mM MgCl2, 0.01% Triton X-100, 2 mM DTT, pH 7.2), and incubated at room temperature for 1 h. Subsequently, 5 μL of ADP-Glo reagent was added to terminate the kinase reaction and deplete the remaining ATP in each well. Finally, the detecting reagent was added to convert ADP into ATP and the newly synthesized ATP was measured using a luciferase/luciferin reaction in Envision (PerkinElmer). Each compound was tested in triplicates, and the IC50 values were calculated by fitting the data to a sigmoidal dose–response (variable slope) equation.

    5. Cell Culture and Adenoviral Infection

    Primary hepatocytes were isolated from C57BL/6 male mice (8 weeks old) by liver perfusion with type II collagenase (Worthington Biochem, Lakewood, NJ) as reported previously.(19,38) Cells were cultured at 37 °C in 5% CO2 in DMEM supplemented with 100 U/mL penicillin, 100 U/mL streptomycin, and 5% fetal bovine serum. The cells were then infected with βGal, NIK, or p100 adenoviruses at 5000 virus particle/cell as described previously(19,39,40) in the presence or absence of the indicated NIK inhibitors for 16 h.

    6. Western-Blot Assay

    Primary hepatocytes were harvested in an L-RIPA lysis buffer [50 mM Tris (pH 7.5), 1% Nonidet P-40, 150 mM NaCl, 2 mM EGTA, 1 mM Na3VO4, 100 mM NaF, 10 mM Na4P2O7, and 1 mM PMSF]. Proteins were separated by SDS–PAGE and immunoblotted with the indicated antibodies. The antibodies used were listed as follows: Flag (F1804, Sigma-Aldrich), NF-κB2 (4882, Cell Signaling Technology), tubulin (sc5286, Santa Cruz Biotechnology).

    7. Real-Time qPCR Assay

    The total RNAs were extracted from primary hepatocytes using TriPure isolation reagent kit (Roche Diagnostics, Mannheim, Germany). Subsequently, the cDNAs were synthesized with random primers and the M-MLV reverse transcriptase (Promega). The abundance of RNA was measured using the qPCR SYBR Mix and a Light Cycler 480 real-time PCR system. The relative expression levels of individual genes were normalized based on the expression of the control housekeeping gene, 36B4. Primers used for real-time qPCR were listed as follows: TNFα, forward, CATCTTCTCAAAATTCGAGTGACAA, reverse, TGGGAGTAGACAAGGTACAA-CCC; CCL2, forward, ACTGAAGCCAGCTCTCTCTTCCTC, reverse, TTCCTTC-TTGGGGTCAGCACAGAC; CCL5, forward, CCACTTCTTCTCTGGGTTGG, reverse, GTGCCCACGTCAAGGAGTAT, CXCL5, forward, TGCATTCCGCTTAG-CTTTCT, reverse, CAGAAGGAGGTCTGTCTGGA, NOS2, forward, CAGGGC-CACCTCTA CATTTG, reverse, TGCCCCATAGGAAAAGACTG.

    8. Pharmacokinetic Studies in Mice

    The pharmacokinetic experiments of 46 in ICR mice were evacuated by Shanghai Medicilon Inc. The tested compound was dissolved in the vehicle containing 5% DMSO/10% Cremophor/85% PBS. The dose for intravenous administration (iv) was 5 mg/kg (12 male mice), or the dose for oral administration (po) was 15 mg/kg (12 male mice). Upon administration of 46, the blood samples were collected from mice at different time points (5 min, 15 min, 30 min, 1 h, 2 h, 4 h, 8 h, and 24 h for iv; 15 min, 30 min, 1 h, 2 h, 4 h, 6 h, 8 h, and 24 h for po) and centrifuged at 8000 rpm for 6 min, while the supernatant plasma was saved at −80 °C until analysis. Plasma concentrations of the tested compounds were analyzed by the LC–MS/MS method. The Phoenix WinNonlin 7.0 software has been used to calculate all pharmacokinetic parameters.

    9. Efficacy Studies in the CCl4-Induced Liver Injury Model in Mice

    Mice were housed on a 12 h light/12 h dark cycle with free access to water. All animal procedures described in this study were performed in adherence with the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD, USA) and approved by the Institutional Animal Care & Use Committee of Harbin Institute of Technology for Prof. Zheng Chen. The approval number is IACUC-2018004. For efficacy studies in the CCl4-induced liver injury mouse model, wild-type (WT) C57BL/6 mice (N = 8–10 per group) were intravenously injected with 46 (40 mg/kg), B022 (25 mg/kg), or an equal volume of vehicle via the tail veins. Half an hour later, mice were intraperitoneally injected with CCl4 (2.5 mL/kg body weight, 10% in olive oil) followed by 46 (40 mg/kg body weight), B022 (25 mg/kg body weight), or an equal volume of vehicle through the tail veins at 3, 6, and 9 h. The mice were euthanized at 12 h after the CCl4 injection. The blood samples were collected from the retro-orbital sinus. The serum alanine aminotransferase (ALT) activity was measured with an ALT detecting kit (C009-2-1, Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute). The liver tissues were harvested directly, and small pieces of liver samples were fixed in 4% PFA for H&E and F4/80 staining (30325T, Cell Signaling Technology). The rest of liver samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and then stored at −80 °C.

    10. Statistical Analysis

    Data were presented as mean values ± SE. Differences between groups were analyzed by Student’s t tests. p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant: *, p < 0.05; **, p < 0.01.
