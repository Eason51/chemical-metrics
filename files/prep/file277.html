    Abstract

    UDP-3-O-((R)-3-hydroxymyristoyl)-N-glucosamine deacetylase (LpxC) is as an attractive target for the discovery and development of novel antibacterial drugs to address the critical medical need created by multidrug resistant Gram-negative bacteria. By using a scaffold hopping approach on a known family of methylsulfone hydroxamate LpxC inhibitors, several hit series eliciting potent antibacterial activities against Enterobacteriaceae and Pseudomonas aeruginosa were identified. Subsequent hit-to-lead optimization, using cocrystal structures of inhibitors bound to Pseudomonas aeruginosa LpxC as guides, resulted in the discovery of multiple chemical series based on (i) isoindolin-1-ones, (ii) 4,5-dihydro-6H-thieno[2,3-c]pyrrol-6-ones, and (iii) 1,2-dihydro-3H-pyrrolo[1,2-c]imidazole-3-ones. Synthetic methods, antibacterial activities and relative binding affinities, as well as physicochemical properties that allowed compound prioritization are presented. Finally, in vivo properties of lead molecules which belong to the most promising pyrrolo-imidazolone series, such as 18d, are discussed.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies antimicrobial resistance (AMR) as one of the biggest threats to global health, acknowledging that AMR steadily erodes the power of antibiotherapy used to cure life-threatening infections.(1) WHO has established a global priority pathogens list where multidrug- and extensively drug-resistant (MDR and XDR) Acinetobacter, Pseudomonas aeruginosa (Pae), Enterobacteriaceae strains (including Klebsiella pneumoniae (Kpn), Escherichia coli (Eco), Serratia, and Proteus) ranked high.(2) These Gram-negative bacteria (GNB) are not only refractory to recommended empiric treatment options (carbapenems and third-generation cephalosporins) but are also becoming increasingly resistant to some of the last resort drugs such as colistin,(3) creating a critical medical need for novel, effective, and safe antibacterial agents. Public health experts as well as authorities have recognized the urgency of the situation and economic incentives have been awarded to boost innovation.(4)
    The success of incremental improvement of old antibacterial classes will soon come to an end as it becomes increasingly difficult to circumvent cross-resistance issues. The level of innovation in the field of anti-Gram-negative bacteria drugs has been low, and this can mainly be attributed to the scarcity of drug targets, novel or validated. Expanding the spectrum of activity of drugs effective against Gram-positive bacteria to include GNB has therefore recently gained a lot of attention.(5) In this context, efforts to identify rationally the processes that drive cytoplasmic drug accumulation in Gram-negative bacteria have been reported.(6) Over the past decade, only a handful of novel targets amenable to the treatment of Gram-negative infections have been studied, resulting in only a few chemical entities suitable for entry-into-man.(7) Among them, LpxC is a biological target that holds much promise and has captured the interest of many biopharmaceutical companies, leading to the first phase I trial of Achaogen’s ACHN-975 1 (Figure 1).(8) Motivated by these successes, we sought to develop an independent program to identify a novel class of LpxC inhibitors.
    LpxC is a cytosolic metal-dependent enzyme found only in Gram-negative bacteria. It catalyzes the first committed step in the biosynthesis of lipid A, a key component of the Gram-negative outer membrane. Utilizing a single active-site metal ion, LpxC hydrolyses the 2-acetamide of UDP-(3-O-((R)-3-hydroxymyristoyl)-N-acetylglucosamine to produce acetate and UDP-(3-O-((R)-3-hydroxymyristoyl)-glucosamine (Figure 1).(9−12)
    Extensive X-ray crystallographic studies of LpxC homologues from several bacterial species revealed a unique β–α–α–β sandwich fold along with a unique metal (Zn2+ or Fe2+)-binding motif defining the catalytic site of the enzyme. These structural investigations further provided detailed information on the binding mode of the deacetylation reaction product, as well as small molecule inhibitors and other ligands.(13) The key attributes that make LpxC an attractive drug target are its essentiality for bacterial survival, its gene conservation across a spectrum of bacterial pathogens, the absence of a human homologue and the availability of specific antibacterial inhibitors with proven efficacy in animal models of infection. Inhibition of LpxC by small molecules represents a new mode of antibacterial action with low chance of being compromised by pre-existing target-specific mechanisms of resistance.(14)
    Since the groundbreaking discovery at Merck that L-573,655 2 (Figure 1) exerted antibacterial activity against Eco by inhibiting lipid A biosynthesis,(15) all reported inhibitors of LpxC that exhibited potent antibacterial activity against Enterobacteriaceae and Pae have shared two structural elements: (1) a hydroxamic acid moiety as metal binding unit(16,17) and (2) a linear and rigid lipophilic tail that interacts with the hydrophobic tunnel otherwise occupied by the fatty acyl chain of the natural substrate (hereafter dubbed substrate-binding passage or passage).(17−19) Families of LpxC inhibitors differ from one another mainly in the nature of the chemical scaffold that links those two recurrent elements. At the time when our research program was initiated, optimization efforts on threonyl-hydroxamate based LpxC inhibitors deriving from CHIR-090 3 had culminated in the identification of phase I candidate 1 at Achaogen and lead compound 4 at Novartis,(20,21) offering only limited opportunity for a fast-follower approach. However, the concurrent disclosure of the structurally novel methylsulfone hydroxamate derivatives such as 5 and 6 with potent antibacterial activities,(22,23) made this motif an excellent starting point for our investigations. Herein, we report the scaffold hopping approach that eventually led to the identification of novel methylsulfone hydroxamate LpxC inhibitors articulated around isoindolin-1-one (core I),(24) 1,2-dihydro-3H-pyrrolo[1,2-c]imidazole-3-one (core II)(25) and 4,5-dihydro-6H-thieno[2,3-c]pyrrol-6-one(24) (core III) (Figure 2). Synthesis, crystallographic, biological, and pharmacological data of compounds generated during the subsequent hit-to-lead optimization program are reported, with a focus on derivatives of the pyrrolo[1,2-c]imidazole-3-one series (core II).

A reductive amination between 30 and chiral amine 22 gave rise to an alkylated amine 31, which upon treatment with carbonyl-diimidazole (CDI) in the presence of sodium hydride afforded the bicyclic compound 32. The t-butyl ester was then transformed to the intermediate TMS–alkyne 33 via a hydrolysis-amidation-Sonogashira coupling sequence using methods described previously in Scheme 1, except that propylphosphonic anhydride (T3P) and N,N-diisopropylethylamine (DIPEA) were used as amide coupling reagents. From intermediate 33, TMS-cleavage using tetrabutylammonium fluoride (TBAF) in tetrahydrofuran (THF) afforded the terminal alkyne 34 as an equimolar mixture of THP anomers.

Subsequent regioselective bromination using bromine in aqueous acetic acid gave rise to bromo-intermediate 37. THP-deprotection under acidic conditions afforded hydroxamic acids 16a,b, 18a–j, and 20a–f. Depending on the nature of the group R2 present in coupling partners 51–60, an intermediate deprotection step (TBAF in THF when a silyl protecting group is present or potassium carbonate in MeOH in the case of an ester) was required prior to the acid-mediated THP-deprotection.     Scaffold Hopping

    With the methylsulfone hydroxamate inhibitor family as a starting point for scaffold hopping, we analyzed the published X-ray cocrystal structures of Pfizer’s inhibitors 5 and 6 in complex with Pae LpxC (PDB 3U1Y and 3UHM). While the methylsulfone hydroxamate headgroups of 5 and 6 establish crucial H-bonding interactions with the catalytic metal and its immediate environment, the aromatic components, i.e., phenyl or pyridone cores of these inhibitors, together with their respective side lipophilic tails appeared to be only involved in van der Waals interactions with the LpxC protein. Furthermore, the central aromatic cores, seated at the entrance of the substrate-binding passage, played an essential role in maintaining lipophilic side chains at the right distance from the hydroxamate anchor and in directing these tails toward the hydrophobic passage. Therefore, the search for replacements of the central scaffold was initiated using pyridone 7 as a starting point.(23) Three design criteria had to be fulfilled. A novel core scaffold had to (1) be planar, (2) provide two exit vectors projecting in opposite direction, and (3) be structurally dissimilar from the pyridone ring. The oxazol-2-one ring met these constraints (Figure 3), although both exit vectors around the five-membered core formed a marked obtuse angle (∼139°), in clear contrast to the one observed in the original six-membered pyridone ring (∼180°). As a side aromatic ring, we chose the 2-fluoro-4-methoxyphenyl residue, which had previously provided superior results in the 2-pyridone series. (Figure 3).(23) The absolute stereochemistry of the chiral methylsulfone hydroxamate headgroup was set to (R), following Pfizer’s findings.(22)
    Oxazol-2-one (R)-8 and (S)-8 were prepared as reported in SI. The in vitro antibacterial activity of compounds (R)-8 and (S)-8 were determined against a panel of clinically relevant Gram-negative pathogens including wild-type Eco ATCC 25922 and Pae ATCC 27853 strains, and Kpn T 6474, an MDR strain.(28) Wild-type (wt) Pae PAO1 and an isogenic efflux mutant (PA2616, Δ(mexAB-OprM)), were also employed to assess the impact of compound efflux processes on the antibacterial activity. Binding affinities to Eco and Pae LpxC enzymes were estimated by differential scanning fluorimetry (DSF) and are expressed as thermal shifts (ΔTm in K).(29)

Binding affinities of compound (R)-8 to Eco and Pae LpxC, determined at 25 μM compound concentration, were lower than those of 7 (Table 1), coinciding well with the observed differences in antibacterial activity. Compounds 7 and (R)-8 were more potent against the efflux-defective Pae strain PA2616 than against its wild-type (wt) PAO1 parent. MIC PAO1/MIC PA2616 ratios (henceforth MIC efflux shifts) calculated for compounds 7 and (R)-8 were found to be ≥8-fold, suggesting that both compounds were efflux pump substrates. Despite its moderate binding affinity to Pae LpxC, an X-ray cocrystal structure of oxazol-2-one 8 bound to Pae LpxC could be obtained (Figure 4).(30)

    The methylsulfone hydroxamate headgroup of oxazol-2-one (R)-8 is engaged in several metal- or hydrogen-bonding interactions, either with the Zn atom or amino acid side chains (Glu77, Thr190, Lys238, and His264), similar to those previously reported for methylsulfone hydroxamate inhibitors 5 and 6 (Figure 4). The sulfone methyl was positioned within a small hydrophobic pocket, pointing toward the phenyl ring of Phe191. As observed in case of the pyridone core, the oxazol-2-one oxygen pointed toward the side chain of Met62. The aromatic tail projected into the substrate-binding passage, i.e., the inner open space of the LpxC insert II motif.(11) The side aromatic ring and the oxazol-2-one were arranged in a nearly coplanar fashion, forming a dihedral angle of around −10° (37° in case of pyridone 6). The low affinity of inhibitor (R)-8 could be explained by the smaller angle at which both exit vectors present on its oxazol-2-one core intersect compared to the one of the pyridone ring. Moreover, the comparison between the X-ray structure of pyridone 7 bound to Pae LpxC generated with the one of compound (R)-8, showed that the inset II α-helix moved by 2.2 Å toward pyridone 7, probably leading to its tighter fit in LpxC compared to oxazol-2-one (R)-8. Concomitant with the characterization of oxazol-2-one (R)-8, several heterocyclic methylsulfone hydroxamate LpxC inhibitors with promising antibacterial activities were published.(31) The scaffold hopping approach thus shifted toward the exploration of bicyclic ring systems as core motifs to maximally diverge from those five-membered heterocyclic templates while taking advantage of our preliminary work. The expectation was that larger bicyclic ring systems would better occupy the vacant space within the substrate-binding passage, hence be less prone to thermal motion and therefore more potent at inhibiting LpxC.
    From compound (R)-8, different types of bicyclic cores were designed by fusing one additional aromatic ring to the oxazol-2-one motif. The carbonyl group was found beneficial to attain drug-like properties,(32) leading to the exploration of either benzoxazolone rings (path a) or isoindolin-1-one-type rings (path b), as depicted in Figure 5.
    The design was further refined by integrating the hypothesis that both exit vectors of the central core intersect with an angle close to 180° (as for pyridones 6 and 7). In the case of benzoxazolone and isoindolin-1-one rings, both exit vectors intersect with angles that are markedly obtuse (around 140°–150°). To restore an overall linear geometry, the phenyl ring was replaced by a five-membered aromatic ring, giving rise to substituted 4,5-dihydro-6H-thieno[2,3-c]pyrrol-6-one (hereafter dubbed thienopyrrolones), 1,2-dihydro-3H-pyrrolo[1,2-c]imidazol-3-one (or pyrrolo-imidazolones, X = CH), and 6,7-dihydro-5H-imidazo[1,5-a]imidazol-5-one ring systems (or imidazo–imidazolones, X = N), for which the vectors form an angle closer to 180° (see Figure 5).
    Seven representative derivatives featuring these designed cores were prepared as reported in SI. Isoindolin-1-one 10,(24) pyrrolo-imidazolone 12,(25) and thienopyrrolone 14(24) exhibited potent antibacterial activities against Eco and Kpn strains (MICs ≤ 0.25 μg/mL) and moderate activity against Pae strains with MICs ≤ 2 μg/mL (Table 2). Binding affinities of compounds 10, 12, and 14, determined by DSF (ΔTm recorded on isolated enzyme at 25 μM compound concentration), were comparable on Eco (ΔTm = 8–9 K) and Pae (ΔTm = 11–12 K) enzymes and comparable with ΔTm values obtained with pyridone 7. MIC efflux shifts determined for these three derivatives were about 4–8-fold, similarly to that of pyridone 7. These efflux shifts indicated that these LpxC inhibitors were to some extent substrates of the Pae MexAB or OprM-dependent efflux systems. Benzoxazolone 9(33) exhibited weaker antibacterial activity against Gram-negative strains than derivatives 10, 12, and 14, although its binding affinities to both Eco and Pae LpxC (ΔTm = 8.2 and 11.0 K, respectively) were comparable. The large MIC efflux shift of benzoxazolone 9 (≥64-fold) suggested that the weaker antibacterial activity against Pae resulted from extensive efflux. Derivatives 11 and 13 were inactive against Kpn and Pae strains, and their MICs against Eco were at least 32-fold higher than those of their direct analogues 10 and 12. The poor antibacterial profile of compounds 11 and 13 could be explained by their lower binding affinities to Eco (ΔTm = 5–6 K) and Pae (ΔTm ∼ 7 K) enzymes, suggesting a negative effect of additional nitrogen atoms present in the annulated aromatic ring. For compounds displaying MICs ≤ 4 μg/mL against Pae, solubility at pH 7 and distribution coefficient (log D7.4) were determined. Compound 12 appeared to be less soluble at pH 7 and more lipophilic than pyridone 7 (log D7.4 1.8 and 1.1 for compounds 12 and 7, respectively). Compounds 10 and 14 were poorly soluble at pH 7 (∼5 μg/mL) and displayed high lipophilicity (log D7.4 ∼2) (Table 2). At pH 9, all compounds exhibited higher solubilities as the hydroxamate moiety is predominantly ionized (measured pKas of hydroxamic acids usually range between 7.3 and 7.7).(34) However, compound 12 remained less soluble than pyridone 7 as this pH.

    Hit-to-Lead Optimization

    The next stages toward identifying an LpxC inhibitor suitable for the treatment of GNB infections consisted of chemical exploration of the hits generated by scaffold hopping (i.e., hits 10, 12, and 14) and then selection of the most suitable series for further lead optimization. Antibacterial potency and spectrum, as well as aqueous solubility, were selected as main parameters to guide the hit-to-lead optimization.
    Upon examination of the cocrystal structures of isoindolin-1-one 10 and pyrrolo-imidazolone 12 (SI, Figures S-6 and S-7, respectively) bound to Pae LpxC,(30) identical electrostatic interactions of the methylsulfone hydroxamate headgroup of both entities to the catalytic site were found to those previously described.(22) As expected, both bicyclic cores of compounds 10 and 12 are located at the entrance of the hydrophobic substrate-binding passage, projecting the side aromatic rings into the space defined by LpxC insert II. The superimposition of LpxC structures bound to oxazol-2-one (R)-8 (binding affinity to Pae LpxC ΔTm: 9.9 K) and 10 (ΔTm: 11.6 K) showed that the tighter complex formed with isoindolin-1-one 10 arose mainly from stronger interactions between the α-helix of insert II and the inhibitor (Figure 6). The entire α-helix was pulled closer toward the inhibitor by 1.9 Å (distance measured between Cα atoms of Ile197, relatively to an all-Cα-fit of both structures with a Cα-RMSD of 0.239 Å) and therefore closed the hydrophobic tunnel to a tighter fit around the molecule compared to what had been observed for compound (R)-8.
    From these observations and previous SAR studies conducted on CHIR-090 3 analogues,(19) we anticipated that the composition of the lipophilic tail that spans the hydrophobic passage would greatly influence the binding affinity and therefore the antibacterial activity of LpxC inhibitors. The lipophilic tails of the disclosed LpxC inhibitors with antibacterial activity can be divided in three segments (A, B, and R), each occupying well-defined binding regions in or outside the substrate-binding passage of LpxC enzymes (Figure 7a). The substructure element directly linked to the bicyclic core, henceforth dubbed segment A, is located within the narrowest part of insert II hydrophobic passage. In the threonyl-hydroxamate series, broad and enhanced antibiotic activity could be achieved for inhibitors featuring a small acetylenic unit (van der Waals (vdW) volume: 38 Å3 per C2H2 unit)(35) in place of a larger phenyl ring (vdW volume: 80.3 Å3 per C6H6 unit).(17) To similarly maximize vdW interactions with LpxC insert II, an acetylenic unit was set as a recurrent segment A for the remainder of the present study.(36) The second segment of the lipophilic tail (chemical substructure element B, Figure 7a) protrudes from the hydrophobic passage and resides at the interphase of the distal end of the hydrophobic passage and the surface of the enzyme (see Figure 7b). Only two previously exemplified hydrophobic groups (ACHN-975 1 and CHIR-090 3; see Figure 1) varying in volume and length were initially considered: a phenyl ring (2.83 Å) and an acetylene unit (1.22 Å). The third substructure element R (Figure 7a,b), positioned at the distal end of the inhibitor is partly or totally exposed to solvent, thus providing an excellent means to increase chemical diversity and to modulate physicochemical properties of inhibitors. Efficient synthetic access to designed LpxC inhibitors could be achieved by coupling reactions involving appropriate partners carrying side chains (defined as the combination of structural elements B and R) and three terminal alkyne building blocks 29, 34, and 40, differing from the nature of their core I, II, and III, respectively (see Scheme 4). To initiate a study on the effects of segments B and R on antibacterial activity, a few archetypical side chains (SC1–4) were selected as they were easily accessible from commercially available building blocks (including CHIR090 3 morpholine-based side chain SC1). For all compounds reported in the following tables, antibacterial activities on wild-type Eco and Pae, MDR Kpn and PAO1 strains, as well as binding affinities (thermal shift, ΔTm in K) to Eco and Pae LpxC enzymes were determined. For all compounds displaying MIC ≤ 2 μg/mL against Eco, log D7.4 and solubilities at pH 4, 7, and 9 were also measured from DMSO stock solutions.
    All derivatives featuring side chains SC1 and SC2 (B = phenyl) displayed good binding affinity to Eco LpxC (ΔTm ranging from 9.5 to 13 K), translating to good antibacterial activity against Eco and Kpn strains with MICs ≤ 0.5 μg/mL (Table 3). Derivatives 17a,b and 19a,b elicited good MICs against Pae as well (0.5–1 μg/mL range), correlating with good binding affinities to Pae LpxC (ΔTm ranging from 13.6 to 15.5 K). While featuring different cores, pairs of derivatives 17a/19a and 17b/19b displayed identical antibacterial profiles against GNBs, even though larger ΔTm values (+1.9 K) were obtained for thienopyrrolones on Eco and Pae LpxC enzymes. These larger ΔTm values of thienopyrrolones 19a and 19b tracked with their higher lipophilicity (higher log D7.4) (Table 3). Groups R present in SC1 and SC2 had a negligible effect on biochemical and antibacterial activities when comparing 17a to 17b and 19a to 19b. Isoindolin-1-one derivatives 15a,b elicited slightly higher MICs (2-fold elevation) against Eco, Kpn, and Pae strains than their analogues featuring either core II or III. The weaker antibacterial activity of isoindolin-1-ones 15a,b may be due to weaker binding to LpxC enzymes of these compounds compared to their analoguous inhibitors 17a,b or 19a,b (featuring cores II or III) (Table 3). This may result from the marked difference that exists between the orientation of the two exit vectors around core I relative to cores II and III (see Figure 4). No efforts to further rationalize this observation were undertaken. Solubilities of compounds 15a and 19a featuring morpholine-containing side chain SC1 were higher within the assessed pH range than those of derivatives 15b and 19b featuring alcohol-based side chain SC2. Pyrrolo-imidazolone derivatives 17a and 17b featuring side chains SC1 and SC2, respectively, shared similar solubility profiles. Morpholine-derivatives 15a, 17a, and 19a had consistently lower log D7.4 than alcohols 15b, 17b, and 19b.

    The binding mode of compound 17a to Pae LpxC was determined by X-ray crystallography.(30) The hydroxamate-based headgroup of 17a was bound to zinc in an identical mode to that of previous analogues. As illustrated in Figure 7a, the acetylenic unit (segment A) of compound 17a occupied the hydrophobic passage, while the phenyl ring (segment B) was located close to the surface of the protein at the exit to the hydrophobic passage. The morpholine ring of 17a was located on the surface of the enzyme, fully exposed to solvent, turnable, and not visible in the electron density map (see Figure 7b). The analysis of the crystal structure of 17a revealed one significant difference in comparison to the structures of hits 8, 10, and 12. While the cores of compounds 8, 10, and 12 were coplanar with the peptide unit connecting Phe191 and Gly192, forming a π–π stacking interaction, the core of 17a was inclined and therefore not able to stack on these peptide bonds (Figure 8a). However, another π–π stacking interaction could be observed for 17a involving the phenyl moiety and the peptide bond between Gly209 and Ser210 at the outer part of the hydrophobic tunnel, close to the protein surface (Figure 8b). This additional interaction could compensate for the former loss of the stacking, leading to decent binding affinity (Eco ΔTm ∼11.9 K and Pae ΔTm ∼13.9 K for 17a).
    The superimposition of 17a and CHIR-090 3 structures (PDB 5VWM) showed that the core of compound 17a resided at the entrance of the hydrophobic passage, while the phenyl core of CHIR-090 3 was seated deeper inside the tunnel (Figure 9). As the morpholine ring of 17a was pushed 0.6 Å further outward, we synthesized shorter bis-acetylenic derivatives to take advantage of the shallow hydrophobic pocket filled by the morpholine ring of CHIR-090 3.(20) Pairs of derivatives differing only in the nature of the structural element B (either a phenyl or and acetylenic unit) were thus produced. Diverse R-groups were attached according to previously reported favorable properties such as substituted cyclopropyl rings (featured in ACHN-975 and analogues).(37) The most readily accessible pyrrolo-imidazolone series was selected to conduct the study.

All these derivatives were potent LpxC inhibitors as indicated by their strong binding affinities recorded on Eco (ΔTm ∼11–12 K) and Pae (ΔTm ∼13–14 K) LpxC enzymes, suggesting that combining the R groups with a phenyl ring has little effect on biochemical potency. Compounds 17c and 17h displayed good solubilities at pH 7 (>600 μg/mL). In contrast to phenyl-acetylenes, the nature of R greatly influenced antibacterial activity of bis-alkyne derivatives. For instance, sulfonamide 18i and diol 18h exhibited elevated MICs against Eco, Kpn, and Pae (MICs ≥ 4 μg/mL), roughly 4–8-fold higher than that of their phenyl-containing analogues 17i and 17h. The weaker antibacterial activity of these derivatives was attributed to their lower ΔTms on LpxC enzymes (especially on Eco, with ΔTm ≤ 8 K). The antibacterial activity of bis-acetylenic derivatives 18a and 18c against Pae (MICs 2 μg/mL) was almost as potent as their analogues 17c and 17a, respectively.     Mouse Efficacy and Pharmacokinetic Parameters.(38)

    The pharmacological profile of representative pyrrolo-imidazolone derivatives eliciting MICs ≤ 1 μg/mL against tested GNBs, and solubility >200 μg/mL at pH 7, was investigated in more depth.
    The activities of 17h, 18d, 18f, and 18g against Escherichia coli (Eco) were explored in vivo in a neutropenic murine thigh infection model.(39) Mice were infected with Eco (inoculum: 1.10(5,6) CFU/thigh), and 2 h later they were subcutaneously (sc) treated with vehicle or 50 or 150 mg/kg of the compounds (one additional group was not treated and was used to determine the bacterial load 2 h after infection). After 22 h (24 h post infection), the bacterial load had increased to 1 × 1010 units/thigh in vehicle treated mice. The increase in bacterial load in those treated with the different compounds was lower (Table 6). All derivatives displayed a dose-dependent increase in efficacy (i.e., reduction in bacterial load with increasing doses) (Table 6). None of these derivatives were efficacious enough at 150 mg/kg to elicit net static efficacy (i.e., difference in CFU/thigh between treatment start and end = 0, Table 6). The pharmacokinetic properties of 17h, 18d, 18f, and 18g, especially AUCs in plasma and free AUCs reached over 24 h were determined experimentally after subcutaneous administration of 50 mg/kg (except for compound 18g for which data were extrapolated from a 150 mg/kg experiment). Whereas compounds 17h, 18d, and 18g displayed higher free AUC values, ranging from 1.16 to 2.81 (μg·h)/mL, corresponding to calculated unbound clearance values Clu/F ranging between 295 and 716 mL/min/kg, 18f was more rapidly cleared, resulting in a lower free AUC (0.37 (μg·h)/mL). Notably, derivatives 17h had a free AUC comparable to that of 18g despite being highly protein bound in mouse plasma. Derivative 18d had a 7-fold higher free AUC than 18f because of its higher unbound plasma fraction. All four derivatives showed higher unbound clearances and consequently lower free AUC (at 50 mg/kg) than the optimized pyridone 7. With this limited set of data, no good predictor for efficacy could be derived from the MIC in vitro and the PK parameters. Indeed, while compounds 17h and 18g elicited similar free AUC50s and roughly similar MICs, 17h was more efficacious against Eco in vivo (1.4 log difference in CFU reduction at a dose of 150 mg/kg) than compound 18g. Moreover, compounds 18d and 18f elicited a similar efficacy profile (Δlog10 ∼+2 and +0.8 CFU/thigh at 50 or 150 mg/kg) even though free AUCs and free AUCs/MIC were much different. As derivative 18d elicited a PK profile in mice at 50 mg/kg (free AUC50= 2.81 μg·h/mL) close to the one of reference pyridone 7,(40) further experiments were conducted with 18d to determine a dosing regimen that would elicit net static efficacy. A single dose of 450 mg/kg, resulted in a net killing of 1 log. In addition, in mice dosed four times a day with 18d (at 112.54 mg/kg per dose), 2, 7.5, 13 and 18.5 h post infection, efficacy improved slightly (1.5 log net reduction in CFU/thigh). To ascertain the origin of this favorable outcome, further PK experiments were conducted in mice, with subcutaneous dosing at 150 and 450 mg/kg. Free exposures derived from these experiments were AUC150= 7.16 and AUC450 = 8.64 μg·h/mL, respectively, clearly demonstrating that free exposures did not dose-proportionally increase between 150 and 450 mg/kg doses. Strikingly, dosing at 450 mg/kg slightly increased free AUC (+1.5 μg·h/mL) but led to a significant improvement in efficacy (nearly 2 log reduction of CFUs). Even if net static efficacy with derivative 18d could only be reached against a wild-type GNB (MIC Eco ATCC 25922 = 0.25 μg/mL) by applying high doses and would likely translate to a nonachievable dosing regimen against less sensitive bacteria in clinics, the significant improvement in efficacy resulting from only a modest increase in free exposure prompted us to pursue the optimization work on this chemotype toward analogues more potent in vivo.(34)

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We have reported herein the discovery of novel chemical series of LpxC inhibitors based on bicyclic cores using structural information to guide design and optimization processes starting from a known methyl sulfone hydroxamate inhibitor. Along this path, 4,5-dihydro-6H-thieno[2,3-c]pyrrol-6-ones or 1,2-dihydro-3H-pyrrolo[1,2-c]imidazole-3-ones emerged as the most promising hit series. Further investigations performed on the lipophilic side chains led to the identification of derivatives with potent antibacterial activities against Enterobacteriaceae and Pseudomonas aeruginosa strains. The pyrrolo-imidazolone series was subsequently prioritized because of its better scalability, offering lower technical costs in the longer run. Four pyrrolo-imidazolone LpxC inhibitors were shown to be efficacious against Escherichia coli in a clinically relevant murine infection model. However, in vivo efficacy of these novel antibacterial agents could only be achieved at high doses that would likely require unattainable human doses to be efficacious. Moreover, the biopharmaceutical profiles of the most advanced derivatives, 18d and 18g, were still suboptimal, with insufficient solubility in a pH range suitable for human intravenous administration.
    After these initial promising results, our future lead optimization program will focus on identifying compounds eliciting higher intrinsic solubility while achieving in vivo efficacy at lower doses. Strategies and subsequent synthetic medicinal chemistry on the pyrrolo-imidazolone series aimed at solving the issues encountered in this preliminary study are discussed in the companion paper.(34)

    Synthesis of Final Compounds

    General Procedure A: Amide Coupling with NH2OTHP

    Method A: To a solution of carboxylic acid (1 equiv) in DMF (1M) were added NH2OTHP (1.5 equiv), HOBT (1.8 equiv), EDC·HCl (1.4 equiv), and TEA (1.8 equiv). The resulting solution was stirred at rt for 16 h. The reaction mixture was partitioned between water (10 mL/mmol) and EtOAc (10 mL/mmol). The organic layer was washed with 10% aq NaHSO4 (10 mL/mmol), satd aq NaHCO3 (10 mL/mmol), and brine (10 mL/mmol). If necessary, the evaporation residue was purified via CC using an appropriate gradient of eluent. Method B: To a solution of carboxylic acid (1 equiv) in THF (5 mL/mmol) were added DIPEA (5.5 equiv) and NH2OTHP (1.5 equiv). After cooling to 0 °C, a solution of propylphosphonic anhydride (T3P 50% in solution in EtOAc, 1.5 equiv) was added over 30 min. The reaction proceeded at 0 °C for 1 h and was warmed to rt over 2 h. After cooling to 0 °C, satd aq NaHCO3 (10 mL/mmol) was slowly added, followed by water (10 mL/mmol) and EtOAc (20 mL/mmol). The aq layer was extracted twice with EtOAc.
