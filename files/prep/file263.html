    Abstract

    PROteolysis TArgeting Chimeras (PROTACs) targeting the degradation of MEK have been designed based on allosteric MEK inhibitors. Inhibition of the phosphorylation of ERK1/2 was less effective with the PROTACs than a small-molecule inhibitor; the best PROTACs, however, were more effective in inhibiting proliferation of A375 cells than an inhibitor.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The protein kinases mitogen-activated protein kinase kinases 1 and 2 (MEK1, MEK2) are part of the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signalling pathway, containing the upstream kinase RAF, in complex with the G-protein Ras and the extracellular signal related kinases (ERK) 1 and 2, collectively called ERK1/2.(1) By a series of proteinâprotein interactions and protein phosphorylation events, gene transcriptional responses are triggered in response to various extracellular stimuli, including cytokines and growth factors, as well as environmental stresses.(2,3) This variety of pathway activating signals can explain the context-specific different cellular responses, which include cell proliferation, survival, and migration, but host immune responses also depend on this pathway, for example, by the release of soluble mediators associated with inflammation [such as Interleukin 6 (IL6)] or with antiviral immunity [such as Interferon beta (IFNb)].
    This signaling pathway has been extensively studied since dysregulated activities have been identified in many diseases, particularly in multiple types of cancer.(4) Inhibitors of MEK have been evaluated in the clinic as antitumor agents with two compounds now having been approved for use(5,6) (Figure 1).
    The efficacy of MEK inhibitors as single agents has been modest, but they have found utility in combination with BRAF inhibitors.(7)
    Unlike most kinase inhibitors, those investigated for the inhibition of MEK do not target the ATP binding site but rather an adjacent allosteric pocket. This pocket, specific to MEK, means that selectivity over other kinases is much less of an issue than for inhibitors of other protein kinases.(8) Despite this selectivity, toxicity has proven to be a problem in clinical use, with both cardiovascular(9) and ocular(10) toxicity being widely observed. Both toxicities have been ascribed to the mechanism of action driven effects.
    One problem that has been observed when MEK inhibitors are administered is an increase in phosphorylation of MEK through the inhibition of a negative feedback loop driven by phospho-ERK.(11) We conceived the idea of investigating degradation of MEK as an alternative to a conventional inhibitor because, although the feedback loop would still be inhibited, the overall levels of MEK will be greatly reduced and thus the feedback effect should be less significant.
    The concept of targeting proteins for degradation by the use of bifunctional ligands binding both a target protein and an E3 ligase was first described in 2001(12) and has since been advanced as a potential new therapeutic modality.(13,14) This approach has been given the acronym PROTAC (PROteolysis TArgeting Chimera). It has been shown that in vitro and in vivo degradation of target proteins can be achieved, and recently the first potential drugs have been reported to have entered development.(15)
    A PROTAC strategy for blocking the MEK/ERK signaling pathway could be advantageous because it bypasses typical limitations of small molecule inhibitors, such as incomplete inhibition due to target accumulation. A degradation mechanism potentially induces a fast and long-lasting depletion of the target proteins, thus blocking downstream signaling cascades and, importantly, offering a reduced dose owing to the substoichiometric catalytic nature of this mechanism. In turn, this provides a potential means to avoid the toxic side-effects of existing MEK inhibitors related to the high doses required.

    Results

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    MEK is amenable to crystallization and multiple structures with ligands bound in the allosteric pocket are available from the PDB. Examination of the crystal structure of refametinib (PDB 3E8N,(16)Figure 2) suggested that an arylsulfonamide analogue (1) might provide a suitable scaffold to explore a vector for the attachment of a linker to connect to an E3 ligase.
    Calculation, however, suggested that the benzenesulfonamide 1 would be highly lipophilic (ACD LogD7.4 7.6(17)) and would result in derived PROTACs that were also lipophilic and likely to be problematic in terms of physicochemical properties. We switched, therefore, to an isosteric pyridone core(18) for synthesis. Compound 2 proved to be an effective inhibitor of MEK1 (pIC50 6.4) with high, but acceptable, lipophilicity (LogD 3.9).
    The advantage of having a benzenesulfonamide as our parent compound was that it was straightforward to obtain a variety of para-substituted analogues for connection to linkers that we could use to explore of the SAR of potential PROTACs. A preliminary exploration by parallel chemistry identified that the optimal arrangement had a 3-atom linker from the para position to an amide which was further connected by a linker to an E3 ligase ligand. Exploration of both Von HippelâLindau protein (VHL) and cereblon ligands revealed that better degradation was obtained with a VHL ligand(19) (Table S1, Figures S1 and S2).
    The combination of the lipophilic VHL ligand and the selected MEK ligand resulted in molecules with high lipophilicity (e.g. 4 4.3) and low aqueous solubilities (pH 7.4 buffer, < 1.5 Î¼M).

    The identified compounds were able to degrade MEK in a cellular assay; compounds were dosed to A375 cells at concentrations of 1 and 10 Î¼M, followed by stimulation with poly(I:C). Levels of MEK1 were measured using a Surefire assay(20) (Table 1).
    In the initial experiments, we measured MEK1 levels only; we were also interested in MEK PROTAC-mediated degradation of MEK2. We therefore performed immunoblotting for MEK2 with cell lysates from MEK PROTAC or parent compound (2)-treated A375 cells using three of the most effective PROTACs, 3, 4, and 5 (Figure 3a). This result shows that MEK2 is also degraded by 3, 4, and 5 in A375 cells.
    Having identified 3, 4, and 5 as effective degraders of MEK1, we decided to characterize these compounds in detail. First, we determined the kinetics of MEK1 degradation and suppression of ERK1/2 phosphorylation. MEK1 degradation becomes significantly detectable after 2 h of incubation time and reaches the maximal inhibition at 8 h (Figure 3b). The parent compound has no effect on MEK1 levels up to 8 h, and the reduction in MEK1 expression at 16 h is probably due to diminished A375 cell proliferation (Figure 3b). The suppression of ERK1/2 by 3, 4, and 5 is very similar to the degradation of MEK1, with significant reduction starting at 2 h, further declining to a complete inhibition from 8 h onwards (Figure 3c). In contrast, 2 blocks ERK1/2 phosphorylation at the first time-point after 1 h of incubation; this inhibition is maintained for the entire time-course of 16 h. This more rapid onset may represent the greater permeability of the parent compared to the larger PROTACs that have increased polar surface areas (calculated PSA: 2 85 Ã2; 3â5 > 260 Ã2).
    We established dose response curves for 3, 4, 5 and their parent 2 (Figure 3d,e). MEK1 degradation only occurs at the two highest concentrations of 1 and 10 Î¼M, whereas lower concentrations surprisingly lead to an increase of MEK1 expression (Figure 3d). The reduction of MEK levels by the parent MEK inhibitor (2) in Figure 3d is around 20% compared to the DMSO control. The total ERK1/2 levels measured from the same samples and used in Figure 3e to normalize the phospho-ERK1/2 levels show a similar tendency, indicating that this observed effect is a result of reduced cell proliferation and therefore total protein amount, rather than being due to an influence on MEK protein stability. Inhibition of ERK1/2 phosphorylation is stronger by inhibitor 2 but in both modalities occurs only at the two highest compound concentrations (Figure 3e).
    MEK activation upon poly(I:C)-stimulation has been implicated in the regulation of secretion of cytokines, such as IL6 and IFNb. [4 has been shown to enhance poly(I:C)-induced IFNb secretion from BEAS2B cells.(21)] We therefore tested 3, 4, and 5 for their ability to suppress IL6 release from A375 cells. All the three studied PROTACs as well as 2, strongly suppress IL6 secretion when used at a final compound concentration of 10 Î¼M (Figure 4a). Only 2, however, also blocks the release of IL6 at 1 Î¼M. This finding is in line with the p-ERK1/2 suppression profile shown in Figure 3e.
    MEK inhibitors have been used widely in oncology therapies as inhibitors of cancer cell growth, and the two FDA-approved drugs, Trametinib and Cobimetinib, have reported IC50 potencies in A375 cells of 0.74 and 5 nM, respectively.(6) We therefore tested the MEK PROTACs for their ability to suppress A375 cell proliferation. We observed a strong inhibition of A375 cell growth with all three MEK PROTACs showing a greater effect than 2 (Figure 4b), despite the more rapid onset of action of 2 in cells.

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We have established that it is possible to build PROTAC molecules based on allosteric MEK inhibitors using VHL as the E3 ligase, though these initial compounds have only modest degradation efficiencies.

    Higher MEK1 Levels upon Treatment with Low Concentrations of MEKi or MEK PROTAC

    We observe an increased MEK1 expression under conditions where either the parent small molecule inhibitor or the MEK PROTACs were used at concentrations below those required to inhibit ERK1/2 (Figure 3a,b). MEK1 cellular levels have been described by a siRNA-mediated knockdown approach to be controlled by the Raf/MEK/ERK pathway in a feedback-regulated manner involving transcriptional mechanisms.(22) This leads us to speculate that under our experimental conditions, low-grade MEK inhibition leads to the inhibition of a negative feedback mechanism controlling MEK1 protein levels, thus resulting in higher MEK1 protein expression, potentially a compensatory mechanism to provide the cell with a sufficient amount of MEK1.

    Effect of MEKi or MEK PROTACs on A375 Cell Proliferation

    We observe complete inhibition of A375 cell proliferation when treated with 3 or 5 and a very strong inhibition when exposed to 4 (Figure 4b). The importance of MEK1 and MEK2 for cell growth has been described extensively,(2,3) and our result could be explained by an inhibition of cell cycle progression when MEK1 and MEK2 expressions are downregulated and their kinase activities inhibited.(2) Interestingly, the compounds 3, 4, and 5 have a stronger effect on cell proliferation than 2, though the latter has shown a better efficacy of suppression of ERK1/2 phosphorylation and IL6 secretion (Figures 3b and 4a). This result could be explained in two ways. First, a better long-term inhibitory effect of PROTACs compared to conventional small molecule inhibitors has been reported previously.(23) Alternatively, the superiority of the PROTACs to their parental small molecule inhibitor in suppressing A375 could be a consequence of affecting scaffolding and adaptor proteins of the MEK/ERK signaling complex. Proteins associated with the MAPK signaling cascade have been described.(24) Given the numerous activities triggered by the MEK/ERK signaling cascade, the fine-tuning of a cellular response depends not only on the catalytic kinase activities but also on scaffolding and adaptor proteins.

        ERK1/2 MAP Kinases become activated in response to multiple intra- and extra-cellular stimuli through a signaling module composed of sequential tiers of cytoplasmic kinases.
