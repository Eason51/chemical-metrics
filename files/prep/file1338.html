    Abstract

    Diabetes is characterized by pancreas dysfunction and is commonly associated with obesity. Hypoglycemic agents capable of improving β-cell function and reducing body weight therefore are gaining increasing interest. Though glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor (GLP-1R)/cholecystokinin 2 receptor (CCK-2R) dual agonist ZP3022 potently increases β-cell mass and improves glycemic control in diabetic db/db mice, the in vivo half-life (t1/2) is short, and its body weight reducing activity is limited. Here, we report the discovery of a series of novel GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists. Starting from Xenopus GLP-1, dual cysteine mutation was conducted followed by covalent side chain stapling and albumin binder incorporation, resulting in a stabilized secondary structure, increased agonist potency, and improved stability. Further C-terminal conjugation of gastrin-6 generated GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists, among which 6a and 6b showed higher stability and hypoglycemic activity than liraglutide and ZP3022. Desirably, 6a and 6b exhibited prominent metabolic benefits in diet-induced obesity mice without causing nausea responses and exerted considerable effects on β-cell restoration in db/db mice. These preclinical studies suggest the potential role of GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists as effective agents for treating diabetes and related metabolic disorders.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    There are over 400 million people with diabetes globally, and the prevalence of diabetes continues to rise.(1) Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) accounts for ∼90% of diabetic cases worldwide, and the number of T2DM has doubled in the past decades, with a particularly rapid increase among adolescents and children.(2) As a complex metabolic disorder characterized by β-cell dysfunction and insulin resistance, T2DM patients may ultimately need insulin treatment due to pancreatic function deterioration.(3) In addition, T2DM is closely associated with obesity as approximately over 80% of T2DM patients are overweight or obese.(4) Although the causal relationship between T2DM and obesity is not fully elucidated, elevated saturated free fatty acids and low-grade inflammation as seen in individuals with obesity have been proposed to exert a negative effect on pancreatic β-cell function.(5) Therefore, it is desirable if an antidiabetic therapy not only controls blood glucose but also reduces body weight and benefits β-cell function. In this regard, traditional antidiabetic agents such as thiazolidinediones and sulfonylureas suffer from some drawbacks such as weight gain.(6)
    Among the approved antidiabetic agents, glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor (GLP-1R) agonists and sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors are the only ones that could achieve effective glycemic control and modest weight reduction.(5) GLP-1 is an incretin hormone produced from intestinal endocrine L cells. Intriguing physiological effects of GLP-1 include improving glycemic control, inhibiting gastric emptying, reducing appetite, preventing β-cell apoptosis, and increasing β-cell proliferation.(7) Importantly, GLP-1R agonists are able to achieve a 3–6% reduction in body weight.(8) Currently, seven GLP-1R agonists have been approved, including exenatide, liraglutide, semaglutide (Figure 1), etc.(9) In 2014, the high dose version of liraglutide (3 mg) was approved for the treatment of obese individuals with overweight-related health conditions.
    Beyond these approved GLP-1 agents, combining the bioactivity of GLP-1 and additional gut hormones to generate unimolecular multitargeting hybrid peptides is known to achieve superior therapeutic benefits and represents the next generation of gut-derived peptide agonists. For example, multifunctional peptides capable of activating not only GLP-1R but also other gut hormone receptors, such as glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP) and/or glucagon receptors, have recently been developed.(10) Owing to the synergistic effects of GLP-1 agonists and other gut hormones, these dual or triacting peptides were found to be effective therapeutics for T2DM and obesity.(11)
    Gastrin is a gastrointestinal peptide belonging to the family of neuroendocrine peptides and is secreted from gastroendocrine G cells during meal ingestion. Gastrin-17 and gastrin-34 are the major forms, while gastrin-6 and gastrin-14 have also been identified.(12) Gastrin stimulates gastric acid secretion through activation of the cholecystokinin 2 receptor (CCK-2R, also referred as the gastrin receptor). Evidence suggests that gastrin and CCK-2R are implicated in fetal pancreas development. Moreover, it has been reported that in rodent models of pancreatic regeneration, gastrin increases β-cell mass, suggesting the potential utility of gastrin for diabetes treatment.(13,14) Previous research studies proved that coadministration of gastrin with GLP-1, exendin-4, or epidermal growth factor in the rodent model of diabetes restored normoglycemia and increased β-cell mass via islet neogenesis.(15−17) In 2013, Zealand Pharma has developed ZP3022 (exendin-4 (1-28)-8Ado-8Ado-gastrin-6, Figure 1) as a GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonist. ZP3022 is an exendin-4 analogue, which potently increases β-cell mass and improves glycemic control in diabetic db/db mice.(18) To date, ZP3022 is the only reported GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonist. Although preclinical studies of ZP3022 indicated its potential to be developed as a novel antidiabetic agent, rare follow-ups were found. In addition, the in vivo half-life (t1/2) of ZP3022 is short, and its body weight reducing activity is limited.(18)
    We therefore are highly interested in developing novel GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists. It is noteworthy that nearly all reported GLP-1R agonists are based on the scaffold of human GLP-1 or exendin-4. Our previous research identified Xenopus GLP-1, a GLP-1 analogue from amphibians, as an alternative GLP-1R agonist.(19) In addition, some Xenopus GLP-1 derivatives were found to be long-acting GLP-1R agonists with better effects on glycemic control and body weight reduction than those of liraglutide and lixisenatide (a C-terminal modified exendin-4 analogue).(20,21)
    Incretin peptides such as GLP-1 and glucagon bind to their receptors in an α-helical conformation. It has been well documented that stabilizing the secondary structures of these peptides leads to increased receptor binding affinity and proteolytic stability, and this holds true for Xenopus GLP-1 peptides as preliminarily demonstrated in our previous work.(22)
    Based on the above premises, the following strategies were applied in the present study in search of novel GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists: (1) Instead of using the GLP-1 or exendin-4-based scaffold as the GLP-1R agonist, Xenopus GLP-1-derived peptides were used to bind to and activate GLP-1R. (2) Covalent side chain cross-linking of Xenopus GLP-1 was employed to generate stapled peptides with a stabilized secondary structure, enhanced agonist potency, improved metabolic stability, and increased bioactivity. (3) A serum albumin binding motif was introduced to further optimize the pharmacokinetics profile of peptides. (4) Gastrin-6 was conjugated to the C-terminus of stapled Xenopus GLP-1 derivatives to construct GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists. Following stepwise rounds of design, synthesis, and bioactivity screening, compounds 6a and 6b outperformed other candidates as novel GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists with demonstrated metabolic benefits in both diabetic db/db mice and diet-induced obesity (DIO) mice.

    Cys Substitutions and Bioactivity Screenings of Xenopus GLP-1 Analogues

    Our group first reported xGLP-1 (1, Figure 1) as a human GLP-1 analogue with demonstrated stability, in vitro GLP-1R activation potency, and in vivo hypoglycemic activity.(19) Further extension of 1 with the C-terminus of exendin-4 and lixisenatide led to the discovery of xGLP-Ex (2) and xGLP-Sen (3) (Figure 1), respectively, with improved stability and bioactivity.(23) These three peptides were thus employed as starting materials for current research. Based on our synthetic strategy (vide infra), the existence of two cysteine (Cys) residues in the peptide backbone is required as the anchoring points for constructing stapled Xenopus GLP-1 derivatives. Therefore, a Cys scanning was performed to identify suitable residues in 1 and its analogues for Cys substitutions. In view of the fact that the N-terminus of GLP-1 analogues is highly conserved and plays an important role in receptor binding and activation, Cys substitutions were conducted through positions 11 to 30 of 1, 2, and 3. The two Cys were spaced at i and i + 7 to fix two turns of the helix as this conformational restriction has been widely used to form α-helices in many gut hormones, such as OXM, exendin-4, and GLP-2.(24−26)
    In addition, previous research revealed that introducing Gly at the position 8 improved the dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP-IV) stability of GLP-1; however, its affinity for the GLP-1R was reduced. The introduction of Aib at the position 8 was the only Ala substitution that resulted in both high GLP-1R affinity and DPP-IV stability.(27) In 2015, Novo Nordisk found that Aib8, Arg34 GLP-1 (7-37) was more potent than native GLP-1. Further derivatization at Lys26 with C18 diacids through various linkers on Aib8, Arg34 GLP-1 (7-37) successfully leads to the discovery of semaglutide, which was the next-generation once weekly dosing GLP-1R agonist.(28) Inspired by these enlightening previous research studies, the Gly2 in 1, 2, and 3 was replaced with Aib. A total number of 39 dual cysteine mutants (1a–1m, 2a–2m, and 3a–3m; Figure 1) were designed based on the above rules and were synthesized by solid-phase chemistry on Rink amide MBHA resin. Crude products were purified by semipreparative reversed-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC), and the purified products were characterized by HPLC and ESI-MS (see the Supporting Information, Tables S1–S3).
    The dual cysteine mutants were screened for their GLP-1R activation ability by the cAMP activity assay. As shown in Table 1 and Figure S1, although weakened after Cys substitution, all mutants remained full GLP-1R agonists with subnanomolar EC50 values. Most mutants derivatized from 2 (2a–2m) and 3 (3a–3m) showed low subnanomolar EC50 values, indicating moderate reductions in agonism. Meanwhile, the disturbance of Cys mutations on 1 was more profound as seen from the EC50 of 1a–1m. In particular, we found that Cys mutations at Leu14/Glu21 (1d, 2d, and 3d) or Glu21/Lys28 (1k, 2k, and 3k) were well tolerated with respect to GLP-1R activation.

    The hypoglycemic activity of 1d, 1k, 2d, 2k, 3d, and 3k were further evaluated by the oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) in normal ICR mice to examine their in vivo GLP-1R agonist potency. As shown in Figure 2, compared with the vehicle (saline) treatment, administration of 1d, 1k, 2d, 2k, 3d, and 3k resulted in significantly decreased blood glucose concentrations after oral glucose loading (P < 0.001). The glucose-lowering activity of 1d, 1k, 2d, 2k, 3d, and 3k was comparable to 1, 2, and 3 and was better than GLP-1. Glucose AUC0–120min values of each group revealed a similar tendency (Figure 2). Taken together, the Cys scanning study revealed mutants 1d, 1k, 2d, 2k, 3d, and 3k as suitable templates for subsequent side chain cross-linking.

    Design and Synthesis of Stapled and Fatty Acid Coupled Peptides

    A bis-maleimide amide molecule (referred to as BisMAL, Scheme 1) was used to cross-link the reserved Cys in 1d, 1k, 2d, 2k, 3d, and 3k based on the specific reaction between thiol and maleimide. This cross-linker could form stable thiomaleimide adducts and has been successfully used in our previous research to construct potent dimeric Xenopus GLP-1 conjugates and by other research group to develop potent PEGylated dimeric exendin-4 conjugates.(29,30) A long-chain fatty acid (palmitic acid) was further coupled with the terminal amine of BisMAL to afford peptides 4a–4f (Figure 3 and Table 2). Fatty acid acts as an albumin binding to facilitate the physical interaction between peptide and human serum albumin (HSA), which is a well-established strategy used during the development of marketed GLP-1 analogues, such as liraglutide and semaglutide, to enhance the pharmacokinetics property.(28) However, the introduction of a fatty acid might lead to potency loss of peptides due to the steric interfering with receptor recognition and the lipophilicity of fatty acid. We reasoned that a short spacer between the peptide backbone and fatty acid could help to alleviate the above negative impacts and provide more opportunities for structural tuning. Therefore, 8-amino-3,6-dioxa-octanoic acid (OEG), OEG-γGlu, and 2OEG-γGlu were used as spacers to conjugate the ω-amino group of BisMAL with octadecanedioic acid, giving peptides 4g–4l, 4m–4r, and 4s–4x, respectively (Figure 3 and Table 2). For the synthesis of 4a–4x, orthogonally protected Fmoc-Cys(Mmt)-OH and Boc-His(Trt)-OH were used as the building blocks for Cys and His1. Specific Mmt deprotection was achieved using 1% TFA in CH2Cl2. BisMAL dissolved in DMF was reacted with the solid support for 1.5 h, and palmitic acid, Fmoc-OEG-OH, Fmoc-Glu-OtBu, and octadecanedioic acid, 1-(1,1-dimethylethyl) ester were added and coupled in order (Scheme 1). Crude products of 4a–4x were purified and characterized using the same method as described above (see the Supporting Information, Table S4).

    In Vitro Efficacy and Circular Dichroism (CD) Spectra of Stapled Peptides

    The receptor agonist potency of 4a–4x was determined by the cAMP activity assay in the presence of 0% HSA or 4.4% HSA (physiological concentrations). This approach was successfully applied in the discovery of semaglutide and was a pragmatic screening plan to select peptides for animal pharmacodynamics and pharmacokinetics studies.(28) As shown in Table 2 and Figure S2, in the absence of HSA, most stapled peptides were equipotent in receptor activation to their corresponding noncross-linked peptides. To our satisfaction, stapled peptides 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x activated GLP-1R more potently than the noncross-linked counterparts. When 4.4% HSA was present in the assay, a large shift was observed between activity in the absence and presence of HSA for BisMAL cross-linkers L3 and L4 (4m–4x). This was attributed to enhanced HSA binding in vitro, an effect which will bring longer in vivo half-life, and previously observed in the discovery of semaglutide. Interestingly, the 4.4%/0% HSA ratio of semaglutide was higher than that of 4s–4x with a similar linker and fatty acid protractor with semaglutide. The possible reasons were that the albumin binding affinity of our stable peptides was lower than that of semaglutide and/or the receptor activation potency of these stable peptides was improved at 4.4% HSA compared to semaglutide.
    Next, the α-helicity of 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x was determined by CD (Figure 4A), and a prominent increase in α-helicity compared with 1 and 2 was observed at room temperature. These results suggested that the thiomaleimide bridge within the stapled peptide stabilized the α-helical segment, resulting in improved agonist potency. Paradoxically, the less potent peptides 4m and 4n showed the highest increase in α-helicity, indicating that the agonist activity is not solely attributed to helical content since lipidation itself was known to increase helicity.

    Albumin Binding, Pharmacodynamics, and Obesity Ameliorating Effects of Stapled Peptides

    The observed differences of the 4.4%/0% HSA ratio among liraglutide, semaglutide, 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were clearly attributed to their different albumin binding abilities. As shown in Figure S3, under physiological conditions (in PBS pH 7.4), 83.8 ± 2.0 and 85.0 ± 1.4% of 4c and 4d were found to associate with albumin resin, lower than those of liraglutide (90.6 ± 3.3%) and semaglutide (97.8 ± 1.4%). For 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x with C18 diacid side chain albumin binders, the binding rates increased to 92.6 ± 2.6, 91.6 ± 2.0, 95.3 ± 1.2, and 96.7 ± 1.7% by 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x, respectively, higher than liraglutide and still lower than semaglutide. Consistent with the previous research, increasing the length of the carbon chain was favorable for the albumin binding affinity of 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x.
    We next determined whether the enhanced in vitro potency observed for 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x led to improved pharmacodynamics in vivo. The pharmacodynamics properties of 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were first evaluated in nonfasted db/db mice by s.c. injection, using liraglutide and semaglutide as the controls. As shown in Figure 4B,C, all the eight peptide-treated groups exhibited a similar lowest glucose level. However, the hypoglycemic duration (from the nadir to 18 mmol·L–1 blood glucose) differs among each group. The hypoglycemic durations in 4c- and 4d-treated groups were slightly longer than that of liraglutide (Figure 4B). In particular, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were able to maintain the hypoglycemic state in db/db mice for at least 24 h, comparable to that of semaglutide (Figure 4B,C). Interestingly, 4c and 4d with lower albumin binding affinities than liraglutide exhibited longer in vivo hypoglycemic durations than liraglutide, and it could be speculated that the Aib8 on 4c and 4d protected the cleavage by DPP-IV and the increased α-helicity of 4c and 4d caused by stable BisMAL was the main reason for the longer hypoglycemic durations of 4c and 4d than liraglutide. The above results demonstrated that peptide stapling and fatty acid modification using proper BisMAL cross-linkers not only enhanced the in vitro agonist potency but also extended in vivo half-lives. Considering that liraglutide and semaglutide are dosed once daily and once weekly,(28) respectively, in human, peptides 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x hold promise for being developed into long-acting hypoglycemic agents.
    Next, the effects of 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x to ameliorate obesity were evaluated in DIO mice and compared to those of liraglutide and semaglutide. Based on the in vivo pharmacodynamics property of each peptide, liraglutide, 4c, and 4d were s.c. treated twice daily while semaglutide, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were s.c. administered every 2 days. The changes in body weight, food intake, and blood glucose of DIO mice were monitored for 1 week. As shown in Figure 5A,B, twice daily administration of 4c and 4d for 1 week reduced body weight by ∼14.4 and ∼15.2%, respectively, significantly better than that of liraglutide (P < 0.001) with the same dosing frequent. Every 2 days s.c. administration of semaglutide, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x reduced body weight by ∼9.8, ∼13.4, ∼13.8, ∼10.6, and ∼10.2%, respectively, indicating that 4m and 4n exhibited better effects on body weight reduction than semaglutide (P < 0.001), whereas the effects of 4u and 4x were less pronounced. Food intakes of DIO mice revealed similar trends, and 4c, 4d, 4m, and 4n potently reduced body weight and also exhibited prominent effects on food intake reduction (Figure 5C,D). Moreover, all the stapled peptides effectively protected DIO mice from fasted hyperglycemia, superior to liraglutide and semaglutide (Figure 5E,F).

    Design of GLP-1R/CCK-2R Dual Agonists

    Stapled peptides 4c, 4d, 4m, and 4n, which were more effective in body weight reduction, were selected to further generate GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists. Native-sulfated gastrin-6 (Tyr(SO3)-Gly-Trp-Met-Asp-Phe-NH2) was previously used as a CCK-2R agonist, and more recently, Zealand Pharma introduced a nonsulfated gastrin-6 analogue (Tyr-Gly-Trp-Leu-Asp-Phe-NH2) to modify the C-terminus of exendin-4 linked by two OEG moieties.(18) There is debate and inconsistence regarding the role of tyrosine O-sulfation in CCK-2R agonist potency. Although previous research showed that the potency (EC50) of nonsulfated and sulfated gastrin-17 is equal, the difference between nonsulfated and sulfated gastrin-6 remains unexplored.(31) As a result, we decided to use both sulfated and nonsulfated gastrin-6 to clarify their bioactivity contributions. Instead of using stapled peptides 4c, 4d, 4m, and 4n, we commenced with unstapled peptides 2d and 2k to avoid any influence of the stapled cross-linkers on GLP-1R/CCK-2R activation potency and get a more direct insight into the role of gastrin-6. Thus, sulfated and nonsulfated gastrin-6 were coupled to the C-terminus of 2d and 2k through two OEG spacers, affording 5a–5d (Figure 6).
    For the synthesis of 5c and 5d containing a sulfated Tyr residue, Fmoc-Tyr(SO3-neopentyl)-OH was used, and the neopentyl-protecting group was specifically removed by aqueous ammonium acetate (Scheme S1). Crude products were purified and characterized using the same method as described above (see the Supporting Information, Table S5). The activation potencies of 5a–5d toward GLP-1R, CCK-1R, and CCK-2R were determined by cAMP or ERK1/2 phosphorylation (pERK1/2) functional assays, using 2 and Leu15-gastrin-17 as controls. As listed in Table 3 and Figure S4, the EC50 values of 5a–5d on GLP-1R were comparable to that of 2. The EC50 values of 5a–5d on CCK-2R were approximately three-fold lower than Leu15-gastrin-17, a full agonist for the CCK-2 receptor. Importantly, no activation of CCK-1R was observed with 5a–5d treatments. Because 5a–5d showed similar CCK-2R agonist efficacy, it indicated that both sulfated and nonsulfated gastrin-6 were suitable to confer high CCK-2R activation potency to Xenopus GLP-1 derivatives.

    OGTT and Insulin Secretion Test of 6a and 6b

    The hypoglycemic and insulinotropic effects of 6a and 6b were evaluated in db/db mice using ZP3022 as the control. As shown in Figure 7A–D, both 6a and 6b reduced the glucose excursion and potently improved glucose tolerance, whereas ZP3022 showed less glucose-lowering activity than 6a and 6b. Enhanced plasma insulin levels were observed in ZP3022-, 6a-, and 6b-treated groups, accompanied by declined blood glucose concentrations. Consistent with the hypoglycemic ability, 6a and 6b showed higher insulinotropic activity than ZP3022. The AUC values further suggested that the glucose-lowering and insulinotropic activities of 6a were better than 6b.

    Pharmacokinetics (PK) Study of 6a and 6b

    To determine the in vivo PK profiles of 6a and 6b, the PK assay was performed by s.c. injection of the tested peptides at 50 nmol·kg–1 in ICR mice. As shown in Figure 7E and Table S7, the PK data revealed highly enhanced in vivo t1/2 for 6a (4.4 h) and 6b (4.7 h) than ZP3022 (1.2 h). The absorption of 6a and 6b was protracted compared with ZP3022, as reflected by the higher maximum plasma concentration (Tmax) values of 6a (4 h) and 6b (4 h) than ZP3022 (2 h). In addition, enhancements of areas under the curve from zero to infinity (AUCinf) were observed for 6a (15,868 ng·h/mL) and 6b (19,638 ng·h/mL), indicating better drug utilizations of 6a and 6b than ZP3022 (9587 ng·h/mL).
    In addition, we designed two 6a and 6b analogues (7a and 7b, Figure S6) only with stable BisMAL and without a fatty acid albumin binder to investigate whether BisMAL itself could also improve the stability of our xGLP-1 analogues as we observed an interesting phenomenon that the selected stapled peptides 4c and 4d with lower albumin binding affinities than liraglutide exhibited longer in vivo hypoglycemic durations than liraglutide, and the possible reason was BisMAL could also improve the stability of 4c and 4d. The in vivo PK profiles of 6a, 6b, 7a, 7b as well as the corresponding unstapled peptides 5a and 5b were studied in SD rats by s.c. injection (50 nmol·kg–1). As shown in Figure S7 and Table S8, the t1/2 values of 7a and 7b were longer than 5a and 5b and shorter than 6a and 6b, respectively, indicating that BisMAL itself could also improve the stabilities of our xGLP-1 analogues. The increased α-helicity brought by stable BisMAL should be the main reason of the improved stability, and another possible reason was that the stable BisMAL blocked the rapid degradation by other enzymes such as neutral endopeptidase (NEP) 24.11.

    Hypoglycemic Duration and Food Intake Assays of 6a and 6b

    The antidiabetic durations of 6a and 6b were examined at two doses (25 and 100 nmol·kg–1) in db/db mice under a nonfasted condition. The amount of food intake was also recorded to evaluate the anorectic effects of 6a and 6b. According to the PK profiles of 6a and 6b, liraglutide, instead of semaglutide, was selected as the positive control. As shown in Figure 7F–I, both 6a and 6b exhibited dose-dependent glucose-lowering efficacy, and the duration of action of 6a or 6b was longer than ZP3022 and liraglutide, independent of the doses. In addition, the nadir of blood glucose levels in the 6a- or 6b-treated group was lower than ZP3022 and liraglutide, further proving the higher in vivo potency of 6a and 6b than ZP3022 and liraglutide. Food intake study revealed that 6a and 6b lead to prominent reductions of food intake in a dose-dependent manner. In addition, both 6a and 6b exhibited better anorectic effects than ZP3022 and liraglutide. Interestingly, although the half-life of 6a was shorter than 6b, 6a exhibited a stronger anorectic effect than 6b.

    Gastric Emptying Study of 6a and 6b

    The concentration of acetaminophen in blood could be used as an indicator of the rate of gastric emptying as the absorption of acetaminophen in stomach was negligible and occurred completely in small intestine. As shown in Figure 8A, both 6a and 6b potently reduced blood acetaminophen concentration, indicating a high inhibitory rate of gastric emptying. The AUC values of acetaminophen indicated that the effects of 6a and 6b on gastric emptying were better than ZP3022 and liraglutide. We further performed a solid-phase gastric emptying study as a direct test to strengthen our findings. The solid gastric emptying rate was obtained as the amount of food intake in the mice relative to the amount of dry stomach contents. As shown in Figure 8B, compared with the saline group, the gastric emptying rate in mice treated with 6a or 6b was decreased, to a more marked extent than ZP3022 or liraglutide treatment.

    Kaolin Consumption Assays of 6a and 6b

    While 6a and 6b were found to potently reduce food intake, the possible side effects such as nausea could not be overlooked. To this end, kaolin consumption was used as a well-established model of nausea to investigate the biocompatibility of 6a and 6b in SD rats. As shown in Figure 8C,D, cisplatin potently decreased food intake, accompanied by increased kaolin consumption in a 24 h acute assay, validating the successful establishment of the rat model. Liraglutide, ZP3022, 6a, and 6b (25 nmol·kg–1) potently reduced food intake without eliciting kaolin intake. In the following 12 day chronic test (Figure 8E,F), liraglutide, ZP3022, 6a, and 6b caused food intake reduction from day 2, and no obvious increase in kaolin consumption was observed even at day 10. All the above results preliminarily indicated that peripheral administration of 6a or 6b did not cause prominent nausea response in rats.

    Toxicity Assays of 6a and 6b

    The in vitro toxicity of 6a and 6b was evaluated by the MTT assay on INS-1 cells. As shown in Figure 8G,I, no impacts on cell viability were measured for both 6a and 6b up to a concentration of 1000 nM. Furthermore, the cell protective effects of 6a and 6b against glucotoxicity were evaluated. As shown in Figure 8H,J, glucotoxicity media reduced the cell viability in the saline-treated group by ∼27%, while 6a or 6b treatment dose-dependently recovered cell viability. At a dose of 100 nM, the cell protective effects of 6a and 6b surpassed those of liraglutide and ZP3022. Additionally, a 2 week toxicity test was performed in ICR mice. Satisfyingly, even if administered at a dose (1000 nmol·kg–1) that was 10 times higher than liraglutide and ZP3022 (100 nmol·kg–1), 6a and 6b led to no prominent changes in hepatotoxicity or renal toxicity indicators (Figure 8K,L), including aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), serum creatinine (SCr), and blood urea nitrogen (BUN). These results supported the safety of 6a and 6b for in vivo application.

    Metabolic Benefits of 6a and 6b in DIO Mice

    The metabolic benefits of 6a and 6b were comprehensively evaluated in DIO mice by s.c. injection twice daily and compared with equimolar doses of liraglutide (twice daily) and ZP3022 (three times a day). It was found that 6a and 6b significantly decreased body weight by ∼26.3 and ∼24.0%, respectively, as compared with ZP3022 and liraglutide (∼17.8 and ∼14.0%, respectively; P < 0.001) (Figure 9A,B). In addition, treatment with 6a and 6b led to ∼32.2 and ∼30.4% reduction of body fat content, respectively, larger than those treated with ZP3022 (∼24.5%) and liraglutide (∼21.4%) (P < 0.001, Figure 9C). Furthermore, 6a and 6b exhibited more profound effects on reducing food intake than ZP3022 and liraglutide (P < 0.001, Figure 9D), and the effects of 6a and 6b on reducing fasting blood glucose (Figure 9E and F) and improving glucose tolerance (Figure 9G–J) were more evident than those of ZP3022 and liraglutide. Moreover, 6a and 6b exhibited stronger lipid-regulating ability than ZP3022 and liraglutide after a 21 day treatment, leading to decreased serum cholesterol (TC) (Figure 9K) and triglycerides (TG) (Figure 9L).

    Three Week Treatment Effects of 6a and 6b in db/db Mice

    In a 21 day treatment in db/db mice, 6a and 6b caused a more profound reduction in cumulated food intake and body weight gain than ZP3022 and liraglutide (Figure 10A,B). The beneficial effects of 6a and 6b on fasted blood glucose and HbA1c were also found to surpass those of ZP3022 and liraglutide in db/db mice (Figure 10C–F). Notably, treatment with 6a or 6b effectively increased % β-cell area per islet, to a greater extent than ZP3022 and liraglutide treatments (P < 0.001, Figure 10G). Meanwhile, 6a or 6b treatment decreased % non-β-cell area (α-cell, Figure 10H), similar to ZP3022 or liraglutide treatment. Significantly increased islet areas and numbers were observed in mice treated with liraglutide, ZP3022, 6a, and 6b, and the increases were more evident in 6a- and 6b-treated groups (Figure 10I,J). Insulin staining results further revealed that the insulin content, which was reflected by the integrated optical density (IOD) values, was remarkably increased after 6a or 6b treatment, larger than those of ZP3022 and liraglutide treatments (Figure 10K). Representative H&E and insulin staining images are shown in Figure 10L–P.

    Long-Term Effects of 6a and 6b on Glycemic Control in db/db Mice

    Finally, the long-term effects of 6a and 6b on glycemic control were investigated in db/db mice during a 12 week treatment. ZP3022 was s.c. treated three times a day, while liraglutide, 6a, and 6b were s.c. treated twice daily. All peptides were dosed at 25 nmol·kg–1, and the treatments lasted for 6 weeks followed by another 6 week saline treatment for each group. On days 0, 42, 56, 70, and 84, OGTTs were performed to evaluate the glucose controlling ability of each group of mice (Figure 11A–E, respectively). As shown in Figure 11F, the AUC values of OGTTs clearly showed a gradually impaired glucose tolerance in mice treated with saline during the first 6 weeks. However, mice treated with ZP3022, liraglutide, 6a, and 6b resulted in prominent improvements in glucose tolerance, with the lowest AUC values observed in 6a- and 6b-treated groups. Importantly, mice treated with 6a or 6b exhibited long-lasting improvements in glucose tolerance during the following treatment-free period (weeks 7–12), as reflected by the AUC values, and the improvement effects were more evident than those in ZP3022- and liraglutide-treated groups (P < 0.001). The fasted blood glucose levels were also determined in each group, and the results showed a similar trend to that of the OGTT (Figure 11G). Moreover, reduced HbA1c values in ZP3022-, liraglutide-, 6a-, and 6b-treated groups were measured on day 43, as well as on day 85, 6 weeks after the cessation of peptide treatments (Figure 11H).

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, we focused on GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists, which we believe to have high prospect to be developed into new generation of antidiabetic agents. The sequence of Xenopus GLP-1, a selective GLP-1R agonist developed by us previously, was reengineered by (1) dual Cys mutation while maintaining the potent GLP-1R agonist activity, (2) side chain cross-linking to stabilize a secondary structure and achieve enhanced stability and bioactivity, and (3) gastrin-6 conjugation to gain CCK-2R agonist activity.
    After incorporating different serum protein binding motifs into stapled peptides linked by various spacers, Xenopus GLP-1 derivatives retained full GLP-1R activation potency and improved stability in rodents. Four peptides, 4c, 4d, 4m, and 4n, exhibited potent body weight reduction activity in DIO mice.
    After gastrin-6 conjugation, peptides 6a and 6b showed potent glucoregulatory effects and induced insulin secretion in a glucose-dependent manner in db/db mice. PK data showed that 6a and 6b had longer t1/2 than ZP3022. In db/db mice, the longer t1/2 of 6a and 6b contributed to better hypoglycemic activity and anorectic effects than both ZP3022 and liraglutide. It was found that 6a and 6b potently inhibited gastric emptying, to a great extent than ZP3022 and liraglutide, which might explain the better anorectic effects of 6a and 6b. Kaolin consumption assays in rat revealed that neither 6a nor 6b induced prominent nausea responses. Toxicity assays further indicated the safety of 6a and 6b in vitro and in vivo. The effects of 6a and 6b on weight loss, fat mass, food intake reduction, glucose tolerance, and lipid regulation in DIO mice were significantly better than those of ZP3022 and liraglutide. Importantly, 6a and 6b increased β-cell areas, islet areas, and amounts in db/db mice following a 3 week treatment. The 12 week study in db/db mice further proved the protective effects of 6a and 6b on pancreas. Preliminary docking studies of the xGLP-1 part in 6a and 6b (6a′ and 6b′, Figure S8 and Table S9) suggested a different binding mode between 6a and 6b to GLP-1R. However, further experimental data, such as the crystal structures of dual agonists and GLP-1R complexes, are required for a more accurate elucidation of the interactions of 6a and 6b with GLP-1R.
    Since the BisMAL we used was structurally asymmetric, 6a or 6b might exist in two forms. Although the HPLC analysis of 6a and 6b presented each peptide as a single peak, it may comprise an inseparable mixture of two isomers. Currently, it is difficult to identify which of the two stapled variants of 6a and 6b elicit the observed pharmacological effects. To circumvent this issue, a next-generation symmetrical serum-binding linker should be developed in the future to control the identity of the final stapled peptide.
    Taken together, the current research contributes to the discussion whether stapled GLP-1R/CCK-2R dual agonists based on Xenopus GLP-1 could serve as new therapeutic agents for diabetes and obesity. Dual agonists 6a and 6b exhibited high metabolic efficacy in DIO mice, exerted prominent effects on β-cell restoration, and achieved long-term glycemic control in db/db mice, supporting their potential roles as therapeutical options for metabolic diseases.

    Synthesis of Peptides

    The peptide backbones of 1, 1a–1m, 2, 2a–2m, 3, 3a–3d, 4a–4x, 5a–5d, and 6a–6d were synthesized by the standard Fmoc-based solid-phase synthesis strategy and were conducted on Rink MBAH resin using a semiautomated PSI-200 peptide synthesizer (Peptide Scientific Inc., USA).(32) For 4a–4x and 6a–d with side chain cross-linkers, Fmoc-Cys(Mmt)-OH was incorporated into the cysteine modification sites, and the N-terminal position 1 was replaced with Boc-His(Boc)-OH. After accomplishment of the peptide backbones, the Mmt protection was specifically removed using 1% TFA in CH2Cl2. Then, BisMAL was dissolved in DMF, and the cross-linking reaction was conducted by incubating BisMAL with resins containing dicysteine peptides with gentle shaking for 2–3 h. After that, palmitic acid, Fmoc-OEG-OH, Fmoc-Glu-OtBu, and octadecanedioic acid mono-tert-butyl ester were added in order according to the structures of 4a–4x and 6a–6d and coupled by HBTU/HOBT/DIPEA. All the peptides were released from the Rink resin with Reagent K cocktail (EDT/thioanisole/water/phenol/TFA, 2.5:5:5:5:82.5) and precipitated in cold ether. For 5c and 5d containing neopentyl-protected sTyr, the crude peptides were treated with 2 M aq ammonium acetate in acetonitrile, and the mixture was kept at 55 °C for 12 h. The reaction mixture was then purified by RP-HPLC (Shimadzu LC-20AP). The purities of the purified peptides were analyzed by analytical HPLC (Agilent 1260 Infinity) with a C18 column (Shimadzu Shim-pack PREP-ODS(H) kit, 5 μm, 250 mm × 4.6 mm) with a linear gradient of 50–90% of solvent B at a flow rate of 0.8 mL/min (A, water with 0.1% TFA; B, methanol) and confirmed to be of ≥95% purity for all peptides as determined by UV absorption at 214 nm. The identity of the product was established by MS (Bruker micrOTOF Q2 or Agilent 6538 Q-TOF mass).

    GLP-1R and CCKR Activation Measurement

    Using previously described methods, activation of human GLP-1R was determined by cAMP formation, and activation of human CCK-1R and CCK-2R was determined by pERK induction.(21,33) For each assay, HEK293 cells expressing receptors were grown in DMEM (Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium) supplemented with 10% v/v FBS, 100 units·mL–1 penicillin, 100 μg·mL–1 streptomycin, 2 mM l-glutamine, and 100 μg·mL–1 G418. HEK293 cells expressing GLP-1R were washed, resuspended in assay buffer (20 mM HEPES, 1× HBSS, 2 mM IBMX, 0.1% BSA), and plated into 96-well plates coated with 0.01% poly-l-lysine before testing. Test peptides were dissolved in DMSO and diluted in medium containing 0.1% BSA and transferred to the cells. For the high HSA (4.4%) assays, peptides 4a–4x, liraglutide, and semaglutide were dissolved in DMSO and diluted in medium containing HSA and transferred to the cells to reach final HSA concentration at a physiological level (4.4%). The cells were incubated for 20 min at 37 °C. Lysis buffer was added, and cAMP content was estimated using a cAMP dynamic 2 kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions.
    HEK293 cells expressing CCK-1R and CCK-2R were seeded into 96-well plates coated with 0.01% poly-l-lysine and kept for 2 days at 5% CO2 and 37 °C. Then, cells were incubated overnight in serum-free medium (DMEM with l-glutamine containing 1× nonessential amino acids and 1 mM sodium pyruvate) at 5% CO2 and 37 °C. On day 3, test peptides dissolved in DMSO were diluted in serum-free medium and added into the cells at room temperature for 5 min. Then, cells were lysed, and the content of pERK was determined using an AlphaScreen SureFire ERK 1/2 assay kit (PerkinElmer). The results for cAMP and pERK were determined using an EnVision 2104 Multilabel reader (PerkinElmer). Data were normalized to maximal GLP-1 or Leu15-gastrin-17 response, and the potency (EC50) was calculated by GraphPad Prism 5.0. All experiments were performed in triplicate and repeated three times.

    OGTT in ICR Mice

    Male ICR mice (20–25 g, n = 6) were acclimated for 1 week and randomly divided. Mice were fasted overnight, and saline, GLP-1, 1, 2, 3, 1d, 1k, 2d, 2k, 3d, and 3k (25 nmol·kg–1) were injected (i.p.) 15 min before oral glucose loading (3 g·kg–1). Tail blood was collected at −15, 0, 15, 30, 60, and 120 min through cutting the tip tail (one drop). A OneTouch Sannuo handheld glucometer (GA-3, Changsha, China) was used to measure the blood glucose levels in each group.

    Circular Dichroism (CD) Measurements

    CD spectra of 1, 2, 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were recorded using a JASCO model J-810 CD spectrometer at room temperature in a path length cuvette (2 mm). Spectral data were recorded from 190 to 250 nm and averaged over 3 scans with a bandwidth of 1 nm and at a scan speed 50 nm/min. All tested peptides were dissolved in phosphate buffer containing 20% TFE to reach a final concentration of 50 μM. The signal of solvent was subtracted, and the spectra were plotted by mean residue ellipticity versus wavelength.(26)

    Pharmacodynamics Studies in db/db Mice

    The glucose homeostasis durations of semaglutide, liraglutide, 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were tested using nonfasted db/db mice.(29) In brief, male db/db mice (8 weeks, n = 6) were randomly divided into nine groups and acclimatized for 1 week before the study. Then, saline and 25 nmol·kg–1 semaglutide, liraglutide, 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were s.c. injected, and blood glucose levels in each group were determined at 0, 2, 4, 6, 24, and 48 h using a Sannuo glucometer. Mice were allowed free access to rodent chow and water during the whole experiment.

    Human Serum Albumin Binding Assay

    The albumin binding characteristics of liraglutide, semaglutide, 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were evaluated using a modified albumin binding assay by albumin-conjugated Sepharose resin, using the previously described method.(34) In brief, 4 mL of NHS-activated Sepharose 4FF (wet resin volume, Amersham Pharmacia Biotech (China) Ltd.) and 30 mg of HSA in 10 mM PBS (pH = 7.4) were mixed and reacted for 4 h at 25 °C by gentle shaking. After centrifugation, the recovered albumin-conjugated resin was washed by PBS three times. The HSA content in the resin used was 7–8 mg/mL wet resin. For the albumin binding assay, liraglutide, semaglutide, 4c, 4d, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x (50 μL, 100 μg/mL in PBS) were mixed with albumin-conjugated resin and incubated at 25 °C for 2 h. The supernatant and resin were separated by centrifugation at 2000 rpm for 10 min, and the amount of unbound peptides was determined by LC–MS/MS. The albumin-free resin was obtained by inactivating the resin through hydrolysis of the NHS-active ester, and nonspecific absorption of tested peptides on the NHS-inactivated resin was determined in a similar manner.

    One Week Study in DIO Mice

    After the body weight reaching a plateau, male C57BL/6 mice (∼6 months, n = 6) were randomly divided into nine groups and housed individually.(35) Saline and 25 nmol·kg–1 liraglutide, 4c, and 4d were s.c. injected twice daily, and 25 nmol·kg–1 semaglutide, 4m, 4n, 4u, and 4x were s.c. injected every 2 days for 1 week. Body weight and food intake were recorded on days 1, 3, 5, and 7. Fasting blood glucose levels were determined on days −1 and 8 from mice fasted for 6 h.

    OGTT and Insulin Secretion Tests in db/db Mice

    Male db/db mice (8 weeks, n = 6) were randomly divided into four groups and acclimatized for 1 week before the study. Each group of mice was fasted overnight (16 h), and saline and 25 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022, 6a, and 6b were i.p. loaded at −30 min followed by an oral loading of 1.5 g·kg–1 glucose at 0 min. One drop of blood was collected at −30, 0, 15, 30, 60, and 120 min for blood glucose level monitoring. At 0, 15, 30, and 60 min, additional eight drops of blood were collected to obtain ∼10 μL of plasma. Plasma insulin levels were determined using a mouse insulin ELISA kit (Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute, catalog no. NJB201909).(20)

    Pharmacokinetics (PK) Tests

    Pharmacokinetics studies of ZP3022, 6a, and 6b were performed in ICR mice using the previously described method.(32) Male ICR mice (20–25 g) were acclimatized until body weight reached ∼30 g. Mice were randomly divided into three groups (n = 12 for ZP3022; n = 18 for 6a and 6b) and fasted overnight before the experiment. On the test day, each group of mice received 50 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022, 6a, and 6b (s.c.). At 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 24 h, three ICR mice were sacrificed, and ∼200 μL of blood samples was collected from fundus venous plexus and stored in ice cooled tubes containing EDTA. The plasma was isolated by centrifugation at 3000 rpm and stored at −80 °C until analysis. After thawing the plasma, two volumes of acetonitrile with 0.5% formic acid were added for precipitation of the protein. Then, the mixture was subjected to centrifugation (15,000 rpm, 10 min) to obtain a supernatant, which was analyzed by LC–MS/MS. The concentrations of ZP3022, 6a, and 6b were calculated using standard curves plotted by multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) signals versus tested peptide concentrations. Pharmacokinetics parameters were calculated using BAPP version 2.2 (China Pharmaceutical University, Nanjing, China).

    Dose Response for Hypoglycemic Duration and Food Intake in db/db Mice

    The dose response studies for hypoglycemic duration of ZP3022, liraglutide, 6a, and 6b were performed using nonfasted db/db mice. Male db/db mice (8 weeks, n = 6) were randomly divided into 10 groups and acclimatized for 1 week. On the experiment day, five groups of mice were s.c. injected with saline or 25 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022, liraglutide, 6a, and 6b, and the other five groups of mice were s.c. injected with saline or 100 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022, liraglutide, 6a, and 6b. The blood glucose levels were determined at 0, 4, 6, 12, 24, and 48 h. For food intake consumption recording, the rodent chows were preweighed and reweighed at 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12, and 24 h to calculate food intake consumption. The food spillage was also checked for accuracy.

    Gastric Emptying Studies

    Two protocols were applied for the gastric emptying studies. The liquid-phase gastric emptying rate was tested by the acetaminophen absorption test.(36) Male ICR mice (20–25 g) were randomly divided into five groups (n = 6). After being acclimatized for 7 days, each group of mice was deprived for food and fasted for 6 h. Saline and 25 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022, liraglutide, 6a, and 6b were i.p. injected at −30 min. Acetaminophen solution (100 mg·kg–1) was then orally administered at 0 min. Blood samples (∼50 μL) were collected from tail vein into tubes containing EDTA. The procedures of obtaining plasma and precipitating protein were the same as described above in PK tests. The concentration of acetaminophen was determined by LC–MS/MS. To measure the rate of solid-phase gastric emptying, ICR mice were divided into five groups (n = 6) and housed individually. Mice were fasted overnight (12 h), and preweighed rodent chows were supplied for 1 h. Saline and 25 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022, liraglutide, 6a, and 6b were i.p. injected. Each group of mice was sacrificed 4 h after peptide injection. Stomach was isolated once pylorus and cardia were quickly ligated. The gastric contents were removed and dried using a VFD-1000B freeze dryer (Bilon, Shanghai, China). The rate of solid-phase gastric emptying was determined as [1 – (weight of stomach content/weight of food intake)] × 100%.(37)

    Kaolin Consumption Test

    Kaolin pellets were made according to the previously described method.(21) In brief, a thick paste was made by mixing kaolin with 1% gum arabic, and the rods with similar diameter to rodent chow were obtained by extruding the thick paste through a syringe. The rods were dried at room temperature and were cut into pellets with comparable length to standard food pellets. Male SD rats were individually housed and randomly divided into six groups (n = 6). Four days prior to the experiment, apart from the standard rodent chow, kaolin pellets were also provided in a separate food hopper. Kaolin and rodent chow were removed for 16 h before the experiment, and each group of mice was i.p. administered with saline (10% DMSO), cisplatin (3 mg·kg–1), ZP3022, liraglutide, 6a, and 6b (25 nmol·kg–1). Preweighted kaolin and rodent chow were immediately provided, and the consumption of kaolin and rodent chow was determined at 24 h. In another experiment, five groups of SD rats (n = 6) were housed and acclimatized using the same procedure as described above. On day 0, the consumption of rodent chow and kaolin in the last 24 h was determined, and each group of mice was s.c. administered three times a day with saline and ZP3022 (25 nmol·kg–1) or s.c. administered twice daily with liraglutide, 6a, and 6b (25 nmol·kg–1) for 12 days. The consumption of kaolin and rodent chow was determined every 2 days.

    Toxicity Assay

    The in vitro toxicity study of 6a and 6b was conducted on INS-1 cells using the previously described method.(22) In brief, cells (∼10,000 cells/well) were kept in RPMI 1640 medium with supplements and cultured in a polypropylene plate (96-well) for 24 h. Cells were then directly treated with ZP3022, liraglutide (100 nM), 6a, and 6b (10, 100, and 1000 nM) or treated with ZP3022, liraglutide (100 nM), 6a, and 6b (10, 100, and 1000 nM) combined with glucotoxicity media (30 mM glucose). After 24 h, 5 mg·mL–1 MTT solution was added and incubated for another 5 h. DMSO was added to dissolve the formazan precipitate, and the absorbance at 570 nm was determined using a Thermo spectrophotometer (Waltham, MA, USA). The absorbance results in the saline, ZP3022, liraglutide, 6a, and 6b groups were normalized to the control cells. For in vivo study, male ICR mice (20–25 g) were divided randomly into five groups (n = 6) and acclimatized for 1 week. Then, mice were s.c. treated twice daily with saline, 100 nmol·kg–1 liraglutide and ZP3022, and 1000 nmol·kg–16a and 6b for 14 days. Mice were sacrificed on day 15 to collect the whole blood followed by centrifugation to obtain serum. The values of AST, ALT, SCr, and BUN were measured using a Beckman AU5811 Coulter automatic analyzer.

    Metabolic Benefits of 6a and 6b in DIO Mice

    Male C57BL/6 mice were randomly divided into five groups (n = 6) and individually housed. Mice were fed with high-fat diet for at least 18 weeks until the body weight reached a plateau.(5) Saline and 25 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022 were s.c. injected three times a day, and 25 nmol·kg–1 liraglutide, 6a, and 6b were s.c. injected twice daily for 21 days. Body weight changes and food intake were monitored daily and every 3 days, respectively. The fat mass was measured using NMR technology (MiniQMR) on days −1 and 22. Fasted blood glucose levels were determined on days −1 and 22 after mice were fasted for 6 h. Two OGTTs were conducted on days −2 and 23 in mice fasted for 12 h. Glucose (1.5 g·kg–1) was orally loaded at 0 min, and at 0, 15, 30, 60, and 120 min, blood glucose levels were determined. The mice were all sacrificed to collect the whole blood. The serum triglycerides and total cholesterol were measured using a Beckman AU5811 Coulter automatic analyzer.

    Short-Term Effects of 6a and 6b on Pancreas Development

    Male db/db mice (9 weeks, n = 6) were randomly divided into five groups and acclimatized for 1 week before the study. Saline and 25 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022 were s.c. injected three times a day, and 25 nmol·kg–1 liraglutide, 6a, and 6b were s.c. injected twice daily for 21 days. The amount of food intake and body weight was measured daily. Fasted blood glucose levels were determined on days −1 and 22 after mice were fasted for 6 h. The HbA1c levels in each group of mice were determined using a DCA 2000 chemistry analyzer before and after the experiment. At the end of the study, mice were killed, and the pancreas was immediately removed and stored in 4% paraformaldehyde. The procedures for hematoxylin–eosin (HE) staining and immunohistochemistry study were the same as described previously.(23,38) The percentage of α- and β-cell areas were determined by glucagon and insulin immunostaining, respectively. The insulin integrated optical density (IOD) levels were recorded by a Nikon DS-Ri2 fluorescence microscope using Image-Pro Plus 6.0 software (Media Cybernetics Inc., USA).

    Long-Term Effects of 6a and 6b on Glycemic Control

    Male db/db mice (8 weeks) were fasted overnight (16 h) and subjected to an OGTT before the experiment.(18) Glucose (1.5 g·kg–1) was orally loaded at 0 min, and at −30, 0, 15, 30, 60, and 120 min, blood glucose levels were determined. Based on the OGTT results, mice with similar glucose tolerance profiles were divided into four groups (n = 6). Saline and 25 nmol·kg–1 ZP3022 were s.c. injected three times a day, and 25 nmol·kg–1 liraglutide, 6a, and 6b were s.c. injected twice daily for 42 days. In the following 42 days, each group of mice was s.c. injected twice daily with saline. OGTTs were performed on days 42, 56, 70, and 84, and on days 0, 35, 49, 63, and 77, mice were fasted for 6 h to determine the fasted blood glucose levels. The levels of HbA1c were measured on days −1, 43, and 85 using the same method as described above.

    Data Analysis

    All statistical calculations were conducted using GraphPad Prism 5.0 software, and P values <0.05 were considered as statistically significant. The statistical evaluations of the data were performed by one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey post-hoc analysis. All results are expressed as means ± SD.
