    Abstract

    We previously discovered that indazole derivative 8 was a highly selective β3-adrenergic receptor (β3-AR) agonist, but it appeared to be metabolically unstable. To improve metabolic stability, further optimization of this scaffold was carried out. We focused on the sulfonamide moiety of this scaffold, which resulted in the discovery of compound 15 as a highly potent β3-AR agonist (EC50 = 18 nM) being inactive to β1-, β2-, and α1A-AR (β1/β3, β2/β3, and α1A/β3 > 556-fold). Compound 15 showed dose-dependent β3-AR-mediated responses in marmoset urinary bladder smooth muscle, had a desirable metabolic stability and pharmacokinetic profile (Cmax and AUC), and did not obviously affect heart rate or mean blood pressure when administered intravenously (3 mg/kg) to anesthetized rats. Thus, compound 15 is a highly potent, selective, and orally available β3-AR agonist, which may serve as a candidate drug for the treatment of overactive bladder without off-target-based cardiovascular side effects.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The International Union of Pharmacology recognizes three adrenergic receptor (AR) subfamilies, the α1-, α2-, and β-AR subfamilies, with β1-, β2-, and β3-ARs as members of the latter.(1) The β1-AR is the dominant receptor in heart and adipose tissue, whereas the β2-AR is responsible for relaxation of vascular, uterine, and airway smooth muscle.(2) The β3-AR mediates metabolic effects such as lipolysis and thermogenesis in adipose tissues.(3) Given that β3-AR mRNA is present in human white fat, gall bladder, stomach, small intestine, prostate, and urinary bladder detrusor,(4) this particular receptor represents an attractive target for drug discovery efforts that may culminate in the development of potent and selective β3-AR agonists.(5) Because many β3-AR agonists induce weight loss in obese animals and exert antidiabetic effects in rodent models of type 2 diabetes, these agonists were investigated in clinical trials for the treatment of obesity and/or diabetes.(6) However, these agonists failed during early clinical investigations, likely because the expression pattern of β3-AR in most animal species differs considerably from that in humans, particularly in adipose tissues.(7) Moreover, many of the agonists also showed either poor pharmacokinetic (PK) profiles and/or cardiovascular adverse effects due to a lack of selectivity for the β3-AR over β1- and β2-ARs.(8) However, these earlier β3-AR agonists were observed to relax isolated rat and human detrusor muscle strips and to increase bladder capacity in rats.(9) Therefore, pharmaceutical companies have recently been endeavoring to discover potent, selective, and orally bioavailable β3-AR agonists for the treatment of overactive bladder (OAB).(10)
    OAB is defined as urgency with or without urinary incontinence, usually associated with frequency and nocturia.(11) Population-based estimations demonstrated that OAB affects 11.8% of adults and that its rates are similar between men and women and increase with age.(12) OAB can be extremely distressing to the patient and has been shown to negatively affect quality of life.(13) OAB is associated with enhanced voiding frequency, increased risk of depression, sleep disorders, skin infections, and falls with fractures.(13) Detrusor overactivity is a urodynamic observation characterized by involuntary detrusor contractions during the filling phase that may be spontaneous or provoked.(14) Muscarinic antagonists have been used as first-line treatment drugs for OAB for many years.(15) These drugs demonstrate improvement in continence, number of micturitions, and urgency.(16) However, they are associated with mechanistic side effects such as dry mouth, constipation, and blurred vision, and in rare instances with urinary retention.(16) A recent study found that the persistence rates with pharmacotherapy for OAB were ≤35% for 12 months after the initial prescription of muscarinic antagonists.(17) The side effects can be related to the limitation of their long-term adherence in the pharmacotherapy of OAB, at least to some extent.(18) Therefore, alternative drugs are sought to both avoid adverse effects and provide more potent therapeutic options. The β3-AR agonists have emerged as a promising class of such drugs because this class may be less prone toward inducing adverse effects in comparison with muscarinic antagonists.(19)
    Clinical studies have examined several β3-AR agonists such as 1 (mirabegron, YM-178),(20)2 (solabegron, GW427353),(21)3 (ritobegron, KUC-7483),(22) and 4 (vibegron, MK-4618),(10a) which are shown in Figure 1. In 2012, Mirabegron was approved for the treatment of OAB,(23) and its treatment showed significant efficacy for OAB and resulted in a lower dry mouth incidence compared to muscarinic antagonists.(19) However, treatment with mirabegron caused hypertension and increased heart rate (HR) in both animals and healthy individuals.(19, 24) We hypothesize that the increased HR resulting from treatment with mirabegron is at least partially induced via activation of the β1-AR,(24a) and accordingly, highly selective novel β3-AR agonists would be effective drugs for the treatment of OAB without the risk of such cardiovascular side effects as increased HR. In contrast to mirabegron, which inhibits CYP2D6 and generally should not be coadministered with CYP2D6 substrates,(25) highly selective novel β3-AR agonists should be inactive to CYP enzymes.
    In our previous publication, we reported on novel indazole derivatives, represented by compound 8, which exhibited a highly selective β3-AR agonistic activity without cardiovascular side effects.(10c) However, PK studies of 8 in rats indicated that it was metabolically unstable and had poor PK properties (Cmax and AUC). On the basis of these findings, we performed a structure–activity relationship (SAR) study of the sulfonamide moiety of the indazole derivatives to improve the metabolic stability while maintaining the excellent selectivity over α1A-AR and obtained the optimized compound 15, which had more desirable PK properties than 8 in rats. We also conducted a docking study at the β3-AR binding site, which identified the structural features required for high affinity for the β3-AR as well as high selectivity for the β3-AR over α1A-, β1-, and β2-ARs. Thus, we have successfully identified compound 15 as a highly potent, selective, and orally available β3-AR agonist lacking cardiovascular side effects.

    These previous results indicated that the selectivity of our indazole compounds for the β3-AR and α1A-AR changed depending on the substituent at the 3-positon. Therefore, when ordered in terms of their selectivity for the β3-AR over α1A-AR, the indazole compounds ranked as follows: iso-propyl (8) > cyclobutyl (7) > ethyl (6) > methyl (5). In contrast, the rank order of these compounds in terms of their metabolic stability (hCLint,u) was as follows: methyl (5) > ethyl (6) > iso-propyl (8) > cyclobutyl (7). Thus, highly selective compounds (7 and 8) showed poor metabolic stability.
    Our aim was to improve the metabolic stability of these compounds while maintaining highly selective β3-AR agonist activity. In sulfonamide-containing AR agonists, the α1A- or β3-AR agonistic activity can be manipulated by changing the sulfonamide structure.(26) Therefore, we decided to optimize the metabolically stable 3-methyl indazole derivative 5 by modifying its sulfonamide moiety in order to improve its β3-AR agonistic selectivity over α1A-AR.
    Altenbach et al. reported a SAR study on potent sulfonamide-containing α1A-AR agonist 9 (Figure 2),(26a) for which increases in the size of the aliphatic group (R1) of the sulfonamide resulted in decreased α1A-AR potency while aromatic substitution of the sulfonamide made the compounds inactive to α1A-AR.(26a) On the other hand, when Sawa et al. compared aliphatic sulfonamide 10a with aromatic sulfonamide 10b, the β3-AR agonistic potency of 10b was higher than that of 10a.(26b) Taking these findings into account, we hypothesized that changing the size of the substitution on the sulfonamide of compound 5 would improve selectivity for β3-AR agonistic activity over α1A-AR without sacrificing its desirable human microsome, rat microsome, and rat PK profile (Figure 2).

    SAR of the Sulfonamide Moiety

    Agonistic activities for the β3-AR of analogues of 5, which were modified at the sulfonamide moiety, are shown in Table 3. Replacement of the methyl substituent of 5 with an n-Pr (12) slightly increased β3-AR agonistic potency (5, EC50 = 21 nM vs 12, EC50 = 6.5 nM). However, by replacing the methyl substituent with i-Pr (13), the β3-AR agonistic activity was decreased (5, EC50 = 21 nM vs 13, EC50 = 70 nM). We next synthesized and evaluated compounds with a cycloalkyl substitution; replacement with a cyclopropyl or cyclobutyl group increased β3-AR agonistic potency (13, EC50 = 70 nM vs 14, EC50 = 43 nM; 15, EC50 = 18 nM). However, replacement with a cyclopentyl group (16) decreased potency (13, EC50 = 70 nM vs 16, EC50 = 309 nM). On the other hand, the phenyl sulfonamide derivative 17 (EC50 = 3.9 nM) showed higher potency than the alkyl congeners. Thus, in order of their β3-AR agonist activity, the compounds were ranked as follows: 17 > 12 > 15, 5 > 14 > 13 > 16.

    ADME Profiles

    ADME profiles of 15 and 17 as well as 8 in vitro are summarized in Table 5. Although human microsomal clearance of 15 was excellent (hCLint,u = 57 mL/min/kg), that of 17 was not acceptable (hCLint,u = 464 mL/min/kg, 8-fold worse than 15). Rat microsomal clearance of 15 was also acceptable (rCLint,u = 52 mL/min/kg, 4-fold better than 8). Both 15 and 17 had moderate MDCK cell monolayer permeability and good solubility in pH 1.2 and pH 6.8 solutions. Compound 15 also had improved Cmax, AUC and half-life values in comparison with 8.

    Compound 15 was next evaluated with a panel of cytochrome P450 enzymes, where it showed only insignificant inhibition of the CYPs (<34% inhibition at 10 μM against CYP1A2, 2B6, 2C19, 2C8, 2C9, 2D6, and 3A4). In the same evaluation, 1 showed a significant inhibition of 87% at 10 μM against CYP2D6. The low inhibitory capacity of 15 against CYPs suggests that 15 has a much lower risk of drug–drug interactions than 1 when coadministered with CYP-metabolized drugs.

    Cardiovascular Safety Profiles and Pharmacology

    We compared the cardiovascular safety profile of 15, which has highly selective β3-AR agonistic activity and an excellent ADME profile, with the clinical drug 1. As shown in Table 6, 1 (iv, 3 mg/kg) clearly increased HR and decreased mean blood pressure (MBP) in anesthetized rats. In the same evaluation, compound 15 (iv, 3 mg/kg) tended to increase HR and decrease MBP less than 1 as a preliminary experiment.(27) These data indicate that 15 possesses a desirable cardiovascular safety profile, likely due to its minimal off-target activities; it is highly active to the β3-AR but completely inactive to all other α1- and β-ARs.

    Next, we investigated the effect of 15 on the relaxation of urinary bladder smooth muscle strips from marmosets.(10c) As shown in Figure 3, 15 fully relaxed isolated marmoset urinary bladder smooth muscle that was precontracted with KCl, and its efficacy was similar to that of the nonselective β-AR agonist isoproterenol.
    On the basis of its excellent β3-AR selectivity, oral availability, low risk of drug–drug interactions, and favorable relaxation effect on marmoset urinary bladder smooth muscle, the optimized compound 15 was identified as a candidate drug for the treatment of OAB without cardiovascular side effects.

    Chemistry

    The synthesis of compounds 12–17 is shown in Scheme 1. Aminoalcohol 20 was prepared by coupling (R)-2-(3-nitrophenyl)oxirane 18(28) with benzylamine 19 in 2-PrOH. Protection of the hydroxyl of 20 with triethylsilyl chloride (TESCl) and imidazole afforded 21. Hydrogenolysis of 21 reduced the nitro group and deprotected N-Bn, and the alkyl amine was protected by using Boc2O and then the O-Bn group was deprotected under hydrogenolysis conditions to afford alcohol 22. Mitsunobu reaction of 22 and 23(10c) using DMEAD(29) and triphenylphosphine proceeded to give the aniline 24. Coupling of a sulfonyl chloride with aniline 24 and subsequent deprotection of Boc and TES groups afforded the corresponding target compounds 12–17.

    Docking Study to Characterize β3-AR Agonistic Activity and Selectivity over α1A-, β1-, and β2-ARs

    A docking study of the indazole series of the agonists was undertaken in order to understand the SAR underlying the β3- and α1A-AR agonistic activities. No crystal structure of the β3-AR is known; therefore, we constructed a homology model of the β3-AR. Previous studies showed that β3-AR homology models, which were constructed using the crystal structure of the β2-AR, were useful for providing structural insight into ligand binding conformation, performing QSAR analyses, and conducting in silico drug discovery.(10a, 10d, 31) Recent resolutions of the β2-AR active conformation(32) allowed us to generate more accurate models of the β3-AR with β3-AR agonist using homology modeling and docking simulation. We constructed an in silico homology model of the β3-AR based on the β2-AR active conformation (PDB 3P0G). The β3-AR sequence was aligned with the β1-, β2-, and α1A-ARs using MOE 2015 software (Chemical Computing Group, Montreal, Canada), and the alignment was refined manually to better match the position of amino acids with the corresponding β2-AR structure (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The sequence identity between β3- and β2-ARs is 55.7% based on sequence alignment.(31b)
    First, we constructed a homology model of the β3-AR with compound 17, which is the most potent β3-AR agonist in the indazole series. Using this model, we performed a docking simulation with a structurally diverse indazole series of eight compounds, which included the previously reported compounds,(10c) in addition to another series that consisted of KUC-7322 (active form of compound 3),(22) two analogues of 10,(26b) and isoproterenol. Previously, our group used a similar strategy for a docking simulation to characterize the histamine H3 receptor binding mode.(33) The correlation between the calculated docking score and the −pEC50 was determined. As shown in Figure 4, a strong correlation was observed between docking score and −pEC50 (R = 0.67 for all 12 compounds; R = 0.90 for the eight indazole compounds). These results show that the homology model and docking pose of the ligand are in agreement with the SAR. To further validate the docking pose of compound 15, which offers the best profile for potency, selectivity, and ADME within our indazole series, a molecular dynamics (MD) simulation was performed. An enormous difference between the pose suggested by docking and final ligand pose after a MD simulation has been reported in spite of a high docking score.(34) To examine temporal changes in compound 15 with the β3-AR during 20 ns of the MD simulation, the root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) was calculated for compound 15 and the β3-AR throughout the simulation time (Figure S2 in Supporting Information). The time series of the RMSD of compound 15 show that the binding mode is overall stable. Accordingly, compound 15 with the β3-AR after the MD refinement was further evaluated for its interaction with and selectivity for the β3-AR over α1A-, β1-, and β2-ARs.
    The proposed binding mode of compound 15 with the β3-AR after the MD refinement is shown in Figure 5A. The obtained mode suggested that compound 15 interacts with the three key conserved residues, Asp117 (transmembrane [TM] 3), Ser208 (TM5), and Asn332 (TM7), as observed in the crystal structure of the BI-167107−β2-AR complex (PDB 3P0G). An additional hydrogen bonding was predicted between the indazole NH of compound 15 and backbone of Cys196 (extracellular loop [ECL] 2); the indazole moiety participates in hydrophobic interactions with residues Leu97 (TM2), Leu329 (TM7), and Trp333 (TM7) (Figure 5A,B). Moreover, hydrogen bonding also occurred between the sulfonamide carbonyl oxygen and Asn312 (TM6), a T-shaped π–π interaction was predicted between the phenyl ring and Phe309 (TM6), and the cyclobutane moiety occupied a hydrophobic pocket formed by residues Val118 (TM3), Ile173 (TM4), and Tyr204 (TM5) (Figure 5A,B).
    We next examined selectivity for the β3-AR over α1A-AR based on the proposed β3-AR binding model of compound 15 and the sequence alignment with the α1A-AR. Previously, we reported that the selectivity of different indazole 3-moieties for the β3-AR over α1A-AR was ranked as follows: i-Pr (8) > cyclobutyl (7) > ethyl (6) > −Me (5) > −CH2OH.(10c) These results suggest that a hydrophobic substituent effectively induces improved selectivity for the β3-AR over α1A-AR. The indazole moiety participates in hydrophobic interactions with residues Leu97 (TM2), Leu329 (TM7), and Trp333 (TM7) of the β3-AR in the binding model. The residue Leu329 is not conserved in the α1A-AR; in α1A-AR, these residues are mutated to the hydrophilic residue Lys309. Therefore, the environment of this pocket in the α1A-AR might be more hydrophilic than that in the β3-AR. Thus, the SAR results on selectivity for the β3-AR over α1A-AR are supported by the results obtained from our docking model of the β3-AR. On the other hand, when compared by their selectivity for the β3-AR over α1A-AR, the substituents at the sulfonamide moiety were ordered as follows: phenyl (17) > cyclobutyl (15) ≫ n-propyl (12) > methyl (5) (Table 2). This ordering suggests that a more bulky substituent improves selectivity for the β3-AR over α1A-AR. The cyclobutyl substituent displayed hydrophobic interactions with Val118 (TM3), Ile173 (TM4), and Tyr204 (TM5) in β3-AR homology modeling. The residue Ile173 is not conserved and is replaced with Leu162 in the α1A-AR. Moreover, Phe198, Ala199, and Ser200 of ECL2, which is located proximal to the sulfone group of the sulfonamide in the β3-AR, are not conserved in the α1A-AR; in the α1A-AR, these residues correspond to Ile178, Asn179, and Glu180, respectively. Interactions of α1A-AR’s residues with the cyclobutyl substituent and sulfone group may thus differ significantly from those of β3-AR’s residues based on differences in their overall size, protonation potential, charge density, and overall hydrogen bond-forming ability with neighboring residues or ligands. Consequently, these differences may account for the selectivity of β3-AR over α1A-AR agonistic activity in our indazole analogues.
    We also considered the selectivity for the β3-AR over β1- and β2-ARs based on the proposed β3-AR binding model of compound 15. As previously observed by Edmondon et al.,(10a) the Ala197 (ECL2) residue of β3-AR is a critical component for selectivity for activation of β3-AR over binding to β1- and β2-ARs. The indazole moiety of compound 15 formed a hydrogen-bonding interaction with the backbone of Cys196, and the Ala197 residue is located proximal to the indazole NH group (Figure 5A). The Ala197 replaces an Asp residue in β1- and β2-ARs (Asp217 in β1-AR, Asp192 in β2-ARs). The Asp residue in β1- and β2-ARs may lead to unfavorable interactions with this hydrogen-bonding with respect to both electrostatic interactions and size. Therefore, compound 15 is likely to show high selectivity for the β3-AR over β1- and β2-ARs.

    Conclusion

    Jump To
    We successfully developed the highly potent and selective β3-AR agonist 15, which possesses a desirable metabolic stability and PK profile. Compound 15 showed dose-dependent β3-AR-mediated responses in marmoset urinary bladder smooth muscle and exhibited an excellent cardiovascular safety profile. These promising characteristics are likely due to its minimal off-target effects: 15 is highly active toward only the β3-AR and completely inactive toward all other α- and β-ARs. Furthermore, 15 is likely to have a lower risk of drug–drug interactions due to its low inhibition of CYPs. Thus, compound 15 could be a drug candidate for the treatment of OAB without cardiovascular side effects. Detailed pharmacology of 15 will be reported in a future pharmacology paper.     Calculation of Human and Rat Unbound Microsomal Intrinsic Clearance (hCLint,u and rCLint,u)

    Metabolic stability was determined by plotting the natural logarithm of the concentration of unchanged test compound as a function of time. The first-order rate constant was calculated using the equation k = [Ln(C0) – Ln(C)]/incubation time, where C0 was the initial concentration of the test compound, C was the concentration of the test compound remaining after incubation (C = C0 × remaining ratio), and the incubation time was 15 min (human) or 20 min (rat). The half-life (t1/2) was estimated using the equation t1/2 = 0.693/k. The hCLint,u was estimated using the equation hCLint,u = k/(microsomal protein concentration) × (microsomal protein per gram of liver) × (liver mass per kilogram of body mass)/(human microsome fu), where k was the first-order rate constant, the microsomal protein concentration was 0.5 mg/mL, the microsomal protein per gram of liver was 48.8 mg, the liver mass per kilogram of body mass was 25.7 g, and the human microsome fu was determined experimentally from the human liver microsomal binding assay.(36) The rCLint,u was estimated using the equation rCLint,u = k/(microsomal protein concentration) × (microsomal protein per gram of liver) × (liver mass per kilogram of body mass)/(rat microsome fu), where k was the first-order rate constant, the microsomal protein concentration was 0.5 mg/mL, the microsomal protein per gram of liver was 44.8 mg, liver mass per kilogram of body mass was 40.0 g, and the rat microsome fu was determined experimentally from the rat liver microsomal binding assay.(36)
