    Enantiomeric Atropisomers Inhibit HCV Polymerase and/or HIV Matrix: Characterizing Hindered Bond Rotations and Target Selectivity

    Abstract

    An anthranilic acid series of allosteric thumb pocket 2 HCV NS5B polymerase inhibitors exhibited hindered rotation along a covalent bond axis, and the existence of atropisomer chirality was confirmed by NMR, HPLC analysis on chiral supports, and computational studies. A thorough understanding of the concerted rotational properties and the influence exerted by substituents involved in this steric phenomenon was attained through biophysical studies on a series of truncated analogues. The racemization half-life of a compound within this series was determined to be 69 min, which was consistent with a class 2 atropisomer (intermediate conformational exchange). It was further found by X-ray crystallography that one enantiomer of a compound bound to the intended HCV NS5B polymerase target whereas the mirror image atropisomer was able to bind to an unrelated HIV matrix target. Analogues were then identified that selectively inhibited the former. These studies highlight that atropisomer chirality can lead to distinct entities with specific properties, and the phenomenon of atropisomerism in drug discovery should be evaluated and appropriately managed.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    An often overlooked source of chirality is atropisomerism, which results from slow rotation along a bond axis due to steric hindrance and/or electronic factors.(1) Atropisomerism can often go both undetected and unsuspected given that enantiomers cannot be distinguished using routine analytical tools (e.g., 1H NMR, HPLC, etc.).(2) Unlike atom-centric chiral centers, hindered bond rotations are not clearly identified by simply viewing a drug’s primary structure. Furthermore, awareness of this time-dependent phenomenon has been known to raise concerns for drug discovery and development projects.
    Recent reviews have categorized atropisomers into three classes based on their slow, intermediate, and fast rotational properties,(4) and it has been acknowledged that some distinct challenges need to be considered for each of the classes. Recently, guidelines have been proposed that are consistent with practical and regulatory perspectives.(3, 4) Overall, the reviews have espoused the view that atropisomeric compounds can be developed similar to compounds having classical chiral centers but with early detection and proper management.(2-4)
    In this work, we describe methods for detecting and monitoring class 2 atropisomers using NMR, HPLC analysis on chiral supports, and the design of related atropisomeric tool compounds. We also apply a dissection strategy involving truncated analogues to better understand concerted multibond rotation properties and the steric involvement of various substituents. In addition, we also encountered an interesting observation where one atropisomer binds to the intended target (HCV NS5B polymerase) whereas its mirror image enantiomer binds to an unrelated protein (HIV matrix). Analogues were subsequently identified that inhibited only the former. Overall, this work provides an example of methods and strategies for addressing the challenges associated with class 2 atropisomers.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The NS5B polymerase of the hepatitis C virus is an attractive target for the discovery of HCV antivirals.(5) During lead optimization efforts on an anthranilic acid series of thumb pocket 2 allosteric inhibitors, we suspected that compound 1 (IC50 = 0.068 μM) exhibited hindered rotation along the anilide bond (the red-colored arrow along the aryl-amine bond in Figure 1). As expected, simple 1H NMR spectra and temperature studies could not detect the existence of mirror image enantiomers (Figure 1A). A single set of resonances was observed for aromatic hydrogens H5 and H6, which shifted in opposite directions upon heating. A tool compound was therefore synthesized whereby the symmetric isopropyl moiety (colored blue in Figure 1A for 1) was replaced by a chiral 2-methyl-1-methoxy ethyl as in compound 2 (colored blue in Figure 1B). As a result, the manifestation of atropisomers in 2 (IC50 = 0.068 μM) combined with the presence of a second chiral element in the molecule should now lead to the detectable occurrence of diastereomeric species. Indeed, this was confirmed by the 1H NMR spectrum of 2, which featured two sets of resonances (e.g., H5A and H5B, H6A and H6B). By inference, compound 1 was thus predicted to display atropisomeric chirality. Given 1H NMR coalescence temperatures ≥120 °C, the energy barrier to rotation was estimated to be >18 kcal/mol (Figure 1B).
    The existence of atropisomer chirality was also confirmed by HPLC on a chiral support (as shown in Figure 2A), where two peaks (A and B) were observed for a related analogue 4. Subsequently, the half-life for atropisomer interconversion was measured by isolating the single enantiomer from peak A followed by reinjection to measure the rate of conversion to the other enantiomer (peak B). The decay of A was measured over time (Figure 2) to calculate a half-life of 69 min and a rotational energy barrier of 22–23 kcal/mol (Figure 2B).
    Possible sources of the observed hindered rotation along the amide-aryl bond were then investigated. The less sterically hindered analogue 3 (which lacked a COOH) (IC50 = 56 μM) was synthesized to study the potential steric influence of the ortho-acid group (Figure 1). This served as a control compound that was highly related and likely not atropisomeric. One purpose was to help focus on and evaluate the influential properties of the teriary amide bond (green arrow). The 1H NMR spectrum of 3 in Figure 1C lacked an extra set of resonances expected from atropisomerism and had two broadened resonances for hydrogen 5 due to cis and trans amide-bond orientations. Refer to Figure 5 for visual models of the cis (e.g., conformer A) and trans (e.g., conformer B) amide orientations. Resonance coalescence was observed at a much lower temperature for compound 3 (Figure 1C), which was consistent with a lower energy barrier in the range of 12–16 kcal/mol for the tertiary amide-bond rotation. This dissection and characterization of the rotational behavior of the tertiary amide bond was also corroborated by ROESY NMR data of compound 3 in Figure 3B, where cross-peaks were noted between the 5cis and 5trans resonances. These cross-peaks reported rotational exchange on the order of the millisecond to second time scale.
    The dynamic properties and interplay of both the anilide and the tertiary amide bonds could also be revealed. Figure 3A showed two sets of distinct resonances in the 1D 1H NMR spectrum that represented atropisomers for compound 2 Furthermore, no ROESY cross-peaks were detected between the sets of atropisomer resonances, which was consistent with slow bond rotation (minute time scale or longer). Also noteworthy was that, compound 2 also displayed a low population of the trans-conformation of the tertiary amide given the very small peaks observed in the 1D 1H NMR spectrum (Figure 1). The presence of ROESY cross-peaks was indicative of relatively fast bond rotation (millisecond to second time scale). Thus, the NMR data were consistent with compound 2 having a predominantly cis-amide orientation and atropisomerically hindered rotation along the anilide bond. Compound 3, on the other hand, displayed both cis- and trans-amide orientations in equivalent populations (similar peak intensities in Figure 1C). This hindered bond had a lower rotation barrier, and the compound had no atropisomeric properties along its anilide bond. The above analyses showed that both bonds in this series of compounds experienced hindered rotation but on different time scales.
    The impact of these conformational properties on biological activities, bioactive conformation, selectivity, and chirality was then addressed. This involved an analysis of the influence of the anilide N-alkyl (R) group using a series of related control analogues that differed only at the amide R group (blue substituent in Figure 4). As expected, the least hindered, unsubstituted amide compound 5 (R = H) had no evidence of atropisomerism. The weak activity (IC50 = 15 μM against HCV polymerase) was largely explained by ROESY NMR data that showed the predominant free-state conformation did not correspond the bioactive L-shape conformation that was required for HCV polymerase activity (vide infra, Figure 7A). When free in solution, the ROESY data lacked the reporter crosspeaks (i.e., between resonances of the cyclohexyl and the H3 aromatic proton of the benzoic acid group) that were signatory of the bioactive conformation (see the top right of Figure 4).
    However, when R = CH3 as in compound 6, the activity improved 75-fold (IC50 = 0.2 μM) and the predominant free-state conformation corresponded to the bioactive L-shape. Cross-peaks were noted between the cyclohexyl and the benzoic acid group (see circle at the center right in Figure 4). A general model of this bioactive cis tertiary amide conformation, which is required for HCV polymerase activity, is displayed as conformer A in Figure 5, vide infra). Thus, the methyl substitution introduced a rotational barrier along the tertiary-amide bond that promoted both the bioactive conformation and the biological activity.
    Notably, further bulk at this position resulted in a similar promotion of the bioactive conformation and biological activity. The enzymatic activities (IC50) of compounds 6, 7, and 8 were similar, suggesting that all bulky R-groups force the tertiary amide into the bioactive cis conformation. For example, similar ROESY peaks are shown for compounds 6 and 8 in Figure 4. The data were consistent with a rigidification role for the R-group that was solvent exposed in the bound state. For example, the large R group of compound 8 had comparable potency to analogues with smaller substituents and did not cause steric clashes within the receptor pocket (refer to Figure 7A). The R position was permissive and provided opportunities for improvement of other biological properties of the molecules such as cell-based replicon potency as shown for the piperidine analogue 8. Overall, this study determined that the steric properties of the R-group, acid, and cyclohexyl together contributed to hinder rotation along the anilide and tertiary amide bonds and favored the bioactive conformation in the free state.
    The above-described exchange phenomena were further explored by calculations of the energy profiles along the anilide bond (torsion angle 1) and the tertiary amide bond (torsion angle 2) (see Figure 5). Using the same method as described elsewhere,(2) a torsion scan was computed and model views of the lower energy states are displayed as conformers A–D. These calculations served to provide valuable predictions of the rotation energy barriers. The lowest barrier to rotation determined for torsion 2 was 16.2 kcal/mol (green in Figure 5). The calculations also predicted the cis conformation to be 0.3 kcal/mol lower in energy as compared to the trans conformation. Although the energy difference of 0.3 kcal/mol was likely not significant given the precision of the method, experimental evidence by NMR confirmed the significant predominance of the cis orientation in solution (1D 1H NMR spectrum in Figure 3A). This cis-amide orientation for torsion 2 must be forced and stabilized by steric bumping of the COOH, with the cyclohexyl resulting in a barrier of 27.3 kcal/mol (Figure 5, direct conformer A to D racemization). An energy minimum for torsion 2 occured when the amide oxygen was anti to the phenyl. However, given a trans orientation along torsion 2, rotation along torsion 1 involved a lower barrier to rotation of 21.8 kcal/mol.
    The combination of the above theoretical and experimental data was useful for formulating a plausible interpretation of the dynamic interplay of torsion angles 1 and 2 (Figure 5). For this series of compounds, the process of atropisomer racemization along torsion angle 1 implicates two rotations along torsion angle 2. The preferred path from conformer A to D (Figure 5) likely proceeds via the lower rotational barriers involving conformers B and C. See the concerted rotation path as illustrated. First, the amide bond must rotate from the predominant cis orientation to the trans orientation (conformer A to B). The anilide bond can then rotate more fluidly and frequently (millisecond to second) given less steric hindrance and lower energy barrier (conformer B to C). This is then followed by rotation of the amide bond back to the more stable cis orientation (conformer C to D).
    This concerted bond mechanism involves a barrier of 21.8 kcal/mol and is consistent with the experimentally derived rotational atropisomeric energy barrier of 22–23 kcal/mol. Otherwise, a direct rotation of conformer A to D would be calculated to be much higher at 27.3 kcal/mol. Thus, atropisomer interconversion likely occurs via the less-populated trans-amide conformation, which gives access to the lower barrier of rotation of torsion 1. Thus, the lower-energy path to rotation is preferred and implicates three bond rotations.
    In addition to the mechanistic studies described above, we also noted an intriguing feature of anthranilic acid thumb pocket 2 polymerase inhibitors that resulted from the atropisomeric properties inherent to some of the analogues. A high-throughput screen of our corporate compound collection that aimed to identify inhibitors of HIV matrix in virion morphogenesis, an essential process for viral replication (see Supporting Information),(6, 7) uncovered hits from a subset of the anthranilic acid series. Figure 6 showed anthranilic acid analogue 9 (HIV matrix IC50 = 3.4 μM) bound to HIV matrix as detected by 15N TROSY NMR, which was an effective filtering step in our hit triaging protocol. An overlay of the 15N TROSY data of apo matrix (blue) with a spectrum after addition of compound 9 (red) demonstrated that specific peaks shifted positions (blue peaks not overlapped with red peaks). Many of these peaks were from residues located near the phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2) substrate binding site of matrix (see Supporting Information). This technique was used to illustrate that a subset of anthranilic acid derivatives were also binders and inhibitors of the HIV matrix. The binding site and L-shaped ligand bioactive conformation of compound 9 bound to the HIV matrix were corroborated by an X-ray structure of the complex (bottom right of Figure 6).
    An intriguing atropisomeric feature of these compounds resulting from atropisomerism was noted when comparing the X-ray structure of compound 9 bound to HCV polymerase versus HIV matrix (Figure 7). At the bottom of Figure 7B, the chiral conformation labeled (Ra) “acid on the left” was found to bind HIV matrix, whereas the opposite atropiomeric orientation (Sa) labeled “acid on the right” was noted to bind to HCV polymerase (bottom of Figure 7A). Thus, one atropisomer of compound 9 bound to an HCV target whereas the mirror image bound to an unrelated HIV target. Note that both chiral compounds had comparable enzymatic and cell culture activity in the two unrelated biological systems and bound to the receptors in the bioactive L-shape (cis-amide conformation).
    Our awareness and study of atropisomerism properties of tertiary anthranilic acid amide derivatives proved beneficial in the context of these observations. A subset of analogues was identified that were specific inhibitors of HCV NS5B that did not inhibit HIV matrix. This divergent SAR was exemplified in Figure 4, where compounds 5–7 were inactive in the matrix assay (IC50 >100 μM).
    The work described above presents a good example of the evaluation of a class 2 atropisomeric compound series and its properties. This class represents atropisomeric compound types that can cover a wide range of rotational rates. Compounds that have intermediate rotational rates can be challenging, and the relevant drug discovery implications must be considered on a compound to compound basis. In this case, the inhibition of both HCV and HIV might be a desirable and beneficial feature, but one should consider that specific binding of an atropisomer to an unwanted target is also possible and may lead to unwanted adverse effects depending on the target. From a drug development perspective, it may be envisaged that class 2 atropisomeric compounds could be employed as racemic mixtures with all the possible implications, such as potential toxicity by binding of one of the isomers to unwanted targets, difficulties in generating crystal forms in development, or stability and challenges in interpreting ADME-PK data where one enantiomer could affect the properties of the others (e.g., one enantiomer may inhibit CYP and the other inadvertently appear more stable). However, the development of atomic-centric racemic drugs has long been a successful strategy in the past and can serve as a reference for atropisomeric racemates.(4)
    It is also valuable to remark that when atropisomerism is detected early during lead optimization, a productive medicinal chemistry evaluation can be beneficial. In this case, the isomers are in intermediate conformational exchange and the receptor is able to select the preferred one out of the mixture. However, even though many of the compounds in this series had the potential to bind to both targets, it was still possible to generate divergent SAR and to identify analogues that were specific for the intended target by capitalizing on other structural elements.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    This work summarizes the detection and mechanistic elucidation of atropisomeric chirality in a series of anthranilic acid thumb pocket 2 HCV NS5B allosteric inhibitors. On the basis of the ensemble of information, a forward perspective could be developed. The compounds discussed in this work had rotational t1/2 of approximately 69 min, and therefore were considered class 2 atropisomers that experience “intermediate interconversion”.(4) On the basis of practical limitations, the isomers could not be isolated and tested separately due to this interconversion under physiologically relevant conditions. Thus, further studies should employ racemic mixtures. This work can serve as a model example for how drug discovery groups may approach compounds or series that are class 2 atropisomers.

    General Compound Characterization

    Procedures for compound syntheses and characterization are described in the Supporting Information and elsewhere.(11) NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker AVANCE II (400 MHz for 1H NMR) spectrometer and were referenced to either DMSO-d6 (2.50 ppm) or CDCl3 (7.27 ppm). Mass spectra were obtained from a Micomass AutoSpec instrument using ES as ionization mode. Purification of crude material was performed either by flash column chromatography or by using a CombiFlash Companion using RediSep silica or SilicaSep columns according to preprogrammed gradient and flow rate separation conditions in hexane/EtOAc or DCM/MeOH. The final compounds were purified by preparative HPLC on a Waters 2767 sample manager with pumps 2525, column fluidics organizer (CFO), PDA detector 2996, and MassLynx 4.1 using either a Whatman Partisil 10-ODS-3 column, 2.2 cm × 50 cm or a YMC Combi-Prep ODS-AQ column, 50 mm × 20 mm i.d., S, 5 μm, 120 Å, and a linear gradient program from 2% to 100% AcCN/water (0.06% TFA). Fractions were analyzed by analytical HPLC, and the pure fractions were combined, concentrated, frozen, and lyophilized to yield the desired compound as a neutral entity or the trifluoroacetate salt for basic analogues. Inhibitor HPLC purity was measured by using a Waters Alliance 2695 separation module with a Waters TUV 2487 UV detector. The column was a Combiscreen ODS-AQ, 5 μm, 4.6 mm × 50 mm, linear gradient from 5% to 100% ACN/H2O + 0.06% TFA in 10.5 min, detection at 220 nm. All final inhibitors had HPLC homogeneity of ≥95% unless noted otherwise (see Supporting Information). High resolution exact mass determinations (HRMS) were performed using either an 6220 Accurate-Mass time-of-flight LC/MS with atmospheric pressure photoionization (APPI+) via flow-injection analysis or a Bruker microOTOF-Q II with electrospray ionization (ESI+) via LC-MS

    NMR Spectroscopy

    Samples used for Figures 1, 3, and 4 were prepared by dissolving 0.5 mg in DMSO-d6. All NMR spectra were acquired on Bruker AVANCE III 600 or 400 MHz spectrometers using standard pulse programs and parameters. Assignment of the inhibitor 1H resonances were secured by careful analysis of standard 1D 1H COSY, ROESY, HSQC, and HMBC experiments. ROESY data were acquired with a continuous spin-lock during mixing (300 ms), a 2.5 kHz spin-lock field, the carrier set at 4.7 ppm, 400 t1 data points from the addition of 32 transients, a 5200 Hz sweep width. and a relaxation delay of 1.2 s
    Samples for the 15N TROSY experiments with 15N matrix (20 mM), 50 mM sodium phosphate pH 5.5. Inhibitor 9 or PIP2 were added via DMSO stocks such that a ligand to matrix of 10:1 was used with a total of 1% DMSO. Resonance assignments were secured (Figure S1, Supporting Information) from the literature.(7, 10)

    Chiral HPLC Determination of the Atropisomer Half-Life of Compound 4

    The racemate of compound 4 was analyzed by chiral HPLC using the column Chiral-AGP, 5 μm, 4.0 mm × 150 mm. The eluent was 19% (CH3CN):81% (0.1%CH3COOH, pH 4.3). The injection volume was 50 μL, flow was 1 mL/min, run time was 15 min, detection was PDA (192–320 nm) Res. 1.2 nm, and integration at 225 nm. Two well-separated peaks (A and B) corresponding to compound 4 were observed. Peak A was isolated and quickly reinjected into the chiral HPLC, and peaks A and B were integrated. Reinjections were performed from the initial isolated peak A and peaks integrated as indicated in Figure 2. The ratios of peaks A/B were determined at time points of 20, 50, 100, 150, 165, and 200 min. The percent of A as a function of time is plotted in Figure 2. The equation employed to determine the atropisomer half-life was the following: y = 24.82 exp( – 0.01x) + 51.24. An R2 = 0.9971 was calculated for the data in Figure 2. The half-life of peak A (rotation from A to an equilibrium of A and B) was 69 min.

    HIV Matrix Construct, Expression, and Assay

    Matrix protein (amino acid sequence 1–132) from HIV-1 strain NL4.3 was inserted in pET11a vector and expressed in BL21(DE3) cells or in 15N labeled bacterial growth medium for use in TROSY experiments. Purification was as described previously(7) with modifications. A cell pellet was lysed in 20 mM Tris-Cl pH 8.0, 500 mM NaCl, 5 mM imidazole, protease inhibitor tablet (without EDTA), and sonicated before centrifugation at 39000g for 30 min. Supernatant was passed on a HisTrap HP column and eluted with 1 M imidazole. Eluate was diluted to 100 mM NaCl and passed on sequential HiTrap DEAE FF and HiTrap SP columns. Columns were disconnected and protein eluted from SP column with 500 mM NaCl. The eluted protein was dialyzed against 20 mM Tris-Cl pH 8.0, 200 mM NaCl, centrifuged at 20000g for 30 min, and stored at −80 °C. Protein concentration was determined in 6 M GuHCl (1:20 dilution) using ε = 16740 and MW = 15665, yielding a stock of 1.04 mg/mL.
    The fluorescence polarization assay was based on the reported interaction of matrix with phosphatidylinositol (PI) 4,5-bisphosphate [PI(4,5)P2]2 and established in order to measure this binding and test compounds which interfere with the interaction. Corning 384-well black plates (no. 3654) contained 500 nM MA-His and 15 nM Bodipy-TMR PI(4,5)P2–C6 (Echelon Biosciences) with or without compound in 50 mM Tris-Cl pH 8.0, 50 mM NaCl, 0.01% Igepal CA-630, 1 mM DTT, and 1% DMSO. The plate was incubated for 30 min at RT and then mP signal read on a Perkin-Elmer Wallac Plate Reader (0.2 s). Apparent affinity of MA-His WT with Bodipy-PI(4,5)P2–C6 was ∼1 μM. See also refs 6 and 7 for more details.

    HCV NS5B Polymerase Constructs for IC50 Activity Assays and X-ray Crystallography

    HCV genotype 1b NS5B strain 1b-40 and J4 were used to clone into baculovirus and Escherichia coli expression vectors.(8) The full length HCV NS5B polymerase used for compound IC50 determination was expressed as a hexa-histidine fusion protein in baculovirus infected cells and purified by metal chelating chromatography on Ni2+ column followed by DEAE-sepharose and heparin-sepharose chromatography. All other details are provided elsewhere.(9) The genotype 1b J4 strain NS5B lacking the C-terminal 21 amino acids and encoding a C-terminal hexa-histidine tag (HCVNS5B1bJ4-ΔC21–H6) was used for X-ray crystallography. This construct was expressed, purified, and concentrated as previously described.(9)

    Determination of X-ray Structures

    Crystallization of apo HCV NS5B polymerase and inhibitor soaking studies proceeded as described elsewhere.(9) Also, the crystallization and structure determination of HIV matrix were determined as follows. HIV matrix (residues 1–111) was prepared at 15.3 mg/mL in 10mM Tris-Cl pH 8.0, 1 mM EGTA, 5 mM β-mercaptoethanol. Hanging drop vapor diffusion was carried out using a crystallization solution of 22.5% PEG2000MME, 100 mM sodium acetate pH 4.5, 100 mM ammonium sulfate, and 7.5% DMSO. Crystallization was obtained by suspending drops containing equal volumes of protein and crystallization solutions over a 0.5 mL reservoir of the same crystallization solution. Triangular crystals appeared within one week of incubation at 18 °C and continuted to grow for one to two weeks thereafter. A single crystal was then transferred to a 5 μL drop of the crystallization solution supplemented with 5 mM compound 9 and was allowed to incubate overnight. The crystal was then harvested in a rayon loop and directly flash frozen in liquid nitrogen. Diffraction data were collected at 100 K on an FR-E x-ray generator equipped with Osmic HiRes(2) optics and a MAR345dtb image plate detector. Data reduction and scaling were performed using HKL2000,(12) and the model of compound 9 in complex with matrix was obtained using CNX,(13, 14) starting from a model ultimately derived from the Protein Data Bank structure of 1HIW.(15)
