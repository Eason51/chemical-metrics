    Abstract

    Accumulating studies have linked inflammation to tumor progression. Dietary omega-3 fatty acids, such as docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), have been shown to suppress tumor growth through their conversion to epoxide metabolites. Alternatively, DHA is converted enzymatically into docosahexaenoylethanolamide (DHEA), an endocannabinoid with antiproliferative activity. Recently, we reported a novel class of anti-inflammatory DHEA-epoxide derivative called epoxydocospentaenoic-ethanolamide (EDP-EA) that contain both ethanolamide and epoxide moieties. Herein, we study the antitumorigenic properties of EDP-EAs in an osteosarcoma (OS) model. First, we show ∼80% increase in EDP-EAs in metastatic versus normal lungs of mice. We found significant differences in the apoptotic and antimigratory potencies of the different EDP-EA regioisomers, which were partially mediated through cannabinoid receptor 1 (CB1). Next, we synthesized derivatives of the most pro-apoptotic regioisomer. These derivatives had reduced hydrolytic susceptibility to fatty acid amide hydrolase (FAAH) and increased CB1-selective binding. Collectively, we report a novel class of EDP-EAs that exhibit antiangiogenic, antitumorigenic, and antimigratory properties in OS.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Recent studies in cancer focus on understanding the role of inflammation in tumor initiation and malignant progression. Inflammatory conditions have been previously studied to promote oncogenic transformation. In addition, the carcinogenic milieu can generate an inflammatory microenvironment, which in turn favors tumor progression.(1−3) Dietary omega-3 fatty acids such as eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) have been shown to suppress tumor growth and progression by their conversion into anti-inflammatory, pro-resolving, and antitumorigenic lipids.(4−7) As inflammation is capable of promoting tumor initiation, proliferation, and progression, omega-3 fatty acid derived lipid metabolites have also been hypothesized to prevent tumor formation and metastasis in a multifaceted manner.(5) Hence, there are biological and clinical justifications to discover potential omega-3 fatty acid derived lipid metabolites that possess antitumorigenic and anti-inflammatory properties.
    Cytochrome P450 (CYP) epoxygenases convert omega-3 fatty acids into epoxides that exhibit anticancer properties. Specifically, EPA-epoxides, also known as epoxyeicosatetraenoic acid (EEQs), have been studied to be antiproliferative.(8) In addition, the terminal epoxide of EPA, 17,18-EEQ, decreases endothelial-cell proliferation, interrupts the cell cycle in S-phase, and down-regulates the cyclin D1–cyclin-dependent kinase (CDK)-4 complex. The corresponding DHA-epoxide, epoxydocosapentaenoic acid (EDP), inhibits metastasis and tumor growth.(9) Furthermore, the actions of the DHA and EPA-epoxides are attenuated in the absence of a soluble-epoxide-hydrolase (sEH) inhibitor, indicating that the functional epoxide moiety is key for their antitumorigenic properties.
    Omega-3 fatty acids are enzymatically converted into docosahexaenoyl ethanolamide (DHEA) and eicosapentaenoyl ethanolamide (EPEA) through the N-acyl ethanolamine synthesis pathway, similar to the conversion of the omega-6 fatty acid arachidonic acid (AA) to arachidonoyl ethanolamide (AEA, anandamide).(10,11) These ethanolamide derivatives are collectively termed endocannabinoids (eCBs) because they elicit similar physiological effects as cannabinoids found in cannabis. The eCB system consists of biosynthetic and degradative enzymes, such as diacylglycerol lipase (DAG) and FAAH;(12) omega-6 eCBs, AEA and 2-arachidonylglycerol (2-AG); and cannabinoids receptors 1 and 2 (CB1 and CB2).(13,14) CB1 is predominantly found in the central nervous system (CNS), CB2 is found in immune cells.(15) CB receptors interact with various eCBs to exert their physiological activities.(16) CB1 and CB2 interact with the active components of cannabis and are responsible for cannabis’s psychoactive and anti-inflammatory properties, respectively. Phytocannabinoids, such as Δ9-tetrahydrocannabinol (Δ9-THC), and cannabidiol (CBD), are two specific components of cannabis that have been shown to induce apoptosis in hepatocellular carcinoma, breast cancer, and colon cancer.(17) As a whole, phytocannabinoids and eCBs have been shown to play a powerful role in cancer-cell proliferation, apoptosis, inflammation, and immunomodulation.(18,19)
    The most studied eCBs in the human body are AEA and 2-AG, which are derived from AA. AEA was shown to have antitumorigenic effects on breast cancer, colorectal cancer, and OS.(20−23) 2-AG has been shown to induce apoptosis in prostate cancer.(24) The omega-3-derived eCBs, DHEA and EPEA, have shown greater antiproliferative activity than their parent counterparts in prostate-cancer cells.(11) Additionally, it was also shown that the activity of eCBs in cancer cells was increased in the presence of a FAAH inhibitor, indicating that the ethanolamide eCBs, AEA, DHEA, and EPEA, contribute to the increased pro-apoptotic and antiproliferative activity.(10,11)
    Recently, we showed that a novel class of eCB epoxides are generated from the cross-talk of the NAPE-PLD pathway and CYP-epoxygenase pathways, which were found in various tissues(25) (Figure 1a). These bifunctional metabolites contain both ethanolamide and epoxide motifs that function through different signaling pathways lead to several overlapping physiological outcomes. Both the eCB and epoxyeicosanoid pathways have been implicated in cancer, as described above.(8,9) Hence, we wanted to evaluate the antitumorigenic properties of dual-functional eCB epoxides in a cancer model. These lipid metabolites, termed EPEA epoxide (i.e., epoxyeicosatetraenoic-ethanolamide (EEQ-EA)) and DHEA-epoxide (i.e., epoxydocosapentaenoic-ethanolamide (EDP-EA)), are synthesized from EPEA and DHEA by cytochrome P450s and are anti-inflammatory and vasodilatory in nature. More importantly, DHEA-epoxides are widely distributed throughout the body. Additionally, we show that there is an ∼80% increase in these levels in metastatic lungs (Figures 1b and S1) compared to levels in normal lungs of mice. This indicates that they might play a physiological role in tumorigenesis and cancer metastasis.
    Herein, we investigate the antitumorigenic activities of the different regioisomers of DHEA-epoxides on OS cell lines. OS is the most common primary bone cancer in humans and presents high rates of invasion and metastasis. The cannabinoid-receptor agonist WIN-55212 has been shown to potentiate the antitumorigenic properties of drugs like adriamycin in OS.(26) Additionally, the administration of cannabinoid-receptor agonists have been shown to reduce bone loss in bone-tumor models and cannabinoid-receptor-mediated nociceptive pain.(27,28) Taken together, we will show that DHEA-epoxide regioisomers and their stable derivatives exhibit antitumorigenic activities in OS, and therefore, can potentially reduce bone-cancer pain through receptor-selective activation.
    In order to examine the antitumorigenic properties of DHEA-epoxides, we first tested the apoptotic potential of the different regioisomers of DHEA-epoxides using an annexin V staining assay in three different OS cell lines (metastatic OS cell lines, MG63 and 143B, and the nonmetastatic cell line HOS) and identified the most potent DHEA-epoxide regioisomer. We further tested the antimigratory potential of the DHEA-epoxides via a wound-healing assay in all three cell lines.(29) Interestingly, we observed significant differences between the DHEA-epoxide regioisomers tested with respect to their pro-apoptotic and antimigratory properties that were uniform across the different OS cell lines chosen for the studies, indicating that the specific isomers of these lipid metabolites triggered common signaling pathways.
    Furthermore, we synthesized stable derivatives of the most pro-apoptotic regioisomer of DHEA-epoxide, 10,11-EDP-EA, to reduce its hydrolytic susceptibility to FAAH and increase its binding to CB1. 10,11-EDP-EA and its stable derivatives were further tested for their antitumorigenic and antimigratory properties and for susceptibility to FAAH hydrolysis.
    Collectively, we report a novel class of DHEA-epoxides and their stable derivatives that exhibit anti-inflammatory,(25) antiangiogenic, antitumorigenic, and antimigratory properties. By virtue of their ability to bind cannabinoid receptors, DHEA-epoxides and its analogues may reduce nociceptive bone pain in OS. OS is commonly accompanied by inflammation and pain; these molecules could aid in alleviating both.

    Endogenous Production of DHEA-Epoxides in Metastatic-Osteosarcoma Lung Tissues

    We developed a targeted lipidomics method using LC-MS/MS in multiple-reaction-monitoring (MRM) mode to identify and quantitate DHEA-epoxide regioisomers (19,20-, 16,17-, 13,14-, 10,11-, and 7,8-EDP-EA).(25) In order to quantify basal levels of endogenous EDP-EA regioisomers in metastatic and non-tumor containing lungs, we measured these metabolites in mice with OS and those without. We used a metastatic-tumor animal model. 106 K7M2 cells (murine OS cells) were prepared in HBSS and intravenously injected into the tail veins of 6–8 week old female BALB/c mice in 200 μL volumes. Lungs from ten mice, five mice with lungs absent of tumors and five with lung-metastasized OS tumors, were isolated. The eCBs were extracted from the lungs and quantified by LC-MS/MS as described in the Experimental Section. As indicated in Figures 1b and S1, both tumor-free lungs without OS and tumor-present lungs in mice produced several-fold fewer eCB-epoxides compared to the levels of DHEA, the parent molecule. There was an increase of ∼87% in the terminal DHEA-epoxide, 19,20-EDP-EA, a 66% increase in 16,17-EDP-EA, a 72% increase in 13,14-EDP-EA, and a 49% increase in 10,11-EDP-EA in lungs with a metastatic-tumor burden, whereas DHEA levels did not change in lung tissue with a metastatic burden compared to the levels in healthy lungs with no tumors. The increase of these metabolites in the metastasized lungs warranted further investigation to determine whether they were promoting tumor growth or exhibiting antitumorigenic properties.
    In order to investigate the effects of eCB epoxides on OS, all five regioisomers were synthesized as described previously.(25)meta-chloroperoxybenzoic acid (mCPBA) was used to oxidize DHA and the epoxide regioisomers formed were purified using reverse- and normal-phase HPLC. The individual regioisomers were coupled to ethanolamine and purified by reverse-phase chromatography. The compounds were quantified using synthesized standards and used for further studies.

    Effects of DHEA-Epoxide (EDP-EA) Regioisomers on Cell Viability

    To evaluate the effects of EDP-EA regioisomers on the cellular viability of OS cells, a cell-titer blue (CTB) assay was performed on 143B metastatic OS cells at 5 μM concentrations. The CTB assay measures cellular viability by measuring the conversion of the dye reazurin into the fluorescent end product resorufin by living cells. The fluorescence produced is directly proportional to the amount of viable cells. As seen in Figure 1c, only 13,14-, 10,11-, and 7,8-EDP-EA reduced total cell viability at 12.5 μM concentrations, while 19,20- and 16,17-EDP-EA were less effective. All regioisomers were tested for their effects on 143B cell viability by CTB assays at various concentrations, as seen in Figure S2. However, at 25 μM concentrations, complete reduction of cell viability was observed for all EDP-EA isomers. Because 7,8-, 10,11-, and 13,14-EDP-EA were more effective at reducing cell viability than the other two regioisomers at lower concentrations, their apoptotic and antimigratory potentials were further evaluated. In order to evaluate if the decrease in cell viability due to the three different DHEA-epoxide regioisomers was the result of cell death or a check in cell proliferation, we performed apoptosis and wound healing assays.

    Induction of Apoptosis in Metastatic-OS Cells by DHEA-Epoxide (EDP-EA) Regioisomers

    The apoptotic potential of 7,8-EDP-EA, 10,11-EDP-EA, and 13,14-EDP-EA were tested in three different cell lines, HOS, 143B, and MG63 cells, using the annexin V/PI staining assay at 12.5 μM concentrations. This concentration was chosen to reflect the point in the CTB assay where cell viability was reduced but not completely diminished. The annexin V/PI binding assay measures apoptotic cells by binding labeled annexin V to phosphatidylserine (PS) species. PS is an inner-leaflet lipid that flips to the outer leaflet of the plasma membrane in apoptotic cells. Fluorescence by annexin V is proportional to apoptotic cells. PI binds to DNA in necrotic cells. A signal from both annexin V and PI indicate cells in late apoptosis. As seen in Figure 1d, all three regioisomers induced apoptosis in all the cell lines at the concentration of 12.5 μM. Specifically, in the 143B cell line (Supplementary Table ST3), 7,8-EDP-EA induced 8.75 ± 0.15% apoptosis (p = 0.001), 10,11-EDP-EA induced 17.92 ± 3.32% (p = 0.05) apoptosis, and 13,14-EDP-EA induced 9.95 ± 1.88% apoptosis (p = 0.09). Similar to these results, in MG63 cells (Table ST4), 7,8-EDP-EA induced 16.4 ± 1.4% apoptosis (p = 0.011), 10,11-EDP-EA induced 26.6 ± 3.5% apoptosis (p = 0.027), and 13,14-EDP-EA induced 16.9 ± 2.3% apoptosis (p = 0.04). In HOS cells (Table ST2), the trend followed, and 7,8-EDP-EA induced 17.1 ± 0.5% apoptosis (p = 0.023 compared to the control), 10,11-EDP-EA induced 31.1 ± 2.3% apoptosis (p = 0.007), and 13,14-EDP-EA induced 19.6 ± 3.5% apoptosis (p = 0.027). In contrast to these results, treatment of all three different cell lines with AA, AEA, DHA, and DHEA at 12.5 μM concentrations showed no induction of apoptosis compared to the no-treatment control (Table ST1).

    Inhibition of Migration of Cancer Cells by DHEA-Epoxides As Determined Using a Wound-Healing Assay

    The wound-healing assay has been shown to be an effective measure for the directional migration of cells in vivo.(30) This assay measures the migration of cells across a scratch or wound over time as an indicator of cell migratory potential. A wound-healing assay was performed for 7,8-EDP-EA, 10,11-EDP-EA, and 13,14-EDP-EA at 5 μM. This concentration was chosen to reflect a concentration at which the compounds are not significantly pro-apoptotic, and the reduction in wound healing would be due to the compounds’ antimigratory properties only. Figure 1e is representative of the effects of 7,8-EDP-EA, 10,11-EDP-EA, and 13,14-EDP-EA on wound closure in 143B cells. Figure 1f represents the ratio of wound remaining in the treatment to the wound remaining in the no-treatment control at three time points (0, 6, and 10 h). Therefore, a ratio of 1 indicates that the same percentage areas of wound remain in the treatment and control, and thus the treatment is no different from control. A ratio higher than 1 indicates that the treatment has a higher percentage area of wound remaining and thus is a compound that restricts migration. As seen from the data in Figure 1f, in HOS cells, all three compounds inhibit cell migration to an extent. For 7,8-EDP-EA (at 6 h, p = 0.07, and at 10 h, p = 0.35) and 13,14-EDP-EA (at 6 h, p = 0.5, and at 10 h, p = 0.4) the difference in wound closure at 10 h is not significant. For 10,11-EDP-EA, the wound closure at 10 h is 1.5 times better than in the control (at 6 h, p = 0.08, and at 10 h, p = 0.17). In 143B cells (Figure 1f), 7,8-EDP-EA and 13,14-EDP-EA do not inhibit wound healing at 10 h. However, 10,11-EDP-EA inhibits wound closure significantly at 10 h (at 6 h, p = 0.21, and at 10 h, p = 0.05). In the MG63 cell lines (Figure 1f), all three isomers restrict wound healing, and at 10 h, 10,11-EDP-EA inhibited wound closure twice as much as the control (at 6 h, p = 0.38, and at 10 h, p = 0.06). For 13,14-EDP-EA, wound healing was restricted at 10 h but not at 6 h (at 6 h, p = 0.018, and at 10 h, p = 0.015). For 7,8-EDP-EA, a similar trend was seen, with p = 0.23 at 6 h, and p = 0.23 at 10 h. Thus, although all three isomers in most conditions demonstrate variable capacity to restrict wound healing and thereby show antimigratory potential, the directional-trend effect is greatest in 10,11-EDP-EA.
    From our apoptosis and wound-healing assays, 10,11-EDP-EA was the most potent regioisomer. This was partly mediated through cannabinoid receptor 1, as the reduction of cell migration by 10,11-EDP-EA in the 143B cell line was completely abolished by using the CB1-receptor antagonist rimonabant (Figure S4).(31)

    Synthesis of Amide Derivatives of 10,11-DHEA Epoxide (10,11-EDP-EA)

    The eCB epoxides are dual-functional molecules. As the amide bond is susceptible to FAAH, we designed more stable analogues of 10,11-EDP-EA. Derivatives were made with different amide modifications at the carboxylate end to reduce susceptibility to hydrolysis by FAAH as well as to increase binding to CB1 and CB2, the purported receptors for apoptotic activity.
    The current literature has mainly focused on AEA and AEA derivatives, because AEA is a biological ligand of the CB1 and CB2 receptors. It has been previously shown that introduction of a methyl group at the 1′ position of the amide results in increased metabolic stability of AEA while affecting the CB1 binding ability.(32) It was shown that the R-isomer has 4-fold higher CB1 binding and the S-isomer has 2-fold lower binding compared to AEA. Thus, the R-1′-methyl isomer was the first modification made to 10,11-EDP, resulting in 10,11-EDP-IA (Figure 2a). Next, the cyclopropyl derivative of AEA has been shown to have modest FAAH inhibition, with an IC50 value of 4.1 ± 2.0 μM and significantly increased CB1 binding;(33) thus, 10,11-EDP-CA was prepared. Finally, the n-propyl derivative of AEA was made to assess the effect of acyl-chain length on FAAH hydrolysis. It has also been shown to have better affinity for CB1 as compared with that of AEA, thus 10,11-EDP-NA was prepared.(34,35)
    The synthesis of these analogues is outlined in the Experimental Section and shown in Figure 2a and is similar to the synthesis of 10,11-EDP-EA. Briefly, 10,11-EDP was incubated with NHS and EDC in acetonitrile for 20 min at 37 °C, followed by the addition of the corresponding amine, and stirred overnight at room temperature. The amines were purified by reverse-phase HPLC and quantified in a similar manner to 10,11-EDP-EA.

    Susceptibility of 10,11-DHEA-Epoxide and Its Amide Derivatives to FAAH Hydrolysis

    Fatty acid amide hydrolase (FAAH) is a ubiquitously expressed protein in various mammals, and the sequence of the protein has been shown to be highly conserved in humans,(36) mice,(36) and pigs.(37) For our studies, we used pig-brain-membrane preparations to analyze the effect of FAAH on 10,11-EDP-EA as well as the three amino-derived modifications.(38) The products of hydrolysis were measured by LC-MS/MS as indicated in the Experimental Section. Previously, 19,20-EDP-EA, the rate of hydrolysis was 150 pmol/min per milligram of protein.(25) As observed from Figure 2b, there was significant reduction of hydrolytic potential in all three derivatives as compared with that of 10,11-EDP-EA (p < 0.01). Whereas the rate of hydrolysis of 10,11-EDP-EA to 10,11-EDP was 30.0 ± 0.3 pmol/min per milligram of protein, the rates of hydrolysis for derivatives 10,11-EDP-NA, 10,11-EDP-IA, and 10,11-EDP-CA were 0.09 ± 0.01, 0.99 ± 0.04, and 5.17 ± 0.06 pmol/min per milligram of protein, respectively. Because the three derivatives show significantly reduced hydrolytic susceptibility, they could have higher bioavailability, and thus their apoptotic and antimigratory potentials were further evaluated.

    Apoptosis Induced by Amino-Modified Derivatives of 10,11 DHEA Epoxide (10,11-EDP-EA)

    All three amino-modified derivatives of 10,11-EDP were tested for their apoptotic activity by an annexin V/PI binding assay. All three compounds showed increased apoptotic potential compared to the no-treatment vehicle control (Figure 2c). The 10,11-EDP-CA analogue had the highest pro-apoptotic potential among the three derivatives. In HOS, MG63, and 143B cells, it increased apoptosis to 27.2 ± 4.1% (p = 0.036 compared to the control), 17.1 ± 4.4% (p = 0.078), and 21.3 ± 1.9% (p = 0.01), respectively. The 10,11-EDP-IA analogue was the second most effective in HOS and MG63 cells, with apoptotic potentials of 20.2 ± 1.8% (p = 0.016) and 17.5 ± 2.8% (p = 0.033), respectively; in 143B cells, it was only 18.8 ± 0.3% (p = 0.0004) for 10,11-EDP-IA, compared to 25.7 ± 0.4% (p = 0.0002) for 10,11-EDP-NA. However, 10,11-EDP-NA was least effective in HOS and MG63 cells, with only 17.1 ± 1.4% (p = 0.017) and 15.2 ± 3.3% (p = 0.06), respectively. Overall, all the derivatives showed pro-apoptotic potentials similar to the parent compound without discernible differences.

    Wound-Healing Assay to Determine the Potential to Inhibit Cell Migration by Amino-Modified Derivatives of 10,11 DHEA-Epoxide (10,11-EDP-EA)

    A wound-healing assay was used to analyze the antimigratory properties of the three derivatives. The data in Figure 2d are represented as ratios, by compound, of the percentage areas remaining in the wounds to that of the control. As seen in Figure 2d, in all the cell lines, all the 10,11-EDP derivatives restrict wound healing at 10 h. Among the derivatives, 10,11-EDP-CA shows significantly greater potency at the 6 and 10 h time points, inhibiting migration 4.5–6 times more than vehicle control (at 6 h, p = 0.19 for 143B cells and p = 0.57 for HOS cells, and at 10 h, p = 0.11 for 143B cells and p = 0.37 for HOS cells). Similarly, as seen in Figure 2d, in the 143B and HOS cells, wound healing was 1–1.9 times more restricted by 10,11-EDP-EA (for HOS cells, p = 0.08 at 6 h and p = 0.17 at 10 h, and for 143B cells, p = 0.21 at 6 h and p = 0.05 at 10 h), 1–1.47 times more restricted by 10,11-EDP-NA (for HOS cells, p = 0.39 at 6 h and p = 0.44 at 10 h, and for 143B cells, p = 0.4 at 6 h and p = 0.0002 at 10 h), and 1–3.9 times more restricted by 10,11-EDP-IA (for HOS cells, p = 0.58 at 6 h and p = 0.99 at 10 h, and for 143B cells, p = 0.059 at 6 h and p = 0.002 at 10 h). Finally, in the MG63 cell line (Figure 2d), wound healing was 1–2 times more restricted by 10,11-EDP-EA (at 6 h, p = 0.38, and at 10 h, p = 0.06) compared to the control. 10,11-EDP-NA (at 6 h, p = 0.7, and at 10 h, p = 0.94) and 10,11-EDP-IA (at 6 h, p = 0.57, and at 10 h, p = 0.34) were slightly poorer than the control in restricting cell migration. 10,11-EDP-CA (at 6 h, p = 0.12, and at 10 h, p = 0.09 compared to the vehicle control, and p > 0.9 compared to 10,11-EDP-EA) was comparable to 10,11-EDP-EA in the MG63 cell line in restricting cell migration. As observed from the various results, it was concluded that 10,11-EDP-CA was the most potent in inhibiting wound healing in 143B and HOS cells and comparable to the parent molecule in MG63 cells.

    Cell-Cycle Analysis of 10,11-EDP-EA and Its Analogues

    The effects of 10,11-EDP-EA and its analogues on cell-cycle progression were tested by treating cells with the compounds for 24 h, staining them with propidium iodide, and then analyzing them with flow cytometry. Staining with PI reflects the percentage of cells in G1, S, and G2 stages of the cell cycle. As seen in Table 1, in 143B cells, there does not appear to be a significant effect on the cell cycle except a slight suppression in S phase with 10,11-EDP-NA. However, with the HOS cell lines, there is an increase in G1 phase with all compounds and a suppression in S phase. Finally, with MG63, there is an increase in G1 phase and a suppression in S phase with 10,11-EDP-IA.

    Prevention of Angiogenesis in HUVEC Cells by 10,11-EDP-EA and Its Analogues

    Increased angiogenesis is one of the hallmarks of cancer. The development of neovascularization is key for the adequate supply of nutrients and oxygen to the site of metastasis.(39) Endothelial-tube formation on a matrix has been established as a cellular model for angiogenesis.(40) Endothelial HUVEC cells form a networks of tubes when plated on matrigel, and tube formation in presence and absence of treatment can be quantified as a measure of restricted angiogenic potential. Briefly, endothelial HUVEC cells were grown on a matrix and treated with the vehicle control, 0.5 or 1 μM concentrations of the compounds. Tube formation was assessed by ImageJ analysis. As seen in Figure 3a,b, the 0.5 and 1 μM concentrations restricted tube formation significantly compared to the vehicle control. We also verified that the 1 μM concentrations of the treatments did not reduce cell viability in HUVEC cells, as seen in Figure S5.

    Expression of CB1 and CB2 Receptors in Model Cell Lines

    Literature evidence suggests that DHEA is an agonist of CB1 and CB2 receptors.(11) Therefore, we investigated whether the effect of DHEA is mediated by the CB1–CB2-pathway . Additionally, CB receptors have been shown to be overexpressed in various cancers, including osteosarcoma.(41) We first determined the expression of CB1 and CB2 receptors in all three cell lines, HOS, 143B, and MG63, by qRT-PCR, which quantifies receptor expression by measuring corresponding mRNA levels and comparing them with those of a control marker like β-actin. As seen in Figure S6, all three cell lines expressed CB1 and CB2 at similar levels. Additionally, the expression of CB1 and CB2 was significantly higher in 143B cells compared to expression levels in HOS cells (p = 0.023 and 0.021 for CB1 and CB2, respectively) as well as MG63 cells (p = 0.033 and p = 0.027 for CB1 and CB2, respectively).

    Activation of CB1 and CB2 by 10,11-EDP-EA and Its Amide Analogues

    To investigate whether 10,11-EDP-EA and its amide-modified analogues activated the cannabinoid receptors, a β-arrestin-recruitment PRESTO–Tango assay was performed in a dose-dependent manner for these compounds.(42) PRESTO–Tango is an open-source resource developed by the Roth group that allows the interrogation of the human GPCRome by drugs and endogenous ligands. In this assay, ligand binding to a specific G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) triggers desensitization mediated by the recruitment of protease-tagged arrestin proteins to the activated receptor. This protease-tagged arrestin then cleaves a protease site fused to the C-terminus of the GPCR that is being studied, thus releasing a non-native transcription factor (TF). This TF regulates the transcription of a luciferase reporter construct, which is measured upon the addition of the luminogenic substrate. The activation of the reporter gene by the ligand or drug provides a quantifiable measurement of the degree of interaction between the target receptor and the protease-tagged arrestin partner. Briefly, HTLA cells transfected with either CNR1-Tango or CNR2-Tango were incubated with the appropriate treatment, and subsequently, luminescence was measured as an indicator of receptor activation (Figure 3c,d). CP55940 was used as a control for this experiment, and its EC50 values were observed to be 0.9 for CB1 and 1.73 nM for CB2, which are in agreement with the literature.(43) As observed in Figure 3c, a dose–response curve was obtained for 10,11-EDP-EA and its analogues. 10,11-EDP-EA has EC50 values of 0.43 and 22.5 nM for CB1 and CB2, respectively. This indicates that it shows higher activation of CB1 than CB2. A similar trend was observed for all 10,11-EDP-EA analogues. 10,11-EDP-NA had EC50 values of 0.19 and 7.3 nM, respectively for CB1 and CB2. 10,11-EDP-CA had EC50 values of 0.03 and 0.46 nM for CB1 and CB2, respectively. Finally, 10,11-EDP-IA showed the smallest differences in the activation of CB1 and CB2, with EC50 values of 0.07 and 0.08 nM, respectively. Therefore, all amide analogues had increased activation of both CB1 and CB2 compared to the parent compound, 10,11-EDP-EA, and all activated CB1 better than CB2. As observed in this data, 10,11-EDP-CA is the best activator of CB1. The cannabinoid-receptor-mediated reduction of cancer-cell migration has been reported in the literature,(19) and it can be hypothesized that 10,11-EDP-CA has increased antimigratory potential as a result of its increased activation of CB1.

    Apoptotic Effect of DHEA-Epoxides in the Presence of CB1 and CB2 Antagonists

    As an initial investigation into the mechanism of action for these eCBs, we assessed their apoptotic activity in the presence of the CB1 and CB2 antagonists, rimonabant and AM 630, respectively. As seen from Supplementary Tables ST2–4, rimonabant and AM 630 exhibit very limited apoptotic activity compared to the control. However, the blocking of CB1 and CB2 has a very limited effect on the apoptotic potential of the eCB epoxides. In the HOS cell line (Table ST2), the decrease in apoptosis in the presence of the CB1 antagonist, the CB2 antagonist, or both is modest with all three regioisomers, indicating this is not the primary pathway of action for the eCB epoxides. In 143B cells (Table ST3), when the CB1 and CB2 antagonists were incubated with 7,8-EDP-EA, there were slight increases in apoptotic activity of 14.6 ± 1.82 and 10.1 ± 1.4%, respectively. A similar result was seen for 13,14-EDP-EA, where the treatment and the CB1 antagonist showed 13.03 ± 3.63%. The treatment and the CB2 antagonist showed no difference in apoptosis compared to the treatment alone at 9.2 ± 0.8%. For 10,11-EDP-EA, there was slight suppression of apoptotic activity with both antagonists. The slight increase in apoptosis might be a combinatorial effect of the treatment with the antagonist. Finally, as seen in Table ST4, the effects of the CB1 and CB2 antagonists on MG63 cells were similar to those observed in 143B cells. Alternatively, because the molecules have two different reactive centers, an epoxide and an amide, they likely act through two different receptor classes: the cannabinoid receptor and the unknown receptor for the epoxy fatty acids. It is possible that the blocking of the CB1 or CB2 receptors shuttles the molecules to the second epoxide-receptor pathway, thereby increasing the apoptotic activity. Finally, 10,11-EDP-EA showed a decrease in apoptotic potential with the CB1 and CB2 antagonists at 7.44 ± 2.19 and 10.9 ± 0.08%, indicating that this molecule mainly goes through the CB1 and CB2 receptors.

    Discussion

    Jump To
    Lipid metabolites that are generated from omega-3 fatty acids, such as DHA, have been shown to exert anti-inflammatory, pro-resolving,(4) and antitumorigenic effects through various mechanisms.(5−7) In one mechanism, DHA is converted by CYP epoxygenase to DHA-epoxides (EDPs) that have been shown to exhibit antitumorigenic properties. Alternatively, DHA can be converted to DHEA, an eCB that has shown antiproliferative efficiency in prostate-cancer cells.(11) Previously, we have shown that CYPs convert DHEA into DHEA-epoxides (EDP-EA), which are dual-functional molecules containing both ethanolamide and epoxide moieties(25) (Figure 1a), and both the eCB and epoxyeicosanoid pathways have been separately implicated in cancer.(8,9) Previously, 19,20 EDP-EA, was shown to exhibit anti-inflammatory properties.(25) Herein, we evaluated the antitumorigenic properties of all the different regioisomers of EDP-EAs.
    To ascertain the biological relevance of endocannabinoid epoxides in the tumor microenvironment, we induced tumors in mice and extracted total eCBs from lung-metastasized tumors, which showed that the DHEA-epoxides were increased 20–80%, depending on the regioisomer (Figure 1b), in the metastatic mice with OS tumors compared to mice with normal lungs. Bioactive lipids, such as EDP-EAs, are generated by various cell types, including immune cells. We hypothesize that the upregulation of cytochrome P450s in these tumor tissues and surrounding immune cells leads to the rapid synthesis of these EDP-EAs, which are anti-inflammatory,(25) antiangiogenic, antimetastatic, and potentially pro-resolving. It has been shown before that pro-resolving mediators help in the reduction of inflammation and modify the tumor microenvironment.(44) We tested all five epoxide regioisomers of EDP-EA for their ability to induce apoptosis in three human OS cell lines: 143B, MG63, and HOS. We further show that the EDP-EAs result in reduction of the migratory potentials of cancer cells and moderately increase apoptosis in OS cell lines.
    In all three cell lines, the various regioisomers show apoptotic effects and antimigratory effects that are significantly higher than those of the parent molecules, DHA and DHEA. However, these effects are differential among the various EDP-EA regioisomers. For instance, 10,11-EDP-EA emerges as the most potent regioisomer in reducing cell migration. Interestingly, this effect was mediated via the CB1 receptor; when a CB1 antagonist was used, the antimigratory potential of 10,11-EDP-EA was significantly reduced (Figure S4).
    Because 10,11-EDP-EA showed the greatest potency in our assays, we wanted to characterize the molecule and explore its therapeutic potential in vivo. However, EDP-EAs have two hydrolytically susceptible groups. The amide group is hydrolyzed by FAAH, and the epoxide group is hydrolyzed by sEH. Thus, we prepared synthetic analogues that demonstrated similar apoptotic and migratory potentials (Figure 2c,d) compared to 10,11-EDP-EA, but with a markedly reduced susceptibility to FAAH hydrolysis (Figure 2b).
    Finally, cannabinoid-receptor-mediated apoptosis has been characterized in the literature and is thought to be mediated through ceramide synthesis.(45) The CB1-mediated restriction of cell migration has also been studied.(46) In our studies, we show that although all eCB epoxides and their derivatives activate both cannabinoid receptors, their activity is not completely mediated through these receptors. Therefore, their activity could also be mediated through other receptors, including the uncharacterized receptor for epoxy fatty acids, PPAR, and TRPV.(47)

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Taking the findings all together, we demonstrate a novel class of eCB epoxides, and their stable derivatives exhibit anti-inflammatory,(25) antiangiogenic, antitumorigenic, and antimigratory properties. These molecules particularly reduced the migratory ability of OS cells and therefore may be of greater therapeutic significance to prevent metastasis of tumors. By virtue of their ability to bind CB receptors, these molecules might also exhibit antinociceptive activity . As OS and other forms of cancer are accompanied by inflammation and pain, these molecules might aid in alleviating both conditions. These molecules are naturally occurring and their levels increase ∼80% in OS metastatic lungs, and increasing their levels in vivo using FAAH and sEH dual inhibition will be an effective adjuvant therapy for OS.(48,49)

    General Synthesis of EDP-EAs (1–5)

    EDP-EA synthesis was performed in a two-step approach. First, a nonspecific epoxidation reaction with 50 mg of DHA dissolved in 2 mL of dichloromethane (DCM) with 2 mol equiv of metachloroperoxybenzoic acid (mCPBA) was reacted for 1 h at room temperature. The reaction was stopped with an equal volume of 10% aqueous NaHCO3 to remove mCPBA from the organic layer, and the aqueous layer was re-extracted thrice with equal volumes of DCM; the combined organic layer was dried in vacuo. Purification of regioisomers was achieved using normal-phase HPLC (NP-HPLC) using a Zorbax-NH2 semipreparative column (5 μm, 9.4 × 250 mm, Agilent, PN880952-208) with an isocratic gradient (hexane/isopropanol/acetic acid, 90:10:0.1) coupled to an HPLC system. For coeluting regioisomers of DHA-epoxides (19,20-EDP and 13,14-EDP), the mixtures were further purified on the same system using reverse-phase HPLC (RP-HPLC), a Sun Fire Prep C18 column (5 μm, 19 × 50 mm, PN 186002566; Waters), a mobile system composed of solvent A (H2O/acetonitrile/acetic acid, 95:5:0.1) and solvent B (H2O/acetonitrile/acetic acid, 5:95:0.1), and a linear gradient from 50 to 0% A in 50 min.
    In the second step, the coupling of the purified EDP regioisomer to ethanolamine was achieved by dissolving the epoxide in acetonitrile and incubating it at 37 °C for 20 min with 1-ethyl-3-(3-(dimethylamino)propyl) carbodiimide (EDC) and N-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS). Ethanolamine was subsequently added, and the mixture was stirred overnight at room temperature. The resulting EDP-EA regioisomers were purified by RP-HPLC using a Sun Fire Prep C18 column (5 μm, 19 × 50 mm, PN 186002566; Waters), a mobile system composed of solvent A (H2O/acetonitrile/acetic acid, 95:5:0.1) and solvent B (H2O/acetonitrile/acetic acid, 5:95:0.1), and a linear gradient from 50 to 0% A in 50 min.

    Isolation of Endocannabinoids from Tissue Samples: K7M2 Experimental Metastatic-Tumor Models of Osteosarcoma

    All animal-experimental procedures were reviewed and approved by the University of Illinois Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (Protocol 15120). K7M2 cells (1,000,000) were prepared in HBSS and intravenously injected into the tail veins of 6–8 week old female BALB/c mice (day 0) in 200 μL volumes. After 14 days, the mice were sacrificed, the lung tissue was isolated and weighed, and total endocannabinoids were extracted. The tissue was mechanically homogenized on ice with a BioHomogenizer (BioSpec) in a 1:1 ratio with PBS supplemented with sEH inhibitor 12-(3-adamantan-1-yl-ureido)-dodecanoic acid (AUDA, 30 μm; Cayman Chemical) and FAAH inhibitor, phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF,1 mM) until a homogeneous slurry was formed. Tissue (1 g) was homogenized with 40 mL of ethyl acetate/hexane (9:1) using a BioHomogenizer (BioSpec) at room temperature and then sonicated for 1 min. The resulting solution was washed with a 30% volume of water and centrifuged for the layers to separate. The organic layer was removed, and the aqueous layer was extracted with an equal volume of ethyl acetate/hexane (9:1) twice. The total organic layer was concentrated in vacuo using a Buchi 120 rotary evaporator and reconstituted in 1 mL of chloroform. The metabolites of interest were isolated by solid-phase extraction (SPE) using a 1 mL silica-gel column (no. 214477, Sigma). The reconstituted samples were added to the silica column, washed in three column volumes of chloroform, and consequently eluted with four column volumes of methanol/chloroform (1:9). The eluent was dried, and samples were reconstituted in ethanol for analysis via LC-MS/MS.
