    Abstract

    Radiolabeled analogs of the frog tetradecapeptide bombesin (BBN) have been proposed for diagnosis and therapy of gastrin releasing peptide receptor (GRPR)-expressing tumors. Following a different and yet unexplored approach, we have developed four novel 111In-labeled truncated analogs of the human 27-mer GRP after conjugation of 1,4,7,10-tetraazacyclododecane-1,4,7,10-tetraacetic acid (DOTA) at the N-terminus of GRP(13/14/17/18–27) fragments. Analog affinities for the human GRPR determined against [125I-Tyr4]BBN were at the nanomolar level and dependent on truncation site. The respective 111In radioligands specifically internalized in GRPR-expressing PC-3 cells. The shorter chain [111In-DOTA]GRP(17/18–27) analogs showed higher metabolic stability in mice. Radioligands specifically localized in human PC-3 xenografts in SCID mice, with [111In-DOTA]GRP(17–27) exhibiting the most favorable pharmacokinetic profile. This study has demonstrated the efficacy of human GRP-based radiopeptides to target GRPR-positive lesions in vivo and has revealed the impact of GRP chain length on key biological parameters of resulting radiotracers.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The overexpression of gastrin releasing peptide receptors (GRPRs) in various frequently occurring human tumors, including prostate, breast, and lung cancer,(1-4) has provided the opportunity for GRPR-directed tumor diagnosis and therapy with the aid of radiolabeled peptide probes. As a result, a considerable amount of data has accumulated over the past two decades referring to the preclinical and the clinical evaluation of radiolabeled analogs of the amphibian tetradecapeptide bombesin (BBN) and its derivatives for visualization and treatment of GRPR-positive cancer.(5, 6) These analogs have been modified by conjugation of suitable chelators at the N-terminus to accommodate a wide range of medically appealing radiometals.(7-9) After administration of such radiolabeled BBN-like peptide carriers, γ and positron emitters specifically accumulate in GRPR-positive primary and metastatic tumor sites, allowing diagnostic imaging with SPECT or PET. On the other hand, lesion-localized particle emitters uploading cytotoxic radiation selectively on cancer cells can induce apoptosis and death.(10-15)
    Native BBN isolated from the skin of the European frog Bombina bombina(16) binds with comparable affinity to the mammalian bombesin receptor subtypes neuromedin B (NMBR, BB1R) and GRPR (BB2R).(17) These two subtypes are physiologically expressed in the human brain and gut, especially in stomach, pancreas, and gastrointestinal tract and are pharmacologically distinguished by their selectivity for different endogenous human homologues of frog BBN.(18) Thus, the decapeptide neuromedin B (NMB) displays high affinity for the NMBR, whereas the 27-mer GRP, the 14-mer GRP(14–27), and the C-terminal decapeptide fragment, known also as neuromedin C [NMC, GRP(18–27)], strongly bind the GRPR.(19) All these peptides and their receptors are implicated in cancer.(6, 20-23)
    In contrast to frog BBN-like peptides, the respective human homologues have surprisingly remained unexploited as radionuclide-carriers for targeting GRPR-positive cancer. Prompted by the lack of relevant studies we decided to expand our current search toward human GRP-sequences and explore their applicability in GRPR-targeted diagnosis and therapy of human tumors. As a first step, we have recently introduced the human NMC-based radiotracer, [99mTc]Demomedin C {[99mTc-N4]GRP(18–27)} and its analogs.(24, 25) Compared to previously reported 99mTc-radiopeptides, which were based on the full-length BBN or its truncated BBN(7–14) octapeptide fragment,(9) [99mTc]Demomedin C showed superior in vivo characteristics in tumor-bearing mice, such as faster renal clearance and improved tumor to background ratios.
    This positive experience motivated us to broaden the spectrum of radionuclides considered for labeling beyond the diagnostic γ-emitter 99mTc to include a variety of trivalent radiometals attractive for clinical diagnosis and therapy, such as 111In, 67/68Ga, 177Lu, 90Y, and others. For this purpose, we have now coupled the universal chelator 1,4,7,10-tetraazacyclododecane-1,4,7,10-tetraacetic acid (DOTA)(26, 27) at the N-terminal Gly of NMC [GRP(18–27)]. This position is occupied by basic Arg17 in the native GRP sequence while in [99mTc]Demomedin C it is taken by the positively charged N4+x/[99mTc(O)2(N4)]+1 moiety.(28)
    Another analog was produced by elongation of the peptide chain of NMC to positions 17 of human GRP and DOTA conjugation to the corresponding Arg17. Further elongation on the natural GRP sequence to Met14 and coupling of DOTA provides the conjugate corresponding to the endogenously occurring GRP(14–27) fragment. Finally, DOTA was coupled to the basic amino acid Lys13 of native GRP. In short, four truncated GRP analogs have been obtained and labeled with 111In, corresponding to the naturally occurring GRP(14/18–27) sequences and their GRP(13/17–27) counterparts containing the basic amino acids Lys13 and Arg17, respectively.
    The four peptide conjugates, [DOTA]GRP(13–27) (1), [DOTA]GRP(14–27) (2), [DOTA]GRP(17–27) (3), and [DOTA]GRP(18–27) (4), were labeled with 111In. The suitability of the resulting [111In]1–[111In]4 radioligands (Figure 1) for diagnostic imaging of GRPR-expressing tumors was directly compared in GRPR-positive in vitro models and in SCID mice bearing human prostate cancer PC-3 xenografts. Accordingly, important information on the impact of GRP-chain length as well as of the positively charged Lys13 and Arg17 residues on several biological parameters of respective (radio)peptides could be acquired. These results are instrumental for early screening of the respective 177Lu/90Y/213Bi-radioligands potentially considered for GRPR-aimed tumor therapy.

    Synthesis of [DOTA]GRPs and 111In Labeling

    The four peptide sequences (i) H-Lys-Met-Tyr-Pro-Arg-Gly-Asn-His-Trp-Ala-Val-Gly-His-Leu-Met-NH2 (for 1), (ii) H-Met-Tyr-Pro-Arg-Gly-Asn-His-Trp-Ala-Val-Gly-His-Leu-Met-NH2 (for 2), (iii) H-Arg-Gly-Asn-His-Trp-Ala-Val-Gly-His-Leu-Met-NH2 (for 3), and (iv) H-Gly-Asn-His-Trp-Ala-Val-Gly-His-Leu-Met-NH2 (for 4) were assembled on the solid support following typical Fmoc-protection methodology, and 1-(acetic acid)-4,7,10-tris(tert-butoxycarbonylmethyl)-1,4,7,10-tetraazacyclododecane (DOTA-tris(tBu ester)) was attached to their N-terminus in the last cycle. After release from the resin and removal of lateral protecting groups with TFA treatment, the four new DOTA–GRP conjugates (Figure 1) were isolated by chromatographic methods in ≥95% purity, as shown by analytical HPLC; MALDI-TOF spectra of the purified products 1–4 were consistent with the expected formulas (Table 1).

    The corresponding four [111In-DOTA]GRP radiopeptides (Figure 1) were easily prepared by ≤20 min heating of respective [DOTA]GRP conjugates with 111InCl3 in acidic medium. Complete incorporation of 111In by DOTA was verified by RP-HPLC, corresponding to >98% radiolabeling yield (less than 2% EDTA-bound 111In3+).(29, 30) The respective [111In-DOTA]GRP radioligands formed in >96% radiochemical purity (percentage of single [111In-DOTA]GRP species versus total peptide-associated radioactivity). Typical specific activities of 3.7–7.4 MBq/nmol peptide were easily achieved, and consequently, [111In]1–[111In]4 were used as such without subsequent purification in all further experiments. The presence of 20 mM Met in the radiolabeling reaction mixtures as well as keeping incubation times at elevated temperatures as briefly as possible (≤20 min at 90 °C) successfully prevented sulfoxide formation (<5%). Without these precautions, up to 30% sulfoxide species (40 min at 90 °C and no Met addition) were detected by radio-HPLC, in agreement with previous reports.(31)
    Furthermore, all solutions of [111In-DOTA]GRP radioligands (used in in vitro and in in vivo studies) contained methionine at a concentration of at least 1 mM. Tracer stock solutions used in biological testing (kept at room temperature and never used for longer than 2 h after their preparation) were always reanalyzed by HPLC, which did not reveal any significant increase in sulfoxide or any other radiochemical impurity.

    In Vitro Studies

    Affinity of [DOTA]GRPs for the Human GRPR

    The binding affinities of the four new [DOTA]GRP conjugates for the human GRPR were determined against [125I-Tyr4]BBN in PC-3 cell membranes using human GRP(14–27) as reference peptide (IC50 = 0.5 ± 0.1 nM). All analogs were able to effectively displace the radioligand from GRPR sites in a monophasic and dose-dependent manner, as shown in Figure 2. According to the IC50 values determined, the following rank of affinity could be established: 1 (IC50 = 2.3 ± 0.2 nM) > 2 (IC50 = 6.6 ± 0.9 nM) > 3 (IC50 = 19.2 ± 2.9 nM) ≫ 4 (IC50 = 112 ± 16 nM). These results demonstrate the impact of peptide chain length and of positively charged Lys13 and Arg17 residues on receptor affinity of the respective DOTA–GRP conjugates.

    Internalization of [111In-DOTA]GRPs in PC-3 Cells

    It is interesting to note that the internalization efficacy of [111In-DOTA]GRP radioligands in PC-3 cells was in line with the receptor affinity of the respective unlabeled analogs. As summarized in Figure 3, [111In]1 showed the highest internalization efficiency (29.0 ± 0.3% specifically internalized of total added radioactivity), whereas the poorest internalization capacity was exhibited by [111In]4 (4.1 ± 0.2% specifically internalized of total added radioactivity). Internalization of [111In]2 and [111In]3 also paralleled receptor affinity results with the respective values amounting to 21.3 ± 0.1% and 17.5 ± 0.5%. In all cases, internalization dropped below 0.5% in the presence of 1 μM [Tyr4]BBN, indicating a GRPR-mediated process.

    Metabolic Stability of [111In-DOTA]GRPs in Mice

    The percentage of [111In-DOTA]GRP radiopeptides found intact 5 min after entry into the bloodstream of mice was measured by RP-HPLC analysis of mouse blood samples. As shown in Figure 4, a different extent of degradation and distinct radiometabolite patterns were observed for individual [111In-DOTA]GRPs. Experimental results ranked the analogs according to stability as follows: [111In]3 (26.6%; 25.3% intact) ≈ [111In]4 (22.5%; 24.5% intact) > [111In]1 (22 ± 6% intact, n = 3) ≫ [111In]2 (4.5%; 4% intact).

    Uptake of [111In-DOTA]GRPs in the experimental tumors substantially differed across compounds. Thus, the highest PC-3 tumor uptake was exhibited by [111In]1 (7.74 ± 1.93% ID/g at 4 h postinjection; Table 2), whereas the lowest was displayed by [111In]4 (3.54 ± 1.09% ID/g at 4 h postinjection; Table 3); the remaining two radiopeptides, [111In]2 and [111In]3, showed very comparable tumor uptake (4–4.6% ID/g at 4 h postinjection). This trend was also preserved in the respective uptake of [111In-DOTA]GRPs in the mouse pancreas, which likewise reached the highest level in the case of [111In]1 (≈40% ID/g at 4 h postinjection; Table 2).

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Over the past two decades significant effort has been invested in the development of radiolabeled BBN analogs with the aim to diagnose and combat GRPR-positive cancer in man.(5, 6, 10) This pursuit has been based on the rationale that GRPRs, which are abundantly expressed in frequently occurring human cancers, can serve as easily accessible and highly selective molecular targets for BBN-like radionuclide carriers. In view of the lower GRPR expression in healthy background tissues, high contrast imaging and/or good therapeutic efficacy can be achieved.(1-3) Frog BBN binds with high affinity to human GRPR, inducing internalization, which in turn is expected to promote the localization of radiolabeled BBN-like agonists in GRPR-positive target cells and further improve diagnostic/therapeutic outcome.(8, 14) Accordingly, a great number of BBN and BBN(7–14) octapeptide analogs designed to carry a wide range of diagnostic and therapeutic radiometals to GRPR-positive cancer have been tested both in animal models and in patients.(9, 11-15)
    Surprisingly, however, reports on GRPR-directed radiopeptides based on the more selective native human homologues of amphibian BBN have been altogether missing. The 27-mer GRP, GRP(14–27), and the C-terminal GRP(18–27) decapeptide fragment, referred to also as NMC, are the three well-known endogenous human homologues of BBN showing high interaction capacity with the GRPR (Figure 1).(6, 19) We have recently been engaged in the study of human GRP(18–27)-based radioligands as an alternative route to target GRPR-expressing tumors in man. In our first attempt we coupled an acyclic tetraamine to the N-terminal Gly of NMC and its derivatives for stable binding of the SPECT radionuclide 99mTc.(24, 25) The resulting radiotracer [99mTc]Demomedin C showed a very promising preclinical profile related to its faster background clearance when compared to previously reported and less GRPR-selective [99mTc]Demobesin 3–6, which were based on BBN.
    In the present study, we first of all aimed toward multipurpose GRP analogs suitable for labeling with a wider spectrum of clinically relevant radiometals, including γ (111In, 67Ga: SPECT), positron (68Ga: PET), and particle emitters (90Y, 177Lu, 213Bi: radionuclide therapy).(26, 27) For this purpose we have used the universal chelator DOTA, instead of a tetraamine unit, for conjugation to the peptide moiety and 111In labeling for a first screening of new compounds.
    In the design of our analogs we used human GRP-based sequences truncated in four different positions and based on the endogenous GRP(14–27) and GRP(18–27) structures. More specifically, we have coupled DOTA at the N-terminus of the following four different chain-length fragments: GRP(13/14/17/18–27) (1–4), whereby the shortest corresponds to the decapeptide NMC. Truncation sites were selected around the two basic amino acid Arg17 and Lys13 residues with the purpose to assess the significance of the positive charge present in these sites on key-biological characteristics of end (radio)ligands, such as GRPR-affinity, metabolic stability, and renal clearance. It should be noted, that in Demomedin C/[99mTc]Demomedin C a positively charged moiety (N4+x/[99mTc(O)2(N4)]+1) is replacing Arg17.(24, 28) In the case of 4, DOTA is instead introduced in this position, which after binding of 111In forms a highly polar metal chelate. In 3, the DOTA chelator is shifted next to Arg17, whereas a parallel pattern is reproduced around Lys13 in the longer chain 1 and 2 members.
    The effect of above structural variations on GRPR affinity was first assessed during competition binding assays against [125I-Tyr4]BBN in PC-3 cell membranes. As shown in Figure 2, both peptide chain length and DOTA position greatly affect receptor affinity. In particular, GRP affinity increases in the longer chain members and especially when basic Lys is preserved in position 13 (IC50 = 2.3 ± 0.2 nM, for 1). The poorest affinity is exhibited by the shortest-chain analog 4 (IC50 = 112 ± 16 nM), whereby DOTA is replacing Arg17. By comparing this affinity with the affinity of Demomedin C (IC50 = 0.36 ± 0.01 nM)(24) it becomes apparent that the presence of the negatively charged DOTA chelator at position 17 is not well tolerated by the GRPR. The internalization of the respective [111In-DOTA]GRP radiopeptides in PC-3 cells after 1 h incubation at 37 °C follows similar but less pronounced differences compared to the relative affinities of nonmetalated conjugates for the GRPR. Again, both peptide chain length and charge at positions 17 and 13 affect internalization capacities, as summarized in Figure 3.
    After entry into circulation, all four [111In-DOTA]GRP radioligands suffered rapid enzymatic degradation in mice. As shown in Figure 4, radiometabolite patterns differed across analogs as well as the extent of breakdown within 5 min. In general, the shorter-chain [111In]3 and [111In]4 survived better (>20% intact at 5 min postinjection) as compared to the longer-chain [111In]1 and [111In]2 members. The poorest stability was displayed by [111In]2 with only <5% detected intact in blood after the brief 5 min exposure in mouse circulation. Interestingly, the shorter-chain members presenting less putative cleavage sites to assailing enzymes survived longer in circulation, showing in vivo stability similar to that of [99mTc]Demomedin C.(24)
    Comparative biodistribution in mice bearing human GRPR-positive PC-3 xenografts reveals how the combination of receptor affinity, internalization capacity, and metabolic stability synergistically translates into the final in vivo profile, with focus primarily on tumor uptake and background clearance. It is interesting to note that the most GRPR-affine and best internalizing [111In]1 exhibits the highest tumor uptake (7.74 ± 1.93% ID/g at 4 h postinjection) in this series of analogs, while the least receptor-affine and poorly internalizing [111In]4 shows the lowest uptake in the experimental tumor (3.54 ± 1.09% ID/g at 4 h postinjection). These two analogs do not differ much in terms of metabolic stability. The high tumor uptake of [111In]1 is however greatly compromised by unfavorably high and persistent accumulation of radioactivity in the kidneys (46.02 ± 3.73% ID/g at 4 h postinjection; 29.00 ± 4.86% ID/g at 24 h postinjection) which exceeds by far the respective values of the other [111In-DOTA]GRP analogs (Tables 2 and 3). Renal uptake seems to be related to the Lys13-residue, as previously reported for other Lys-containing radiopeptides.(32-34) Co-injection of Lys and/or gelofusine or other kidney-protection regimens are currently considered as potential means to reduce the observed high renal uptake.(35) Alternatively, replacement of Lys13 by another positively charged residue, e.g., by Arg, amino acid d-enantiomers, or other unnatural basic residues, may prove to be effective for this purpose.(36)
    Despite its higher affinity and internalization efficiency, the tumor uptake of [111In]2 is markedly lower (4.13 ± 0.40% ID/g at 4 h postinjection), presumably as a consequence of rapid enzymatic degradation upon entry into the bloodstream of mice. Slightly higher tumor uptake is shown by the more stable albeit less GRPR-affine [111In]3 (4.63 ± 0.59% ID/g at 4 h postinjection). This analog shows faster renal clearance and lower accumulation in GRPR-rich pancreas. In view of the above considerations, [111In]3 displays the most preferable overall pharmacokinetic profile. It is interesting to compare this profile with previously reported [99mTc]Demomedin C in the same animal model. The 99mTc-radioligand displays higher uptake in the PC-3 xenografts (6.36 ± 0.85% ID/g at 4 h postinjection) but also higher uptake in both pancreas and kidneys.(24)
    Extrapolation of stability and biodistribution data of [111In]3 to [177Lu/90Y/213Bi]3, intended for radionuclide therapy, should be made with caution and should be supported by experimental data. Potential changes in biological responses may ensue by using a different radiometal, owing to coordination chemistry differences across metals causing changes in the physicochemical properties of end radioligands.(26) Interestingly, the metabolic stability of bombesin-like radiopeptides has been elegantly investigated thus far in vivo only for [177Lu]AMBA and is in the same range with [111In]3.(37) This stability appears to be inferior compared to the highly in vivo stable analogs of octreotide (e.g., Lutathera, [90Y]DOTATOC) used in radionuclide therapy(38) and may eventually prove to be a hurdle for application in human. On the other hand, the blood clearance of systemically administered radiopeptides is in general also a very fast process, and highly enzyme-resistant analogs may inadvertently show unfavorably prolonged retention in excretory organs (e.g., in the kidneys), reducing their therapeutic index. Ongoing studies will eventually address these issues and systematically explore the applicability of human GRP-related radioligands in radionuclide therapy of GRPR-expressing cancer.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    As a part of our ongoing quest for human GRP-based radioligands to visualize and treat GRPR-expressing human tumors, we present four new DOTA-coupled GRP analogs truncated at positions 13, 14, 17, and 18 in the natural GRP 27-mer sequence and their respective 111In-radioligands. Receptor affinity, internalization efficiency, and tumor uptake of new analogs in mice were favored both by longer peptide chain and by the presence of basic amino acids in positions 17 and 13 in the parent peptide. However, Lys13 provokes unfavorably high renal accumulation of [111In]1, which could be probably reduced by the application of kidney protection regimens or after Lys13 substitution by Arg or another positively charged residue. Due to its better metabolic stability in combination with a faster clearance from background, [111In]3 seems to represent the best choice for further studies in animal models and eventually in man, labeled not only with 111In but also with other radiometals useful for diagnosis (e.g., 68Ga) and therapy (e.g., 177Lu, 90Y, 213Bi).
    This work has further confirmed previous findings from the preclinical evaluation of NMC-based 99mTc-radioligands on the efficacy of human GRP analogs to target GRPR-positive cancer with high sensitivity and specificity. Currently ongoing studies will establish potential benefits of their use against their frog-originating and less GRPR-selective counterparts. It is reasonable to assume that competent structural interventions on the GRP chain, involving for example smart selection of truncation site and/or strategic amino acid replacements, may yield radioligands of high diagnostic/therapeutic impact.

    Analysis—Radiochemistry

    Synthesis of analogs was carried out on an automated peptide synthesizer (PSSM 8, Shimadzu). Purification of the peptide conjugates was performed by RP-HPLC using an AKZO Nobel Kromasil Semi/Prep C18 column (250 mm × 20 mm) eluted with a linear gradient of solvent A (0.1% TFA in H2O) and solvent B (MeCN containing 0.1% TFA) from 10% B to 70% B in 50 min with a flow rate of 12 mL/min. The eluent was monitored by measuring the UV absorption at 215 nm.
    Mass spectra were obtained on a MALDI-TOF spectrometer (Kompact Kratos Axima Analytical, Shimadzu, Manchester, UK). Analytical HPLC characterization of the conjugates was performed by RP HPLC on an Agilent system equipped with a Nucleosil-100 (Macherey Nagel) C18 column (5 μm, 150 mm × 4 mm) and UV detection at 215 nm, applying a linear gradient of MeCN containing 0.1% TFA (10–90% in 30 min) and water containing 0.1% TFA with a flow rate of 1 mL/min (system 1). Analysis and radiochemical tests were additionally performed on a Waters Chromatograph (Waters, Vienna, Austria) with a 600 solvent delivery system coupled to both a 996 photodiode array UV detector (Waters, Vienna, Austria) and a Gabi γ-detector (Raytest RSM Analytische Instrumente GmbH, Germany); the Millennium Software (Waters, Vienna, Austria) was applied to control the HPLC system and process the data. Analyses were conducted on a Waters Xterra RP18 (5 μm, 4.6 mm × 150 mm) eluted with a linear gradient system of 15% B to 30% B in 30 min at a flow rate of 1 mL/min, where A = 0.1% TFA in H2O (v/v) and B = MeCN (system 2).
    Metabolic studies were conducted using a chromatograph (600 solvent delivery system, coupled to both a 2998 photodiode array UV detector; Waters, Vienna, Austria) and a Gabi γ-detector (Raytest RSM Analytische Instrumente GmbH, Germany). The Empower Software (Waters, Vienna, Austria) was used to control the HPLC system and process the data. A RP18 Symmetry Shield column was employed (5 μm, 3.9 mm × 20 mm, Waters, Vienna, Austria), and the column was eluted at a flow rate of 1 mL/min with the following gradient: 100% A to 90% A in 10 min and from 90% A to 60% for the next 60 min, where A = 0.1% aqueous TFA (v/v) and B = MeCN (system 3).
    For radioactivity measurements an automatic well-type γ-counter calibrated for 111In and 125I was used [NaI(Tl) 3′′-crystal, Canberra Packard Auto-Gamma 5000 series instrument].
    As shown by analytical HPLC (systems 1 and 2), products 1–4 were obtained in ≥95% purity (Table 1).

    Synthesis of [DOTA]GRP Peptide Conjugates

    Synthesis of the four [DOTA]GRP(13/14/17/18–27) peptide analogs was performed on an automated synthesizer on a Tenta Gel S Ram resin as solid support. The elongation was carried out by coupling of a 10-fold excess of Fmoc amino acid derivatives, in the presence of 1-hydroxybenzotriazole (HOBt), N,N′-diisopropylcarbodiimide (DIC) and diisopropylethylamine (DIPEA) in N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) and dichloromethane (DCM) 90:10 v/v as solvent. After each coupling step Fmoc deprotection was conducted with 30% piperidine in DMF. The assembled amino acid sequences were coupled with a 3-fold excess of DOTA-tris(tBu ester), benzotriazol-1-yloxy-tris(pyrrolidino)phosphonium hexafluorophosphate (PyBOP), and DIPEA in DMF on the resin. Cleavage of the conjugates from the solid support was achieved by treating the fully protected peptide chains with a mixture of trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), 1,2-ethanedithiol (EDT), thioanisol (TA), and H2O in a ratio of 90:4:4:2 v/v/v/v at room temperature. After removal of the resin from the cleavage mixture by filtration, each crude peptide was collected by precipitation with ice-cooled diethyl ether. Finally, the crude [DOTA]GRP conjugates were purified by RP-HPLC on a Semi/Prep C18 column (250 mm × 20 mm). Analytical RP-HPLC data (systems 1 and 2) as well as MALDI-TOF MS results are summarized in Table 1.

    Radiolabeling and Quality Control of [111In-DOTA]GRPs

    Lyophilized analogs were dissolved in water to a final concentration of 2 mg/mL and stored at −20 °C in 50-μL aliquots. Radiolabeling with 111In was conducted by adding 10 nmol of peptide analog per 37–74 MBq of 111InCl3 in 0.1 M sodium acetate buffer and 10 mM sodium ascorbate. Typical end pH was 4.6. Labeling was completed after incubation in a boiling water bath for ≤20 min.(29) To prevent oxidation of Met residues to the corresponding sulfoxides, 0.1 M Met solution was added in the labeling reaction mixture to a final concentration of 20 mM.(31)
    Quality control was conducted after addition of EDTA (in 0.1 M acetate buffer pH 4.6) to a final concentration of 1 mM in the labeling reaction mixture to capture free In3+.(30) Analyses were performed on a RP18 column using system 2.

    In Vitro Assays

    Competition Binding of [DOTA]GRPs in PC-3 Cell Membranes

    For in vitro assays human androgen-independent prostate adenocarcinoma PC-3 cells were used as GRPR source.(39) PC-3 cells (by LGC Promochem, Teddington, UK) were cultured as previously reported and PC-3 cell membranes used in competition binding experiments were cropped according to a published method.(9, 24) The [125I-Tyr4]BBN radioligand was prepared as described previously and GRP(14–27) served as reference.(9) The radioligand (50 pM per assay tube) and increasing concentrations (10–5–10–13 M) of test peptide in triplicates were incubated with membranes at 22 °C for 1 h in an incubator–orbital shaker (MPM Instr. SrI). Samples were measured for radioactivity in an automatic well-type γ-counter. IC50 values were determined by nonlinear regression according to a one-site model applying the PRISM 2 program (Graph Pad Software, San Diego, CA).

    Internalization of [111In-DOTA]GRPs in PC-3 Cells

    For internalization, confluent PC-3 cells were seeded in six-well plates (∼1.0 × 106 cells per well) 24 h before conducting the experiment. Test [111In-DOTA]GRP analog (≈50 000 cpm corresponding to 250 fmol total peptide in 150 μL of 0.5% BSA/PBS) was added alone (total) or in the presence of 1 μM [Tyr4]BBN (nonspecific) and the experiment was performed following a published protocol.(9) Results were calculated as percent internalized activity per total added for each time point by applying the Microsoft Excel program and represent the average of at least two experiments performed in triplicate.

    Metabolism of [111In-DOTA]GRPs in Mice

    [111In-DOTA]GRP was injected as a 100 μL bolus (11–22 MBq, 3 nmol total peptide) in the tail vein of male Swiss albino mice (30 ± 5 g, NCSR “Demokritos” Animal House Facility). Mice were anesthetized with ether and blood (0.5–1 mL) was collected from the heart at 5 min postinjection. Blood samples were immediately transferred in prechilled polypropylene tubes placed on ice containing EDTA and Met. Samples were prepared as previously described(40) and analysis by HPLC was conducted by applying system 3; experiments were conducted at least twice. Co-injection with labeling reaction samples was performed to identify intact [111In-DOTA]GRPs by coelution (same tR) through the HPLC column.

    Biodistribution of [111In-DOTA]GRPs in SCID Mice Bearing Human PC-3 Xenografts

    A ≈150 μL bolus containing a suspension of ≈1.5 × 107 freshly harvested human PC-3 cells in saline was subcutaneously injected in the flanks of female SCID mice (15 ± 3 g, 6 weeks of age on arrival day, NCSR “Demokritos” Animal House Facility). After 3 weeks the animals developed well-palpable tumors at the inoculation site (80–150 mg) and biodistribution was conducted. On the day of the experiment, test [111In-DOTA]GRP radioligand was injected in mice via the tail vein as a 100 μL bolus (37–74 kBq, 10 pmol total peptide; in saline/EtOH 9/1 v/v containing 1 mM Met), and the animals were sacrificed in groups of four at 4 and 24 h postinjection; for in vivo GRPR blockade a separate 4-h animal group was co-injected with excess [Tyr4]BBN (40 nmol). Biodistribution was conducted as previously described,(9) and data were calculated as percent injected dose per gram of tissue (% ID/g) with the aid of suitable standards of the injected dose using the Microsoft Excel program.
    Statistical analysis using the unpaired two-tailed Student’s t test was performed to compare values between the control and the in vivo GRPR blockade animal groups at 4 h postinjection; values P < 0.005 were considered statistically highly significant.
    All animal experiments were carried out in compliance with European and national regulations and after approval of protocols by national authorities.

        Kidney protection during peptide receptor radionuclide therapy with somatostatin analogues
        Rolleman Edgar J; Melis Marleen; Valkema Roelf; Boerman Otto C; Krenning Eric P; de Jong Marion
        European journal of nuclear medicine and molecular imaging (2010), 37 (5), 1018-31 ISSN:.
        This review focuses on the present status of kidney protection during peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT) using radiolabelled somatostatin analogues. This treatment modality for somatostatin receptor-positive tumours is limited by renal reabsorption and retention of radiolabelled peptides resulting in dose-limiting high kidney radiation doses. Radiation nephropathy has been described in several patients. Studies on the mechanism and localization demonstrate that renal uptake of radiolabelled somatostatin analogues largely depends on the megalin/cubulin system in the proximal tubule cells. Thus methods are needed that interfere with this reabsorption pathway to achieve kidney protection. Such methods include coadministration of basic amino acids, the bovine gelatin-containing solution Gelofusine or albumin fragments. Amino acids are already commonly used in the clinical setting during PRRT. Other compounds that interfere with renal reabsorption capacity (maleic acid and colchicine) are not suitable for clinical use because of potential toxicity. The safe limit for the renal radiation dose during PRRT is not exactly known. Dosimetry studies applying the principle of the biological equivalent dose (correcting for the effect of dose fractionation) suggest that a dose of about 37 Gy is the threshold for development of kidney toxicity. This threshold is lower when risk factors for development of renal damage exist: age over 60 years, hypertension, diabetes mellitus and previous chemotherapy. A still experimental pathway for kidney protection is mitigation of radiation effects, possibly achievable by cotreatment with amifostine (Ethylol), a radiation protector, or with blockers of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system. Future perspectives on improving kidney protection during PRRT include combinations of agents to reduce renal retention of radiolabelled peptides, eventually together with mitigating medicines. Moreover, new somatostatin analogues with lower renal retention may be developed. Furthermore, knowledge on kidney protection from radiolabelled somatostatin analogues may be expanded to other peptides.
