    Abstract

    Scaffold hopping and structure-based drug design were employed to identify substituted 4-aminoquinolines and 4-aminonaphthyridines as potent, small molecule inhibitors of tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNFα). Structure–activity relationships in both the quinoline and naphthyridine series leading to the identification of compound 42 with excellent potency and pharmacokinetic profile are discussed. X-ray co-crystal structure analysis and ultracentrifugation experiments clearly demonstrate that these inhibitors distort the TNFα trimer upon binding, leading to aberrant signaling when the trimer binds to TNF receptor 1 (TNFR1). Pharmacokinetic–pharmacodynamic activity of compound 42 in a TNF-induced IL-6 mouse model and in vivo activity in a collagen antibody-induced arthritis model, where it showed biologic-like in vivo efficacy, will be discussed.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    TNFα is a multifunctional cytokine produced by monocytes and macrophages as part of the inflammatory process and can lead to a diverse range of cellular responses including differentiation, proliferation, inflammation, and cell death.(1) TNFα is the archetypal member of the TNF superfamily (TNFSF), a diverse family of ligands involved in the orchestration of immune and inflammatory processes.(2) Similar to other TNFSF members, TNFα is a type II transmembrane protein that can be cleaved and secreted as a soluble form.(2) Both the transmembrane and soluble forms of TNFα exist predominantly as biologically active trimeric complexes that signal through TNF receptors 1 and 2.(3)
    Based on the proinflammatory role of TNFα, a number of biologics that act to inhibit TNF binding to TNF receptors have been tested clinically. These agents include antibodies specific to TNFα that interfere with binding to TNFRs (adalimumab, golimumab, certolizumab, and infliximab) and a dimeric soluble form of TNFR2 (etanercept). These agents have proven to be clinically effective in a number of autoimmune and autoinflammatory diseases, including rheumatoid arthritis (RA), psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, Crohn’s disease, psoriasis, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, ulcerative colitis, hidradenitis suppurativa, and certain types of uveitis.(4)
    Owing to the success of biologics targeting TNFα or its receptor, significant efforts have been made to identify small molecule inhibitors that disrupt the TNFα/TNFR interaction.(5,6) Small molecule approaches have significant advantages over biologics, in terms of oral delivery, being nonimmunogenic, and cost-effective, particularly for chronic indications. Therefore, small molecules with drug-like properties that deliver equivalent activity to anti-TNF biologics are highly desirable. Although significant work has been done in this area, a majority of these efforts have led to compounds that are micromolar inhibitors of this interaction at best, thus hindering their progressability. More recently, seminal work conducted by scientists at UCB Pharma has led to the identification of potent small molecule inhibitors of TNFα.(7) These compounds were identified using fragment-based approaches and were shown to bind to an allosteric site within TNFα and stabilize the trimeric unit in a nonsymmetrical conformation that allows the TNFα trimer to recruit only two out of the three copies of TNFR1, leading to an incompetent TNF-TNFR1 signaling complex.
    Inspired by this discovery, we adopted a scaffold hopping approach starting from the reported benzimidazole-based scaffold (1)(7) to identify novel inhibitors of TNFα, generically represented by 2 (Figure 1).
    For the purposes of progressing compounds we mostly relied on a high-throughput human cell-based reporter gene assay measuring the response of secreted alkaline phosphatase (SEAP)-linked NF-κB, to TNF stimulation, in the HEK 293 cell-line (HEK-Blue). An analogous assay employing the CD40 ligand (CD40L) as the stimulant was used as an initial selectivity measure of the compounds within the TNFSF. Because the human assays were run in 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), it gave us a preliminary indication of the extent of protein binding of the compounds and allowed us to optimize for cellular potency. In addition, we employed a homogeneous time resolved fluorescence (HTRF) assay to determine the binding of compounds to TNFα.

    As the data from Table 1 indicates, the parent quinoline compound (5) was inactive in the HEK-Blue assay. Although this result was disappointing, we were delighted to see that the 3-chloroquinoline analogue (7) exhibited very good potency in the same assay. The fact that a single atom change, H to Cl, led to this remarkable result is intriguing and this SAR trend was observed with other analogues in this series (vide infra). It is also interesting to note that the bromo analogue (6), which was an intermediate in the synthetic sequence, also showed sub-micromolar activity in the HEK-Blue assay, clearly suggesting a key role for the chloro or methyl substituent (9) in potentially stabilizing the TNFα trimer (vide infra) and interfering with TNFα signaling.

In addition, we noted potent CYP2C9 and 2C19 inhibition with compounds unsubstituted at the C-2 position of the quinoline ring. However, we were able to right shift the potency in the CYP assays without significantly compromising potency in the cellular assay, by introducing a methyl substituent at the C-2 position (compare 8 and 10).     We were pleased to note that the parent benzyl compound 11 showed reasonable binding in the HTRF assay, although it was inactive in the cellular assay. Our first breakthrough in the benzyl series came with the (R)-methylbenzyl analogue (12), which showed excellent binding in the HTRF assay and moderate potency in the HEK-Blue assay. The (S)-methyl analogue (13) showed significantly reduced binding in the HTRF assay and was inactive in the cellular assay. In order to improve the potency, we explored substitutions on the phenyl ring of the benzylamine moiety. As is evident from Table 2, the (R)-1-(2-fluorophenyl)ethan-1-amine analogue (16) showed a 4–5 fold improvement in cellular potency versus the des-fluoro analogue (12). It is interesting to note that although the meta-fluoro and para-fluoro analogues (17 and 18, respectively) retained potency in the HTRF assay, they were significantly less potent in the HEK-Blue assay.
    In order to understand the binding mode of these compounds and the potential mechanism of action, we obtained an X-ray co-crystal structure of 16 with TNFα (Figure 2).
    Examination of the X-ray co-crystal structure(8) revealed that the quinoline nitrogen engages in a critical H-bond with Tyr227C and the quinoline ring is engaged in a face to face π–π interaction with Tyr135C. The fluorophenyl moiety is engaged in an edge to face π–π interaction with Tyr135A and the quinoline −NH is engaged in an H-bonding interaction with Tyr195B. The proximal section of the C-6 pyrimidine substituent is located in a hydrophobic pocket formed by Leu233A, Leu133B, and Leu133C with a hydrogen bond with the hydroxyl group and carboxy terminus of Leu233A. Beyond this pocket, a number of water molecules are localized and the distal portion of the pyrimidine moiety appears to be exposed to the solvent. The chloro substituent is located in a small hydrophobic pocket formed by the side chains of Tyr195B and Tyr195C and the main chains of Leu196C and Gly197C. As discussed earlier and as will be apparent later, engagement of these residues appears to be critical in imparting functional activity for this series of TNFα inhibitors. In addition, it is possible that the chloro (and methyl) substituent can orient the N-benzyl moiety in the bioactive conformation required for functional activity. The improvement in cellular potency seen with the ortho-fluoro substituent could potentially be because of the increased H-bonding donor capability of the quinoline −NH with Tyr195B (due to the negative inductive effect of fluorine) leading to the stabilization of the trimer. This is consistent with the decreased cellular potency observed for the unsubstituted benzyl compound (12) and the meta and para-fluoro analogues (17 and 18, respectively).
    Other observations from the X-ray co-crystal structure are as follows: (a) the inhibitor binding site is a cavity within TNFα trimer created via movement of monomer A, (b) the inhibitor stabilizes the trimer in an inactive conformation by forming key π–π and hydrogen bonding interactions, (c) inhibitor 16 binds to TNFα trimer leading to major disruption of one TNFR binding site and minor disruption of a second site, while the third site remains unchanged, and (d) the inhibitor modulates TNFR activity through an allosteric mechanism rather than direct competition with TNFR. Based on these observations, it is reasonable to assume that binding of the inhibitor to the symmetric TNFα trimer leads to the formation of an asymmetric trimer which prevents the recruitment of three TNF receptor molecules that are necessary for signaling.
    In addition to the SAR for the α-methylbenzyl moiety described above, other key takeaways from Table 2 are as follows—the methyl group on the benzylic carbon appears to be optimal because increasing the size to ethyl (19) or isopropyl (20) leads to a drop in functional potency. Similarly, replacing the ortho-fluoro substituent with a more electron-withdrawing but bulkier CF3 group (21) leads to a ∼2-fold decrease in binding and functional potency compared to 15. The 2-pyridyl analogue (22) is significantly less potent in the HTRF assay and inactive in the HEK-Blue assay. A similar trend was observed for the 3- and 4-pyridyl analogues (data not shown). In order to improve the potency of the series, we examined the aforementioned X-ray co-crystal structure of 16 with TNFα and noticed a structural water that forms hydrogen bonds to the Tyr195A side chain and Gly197B and is in the vicinity of the C-5 position of the fluorophenyl ring. We reasoned that we could engage the structural water with an H-bonding donor substituent projecting off the C-5 position of the phenyl ring or displace it using a nitrile moiety. We also noted that the Tyr195A side chain was flexible, suggesting the tolerance for bulkier substituents in this region of the protein. To test this hypothesis, we synthesized the nitrile (23) and the aniline analogues (24)—both compounds were approximately 2-fold more potent than the parent fluorophenyl analogue 16 in the cellular assay. Bulky substituents at the C-5 position are tolerated as exemplified by the pyrrolidine analogue 26; however, it is approximately 3-fold less potent than 16. Consistent with the earlier observation of the importance of the chlorine atom on the quinoline ring for binding and cellular potency, analogue 25 is significantly less potent/inactive in the HTRF and HEK-Blue assays versus the chloro analogue 26. Of the different analogues shown in Table 2, the nitrile analogue 23 appeared particularly appealing for further testing and progression. Unfortunately, the in vitro metabolic stability of the compound in the mouse liver microsomal assay was less than desirable for advancement into an in vivo pharmacodynamic (PD) model. All of the compounds shown in Tables 1 and 2 (and in Tables 3–5 below) are very selective against CD40L (IC50 > 10 μM, data not shown).

    Having identified the key SAR drivers at the 4-position, we turned our attention to explore the SAR at the 6-position of the aminoquinoline moiety. A number of compounds were synthesized to explore the SAR in this region of the molecule—a representative set is shown in Table 3. Consistent with the X-ray co-crystal structure, a variety of substituents are tolerated at this position because this region of the ligand, particularly substituents coming off at the 2-position of the pyrimidine ring, is open to the solvent. However, as is apparent from Table 3, the cellular potencies of the compounds varied significantly although the binding potencies were within a few fold of each other. We decided to keep the pyrimidine moiety constant for subsequent SAR studies because it gave us an ideal combination of potency, hydrophilicity, and the ability to be used as a prodrug, in case of potential PK issues with tool compounds during the optimization process. In addition, we were able to synthesize a fluorescein probe for use in our HTRF assay (See Experimental Section for details) because the X-ray co-crystal structure of 16 suggested that the 2-position of the pyrimidine ring was open to the solvent.
    Next, we briefly examined SAR at positions 5, 7, and 8 of the quinoline scaffold, and this data are summarized in Table 4.
    As the table clearly suggests, chloro and fluoro substitutions at 5-, 7-, and 8-positions are tolerated from a binding perspective. However, the best functional potency, within this data set, is seen with the 7-fluoro analogue (35). The C-5 chloro analogue (32) is approximately 2–3 fold less potent in the binding and functional assays, compared to the corresponding des-chloro analogue 16. On the other hand, although the C-8 chloro analogue (34) is moderately potent in the binding assay, it is inactive in the HEK-Blue cellular assay. Consistent with this observation, the smaller C-8 fluorine analog (36) is active both in the binding and functional assays. As is clear from the table, the binding potency for the C-5 and C-8 chloro analogues (32 and 34) are similar; however, 32 is moderately potent in the functional assay, whereas the C-8 analogue 34 is completely inactive. One potential explanation for the binding and functional assay disconnect seen here, and with other analogues, could be that in the HTRF binding assay we are looking purely at the displacement of the probe molecule by the ligand. In this situation, some movement of residues within the TNFα binding pocket may accommodate the ligand (potentially the case for the C-8 chloro analogue) or the neighboring moieties in the ligand itself move slightly (potentially in the case of the C-5 chloro analogue, resulting in a slightly different orientation of the pyrimidine moiety at C-6). However, in the cellular assay, the ligand-bound TNFα trimer engages TNFR1, and the inhibition of the signaling process may also depend on the relative strength of the interaction between the binding partners (ligand-bound TNFα trimer and TNFR1).
    As a final exercise, we briefly examined naphthyridines as alternatives to the quinoline core and this SAR is summarized in Table 5.
    Of the three naphthyridines tested, the 1,5-naphthyridines (37–39) were the most potent in the HTRF assay. The improved potency seen with 1,5 naphthyridine analogue versus the 1,7- and 1,8-analogues (40 and 41 respectively) may be due to better π-stacking capability of this ring system with Tyr135C (vide supra).     Although the cellular potency of the 1,5-naphthyridine analogue 39 was approximately 2.5-fold lower than the corresponding quinoline analogue (16), the metabolic stability of the compound in the in vitro human or mice microsomal assay appeared promising (compare 39 versus 16 for human and 39 versus 23 and 35 for mouse). Therefore, our SAR efforts were focused on further improving potency, metabolic stability, and the CYP inhibition profile of this compound by incorporating key learnings from the aforementioned SAR studies, as represented in Figure 3.
    As outlined in Figure 3, we were very pleased to note that the proposed SAR changes to the 1,5-naphthyridine core were additive, leading to compound 42 with excellent cellular potency and metabolic stability in the mouse and human liver microsomal assays along with a significantly improved CYP inhibition profile. In addition, the compound was selective for TNFα compared to other TNFSF ligands such as CD40L, FasL, and LT beta (IC50, > 10 μM, data not shown). The compound was also tested against a panel of >150 kinases and was found to be inactive at the highest concentration tested.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, scaffold hopping and structure-based drug design (SBDD) approaches have led to the identification of a novel series of quinolines and 1,5-naphthyridines as potent inhibitors of the TNFα/TNFR interaction. To the best of our knowledge, this may be one of the very few reports describing low nanomolar small molecule inhibitors of TNFα, in a cellular setting. From a SAR perspective, a single atom/group change (H to Cl or Me) acting like an “on/off” switch for cellular activity is worth noting. In terms of the mechanism of action, X-ray co-crystallography and ultracentrifugation experiments provided insights on how the ligand induces structural changes to the TNF trimer, interfering with contact points and engagement with the receptor to modulate the TNF/TNFR signaling cascade. Finally, compound 42 was tested in a CAIA efficacy model, a preclinical model of rheumatoid arthritis, where it showed biologic-like efficacy, providing in vivo proof-of-principle for this mechanism from a small molecule perspective.

    HTRF Assay Protocol

    Compounds were received in DMSO at a top concentration of 10 mM (50 μM final concentration in the assay). Serial dilutions of 1:3 were prepared for 11 total concentrations per compound. A 2× concentration of anti-his antibody Tb-labeled (Cis Bio) was prepared in assay buffer. The 2× antibody stock (1 mL) was removed and added to column 1 of the Corning plate for a final assay concentration of 0.2 nM (stock concentration was 4034 nM). This was to be used as the assay background. To the remaining 2× antibody stock, a 2× His TNF concentration was added (in-house sources) for a final concentration of 1 nM.
    Antibody/antibody-enzyme (10 μL) was added to each assay plate by a Multidrop Combi (Thermo),covered to protect from light, and preincubated with the compound for 1 h at room temperature. Plates were moved by a Thermo robotic arm to the carousel for incubation. After the 1 h preincubation, the Thermo robotic arm transferred plates back to the Multidrop Combi.
