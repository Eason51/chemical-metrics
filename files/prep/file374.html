    Abstract

    Increased fructose consumption and its subsequent metabolism have been implicated in metabolic disorders such as nonalcoholic fatty liver disease and steatohepatitis (NAFLD/NASH) and insulin resistance. Ketohexokinase (KHK) converts fructose to fructose-1-phosphate (F1P) in the first step of the metabolic cascade. Herein we report the discovery of a first-in-class KHK inhibitor, PF-06835919 (8), currently in phase 2 clinical trials. The discovery of 8 was built upon our originally reported, fragment-derived lead 1 and the recognition of an alternative, rotated binding mode upon changing the ribose-pocket binding moiety from a pyrrolidinyl to an azetidinyl ring system. This new binding mode enabled efficient exploration of the vector directed at the Arg-108 residue, leading to the identification of highly potent 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane acetic acid-based KHK inhibitors by combined use of parallel medicinal chemistry and structure-based drug design.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The utilization of the simple sugar fructose as a common food additive and sweetener has increased in recent decades. As a result, fructose is now a common sweetening ingredient in many foods, usually in the form of high-fructose corn syrup (HFCS). Fructose metabolism, unlike its isomer glucose, is not subject to feedback regulation and excess fructose consumption promotes metabolic disease in humans.(1) Specifically, fructose intake has been associated with nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD),(2) insulin resistance,(3) and cardiovascular disease (CVD).(4) In support of the concept that fructose may promote deleterious metabolic effects, when dietary fructose was replaced with starch (a glucose polymer), steatosis and insulin resistance were reversed in human subjects.(5)
    Ketohexokinase (KHK) initiates the metabolic cascade by phosphorylation of the 1-hydroxyl position of fructose utilizing adenosine triphosphate (ATP) as a cofactor, generating fructose-1-phosphate (F1P) and adenosine diphosphate (ADP).(6) Two isoforms of KHK exist, KHK-A and KHK-C.(6b) KHK-C is the primary enzyme for fructose metabolism as it has both a lower KM and a higher Vmax than KHK-A.(7) Upon oral ingestion, fructose is primarily metabolized by the intestines, followed by the liver, pancreas, and kidney, all tissues expressing KHK-C.(7,8) Inhibition of KHK has been considered a promising therapeutic hypothesis for the treatment of metabolic diseases supported by studies in KHK-null mice, demonstrating protection from fructose induced hyperlipidemia, insulin resistance, obesity, and NAFLD.(7,9)
    To date, three structurally distinct small molecule KHK inhibitors have been reported in literature.(10) A previous report from Pfizer disclosed compound 1 discovered through a combination of fragment-based screening, structure-based drug design (SBDD) and parallel medicinal chemistry (PMC).(10c) Compound 1 demonstrated several positive attributes of a lead (Figure 1A), including low metabolic turnover in human liver microsomes (HLM), high aqueous solubility, and minimal off-target activity. The favorable pharmacokinetic profile of 1 in rats enabled preclinical in vivo pharmacology studies, which demonstrated robust target engagement as determined by a dose-dependent decrease in F1P formation in liver and kidney with concomitant increases in plasma fructose concentrations. As previously described, compound 1 was shown to bind at the ATP site of KHK with four notable interactions within this binding site (Figure 1B): (1) the (S)-3-methylpyrrolidin-3-ol group occupancy of the ATP-ribose pocket with the methyl group filling a small but crucial subpocket defined by Phe-260 (normally filled by the methylene of the ATP ribose side chain), (2) a hydrogen-bonding interaction between the nitrile group and a conserved water that forms a hydrogen-bond with the backbone NH of Phe-245 and the backbone CO of Cys-282, (3) the trifluoromethyl group filling the hydrophobic pocket defined by the Pro-246–247 residues, and (4) the (3S,4S)-pyrrolidine-3,4-diol hydrogen bonding interactions between the hydroxyls and Glu-227 and Asn-107, thus polar interactions at the solvent exposed edge opening of the ATP pocket. Our goal for a clinical candidate was to increase the potency significantly by taking full advantage of these elements of the binding pocket and possibly adding additional interactions, all while retaining the advantageous clearance and permeability properties of 1. Herein we report the discovery of a first-in-class clinical candidate PF-06835919 (8), a potent inhibitor of KHK demonstrating >40-fold improvement of potency over the lead (1).

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Our initial observation was that the (3S,4S)-pyrrolidine-3,4-diol (trans) substituent could be converted to the achiral cis analogue 2 without compromising potency or changing the binding mode. This change simplified subsequent analogue synthesis, thus 2 was used as a template for optimizing other changes in the molecule, the ribose binding element in particular. Limited examination to modify the trifluoromethyl group of 2 and its related system did not lead to potency improvement among tested (data not shown). Hence, the trifluoromethyl group was kept unchanged during the course of potency optimization reported here.
    Structurally diverse primary and secondary amine monomers were chosen for replacement of the (S)-3-methylpyrrolidin-3-ol, with emphasis on ring structures with varying methyl substituents using standard SNAr chemistry. Docking-based calculations along with estimated physicochemical properties of the target molecules were also factored into the choice of amines for coupling. This effort led to the rapid identification of 3, a (2S,3R)-2-methylazetidin-3-ol, with comparable potency relative to 2 (Figure 2A).
    Significantly, X-ray crystallographic analysis after soaking 3 into KHK crystals revealed a rotated binding mode relative to 2 (Figure 2B). The azetidinyl group of 3 was positioned deeper in the ribose pocket while maintaining the hydrophobic interaction of the methyl substituent in the Phe-260 subpocket. The hydroxyl group of the azetidine ring of 3 formed a hydrogen-bonding interaction with the backbone CO of Ala-226. The trifluoromethyl group was bound in the Pro-246–247 hydrophobic region and thus unchanged relative to the previous poses. Notably, the conserved water molecule was displaced by the rotated binding mode such that the nitrile of 3 bound directly to the backbone NH of Phe-245. In addition, the rotation placed the vector of the cis-dihydroxypyrrolidine substituent in line with the Arg-108 residue at the polar back portion of the ATP binding site normally occupied by the triphosphate side chain in KHK.
    We sought to exploit this vector in the direction of Arg-108 in an effort to improve potency, possibly utilizing a Coulombic interaction.(11) Additionally, a HydroSite(12) calculation predicted a high-energy virtual “water” molecule in this pocket (ΔG, ΔH, and −TΔS are 3.04, 0.64, and 2.4 kcal/mol, respectively) present within the hydrogen-bonding distance between Arg-108, a backbone carbonyl oxygen atom of Thr-253, and a backbone NH of Gly-257, respectively, suggesting the possibility of making energetically favorable interactions with an appropriately chosen side chain attachment in this region (Figure 2C).
    By employing previously described sequential SNAr reactions, a diverse array of primary and secondary amines possessing polar functional groups were rapidly examined through PMC after both calculated property and docking-based(13) prioritization. The initial exploration of this vector culminated in the identification of the pivotal compound 4 possessing a 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane ring system as one of the most potent neutral azetidine analogues among those tested from this PMC effort (Figure 3A).
    The crystal structure of 4 in human KHK (Figure 3B) showed that the terminal hydroxyl group on the newly introduced 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane ring system effectively reached proximally to Arg-108. This terminal hydroxyl group of 4 indirectly forms a hydrogen-bonding interaction with Arg-108 through a bound water, while within the hydrogen-bonding distance with the backbone carbonyl oxygen atom of Thr-253, respectively. Relative binding poses between the progenitor analogue 3 and 4 are otherwise largely comparable suggesting that the rest of the key protein–ligand interactions identified in 3 were maintained for 4. Taken together, the conformationally rigid 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane ring of 4 was considered as a more efficient spacer to reach the targeted buried area nearby Arg-108 relative to the monocyclic pyrrolidine-based ring system.
    With compound 4 in hand, further optimization of the terminal polar groups on the 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane ring system of 4 was targeted as the next design strategy to improve potency. Among the polar groups known to interact with the arginine residue, the carboxylic acid group was considered as one of the top candidates given its potential to form a strong ionic interaction with the guanidinium group of Arg-108.(14)
    With this in mind, the spacer length between the 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane ring and the terminal carboxylic acid group was varied to optimize the targeted interaction with Arg-108 (compounds 5 and 6, Table 1). Compound 6, with a one carbon spacer between the 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane ring and the terminal carboxylic acid group, demonstrated a significant potency improvement over 4 (>20-fold potency boost). It was apparent that the optimal spacer length between the terminal carboxylic acid group and the 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane unit was critical in order to achieve significant potency improvement (compounds 5 vs 6, Table 1). X-ray crystallographic analysis of the compound 6 bound in human KHK protein was instructive (Figure 4).

    The overall binding mode of 6 was determined to be comparable to that of the neutral azetidine analogue 4 except that additional polar interactions with the newly introduced carboxylic acid group of compound 6 were evident from the X-ray structure. Within the proximity of the carboxylic acid group of 6, one of the oxygen atoms of carboxylate group was well-positioned to interact with cationic guanidinium group of Arg-108. In addition, the backbone NH of Gly-255 and Gly-257, respectively, as well as a bound water nearby, were all within the hydrogen-bonding distance. Thus, a complex network of polar interactions through the carboxylic acid group of 6 is likely responsible for the significant potency improvement achieved by 6, and not solely a Coulombic interaction.
    The significant potency improvement demonstrated by 6, coupled with its favorable physicochemical property space (MW = 396, log D = 1, TPSA = 101), prompted us to profile compound 6 in depth as an advanced lead (Table 2). Because the potency of 6 approached the enzyme concentration used in the initial biochemical assay ([KHK] = 10 nM), a more sensitive biochemical assay was developed using the same substrate concentrations but with 10-fold less enzyme while monitoring the reaction over a longer period of time. Compound 6 showed KHK IC50 of 1 nM under this new assay format. Compound 6 demonstrated moderate cell permeability and exhibited low metabolic turnover in HLM supplemented with cytochrome P450 (CYP) cofactor NADPH. Incubation of 6 in human hepatocytes (HHEPs), however, resulted in relatively high turnover, indicating that 6 was subject to metabolism by a non-CYP enzyme(s). Follow-up metabolite identification studies in human and rat hepatocytes revealed that 6 was principally subject to a uridine glucuronosyl transferase (UGT) mediated glucuronidation on its secondary hydroxyl group (Supporting Information (SI), Figure S1). The carboxylic acid moiety was ruled out as the site of glucuronidation because inclusion of hydroxylamine in the incubation mixture did not result in the formation of the corresponding hydroxamic acid product, which is expected from the reaction of the amine nucleophile with the acyl glucuronide metabolite(15) (SI, Figures S1,S2).

Metabolite identification studies described above were instrumental in formulating the design strategy toward reducing glucuronidation (Figure 5). The pyrimidine core was considered as a bioisostere of the cyanopyridine group with lower log D.(17) The optimization effort within the scope of this design strategy is summarized in Table 3.     The pyrimidine analogue 7 showed significant improvement in metabolic stability in human hepatocytes, presumably due to the reduction of lipophilicity (ΔlogD (6 → 7) = −0.9). Against backdrops of decrease in KHK potency and permeability relative to 6, a significant improvement in clearance prompted us to shift the focus to the pyrimidine analogue 7 with the goal to increase permeability while maintaining low metabolic clearance in human hepatocytes. To this end, des-hydroxyazetidine analogue 8 was targeted with the expectation to improve permeability through reduction of hydrogen bond donor counts while concomitantly removing the site of glucuronidation in order to mitigate an increase in clearance. Compound 8 showed comparable KHK potency relative to 7. Gratifyingly, compound 8 maintained low metabolic turnover in human hepatocytes while significantly improving permeability. Upon the basis of improvements in clearance and permeability over 6, compound 8 was selected for further in vitro characterizations (Table 4).

    Selected in vitro characterization of 8 is summarized in Table 4. To evaluate potency of 8 under physiologically relevant substrate concentrations, the IC50 was measured at 2 mM ATP and 8 mM fructose. In this setting, the IC50 of 8 increased to 0.028 μM from 0.01 μM under substrate concentrations at Km, suggesting a degree of competition with at least one of the substrates (Table 4; Figure 6i). At physiological substrate concentrations, compound 8 showed a 6-fold higher IC50 value against human KHK-A relative to human KHK-C while inhibiting rat KHK with an IC50 of 0.21 μM. The higher IC50 value of compound 8 in KHK-A can be reasoned with a mutation of Leu-112 in KHK-C to Tyr-112 in KHK-A. Tyr-112 in KHK-A would likely attenuate the critical Arg-108 interaction with the carboxyl group of compound 8 through a hydrogen bond network (Arg-108 → Glu-29 → Lys-174 → Tyr-112). In addition, compound 8 was highly selective against other sugar kinases, showing >3000-fold selectivity for human KHK-C compared to human ribokinase, glucokinase, hexokinase-1, and hexokinase-2 (SI, Table S1). By using a relay method for low clearance compounds in human hepatocytes,(18) the apparent intrinsic clearance of 8 was determined to be 1.2 μL/min/million cells, suggestive of very low metabolic turnover. The thermodynamic solubility of the crystalline form of 8 was measured in simulated fasted intestinal fluid with bile salts (FaSSIF). Consistent with its amphoteric nature (acidic pKa = 4.4, basic pKa = 3.6), it showed high solubility in this media. Compound 8 showed low, but measurable fraction unbound in plasma across the species.
    Detailed biochemical characterization of 8 was further conducted to reveal its inhibition mechanism (Figure 6). The IC50 dependence of 8 on enzyme concentration indicates tight binding inhibition under these assay conditions (Figure 6ii). Varying concentrations of ATP, but not fructose, had a significant effect, and the Lineweaver–Burke plot (1/rate vs 1/[ATP]) reveals a mixed noncompetitive mechanism (Figure 6iii). While a purely noncompetitive binding modality displays equivalent affinities for the free enzyme and the enzyme–substrate complex, mixed-type noncompetitive inhibition describes cases where these affinities are different.(19) Despite the dual affinity of the inhibitor for both free and substrate-bound forms of the enzyme, compound 8 prefers binding to the free enzyme by 19-fold and essentially behaves like a competitive inhibitor of ATP (SI, Figure S3). Applying the correct mathematical model for mixed noncompetitive inhibition gave an inhibition constant (Ki) of 4.53 nM for 8 (SI, Figure S4). Despite tight binding inhibition of 8, there was no evidence of time-dependence, and rapid dilution conditions allowed measurement of a regain in activity which supports a reversible mechanism (Figure 6iv; SI, Figure S5 and Table S2). Taken together, compound 8 was determined to be a potent reversible inhibitor of recombinant human KHK-C through a mixed noncompetitive mechanism.
    The cocrystal structure of 8 in human KHK is shown in Figure 7. The binding mode of 8 was comparable to that of 6, retaining multiple polar interactions by the carboxylate group (Figure 7). Because of the change from the cyano pyridine core to the pyrimidine core (6 → 8), the replaced conserved water molecule seen in cocrystal structure of 1 in human KHK was observed in this binding mode with 8. Compound 8 makes a hydrogen-bonding interaction with a conserved water through the pyrimidine nitrogen, while a conserved water makes a hydrogen-bonding interaction with the backbone NH of Phe-245. A pairwise comparison (compound 7 → compound 6, Table 3) suggests replacing a conserved water molecule likely contributed to 14-fold improvement in potency (0.014 μM → 0.001 μM). However, this permutation is also associated with increase in lipophilicity (log D 0.1 → 1) and metabolic clearance (HHEP Clint,app < 2 → 26) in this matched molecular pair. From the optimization perspective, the value of replacing this conserved water molecule is likely chemo-type dependent, and it needs to be balanced with other design parameters beyond potency.
    On the basis of attractive in vitro disposition attributes of 8, the in vivo pharmacokinetic properties of 8 was determined in rats and dogs, the preclinical species for safety assessments in the program (Table 5). Following IV administration, 8 demonstrated low total plasma clearance (CLp) and low steady state distribution volume (Vdss), leading to moderate-to-long elimination half-lives (t1/2) in rats and dogs, respectively (Table 5). Moreover, 8 demonstrated negligible renal excretion as unchanged parent in both animal species. The corresponding oral bioavailability (F) of the crystalline form of 8 was high (>90%) in both species, consistent with a combination of high aqueous solubility, high passive permeability, and low metabolic clearance as shown in Table 4. With the robust in vivo pharmacokinetic profile demonstrated, compound 8 was evaluated for KHK inhibition in vivo by utilizing Sprague–Dawley rats as previously described (Figure 8).(10c) All procedures performed on these animals were in accordance with regulations and established guidelines and were reviewed and approved by the Pfizer Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. Sprague–Dawley rats were administered vehicle or increasing doses of compound 8 followed by iv administration of fructose. Two minutes after the administration of fructose, rats were euthanized and liver and kidney tissues were removed and snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen. F1P was quantified in liver and kidney homogenates by liquid chromatography/mass spectrometry (LC/MS) as previously described.(10c) F1P decreased dose dependently with measured ED50 values of 1.93 mg/kg in liver and 4.74 mg/kg in kidney (Figure 8). The observed dose-dependent decrease in hepatic and kidney F1P as depicted in Figure 8 indicates inhibition of liver and kidney KHK.

    Synthesis

    The chemistry to make the described pyridine analogues is shown in Scheme 1. Amines described in Schemes 2–4 were employed in sequential SNAr chemistry beginning with commercially available dichloropyridine 9. The first SNAr reaction proceeds at room temperature to install the 6-position substituent selectively. The second SNAr reaction proceeds at elevated temperatures to install the 2-position substituent leading to pyridines 2–4. For ester-containing intermediates, a hydrolysis reaction was used in step 3 to form pyridine acids 5 and 6.
    The synthesis of azetidine 13 is shown in Scheme 3. Following modifications of literature conditions,(20,21) (2E)-buten-1-ol 18 was treated with bromine to from dibromide 19 which was then reacted with potassium hydroxide to form epibromohydrin 20. Treatment with benzhydryl amine led to displacement of the bromide and concomitant epoxide opening to form protected azetidine rac-21, which could be separated via SFC on a chiral column or taken forward to hydrogenolysis to remove the benzhydryl protecting group and provide rac-13. Treatment of rac-21 with (R)-camphor sulfonic acid (CSA) under classical chiral resolution conditions led to the isolation of the single enantiomer of 21 as a salt with (R)-CSA. Hydrogenolysis of the protecting group then led to the isolation of the 13/(R)-CSA salt that could be used directly in the SNAr chemistry.
    The synthesis of amino ethyl esters 16 and 26 are shown in Scheme 4. Starting from benzyl protected amino alcohol 22,(22) activation of the alcohol as the mesylate and SN2 displacement with sodium cyanide led to formation of nitrile 23. Acid-mediated solvolysis led to formation of the ester and hydrogenolysis of the benzyl protecting group led to amino ester 16. Methyl ester 26 was employed in larger scale syntheses of 8 because it could be stored as a stable solid intermediate, in contrast to ethyl ester 16, which was an oil that partially decomposed upon long-term storage. Improvements were also made during homologation step: (1) conversion of the alcohol to the chloride with thionyl chloride and formation of the HCl salt led to a more stable intermediate than the analogous mesylate and (2) after free-basing the salt, the chloride was displaced with sodium cyanide and the HCl salt of 23 was formed. Solvolysis with methanol rather than ethanol also led to a solid amino ester HCl salt rather than an oil. Benzyl deprotection then led to amino methyl ester hydrochloride 26 that was used directly in the SNAr chemistry.
    Pyrimidine 7 was synthesized in an analogous manner to 6, but the chemistry began with commercially available dichloropyrimidine 24 (Scheme 5). Pyrimidine 8 was scaled up under similar conditions, but the first SNAr reaction was conducted with methyl aminoester 26 and cryogenic temperatures were used to maximize selectivity. After a second SNAr with (S)-methylazetidine (17)/(R)-CSA salt,(23) followed by saponification, 8 was recrystallized from MTBE–heptane.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, highly potent and selective acidic KHK inhibitors were identified starting from the pyrrolidine-based lead 1. The initial phase of lead optimization focused on potency improvement. Early realization of rotations in the binding mode driven by the azetidine-based inhibitors was a critical step toward targeting Arg-108 and a simulated high-energy water proximal to Arg-108. Subsequent optimization through combination of PMC and the ionization-switch through SBDD led to the identification of highly potent KHK inhibitors bearing 3-azabicyclo[3.1.0]hexane acetic acid group. Final optimization efforts toward striking the balance of potency, glucuronidation metabolism, and cell permeability profile were achieved by strategic removal of the hydroxyl group (the major site of the glucuronidation metabolism) and the use of the pyrimidyl central core, culminating in the discovery of 8 (PF-06835919) as a clinical candidate. To our knowledge, PF-06835919 represents a first-in-class small molecule KHK inhibitor to have entered clinical trials. PF-06835919 is currently being evaluated in phase 2 clinical trials for the treatment of NAFLD/NASH. Detailed pharmacological characterizations of PF-06835919 will be reported in due course.
