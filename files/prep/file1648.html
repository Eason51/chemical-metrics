    Abstract

    The recently disclosed next generation of reversible, selective, and potent MetAP-2 inhibitors introduced a cyclic tartronic diamide scaffold. However, the lead compound 1a suffered from enterohepatic circulation, preventing further development. Nevertheless, 1a served as a starting point for further optimization. Maintaining potent antiproliferation activity, while improving other compound properties, enabled the generation of an attractive array of new MetAP-2 inhibitors. The most promising derivatives were identified by a multiparameter analysis of the compound properties. Essential for the efficient selection of candidates with in vivo activity was the identification of molecules with a long residence time on the target protein, high permeability, and low efflux ratio not only in Caco-2 but also in the MDR-MDCK cell line. Orally bioavailable, potent, and reversible MetAP-2 inhibitors impede the growth of primary endothelial cells and demonstrated antitumoral activity in mouse models. This assessment led to the nomination of the clinical development compound M8891, which is currently in phase I clinical testing in oncology patients.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Removal of N-terminal methionine by methionine aminopeptidases (MetAP) is a crucial step in the maturation of proteins during protein biosynthesis. MetAP-2, one of the two cytoplasmic methionine aminopeptidases expressed in mammalian cells, has been identified as a target of the highly potent and irreversible inhibitor fumagillin, to which antiangiogenic and antitumoral properties have been ascribed.(1−3) These and other findings rationalize the development of MetAP-2 inhibitors for cancer therapy.(4) Fumagillin derivatives like CKD-732, PPI-2458, and TNP-470 have been evaluated in oncology clinical trials, but development was discontinued likely due to unfavorable pharmacokinetics and adverse events attributed to the inherent reactivity of these molecules.(5) In addition to the fumagillin class of MetAP-2 inhibitors, structurally divergent MetAP-2 inhibitors have also been described including the substrate-like bestatin class,(6) modified marine natural products like the bengamide class,(7) anthranilic acid sulfonamide analogues,(8) triazole(9) or purine(10) derivatives, and others. Structural investigations of this chemical matter revealed divergent interaction patterns.(11) Recently, a new binding motif was introduced, which includes a key cyclic hydroxy-malonic acid (tartronic)-diamide scaffold.(12) Initial structure-based SAR exploration and compound optimization led to 1a. This lead showed in vivo tumor growth inhibition in a human glioblastoma efficacy model (U87-MG; T/C = 45% at 10 mg/kg per os daily dosing). In-depth characterization and the multiparameter optimization are described herein culminated in the identification of the clinical candidate M8891.

    Key MetAP-2 Interactions of Lead Compound 1a

    Critical polar and lipophilic interactions between 1a and the MetAP-2 protein are well-understood due to the evaluation of the molecule in the protein crystal structure (Figure 1).
    The depiction in Figure 1A shows that the cyclic tartronic diamide scaffold coordinates to two manganese ions in a chelating manner. The tertiary alcoholic oxygen is 2.22 Å from the more buried Mn2+ ion and 2.25 Å from the Mn2+ ion closer to the pocket entrance. The lactam oxygen interacts with the latter Mn2+ ion and the imidazoles of His331 and His339 (2.75 Å, 3.12 Å, and 2.72 Å, respectively). The amide moiety makes two interactions, as the amide oxygen coordinates with the buried Mn2+ ion (2.37 Å), while the amide NH interacts with the imidazole of His231 (3.11 Å). His231 imidazole also makes lipophilic contacts to the benzylamide residue (4.15 Å) and the tetrahydroquinolinone bound to the lactam nitrogen (3.72 Å). The tetrahydroquinolinone residue is rotated out of the pyrrolidinone plane by 53.3° and makes several van der Waals (vdW) interactions at the top of the pocket (Leu447, 3.62 Å; His231, 3.72 Å; Leu328, 3.74 Å) and a hydrogen bond with the lactam-NH and Asn329 side chain (3.06 Å) near the bottom. The halogenated benzyl moiety occupies the substrate recognition pocket and is involved in a couple of vdW contacts with MetAP-2. The fluorine points upward to the side chain of Ala414 (3.24 Å), while the chlorine approaches Ile338 (3.68 Å). In addition, the chlorine is involved in a reverse cation−π interaction: the negatively polarized crown around the σ-hole of the chlorine atom is stacking on the putatively charged imidazole moiety of His339 (3.52 Å and 3.76 Å., not shown in Figure 1). This structural basis was considered as a suitable starting point to investigate different halogen substitution patterns on the benzylic residue and their effect on potency.

    Occupancy of the Substrate Recognition Pocket Effects HUVEC Proliferation Inhibition

    The introduction of halogen substituents can provide additional and specific bonding interactions with protein substructures, impacting the activity of ligands significantly.(13) A halogen scanning approach was applied to study the effect of double and triple halogen substitution of the benzyl moiety buried in the substrate recognition pocket (Table 1).
    The synthesized derivatives were tested in enzymatic and cellular assays. Enzyme assay evaluation revealed minimal potency differences. Unexpectedly, however, a broad potency range was observed in the HUVEC proliferation assay. Seven of the compounds, 1a–1d and 1h–1j, each inhibit the endothelial cell proliferation with sub-200 nM IC50 values, whereas all other compounds have a cellular activity around or above 500 nM. Attempts to delineate a structure–activity relationship among the compounds reveals that an ortho-chlorine moiety, like in compounds 1e and 1f, is not tolerated. Derivative 1g is characterized by double ortho-fluorine substitution, which is also not accepted. Fluorine, on the other hand, is tolerated in the ortho position, as analogues 1h–1j prove, but only in the presence of a meta-substituted halogen (see 1g). Introduction of a para-substituted fluorine, as in compounds 1k and 1l, is modestly tolerated, perhaps due to fluorine-derived perturbations in the edge-to-face arrangement of the benzyl moieties of the MetAP-2 inhibitors and Tyr444 of the protein. Overall, these efforts reveal that the cellular activity of 1a could not be increased by lipophilic or halogen-bonding interactions.

    Investigation of the H-Bond Interaction of MetAP-2 Inhibitors to Asn329

    The previously described hydrogen bond between 1a lactam NH to Asn329 (Figure 1A) was considered to be essential for activity. Further optimization of the H-bond interaction was envisioned via increasing the acidity of the NH proton. The calculated pKa of the unsubstituted 1,2,3,4-tetrahydroquinolin-2-one is 13.9. The introduction of fluorine into the 8-position of the tetrahydroquinolinone moiety (1m) reduces the calculated pKa to 11.9, but negatively impacts cell proliferation (1m is 60× less potent than 1a). Directly removing the proton available for hydrogen bonding via N-methylation led to compound 1n. The modest cellular activity of 1n challenges the relevance of this polar interaction and provides the impetus for further evaluating the importance of the hydrogen bond to Asn329 with an array of different hydrogen donor functions.     Methionine Aminopeptidase Selectivity

    Selective inhibition of MetAP-2 over MetAP-1 is a relevant design parameter as complete inhibition of N-terminal methionine cleavage is considered to be too toxic.(14,15) As such, a MetAP-1 enzymatic assay was established. All derivatives tested, including 1a and 16, did not inhibit MetAP-1 (IC50 > 10 μM, data not shown), suggesting that MetAP-2 selectivity is a class-specific property for this series of compounds.

    A MetAP-2 Binding Assay Based on Fluorescence Cross-Correlation Analysis Confirms High-Affinity Inhibitors

    Target binding affinity and residence times are important information for compound characterization and for the rational selection of promising candidates. No suitable conditions for the immobilization of functional MetAP-2 protein to allow for surface plasmon resonance (SPR) measurements could be found. Fluorescence cross-correlation spectroscopy (FCCS) served as an alternative method to analyze the affinity and residence time of the compounds in cell lysates. The FCCS binding assay was established,(16) using a GFP-MetAP-2 fusion protein expressed in HEK293 cells and a fluorescently labeled derivative, 19-Dy647, as a tracer for the compound binding side at the target (Supporting Information).     The equilibrium FCCS assay confirmed the high affinity of the molecules to the target protein, well below the apparent assay limit of the enzyme assay at approximately 50 nM.     FCS-Based Target Occupancy Assays Discriminate MetAP-2 Inhibitors in Cells

    Target occupancy measurements are critical to evaluate and confirm the binding of candidates to the endogenous target in a cellular environment and were performed using intact A549 cells as a disease-relevant model system. The absence of any significant background binding of the tracer to lysates from cells lacking MetAP-2 overexpression enabled the investigation of the binding to endogenous MetAP-2. The specificity of the signal was confirmed in a cell lysate, where the MetAP-2 transcript was degraded using a target-specific siRNA pool (siPOOL).(18) The absence of the protein was confirmed by Western blot (Supporting Information). Now, the binding was analyzed by fluorescence correlation spectroscopy (FCS), following the increased diffusion time of the bound compared to the unbound tracer. In order to assess the target occupancy, cells were incubated with different concentrations of compounds or DMSO for 4 h (Figure 2). After the cells were washed and after a quick lysis step, the unbound fraction of the endogenous MetAP-2 was probed with the tracer 19-Dy647.
    The target engagement of all compounds was confirmed in this assay. Significant occupancies of 50% or more were detected at 30 nM or higher compound concentrations. The lowest dose required for full occupancy was determined for 1a, followed by 11 and 16. For 12, significant occupancy was only found for 300 nM or higher concentrations. Interestingly, this lower occupancy is consistent with a shorter target residence time of this inhibitor. MetAP-2 occupancy of 16 in HCT116 cells in a pulse-chase experiment is described in the Supporting Information.

    Pharmacokinetic Characterization of Interesting MetAP-2 Inhibitors

    Biochemical, biophysical, and cellular data qualified the lead compound 1a as an attractive candidate for extended PK analysis (Figure 3).
    While the compound had a favorable PK in rats (Figure 3, red), further profiling in nonrodent species revealed the reappearance of the drug in the central circulation up to 72 h after administration, likely associated with an enterohepatic circulation (EHC) in these species (Figure 3, dog (black), monkey (blue)). This characteristic was considered as suboptimal for an accurate human PK and dose prediction as well as for controlling exposure in the clinical setting. In line with the anticipated EHC, 1a also showed a relatively great extent of unchanged drug in feces after iv administration in both rodent species (mouse data given in Table 4), indicative of active secretion of the drug, together with a subsequent reabsorption. It was found that the extent of active biliary secretion correlated nicely with the efflux ratio observed in Caco-2 permeability tests (Table 4), and therefore, the latter was considered as an essential parameter for further evaluation. In line with the careful analysis of Caco-2 permeability, results from an iv PK study in P-gp knockout mice show that approximately 50% of the overall clearance of 1a is mediated by active secretion via P-gp (data not shown). These drawbacks for 1a required in-depth analysis of additional putative development candidates (Table 4).

    Moreover, the optimization approach to get rid of the EHC apparently was successful because systemic elimination of 16 in the two higher nonclinical species, dog and monkey, followed linear kinetics (Figure 4) and showed no transient increases at later time points as it was observed for 1a (Figure 3).

    Permeability Investigations to Gauge CNS Availability

    The in vivo efficacy of 1a had been demonstrated in a glioblastoma model implanted subcutaneously in immunodeficient mice.(12) However, efficacy in a glioblastoma model in situ required further efforts to identify brain penetrant molecules. The MDR-MDCK cell monolayer was established as an in vitro model to classify compounds into CNS-positive or CNS-negative based on permeability coefficients (Papp, a > b). Accordingly, triaging was further tuned by permeability and efflux investigations in the MDR-MDCK cell line. Based on published data, compounds showing Papp (a > b) > 3 × 10–6 cm/s are classified as compounds with a high brain uptake potential, whereas compounds with Papp (a > b) < 1 × 10–6 cm/s are almost unable to penetrate the blood–brain barrier (BBB).(22) According to this classification, the aza-indole matched pair, 11 and 12, was considered to exhibit a low potential for significant brain uptake because they showed only moderate permeability but high efflux ratios [(11) a > b, 3.34; ER 17; (12) a > b, 1.28, ER 41]. In contrast, the indole derivative 16 displayed high permeability (a > b: 15) with little efflux (ER: 4), suggesting the potential for high brain permeability for this compound. Subsequent mice PK studies reveal that 16 is able to pass the blood–brain barrier, with a kp,uu ratio of ∼0.6 (data not shown).

    In Vivo Investigations of Development Candidates

    The results of the biophysical, enzymatic, cellular, and PK analyses supported the decision to evaluate 11, 12, and 16 in in vivo efficacy studies using xenograft models. As indicated before, glioblastoma is an important indication, and thus, the human U87 MG model was used as a screening model. The highly permeable compound, 16, with a long residence time was compared with the medium residence time derivative, 11 (Figure 5).
    Both compounds, 11 and 16, exhibited strong tumor growth inhibition when dosed with 20 mg/kg every day. Improved efficacy was achieved with 16 (T/C value of 19%), while 11 only showed a T/C value of 45%. Both treatments were well-tolerated, and no body weight loss was observed during the treatment period with either compound (data not shown). The in vivo efficacy of 16 in this glioblastoma xenograft model appeared to be at least as good as 1a (T/C = 45% at 20 mg/kg),(12) considering the variability of such in vivo models.
    In addition to this glioblastoma model, the human renal Caki-1 tumor model was also used to investigate the tumor growth inhibition potential. In this approach, 16 was compared with 12, which has a 4-fold higher affinity to MetAP-2 but a 3-fold shorter residence time (Figure 6).
    Both compounds showed strong tumor growth inhibition with an administered dose of 25 mg/kg bidaily. A slightly better efficacy was achieved with 16 (T/C value of 22%), while 12 resulted in a T/C value of 39%. Both treatments were tolerated well during the treatment period of 36 days (tumor volumes of the individual animals and body weight change in Supporting Information).
    The comprehensive analysis of different attractive derivatives led to the nomination of 16 as clinical candidate M8891.

    Asymmetric Synthesis of Development Compound M8891

    The nomination of M8891 for clinical development required a reanalysis of the synthetic route, as the requisite chiral separation after racemic synthesis reduced potential yields by 50%. The asymmetric synthesis of M8891 was considered to be the most attractive approach. The asymmetric oxidation of amide-Cα is described in the literature, and accordingly, different protocols were investigated.(23) Davis and co-workers had identified oxaziridines as electrophilic oxidants for the hydroxylation of enolates,(24,25) including chiral camphorsulfonyl oxaziridine (Figure 7A), which has been employed to a greater extent in the synthesis of several natural products.(26) The implementation of this oxidant required an extensive validation, and the final scheme adopted is depicted below (Scheme 3).
    The first two synthetic steps are comparable to the aforementioned synthetic route discussed in Scheme 2. 1-(Phenylsulfonyl)-1H-indol-5-amine was reacted with cyclopropyl-Meldrum’s acid in an acetonitrile/DMF mixture to give 20. This intermediate was amidated in the presence of T3P to 21. Deprotonation with NaHMDS between −70 to −55 °C and oxidation with Davis oxaziridine at −70 °C gave 22 in 76% yield. The deprotection of the benzene sulphonyl group was carried out using NaOH in a THF/EtOH mixture and delivered M8891 in an 87% yield. Final crystallization led to the isolation of the product in >99.8% enantiomeric excess (ee). The observed chirality during the oxidation with Davis oxaziridine might be rationalized by a preferred conformation of the enolate substrate and the facial selectivity of the attacking oxaziridine (Figure 7).
    The preference for the si side attack is triggered by the different substitution patterns of the nitrogen of the M8891 precursor. The steric demand of the arylated, 3-fold substituted lactam nitrogen is higher than for the benzylated 2-fold substituted amide nitrogen. The steric clash between chlorine of the Davis reagent and the lactam nitrogen substituents disfavor re side attack, and accommodation of the “Davis chlorine” close to the amide nitrogen proton is preferred. This is also in line with the observation that hardly any enantiomeric excess was obtained when oxidizing the M8891-precursor enolate with the des-Cl oxaziridine analogue (results not shown). It was also possible to obtain the (R)-enantiomer of M8891 in >80% ee when using the enantiomeric (−)-oxaziridine. Besides this substrate-controlled asymmetric synthesis, alternative catalytic oxidations are currently considered. Organic catalysis based on chiral phosphoric acids(27) as well as Lewis-acid-catalyzed mechanisms(28) are investigated to optimize the production of the development compound even further.

    Biological Assays

    Biochemical Activity Testing of MetAP-2

    MetAP-2 activity was determined by an enzyme-coupled assay using the tripeptide Met-Ala-Ser (MAS) as a substrate and recombinant human MetAP-2 (His-Tev- MetAP-2, prepared at Merck). The released methionine is converted by l-amino acid oxidase (AAO) to oxidized methionine, and hydrogen peroxide is released. In the second step, horse radish peroxidase catalyzes the oxidation of the leuko dye dianisidine to oxidized dianisidine using hydrogen peroxide as a cosubstrate. The produced oxidized dianisidine was detected photometrically as an increase in absorbance at 450 nm. MetAP-2 activity was determined in the kinetic measurement mode. The release of one molecule methionine corresponds to the production of one molecule oxidized dianisidine. The MetAP-2 enzymatic activity directly corresponds to the increase in absorbance per time. In detail, the assay was performed in 384-well microtiter plate (Greiner 78110 MTP, transparent) in a total reaction volume of 50 μL at 22 °C; 0.35 μg of N-terminal His tag human rec MetAP-2 (prepared in-house, AA 2-478, final concentration (fc) 123 nM), 1 unit horse radish Peroxidase (Roche, Mannheim), 0.02 unit l-amino acid oxidase (Merck, Darmstadt), and 0.6 mM dianisidine (Merck, Darmstadt, dissolved in 50 mM HCl, 10% DMSO) were incubated in the absence or presence of the test compound (10 dilution concentrations) in 100 mM Hepes, 50 mM NaCl, and 50 μM MnCl2 at pH 7.0 for 15 min at 22 °C. The reaction was started by the addition of 500 μM (fc) MAS peptide (Merck, Darmstadt). After mixing, the first absorbance measurement was performed on an Envision multimode reader (Perkin–Elmer, Waltham) at a wavelength of 450 nm. The reaction was incubated at 22 °C for an additional 45 min, and the second absorbance measurement was performed. The increase of absorbance per time was determined. The control value used was the inhibitor-free reaction with 0.5% DMSO (fc). To define the pharmacological zero value, Fumagillin (Merck, Darmstadt) at a final concentration of 5 μM was used. The inhibitory values (IC50) were determined using the program Assay Analyzer from GeneData (Basel, Switzerland).

    HUVEC Proliferation Assay

    Proliferation of HUVEC primary endothelial cells was used as a cell-based assay.(29) Applying the CyQUANT Direct Cell Proliferation Assay (Invitrogen C35011), which is based on a cell-permeant fluorescent DNA-binding dye, DNA content is used as a direct measure for cell number. For the assay, 500 cells/well were seeded into black 384-well culture plates with a clear bottom in 70 μL of a culture medium and incubated for 6 h at 37 °C and 5% CO2. Ten μL of prediluted test compounds were added and cells incubated for 3 days at 37 °C and 5% CO2 before measurement of DNA content. Transfected cells were investigated microscopically or harvested 24 h after the transfection. During the harvest, the cells were washed twice with PBS, pelleted for 5 min at 1100g, and then frozen at −80 °C.

    Labeling of the Tracer Molecule

    Tool compound 19 was dissolved in DMSO and labeled via the reactive amino group to Dy647-Peg4 (Dyomics) utilizing a reactive NHS ester group. Labeling has been carried out in 100% DMSO overnight at room temperature on a head over tail incubator (Hybridization Oven 6/12 V UniEquip, Martinsried, Germany). The reaction was run under light protection conditions. The labeled compound was purified on a C18 10 μ reverse phase column. The ACN/H2O gradient was optimized, a gradient from 20% to 80% ACN, allowing separation of unlabeled and labeled compounds. The elution fractions containing a purified labeled compound were collected and predried in a vacuum concentrator and lyophilized overnight. The product was dissolved in DMSO and analyzed for concentration by spectrophotometry.

    Steady-State (Equilibrium) FCCS

    FCCS measurements (typically with a sample volume of 20 μL) were performed either with a ConfoCor2 FCS unit connected to an Axiovert 100 M equipped with a C-Apochromat 40× water immersion lens, NA 1.2 (Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany) or with an Insight plate reader (Evotec Technologies, Hamburg, Germany) fitted with a U-Apo300 40× water immersion lens, NA 1.15 (Olympus, Germany). Both systems used a 633 helium–neon laser for excitation of Cy5, Atto647, or Dy647 dyes and the 488 nm laser line of an argon–ion laser to excite GFP or Atto488. At the ConfoCor2 instrument, the emitted fluorescent light was directed over an HFT 488/633 and an NFT 610 beam splitter to the Avalanche Photo Diodes (APD). Fluorescence in the green channel was detected using a BP500–550 band-pass filter, fluorescence in the red channel with a LP650. At the Insight instrument, the emitted light was split into two APD channels set with a HQ 535/50 band-pass filter for green fluorescence and 670DF40 band-pass filter for red fluorescence. Data analysis and the fit were carried out either in the ConfoCor2 software or with FCS+plus Analyze (Evotec technologies software). For calculating the target protein level and the equilibrium dissociation constant for a labeled ligand (KD) from the saturation binding data, we used a single-site binding model as described before.(16)

    Competition Experiments

    IC50 values were determined from inhibition binding data by using a previously described four-parameter logistic function.(16) The equilibrium dissociation constant for an inhibitor (inhibition constant KI) was calculated by using the Cheng–Prusoff equation.(17)

    FCCS in Kinetic Mode

    Assuming a simple one binding site model for the reversible interaction between receptor and ligand and only a negligible change in concentration of free ligand during the binding of fluorescent probe to its cognate receptor, the kinetics for the receptor interaction with a fluorescent probe was analyzed as described before.(16)

    Competition Kinetics between a Fluorescent Probe and Unlabeled Ligand

    The kinetics parameters of unlabeled ligands were calculated based on the theoretical method developed by Motulsky and Mahan.(30) Following this method, a fluorescent probe with known binding kinetics was simultaneously coincubated with an unlabeled ligand in a competitive binding assay. The competitive binding kinetics of the fluorescent probe is dependent on both the concentration and kinetic parameters of the unlabeled competitor, and therefore, the experimentally derived rate of specific ligand binding can be fitted to provide the association and dissociation rates of the unlabeled ligand.(16)

      The kinetics of competitive radioligand binding predicted by the law of mass action
      Motulsky H J; Mahan L C
      Molecular pharmacology (1984), 25 (1), 1-9 ISSN:0026-895X.
      Although equilibrium competitive radioligand binding studies are often used to characterize hormone and neurotransmitter receptors, the kinetics of such experiments have not been extensively explored. The interactions of the radioligand and competitor with the receptors can be described by two differential equations which can be solved to yield a single equation describing the binding of the radioligand as a function of time. This equation has several applications: First, it can be used to simulate competitive binding reactions under defined conditions. Second, fitting experimental data to this equation allows one to determine the association and dissociation rate constants of the competing ligand, parameters that cannot be derived from equilibrium experiments. Furthermore, this method can be used to determine the KI of the competing drug from data acquired before equilibrium is reached. Third, mathematical analysis of the binding equation allowed us to answer two specific questions regarding the kinetics of competitive radioligand binding: how long such an incubation takes to equilibrate, and how the IC50 varies over time. The answers to these questions depended, to a large extent, on the relative values of the dissociation rate constants of the radioligand and competitor, which can be determined as noted above. When the competitor dissociates from the receptors more rapidly than the radioligand, the IC50 first decreases and then increases, but never has a value less than the KI. At low radioligand concentrations, equilibrium is reached in the same amount of time required of the radioligand to dissociate completely from the receptors as determined in an "off-rate experiment." At higher concentrations of radioligand this time is halved. When the competitor dissociates from the receptor more slowly than does the radioligand, then the time required to equilibrate depends only on the dissociation rate constant of the competitor, and the IC50 decreases over time.

        The kinetics of competitive radioligand binding predicted by the law of mass action
        Motulsky H J; Mahan L C
        Molecular pharmacology (1984), 25 (1), 1-9 ISSN:0026-895X.
        Although equilibrium competitive radioligand binding studies are often used to characterize hormone and neurotransmitter receptors, the kinetics of such experiments have not been extensively explored. The interactions of the radioligand and competitor with the receptors can be described by two differential equations which can be solved to yield a single equation describing the binding of the radioligand as a function of time. This equation has several applications: First, it can be used to simulate competitive binding reactions under defined conditions. Second, fitting experimental data to this equation allows one to determine the association and dissociation rate constants of the competing ligand, parameters that cannot be derived from equilibrium experiments. Furthermore, this method can be used to determine the KI of the competing drug from data acquired before equilibrium is reached. Third, mathematical analysis of the binding equation allowed us to answer two specific questions regarding the kinetics of competitive radioligand binding: how long such an incubation takes to equilibrate, and how the IC50 varies over time. The answers to these questions depended, to a large extent, on the relative values of the dissociation rate constants of the radioligand and competitor, which can be determined as noted above. When the competitor dissociates from the receptors more rapidly than the radioligand, the IC50 first decreases and then increases, but never has a value less than the KI. At low radioligand concentrations, equilibrium is reached in the same amount of time required of the radioligand to dissociate completely from the receptors as determined in an "off-rate experiment." At higher concentrations of radioligand this time is halved. When the competitor dissociates from the receptor more slowly than does the radioligand, then the time required to equilibrate depends only on the dissociation rate constant of the competitor, and the IC50 decreases over time.
