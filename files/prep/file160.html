    Abstract

    Nicotinamide phosphoribosyltransferase (Nampt) is a promising anticancer target. Virtual screening identified a thiourea analogue, compound 5, as a novel highly potent Nampt inhibitor. Guided by the cocrystal structure of 5, SAR exploration revealed that the corresponding urea compound 7 exhibited similar potency with an improved solubility profile. These studies also indicated that a 3-pyridyl group was the preferred substituent at one inhibitor terminus and also identified a urea moiety as the optimal linker to the remainder of the inhibitor structure. Further SAR optimization of the other inhibitor terminus ultimately yielded compound 50 as a urea-containing Nampt inhibitor which exhibited excellent biochemical and cellular potency (enzyme IC50 = 0.007 μM; A2780 IC50 = 0.032 μM). Compound 50 also showed excellent in vivo antitumor efficacy when dosed orally in an A2780 ovarian tumor xenograft model (TGI of 97% was observed on day 17).

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Nicotinamide phosphoribosyltransferase (Nampt, NAMPTase, PBEF, Visfatin, EC 2.4.2.12) catalyzes the condensation of nicotinamide (NAM) with 5-phosphoribosyl-1-pyrophosphate (PRPP) to form nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN), an intermediate in the biosynthesis of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD). In mammalian cells, there are multiple pathways for NAD biosynthesis: a de novo pathway using tryptophan (Trp) as the precursor and three pathways using exogenous vitamin sources as the precursors: NAM, nicotinic acid (NA), and nicotinamide riboside (NR). Nampt is the rate-limiting enzyme in the NAD biosynthetic pathway using NAM. This NAM-dependent pathway is used in cells to recycle the NAM produced by NAD-consuming enzymes such as poly-ADP-ribose polymerases (PARPs) and sirtuins (Figures 1 and 2).(1) Tumors have elevated PARP activity and thereby consume NAD at a higher rate than normal tissues and are thus more dependent upon Nampt to maintain required NAD levels.(2) NAD is an essential cofactor in multiple energy producing processes such as mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, β oxidation, glycolysis, and the citric acid cycle. Cancer cells have an increased demand for ATP and thus are more sensitive to perturbations of these processes. Rapidly proliferating cancers also have additional biosynthetic requirements that require NAD as a cofactor.(3) In addition, NAD is essential in maintaining the reductive environment that protects cells from reactive oxygen species (ROS), which are elevated in cancers.(4) The combination of increased NAD consumption and dependence upon NAD-driven biosynthetic and cellular redox homeostatic processes may make many cancer cells highly dependent on Nampt activity for proliferation and/or survival. Nampt thus represents an attractive target for the development of new cancer therapies.
    At the outset of our Nampt program, only three classes of Nampt inhibitors were known in the literature. Fujisawa/TopoTarget’s 1(5) (APO866) and Leo/Gemin-X/Cephalon/Teva’s 2(6) (GMX1778) were the first two published Nampt inhibitors. 1 is currently in phase 2 clinical trials against cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (CTCL), while 2 is in phase 1/2 trials for metastatic melanoma. Triazole-containing compounds were also reported to be potent Nampt inhibitors,(7) and a representative example, compound 3, exhibited an SH-SY5Y cytotoxicity IC50 value of 93 nM. More recently, Myrexis,(8) Sanofi-Aventis,(9) and Abbott(10) disclosed additional classes of potent Nampt inhibitors. For example, compound 4 from Myrexis showed a Nampt enzyme IC50 of 0.5 nM and HCT116 cytotoxicity of 3.0 nM (Figure 3). In this paper, we report the identification of highly potent urea-containing small-molecule inhibitors of Nampt, which were designed using the known crystal structure of the enzyme with an inhibitor present. During the course of this work, Myrexis and Sanofi independently reported their own related urea-containing Nampt inhibitors.(8a, 9)

    Structure-Based Identification of a New Chemical Scaffold to Target Nampt

    Nampt acts both as a phosphoribosyltransferase and as an ATPase, with phosphorylation of His247 increasing the enzyme’s catalytic activity in a stoichiometric manner.(11) Structurally, Nampt functions as a homodimer, with two identical catalytic sites located at opposite ends of the dimer interface.(11) The binding site of ATP overlaps with the PRPP and NAM binding sites. All three sites combine to form a protein surface of >1000 Å2, which represents a large area that a potential competitive inhibitor could target. The NAM binding site lies at the entrance to a tunnel through the protein that further extends along the dimer interface to a funnel shaped opening. 1 occupies this tunnel, with the piperidyl moiety overlapping with the NAM binding site(14) (Figure 4).
    To pinpoint locations likely to be druggable within these regions, we utilized computational solvent mapping(12) techniques employing CS-Map(13) software. The consensus sites(13) in the Nampt binding region were used to define six pharmacophore points (A–F, Figure 5) with A and B–C ranking significantly higher than the other three (see Supporting Information for details).
    Three pharmacophore models were then created by separately combining the ABC points with one of lower rank (D–F). The resulting models (ABCD, EABC, and FABC) were used to virtually screen the eMolecules database,(16) resulting in a set of 228 compounds which were subsequently purchased and tested in our biochemical and cell-based assays. Of the three pharmacophore models, only ABCD yielded compounds with submicromolar anti-Nampt activity. In particular, compound 5 (Table 3) showed a biochemical IC50 of 0.007 μM and a cellular IC50 value of 0.19 μM. The cellular activity of compound 5 was completely reversed by the addition of 0.33 mM of NMN, which proved that the antiproliferation effect was mechanism based. Thus, compound 5 represented an ideal starting point for additional SAR studies with respect to ligand efficiency (LE = 0.4) and lipophilicity efficiency (LipE = 5.7).(17)

    Simultaneously, in an attempt to better understand how compound 5 bound to Nampt and to use this information to guide our further SAR optimization, a cocrystal structure of 5 in complex with the protein was generated (Figures 6 and 7). In the NAM-binding region (corresponding to pharmacophore points A, B, and C), the binding mode of compound 5 was noted to be similar to that observed for 1 with the pyridyl group stacked between the aromatic side chains of Nampt residues Phe193 and Tyr18′. A crystallographic water was also observed mediating a hydrogen bonding network between the thiourea nitrogens, the backbone carbonyl of Val242, and the Asp219 and Ser241 side chains. The hydroxyl group of Ser275 was located nearly perpendicular to the thiourea moiety of 5, and this conformation did not allow for the formation of a typical hydrogen bond with the nearby sulfur atom. Instead, hydrophobic contacts between the sulfur and the side chains of Ile351 and Phe193 were observed. The phenyl ring of 5 occupied the Nampt tunnel region and participated in an edge-to-face interaction with the side chain of His191. The ligand’s sulfone oxygen atoms filled a hydrophobic pocket formed by the side chains of Ala379, Ile351, and Ile309 but also made hydrogen bonds with crystallographic waters which, in turn, formed other hydrogen bonds to Nampt backbone carbonyl groups. The piperidine moiety of 5 occupied space close to the piperidine present in 1 but was situated in a perpendicular orientation. Hydrophobic contacts were noted between the piperidine C2 carbon atom and the side chain of Ile309, as well as between the piperidine C3 and C4 carbons and the Val242 and Pro273 residues.
    To facilitate further optimization, we investigated three distinct areas of these Nampt inhibitors: the left-hand side (LHS), the right-hand side (RHS), and the linker (Figure 8). Because we were unsure how improvements in one portion would translate to the others, our initial inhibitor design strategy involved concurrent optimization of the various sections to define a preferred pharmacophore. Accordingly, we first chose to optimize the linker region in tandem with the RHS.
    On the basis of the cocrystal structure information obtained with compound 5, the thiourea sulfur atom did not form a hydrogen bond with the Nampt Ser275 residue (the serine hydroxyl oxygen was within a suitable distance of 3.2 Å but was perpendicular to the thiourea plane). The corresponding urea compound (9) was therefore made to possibly enable a more favorable binding geometry and at the same time reduce compound 5’s lipophilicity (calculated ClogP was reduced from 2.4 to 1.5). In fact, compound 9 exhibited biological potency and microsomal stability similar to that displayed by compound 5 (Table 2) and a significantly improved solubility profile (82 vs 3.1 μM). Accordingly, further SAR exploration was focused on molecules containing the urea linker.

    In the cocrystal structure of compound 5, the phenyl ring sits in a narrow portion of the tunnel where meta and particularly ortho substitution are sterically restricted by the tunnel walls vs para substitution which points toward the opening. This was confirmed by the meta-attachment of the sulfonamide RHS in compound 10 (Table 2). In the absence of further significant structural guidance for RHS optimization, we initiated general exploratory SAR studies of the RHS with a broad survey of functional group tolerability (Table 2). Aryl or heteroarylsulfones (11–13) were found to be equipotent compared to the aliphatic sulfonamide 9 and also displayed improved mouse microsomal stability. However, the corresponding sulfide was found to exhibit inferior activity in both biochemical and cell-based assays (compare 13 with 14). Similarly, compounds containing ether, ketone, or amide groups in their respective RHS moieties also displayed weaker anti-Nampt properties relative to the sulfones and sulfonamides (15–17, Table 2). The importance of maintaining an SO2 moiety in the inhibitor RHS is likely due to the need to maintain hydrogen bonds to the structural water molecules observed in the Nampt–5 cocrystal structure. The attractive biological properties exhibited by the sulfonamide and sulfone-containing inhibitors prompted the inclusion of these functional groups into subsequent Nampt inhibitors.
    With preliminary RHS SAR information in hand, our focus next turned to exploration of the LHS (Table 3). We first examined the importance of the pyridyl nitrogen atom present in compounds 9 and 11. The phenyl analogue corresponding to 9 (inhibitor 18) showed a dramatic loss of potency in both biochemical and cell-based assays. However, moving the nitrogen out of the aromatic ring afforded a compound (19) with similar biochemical potency compared to the related pyridine-containing inhibitor (11). Next, we introduced a fluorine atom at different positions on the phenyl ring in an attempt to mimic the electron-deficient pyridyl moiety present in 9. In doing so, we found that 2- or 3-fluoro-substituted compounds (20 and 21) displayed relatively potent biochemical activities that were much better than that exhibited by the 4-fluoro-substituted compound 22. However, all three compounds lacked meaningful cellular activity. Interestingly, introducing a nitrogen atom into the 3-fluoro-benzyl LHS substituent partially restored the lost cell potency (23). These observations emphasized the importance of including an appropriately positioned and sufficiently nucleophilic nitrogen atom in the LHS design with respect to obtaining potent cellular activity. Additional details regarding the biological rationale for this requirement are presented later in this work.

    Continuing our efforts to search for novel LHS heterocycles, bicyclic LHS compounds were synthesized and tested. As shown in Table 3, the imidazopyridine LHS compound 24 improved anti-Nampt cellular potency compared to compound 11, but poor MLM stability still remained an issue. When the imidazopyridine system was partially saturated (25), much of the biological activity was lost as compared with compound 24. Docking studies of 25 (not shown) suggested that beneficial π–π interactions were likely impeded by the loss in LHS planarity relative to inhibitor 24. The unsaturated ring present in 23 also required a shift of the linker toward Ser275, which further reduced the efficiency of the hydrogen bond networks in the area. As a follow-on to compound 24, we converted the fused bicyclic system into a linked biaryl moiety (compound 26). This molecule, however, showed a 10-fold biochemical activity loss, likely due to steric clashes with Nampt residues Tyr195 and Arg196. Replacing the pyridine of 11 with an alternate heterocycle (e.g., a pyrazole ring; 27) resulted in a complete loss of activity. The described exploration identified the 3-pyridyl moiety as a preferred LHS substituent for obtaining both potent biochemical and cellular anti-Nampt activity. Accordingly, this functional group was retained in the inhibitor design throughout the remainder of the SAR studies.
    We next turned our attention to the linker region (Table 4). Given the expected participation of both urea NH groups in an intricate, water-mediated hydrogen-bond network (cf. Figure 7), we anticipated that perturbation of either moiety might lead to reduced Nampt inhibition activity. Accordingly, compound 28 confirmed that NHβ was critical for biological potency. However, replacing NHα with an oxygen (29) or carbon atom (30 and 32) retained potent biochemical activity while causing a complete loss in cellular potency. The lack of cellular activity exhibited by these molecules may be due to incorrect positioning of the pyridyl nitrogen atom with respect to the PRPP cofactor in the NAMPT active site (see additional discussion below). In contrast, methylation of the NHα (31) caused more than a 100-fold loss in biochemical potency compared with compound 11. Although the Nampt-5 cocrystal structure suggested that space may exist for methylation at the LHS benzylic position, compound 33 showed that branching at this location was not tolerated.

    Following this discovery, we examined the effect of varying the linker lengths. As shown by compound 34, shortening the linker abolished all activity (compare to compound 11; Table 4). A one-carbon extension on either side of the urea moiety present in 9 was somewhat tolerated and afforded similar biochemical activity but also a 5-fold loss of cellular potency (35 and 36). Transfer of the methylene group of compound 9 to the right side of the linker (37) led to a roughly 5-fold loss of biochemical activity and almost a complete loss of cellular potency (IC50 = 2.1 μM) compared to the parent molecule (9). When we replaced the urea moiety present in compound 11 with the corresponding sulfonylurea, the resulting compound (38) lost almost all biological activity. Additionally, many of the described linker modifications had significant negative impacts on the microsomal stability and solubility of the resulting compounds. For example, inhibitors 30–33, 35, and 38 all exhibited inferior metabolic stability toward mouse and/or human liver microsomes relative to the parent compounds 9 and 11. Similarly, inhibitors 29 and 34 exhibited drastically reduced solubility as compared with 9 and 11. The origins of these changes are not known with certainty but may involve unfavorable alterations in the lipophilicity and/or hydrogen-bonding potential of the described molecules. The preceding SAR studies confirmed the urea linker moiety present in compounds such as 9 and 11 as optimal for obtaining potent anti-Nampt biochemical and cellular activity. This functional group was therefore incorporated into all subsequent inhibitors described in this work.
    With the completion of LHS and linker region optimizations, we turned our SAR exploration efforts back to the RHS with a focus not only on further potency improvements but also on improving microsomal stability and solubility (Table 5). We first investigated the impact of cyclic sulfonamide variations with respect to these parameters and found that the ring size correlated strongly with enzyme and cellular potency (compounds 9, 39–41). Although the 7-membered sulfonamide 39 was comparable to the 6-membered analogue 9, it did not offer any advantages with respect to microsomal stability and solubility (39 was less stable in MLM and was also less soluble relative to 9). In general, aliphatic primary sulfonamides such as compound 42 were less potent than the cyclic secondary sulfonamides, especially in cell-based assays. Phenyl sulfonamides, such as compound 43, did not have good activity and were therefore not extensively explored. Noncyclic secondary sulfonamides such as compound 44 were also much weaker in the cellular assay relative to the cyclic analogues and were similarly deprioritized.

    With the above structure–activity relationships in hand, we focused further optimization efforts on piperidine-based sulfonamides (the optimization of biaryl sulfone-containing Nampt inhibitors will be described in a separate disclosure). In general, methyl substitution at the 2-, 3-, or 4-position of the piperidine ring resulted in equivalent cellular potency (compare 45–47 with 9; Table 7). However, the methylated compounds exhibited inferior microsomal stability properties relative to 9 and additional alkylated piperidines were therefore not pursued. In contrast, introduction of a primary amide at the piperidine 4-position (48) dramatically improved MLM stability compared to 9. However, this benefit was realized at the expense of cell potency, possibly due to the highly polar nature of the amide substituent. We therefore prepared the morpholine-containing compound 49 to test whether incorporation of a less polar electron-withdrawing 4-substituent would also afford good metabolic stability properties. Encouragingly, inhibitor 49 exhibited improved microsomal stability relative to compound 9 albeit with a concomitant 7-fold loss in cellular activity. Satisfyingly, a compound containing a bridged morpholine moiety (50) showed an excellent overall profile with respect to biochemical and cellular potency, microsomal stability, and solubility. Compared to the initial lead compound (5), compound 50 not only maintained excellent ligand efficiency but also greatly improved lipophilicity efficiency (5 vs 50; LE, 0.4 vs 0.4; LipE, 5.7 vs 7.2, respectively).
    Given the encouraging biological profile of compound 50, the molecule was progressed to in vivo mouse PK and xenograft efficacy studies to evaluate its oral exposure and antitumor effects. As summarized in Table 6, compound 50 demonstrated reasonable PK properties in mice with oral bioavailability of 26%. It showed a moderate clearance (2.7 ± 0.36 L/h/kg) and a high volume of distribution (1.1 ± 0.22 L/kg). The compound also exhibited maximum plasma exposures that were about 30-fold greater than the corresponding cell IC50 value, and its plasma levels remained at least 5-fold above this IC50 for 2 h (all values uncorrected for protein binding effects).

    In an efficacy study, compound 50 exhibited significant antitumor effects against an A2780 human ovarian carcinoma tumor xenograft model without significant body weight loss. In mice bearing A2780 xenografts, oral administration of the compound for 10 days (125 mg/kg bid) produced rapid tumor regression (Figure 9). Tumor growth inhibition effects were also noted after cessation of compound dosing during the duration of the xenograft experiment. For example, a tumor growth inhibition value (TGI) of 97% was observed on day 17 of the study. The described xenograft results confirmed that a urea-containing Nampt inhibitor with appropriate biological and DMPK properties can function as a potent in vivo anticancer agent.

    Phosphoribosylated Adduct Detection and Additional Biochemical Studies

    During the LHS SAR optimization process described above, we identified several compounds which exhibited potent inhibition in the Nampt biochemical assay but little activity in the A2780 cell culture assessment (e.g., 20–22, 29, 30, and 32). In the case of inhibitors 20–22, these outcomes seemed to be associated with the absence of a nucleophilic nitrogen atom in the LHS portions of the molecules. It was previously shown that other Nampt inhibitors bearing similar nucleophilic nitrogen atoms (e.g., compound 2) could serve as phosphoribosylation substrates and form adducts with PRPP via a Nampt-catalyzed process.(19) We suspected that the described urea-containing inhibitors might function in a related manner and therefore conducted detailed mass spectrometry experiments to verify this hypothesis. Accordingly, compound 21 (a non-nitrogen containing LHS inhibitor devoid of cell activity) and compound 50 (a nitrogen containing LHS inhibitor with potent cell activity) were incubated with the Nampt enzyme under the standard biochemical assay conditions, and the formation of the corresponding PRPP-derived phosphoribosylated adducts was assessed by LC-MS/MS. The phosphoribosylated adduct was only detected for compound 50 (Figure 10) and was not observed for compound 21 (data in Supporting Information). Importantly, the 50–PRPP adduct was not detected when the Nampt enzyme was omitted from the biochemical experiment, strongly suggesting that the adduct was formed through a Nampt-catalyzed process (Supporting Information). These results indicate that the ability of a given urea-containing Nampt inhibitor to form a phosphoribosylated adduct may be a necessary requirement for obtaining potent anti-Nampt cell activity.
    However, the PRPP-derived ribose adducts corresponding to cell-inactive compounds 29, 30, and 32 were also detected by mass spectrometry in similar biochemical experiments (Supporting Information). These data suggest that adduct formation itself is not sufficient to impart potent cell activity to a given Nampt inhibitor and that other factors also influence these outcomes. While compound permeability and protein binding characteristics are two likely determinants of cell potency, we suspected that, due to high structural similarity, these parameters would not vary significantly among the cell potent (50) and cell-inactive (21, 29, 30, and 32) molecules described above. We therefore conducted more detailed biochemical experiments with these compounds in an attempt to better understand their cell culture behavior.
    As shown in Figure 11 and Table 7, competition experiments indicated that all these molecules inhibit Nampt through a NAM-competitive manner with linear increases in IC50 values observed with increasing NAM concentrations.(20) Importantly, varying NAM concentrations had a relatively minor impact on the IC50 value of the cell active compound (50) as compared with effects on IC50 outcomes for the other (cell-inactive) molecules studied. This behavior resulted in compound 50 having the most potent calculated Ki value among these inhibitors (Table 7).(21) Thus, the ability of the compounds and/or their PRPP–ribose adducts to effectively inhibit Nampt in the presence of NAM may also influence their ability to function as potent cell-active agents.(22, 23) However, given the similarities between the cell-active molecule 50 and the cell-inactive compound 29 in these experiments, it is possible that additional factors also contribute to the cellular outcomes.(24)

    Chemistry

    The urea derivatives were prepared by utilizing the general procedures shown in Schemes 1–5. Scheme 1 summarizes the isocyanate approach. Pyridin-3-ylmethanamine 51a was condensed with several commercially available isocyanates (52) to give the ureas (15, 53, and 56). Treatment of the sulfonyl chloride (53) with different amines/anilines (54) then afforded compounds 9, 39–40, and 48–49. To speed up our SAR exploration, a one-step parallel synthesis protocol (reaction of 53 with 54) was also developed to provide the final compounds 42–46.     In summary, using structure-based design and leveraging key information from literature-disclosed cocrystal structures, we discovered a structurally novel thiourea-containing Nampt inhibitor (5). Guided by the cocrystal structure of compound 5 in complex with Nampt, several rounds of SAR optimization ultimately yielded the urea-containing compound 50, which possessed excellent cellular potency and in vitro and in vivo ADME properties. Compound 50 demonstrated reasonable PK properties in mice with an oral bioavailability of 26% and also showed excellent in vivo antitumor efficacy when dosed orally in an A2780 ovarian tumor xenograft model. In addition, SAR and mechanistic studies indicated that an appropriately positioned and sufficiently nucleophilic nitrogen atom was required in the LHS of a given inhibitor to enable Nampt-mediated formation of the corresponding phosphoribosylated (PRPP) adduct and thereby impart potent cellular activity. Further details of related compounds which build on these discoveries will be disclosed in due course.

        FK866, a Highly Specific Noncompetitive Inhibitor of Nicotinamide Phosphoribosyltransferase, Represents a Novel Mechanism for Induction of Tumor Cell Apoptosis
        Hasmann, Max; Schemainda, Isabel
        Cancer Research (2003), 63 (21), 7436-7442CODEN: CNREA8; ISSN:0008-5472. (American Association for Cancer Research)
        Deregulation of apoptosis, the physiol. form of cell death, is closely assocd. with immunol. diseases and cancer. Apoptosis is activated either by death receptor-driven or mitochondrial pathways, both of which may provide potential targets for novel anticancer drugs. Although several ligands stimulating death receptors have been described, the actual mol. events triggering the mitochondrial pathway are largely unknown. Here, we show initiation of apoptosis by gradual depletion of the intracellular coenzyme NAD+. We identified the first low mol. wt. compd., designated FK866, which induces apoptosis by highly specific, noncompetitive inhibition of nicotinamide phosphoribosyltransferase (NAPRT), a key enzyme in the regulation of NAD+ biosynthesis from the natural precursor nicotinamide. Interference with this enzyme does not primarily intoxicate cells because the mitochondrial respiratory activity and the NAD+-dependent redox reactions involved remain unaffected as long as NAD+ is not effectively depleted by catabolic reactions. Certain tissues, however, have a high turnover of NAD+ through its cleavage by enzymes like poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase. Such cells often rely on the more readily available nicotinamide pathway for NAD+ synthesis and undergo apoptosis after inhibition of NAPRT, whereas cells effectively using the nicotinic acid pathway for NAD+ synthesis remain unaffected. In support of this concept, FK866 effectively induced delayed cell death by apoptosis in HepG2 human liver carcinoma cells with an IC50 of ∼1 nM, did not directly inhibit mitochondrial respiratory activity, but caused gradual NAD+ depletion through specific inhibition of NAPRT. This enzyme, when partially purified from K562 human leukemia cells, was noncompetitively inhibited by FK866, and the inhibitor consts. were calcd. to be 0.4 nM for the enzyme/substrate complex (Ki) and 0.3 nM for the free enzyme (Ki'), resp. Nicotinic acid and nicotinamide were both found to have antidote potential for the cellular effects of FK866. FK866 may be used for treatment of diseases implicating deregulated apoptosis such as cancer for immunosuppression or as a sensitizer for genotoxic agents. Furthermore, it may provide an important tool for investigation of the mol. triggers of the mitochondrial pathway leading to apoptosis through enabling temporal sepn. of NAD+ decrease from ATP breakdown and apoptosis by several days.
