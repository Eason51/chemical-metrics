    Abstract

    To develop potent and orally bioavailable melatonin receptor (MT1 and MT2) agonists, a novel series of 5–6–5 tricyclic derivatives was designed, synthesized, and evaluated. The synthesized indeno[5,4-d][1,3]oxazole, cyclopenta[c]pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine, indeno[5,4-d][1,3]thiazole, and cyclopenta[e]indazole derivatives showed potent binding affinities for MT1/MT2 receptors. Further optimization of these derivatives based on their metabolic stability in human hepatic microsomes revealed that (S)-3b ((S)-N-[2-(2-methyl-7,8-dihydro-6H-indeno[5,4-d][1,3]oxazol-8-yl)ethyl]acetamide) was a potent MT1 and MT2 ligand (MT1, Ki = 0.031 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.070 nM) with good metabolic stability in human hepatic microsomes. Moreover, compound (S)-3b showed good BBB permeability in rats, and its in vivo pharmacological effects were confirmed by its sleep-promotion ability in cats.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Melatonin, N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine (Figure 1), is a neurohormone primarily released from the pineal gland at nights in both diurnal and nocturnal species.(1) Its production is regulated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus, which is located in the hypothalamus and contains the master clock that programs circadian rhythms.(2) Melatonin works through the activation of GPCRs, MT1 and MT2,(3,4) expressed primarily in the brain, retina, pituitary, and blood vessels.(5) Some reports indicate that MT1 and MT2 receptors mediate sleep-promotion effects and circadian rhythms.(6−8) It has been suggested that melatonin administration in humans could ameliorate the symptoms of insomnia,(9,10) jet lag,(11) and shift work disturbances.(12) Moreover, based on its chronobiotic effects, melatonin has also been proposed to have a potential for ameliorating mood disorders,(13) depression,(14) anxiety,(15) cancer,(16,17) and Parkinson’s disease.(18) However, the therapeutic use of melatonin is limited by its poor pharmacokinetic profile.(19,20) These pharmacological and pharmacokinetic aspects of melatonin have encouraged us to develop potent and orally bioavailable MT1/MT2 receptor agonists.
    During the past two decades, several structurally different MT1/MT2 receptor ligands have been reported in the literature.(21) Ramelteon (Figure 1), which was designed and synthesized in our laboratory,(22,23) possesses an angularly fused 5–6–5 tricyclic chemical scaffold, whereas most of the MT1/MT2 receptor ligands are characterized by mono- or bicyclic ring systems. Ramelteon showed potent binding affinity for MT1 (Ki = 0.014 nM) and MT2 (Ki = 0.112 nM) and has been marketed for the treatment of insomnia.(24) The discovery of this unique tricyclic ring system has opened the possibility of MT1/MT2 receptor agonists for human use. To enhance the therapeutic value of melatonin receptor agonists, further exploration of the tricyclic ring is desired.
    In a clinical study of ramelteon, its mean bioavailability was found to be less than 2%, and the main cause was possibly first-pass oxidative metabolism.(25) Our next focus is to develop potent MT1/MT2 receptor agonists with higher oral bioavailability by reducing the oxidative metabolism.
    The strategies for enhancing metabolic stability can be generally divided into two major groups. One is the modification of metabolically unstable sites, and the other is the reduction of compound lipophilicity.(26) While the modification of metabolically unstable sites is a powerful and direct approach to prevent metabolism, it would be inefficient when compounds are metabolized at multiple sites. We focused on reducing the lipophilicity because ramelteon has been reported to be metabolized at multiple sites such as the dihydrofuran ring, cyclopentane ring, and amide chain.(25)
    Our initial trial to reduce the lipophilicity involved incorporating nitrogen atom into the indan ring (1a,(27)1b,(28) and 2a–c(29)), resulting in a decreased MT1/MT2 receptor binding affinity. This result suggested that the binding pocket occupied by the indan ring was hydrophobic. We therefore aimed to reduce the lipophilicity by changing the dihydrofuran ring moiety to more polar heterocycles. Very recently, X-ray crystal structures of MT1/MT2 in complex with ramelteon revealed that the oxygen atom of the dihydrofuran ring acts as a hydrogen bond acceptor,(30,31) suggesting that oxygen atom at this position might be essential for the high MT1/MT2 affinity. On the other hand, some of the previous papers suggested that the nitrogen atom of oxazole ring might also serve as a surrogate hydrogen bond acceptor.(32,33) In this paper, we designed a novel series of 5-6-5 tricyclic derivatives by converting the dihydrofuran moiety of ramelteon to more polar nitrogen-containing heterocycles (Figure 2), which are expected to achieve not only reduced lipophilicity but also potent binding affinity.
    Here, we report the SAR of tricyclic derivatives with azole rings such as oxazole (3), thiazole (4), and pyrazole (5 and 6) and a subsequent optimization to identify a potent and orally bioavailable MT1/MT2 receptor agonist. The lipophilicities of the designed derivatives that correspond to ramelteon (R1 = H, R2 = H, R3 = Et) were calculated to be lower (cLogP = 1.7–2.4) than that of ramelteon (cLogP = 2.5).(34) The installation of these azoles can therefore enhance metabolic stability and oral bioavailability. Moreover, the designed tricycles enabled us to explore the R1 and R2 substituents, which will provide new findings on the SAR of MT1/MT2 receptor ligands.

    Chemistry

    The indeno[5,4-d][1,3]oxazole derivatives 3a, 3b, and 3j were synthesized as demonstrated in Scheme 1. The selective nitration of the position-6 of 4-bromo-7-hydroxyindan-1-one 7(35) using fuming HNO3 afforded the nitro form 8. Both the nitro and bromo groups of 8 were simultaneously reduced by hydrogenation to provide the aniline 9 as a hydrobromide salt. The oxazole ring of compound 10a was constructed by refluxing 9 in the presence of triethyl orthoformate. The methyloxazole derivative 10b was prepared by the acetylation of 9 with acetic anhydride followed by cyclization in the presence of catalytic pyridinium p-toluenesulfonate. Moreover, 10a and 10b were converted to the target compounds 3a, 3b, and 3j by a previously reported procedure, with some modification.(22) The Horner–Wadsworth–Emmons reaction of 10a and 10b with diethyl cyanomethylphosphonate gave the nitriles 11a and 11b, respectively. The hydrogenation of their nitrile group over Raney cobalt followed by acylation and hydrogenation over Pd/C afforded the target compounds 3a, 3b, and 3j. Chiral column chromatography was employed to prepare optically active isomers of 3b, namely, (S)-3b and (R)-3b.
    The indeno[5,4-d][1,3]oxazole derivatives 3c–3g and 3i with various R1 groups were prepared by diversity-oriented synthesis (Scheme 2). To cyclize the oxazole ring at the last steps, we first constructed the amide side chain. The hydroxy group of compound 8 was protected by the methyl group. The methoxy form 12 was transformed into the amide 16 by a procedure similar to that in Scheme 1 and by the reduction of the nitro and bromo groups. The removal of the methyl group with boron tribromide provided the key intermediate 17. The oxazole ring for compounds 3c–f and 3h was formed by acylation and subsequent cyclization using a certain amount of catalytic pyridinium p-toluenesulfonate. The methoxy derivative 3g was prepared by refluxing 17 in the presence of tetramethoxymethane in THF. The debenzylation of compound 3h with palladium on carbon yielded the hydroxymethyl derivative 3i.
    The synthetic route for the indeno[5,4-d][1,3]thiazole derivatives 4a and 4b is illustrated in Scheme 3. The known aminoindan derivative 18(36) was regioselectively halogenated at position-7 by iodine(I) chloride to provide compound 19. The thiazole ring was constructed by a palladium(0)-catalyzed thiation of 19 with thioacetamide.(37) The obtained indenothiazole derivative 20 was converted into the desired compounds 4a and 4b in a manner similar to that in Scheme 1.
    The cyclopenta[c]pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine derivatives 5a–c were synthesized using the known pyridine derivative 24 as the starting precursor(38) (Scheme 4). The cyclopentane ring was constructed via the Dieckmann condensation of 24 and subsequent decarboxylation to form the ketone 25. The Horner–Wadsworth–Emmons reaction followed by hydrogenation provided the nitrile 26. The angular tricyclic compound 27a was obtained by the 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition reaction(39) of the N-aminopyridinium salt prepared by the amination of 26 with 1-(aminooxy)-2,4-dinitrobenzene.(40) The linear tricyclic compound 27b was also obtained (27a:27b = 2.5:1) due to the poor selectivity of this cycloaddition reaction. The nitrile moiety of 27a was reduced over Raney cobalt to provide the amine 28. The hydrolysis and decarboxylation of the ester moiety of 28 under acidic conditions followed by acylation furnished the monomethyl derivatives 5a and 5d. The dimethyl derivative 5b was prepared by reduction with diisobutylaluminum hydride before acetylation, and the ester derivative 5c was formed by the acetylation of 28.
    The synthesis of the cyclopenta[e]indazole derivative 6a is illustrated in Scheme 5. The position-6 of commercially available methyl 3-amino-2-methylbenzoate 29 was regioselectively brominated with N-bromosuccimide to form compound 30. Compound 30 was then treated with sodium nitrite in acetic acid to provide the indazole 31 via diazonium salt formation. Trimethyloxonium tetrafluoroborate regioselectively methylated the nitrogen atom at the position-2(41) of 31 to prepare compound 32. To construct the cyclopentane ring, methyl acrylate was introduced via Heck reaction to afford the cinnamic acid derivative 33 followed by hydrogenation to yield the propanoic acid derivative 34. Dieckmann condensation and subsequent decarboxylation under acidic conditions resulted in the construction of cyclopenta[e]indazole to yield the ketone 35. The target compound 6a was obtained by following the same procedure as described in Scheme 1.

    We initially focused on the SAR studies of the indeno[5,4-d][1,3]oxazole derivatives. The binding affinity of the unsubstituted form 3a (R1 = H; MT1, Ki = 0.45 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.073 nM) was comparable to that of melatonin (MT1, Ki = 0.24 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.21 nM); this suggests that the nitrogen atom on the oxazole ring functioned as a hydrogen bond acceptor. The SARs of R1 substituents in the indeno[5,4-d][1,3]oxazole series (3b–3g and 3i) were explored. The introduction of a methyl group (3b) resulted in a sevenfold enhancement of the MT1 receptor binding affinity (MT1, Ki = 0.067 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.13 nM). The introduction of more bulky groups such as ethyl (3c), cyclopropyl (3d), phenyl (3e), or benzyl group (3f) resulted in decreased MT1 receptor binding affinity. By contrast, the R1 conversion had little impact on the MT2 receptor binding affinity, leading to the identification of the MT2 selective ligand 3f (MT1/MT2 = 37). This result is consistent with the reported SAR of the bicyclic benzoxazole derivatives.(32) Compound 3g, which possesses an electron-donating methoxy group, exhibited a binding affinity similar to that of the ethyl derivative 3c. The electron density of the benzoxazole ring might not affect the MT1 and MT2 receptor binding affinities. The introduction of a hydrophilic hydroxymethyl group (3i) resulted in a decreased binding affinity for both MT1 and MT2 receptors (MT1, Ki = 1.2 nM; MT2, Ki = 1.1 nM). The X-ray crystal structures of MT1 and MT2 revealed that the binding pocket around the R1 position was hydrophobic.(30,31) The SAR is consistent with the observation from the X-ray crystal structures. We conclude that the methyl group was the best R1 substituent to achieve a potent binding affinity for both receptors.
    The indeno[5,4-d][1,3]thiazole derivative with methyl group on the R1 substituent (4a) exhibited a slight enhancement in the MT1/MT2 receptor binding affinity (MT1, Ki = 0.037 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.086 nM) compared with the indeno[5,4-d][1,3]oxazole derivative 3b. The thiazole ring also effectively worked to achieve a potent binding affinity for MT1/MT2 receptors.
    The cyclopenta[c]pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine derivative 5a (R1 = Me, R2 = H) showed a potent MT1 receptor binding affinity comparable to that of melatonin but a lower binding affinity for MT2 receptors (MT1, Ki = 0.20 nM; MT2, Ki = 1.4 nM, MT1/MT2 = 0.14). This result indicates the possibility of developing MT1 selective ligands by exploring heteroaromatic rings. The tricyclic nucleus of the derivatives easily allows for the exploration of the R2 substituent, whose SAR has not been reported in the literature. The introduction of a methyl group (5b) extremely decreased the binding affinity for both MT1 and MT2 (MT1, Ki = 9.3 nM; MT2, Ki = 47 nM). Moreover, the ester derivative 5c, even at a concentration of 100 nM, exhibited less than 50% binding for MT1/MT2 receptors. The X-ray crystal structures of MT1/MT2 in complex with ramelteon revealed that the valine residues (MT1: V1915.42, MT2: V2045.42) are located at the close position of the dihydrofuran ring.(30,31) The introduction of R2 substituents might cause the steric repulsion against these valine residues. The cyclopenta[e]indazole derivative with the optimal substituents (6a, R1 = Me, R2 = H) showed a binding affinity (MT1, Ki = 0.35 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.75 nM) comparable to that of melatonin. The two types of pyrazole-fused tricyclic derivatives (5 and 6) indicated that the pyrazole ring was also a good hydrogen bond acceptor for MT1/MT2 receptors.
    Regarding the amide side chain, we have previously reported that the propanamide group exerted the most potent binding affinity.(22) The installation of the propanamide group slightly enhanced the MT1 or MT2 receptor binding affinity or both (3j, 4b, 5d, and 6b) compared with the acetamide derivatives 3b, 4a, 5a, and 6a.

    The propanamide derivatives (R3 = Et) with less lipophilicity (3j, cLogP = 2.0; 5d, cLogP = 2.0; and 6b, cLogP = 1.9) than ramelteon (cLogP = 2.5) showed enhanced metabolic stability in human hepatic microsomes (3j, 29 μL/min/mg; 5d, 20 μL/min/mg; and 6b, 23 μL/min/mg) compared with ramelteon (51 μL/min/mg). Moreover, the more lipophilic indeno[5,4-d][1,3]thiazole derivative 4b (cLogP = 2.9) showed a stability (41 μL/min/mg) comparable to that of ramelteon. Less lipophilic acetamide derivatives 3b, 5a, and 6a were more stable (3b, 0 μL/min/mg; 5a, 14 μL/min/mg; and 6a, 5 μL/min/mg) than the corresponding propanamide derivatives 3j, 5d, and 6b. Figure 3a demonstrates a clear correlation between the metabolic clearance in human hepatic microsomes and the cLogP values, strongly suggesting the effectiveness of our metabolic stability enhancement strategy.
    Similar results were obtained for the metabolic stability in rat hepatic microsomes. A definite correlation also existed between the metabolic clearance in rat hepatic microsomes and the cLogP values (Figure 3b). Compounds with good metabolic stability in rat hepatic microsomes showed a better rat oral bioavailability (3b, F = 18.5%; 5a, F = 14.3%; and 6a, F = 20.9%) than ramelteon (F = 8.5%). Figure 4 shows that the rat oral bioavailability highly correlates with the metabolic stability in rat hepatic microsomes. This correlation suggests that an improved first-pass metabolism contributes to the oral bioavailability of these derivatives. Hence, compounds with superior metabolic stability in human hepatic microsomes have the potential to show good oral bioavailability in humans. We selected the oxazole derivative 3b owing to the good metabolic stability against human hepatic microsomes (0 μL/min/mg) and the highly potent binding affinity (MT1, Ki = 0.067 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.13 nM).
    The binding affinity for the optically active isomers of 3b was evaluated (Table 1). The S-isomer (S)-3b showed much higher MT1 and MT2 receptor binding affinities (MT1, Ki = 0.031 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.070 nM) than (R)-3b (MT1, Ki = 23 nM; MT2, Ki = 6.1 nM). Compound (S)-3b was determined to be a eutomer. The eutomer demonstrated a binding affinity comparable to that of ramelteon(23) (MT1, Ki = 0.014 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.11 nM). The metabolic clearance in human hepatic microsomes (0 μL/min/mg) and rat oral bioavailability (F = 27.0%) were confirmed to be similar to those of the racemate 3b. Compound (S)-3b was subjected to further pharmacokinetic and pharmacological evaluation.

    The functional activity of (S)-3b was evaluated using cAMP production in CHO cells expressing human MT1 or MT2 receptors (Figure 5). Melatonin reduced forskolin-stimulated cAMP accumulation in a concentration-dependent manner (MT1, EC50 = 0.022 nM; MT2, EC50 = 0.85 nM). Compound (S)-3b also exhibited full agonism and superior MT1 and MT2 agonistic activities (MT1, EC50 = 0.015 nM; MT2, EC50 = 0.51 nM). The other tricyclic series also showed full agonism and superior MT1 and MT2 agonistic activities (see the Supporting Information).
    The in vivo pharmacological effects of (S)-3b were evaluated using a previously reported cat model.(42) The oral administration of (S)-3b at 10 mg/kg significantly increased the total sleep time compared with the vehicle-treated control (Figure 6). We confirmed the sleep-promoting effect of compound (S)-3b in cats after oral administration.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, novel 5–6–5 tricyclic derivatives were investigated for developing potent and orally bioavailable MT1 and MT2 receptor agonists. Among the synthesized compounds, the indeno[5,4-d][1,3]oxazole derivative (S)-3b was identified as a potent MT1 and MT2 receptor agonist (MT1, Ki = 0.031 nM; MT2, Ki = 0.070 nM) with good in vitro clearance in human hepatic microsomes (0 μL/min/mg). In this chemical series, a clear correlation existed between in vitro clearance in rat hepatic microsomes and oral bioavailability in rat, suggesting that compound (S)-3b could show good oral bioavailability in humans given its improved in vitro clearance compared to that of ramelteon (51 μL/min/mg). Moreover, compound (S)-3b showed good BBB permeability in rats, and its sleep-promoting effect was confirmed by oral administration in cats. These results support the therapeutic potential of compound (S)-3b as an orally available MT1 and MT2 receptor agonist. Further evaluation will be reported in due course.

Coupling constants (J values) are given in hertz (Hz). LCMS analysis (ESI+) was performed on a Waters 2795 Separations Module (L-column2 ODS (3.0 × 50 mm I.D., CERI, Japan); 0.05% TFA in ultrapure water/acetonitrile gradient; UV detection 220 nm or 254 nm), and MS spectra were recorded using a Waters ZQ2000 with electrospray ionization.     Sleep-Promoting Effect of (S)-3b in Cats

    Healthy male and female European Shorthair cats were purchased from Nisseiken Company, Ltd. (Tokyo, Japan) and housed individually in a room maintained at 22–24 °C with a 12 h light–dark cycle (light on at 7:00 a.m.). They were fed once daily (7:00 a.m.), and water was available ad libitum.
    Surgery for EEG/EMG recordings was performed as previously described.(42) Cats were placed on a stereotaxic apparatus under general anesthesia. Electrodes for electroencephalogram (EEG) recording were implanted bilaterally in the frontal and parietal cortices and hippocampus according to the cat brain atlas of Snider and Niemer (1961). Stainless-steel screws were used as cortical electrodes. The depth bipolar recording electrode consisted of insulated stainless-steel wires (0.3 mm in diameter) except at the tips (0.5 mm). A pair of stainless-steel wires was implanted into the back cervical muscles to record electromyogram (EMG) data.
    The cats were allowed to recover from surgery for at least 7 days before habituation to the test chamber (an acrylic cage 75 × 65 × 40 cm located in a soundproof, electrically shielded room that was maintained under conditions similar to those of the home cage). The animals were connected to the recording equipment via a light flexible cable with a slip-ring connector that permitted them free movement. After we confirmed that animals sleep sufficiently in the experimental room, polysomnographic recordings were conducted. The behavioral and postural changes of the animals were observed using a video camera continuously throughout the experiment. The EEG–EMG signals were acquired using an electroencephalograph (GE Yokogawa Medical System, Osaka, Japan). These recordings were performed for 8 h after drug administration. Compound (S)-3b (10 mg/kg) or vehicle was orally administered between 9:30 and 10:00 a.m. The signals were digitally filtered (EEG, 0.5–30 Hz; EMG, 20–200 Hz) and semiautomatically scored in 20 s epochs as wakefulness, slow wave sleep (SWS), or rapid eye movement sleep (REM) by a sleep scoring system (Sleep Sign ver. 2; Kissei Comtec, Matsumoto, Japan). This preliminary scoring was visually inspected and corrected if necessary. Compound (S)-3b was suspended in 0.5% (w/v) methylcellulose solution. The solution was administered orally to each cat in a gelatin capsule. In the control trial, each cat was given a capsule-containing vehicle. We used a crossover design with seven cats per group and at least 7 days of washout periods. Data were expressed as the total time spent in sleep stages (SWS and REM) during 8 h following the oral administration. The statistical differences between the vehicle-treated group and compound (S)-3b-treated group were analyzed using a crossover analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by a contrast test with significance set at p < 0.05.

Thus, the sleep times selected by night shift workers may not be well-synchronized to their melatonin rhythm, assumed to mark the phase of their underlying circadian pacemaker.

        The sleep-promoting action of ramelteon (TAK-375) in freely moving cats
        Miyamoto Masaomi; Nishikawa Hisao; Doken Yayoi; Hirai Keisuke; Uchikawa Osamu; Ohkawa Shigenori
        Sleep (2004), 27 (7), 1319-25 ISSN:0161-8105.
        INTRODUCTION: Ramelteon (TAK-375) is an MT1/MT2 receptor agonist being studied for the treatment of insomnia and circadian rhythm sleep disorders. We compared the behavioral effects of ramelteon and exogenous melatonin in freely moving cats. METHODS: Ramelteon and melatonin were each suspended in a 0.5% (weight per volume) methylcellulose solution and administered orally to freely moving cats. In the control trial, each cat was given vehicle. Each dose of ramelteon or melatonin was compared with the vehicle control in a crossover design. Electroencephalogram, electromyogram, and electrooculogram recordings were assessed. RESULTS: Ramelteon significantly decreased wakefulness at doses of 0.001,0.01, and 0.1 mg/kg, increased slow-wave sleep at doses of 0.001, 0.01, and 0.1 mg/kg, and increased rapid eye movement sleep at a dose of 0.1 mg/kg, compared with the vehicle controls, as assessed by analysis of variance. The effects of ramelteon lasted for up to 6 hours when evaluated by reduction of wakefulness. Exogenous melatonin (0.01-1 mg/kg) significantly increased slow-wave sleep, but the effect was weaker than that of ramelteon and lasted for only 2 hours. The lowest doses of ramelteon (0.0001 mg/kg) and melatonin (0.001 mg/kg) had no significant effect on sleep-wakefulness stage. CONCLUSIONS: Ramelteon was more effective than exogenous melatonin in promoting and maintaining sleep in freely moving cats. Based on its unique mechanism of action, ramelteon should be studied further to evaluate its potential for the treatment of sleep disorders.
