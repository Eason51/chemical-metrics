    Abstract

    Constitutive activation of the canonical Wnt signaling pathway, in most cases driven by inactivation of the tumor suppressor APC, is a hallmark of colorectal cancer. Tankyrases are druggable key regulators in these malignancies and are considered as attractive targets for therapeutic interventions, although no inhibitor has been progressed to clinical development yet. We continued our efforts to develop tankyrase inhibitors targeting the nicotinamide pocket with suitable drug-like properties for investigating effects of Wnt pathway inhibition on tumor growth. Herein, the identification of a screening hit series and its optimization through scaffold hopping and SAR exploration is described. The systematic assessment delivered M2912, a compound with an optimal balance between excellent TNKS potency, exquisite PARP selectivity, and a predicted human PK compatible with once daily oral dosing. Modulation of cellular Wnt pathway activity and significant tumor growth inhibition was demonstrated with this compound in colorectal xenograft models in vivo.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Constitutive activation of the canonical Wnt signaling pathway is a hallmark of colorectal cancer, which is in most cases (∼80%) driven by the inactivation of the tumor suppressor adenomatous polyposis coli (APC).(1) Different loss-of-function mutations within APC impair the β-catenin destruction complex, resulting in tumor-promoting and tumor-maintaining activation of Wnt/β-catenin signaling.(2) Tankyrase enzymes gained significant interest over recent years as druggable key regulators in APC mutant colorectal cancer.(3,4) The two highly homologous human tankyrase isoforms, TNKS1 and TNKS2, are members of the poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) superfamily. This unique class of enzymes consists of 17 members that catalyze ADP-ribosylation of their target proteins via a common catalytic PARP domain.(5,6) Mechanistically, tankyrases mark axin proteins, the concentration-limiting components of the β-catenin destruction complex, for degradation by PARsylation and subsequent ubiquitination.(7,8) Inhibition of tankyrase is suggested to block the Wnt signal transduction pathway by accumulation of axin proteins and thus triggering enhanced β-catenin degradation.(9,10) Therefore, inhibition of tankyrase activity is considered as a promising strategy for the treatment of colorectal cancer, which is characterized by constitutive Wnt pathway activity.(11−14)
    Accordingly, in the ambition to discover novel anticancer agents, many research groups have worked on the development of tankyrase inhibitors. As a result, a plethora of diverse chemotypes have been identified that inhibit TNKS1/2 by binding to the substrate NAD+ binding site. Depending on the cavity they were occupying, these compounds were classified as nicotinamide pocket inhibitors, adenosine binding pocket inhibitors, or dual inhibitors addressing both. Despite these tremendous efforts, the number of compounds exhibiting antitumor efficacy in preclinical mouse models after oral application is rather limited. For instance, a few nicotinamide pocket inhibitors (Figure 1) showed effects on tumor growth upon oral treatment as monotherapy (RK-287107, RK-582, AZ1366).(15−17) The number of reports about orally efficacious adenosine pocket binders or dual inhibitors in preclinical models is even less,(18) indicating that optimization of chemotypes with these binding modes toward suitable candidates for in vivo studies seems to be very challenging. Moreover, pharmacokinetic characterization of these inhibitors in higher species and their assessment toward applicability as anticancer therapeutics in humans is limited. Hence, there is a continued need for the development of potent, orally available small molecule inhibitors directed toward TNKS1/2 and the WNT/β-catenin signaling pathway.
    We recently described the identification of 2-aryl-quinazolin-4-one derivatives as active TNKS1 inhibitors in a biochemical high-throughput screen (HTS) and their optimization toward compound 1, which was confirmed to bind to the nicotinamide pocket by X-ray crystallography. Owing to its low MW (316), this TNKS inhibitor had favorable drug-like properties and exhibited target modulation of tankyrase and subsequent reduction of Wnt pathway activity in a human xenograft tumor model.(19) Encouraged by these promising results, we reasoned if the biochemical potency and consequently the moderate cellular mechanistic activity of this quinazolin-4-one type inhibitor 1 (EC50 = 319 nM) could be improved by exchanging the heterocyclic core while keeping the MW in the same range and maintaining the favorable selectivity over related PARP proteins. This could potentially decrease the efficacious dose in pharmacological studies and consequently have a beneficial impact on the predicted human dose. To this end, we surveyed the hit list obtained by the abovementioned biochemical screen to identify alternative core structures and selected a fragment-like hit series containing a 5,6-membered imidazo[1,2-a]pyrazin-8-one ring system as a starting point for an optimization campaign. Herein, the modification of the initial hit series by a scaffold hopping approach and subsequent exploration of the SAR of the discovered pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one scaffold is described. These efforts yielded the highly potent and selective tankyrase inhibitor 3-[4-(1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl)-phenyl]-6-methyl-2H-pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one M2912 (30g) with favorable physicochemical and pharmacokinetic properties. Significant tumor growth inhibition in two colorectal xenograft models with APC mutations could be demonstrated for this compound.

    Identification and In Vitro Characterization of the HTS Hit

    In a biochemical HTS of the Merck screening collection against TNKS1 using a homogeneous TR-FRET assay, which monitored the inhibition of the physiological autoPARsylation activity of tankyrase,(19) a small hit series with an imidazo[1,2-a]pyrazin-8-one scaffold was identified. The biochemical potency of the most potent series member 4a was only moderate, which is in accordance with the findings of Shultz et al. that more polar alterations of the sulfur-containing ring of XAV-939 decreased activity.(20) However, there are several attributes of screening hit 4a, which made it an attractive starting point for further investigation including its fragment-like structure, polar nature (LogD = 1.6), and favorable binding efficiency (BEI = 25.9).
    Many nicotinamide pocket inhibitors described in the literature contain a pivotal lactam binding motif forming key hydrogen bonds with the protein and consequently leading to a similar orientation of the ligand in the binding cavity. Potency of this inhibitor type could significantly be improved by introducing a tert-butyl group in the para position of the distal phenyl residue, which fits very well into a small hydrophobic tunnel.(21) Accordingly, a tert-butyl residue was introduced to access the inhibitory potential of the hit scaffold. Indeed, biochemical activity of 4a could significantly be improved by this structural modification and compound 4b was a double digit nanomolar inhibitor of TNKS1. Further, despite its increased lipophilicity, compound 4b showed decent aqueous solubility and metabolic stability in in vitro microsomal assays and was also active in the cellular mechanistic axin2 assay with low micromolar potency (EC50 = 1.4 μM, Table 1), which however was still far above the activity level required for a compound suitable for therapeutic pharmacology studies.(19) Nevertheless, biochemical potency of compound 4b demonstrated that also the introduction of a 5,6-bicyclic core can result in a highly binding efficient tankyrase inhibitor (BEI = 28.7). This led us to perform a scaffold hopping approach and systematically study the impact of modifications of the central bicyclic core on TNKS1/2 inhibition and cellular potency. Thus, we introduced other 5-membered heterocycles to the nicotinamide mimetic bicyclic system and subsequently explored the SAR of the preferred scaffold to identify TNKS inhibitors with increased cellular potency, good selectivity, and drug-like properties.

    Chemistry

    For the preparation of the different scaffolds, individual synthesis routes were established as depicted in Schemes 1–3. Compound 4b, the tert-butyl-substituted analog of the initial hit containing the imidazo[1,2-a]pyrazin-8-one core was obtained in two steps (Scheme 1). Imidazol-2-carboxylic acid ethyl ester 2 was alkylated with 2-bromo-1-(4-tert-butyl-phenyl)ethanone to give intermediate 3. The ester moiety of 3 was transferred to the primary amide with ammonium acetate in acetic acid at elevated temperature, which cyclized spontaneously under these conditions affording 4b. Synthesis of compounds 7a–c with the isomeric imidazo[1,5-a]pyrazin-8-one scaffold is also illustrated in Scheme 1. First, imidazole ester derivatives 5a,b(23,24) were converted to their corresponding primary amides. Treatment of 6a (R = H) with 2-bromo-1-(4-tert-butyl-phenyl)ethanone produced an imidazo-pyrazinium salt, which after benzyl deprotection with imidazole yielded compound 7a.(22) Alternatively, 7b was obtained in one step by treatment of the primary amide 6b with the same 2-bromoacetophenone derivative under microwave irradiation at high temperature. This method was also applied for the preparation of compound 7c. In this case, reaction of 6b with 2,4′-dibromoacetophenone afforded 6-(4-bromophenyl)-3-methyl-7H,8H-imidazo[1,5-a]pyrazin-8-one, which was converted to compound 7c via Pd-catalyst carbonylation and the subsequent Grignard reaction of the methyl ester with methyl magnesium chloride. For the synthesis of the pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyrazin-4-one derivative 12, the key intermediate 10 was also assembled by a two-step approach. In this example, pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylic acid diethyl ester was alkylated with 2-bromo-1-(4-bromophenyl)ethanone and followed by cyclization under acidic conditions in the presence of ammonium acetate at high temperature. Transformation of the ester functionality of compound 10 into the methyl group of compound 11 was accomplished in three steps via lithium aluminum hydride reduction and conversion of the hydroxy group to the bromide using PBr3 followed by removal of the bromo substituent with zinc in acetic acid. Finally, the methyl ester function was introduced via Pd-catalyst carbonylation and transformed into a 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl moiety via a Grignard reaction, leading to the pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyrazin-4-one derivative 12.
    The synthesis of the pyrrolo[2,1-f][1,2,4]triazin-4-one derivative 16 with a nitrogen in position 1 of the bicycle started with the introduction of an amino group onto 5-methyl-1H-pyrrole-2-carboxylic acid ethyl ester 13i by N–N bond formation (Scheme 2). This reaction was performed with freshly prepared chloramine solution and potassium tert-butylate. The primary amine was then acylated with 4-bromobenzoylchloride in pyridine followed by transformation of the ethyl ester to the primary amide 14 with ammonium hydroxide solution in methanol. Under these conditions, compound 14 did not cyclize spontaneously but required treatment with polyphosphoric acid to form key intermediate 15, which was converted to the pyrrolo[2,1-f][1,2,4]triazin-4-one derivative 16 by functional group manipulations already described before. A similar synthesis strategy was applied for the preparation of pyrazolo[3,2-f][1,2,4]triazin-4-one derivative 21 containing in total 4 nitrogen atoms in the bicyclic core. For the introduction of the amino group onto the ring nitrogen of 5-methyl-2H-pyrazole-3-carboxylic acid ethyl ester 17, O-(diphenylphosphoryl)hydroxylamine was the reagent of choice. This transformation was performed in dimethylformamide in the presence of sodium hydride. Compared to the synthesis of compound 14, milder reaction conditions could be applied for the acylation of the primary amine with 4-bromobenzoylchloride. Interestingly, treatment of this intermediate with ammonium hydroxide solution in methanol not only provided the desired primary amide 19 but also delivered in parts the cyclized product 20, which were not separated. Instead, cyclization of the remaining primary amide was driven to completion by treating the product mixture with sodium hydroxide in 2-propanol at elevated temperature. Introduction of the hydroxy-dimethyl residue at the para position of the phenyl residue via carbonylation followed by the Grignard reaction were performed as usual. A similar reaction sequence was used for the preparation of thieno[3,2-d]pyrimidin-4-one derivative 25. Intermediate 23 was obtained by acylation of 3-amino-4-methyl-thiophene-2-carboxylic acid methyl ester 22 with 4-bromo-benzoyl chloride and then cyclized with ammonium hydroxide solution in methanol at elevated temperature. Finally, deployment of the established functional group transformations afforded the hydroxy-dimethyl-substituted compound 25.
    The various synthesis pathways for the preparation compounds 30a–g,i and 31–35 containing the favorable pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one scaffold are summarized in Scheme 3. The established routes assure the exploration of the structure activity and property relationship of the core substitution as well as diverse residues at the distal phenyl ring. Varying core substitution was achieved by either using substituted pyrrole-2-carbonitrile derivatives (13a,f–h) or pyrrole-2-carboxylic acid esters (13b–e) as starting materials, which were commercially available or prepared by literature methods.(25,26) Compound 13e was obtained from 4-fluoro-1H-pyrrole-2-carboxylic acid ethyl ester by fluorination with Selectfluor in acetonitrile under microwave irradiation. Alkylation of these starting materials was performed in DMF or acetone in the presence of potassium carbonate. While the yield of reactions with cyano-substituted pyrroles (13a,f–h) was low to moderate, conversion of pyrrole-2-carboxylic acid esters (13b–e) delivered intermediates 26 in high yield. Subsequent formation of the bicyclic key intermediates 28a–h by cyclization required different conditions depending on the functional group involved in the ring formation. Treatment of the pyrrole-2-carboxylic acid esters 26b–e with ammonium acetate in glacial acetic acid furnished the desired substituted pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-ones 28b–e at elevated temperature and long reaction times. On the contrary, saponification of the cyano-substituted pyrroles 26a,f–h in methanol in the presence of potassium carbonate, dimethylsulfoxide, and hydrogen peroxide at room temperature afforded the corresponding amides, which spontaneously cyclized under these conditions. It is worth noting that in the case of compounds 26a,f even after extended reaction times, the expected products 28a,f were accompanied by a non-aromatized impurity 27a,f, which fortunately could be isolated and successfully dehydrated with TFA in dichloromethane. Hence, for the conversion of intermediates 26g,h, a slightly modified procedure was used, which avoided the isolation of the individual cyclization products. Instead, the formation of the desired products was driven to completion by subsequent treatment of the obtained mixture after workup with TFA in dichloromethane and compounds 28g,h were obtained in good yield. Reduction of 28h (R1 = CO2Me) with lithium aluminum hydride in THF delivered compound 28i bearing a hydroxymethyl residue in position 6. The established reaction conditions for the carbonylation as well as the subsequent Grignard reaction were also applicable to the substrates 28a-g,i and delivered the final compounds 30a–g,i in moderate to good yields. As the substitution of the pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one scaffold had been largely mapped out, subsequent efforts were focused on improving the solubility of the so far most favorable derivative 30g. To this end, a set of diverse polar residues in the para position of the distal phenyl ring was introduced. Preparation of a close analog (31) of compound 30g was accomplished by functionalization of the terminal hydroxy group with excess ethane-1,2-diol under acidic conditions. In this context, intermediates 28g and 29g proved to be versatile substrates for subsequent functional group manipulations, which allowed the facile evaluation of the SAR at the para position of the distal phenyl ring. For instance, the methyl ester function of 29g could be transformed into a cyclopropanol moiety via a Kulinkovich reaction leading to compound 32. Further, compound 33, bearing a 3-hydroxy-oxetan-3-yl residue, was obtained though only in a low yield via lithiation of the bromo substituted intermediate 28g and subsequent treatment with oxetan-3-one. The racemic mixture of the diol derivative 34a,b was also prepared from intermediate 28g via the Suzuki reaction using isopropenyl boronic acid pinacol ester followed by dihydroxylation of the alkene with N-methylmorpholine N-oxide and catalytic amounts of osmium tetroxide. Both enantiomers (34a, 34b) were obtained by means of chiral separation. Finally, the racemic mixture of the homologous diol 35a,b was prepared in four steps starting with the Pd-catalyst cyanation of intermediate 28g in the presence of zinc cyanide. The nitrile was converted via the Grignard reaction to the corresponding methyl ketone, which was subjected to a Reformatzki reaction with excess ethyl bromoacetate and zinc in the presence of zinc iodide and bis(cyclopentadienyl)titanium(IV) dichloride in dioxane. The resulting β-hydroxyester was reduced to the racemic diol 35a/b, which was separated into the enantiomers (35a, 35b) by chiral separation.

    Structure–Activity Relationship of the Bicyclic Core

    The aim of the SAR study was to identify a new core scaffold with optimized tankyrase potency and high selectivity over PARP1 as well as favorable physicochemical and pharmacokinetic properties to deliver a suitable compound for in vivo studies. To this end, novel TNKS inhibitors were profiled using ELISA assays (TNKS1 and TNKS2) and their selectivity was determined in a PARP-1 assay. Cellular potency was assessed by detection of axin2 accumulation using a quantitative antibody-based ELISA-like readout. Early assessment of drug-like properties was based on solubility measurement, assessment of metabolic stability in mice and human microsomes. Most nicotinamide pocket inhibitors share a lactam motif, which formed key hydrogen bonds to Gly1185 and Ser1221. In addition, a π–π stacking interaction of the central aromatic core with Tyr residue 1224 was observed in many crystal structures of ligands, for example in complex with TNKS1. While the lactam pharmacophore would be conserved when modifying the anellated five-membered heterocycles of compound 4b, the impact on TNKS potency resulting from π–π stacking with Tyr and on selectivity within the PARP family was not easily predictable.
    Exploration of the SAR commenced with imidazo[1,5-a]pyrazin-8-one derivative 7a, in which compared to the initial hit scaffold, the nitrogen atom was shifted to the adjacent position of the five-membered ring (Table 2). This derivative was three times more potent in the biochemical assay than its matched pair compound 4b and showed sub-micromolar activity in the cellular assay while favorable microsomal stability and solubility were retained. Several studies about nicotinamide pocket inhibitors revealed that small residues like the fluoro atom or methyl group on the opposite side of the lactam motif significantly enhanced potency.(19,21) In order to prove the applicability to this scaffold, a methyl substituent was introduced in this part of the molecule. Indeed, gain of potency by this modification was remarkable and compound 7b was about 10-fold more potent than 7a inhibiting TNKS1 and TNKS2 in the single-digit nanomolar range (IC50 = 2.9 and 3.8 nM, respectively). In addition, this derivative showed excellent selectivity over PARP 1 (>7000-fold) and a promising cellular EC50 of 210 nM. However, an increase of lipophilicity (LogD = 3.3) by this methyl group deteriorated solubility and metabolic stability limiting further evaluation in pharmacology studies. In our optimization efforts of quinazoline type inhibitors, we discovered a hydroxy-dimethyl substituent at the distal phenyl ring as a key design feature resulting in potent TNKS inhibitors with a balanced activity and property profile.(19) In order to revoke the lipophilic nature of compound 7b, this residue was introduced. Albeit potency was slightly diminished by this modification, compound 7c remained a potent inhibitor of TNKS1 and TNKS2 with low double-digit nanomolar IC50 values (IC50 = 12 and 14 nM, respectively) and decent cellular activity. More importantly, lipophilicity was substantially decreased (LogD = 1.3) and solubility and metabolic stability liabilities of compound 7b were overcome. Furthermore, selectivity over PARP1 was largely retained (>1600-fold). Hence, the beneficial impact on the overall activity and property profile of compound 7c prompted us to preserve this hydroxy-dimethyl substituent as the distal phenyl decoration for all following examples of the scaffold hopping approach.

    Next, the positions of the nitrogen atom and the Me-substituent of the five-membered ring were swapped, resulting in pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyrazin-4-one derivative 12. By this modification, the inhibitory activities against both enzymes were slightly decreased, suggesting that this scaffold was in general well accommodated in the binding pocket, but the position of the CH3 group was suboptimal. This was in accordance with the observation that introduction of this CH3 substituent only slightly enhanced potency compared to the unsubstituted scaffold (data not shown). While 12 showed reasonable cellular potency and was metabolically as stable as compound 7c, aqueous solubility was reduced, which however was still in an acceptable range. It is worth noting that selectivity of this compound over PARP1 was decreased and a 3-fold split between the IC50 values of TNKS1 and TNKS2 inhibition was determined. In the fourth variant of the central scaffold, the nitrogen atom was introduced to the six-membered ring of the bicycle, providing a pyrrolotriazinone core. Given the fact that this version of the 5,6-fused ring system allowed the introduction of a methyl substituent in the favorable position, potent inhibition of both enzymes was anticipated. Encouragingly, compound 16 proved to be a tankyrase inhibitor with outstanding biochemical potency in the sub-nanomolar range (TNKS1 IC50 = 0.8 and TNKS2 IC50 = 0.9 nM) but more importantly also cellular potency was strikingly improved (EC50 = 28 nM). Even though this structural modification was accompanied by an increase of lipophilicity (LogD = 2.9) compound properties remained in an acceptable range and therefore compound 16 represented a suitable inhibitor for further investigation. In contrast, formation of the pyrazolotriazinone core of derivative 21 by combining the substructures of compound 12 and 16 almost obliterates biochemical as well as cellular activity. Interestingly, like for compound 12, a split between the IC50 values of TNKS1 and TNKS2 inhibition was observed for this derivative. Compound 25, which is structurally very similar to XAV939 comprising a thiophene ring, was determined to be a single digit nanomolar TNKS inhibitor with promising cellular potency (EC50 = 96 nM) and acceptable solubility, but this compound was the least PARP1-selective example among the studied chemotypes. Moreover, introduction of the thiophene ring was accompanied by an increase in lipophilicity, impairing intrinsic clearance in mice liver microsomes, which was determined to be rather high. Finally, intrigued by the prediction of a reasonable lipophilicity (cLogD = 2.5) for the pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one derivate 30g, this compound was synthesized. Encouragingly, 30g was determined to be another sub-nanomolar TNKS inhibitor (TNKS1 IC50 = 0.6 and TNKS2 IC50 = 0.9 nM) with the lowest EC50 value of cellular axin2 induction (EC50 = 16 nM) within the list of investigated scaffolds. Good selectivity over PARP1 (>1000-fold), acceptable solubility, decent lipophilicity (LogD = 2.3), and stability in mouse und human liver microsomes contributed to the overall favorable in vitro profile of this compound.
    In summary, the scaffold hopping approach yielded different chemotypes with a striking impact on biochemical TNKS potency (0.6–250 nM for TNKS1 and 0.9–2300 nM for TNKS2, respectively), cellular activity (16 nM–12 μM), and PARP1 selectivity. Due to its drug-like properties, compound 30g was selected from this set of diverse TNKS1/2 inhibitors for further characterization and SAR exploration. To this end, close analogs of 30g (30a–f,i and 31–35, Table 3) were prepared. The initial focus was directed toward determining the impact of the substitution pattern at the pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one core.

    In general, small substituents at both investigated positions were well tolerated, resulting in inhibitors with consistently single digit nanomolar IC50 values in the biochemical assays. Consequently, assessment of the potential of a compound for further characterization was focused on selectivity over PARP1, cellular activity, and property data. While solubility and metabolic stability were maintained, cellular activity of the unsubstituted derivative 30a was 7-fold lower compared to compound 30g, verifying the strong impact of the 6-CH3 group on potency. The 7-Cl derivative 30b was the least PARP1-selective example within this series, and in addition, solubility was marginal. Poor solubility of the 7-fluoro derivative (30c) and a drop of cellular potency (EC50 = 230 nM) of the 7-CH3 analog (30f) made these two compounds overall less attractive. Noteworthily, analogs with a substituent in position 7 of the bicyclic core consistently showed a split between the IC50 values of TNKS1 and TNKS2 inhibition (about 3–4-fold), which was in agreement with data obtained for compounds 12 and 16 bearing a CH3 group at the same position. A fluoro atom in position 6 (30d) was beneficial for selectivity and surprisingly also for solubility, but the cellular EC50 was 7-fold higher (EC50 = 113 nM) compared to the 6-CH3 derivative 30g. The 6,7-difluoro derivative 30e exhibited an unfavorable profile primarily due to its poor solubility. Introduction of a polar CH2OH group in the 7-position (30i) was beneficial for the compound properties with a favorable microsomal stability and a substantial improvement in solubility, which however came at the expense of cellular potency (EC50 = 580 nM). Further, residues at this position, e.g., a CF3 group, were not tolerated, diminishing TNKS inhibitory activity (data not shown). As the SAR of the pyrrolo-pyrazinone scaffold had been mapped out, subsequent efforts were focused on further improving the solubility of compound 30g, which remained the lead molecule of this series. To this end, the Me-substituted core was kept constant and a few substituents, partially with increased polarity, at the 4-position of the distal phenyl residue were surveyed. This endeavor commenced with compound 31, a close analogue of 30g functionalized with a hydroxyethyl group, which sparked our interest due to a similar cLogD value compared to 30g. This modification delivered a very potent TNKS inhibitor with improved solubility and good selectivity, which was as potent as 30g in the cellular assay. Only a decreased in vitro stability in mouse liver microsomes impeded further consideration of this derivative. While the profile of the hydroxy-cyclopropyl derivative 32 displayed some shortcomings (solubility, metabolic stability) compared to 30g, compound 33 containing a hydroxy-oxethanyl residue exhibited an attractive profile. This compound potently induced axin2 in the cellular assay (EC50 = 28 nM), showed acceptable stability in mouse und human liver microsomes and an improved solubility (60 μg/mL). Only, selectivity over PARP1 was reduced compared to 30g. The common feature of the following analogs (34a/b, 35a/b) was a second hydroxy group in a side chain of the substituent, by which lipophilicity was significantly decreased and excellent solubility was induced. The absolute configuration of these bis-hydroxylated analogs 34a/34b and 35a/35b is unknown and was therefore arbitrarily assigned. Although the optimization goal was achieved with both enantiomers of the 1,2-diol 34a/34b (solubility = 784 and 826 μg/mL, respectively), further consideration of these analogs was hampered by their decreased cellular activity. It is worth noting that in silico prediction of metabolic transformations of compound 30g using Metasite proposed an oxidation at the 6-methyl group and hence compound 30i as the main metabolite. However, metabolite identification studies (in vitro and in vivo) using LC–MS/MS elucidated that primarily hydroxylation took place at one of the methyl groups of the substituent at the distal phenyl moiety resulting in the formation of the 1,2-diol 34a/34b. Encouragingly, cellular activity of both enantiomers 35a/35b containing the homologues 1,3-diol moiety was not impaired by their increased polarity and both analogs were as potent as 30g in the axin2 induction assay (EC50 = 18 nM and 16 nM, respectively). Excellent solubility and acceptable metabolic stability in liver microsomes contributed to their overall appealing profiles. Interestingly, one enantiomer (35a) was determined to be more selective over PARP1 (≥770-fold) than the other one (≥162-fold).
    The SAR of the pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one scaffold had largely been explored and yielded molecules like 30g, 33, and 35a/35b with promising potency, good selectivity, and drug-like properties (solubility and metabolic stability). For the ultimate selection of the most suitable inhibitor for in vivo studies, this set of optimized compounds was investigated in the Caco-2 assay and in mouse pharmacokinetic studies to assess their applicability for po dosing.
    As shown in Table 4, compound 30g was highly permeable and had a low efflux ratio in Caco-2 cells, indicating low affinity to drug transporters. The results for compounds with increased polarity (33, 35a/35b) compared to 30g revealed that their passive permeability was impaired whereas efflux was increased, which presumably can be attributed to the additional acceptor or donor motives, respectively. In addition, 30g showed a reasonable in vitro–in vivo correlation of mouse CL predicted based on scaling of metabolic stability data obtained in liver microsomes. On the contrary, 33, 35a, and 35b showed high CL in mouse PK studies and were significantly underpredicted by the in vitro data, suggesting Phase II and/or extrahepatic CL pathways for these compounds (Table 4). Hence, based on the PhysChem and ADME characteristics, 30g was nominated as development candidate M2912 and further characterized in vitro and in vivo.

    Co-crystal Structure of M2912 (30g) and ITC Assessment

    We were able to solve the X-ray structure of M2912 (30g) in complex with TNKS1 (Figure 2) to a resolution of 1.95 Å (PDB code 7OCV). The observed interactions of the bicyclic core of this inhibitor are similar to known nicotinamide pocket binders containing a lactam moiety. Two hydrogen bonds to Gly1185 and an additional hydrogen bond to the side chain of Ser1221 by the lactam oxygen are formed. The pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one scaffold shows π–π stacking interactions of the central aromatic core with Tyr residue 1224. The methyl group at the 6-position points toward a lipophilic subpocket and is in a van der Waals distance to the phenyl ring atoms of Tyr1203, Tyr1213, and Tyr1224, forming a sandwich-like orientation. The terminal 1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl residue points toward the exit of the narrow nicotinamide pocket and the hydroxyl group does not make directed interactions to surrounding amino acids or water molecules. A detailed Watermap analysis of M2912 (30g) in complex with TNKS1 was performed (Supporting Information, Figure S1), which interestingly revealed a high energy water site at the position where the 6-methyl group is located. It was predicted that filling this water site would result in an increase of ΔG, which prompted us to investigate this by isothermal calorimetry (ITC) measurements.
    Substitution at the 6-position, especially introduction of a methyl substituent, was key for the substantial improvement of biochemical as well as cellular potency of compound M2912 (30g). We investigated the thermodynamics of inhibitor binding to elucidate what factor contributes to the observed potency enhancement. To this end, binding affinities and thermodynamic signatures of selected pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one derivatives from the optimization process were measured using isothermal calorimetry (ITC) (Figure 3; Supporting Information, Table S2). In general, all compounds displayed low nanomolar binding affinities that are fully consistent with respect to absolute and relative affinities (Supporting Information, Table S2) with the biochemical potencies measured for TNKS1 (Table 3). The binding affinity for M2912 (30g) is too tight to be exactly resolved and could only be estimated to be KD < 2 nM, matching with the sub-nanomolar biochemical potency. The thermodynamic signature for all compounds is dominated by the binding enthalpy in a range of −54 to −65 kJ/mol as driving force for the compound interaction. This observation is in line with the high number of direct interactions that the present scaffold is able to establish in the binding pocket. In Figure 3, the impact of different substituents at the 6-position of the central core is compared.
    The slightly higher enthalpic contribution for the unsubstituted analog 30a compared to the 6-F-substituted derivative 30d is compensated for by a slight entropic penalty, resulting in an almost identical binding constant (KD ≈ 6 nM) for both compounds. We surmised that the electron withdrawing effect of the 6-F substituent on the central core (30d) slightly attenuates binding enthalpy of this compound. In contrast, binding of the 6-CH3-substituted inhibitor M2912 (30g) has a strongly enhanced enthalpic contribution (ΔΔH = −15.8 kJ/mol), which can likely be attributed to additional interactions of the Me group with amino acid side chains (Tyr1203, Tyr1213, and Tyr1224) in this region and an electron donating effect strengthening the observed π–π stacking interaction of the central aromatic core with Tyr1224. However, the enthalpic gain is partly compensated for by a strong entropic penalty for this compound, which is indicative of a loss of conformational degrees of freedom that is larger than a possible entropy gain from displacing a localized water molecule as identified in Watermap analysis. Thus, the additional methyl group of M2912 (30g) may lead to a structural rigidification, e.g., by cooperative tethering interactions toward amino acid side chains. For the methyl substituted variant 35b, we should expect a comparable binding interaction and indeed saw a similar trend for the thermodynamic signature as for M2912 (30g) albeit with a smaller enthalpy gain. Docking analysis showed that the terminal dihydroxyl group of 35b exhibits greater flexibility, and different binding poses were identified where the ethyl-hydroxyl group interacts with Ala1202 or His1201, respectively. This could contribute to the lower binding enthalpy of this compound if cooperativity between binding interactions to the protein is lost. By contrast, the hydroxymethyl-substituted 30i has almost no enthalpy gain compared to 30a but still a substantial entropic penalty leading to a loss in binding affinity.

    In Vivo Studies with Compound M2912 (30g)

    The overall favorable profile of the selected candidate molecule prompted us to investigate pharmacodynamic (PD) effects in the colorectal cancer xenograft model DLD1. Tumor-bearing mice received a single oral administration of two different doses (10 and 30 mg/kg) of M2912 (30g), and PD biomarkers were assessed at three different time points (2, 8, and 18 h) in tumor tissue. A time-dependent increase of tankyrase and axin2 abundance was observed at 2 and 8 h, while levels declined back to baseline at the later time point of 18 h. The observed PD modulation is in line with recently reported results in a Colo320 xenograft model.(27) Consistent with the increase of axin2 level, the active β-catenin level decreased with a maximum effect at 8 h, indicating inhibition of Wnt pathway activity (Figure 7).
    Based on the cellular growth sensitivity data (Figure 5), we selected Colo320 and HT55 xenograft models to assess the tumor growth inhibition potential of M2912 (30g). Mice bearing Colo320 (Figure 8a) and HT55 (Figure 8b) were treated orally bi-daily with 20 mg/kg, which resulted in simulated exposure levels above the cellular EC50 for almost 18 h, suggesting sufficient target engagement (Supporting Information, Figure S5). M2912 (30g) demonstrated significant tumor growth inhibition of both human colon cancer xenograft tumor models, Colo320 and HT55, respectively. The regimen of 20 mg/kg bi-daily was selected based on a tolerability study in mice treated with 15, 30, and 50 mg/kg bi-daily for 20 days (Supporting Information, Figure S6a). 15 mg/kg bi-daily was tolerated without any treatment-related histopathological findings. The dose of 20 mg/kg bi-daily was tolerated based on clinical signs and body weight (Supporting Information, Figure S6b,c). In addition, plasma levels of l-citrulline, a well-characterized preclinical safety biomarker for gastrointestinal toxicity,(29,30) were not affected in mice treated for 17 days with 20 mg/kg bi-daily of M2912 (30g) (Supporting Information, Figure S7). However, at higher doses of 50 mg/kg bi-daily, adverse histopathological findings were observed in the gastrointestinal tract, which was previously described as the key target organ for tankyrase inhibitors.(31,32)

    A biochemical HTS against TNKS1 using a TR-FRET assay delivered a small series of imidazo[1,2-a]pyrazin-8-ones. Hit optimization through scaffold hopping yielded a potent tankyrase inhibitor with a pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one scaffold, which was further investigated by SAR exploration around the central core and the 4′-position of the distal phenyl residue. The exchange of the heterocyclic core was key to improving cellular activity compared to 2-aryl-quinazolin-4-one 1 and was achieved while maintaining low MW (282) and high binding efficiency (32.7). The systematic study resulted in the discovery of 3-[4-(1-hydroxy-1-methyl-ethyl)-phenyl]-6-methyl-2H-pyrrolo[1,2-a]pyrazin-1-one M2912 (30g), a very potent TNKS1/TNKS2 inhibitor with exquisite selectivity over other PARP family enzymes and favorable compound properties. This inhibitor potently modulated the Wnt/β-catenin pathway by elevating the levels of axin2 and tankyrase in DLD1 cells in a dose-dependent manner resulting in reduced cellular Wnt reporter activity. Supported by its favorable rodent pharmacokinetic profiles, M2912 (30g) exhibited statistically significant tumor growth inhibition in two APC mutant human colon cancer models after oral administration of a well-tolerated dose of 20 mg/kg twice daily. Overall, these findings together with a predicted human PK compatible with once daily oral dosing and the option of appropriate combination treatment regimens with other anticancer agents(27) qualify M2912 (30g) as a candidate to explore the potential and utility of tankyrase inhibitors as cancer therapeutic agents for the treatment of patients with Wnt pathway-driven colorectal cancer.

        Tankyrase inhibition causes reversible intestinal toxicity in mice with a therapeutic index < 1
        Zhong, Yu; Katavolos, Paula; Nguyen, Trung; Lau, Ted; Boggs, Jason; Sambrone, Amy; Kan, David; Merchant, Mark; Harstad, Eric; Diaz, Dolores; Costa, Mike; Schutten, Melissa

        Activated Wnt/β-catenin signaling is frequently assocd. with colorectal cancer. Wnt inhibitors, including tankyrase inhibitors, are being explored as potential anticancer agents. Wnt signaling is also crit. for intestinal tissue homeostasis, and Wnt inhibitors have been shown to cause intestinal toxicity in mice by affecting intestinal stem cells. This study sought to characterize the intestinal toxicity of tankyrase inhibitors, including reversibility, and to assess their therapeutic index.
