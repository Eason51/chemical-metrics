    Abstract

    α-Conotoxins exhibiting analgesic activity, such as Vc1.1, have been shown to inhibit α9α10 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs) and GABAB-receptor (GABABR) coupled N-type (CaV2.2) calcium channels. Here, we report two Vc1.1 variants, Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R], that selectively inhibit CaV2.2 channels via GABABR activation but exhibit reduced inhibitory activity at α9α10 and other neuronal nAChR subtypes compared with Vc1.1. Surprisingly, the analgesic activity of Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] was more potent than that of Vc1.1 when tested in partial sciatic nerve ligation injury and chronic constriction injury models. Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] exhibited either similar or tenfold higher activity of GABABR-mediated CaV2.2 inhibition but no activity at CaV2.2 alone; however, the mechanism of increased analgesic activity is unknown. The effects on analgesic activity and α9α10 nAChR of other Vc1.1 variations at position 9 and the N-terminus were also determined. Our findings provide new insights for designing potent inhibitors for GABABR-coupled N-type (CaV2.2) calcium channels.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs) are a diverse family of ligand-gated cation-selective channels that are widely distributed in the central and peripheral nervous systems.(1,2) Some nervous system diseases are involved with nAChR dysfunctions,(3,4) such as neuropathic pain,(5−7) Parkinson’s disease,(8,9) and schizophrenia.(10) Recently, several nAChR subtypes have been identified as potential targets for treatment of neuropathic pain with α-conotoxins.(11−13) A number of α-conotoxins, such as Vc1.1,(11,14) Rg1A,(15) MII,(16) and AuIB,(17) which are composed of 11–20 amino acids and two disulfide bridges and target α9α10, α3β2, or α3β4 nAChRs, have been shown to possess analgesic activity.
    Vc1.1 is the first α-conotoxin to exhibit potent analgesic activity and accelerate the functional recovery of injured neurons,(14) and has entered phase II of clinical studies.(18) Vc1.1 reversibly inhibits α9α10 nAChR and weakly inhibits α3β2 and α3β4 nAChRs.(19,20) Vc1.1 also potently inhibits N-type (CaV2.2) Ca2+ channels via GABABR activation.(21) The coadministration of a selective GABABR antagonist with Vc1.1 in rats completely abolishes its analgesic action, suggesting that the analgesic activity of Vc1.1 is mediated by GABABR activation. However, to date, α-conotoxins that exhibit potent analgesic activity are largely inhibitors of α9α10 nAChR and GABABR-coupled CaV2.2 channels.(22,23)
    In a primary screening of analgesic activity of Vc1.1 variants, modifications at position 9 affected Vc1.1’s analgesic activity, and the substitution of Asn9 with Arg improved the analgesic activity of Vc1.1 in rat partial sciatic nerve ligation (PNL) injury. Therefore, in the present study, we investigated the effects of charge and hydrophobic features at the N-terminus and position 11 on the analgesic activity of Vc1.1[N9R]. The structure–activity relationships of these variants on α9α10 nAChR and GABABR-coupled CaV2.2 channels were determined. The results showed that benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] apparently improved analgesic activity as well as Vc1.1[N9R] in rat PNL and chronic constriction injury (CCI) models when compared with Vc1.1, whereas their inhibitory activities at α9α10 nAChR were 29- and 172-fold lower than that of Vc.1. In contrast, the two Vc1.1 variants displayed similar or tenfold higher inhibitory activity at CaV2.2 channels in HEK293T cells coexpressing CaV2.2 and GABABR but not in those expressing CaV2.2 alone. The results demonstrate that the target specificity of the two Vc1.1 variants is significantly different from that of Vc1.1. However, the further replacement of Asp11 with Asn or acetylation at the N-terminus of Vc1.1[N9R] significantly reduced the inhibitory activity at rat α9α10 nAChR and GABABR-coupled CaV2.2. These findings provide new insights into designing potent inhibitors for GABABR-coupled CaV2.2 channels.

    Chemical Synthesis and Characterization of Vc1.1 Variants

    Linear Vc1.1 and its variants (Table 1) were folded individually in NH4HCO3 buffer and analyzed by HPLC. The typical folding results of Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] are shown in Figure 1; those of the other Vc1.1 variants are shown in Figures S8–S16. Linear Vc1.1 and all its variants folded to form major products. The resulting peptides were further purified and assessed with analytical reversed-phase HPLC, and the purities of the peptides were determined as being ≥95% (Figures S8–S16). The mass-spectral analysis showed that Vc1.1 and the variants were the correctly folded products (Figures S17–S25). The circular dichroism (CD) spectra of Vc1.1 and its variants in 0.01 M phosphate buffer showed some ellipticities around 208 and 220 nm (Figures S26–S34).     Effect of Residues at Positions 9 and 11 on the Analgesic Activity of Vc1.1

    The analgesic activity of Vc1.1 and its variants at positions 9 and 11 was assessed using rat PNL and CCI models. In the PNL model, ipsilateral Vc1.1[N9R] injection (0.3, 1.5, and 15 nmol/kg) increased the pain-threshold-elevation percentages (determined by the PWT protocol) by 16.7 ± 10.0, 47.2 ± 17.8, and 85.0 ± 30.0% (n = 8), respectively, in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 2A); these values were higher than those achieved by Vc1.1 at the same doses, which increased the pain-threshold-elevation percentages by (determined by the PWT protocol) 12.2 ± 14.4, 25.6 ± 19.4, and 51.1 ± 21.1% (n = 8), respectively. The same trend was observed in the rat CCI model (Figure 2B). Next, the analgesic effect of residues at position 11 on Vc1.1[N9R] was assessed. The replacement of Asp at position 11 with Arg, Ser, or Asn (Vc1.1[N9R,D11R], Vc1.1[N9R,D11S], and Vc1.1[N9R,D11N]) led to decreased analgesic activity, with pain-threshold-elevation percentages of 35.3 ± 20.0, 9.0 ± 22.5, and 40.8 ± 21.5% (n = 8), respectively (Figure S1), whereas the values for Vc1.1 and Vc1.1[N9R] were 58.8 ± 17.5 and 83.8 ± 29.5% (n = 8), respectively, in the same experiment. These results demonstrate that the residue at position 11 is also important for the analgesic properties of Vc1.1[N9R].

    Effect of N-Terminal Modification on Vc1.1[N9R] Analgesic Activity

    The N-terminus of Vc1.1[N9R] was further modified with benzoyl, acetyl, and other amino acids, and the analgesic activity was determined in PNL and CCI rats. When benzoyl was introduced to the N-terminus of Vc1.1[N9R], the analgesic activity remained higher than that of unmodified Vc1.1 (Figure 3). At a dose of 15 nmol/kg, benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] increased the pain threshold to 80.0 ± 17.3%, approximately 72% higher than that from Vc1.1 (46.4 ± 10.0%, Figure 3A). A similar effect was observed in CCI-model rats (Figure 3B). N-terminal acetyl (Ac) modification and Gly deletion (des-Gly) from the N-terminus of Vc1.1[N9R] both negatively affected analgesic activity, although the activities of these modified peptides were comparable to that of Vc1.1 in PNL rats (Figure S2).

    Potency of Vc1.1 Variants at nAChR Subtypes

    The two-electrode voltage clamp method was used to assess the effects of Vc1.1 variants on ACh-evoked currents of nAChR subunits expressed in Xenopus laevis oocytes. The inhibitory activity of Vc1.1 and several variants against rat and human α9α10 nAChRs is shown in Figures 4 and S3, respectively. The IC50 values obtained for the inhibition of rat α9α10 nAChR by Vc1.1, Vc1.1[N9R], and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] were 28.3, 4901, and 875 nM, respectively (Table 1). The replacement of Asn9 by Arg and its further benzoylation at the N-terminus resulted in 29- and 172-fold reductions, respectively, in the inhibitory activity of the peptide at rat α9α10 nAChRs. The IC50 values obtained for the inhibition of human α9α10 nAChR by Vc1.1[N9R], benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R], and benzoyl-Vc1.1 were 363 nM, 5.93 μM, and 12.50 μM, respectively (Table 1).
    Furthermore, rat α3β2, α3β4, α2β4, α2β2, α4β2, and α7 nAChR-mediated current amplitude were reduced by ≥50% in the presence of either 10 μM Vc1.1[N9R] or 10 μM benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] (Figures S4 and S5). These results indicate that the analgesic activity of Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] is unlikely to be due their inhibitory activity at α9α10 or other neuronal nAChR subtypes. Similarly, the replacement of Asn9 with Asp, the acetylation of [N9R]Vc.1 at the N-terminus and the substitution of Asn9 and Asp11 with Arg and Asn led to significant decreases in the potency of Vc1.1 at rat α9α10 nAChR (Figure 4B). At human α9α10, Vc1.1[N9R] was ∼3 times more potent than Vc1.1 (IC50 = 1 μM,(24)Table 1) .

    Potency of Vc1.1 Variants at GABABR-Coupled CaV2.2 Channels

    The inhibitory activity of the Vc1.1 variants on CaV2.2 channels in HEK293T cells coexpressing CaV2.2 with GABABR was also investigated. Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] inhibited CaV2.2 channels via GABABR activation to similar or greater degrees, with IC50 values of 4.9 and 0.2 nM, respectively, compared with 2.4 nM for Vc1.1 (Figure 5F). The effects of the three peptides were antagonized in the presence of 1 μM CGP55845 (n = 4), a selective GABABR antagonist. A representative effect of benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] on HEK293T cells coexpressing GABABR and CaV2.2 is shown in Figure 5E. In addition, benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] was inactive in HEK293T cells expressing CaV2.2 alone (Figure 5E). However, further substitutions of Asp with Asn, Ser, and Arg or acetylation or deletion of the first amino acid at the N-terminus resulted in a decrease in inhibitory activity (Figure 5G,H). Similar to benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R], single modifications of benzoylation at the N-terminus led to an increase in potency (Figure 5H). Furthermore, the three peptides, Vc1.1, Vc1.1[N9R], and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R], tested at concentrations of 10 μM, had no significant effect on either TTX-resistant (TTX-R, Figure S6) or TTX-sensitive sodium channels (TTX-S, Figure S7) in rat DRG neurons. These results suggest that Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] specifically inhibit GABABR-coupled CaV2.2 channels.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    According to the residue numbers of the intercysteine loops (−CC–(loop1)–C–(loop2)–C−), α-conotoxins are divided into several subfamilies (α3/5, α4/3, α4/4, α4/6, α4/7, and α5/5).(12) ImI and Rg1A belong to the α4/3 subfamily, whereas PnIA, PnIB, and Vc1.1 belong to the α4/7 subfamily. Mutagenesis studies of α-conotoxins ImI, Rg1A, PnIA, PnIB, and Vc1.1 have shown that loop 1 of α-conotoxin plays a key role in recognizing nAChR subtypes and that loop 2 is important to both activity and selectivity.(25−32) Mutations of Asp5–Arg7 and Asp11–Ile15 in Vc1.1 result in loss of activity against rat α9α10 nAChR.(33) However, replacing the polar residue Asn9 with a hydrophobic residue, such as Leu, Ile, Phe, or Trp, significantly increases the potency of Vc1.1 against both rat α9α10 and human–rat hybrid α9α10 nAChRs.(32−35) In the present study, substitution of Asn9 with Arg in Vc1.1, further replacement of Asp11 with Asn, and acetylation at the N-terminus of Vc1.1[N9R] significantly reduced the inhibitory activity against the rat α9α10 nAChR, which is consistent with a previous report that substitution of Asn9 with Lys reduced Vc1.1 activity.(34)
    Surprisingly, the substitution of Asn9 with Arg in Vc1.1 and further benzoylation at the N-terminus apparently increased the analgesic activity (Figures 2 and 3). These results suggest that the analgesic activity of Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] is not due to their reduced inhibitory activity against α9α10 nAChR. Similar examples are found with Vc1.1 post-translationally modified analogues, such as Vc1a, Vc1.1[P6O], and Vc1.1[E14γ],(32) which are equipotent at inhibiting α9α10 nAChR but lack analgesic activity in vivo.
    It has been reported that α-conotoxins Vc1.1,(21) Rg1A,(15,35) PeIA,(36) AuIB,(17) and Vc1.2(37) also inhibit GABABR-coupled N-type (CaV2.2) calcium channels. The discrepancy between the inhibitory activity of Vc1.1[N9R] and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] against α9α10 nAChR and analgesic activity led to the demonstration of their potent inhibition of GABABR-coupled CaV2.2 channels in HEK293T cells. Our results showed that Vc1.1[N9R] exhibited inhibitory activity against GABABR-coupled Cav2.2 channels similar to that of Vc1.1, whereas benzoyl-Vc1.1 and benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] were 1- and 10-fold more potent than Vc1.1 (Figure 5). To our knowledge, benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] is the most potent inhibitor of GABABR-coupled CaV2.2 channels. In addition, Vc1.1 and the two variants (10 μM) exhibited no significant effects on CaV2.2 expressed alone in HEK293T cells nor on TTX-R (Figure S6) and TTX-S Na+ channels (Figure S7) in rat DRG neurons. The effect of benzoyl-Vc1.1[N9R] was antagonized in the presence of the selective GABAB-receptor antagonist CGP55845. However, the analgesic activity of Vc1.1 and Vc1.1[N9R] is not strictly coupled to its potency at GABABR–CaV2.2 channels. The in vitro degradation of the two peptides (data not shown) appears to exclude the possible effects of pharmacokinetics in vivo, but the mechanism of increased analgesic activity is unknown.
    In conclusion, the substitution of Asn9 with Arg and further modification of the N-terminus with a benzoyl group improves Vc1.1 analgesic activity. Our findings will be valuable for designing new potent inhibitors for GABABR-coupled CaV2.2 channels.

    Experimental Section

    Animals

    Adult male Sprague–Dawley rats (200–220 g, 6–7 week old; Beijing Animal Center) were housed in groups of eight on a 12 h light–dark cycle (light cycle from 8 a.m. to 8 p.m.) at a temperature of 23 ± 2 °C and a relative humidity of 50%. Food pellets and water were available ad libitum. All experiments were conducted in accordance with the guidelines of the Beijing Institutes for Biological Sciences Animal Research Advisory Committee and conformed to the European Community directives for the care and use of laboratory animals.

    Reagents

    N-Fmoc-amino acids, HOBt, HBTU, DIEA, and TFA were purchased from GL Biochem Ltd. Wang resin and Rink resin were obtained from Tianjin Nankai Hecheng S&T Company. DTT was obtained from Promega. Baclofen and GABA were purchased from Aladdin Reagent. All other reagents were of analytical grade. Human GABAB1R and GABAB2R subunit cDNA clones were obtained from OriGene Technologies, Inc. The rat CaV2.2 channel α1B splice variant e37a and auxiliary subunits β3 and α2δ1 were obtained from Addgene.

    Peptide Synthesis and Folding

    Vc1.1 and its variants (Table 1) were synthesized using the solid-phase method on an ABI 433A peptide synthesizer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) using standard Fmoc chemistry and side-chain protection, as described previously.(38−40) The lyophilized crude linear peptide was folded in 0.1 M NH4HCO3 buffer (pH 8.2–8.4) at a concentration of 0.3 mg/mL in an open flask for 38 h.
    Following oxidation of the peptide, the reaction mixture was acidified (pH < 4.5) with acetic acid and loaded on a Nucleosil 25 × 250 mm preparative C18 column using a preparative HPLC pump (Waters Delta Prep 4000). The column was washed with 95% acetonitrile containing 0.1% TFA at a flow rate of 5.0 mL/min. The resulting peptide was further purified by semipreparative reversed-phase HPLC using a 10 × 250 mm Kromasil C18 column. The purity of the peptide was assessed by analytical reversed-phase HPLC using a Calesil C18 column (5 μm, 100 Å, 4.6 × 250 mm) with a 25 min linear gradient of 8–40% buffer B (0.1% TFA in acetonitrile) at a flow rate of 1 mL/min. The purities of all final products were ≥95% . Confirmation that the product was of the correct molecular mass was obtained by mass spectrometry on a ProFLEXTM-III MALDI-TOF spectrometer.

    Circular-Dichroism (CD) Spectra

    CD spectra of Vc1.1 and its variants were measured between 190 and 340 nm on Chirascan-plus CD (Applied Photophysics Ltd.). The peptide was dissolved in 0.01 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) to a final concentration of 35 μM. A 1 mm path length quartz cell was employed. Each spectrum represented the accumulation of three individual scans collected at 1.0 nm intervals at a bandwidth of 1.0 nm.

    Analgesic-Activity Test

    Under pentobarbital (48 mg/kg) anesthesia, PNL injury was established according to the method of Seltzer(41,42) and chronic constriction injury (CCI) was achieved using the CCI model of Bennett and Xie.(43) All behavioral testing was performed 7 days after surgery. A successful PNL model was defined as a 30–50% decrease in mechanical paw withdrawal thresholds (PWT) for the ipsilateral hind paw. Mechanical PWT were assessed using an Ugo Basile Analgesy-Meter.
    For pharmacological testing, all drugs were dissolved in sterile 0.9% saline. The qualified PNL rats and CCI rats (with 30–50% decreases in PWT) were randomly divided into peptide groups and a saline group with eight animals in each group. Saline, Vc1.1 (0.3, 1.5, or 15 nmol/kg or a single dose), or Vc1.1 variants (0.3, 1.5, or 15 nmol/kg or a single dose) was administered ipsilaterally close to the injury site at a midthigh region of each mouse in a volume of 200 μL. The number of groups in each experiment, including the saline and Vc1.1 control groups, was no more than seven. Paw-withdrawal thresholds were measured at 2–2.5 h following intramuscular administration. Data are expressed as mean pain threshold elevation percentages (PTEPs) ± SD, and PTEPs were calculated using the equation PTEP (%) = [(postdrug PWT – predrug PWT)/predrug PWT] × 100. Unpaired t tests were performed, and a p value of <0.05 was considered statistically significant.

    Two-Electrode Voltage Clamp

    cRNA preparation, oocyte harvesting, and expression of nAChR subunits and oocyte electrophysiology were performed as described previously.(38,39) Briefly, the Xenopus laevis oocytes were incubated in ND96 solution (96.0 mM NaCl, 2.0 mM KCl, 1.8 mM CaCl2, 1.0 mM MgCl2, and 5 mM HEPES; pH ∼7.3) containing 2.5 mM pyruvic acid sodium (Sigma) and antibiotics (100 U/mL penicillin and 100 mg/mL streptomycin, Sigma) at 18 °C. Oocyte recordings were performed 2–4 days postinjection at room temperature (∼22 °C). The oocyte was superfused with peptide solution until equilibrated (5–10 min). At high concentrations (1 μM or greater), 5.5 μL of a 10-fold concentrated peptide solution was directly pipetted into a static bath 5 min prior to the exposure of ACh pulses. Using nonlinear-regression analysis in GraphPad Prism (GraphPad Software), the concentration–response relationships were fit to the following equation: response (%) = 100/[1 + ([toxin]/IC50)n], where n is the Hill coefficient and IC50 is the antagonist concentration giving the half-maximal response.

    HEK293T Cell Electrophysiology
