    Abstract

    In preclinical and phase I and II clinical studies, 2′-deoxy-2′-β-fluoro-4′-azidocytidine (FNC) displays a potent and long-lasting inhibition of HIV-1 infection. To investigate its mechanism of action, we compared it with the well-documented lamivudine (3TC). Pharmacokinetic studies revealed that the intracellular retention of FNC triphosphate in peripheral blood mononuclear cells was markedly longer than that of the 3TC triphosphate. FNC selectively enters and is retained in HIV target cells, where it exerts long-lasting prevention of HIV-1 infection. In addition to inhibition of HIV-1 reverse transcription, FNC also restores A3G expression in CD4+ T cells in FNC-treated HIV-1 patients. FNC binds to the Vif-E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, enabling A3G to avoid Vif-induced ubiquitination and degradation. These data reveal the mechanisms underlying the superior anti-HIV potency and long-lasting action of FNC. Our results also suggest a potential clinical application of FNC as a long-lasting pre-exposure prophylactic agent capable of preventing HIV infection.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Since the approval by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 1987 of the first antiretroviral drug zidovudine, a nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor (NRTI), more than 30 antiretroviral drugs that target either viruses or host molecules to block different stages of the HIV-1 life cycle have been developed and used clinically. Since 1995, the use of combination antiretroviral therapy (cART) has substantially reduced morbidity and mortality related to HIV and has changed HIV-1 infection from a fatal condition into a generally manageable medical condition. Current antiretroviral treatments, however, need daily administration, and their long-term use can lead to cumulative toxicity. Drug-resistant viral strains reduced the efficacy of cART and create a major economic burden on individuals and medical care systems.(1) The development of anti-HIV-1 drugs with higher efficiency, lower toxicity, and longer-lasting effects could help to solve these problems.
    Clinically available NRTIs are 2′,3′-dideoxynucleosides (ddN).(2) Although they are effective in most of the drug-naïve patients, they are less useful with HIV mutants that can discriminate between ddN and the physiological 2′-deoxynucleosides (dN). These HIV mutant strains cannot take ddN into the active centers of their reverse transcriptase (RT), and thus, they selectively separate the incorporated ddN from their proviral DNA terminus.(3) Consequently, nucleoside drugs capable of inhibiting drug-resistant HIV variants must have a 3′-hydroxy group as the chain terminator of proviral DNA biosynthesis.
    2′-Deoxy-2′-β-fluoro-4′-azidocytidine (FNC) (1 in Table 1) is a novel NRTI developed recently in our laboratory.(3b,4) It was tested in phase II clinical trials, in which lamivudine (3TC) was replaced by FNC in a three drug combination, which also includes tenofovir and efavirenz (study number: GQ-FNC-201, 2017–2019), and was demonstrated to be safe and possibly efficacious. The superior potency of FNC was explored previously. FNC is a 4′-C-substituted-2′-deoxynucleoside (4′sdN) bearing a 3′-OH group. It mimics dN, and its 2′-fluoro substituent improves its stability in acidic media.(5) The 4′sdNs retain all functional groups of dN, and HIV can discriminate between a 4′sdN and dN only with difficulty. The 3′-OH group of 4′sdN is unlikely to be used by HIV for elongation of proviral DNA synthesis, and 4′sdN, once incorporated, can cause immediate chain termination of proviral DNA biosynthesis by blocking further addition of nucleotides.(3b) For these reasons, FNC shows potent activity against both wild-type and drug-resistant HIV strains in preclinical studies(3b,4) and has demonstrated desirable pharmacokinetics and safety profiles in the phase I study: GQ-FNC-2014-2; 2015–2016.

    In addition to its higher anti-HIV activity compared to conventional NRTIs such as 3TC,(3b,4) FNC also displayed a long-lasting effect on HIV-1 infection in our preclinical and phase I and II clinical studies (unpublished results). Although other anti-HIV drugs with RT binding affinity comparable to that of FNC have been reported, none have exhibited a similar anti-HIV efficacy. This suggests that FNC may also have additional anti-HIV pathways.
    The discovery of HIV-1 restriction factors has offered new and attractive opportunities for the development of adjunct therapeutic agents. A major strategy for new drug development involves targeting the interactions between host factors and HIV-1 proteins. One of these host factors is a cytidine deaminase, APOBEC3G (A3G). A3G can be encapsulated into the HIV-1 virions where it catalyzes the conversion of cytosine to uracil in the minus-sense single-strand viral DNA, thus causing G-to-A hypermutations in the subsequent plus-strand viral DNA and repressing HIV-1 replication. To antagonize the A3G-mediated HIV-1 restriction, the viral protein Vif recruits A3G into the E3 ubiquitin ligase complex composed of Cullin5, Elongin B/C, and a core binding factor subunit beta (CBFβ), thus inducing A3G degradation through the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway.(6) Due to the preferential recognition of the deamination motif, A3G-induced hypermutagenesis of HIV-1 proviral DNA reduces the infectivity of newly produced virions without inducing the viral evolution that may lead to drug resistance.(7) Consequently, targeting Vif or A3G or their interaction has become an attractive strategy for anti-HIV drug development.(7c,8)
    In this study, we uncovered the mechanisms of FNC-mediated potent and long-lasting HIV-1 inhibition. In vivo pharmacokinetic studies demonstrate that FNC could be rapidly absorbed and retained for a long time in rhesus macaque peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), rendering these HIV-1 target cells resistant to infection for an extended time. We also found that FNC restored A3G expression in peripheral blood CD4+ T cells derived from FNC-treated HIV-1 patients. Biochemical and cell-based assays showed that FNC specifically bound to the Vif-containing E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, preventing Vif-induced degradation of A3G. Together with the previous demonstrations of inhibition of HIV-1 reverse transcription,(3b,4) these data reveal a dual role of FNC in the prevention of HIV-1 infection, a direct antiviral effect blocking viral replication and an indirect antiviral effect of augmenting the level of the host antiviral factor, A3G. The long-term retention of FNC in target cells suggests the possibility that this small molecule can be used as a long-lasting pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP) agent for the prevention of HIV-1 infection.

    2.1. FNC Exhibits Potent HIV-1 Inhibitory Activity

    We have previously designed and synthesized a series of N4-substituted 2′-deoxy-2′-β-fluoro-4′-azido-β-d-arabinofuranosyl cytidines (2).(5b,9) We analyzed their anti-HIV activity using a vesicular stomatitis virus glycoprotein (VSVG) pseudotyped single-cycle infectious virus (HIV-luc/VSV-G) (Table 1). Based on infection of HEK293T cells, the EC50, the concentration that causes 50% inhibition of HIV-1 infection, was determined. The cytotoxicity of these compounds, represented by the concentration required to induce 50% cell death (CC50), was also evaluated by quantifying the viability of cells. As shown in Table 1, FNC (1), with an EC50 of 0.063 nM, displayed the most potent inhibition of HIV-1 infection among the compounds tested. Thus, FNC is 4700-fold better than the commercial drug 3TC at a 0.3 μM concentration. FNC also showed a low cytotoxicity, with a CC50 value above 25 μM (Table 1). N4-modification, the specific structural feature important for the anti-HIV activity of FNC, impairs the antiviral activity of FNC. For example, methylation at this position, giving compound 2a with an EC50 of 6.7 μM, led to a 100,000-fold decrease of the anti-HIV-1 activity of FNC. Incremental extension of the group or introduction of a cyclohexyl ring gave compounds 2b–2f and a complete loss of the antiviral activity (EC50 > 10 μM). The N4-hydroxylated analog (2g) displayed an anti-HIV activity of EC50 0.7 μM, but further N- and/or O-alkylation (2h and 2i) completely abolished the activity (EC50 > 10 μM). Among the compounds bearing hydroxyalkyl groups, the bis(2-hydroxyethyl) analog (2m) maintained moderate activity with an EC50 of 0.8 μM, but the corresponding cyclized morpholine derivative (2n) is inactive. Analogs containing aromatic rings in the N4 side chain (2o–2r) exhibit moderate antiviral activity. None of the tested 4′-C-substituted nucleoside analogs were obviously cytotoxic (CC50 ≥ 25 μM). Overall, among the 4′-C-substituted nucleoside analogs, FNC shows the most potent activity in inhibition of HIV-1 infection and low cytotoxicity. Of note, by performing in human CD4+ T cells, we have previously demonstrated the potent inhibitory activity of FNC on the replication of both wild-type and drug-resistant HIV-1 strains.(3b,4)

    2.2. Synergy between FNC and Other Antiretroviral Drugs

    By determination of the EC50 values of FNC in HIV-1-infected human CD4+ T cells treated with different antiretroviral drugs, we investigated whether FNC is synergistic with other antiretroviral drugs. We first tested FNC alone in human CD4+ T cells (Hut/CCR5) infected with pseudotyped HIV-luc/JRFL for 72 h by examination of luciferase activity. FNC potently suppressed HIV-1 infection with an EC50 of 0.0136 nM (Figure 1A) and had low cytotoxicity with a CC50 of 340 nM (Figure 1B). The selective index (CC50/EC50) is 25,000. We tested whether FNC has synergy with routinely used antiretroviral drugs such as dolutegravir, an HIV-1 integrase inhibitor, and rilpivirine, a non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor, (NNRTI) using similar assays. The combination index (CI) values for the dolutegravir/FNC (Figure 1C) or rilpivirine/FNC (Figure 1D) combinations at 50, 75, 90, and 95% inhibition of infection were no more than 0.1, indicating a strong synergy between FNC and these two anti-HIV drugs. These results confirm that FNC has potent HIV-1 inhibitory activity and show that it has strong synergy with other antiretroviral drugs.

    2.3. Rapid Accumulation and Long-Term Intracellular Retention of FNC in Rhesus Macaque PBMCs

    The pharmacokinetics of FNC was evaluated in rhesus macaques. Three 7–10 kg adult male rhesus macaques were treated with a single oral administration of 1 or 6 mg/kg of FNC or 20 mg/kg of 3TC. FNC was rapidly absorbed, with peak plasma levels of 196.5 and 2068.3 nM at 1 h after intragastric administration of 1 and 6 mg/kg dose, respectively (Figure 2A), while the plasma FNC dropped 320-fold to 6.5 nM at 24 h in the animal that was administered 6 mg/kg. In comparison, 3TC reached a high peak plasma level of 31,349.7 nM at 1 h after intragastric administration and dropped 330-fold to 93.57 nM at 72 h (Figure 2A).
    Failing to have found substantially better plasma levels of FNC over 3TC, we examined the cell distribution of these drugs. FNC, but not 3TC, could enter rhesus macaque PBMCs and form the active nucleoside triphosphate FNC-TP, which is retained in these cells for several days (Figure 2B), and had intracellular half-lives of 133.15 and 112.40 h in the 1 and 6 mg/kg dosed rhesus macaques, respectively (Table 2). In contrast, 3TC was mainly distributed in the plasma with a t1/2 of 13.5 h. The intracellular concentration of 3TC-TP was extremely low and was undetectable 24 h after a single oral administration (Figure 2B).

    We then tracked the tissue distributions of FNC. Sprague–Dawley rats were administered a single oral dose of 0.5 mg/kg of FNC by gavage, and then tissues and organs were excised at different times post-administration. FNC in the tissues and organs was quantified with LC–MS/MS. The results showed that the FNC derivatives were mainly distributed in the thymus, reaching a maximum concentration at 7–12 h post-administration (Figure 2C).
    These in vivo pharmacokinetic studies demonstrate that FNC can be rapidly absorbed and accumulated in tissues and PBMCs and retains a long-term intracellular retention.

    2.4. The Selective Entry of FNC into Target Cells Provides a Long-Lasting Prevention of HIV-1 Infection

    Following our demonstration of the entry and retention of FNC in PBMCs, we delineated the cell type-specific retention of FNC. Human PBMCs from healthy donors were isolated and incubated for 4 h with FNC. HIV-1 target cells, CD14+ monocytes, and CD4+ T cells were subsequently selected using antibody-coated magnetic beads and used for quantification by LC–MS/MS of the intracellular active form, FNC-TP. FNC was found mainly to accumulate in these HIV-1 target cells, and the retention of FNC-TP was 2–4 times higher than in other types of cells (Figure 3A).
    To investigate whether the retention of FNC in HIV-1 target cells can provide a long-lasting prevention of HIV-1 infection, human CD4+ T cells Hut/CCR5 were incubated for 4 h with different concentrations of FNC or 3TC as a control. The cells were then washed, incubated, and infected by HIV-luc/JRFL at different time points. HIV-1 infection was measured by the luciferase activity, and the inhibitory effect was calculated. In terms of prevention of HIV-1 infection, FNC was much more active than 3TC (Figure 3B), and 3TC lost activity more quickly than FNC. Inhibition of HIV-1 infection by 0.1 nM FNC was still observed even 72 h post-treatment, when 10,000 nM 3TC exhibited a much lower activity (Figure 3B). To quantitatively compare FNC and 3TC, we calculated the time needed to reach 50% reduction of HIV-1 prevention (T50%-prevention) based on a regression analysis and found that the T50%-prevention values of FNC were higher than those of 3TC at all concentrations tested (Figure 3C). The T50%-prevention values of 0.1 and 10 nM FNC were 68.6 and 123.1 h, respectively. In comparison, the T50%-prevention value of 3TC at 10,000 nM was 42.7 h. The differences are unlikely to be due to cytotoxicity of these drugs because FNC and 3TC both had no adverse effect on cell growth during drug treatment for 120 h (Figure 3D). Overall, these data demonstrate that FNC has a long-lasting prophylactic effect on HIV-1 infection.
    Non-specific activation of host cells can provide more susceptible target cells for HIV-1 infection,(10) and we investigated whether FNC could activate host cells non-specifically by incubating human PBMCs from healthy donors with a high concentration of FNC (100 nM) for 48 h. Subsequently, CD4+ T cells and CD14+ monocytes were gated and analyzed by flow cytometry. The surface expressions of CD25, CD69, and HLA-DR were detected to monitor the non-specific activation of these cells. The results showed that the treatment of FNC had little effect on the expressions of these markers (Figure S1), indicating that FNC does not have a non-specific activation effect on these HIV-1 target cells. In control cells, the expression of these markers was strongly upregulated by a mitogen PHA-P.
    Taken together, these data demonstrate that the selective entry of FNC into the target cells of HIV-1 provides a long-lasting prevention of HIV-1 infection.

    2.5. FNC Blocks Vif-Induced A3G Degradation through Its Interaction with a Vif-Containing E3 Ligase

    Targeting the Vif/A3G interaction represents a novel approach to the design of antiviral drugs.(8a,i) Having shown that FNC is retained in HIV-1 target cells, we investigated whether FNC could upregulate A3G expression to potentiate host anti-HIV activity. Three HIV-1-infected human individuals were recruited and were administered daily with FNC (trade name azvudine) tablets containing 4 mg of FNC for a total of 6 days (Figure 4A, upper panel). Blood was drawn at day 0, immediately before initiation of treatment, and on day 7, 1 day after completion of treatment, and CD4+ T cells were isolated to detect A3G expression by Western blotting. Before treatment with FNC, the expression of A3G was barely detectable, but after the 6 day FNC monotherapy, A3G levels in the T cells were markedly increased (Figure 4A, lower panel). Although this short-term treatment obviously failed to restore CD4+ T cell counts, FNC completely suppressed plasma viremia (Figure 4B).
    The HIV-1 accessory protein, Vif, antagonizes A3G-mediated HIV-1 restriction by recruiting A3G into an E3 ubiquitin ligase complex for degradation by proteasomes.(6) To determine if FNC can block Vif-induced A3G degradation, HEK293T cells were co-transfected with A3G-expressing plasmid pcDNA-A3G-Myc and the Vif-expressing plasmid pcDNA-Vif-HA and then treated with FNC for 24 h, using 3TC treatment as a control. The results showed that the co-expression of the two plasmids led to a pronounced reduction in A3G expression of HEK293T cells and that the treatment of the cells with the proteasome inhibitor, MG132, strikingly restored the expression of A3G (Figure 4C). Treatment with FNC, but not with 3TC, similarly blocked Vif-mediated reduction of A3G expression (Figure 4C). A fluorescence-based approach was used to confirm this observation. HEK293T cells were co-transfected with plasmids of pcDNA-A3G-eGFP and pcDNA-Vif-HA in the presence of FNC or 3TC. Similarly, Vif-mediated reduction of A3G expression was observed, as indicated by the decreased GFP intensity (Figure 4D, left panel). Treatment with FNC, but not with 3TC, blocked Vif-induced A3G reduction (Figure 4D, right panel).
    We then investigated the underlying mechanism by which FNC blocks Vif-reduced expression of A3G. Vif antagonizes A3G-mediated HIV-1 restriction by recruiting A3G into the E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, which is composed of Cullin5, Elongin B/C, and CBFβ, to induce A3G degradation through the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway.(6b,c,e) We examined whether FNC can interact with the E3 ubiquitin ligase complex using a CM5 chip-based Biacore T200 system and found that FNC binds to the Vif–CBFβ–ELOB–ELOC complex very quickly, reaching saturation in about 100 s (Figure 4E). This incorporation is stable, 36% of the FNC-containing complex still surviving after dissociation for 150 s, indicating slow dissociation between FNC and the E3 complex. The calculated dissociation equilibrium constant was 87 nM. In a parallel study, the Kd value of 3TC with the Vif complex was 1150 nM, indicating a much weaker affinity between 3TC and the complex (Figure 4E).
    Having demonstrated the association of FNC with the E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, we next examined whether FNC could inhibit A3G ubiquitination mediated by the complex. HEK293T cells were co-transfected with the A3G-expressing plasmid pcDNA-A3G-Myc, the ubiquitin-expressing plasmid pcDNA-ubiquitin-His, and the Vif-expressing plasmid pcDNA-Vif-HA for 24 h, during which cells were treated with or without FNC. The polyubiquitination of A3G was detected by Western blotting, which revealed a basal polyubiquitination in the presence of exogenous ubiquitin (Figure 4F, lane 2). Vif-induced A3G polyubiquitination and degradation were observed and were blocked by the treatment with the proteasome inhibitor MG132 (Figure 4F, lanes 4 versus lane 3). Notably, the treatment with FNC also reduced Vif-triggered polyubiquitination of A3G (Figure 4F, lanes 5 and 6).
    Taken together, these data demonstrate that FNC preserves A3G expression by being incorporated into the Vif/E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, thereby interfering with A3G ubiquitination and Vif-induced A3G degradation.

    2.6. Molecular Modeling Studies

    As known, molecular simulations can provide detailed structural insights into the molecular interactions. In the present work, molecular docking and molecular dynamics simulations were performed to predict the binding mode of FNC-TP with an HIV-1 reverse transcriptase and elucidate the structural-activity relationship shown in Table 1. As shown in Figure 5, FNC-TP fits well in the active site, and the complex is stabilized by extensive hydrogen bonding and salt bridge as well as van der Waals interactions. Specifically, the base moiety of FNC-TP forms three hydrogen bonds with the complementary base pair, dGMP, in the template chain. The negatively charged phosphate group of FNC-TP forms strong salt bridge interactions with Lys65, Arg72, and Lys220. Asp110, Asp185, and the carbonyl group of Val111 together with the TP group form a metal-chelating interaction network with the two Mg2+ ions. The azido group is well positioned and forms extensive van der Waals interactions with the side chains of Tyr115, Phe160, Met184, and the sugar ring from the adjacent DNA base. In addition, the terminal nitrogen atom of the azido group forms N···H–C hydrogen bond interaction with the side chains of Met184. The fluorine atom is involved in N–H···F hydrogen bond interaction with the side chains of Arg72. The hydroxyl group in the sugar ring is hydrogen-bonded with the backbone of Tyr115. The binding mode can help us understand why the other compounds were less potent than FNC. For example, compounds 2d, 2e, 2f, 2i, 2m, and 2n are less potent than FNC since the NR2 group cannot form hydrogen bond interactions with the oxygen atom in the complementary base pair, dGMP. While for the other compounds with an NHR group, which can be hydrogen-bonded with the oxygen atom, it is energetically unfavorable for the hydrophobic group to be positioned in the polar water environment. Therefore, all the other compounds were less potent than FNC.

    3. Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, we examined the mechanisms of action of FNC, a novel NRTI anti-HIV drug. In addition to having much higher anti-HIV activity than conventional NRTIs such as 3TC, FNC also displays a long-lasting effect on HIV-1 infection in our preclinical and phase I and II clinical studies. Although other anti-HIV drugs with RT binding affinity comparable to that of FNC have been reported, none of the drugs exhibited a similar anti-HIV efficacy. Our data reveal the indirect antiviral effect of FNC in augmenting the level of the host antiviral factor A3G, together with the previous demonstration of the direct inhibition in HIV-1 reverse transcription,(3b,4) and the dual role of FNC in the prevention of HIV-1 infection can explain its superior anti-HIV potency. Our data also show that FNC selectively penetrates the HIV-1 target cells where it exhibits long-term retention. These properties make FNC a potential drug of choice for a PrEP agent. FNC does not activate uninfected bystander cells and thus has diminished side effects that are commonly observed in other NRTIs and explains its low cytotoxicity. In addition to these advantages, FNC also displays strong synergy with other antiretroviral drugs.
    An unusual feature of FNC is its ability to target the interactions between host factors and HIV-1 proteins, thus augmenting its efficacy. A3G-induced hypermutation of HIV-1 proviral DNA inactivates the virus but fails to introduce viral mutations and subsequent drug resistance,(7) thus making A3G and its interaction with HIV-1 Vif desirable drug targets. Many small molecules directly targeting A3G, blocking Vif-ELOC interaction, or disrupting the assembly of the Vif-containing ubiquitin ligase complex have been reported to possess potent anti-HIV activity.(8b−h,k,11) Our recent results on the crystal structure of the Vif–CBFβ–CUL5–ELOB–ELOC complex(6c) can also facilitate the design of more attractive drugs that target the Vif/A3G interacting complex.
    Despite the absence of direct evidence, we obtained several lines of indirect evidence that FNC inhibits Vif-induced degradation of the anti-viral host protein A3G. First, FNC substantially promotes accumulation of A3G in CD4+ T cells from HIV-1-infected individuals; second, FNC but not 3TC directly interacts with the Vif–CBFβ–ELOB–ELOC complex; third, FNC greatly impedes Vif-mediated polyubiquitination and proteasome-mediated degradation of A3G in HEK293T cells. In view of the established mechanisms of A3G-mediated anti-HIV activity, our results suggest that FNC can activate A3G-mediated inhibition of HIV-1 infection by stabilization of A3G. Together with the selective entry into HIV-1 target cells and long-term retention within the cells, the FNC-restored A3G expression further enhances its efficacy. In addition, FNC has an advantage in that it can restore A3G expression more successfully than other clinically used antiretroviral regimens. The long-term (48 weeks) treatment of patients with cenicriviroc (CVC) or efavirenz (EFV) did not upregulate A3G expression, but a slight increase of APOBEC3A, another member of the APOEC3 family, was observed in the CVC treatment.(12)
    Although FNC also targets the Vif/A3G interaction, it clearly has mechanisms distinct from those of reported small molecules that stabilize A3G because it interacts with the Vif–CBFβ–ELOB–ELOC complex. In contrast to other known small molecules, FNC binding does not affect the assembly of the complex. Despite a lack of knowledge of the precise site on the complex where FNC binds, FNC appears to function by inhibiting Vif-mediated polyubiquitination of A3G and consequent proteasome-mediated A3G degradation. Collectively, our data suggest that the Vif–CBFβ–ELOB–ELOC complex as a whole may be a valid target and small molecules capable of manipulating its activity could be developed.
    In addition to defining a novel mechanism of FNC restoring A3G expression, another interesting finding in this study is the selective entry and the long-term retention of FNC in HIV-1 target cells. The latter provides a mechanistic basis for FNC to be used as a PrEP agent for HIV prevention.

    4.1. Compounds

    Compounds 1 and 2 were prepared from 1,3,5-O-tribenzoyl-2-deoxy-2-fluoro-d-arabinofuranoside according to our previously reported procedures.(3b,5b,9a) All the tested compounds possess a purity of at least 95%. Analytical HPLC was run on the Agilent 1260 HPLC instrument, equipped with an Agilent XDB-C18 column and UV detection at 270 nm. The eluent system was 40% MeOH in H2O; flow rate = 0.8 mL/min. The compounds were dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) for stock preparation and further diluted with RPMI-1640 cell culture medium (Gibco) to produce a working solution.

    4.2. Cells

    Human embryonic kidney cells HEK293T were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) (Gibco) containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (Gibco) and 100 U/mL penicillin (Invitrogen) and 100 μg/mL streptomycin (Invitrogen). The human CD4+ T-lymphocyte cell line Hut/CCR5 was provided by Li Wu (Ohio State University, USA). Human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were purchased from the Blood Center of Shanghai, China, and PBMCs were isolated from the buffy coats of healthy donors by Ficoll density gradient centrifugation. CD14+ monocytes and CD4+ T cells were further isolated from PBMCs using anti-CD14- or CD4-specific antibody-coated magnetic beads (Miltenyi Biotec) according to manufacturer’s instructions. Hut/CCR5, CD4+ T cells, and CD14+ monocytes were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS)(Hyclone) with 100 U/mL penicillin and 100 μg/mL streptomycin.

    4.3. Plasmids

    The plasmid expressing APOBEC3G (pcDNA-APOBEC3G-Myc) was provided by Yong-Hui Zheng (The Michigan State University, USA), and the gene of APOBEC3G was also subcloned into pcDNA-eGFP at the carboxyl-terminus to construct the plasmid pcDNA-APOBEC3G-eGFP; the HIV-1 Vif-expressing plasmid (pcDNA-Vif-HA) was provided by Li Wu (Ohio State University, USA), and pcDNA-ubiquitin-His was a gift from Ke Lan (Wu Han University, China).

    4.4. Viral Stocks

    Calcium phosphate-mediated transfection of HEK293T cells was used to generate virus stock. Pseudotyped single-cycle infectious HIV-luc/VSV-G or HIV-luc/JRFL was co-transfected with the luciferase reporter HIV-1 proviral plasmid pLAI-Δ-env-luc and the expression plasmid vesicular stomatitis virus G (VSV-G) protein or HIV-1-JRFL Env. Plasmids were kindly provided as gifts from Li Wu (Ohio State University, USA). Harvested supernatants of transfected cells that contained viral particles were filtered and titrated with p24gag capture enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). The HIV-1 p24gag-specific monoclonal antibodies were kindly provided as gifts by Yong-Tang Zheng (Kunming Institute of Zoology, Chinese Academy of Sciences, China).

    4.5. Anti-HIV Activity Assay

    Hut/CCR5 cells (0.5 × 105) were infected with HIV-luc/JRFL (1 ng p24Gag), or HEK293T cells (1 × 105) were infected with HIV-luc/VSV-G (1 ng p24Gag), in the presence of different concentrations of compounds for 3 h. After washing, the cells were further cultured for an additional 72 h in the presence of the same concentrations of compounds. Cells were harvested and lysed, and viral infection was measured by detecting luciferase activity using the Luciferase assay system (Promega). The concentration of compounds inhibiting 50% of HIV-1 infection (EC50) was determined from the dose response curves. The cell viability with compound treatment was measured by MTT colorimetric assay,(13) and the concentration of compounds inducing 50% cytotoxicity (CC50) was calculated from the dose response curves.
    To test the synergy of anti-HIV-1 activity of combinations of compounds, FNC was combined with either dolutegravir or rilpivirine in different ratios based on the calculated EC50 value of each compound. The software of Chou and Talalay and CompuSyn (ComboSyn Inc., Paramus, New Jersey, USA) was used for quantification of synergy by calculating the combination index (CI). The CI value offers a quantitative definition of antagonism (CI > 1), additive effect (CI = 1), weak synergism (0.7 < CI < 1), moderate synergism (0.3 < CI < 0.7), and strong synergism (CI < 0.3) with drug combinations.
    To detect the long-lasting prevention effect, different concentrations of FNC (0.1, 1, and 10 nM) or 3TC (1,000 and 10,000 nM) were incubated with Hut/CCR5 cells (1 × 106) at 37 °C for 4 h. After washing and removal of cell-free drugs, the cells were further incubated, and at the indicated times, 0.5 × 105 cells were used for infection with HIV-luc/JRFL (1 ng p24Gag) for an additional 72 h; and viral infection was then determined by detection of luciferase activity. At the same time points, the cell number was counted by microscopy. The inhibition of HIV-1 infection was analyzed, and the time necessary for 50% reduction of HIV-1 prevention (T50%-prevention) was calculated based on the regression curve of HIV-1 prevention.

    4.6. Ethics Statement and Cohort of Patients

    Three HIV-1-infected antiretroviral treatment–naïve human individuals were recruited. These recipients have a plasma viral load higher than 1000 copies/mL and a CD4+ T cell count greater than 200 cells/mm3. Informed consent was obtained, and experimental protocols were approved by the Medical Ethics Review Committee of Zhengzhou Sixth People’s Hospital. All experiments were performed in accordance with relevant national guidelines and regulations. CD4+ T cell counts were analyzed by flow cytometry, and HIV-1 viral loads were measured by a polymerase chain reaction (PCR) (Cobas Amplicor, Roche, Basel, Switzerland).
    Each day for six consecutive days, the recipients were administered 4 tablets of azvudine, each containing 1 mg of FNC. At day 0 (immediately before initiation of treatment) and day 7 (1 day after treatment completion), 20 mL of peripheral blood was collected, and the peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated with Ficoll-Paque density gradient centrifugation (GE Healthcare Life Sciences). CD4+ T cells were further purified from PBMCs using anti-CD4 antibody-coated magnetic beads (Miltenyi Biotec). Cells were lysed with buffer [50 mM HEPES (4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1-piperazineethanesulfonic acid) pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl (sodium chloride), 0.5 mM EGTA [ethylene glycol-bis(β-aminoethyl ether)-N,N,N′,N′-tetraacetic acid], 1% protease inhibitor cocktail (Sigma), 1 mM sodium orthovanadate, 1 mM NaF (sodium fluoride), 1% (vol/vol) Triton X-100, and 10% (vol/vol) glycerol] for 1 h on ice. The endogenous expression of APOBEC3G was detected by Western blotting.

    4.7. Pharmacokinetic Studies in Rhesus Macaque Monkeys and Sprague–Dawley Rats

    Three 7–10 kg adult male rhesus macaques (Macaca mulatta) were provided by Henan Normal University. The study was approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Henan Normal University. Animals were housed in accordance with the Association for Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care International Standards. Animals were treated with a single oral administration of FNC at 1 or 6 mg/kg or lamivudine at 20 mg/kg. Peripheral blood (20 mL) was collected at 1, 6, 24, 72, 120, and 168 h after administration. FNC was dissolved in distilled water at a concentration of 1 or 6 mg/mL. Lamivudine was suspended in 0.5% (w/v) carboxyl methyl cellulose sodium aqueous solution at a concentration of 20 mg/mL. All blood samples were centrifuged at 1000g for 10 min, and the plasma was separated and stored at −80 °C for FNC or 3TC analysis. PBMCs were isolated by Ficoll density gradient centrifugation and used for FNC-TP or 3TC-TP analysis.
    For quantification of drugs in plasma, a 50 μL aliquot of plasma was pipetted into a 1.5 mL capped polypropylene tube, and then, 50 μL of internal standard solution (50 ng/mL of 3TC) and 200 μL of methanol were added and the mixture was vortexed for 30 s. After centrifuging at 16,000g for 5 min, 250 μL of supernatant was transferred into a 10 mL glass tube and evaporated to dryness under a steam of nitrogen at 50 °C. The residue was reconstituted with 250 μL of the mobile phase and vortexed briefly. After centrifuging again at 16,000g for 5 min at room temperature, the supernatant was analyzed by liquid chromatography-triple quadrupole mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS) as previously reported.(4) Quantification of 3TC was performed in the selected-reaction monitoring (SRM) mode with the transitions of m/z of 230.0 → 112.0.
    For quantification of drugs in PBMCs, these isolated PBMCs from animals were treated with the ultrasonic cell disintegrator and centrifuged at 16,000g for 5 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was mixed with three volumes of acetonitrile by vortexing for 30 s, and then, one volume of water was added and mixed again. After centrifuging at 16,000g for 5 min, the supernatant was evaporated to dryness under a steam of nitrogen at 40 °C. The residue was reconstituted with 500 μL of the mobile phase and briefly vortexed. After centrifuging at 16,000g for 5 min at room temperature, the supernatant was analyzed by LC–MS/MS. The HPLC was a Thermo Fisher ACCELA LC system. Chromatographic separation was performed on a Phenomenex Luna PFP column (100 mm × 2.0 mm; 3 μm; Kyoto, USA). The mobile phase, consisting of a mixed aqueous solution of methanol–ammonia (0.1%, pH 10) (10:90, v/v), was delivered at a flow rate of 0.18 mL/min. The column temperature was maintained at 30 °C. The injection volume was 10 μL. Detection was achieved with a Thermo Fisher TSQ QUANTUM ULTRA triple quadrupole mass spectrometer with an ESI interface in the negative-ion mode. The parameter settings were spray voltage, 2500 V; capillary temperature, 350 °C; sheath gas (nitrogen), 40 arbitrary units; auxiliary gas (nitrogen), 10 arbitrary units; and vaporizer temperature, room temperature. Detection and quantification of the compounds were performed in the selected-reaction monitoring (SRM) mode with the transitions of m/z of 524.7 → 158.7 and m/z of 468.0 → 159.0 for FNC-TP and 3TC-TP, respectively.
    For delineation of FNC distribution in tissues or organs, 56 healthy Sprague–Dawley rats, 50% female and 50% male, were randomly divided into seven groups with eight rats in each group. Rats were administered a single dose of 0.5 mg/kg by gavage. The rats were sacrificed at 1, 3, 7, 12, 24, 48, and 72 h post-administration, and the tissues and organs, including the heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidneys, stomach, intestines, thymus, brain, and testicles (or ovaries), were excised to prepare tissue homogenates. Rat tissue homogenates (100 μL) were added with 100 μL of internal standard solution (500 ng/mL 3TC) and 400 μL of methanol; then, the samples were mixed well on vortex for 30 s and centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 5 min. Supernatants (400 μL) were harvested to 10 mL in a round-bottom glass tube and dried under nitrogen flow at 50 °C. Samples were then dissolved with 100 μL of mobile phase solution, vortexed for 60 s, transferred to a 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube, and centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 5 min; finally, 10 μL supernatants were collected for LC-MS/MS analysis. A chromatographic column YMC-Pack Pro.C18, 3 μm, 150 mm × 4.6 mm i.d., was used; the mobile phase was methanol (containing 0.3% formic acid) and 10 mM ammonium acetate (containing 0.3% formic acid) in a 25:75 (V/V) ratio; a flow rate of 0.3 mL/min, a column temperature of 30 °C, a volume of injection of 10 μL, electrospray ionization (ESI) in the positive-ion mode, a capillary voltage of 3.5 kV, nitrogen for sheath gas with a pressure of 40 Arb, nitrogen for auxiliary gas with a pressure of 10 Arb, an atomizing chamber at room temperature, and selective monitoring of the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) were also utilized. The transition for FNC is 287 → 112, and for the 3TC is 230 → 112.

    4.8. Assay of Drug Distribution in Human Cell Subsets

    Human PBMCs from healthy donors were isolated and incubated with FNC (350 nM) for 4 h. CD14+ monocytes and CD4+ T cells were subsequently isolated by using antibody-coated magnetic beads with positive selection. The intracellular active form FNC-TP from CD14+ monocytes, CD4+ T cells, and the remaining cells were quantified by LC–MS/MS assay.

    4.9. Assay of A3G Expression

    A3G expression was detected by both Western blotting and flow cytometry. For Western blotting, HEK293T cells were transfected by lipofectamine 2000 (Life Technologies) with pcDNA-APOBEC3G-Myc and/or pcDNA-Vif-HA for 24 h, and cells were further treated with FNC (10 nM) or 3TC (1000 nM) for an additional 48 h; some cells were treated with the proteasome inhibitor MG132 (10 μM) 16 h prior to harvesting. Cells were harvested and lysed for Western blotting to detect the expression of A3G (Myc) and exogenous Vif (HA), and GAPDH was used for loading normalization. The antibodies below were used: rabbit polyclonal antibodies anti-HA or anti-Myc antibodies (Abcam), mouse anti-His monoclonal antibody (Abmart), and HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies against a mouse or rabbit (Sigma-Aldrich). For detection of the endogenous A3G in patient CD4+ T cells, the specific mouse monoclonal antibody antihuman A3G (Bio-Rad) was used for Western blotting.
    For flow cytometry detecting A3G expression, HEK293T cells were transfected with pcDNA-APOBEC3G-eGFP and/or pcDNA-Vif-HA for 24 h, and cells were further treated with FNC (10 nM) or 3TC (1,000 nM) for an additional 48 h; some cells were treated with the proteasome inhibitor MG132 (10 μM) 16 h prior to harvesting. A3G expression is measured by detecting GFP production with a Fortessa flow cytometer (BD Pharmin-Gen) and analyzed with the FlowJo 7.6.1 software.

    4.10. Assay for A3G Ubiquitination

    HEK293T cells were co-transfected by lipofectamine 2000 (Life Technologies) with pcDNA-APOBEC3G-Myc, pcDNA-Vif-HA, and pcDNA-ubiquitin-His for 24 h, and cells were further treated with FNC (3.5 or 35 nM) for an additional 48 h; some cells were treated with the proteasome inhibitor MG132 (10 μM) 16 h prior to harvesting. Cells were harvested and lysed for Western blotting to detect the expression of His-ubiquitin with immunostaining with anti-His antibodies (Abmart).

    4.11. Kinetic Analysis of the Association of FNC with the Vif–CBFβ–ELOB–ELOC Complex

    A Biacore T200 system (CM5 chip) was used to detect the kinetic parameters of small molecules binding to proteins. Amino conjugation of the Vif complex to the CM5 chip was performed using GE’s original amino conjugation kit (item no. Br-1000-50). The running buffer used was HBS-T containing 0.01 M HEPES pH 7.4, 0.15 M sodium chloride, and 0.005% Tween-20. The Vif–CBFβ–ELOB–ELOC complex was first diluted to 343 μg/mL with HBS-T solution and then diluted to 40 μg/mL with sodium acetate solution (GE original) at pH 4.5. When coupling, the contact time was 600 s, and the flow rate was 10 μL/min. For kinetic analysis of the FNC with the Vif–CBFβ–ELOB–ELOC complex, FNC was diluted from 200 μM with PBS-T solution to 13 concentration gradients for detection; during detection and analysis, the contact time was 180 s, the flow rate was 20 μL/min, and the dissociation time was 300 s; after detection, five contagious concentrations were selected for dynamic analysis. Since FNC solution contains DMSO as an organic solvent, the solution calibration was carried out, and a reference channel was arranged to minimize errors. All sample solutions with CM5 chips were filtered with a 0.22 μm membrane, and the buffer was degassed.

    4.12. Detection of Non-specific Activation of Human Cells

    Isolated PBMCs from healthy human individuals were incubated with FNC (100 nM) or phytohemagglutinin-P (PHA-P) (Sigma-Aldrich) (5 μg/mL) for 48 h, in the presence of recombinant interleukin (IL)-2 (20 IU/mL). Cells were harvested and immunostained with anti-CD3, anti-CD4, anti-CD14, anti-CD25, anti-CD69, and anti-HLA-DR antibodies (eBioscience) and detected using a Fortessa flow cytometer (BD Pharmingen) and analyzed with the assistance of the FlowJo 7.6.1 software.

    4.13. Statistical Analysis

    Statistical analysis was performed using a paired t-test with SigmaStat 2.0 (Systat Software, San Jose, CA, USA).

    4.14. Computational Details

    Molecular docking and molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were performed to construct and optimize the binding complex structure of FNC-TP with an HIV-I reverse transcriptase (PDB ID 5J2M(14)). In order to simulate appropriate base pair interactions (A-T, G-C), the original base T paired with EFdA in the crystal structure of 5J2M was modified to G. Then, the bioactive form of nucleoside analogs, i.e., FNC-TP, was docked into the possible active site of the enzyme by using the AutoDock 4.2 program.(15) After the initial model was prepared, the ionizable residues were set to the standard protonated or deprotonated states at the physiological conditions (pH = ∼7.4). Then, the complex structure was solvated in a rectangular box of TIP3P water molecules with a minimum solute–wall distance of 15 Å. Additional Na+ was added in the solvent as counter ions to neutralize the whole system. In the following, a series of energy minimizations were carried out by using the Sander module of the Amber 16 program(16) with a non-bonded cutoff of 10 Å and a conjugate gradient energy minimization method. Subsequently, the two systems were gradually heated from 10 to 298.15 K by a weak-coupling method with a constraint force constant of 5 kcal mol–1 Å–2 on the solute. The constraint force constant was gradually decreased during a period of 600 ps equilibration and finally removed for the production MD simulation. To obtain a stable MD trajectory, the production MD simulation was kept running 5.0 ns in the NTP ensemble, at T = 298.15 K. Afterward, the representative snapshots of the complex structure were extracted from the production MD.

        Structural basis of HIV inhibition by translocation-defective RT inhibitor 4'-ethynyl-2-fluoro-2'-deoxyadenosine (EFdA)
        Salie, Zhe Li; Kirby, Karen A.; Michailidis, Eleftherios; Marchand, Bruno; Singh, Kamalendra; Rohan, Lisa C.; Kodama, Eiichi N.; Mitsuya, Hiroaki; Parniak, Michael A.; Sarafianos, Stefan G.
        Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America (2016), 113 (33), 9274-9279CODEN: PNASA6; ISSN:0027-8424. (National Academy of Sciences)
        4'-Ethynyl-2-fluoro-2'-deoxyadenosine (EFdA) is the most potent nucleoside analog inhibitor of HIV reverse transcriptase (RT). It retains a 3'-OH yet acts as a chain-terminating agent by diminishing translocation from the pretranslocation nucleotide-binding site (N site) to the posttranslocation primer-binding site (P site). Also, facile misincorporation of EFdA-monophosphate (MP) results in difficult-to-extend mismatched primers. To understand the high potency and unusual inhibition mechanism of EFdA, we solved RT crystal structures (resolns. from 2.4 to 2.9 Å) that include inhibition intermediates (i) before inhibitor incorporation (catalytic complex, RT/DNA/EFdA-triphosphate), (ii) after incorporation of EFdA-MP followed by dT-MP (RT/DNAEFdA-MPSUP°P/SUP°buldT-MPSUP°N/SUP°), or (iii) after incorporation of two EFdA-MPs (RT/DNAEFdA-MPSUP°P/SUP°bulEFdA-MPSUP°N/SUP°); (iv) the latter was also solved with EFdA-MP mismatched at the N site (RT/DNAEFdA-MPSUP°P/SUP°bulEFdA-MPtSUP°N/SUP°). We report that the inhibition mechanism and potency of EFdA stem from interactions of its 4'-ethynyl at a previously unexploited conserved hydrophobic pocket in the polymerase active site. The high resoln. of the catalytic complex structure revealed a network of ordered water mols. at the polymerase active site that stabilize enzyme interactions with nucleotide and DNA substrates. Finally, decreased translocation results from favorable interactions of primer-terminating EFdA-MP at the pretranslocation site and unfavorable posttranslocation interactions that lead to obsd. localized primer distortions.
