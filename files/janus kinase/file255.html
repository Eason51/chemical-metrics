    Abstract

    Exportin-1 (also named as CRM1) plays a prominent role in autoimmune disorders and has emerged as a potential therapeutic target for colitis. Here we report on the rational structure-based discovery of a small-molecule antagonist of exportin-1, LFS-829, with low-range nanomolar activities. The co-crystallographic structure, surface plasmon resonance binding assay, and cell-based phenotypic nuclear export functional assay validated that exportin-1 is a key target of LFS-829. Moreover, we demonstrated that the C528S mutation or the knockdown on exportin-1 can abolish the cellular activities of LFS-829. Strikingly, oral administration of LFS-829 can significantly reverse the pathological features of colitis model mice. We revealed that LFS-829 can attenuate dual NF-κB signaling and the Nrf2 cytoprotection pathway via targeting exportin-1 in colitis mice. Moreover, LFS-829 has a very low risk of cardiotoxicity and acute toxicity. Therefore, LFS-829 holds great promise for the treatment of colitis and may warrant translation for use in clinical trials.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The incidence of inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD), including ulcerative colitis (UC) and Crohn’s disease (CD), has been increasing very rapidly during the past decade worldwide, affecting more than two million individuals in North America, more than three million individuals in Asia, and around two million individuals in Europe.(1) The chronic inflammatory disorders of colitis also harbor a huge risk of colorectal cancer development.(2) Unfortunately, to date, there is no known cure for colitis, and available first-line treatment options are aimed toward controlling symptoms for mild patients.(3) Therefore, new therapeutic strategies are urgently needed to combat colitis.
    Exportin-1 (also named as CRM1) is responsible for the nuclear export of critical transcription factors and tumor suppressor proteins.(4−8) There is a considerable amount of evidence implicating that the dysregulation of CRM1 contributes to the pathogenesis of a panel of human cancer and autoimmune disorders. In particular, prior reports demonstrated that CRM1 is highly upregulated in the intestinal epithelial cells (IEC) of both CD patients and model animals.(9) Therefore, CRM1 has emerged as a viable target for developing new therapeutics against colitis. Previously, we found that sulforaphene, the major active chemical component from the seeds of Raphanus sativus, can alleviate colitis in model mice and protect the epithelial barrier by covalently targeting a conserved cysteine residue in the NES (nuclear export signal) binding cleft of CRM1.(5,10)
    Herein, we reported the rational structure-based discovery and extensive evaluation of the synthetic analogue of sulforaphene, LFS-829, in colitis model mice. Importantly, the co-crystallographic structure, cell-based phenotypic nuclear export functional assay, and SPR (surface plasmon resonance) binding assay firmly establish that CRM1 is a major cellular target of LFS-829. Furthermore, oral treatment of LFS-829 can reverse the pathological development of colitis in model mice. Our proteomics analysis study also showed that LFS-829 can reverse the disease-affected signatures in colitis mice. Remarkably, LFS-829 exhibits excellent safety profiles and pharmacological properties compatible with treating colitis. Our results not only provide the mechanistic insights to explain the activities of LFS-829 in reversing colitis but also open new avenues of developing novel pharmacological treatment of colitis.

    Results

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    CRM1 Is Significantly Upregulated in the IEC of Colitis Patients

    To investigate the intestinal expression of CRM1 in colitis patients, we performed an analysis of the endoscopic mucosal biopsy tissues from the inflamed and noninflamed areas of UC patients and normal individuals. We employed Western blot experiments and confirmed that CRM1 was significantly overexpressed in the intestinal epithelial cells (IEC) of colitis patients as compared with normal controls (Figure S1). Hence, our results indicated that CRM1 is closely associated with the progression and pathogenesis of colitis.

    Structure-Based Discovery of Small-Molecule Antagonist LFS-829

    In our prior work, we identified that sulforaphene (LFS-01) is a natural antagonist of CRM1, and the isocyanate group of LFS-01 can trap the cysteine residue in the nuclear export signal (NES) binding cleft of CRM1 via a covalent bond.(5) We revealed that oral administration of LFS-01 can reverse colitis in model mice.(10) Herein we wanted to develop a potent antagonist based on the scaffold of LFS-01 and evaluate its efficacy in the colitis model. First, to increase the noncovalent interactions with the NES binding groove of CRM1, we installed a phenyl ring next to the sulfoxide moiety in the parent structure of LFS-01, which leads to compound LFS-06 that inhibits nuclear export with an IC50 value of 1.5 μM, 3-fold better than that of LFS-01 determined by the cell-based phenotypic nuclear export functional assay as previously described.(5) We next sought to optimize the scaffold of LFS-06 with better potency and drug-like properties for colitis. To this end, we generated a virtual combinatorial library based on the phenyl ring of LFS-06. In total, 45 substitution moieties were used in the generation of a virtual library consisting of 2025 analogue molecules with disubstituted groups on the phenyl ring (see Data set_S1). Furthermore, we used the ADME/T module as well as the drug-likeness module implemented in the DiscoveryStudio suite to filter out the top 100 analogue molecules from the combinatorial library with acceptable ADME/T and drug-like properties. Subsequently, we employed covalent docking to predict the binding of the analogue molecules with the nuclear export signal (NES) cleft of CRM1. Lastly, we selected LFS-829 from the top 15 analogue molecules to prepare on the basis of binding energy, drug-like properties, ADME/T properties, solubility, and easy synthesis (see Scheme 1, also see Data set_S1).

    Crystallographic Structure of LFS-829 Bound with CRM1

    To establish the precise mode of actions of LFS-829, we determined its cocrystal structure complexed with humanized yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) CRM1 protein, yeast RanBP1, and human RanGTP at 2.60 Å resolution (PDB code: 5ZPU; Figure 1; Figures S2 and S3). The cocrystal structure clearly shows that LFS-829 forms a covalent bond with the sulfur atom of Cys539 located in the NES binding cleft of CRM1, similar to that of the existing CRM1 inhibitor KPT-330.(11) The ditrifluoromethyl-phenyl moiety fits well into a subpocket in the NES binding cleft of CRM1 formed by Ile555, Met556, Thr575, Val576, Lys579, and Leu580 (Figure 1B,E). Importantly, the two trifluoromethyl substituent groups form strong hydrogen bonding with Leu536, Lys548, Lys579, and Leu580 (Figure 1C,E). Moreover, the phenyl ring of LFS-829 enjoys hydrophobic contacts with Phe583 and Phe572 (Figure 1E). This cocrystal structure provides a solid structural basis for the specific and covalent binding of LFS-829 with Cys539 in the NES binding cleft of CRM1. Additionally, our mass spectroscopic analysis and pull-down assay experiments also showed that LFS-829 is a potent and covalent CRM1 inhibitor (Figure S4). In particular, we showed by the pull-down assay that LFS-829 binds to both human CRM1 and humanized yeast CRM1 strongly by inhibiting the binding of GST-NESMVM to the NES binding groove (Figure 1D). In addition, C528S mutation on CRM1 completely abolished LFS-829’s binding, suggesting that the covalent bond formation is essential for its function (Figure 1D and Figure S4, C528 in human CRM1 is equivalent to C539 in yeast CRM1).

    CRM1 Is a Major Cellular Target of LFS-829

    In addition to the cocrystal structure, we revealed that CRM1 is a major target of LFS-829 through a series of experiments. First, the incubation of the CRM1 protein with LFS-829 caused an increase in molecular weight of 373 Da as determined by LC–MS/MS via the LTQ Orbitrap method, corresponding to the covalent addition of the LFS-829 moiety (Figure S4). We demonstrated that the activities toward HCT-15 cells by LFS-829 was significantly abrogated when CRM1 was knocked down by siCRM1, implicating that CRM1 is the main target responsible for the cellular activities of LFS-829 (Figure 2B). Next, we tried to evaluate the binding specificity of LFS-829 with CRM1 as compared to other possible protein targets with reactive cysteine in the binding pocket. Here, we chose IkBα and Keap1 as two control probes to investigate because both of them are reactive for covalent inhibitors and thereby frequently used for testing selectivity. Our SPR (surface plasmon resonance, Biacore T200) results revealed that LFS-829 binds strongly with CRM1 (Kd ∼ 26.95 nM, Figure 2A) as compared to KPT-330 (Kd ∼18.74 nM). In contrast, LFS-829 does not bind with either two control probes IkBα or Keap1 in SPR binding assay experiments.
    Importantly, we investigated the time course of CRM1-LFS829 complex degradation by the cell-based phenotypic nuclear export functional assay (Figure 2C). Here, KPT-330 was used as a control compound because prior studies have shown that KPT-330 is a reversible CRM1 inhibitor. Briefly, NES-GFP was quickly accumulated in the nucleus upon the treatment of KPT-330 or LFS-829. Subsequently, cells (293T cell line) were washed with PBS to remove the KPT-330 or LFS-829, and we monitored the localization of NES-GFP per hour by confocal microscopy. Only after two hours, NES-GFP in cells incubated with LFS-829 was almost completely translocated to the cytoplasm, whereas, in cells incubated with KPT-330, NES-GFP was still localized in the nucleus of the cells. This result implicates that LFS-829 binds with CRM1 in a more reversible fashion as compared to that of KPT-330, indicating that LFS-829 may exert a lower toxicity profile. Moreover, we used the GSH/GSSG detection assay kit to show that the GSH/GSSG ratio keeps constant upon the treatment of LFS-829, and therefore, we ruled out the possibility that LFS-829 is a general redox modulator (see Figure S7). Collectively, our results revealed that CRM1 is a major cellular target of LFS-829, and it appears that LFS-829 is a reversible inhibitor of CRM1.

    LFS-829 Exhibits Superior Efficacy than First-Line Therapy in a Colitis Model

    Next, we assessed the therapeutic efficacy of LFS-829 in DSS (dextran sodium sulfate)-induced colitis mice as compared to 5-ASA (5-aminosalicylicacid), the first-line therapy for colitis patients.(12,13) Oral treatment of LFS-829 significantly attenuated the severity of DSS-induced colitis in model mice (Figure 3). The dosage of 30 mg/kg that we used in the murine colitis model was translated from the clinical dosage of 5-ASA for humans. Remarkably, LFS-829 treatment can reduce body weight loss (Figure 3B), reverse colon shortening (Figure 3F), decreased diarrhea (Figure 3C), and improve colonic histology (Figure 3D). On the other hand, mice subjected to 7 days of administration of DSS supplemented in drinking water developed acute colitis characterized by bloody diarrhea, ulcerations, and infiltrations with granulocytes and showed severe weight loss resulting in a mortality of 20% (Figure 3A). Furthermore, we observed extensive destruction of the colon mucosal layer and severe colonic damage in the untreated mice group characterized by ulceration, epithelial cell loss, reduction of the density of the tubular glands, and disseminated fibrosis. In contrast, the LFS-829-treated group showed a preserved mucosal architecture, reduced swelling, inflammation and ulceration, and more complete re-epithelialization (Figure 3D). Noteworthy, oral treatment of 5-ASA failed to prevent DSS-induced colitis at the same dose. Importantly, we demonstrated that LFS-829 can attenuate colitis in a dose-dependent fashion, and a 3 mg/kg dose of LFS-829 could lead to increased severity of colitis. These data provide strong evidence that LFS-829 oral treatment can reverse the course of DSS-induced acute colitis.

    Comparison of LFS-829 with Clinical Candidate Compound APD334

    APD334 (also known as etrasimod) is the next-generation small-molecule modulator of sphingosine-1-phosphate (S1P) currently in the evaluation of Phase 3 clinical trials for the treatment of ulcerative colitis (UC).(14) Of note, APD334 achieves rather positive results in clinical remission for colitis patients (2 mg per day for patients, which can be translated into 10 mg/kg dosage in a colitis murine model). Therefore, we wanted to compare the therapeutic potential of LFS-829 with APD334 in a colitis model. In brief, the group of mice treated with LFS-829 after DSS administration recovered more body weight and achieved a better disease activity index score (DAI score) as compared to the APD334 treatment group under the same dose (Figure 4A,B). Moreover, animals treated with LFS-829 exhibited much less incidence of diarrhea over the course of treatment as compared with the APD334 treatment group (Figure 4D). In addition, oral treatment of LFS-829 to colitis model mice significantly restored colon length as compared to the APD334 treatment group (Figure 4D,E). Note that we observed a much better recovery of swelling, inflammation, and ulceration in the LFS-829 treatment group as compared to the APD334 treatment group within 1 week. This is consistent with the results that LFS-829 treatment can reduce more proinflammatory cytokines levels in the serum of colon tissues as compared to the APD334 treatment group as measured by the ELISA assay (Figure 7D). We found that LFS-829 treatment clearly restored the damaged colon mucosal layer in the DSS group, as observed by the histopathological staining analysis (Figure 4E). Together, our results indicate that LFS-829 treatment promotes better efficacy and mucosal healing in colitis model mice than APD334 in a one-week dosing schedule. However, we wanted to remind the reader that our results do not infer that APD334 is not efficacious for colitis. Our results only implicate that LFS-829 can exert its biological effects against colitis in a relatively short period of dosing time as compared to APD334. More studies may be warranted to compare the two compounds in a long period of dosing time for colitis treatment.

    Proteomics Analysis of the Expression of Disease Signatures

    To dissect the molecular pathways underlying the pathogenesis of colitis, we assessed the gene expression signatures determined by RNA-seq techniques in both UC patients and the DSS animal model derived from previous literatures.(15,16) Of 92 IBD-associated genes, functional enrichment analysis revealed that gene signatures involved in host defense, inflammatory response, NF-κB signaling, immune response, and gastrointestinal disease are mostly dysregulated in both UC patients and the DSS-induced animal model (Figure 5, Data set_S2). Next, we employed a proteomics technique to detect the global protein expression change in the intestinal epithelial cells (IEC) of the DSS animal model group and LFS-829 treatment group, respectively. Comparative inspection of the protein expression in the DSS model group and LFS-829 treatment group revealed the expected antithetical expression patterns between the two groups (Figure 5). Interestingly, functional enrichment analysis implicated that protein signatures involved in the epithelial barrier, wound healing, immune response, inflammatory response, host defense, gastrointestinal disease, and NF-κB signaling were reciprocally expressed between the LFS-829 treatment group and the colitis model group. These results implicated that LFS-829 treatment can restore the disturbed disease signature patterns in colitis model mice.

    LFS-829 Attenuates Both NF-κB Signaling and the Nrf2 Cytoprotection Pathway in a Colitis Model

    Previous work has suggested that NF-κB signaling and host defense pathways play predominant roles in the pathogenesis of colitis. In accordance, our proteomics results also revealed that gene signatures involved in host defense and NF-κB signaling were dysregulated in the DSS model group. Therefore, we wanted to investigate the biological effects of LFS-829 toward NF-κB signaling and host defense pathways in the colitis model. First, we observed a significant accumulation of IkBα and Nrf2 in the nucleus by confocal microscopy upon treatment with LFS-829 (Figure 6). Consequently, the accumulated IkBα in the nucleus can bind to NF-κB and inhibit its transcriptional activities. Analogously, the accumulated Nrf2 in the nucleus can induce the cellular defense pathway. Next, as assessed by Western blots, we found a significant upregulation of p65 levels in the intestinal epithelial cells obtained from the DSS-treated group as compared to the control group (see Figure 7A). This constitutive activity of NF-κB p65 was accompanied by the increased production of a few proinflammatory cytokines including Cox-2, TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β, and IL-23 as measured by the ELISA assay (see Figure 7D). Remarkably, when colitis mice were treated with LFS-829, a significant reduction of NF-κB p65 as well as the proinflammatory cytokines mentioned above were observed (Figure 7A,D). Accordingly, we found that oral treatment of LFS-829 can abrogate the clinical signs of established intestinal inflammation in colitis mice. Moreover, LFS-829-treated colitis mice no longer had diarrhea and started to gain weight (Figures 3 and 4).
    On the other hand, prior reports have shown that a few important antioxidants and anti-inflammatory enzymes regulated by the Nrf2 cellular defense pathway play protective roles in the pathogenesis of colitis.(17,18) As mentioned before, the nuclear retention of Nrf2 by LFS-829 treatment may activate the cytoprotection pathway in the colon of colitis mice. Hence, we evaluated the expression levels of several antioxidant and phase II detoxifying enzymes among different animal groups. As expected, we found that the DSS-induced colitis group was associated with increased expression of Nrf2-regulated enzymes such as NQO1 and reduced expression of Nrf2 and phase II detoxifying enzyme HO-1 as compared to the control group (Figure 7B). In contrast, in LFS-829 treatment group, we found a significant reduction of expression levels of NQO1 and an increase of expression levels of Nrf2 and HO-1 as compared to the DSS model group (Figure 7B). Of note, the overexpression of MRP8 (S100A8) and MRP14 (S100A9), which is called as the calprotectin complex, has been regarded as a very valuable biomarker for monitoring the colonic inflammatory conditions in colitis patients.(19) Interestingly, we observed that the upregulation of MRP8 (S100A8) and MRP14 (S100A9) in the DSS model group as compared to the control group was restored upon the treatment of LFS-829 as measured by Western blot (Figure 7C). The tight junction protein, occludin, is crucial to the integration of the intestinal epithelial barrier and, therefore, often used as a critical pathological marker for colitis patients. Our immunohistochemical analysis results revealed that occludin expression was significantly reduced in colitis mice (Figure 4F). Conversely, LS-829 treatment restored the dysregulation of occludin, which suggested that LFS-829 protects the epithelial barrier during intestinal damage of colitis. Taken together, our results implicate that LFS-829 can reverse the pathogenetic course of colitis by regulating both NF-κB signaling and the Nrf2 cellular defense pathway.

    Encapsulation of LFS-829 in Nanoparticles

    IBD is a chronic relapsing disease, and patients may require a rather long-term medication. Therefore, an oral colon-specific drug delivery system can enhance the drug efficacy and reduce adverse effects for patients.(20) In our prior work, we have prepared a water-soluble nanoparticle encapsulated form of sulforaphene by α-cyclodextrin.(10) Analogously, to enhance the oral bioavailability, stability, solubility, and other therapeutic properties of LFS-829, we prepared a nanoparticle encapsulated form of LFS-829 by α-cyclodextrin as previously described (named as CD-LFS-829 or Cyclone-829, Figure 8). Molecular modeling and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) demonstrate that LFS-829 can be well stabilized in the cavity of α-CD (Figure 8A,B). In brief, we examined the therapeutic potential of CD-LFS-829 in the DSS-induced colitis rodent model. Notably, oral treatment of CD-LFS-829 (30 mg/kg/day) significantly restored the colon length and histological score as compared to the untreated DSS group (Figure 8C–E). Taken together, our study provides a demo encapsulation system for the potential use of LFS-829 in the clinical treatment of colitis.

    Permeability and Stability Properties of LFS-829

    The parallel artificial membrane permeability assay (PAMPA) study and the Caco-2 cell permeability study determined that the permeability index of LFS-829 was high with approved drugs as the control (SIM 2–3), suggesting that LFS-829 may achieve good bioavailability in humans. Moreover, we employed an artificial gastric juice stability assay and artificial intestinal juice stability assay to determine the stabilities of LFS-829 in an alimentary system, critical properties for IBD new drug. LFS-829 is rather stable in both artificial gastric juice (half-life ∼24 h) and artificial intestinal juice (half-life ∼8 h), suggesting a pharmacokinetic profile compatible with once-daily dosing (Figure 9A,B).

    Assessment of the Acute Toxicity Profile of LFS-829

    To determine the suitability of LFS-829 in clinical practice, we assessed the acute toxic effects of LFS-829 in mice. Ten C57BL6 mice were exposed to oral administration of LFS-829 at an increasing dose of 300 mg/kg to 600 mg/kg over the course of 1 day, whereas another ten mice were exposed to vehicle only (50% ethanol, n = 10). The LFS-829-treated animals did not show lethargy, weight loss, or other physical indications of sickness. The mice were sacrificed after the 14 day washout period to assess delayed toxicity. The colon, spleen, and other tissues were examined, and no toxic effects or tissue damage (either macroscopic or microscopic) were observed (Figure 9C and Figure S10).

    Evaluation of the Cardiotoxicity Profile of LFS-829

    Cardiotoxicity is a crucial factor and major safety concern for preclinical or clinical evaluation of pharmacological therapeutics for the treatment of chronic relapsing diseases such as IBD.(21) Therefore, it is necessary to evaluate the potential cardiotoxicity for LFS-829. To this end, we used an hERG potassium channel patch-clamp assay to test whether LFS-829 would cause QT interval prolongation as a result of hERG potassium channel inhibition. LFS-829 did not inhibit hERG even at a high concentration, suggesting a very low risk for cardiotoxicity (Figure 9D, Figure S11, Table S3). These studies clearly established the excellent safety profiles of LFS-829 for its potential use in the clinical treatment of colitis.

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    There are unmet medical needs for disease-modifying therapies for IBD with excellent response rates and safety profiles due to the relative inefficacy of the current maintenance therapies. It is rather challenging to develop therapeutics for colitis because the pathogenesis of colitis is extremely complicated and remains elusive. For instance, two recent clinical trials with IL-23 antibodies, Secukinumab(22) and Brodalumab,(23) showed no efficacy or even higher rates of adverse events for colitis patients. Moreover, in the past decades, no therapeutics have been introduced in clinical practice for the treatment of colitis. Of note, a few small-molecule compounds for colitis are currently under clinical development including JAK inhibitors and the sphingosine-1-phosphate receptor 1 (S1P1) modulator such as Etrasimod (also named as APD334).(14)
    In light of this, here, we designed a rational structure-based strategy to discover a potent small-molecule antagonist for exportin-1 based on the scaffold of sulforaphene with a distinct mode of action. Our previous work has found that sulforaphene, a natural antagonist of CRM1, exhibits therapeutic effects toward colitis. To improve the potency, we first introduced a hydrophobic phenyl ring next to the sulfoxide moiety in the parent structure of LFS-01. Next, through the screening of a virtual combinatorial library, we identified LFS-829 with the replacement of two trifluoromethyl groups at the R3 and R5 position, resulting in a potency nearly 30-fold higher than that of the parent compound as determined by a cell-based phenotypic nuclear export functional assay. Our strategy has the advantages of considering affinity, ADME/T properties, and other properties. LFS-829 is amenable to large-scale synthesis and has a low cost of chemicals, which may be advantageous as a therapy for colitis.
    We revealed that LFS-829 can inhibit CRM1 in the low nanomolar range (∼26.95 nM) through an SPR binding assay. We revealed that the cellular activities by LFS-829 were significantly diminished when CRM1 was suppressed by siCRM1, implicating that CRM1 is a main cellular target of LFS-829. The study of the time course of CRM1-LFS829 complex degradation suggests that LFS-829 is a more reversible inhibitor as compared to KPT-330. This indicates that LFS-829 may exert a lower toxicity profile, which is advantageous because colitis patients typically require very long-term medication. Strikingly, our study showed that LFS-829 was very efficacious in a mouse model of colitis and exhibited superior therapeutic potential than first-line therapy 5-ASA as well as APD334, a promising candidate, compounds under clinical trials for colitis. Importantly, our study provides the co-crystallographic structure of LFS-829 bound to CRM1, which also firmly establishes the working mechanism of LFS-829. Therefore, our study may provide insights to guide functional elucidation of the mechanism of action for LFS-829 and may open new avenues for future drug development based on similar chemical scaffolds.
    It has been well recognized that colitis is a complex and heterogeneous disease with several molecular pathways contributing to the disease pathophysiology. Therefore, the next-generation therapeutics for colitis may allow the simultaneous modulation of multiple disease-causing cytokines or pathways. Our study, for the first time, demonstrated that a small-molecule antagonist of exportin-1 can reverse the pathogenesis of colitis by regulating both NF-κB signaling and the Nrf2 cellular defense pathway (Scheme 2). The activation of NF-κB signaling plays a dominant role in the inflamed gut of colitis patients. In particular, it has been found that the upregulation of NF-κB (p65 or p50) is significant in the macrophages and epithelial cells isolated from the inflamed gut specimens of IBD patients.(24) Moreover, there has been mounting evidence suggesting that NF-κB signaling in the gut epithelium plays as a critical regulator of epithelial integrity and intestinal immune homeostasis and thereby is closely associated with the pathogenesis of colitis.(25,26) We revealed that LFS-829 treatment can lead to significant nuclear accumulation of IkBα, which consequently strongly suppresses NF-κB signaling and its downstream targets. In particular, we found that the exposure of colitis mice with LFS-829 leads to the suppression of a few critical downstream targets of NF-κB signaling including Cox-2, IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-23, all of which are hallmarks in the pathogenesis of colitis. On the other hand, Nrf2 plays a fundamental role in the cellular defense pathway against inflammation and intestinal integrity through the regulation of proinflammatory cytokines and the induction of Phase II detoxifying enzymes. Of note, Nrf2 deficient mice were more susceptible to DSS-induced colitis.(27) Our data clearly show that LFS-829 treatment can lead to the nuclear retention of Nrf2 and enable the activation of the Nrf2 cellular defense pathway. Particularly, we found that the LFS-829 administration restored the dysregulation of detoxifying enzymes HO-1 and NQO1 in the colon tissues of colitis mice, both of which are essential for the cytoprotective effects during inflammation and the maintenance of mucosal homeostasis in the gastrointestinal tract of colitis patients. Moreover, one of the current focuses in colitis research attends to a group of immunogenic endogenous damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs).(28) The calprotectin, also named as the S100A8/S100A9 complex, belongs to DAMPs and has been recognized to play a key role in the etiology of colitis during colon tissue damage. Moreover, the complex S100A8/S100A9 proteins are present at high levels in the serum of patients, and therefore, S100A8/S100A9 has been established as sensitive and specific biomarkers for colitis.(19) Notably, we observed that S100A8/S100A9 was upregulated in colitis mice, and the expression level of S100A8/S100A9 was reduced upon the administration of LFS-829, indicating that LFS-829 reverses the pathogenesis of colitis. Collectively, we concluded that LFS-829 exerted its therapeutic effects toward colitis through dual targeting of the NF-κB signaling and Nrf2 cellular defense pathway. Moreover, it has been documented that there exists functional crosstalk between NF-κB signaling and the Nrf2/ARE pathway.(17,29,30) Therefore, we argued that the dual attenuation of both NF-κB signaling and the Nrf2 cytoprotection pathway by LFS-829 might have synergistic effects to prevent inflammation and restore intestinal homeostasis (Scheme 2).
    The pathological characteristics of colitis determine that the onset of the disease may translate into long-standing disease activity and thereby require very long-term medication. Hence, the safety profile of a candidate compound for treating colitis is of utmost importance. Noteworthy, our results suggest that LFS-829 has a rather low risk of both cytotoxicity and cardiotoxicity. Furthermore, we evaluated the acute toxicity of LFS-829 in animals, and our results suggest that LFS-829 barely shows any acute toxicity at a dose of 600 mg/kg. Moreover, we showed that LFS-829 is quite stable in artificial gastric juice and intestinal juice with an excellent permeability index. Hence, LFS-829 may hold great potential as colitis therapy with a good safety profile. Finally, there is an increasing risk for colitis patients to develop colon cancer. Our results implicated that LFS-829 also exerts excellent biological effects toward colon cancer cells. Therefore, we argue that LFS-829 may have protective effects in colitis as well as its malignant transition to colon cancer. This may be beneficial for treating colitis patients in the severe stage and is warranted for further investigation.
    In summary, we report the rational structure-based discovery and extensive evaluation of a potent antagonist of CRM1 toward the treatment of colitis. We demonstrated that oral treatment of LFS-829 holds great promise as an effective remedy for colitis, acting through dual attenuation of NF-κB signaling and the Nrf2 cellular defense pathway. Our data suggest that LFS-829 is a promising new candidate compound and is expected to feature prominently in the future of colitis treatment.

    The Purity of the Compound

    Compound LFS-829 was confirmed by 1H NMR, LC–MS, and HPLC with 99% purity (≥95% purity, see Supporting Information). The organic synthesis was conducted following standard procedures (see Supporting Information). All of the chemicals and solvents were purchased from Sigma.

    Human Biopsy Samples

    The biopsy samples of actively inflamed and noninflamed intestinal regions were collected from patients with colitis indicated by clinical pathology (UC, n = 5). The control biopsy specimens were obtained from normal areas of healthy subjects (n = 5). All patients and healthy subjects were approved by the Digestive System Department of the First Affiliated Hospital of Nanjing Medical University. The intestinal samples were immediately fixed in formalin and embedded in paraffin until use for future immunohistochemistry analysis. All of the written informed consents were obtained before sample collection. The study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of Nanjing Medical University.

    Co-crystallographic Structure

    After purification of the CRM1-Ran-RanBP1 complex by the Superdex 200 increase gel filtration column,(31) protein complexes were concentrated to 6 mg/mL and mixed at a 1:1 ratio with crystallization solution containing 18% PEG3350, 200 mM ammonium nitrate, 1 mM LFS-829, 100 mM Bis·Tris, pH 6.6. Next, 12% (v/v) glycerol was supplemented with crystallization conditions as the cryo-protectant. X-ray diffraction data were collected at the Shanghai Synchrotron Radiation Facility (SSRF) beamline BL17U1 and BL19U1. Coordinates of yCRM1-hRan-yRanBP1 (PDB code: 4HAT) were used as the search model and refined with a rigid body briefly then restrained refinement using the program Refmac5. The data collection and refinement statistics are provided in Table S1. The coordinates are deposited to PDB with accession code 5ZPU.

    LC–MS/MS via LTQ Orbitrap

    CRM1 protein was incubated with 20-fold excess (molar ratio) of compound LFS-829, which was then removed by ultrafiltration (Millipore). Subsequently, CRM1 protein was cleaved by trypsin with an enzyme-to-protein ratio of 1:60 (w/w) in 150 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.5) at 37 °C for 6 h. The samples were then analyzed using an LTQ Orbitrap Velos (Thermo Scientific, San Jose, CA, USA) coupled with an Accela 600 HPLC system (Thermo Scientific). The samples were automatically loaded onto the trap column (3 cm × 200 μm i.d.) packed with C18 AQ beads (5 μm 120 Å) followed with binary gradient separation using an analytical column (15 cm × 75 μm i.d.) packed with C18 AQ beads (5 μm, 120 Å). The MS full scans were acquired in positive ion mode from m/z 350 to 1850 with a mass resolution of 60 000 at m/z 400, and the MS/MS scans were acquired in LTQ positive ion linear trap mode. All MS and MS/MS spectra were acquired in the data-dependent analysis mode. Peptides with a Mascot score >20 were retained for the analyses.

    DSS-Induced Colitis, LFS-829, 5-ASA, and APD334 Oral Administration

    To induce acute colitis, 6–8 weeks old male C57BL/6J mice were given drinking water containing 3.0% (w/v) DSS (MP Biomedicals) ad libitum for 7 days and distilled water for one additional day before sacrifice. Mice were observed for weight and signs of diseases. The disease activity index (DAI) was scored based on a nonblinded fashion. LFS-829 or 5-ASA (Energy Chemical) were first dissolved in ethanol (70%) and then diluted in saline water. The solutions were administrated to DSS-drinking mice with a gastric tube by a dose of 30 mg/kg body weight.

    General Treatment of the Animals

    In the therapeutic treatment protocol, 30 mg/kg body weight of LFS-829 was administered to mice with a gastric tube starting from day 0 (DSS administration) and on every following day. The 5-ASA treatment group and the APD334 treatment group followed the same protocol. Control mice were given vehicle solution, phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) with a cosolvent such as ethanol or DMSO, and subjected to the same protocols and schedules as described for LFS-829. In all animals, body weight and diarrhea severity were monitored each day. Thirteen days after disease induction, the mice were killed, and the intestine was collected and evaluated for colon length and microscopic damage. In addition, proximal, medial, and distal portions of the colon were fixed in 10% phosphate-buffered formalin, and paraffin-embedded sections were stained with H&E. The degree of histological damage and intestinal inflammation was graded in a blinded fashion by a panel-approved pathologist.

    Histopathological Analysis, Macroscopic Damage, and Microscopic Damage Assessment

    After the animals were sacrificed, the colon was extracted, cleaned, and weighed. The colonic tissue samples were preserved in buffered formalin, embedded in paraffin, cut into 5 μm sections, and stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) for histopathological analysis. The macroscopic damage score (MDS) was assessed in a blinded fashion by examination of excised colons, and the disease activity index (DAI) of mice was evaluated as previously described.(32) The colonic tissue was homogenized in a Greenburger buffer supplemented with protease inhibitors (complete Mini EDTA free, Roche). After sonication for 10 s, the suspension was centrifuged at 8000g for 20 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was used to quantify the myeloperoxidease (MPO) activity and the levels of cytokines using commercial ELISA kits (USCN, USA) following the manufacturer’s protocol.

    Cell-Based Phenotypic Nuclear Export Functional Assay

    293T cells were seeded onto 96-well, black, clear-bottom plates at a density of 3000–5000 cells/well in 100 μL of complete media. After incubation at 37 °C and 5% CO2 for 24 h, cells were treated with CRM1 inhibitors for 2 h. Small-molecule compounds were serially diluted 1:2 starting from 1 μM in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS. Following the indicated treatments, cells were fixed for 20 min with 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS. Next, cell membranes were permeabilized by 0.3% Triton X-100 in PBS for 20 min. After blocking with 5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) in PBS for 1 h at 37 °C, cells were treated with IkBα antibodies in blocking buffer for 24 h at 4 °C. Antimouse Alexa 594 were used as secondary antibodies. Cell nuclei were stained with DAPI for 20 min. After washing, photomicrographic images were recorded with a confocal laser scanning microscope, Fluoview FV10i. For cell counts, at least 200 cells exhibiting nuclear, nuclear and cytoplasmic, or cytoplasmic staining were counted from three separate images. Percentages of N (nuclear), N/C (nuclear/cytoplasmic), and C (cytoplasmic) cells were calculated, and standard deviations were determined (Figure S7).

    SPR Binding Assay

    Studies of binding kinetics were performed on a Biacore T200 (GE Healthcare). His-tagged CRM1, IkBα, and Keap1 proteins were immobilized in 0.5 mM NiCl2 on a Biacore Series S NTA chip. LFS-829 and control compound KPT-330 (10 μM) were tested in immobilization buffer supplemented with 5% (v/v) DMSO, with a flow rate of 30 μL/min, a contact time of 60 s, and a dissociation time of 300 s. A solvent correction curve from 4.5–5.8% (v/v) DMSO was included. The analysis was performed using the Biacore Evaluation software, version 3.0, using a kinetic fit and a 1:1 binding model. Representative curves were exported and replotted in GraphPad Prism.

    ELISA Assay

    The peripheral blood of the animals from different experimental groups (the control group, DSS group, LFS-829-treated group, and APD334-treated group) was collected, centrifuged at 1500g for 15 min at 4 °C, and incubated overnight to obtain serum. The concentrations of serum cytokines were assayed by the ELISA kit (Elabscience, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

    Western Blot

    The total protein was extracted from colon tissues of animals. Equal amounts of proteins were fractioned with sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) followed by electrophoretic transfer of proteins onto nitrocellulose membranes. The blots were probed with antibodies against Cox-2, IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-23, etc. and followed by incubation with secondary antibodies conjugated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP, USCN, USA). The immune complexes were detected with a Western Bright ECL Western Blotting HRP Substrate kit and analyzed with image lab software (Bio-Rad, USA).

    Functional Analysis of IBD Disease Signatures

    Functional analysis of the up- and downregulated protein signatures determined by proteomics techniques (Figure 5, heatmap analysis) was performed through the use of IPA (Ingenuity Systems, trial version). The functional analysis was able to identify the biological functions/pathways and/or diseases that were most relevant/significant to the data set. Signatures in the top and bottom 25% of rank change versus normal in both the DSS model group and LFS829 treatment group that were associated with biological functions and/or diseases in the Ingenuity Knowledge Base were considered for the analysis.

    Evaluation of Compound Cardiotoxicity

    Briefly, the cell line HEK293 stably expressing the hERG K+ channel was used in the study. Cells were grown in essential medium-α (MEM; Gibco) supplemented with 10% (v/v) heat-inactivated fetal calf serum, 1% (v/v) penicillin/streptomycin/glutamine solution (Gibco), and 1 mg/mL G418. Compound LFS-829 for testing was dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) at a concentration of 10 mM and diluted in extracellular saline to the desired concentration. The whole-cell configuration of the patch-clamp technique was used for the current recording. The results obtained from at least three cells were pooled and fitted to estimate the mean values of IC50.

    Statistics

    All data were evaluated as mean ± SEM. Statistical analysis of the quantitative multiple group comparisons was performed using the one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukeyʼs test, whereas pairwise comparisons were performed using the t test by GraphPad Prism 5 (Graph Pad Software, La Jolla, CA, USA). Results were considered to be statistically significant with p < 0.05.
