    Abstract

    Improvements in in vitro ADME tools and pharmacokinetic prediction models have helped to shift attrition rates in early clinical trials from poor exposure to drug safety concerns, such as drug-induced liver injury (DILI). Assessing a new chemical entity’s potential for liver toxicity is an important consideration for the likely success of new drug candidates. Reactive intermediates produced during drug metabolism have been implicated as a cause of DILI, and their formation has been correlated to the addition of a black box warning on a drug label. In this work, we will present contemporary examples of the bioactivation of atypical structures usually regarded as benign and often used by medicinal chemists when attempting to avoid bioactivation. Medicinal chemistry strategies used to derisk bioactivation will be discussed, and an emphasis will be placed on the necessity of a multidisciplinary approach.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Drug induced liver injury has become the main cause of attrition in clinical trials and often is the reason for regulatory agencies to add black box warnings to drug labels. There has been an increased emphasis on identifying earlier means to predicting and overcoming drug induced liver injury, thus providing the opportunity to circumvent the potential liability during the drug discovery phase. The latest literature and review articles have focused on prediction models, which span from simple models(1) based on physical and chemical properties to more complex models(2) with multipronged approaches. Simple approaches like the rule of two(3) or empirically determining reactive metabolite (RM) formation can have a high predictive value and low false negatives, as outlined by Chan and Bennet (2017).(1)
    The bioactivation of structural alerts (SA) has been discussed at length in recent literature because understanding bioactivation is an important predictive strategy.(4−6) Unfortunately, bioactivation beyond established SA has not been examined in depth. Herein, a focus will be placed on the bioactivation of functional moieties that are generally considered acceptable, such as the pyrazole, imidazole, cyclopropyl, ethylurea, azetidine, and aliphatic groups. We will survey both the top selling and most prescribed drugs on the market(7) for such structural entities and provide examples that, under the right circumstances, are bioactivated to generate reactive metabolites. We will highlight both the mechanisms by which RMs are formed and the trapping experiments performed with nucleophiles or reaction with endogenous protein to identify bioactivation pathways.

    Aromatic Bioactivation

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Medicinal chemists and metabolism scientists have studied the bioactivation of aromatic groups for many decades. In fact, the enzymatic systems in the liver that deactivate aromatic quinones, quinone amines, and quinone methides have been recently reviewed by Bolton and Dunlap.(8) For example, a simple aromatic group can be hydroxylated followed by a two-electron oxidative step to form ortho and para quinones, which are reactive as shown in Figure 1. Below are a few examples of aromatic groups that can sometimes be bioactivated independent of the quinone pathway.

    Aminopyrazole

    The aminopyrazole motif is a popular building block employed in many drug discovery programs due to its favorable physiochemical properties and synthetic tractability. Considered a bioisostere of aniline, the aminopyrazole scaffold is a heteroanalog that is more capable of progressing in a medicinal chemistry campaign because it avoids many of the DILI risks associated with aniline-containing candidates. Specifically, the pyrazole core is less susceptible to oxidative metabolism than other five-membered heterocycles, presumably due to the extra heteroatom within the ring compared to thiophenes or pyrroles that contain only one electronegative heteroatom. Figure 2 gives some examples of approved drugs that contain aminopyrazoles. The fact that there are currently no aminopyrazole containing FDA-approved drugs that have been known to cause DILI, combined with the prevalence and relative chemical stability of this moiety, would suggest an assumption of safety; nonetheless, there has been emerging evidence that not all aminopyrazoles are created equally, and recently, RM formation has been discovered. Aminopyrazole motifs exist in preclinical and clinical programs spanning therapies aimed to treat leishmaniasis, cancer chemotherapy, Alzheimer’s disease, and other ailments, and thus further discussion of this motif should be given due consideration.
    Ryan and co-workers have uncovered the bioactivation of 3- and 4-aminopyrazoles with a glutathione trapping agent in the presence of human liver microsomes.(9) They investigated a series of pyrimidine-substituted aminopyrazoles 5 and reported thioether adduct formation 8 regardless of electron-withdrawing or electron-donating substituents present on the pyrimidine 9 (Scheme 1). When the pyrazole-NH is substituted with a methyl group 5a–d, glutathione conjugates are not detected. The authors propose a mechanism that is consistent with these findings. When the amino group is a secondary amine, P450 oxidation can occur to generate a pyrazolohydroxyamine (intermediate 6). In the case of the unsubstituted pyrazole, this intermediate can undergo elimination of water to produce a reactive iminopyrazole 7. However, N-substituted pyrazoles are unable to generate an iminopyrazole 7 because there is no hydrogen available for abstraction and it is unlikely that direct N-oxidation occurs on the pyrazole N–H since the ring is quite electron poor. This strategy would be ideal if a particular structure–activity relationship (SAR) effort could tolerate the loss of a H-bond donor, yet substituents at that position would likely create limitations to a drug discovery effort. An alternative bioactivation pathway could include two electron oxidation of 5 to form intermediate 7. Other aminopyrazole substituents, either on the amino side or the pyrazole nitrogen, have not been investigated, and their substitution could play a role in the bioactivation of a compound. Interestingly, out of four FDA-approved compounds that contain an amine at the 3 or 4 position of a pyrazole ring, only one (entrectinib 3), a metastatic non-small-cell lung cancer treatment, is N-unsubstituted at the pyrrole nitrogen. There are 57 other clinical candidates reported that are not approved, and 29 of these contain an N-unsubstituted pyrazole (tozasertib, barasertib, danusertib, etc.). Though the work of Ryan et al. is context-specific, this important discovery of bioactivation on a seemingly innocuous aminopyrazole motif suggests a watchful eye should be given to monitor for potentially similar bioactivation patterns when an unsubstituted pyrazole nitrogen is employed.

    Pyrimidine

    Replacing the carbon atoms in aromatic rings with nitrogens can dramatically affect the physiochemical properties of compounds in comparison to the carbon-based phenyl system.(10) Specifically, pyrimidine can be a useful building block in medicinal chemistry and has been utilized to increase binding affinity, intramolecular interactions, and target selectivity.(10) Pyrimidine has also been shown to decrease the bioactivation potential by destabilizing quinone-like intermediates 10 and 11, such as in the case of the pyrimidine-containing buspirone 12 compared to the aniline-containing nefazodone 13 as shown in Scheme 2.(11)
    The bioactivation of fluoropyrimidine is a rare occurrence and is contrary to documented cases that show that adding fluorine and nitrogen to aromatic rings can slow P450 metabolism.(10,12,13) Some fluoropyrimidine-containing compounds were being developed as BACE1 inhibitors 14 for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease.(14) The molecules investigated were found to have time-dependent inhibition (TDI) of CYP 3A4, a predominant P450 enzyme, indicating an increased potential for drug–drug interactions in the clinic.(15) With further assessment into the mechanisms of CYP-mediated TDI, the authors identified that the fluoropyrimidine motif in 14 was responsible for CYP 3A4 inhibition. Metabolite identification studies were conducted, and metabolite 18 was discovered, corresponding to the mass of the parent compound with loss of fluorine and the addition of a hydroxyl group. When glutathione (GSH) was added to human liver microsome incubations, the same oxidative defluorination was detected along with the addition of GSH 17, suggesting the oxidative defluorination proceeds through a reactive intermediate pathway. The proposed mechanism of bioactivation starts with the epoxidation of the double bond 15 in the pyrimidine ring, forming a reactive intermediate. The GSH adducts could then be formed by direct nucleophilic attack of the thiol by GSH causing ring-opening of the epoxide and loss of fluorine. Alternatively, a reactive quinone imine intermediate 16 could explain the oxidative defluorination metabolite. Changing the fluoropyrimidine 14a to a fluoropyridine 14b reduced the IC50 from 1.2 μM to 6.4 μM, while removing the nitrogen from the ring altogether (14c) showed no evidence of CYP 3A4 TDI up to 30 μM (Scheme 3).

    Imidazole

    Imidazole is an amphoteric heterocycle present in important biological building blocks such as histidine or histamine and is a ubiquitous building block in medicinal chemistry. It is found in six compounds on the market including the anthelmintic agents mebendazole and albendazole. In most compounds, the imidazole core is fused to a benzene ring and the free aminoimidazole motif is only found in one currently approved drug, benznidazole, for the treatment of Chagas disease.
    An interesting bioactivation pathway involving an imidazole was found during the discovery of inhibitors of the microsomal prostaglandin E synthase-1 enzyme (mPGES-1) for the treatment of osteoarthritis pain.(16) Two compounds (LY3031207 19 and LY3023703 20) entered into the clinic but failed due to a significant increase in alanine aminotransferase (ALT) serum levels (a signature of liver toxicity) and hypersensitivity in a multiple ascending dose study.(17) Both compounds contain a 2-aminoimidazole moiety, which was found to undergo bioactivation.
    The proposed bioactivation pathway for LY3031207 originates with the P450 oxidation of 21 across the 4–5 double bond of the 2-aminoimidazole, which disrupts the aromaticity to form an epoxide intermediate (Scheme 4). This intermediate (22) can rearomatize to generate hydroxylated metabolite 23, or diol 24 can form through the addition of water. The epoxide is also susceptible to nucleophilic attack by GSH 25 followed by loss of water in the process of rearomatization to 27. The diastereometric diol 24 can undergo ring scission analogous to the aminothiazole activation of sudoxicam(18) with the loss of a glyoxal moiety, resulting in an acylguanidine metabolite 26 found in Scheme 4. The formation and detection of the acylguanidine metabolite were used as evidence that the epoxide intermediate was formed during further medicinal chemistry efforts.
    Metabolite identification experiments were performed on in vivo (rat, dog, and human plasma and human urine) and in vitro samples (hepatocytes and liver microsomes fortified with glutathione). Metabolite 23 was found across all species in hepatocytes and plasma as well as in human urine samples. Thioether adducts 27 were only found in HLM with GSH and in human urine. A compound corresponding to compound 25 was found in HLM with GSH. The acylguanidine metabolite 26 was found in dog hepatocytes and in plasma of rat, dog, and human as well as human urine.
    From a medicinal chemistry perspective, substitution on the imidazole did not change the bioactivation potential of these compounds. The bioactivation pathway for another substituted 2-aminoimidazole containing compound (LY3023703 28) was similar to that of LY3031207 with some potentially competing pathways. It is possible that oxidation of the methylated aminoimidazole 29 produces an imine methide metabolite 30 by hydrogen atom abstraction, followed by single electron oxidation (proposed by Rettie et al.) leading to glutathione adduct 31.(19)
    This example provides further evidence to the argument that reactive metabolites should be considered before starting expensive clinical trials, since the researchers knew these particular compounds formed RM but permitted the compound to enter the clinic. Though predicting DILI is not an easy task, it is important to check for reactive metabolites iteratively throughout the discovery process. As this example illustrates, discovery of the reactive metabolites was essential for informing clinicians on the need to dose-adjust to prevent adverse events.

    Aliphatic Bioactivation

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Aliphatic groups exist on most drugs and have the potential to be hydroxylated by P450 enzymes. During the enzymatic reaction, the first step is hydrogen atom abstraction that generates a radical, enabling oxygen rebound to produce a hydroxylated metabolite. However, several examples exist in the literature where the P450-generated radical can escape the oxygen rebound step and bind to the inside of a binding pocket and cause inactivation of the enzyme (Scheme 5).(20,21) In both of these mechanisms, it is suggested that bioactivation is achieved through a carbon-centered radical (33a, 37, 38) intermediate en route to the hydroxylated product. In the case of tacrine 32, the 1-OH product 35 did not cause inactivation of the enzyme when a synthetic standard was incubated with HLM, suggesting the carbon-centered radical 33a, carbocation 33b, or perhaps the quinoline-imine 34 is the culprit in the inactivation process. In the case of phencyclidine, when a para-ketone substituent 40 was added to the cyclohexane ring of phencyclidine 36 to protect from oxidation, less inactivation of CYP2B6 was observed. It should be noted that multiple mechanisms of phencyclidine have been proposed(22−24) including iminium formation and formation of a quinone methide intermediate, which could be the reason the keto group did not abolish TDI completely. Given this evidence, the bioactivation of aliphatic groups is possible if the carbon-centered radical can escape the oxygen rebound step in the binding pocket of CYP P450 enzymes.

    Cyclopropyl Group

    The cyclopropyl ring has been extensively used as a motif to improve drug-like properties and is often employed in medicinal chemistry as a tool to achieve desirable ADME properties. Due to its characteristic ring strain, the cyclopropyl group has enhanced π character of its C–C bonds with both shorter and stronger C–H bonds, making hydrogen atom abstraction more energetically difficult compared to a methyl group. Furthermore, the cyclopropyl ring features a more pronounced three-dimensional character which results in disruption of planarity. Because of this enhanced three-dimensional and π character, cyclopropane-containing compounds can have improved properties ranging from higher aqueous solubility, bioavailability, and increased metabolic stability.(25) This motif is currently found in 60 FDA-approved drugs, ranging from small molecules to cyclic peptides. Of these, 23 have cyclopropyl rings directly bound to a nitrogen (see 41 and 44 as examples) and six are directly bound to the sulfur of a sulfonamide group (see Figure 342 for examples). According to DrugBank.com, there are 104 cyclopropyl-containing compounds at various stages in clinical trials with 81 at the preclinical stage. Of the top 200 best-selling drugs approved by the FDA, eight contained a cyclopropyl ring, clarifying that this synthon has demonstrated significant biological activity across various human diseases.
    The bioactivation of cyclopropylamines has been well understood to proceed through a single electron transfer from the nitrogen resulting in the formation of a double bond or through hydrogen atom transfer at the α-carbon of the cyclopropyl group, deemed to be the possible mechanisms.(26) The mechanism of bioactivation described here proceeds through a different process since the α-carbon is substituted. Zhuo and co-workers employed the cyclopropyl group during the optimization of a series of hepatitis C virus NS5B inhibitors.(27) The authors found that the cyclopropyl group 45 was being bioactivated by P450 enzymes (Scheme 6). High resolution mass spectrometry and NMR spectroscopy were used to identify the site of metabolic activation, and it was confirmed that the cyclopropyl was oxidized by P450. Hydrogen abstraction of the cyclopropyl ring 45 mediated by P450 was hypothesized to be the first step of its bioactivation. The radical formed was proposed to rearrange to ring opened propenyl radicals 47 and 48; these radicals could then react with inbound hydroxy radicals to produce the allylic alcohols 49 and 50, primed for nucleophilic attack by glutathione 51 and 52 as shown in Scheme 6. The authors also propose that the tertiary alcohol 50 may undergo elimination of a water molecule to form a carbonyl imine intermediate 53 which undergoes reductive conjugation with GSH to form 54 and 55. Changing the cyclopropyl to a gem-dimethyl alleviated the bioactivation by making only small changes to the core of the molecule, maintaining potency. These molecules were devoid of reactive metabolite formation.

    Azetidine

    The azetidine ring has a long history in the medicinal chemistry design world, most notably within β-lactams found in antibiotic drugs. Azetidines also exist in the Janus kinase inhibitors, cobimetinib and baricitinib, and a lactam version also exists in the cholesterol lowering medication ezetimibe. Recently, Obach et al. investigated the use of azetidine as an isostere for piperidine in a 5HT-4 partial agonist series, in which the piperidine moiety was bioactivated to an iminium intermediate, causing internal cyclization.(28) During their investigation it was found that computationally the energy required for α-carbon hydrogen atom abstraction was significantly higher for azetidine 58 than for piperidine 56 or pyrrolidine 57 analogues as shown in Figure 4. The α-carbon hydrogen abstraction can lead to potentially reactive iminium metabolites. Given this favorable property, it is expected that azetidines are likely to appear in more medicinal chemistry efforts to reduce metabolic clearance and give favorable pharmacokinetic properties.
    During efforts to develop a sphingosine-1-phosphate-1 receptor agonist for organ transplant immunosuppression, a radiolabeled advanced candidate (MRL-A) 59, containing an azetidine ring, was dosed to rats in a bile duct cannulated study.(29) During the metabolite identification process, evidence for the formation of reactive metabolites was found through covalent adduction to liver proteins. After digestion of liver proteins with nonspecific protease, the authors observed a lysine adduct 63 (Scheme 7). With the use of high resolution mass spectrometry and 1H and 13C NMR, the structure was determined to be a product of a ring-opened azetidine in a multistep process outlined in Scheme 7. The authors proposed hemiaminal formation 60 followed by ring opening 61. An additional desaturation step by P450 leads to intermediate 62, an α,β unsaturated aldehyde, where lysine can then attack to form the imine 63.
    In addition, a glutathione adduct was detected, confirmed by the high-resolution mass spectrum fragmentation pattern, which indicated addition of glutathione to the ring-opened azetidine 66. The authors propose the aldehyde 61 will be reduced to the alcohol intermediate 64 which can be dehydrated yielding an α,β unsaturated carboxylic acid 65. This intermediate is a good Michael acceptor and can produce the glutathione adduct 66. Alternatively, a mechanism that involves coenzyme A and this carboxylic acid intermediate (a known structural alert) is discussed elsewhere.(30)
    The azetidine-containing AZD1979 67 is being developed as a melanin-concentrating hormone receptor 1 antagonist designed for the treatment of obesity, containing a spiro-moiety with an azetidine ring and an oxetane ring.(31) When radiolabeled [C14]-AZD-1979 was incubated with human liver S9 fraction in the presence of aminobenztriazole, a pan P450 inhibitor, seven metabolites were identified by liquid chromatography with radiometric detection. One metabolite (59) was characterized by high resolution mass spectrometry to be the m/z of AZD1979 with the addition of 307.0706 Da, consistent with the addition of glutathione. Three of the other metabolites were characterized as glutathione degradation products; cysteineglycinyl, cysteinyl, and the mercapturic acid-conjugated metabolites (Scheme 8). Further investigations revealed that glutathione-S-tranferases (GSTs) were involved in the formation of the adduct by using chemical inhibitors and recombinant enzymes.
    The NADPH-independent formation of GSH adducts is an important finding for the safety profile of AZD1979. If the conjugation by GST is rapid, then the compound could facilitate the depletion of intracellular GSH and potentially lead to the loss of hepatic protection. Given these two examples of azetidine metabolism, it is important to work closely with metabolism scientists to ensure there are no surprises.

    Ethylurea

    Urea derivatives have played a useful role in drug discovery due to their strong hydrogen bonding potential with proteins and other targets. This feature makes them strong candidates for antibacterial, anticancer, and anticonvulsive drug therapies.(35) In general, linear methyl- and ethylureas are a topic of investigation because of their antiviral or antibacterial properties; however, they are usually more stable when incorporated into the backbone of a molecule due to their minimal hydrolysis compared to amide bonds. To the best of our knowledge, only two ethylureas (lisuride and celiprolol, both diethyl substituted) and two methylureas (ritonavir amd cariprazine, both monosubstituted) have been FDA-approved. Most FDA-approved compounds containing a urea are unsubstituted or cyclized within a ring system, or one nitrogen is part of a sulfonamide moiety. This could be due to the fact that ethylureas have been identified as potential culprits in RM formation.(33) In previous work, ethyl- and methylureas have been considered mutagenic, which could be a reason they are utilized to a lesser extent as substituents in drug scaffolds.(32) Another possible explanation for the lack of unsubstituted ethylurea being used in drugs scaffolds is the potential to solvate with water by hydrogen bonds and cause poor membrane permeability.(34)
    In a series of antibacterial benzimidazole ureas, Grillot et al. found their lead compound 70 was covalently binding to liver proteins in vivo and in vitro.(33) They uncovered a −2 amu metabolite on the ethylurea motif and proposed that an aliphatic carbon on the ethylurea underwent α-hydroxylation 71 as shown in Scheme 9. Consequently, the hydroxylated metabolite loses water and forms an imine intermediate 72. This mechanism is mediated by CYP3A4, CYP1A2, and CYP2D6. After attempting to replace the ethylurea with several isosteres unsuccessfully, the authors planned a metabolic shift strategy by adding metabolic “soft spots” or easily metabolized groups (1-pyrazole 75 and 2-tetrahydrofuran 74) as substituents to the benzimidazole. From a medicinal chemistry perspective, both substituents maintain the necessary intramolecular forces for ligand preorganization and planarity, two properties that increased the binding affinity of the compounds. Both substituents also exhibit lower clogP values, a favorable property due to the correlation between high clogP values and increased toxicity.(35)

    Dealkylation to Problematic Aldehydes

    Dealkylation reactions occur when hydroxylation by P450 ensues α to a heteroatom (O, N, S of 76), usually resulting in smaller, more easily excretable metabolites. The instability of a hemiaminal, hemiacetyl, or sulfur and oxygen bound to the same carbon 77 will cause the heteroatom–carbon bond to break (Scheme 10) resulting in XH 78 and an aldehyde. This aldehyde 79 is usually further metabolized to the benign alcohol 81 or a carboxylic acid 80, though there are cases where aldehyde formation turns out to be toxic.(38) The local chemical environment of the aldehyde resulting from this mechanism plays an important role in its resulting reactivity.
    As an example, terbinafine 82 was found to undergo N-dealkylation to produce a reactive aldehyde 83.(37) The authors noted this unsaturated aldehyde is a Michael acceptor and reacted with GSH by either a 1,4- 84 or 1,6-addition 85 to form two adducts (Scheme 11). The case of terbinafine may be extreme with the formation of the Michael acceptor as a byproduct of the dealkylation, and similar results were found with other α,β-unsaturated carbonyl derivatives with varying side groups.(38) Their work found that short chain aldehydes and longer alkanals were hard electrophiles (see compound 86 as an example), and amino acid residues such as lysine 87 could be biological targets resulting in Schiff base products 88. The carbon–carbon double bond in α,β-unsaturated carbonyl derivatives such as alkenals and α-oxoaldehydes are softer electrophiles (see 89 as an example compound) and will likely target cysteine 90 residues leading to Michael addition product 91 (Scheme 11). When the reactivity of aldehydes resulting from N-dealkylation reactions was assessed through computational methods, the authors suggested a novel explanation for the hepatoxicity of several FDA-approved drugs including indinavir, piperacillin, verapamil, and ziprasidone.(36) According to the computational model, the aldehydes formed from the dealkylation reaction of these compounds were more reactive than the parent and all the other reported metabolites (Scheme 12).

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The examples presented herein aim to highlight the importance of working closely with metabolism scientists during the initial drug discovery process, since no compound or moiety can be assumed to be benign or “safe.” Time-dependent inhibition experiments are crucial to determine if reactive metabolites are formed, while well thought-out metabolite identification experiments can help to identify the substructures responsible for RM formation. This close relationship between chemist and metabolism scientists will prove invaluable and should serve to decrease the potential for DILI in the clinic. Nevertheless, the avoidance of structural alerts in drug design is still warranted and medicinal chemistry approaches to evade bioactivation have been nicely outlined.(39) This review summarized several presumed moieties that, given their chemical context, were bioactivated and led to in vivo or in vitro complications. It should be noted that even if a compound is bioactivated to a reactive intermediate and the compound binds to liver protein, it does not necessarily mean the compound will be toxic, and oftentimes covalent binding studies alone cannot distinguish toxic from nontoxic drugs.(40) Prediction methods for determining if covalent binding will have an effect and cause idiosyncratic adverse drug reactions have improved, and daily covalent binding burden should be taken into account.(41) For the medicinal chemist and DMPK scientist, a strong alliance should be formed to evaluate presumably safe functional groups within their specified molecular context so that the most effective and least harmful therapies make it to the clinic. We do not however recommend these motifs to be included as structural alerts since the bioactivation seems to be rare; however, we do recommend checking for bioactivation on lead compounds advancing along the drug discovery pipeline. With this in mind, we have no doubt that the development of small molecule drugs with improved safety profiles will continue to improve as long as we continue to expect the unexpected for the sake of making safer drugs.
