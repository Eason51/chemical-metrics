    Abstract

    The popularity of fragment-based drug discovery (FBDD) is demonstrated by the number of recent successful fragment-to-lead (F2L) publications. This Miniperspective provides a tabulated summary of the F2L literature published in the year 2016, along with discussion of general trends. It uses the same format as our summary of the 2015 literature and is intended to be a resource for both FBDD practitioners and medicinal chemists in general.

    Fragment-based drug discovery (FBDD) continues to be a popular technology for generating chemical leads across several different protein families. A previous Miniperspective provided a tabulated overview of fragment-to-lead (F2L) literature published during the calendar year 2015, together with a short commentary on trends observed.(1) The aim was to provide a resource for the medicinal chemistry community and to capture the state-of-the-art in FBDD for the time period in question. This Miniperspective has the same purpose, but the focus now is on articles published during the year 2016. In order to identify relevant articles for inclusion, searches were performed using SciFinder(2) for articles having publication year 2016 (as defined by the primary citation) AND any of the following phrases: fragment-based, fragment hit, fragment hits, fragment screen, fragment screening, fragment library, fragment growth, fragment linking, fragment design, fragment-like, fragment binding, fragment optimization, fragment-derived, and fragment discovery. In addition, we included articles having publication year 2016 that cited a key 2009 review.(3) The combined answer set of >350 unique articles was manually triaged using objective criteria, as in the 2015 Miniperspective, in order to generate the final list of articles for inclusion in the tabulated summary. The guiding principles for inclusion were the following: (i) the starting point was derived from a recognizable FBDD approach and (ii) optimization of the fragment hit gave a meaningful affinity increase, appropriate for an early lead. For consistency with the earlier article, identical criteria have been employed, with the exception of the publication year, viz.,

    • Fragment hit has molecular weight of <300.

    • Fragment hit must originate from a recognizable screening process (e.g., bioassay, biophysical method, X-ray, virtual screen, or any combination of these modalities) or by deconstruction of a known ligand.

    • Potency/affinity of lead is equal to or better than 2 Î¼M AND the change in potency/affinity between fragment and lead is at least 100-fold.

    • Date of publication falls within the calendar year 2016.

    Where fragment to lead chemistry from a single fragment starting point is spread over more than one journal article, the authors have applied the same guidelines as in the previous Miniperspective: the article where the inclusion criteria threshold is crossed for the first time is included in the tabulated summary and prior articles in which interim leads do not meet the potency/affinity or fold improvement benchmarks are cited. In setting these criteria, there have been inevitably some high quality articles that have not achieved lead potency/affinity criteria, particularly for challenging biological targets.(4â7) Similarly, articles that report the continued advancement of fragment-derived leads reported before 2016 are not covered. As FBDD continues to impact medicinal chemistry, the number of such articles is likely to increase.

    A total of 28 F2L campaigns met the inclusion criteria (Table 1), a similar number as for the 2015 survey,(1) and once again a wide variety of organizations are represented across both industry and academia. Around half of the examples feature collaborations between two or more separate organizations, underlining the importance of collaborative models in todayâs drug discovery environment. Enzyme targets are by far the best-represented category (Figure 1A) with 23 entries, though kinases as a proportion have lower representation than in 2015 (5 compared to 9). Of the four proteinâprotein interaction (PPI) entries, three target bromodomains, a subclass of PPI that is at the more chemically tractable end of the spectrum. In contrast to 2015, there was no GPCR example, but there was a single example of lead generation for a transcriptional repressor (Table 1, entry 28), where the compounds were shown to modulate a proteinâDNA interaction, presumably by an allosteric mechanism. This example represents an application of FBDD to an unusual mode of action, and it will be interesting to monitor the situation with respect to such nonstandard modalities in the future.

    Choice of screening technique for 2016 F2L articles was also analyzed (Figure 1B and Table 1) and showed a similar picture to 2015, in that biochemical assay, thermal shift (Tm), and ligand observed NMR constituted more than half of the total of screening assays used. Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) and X-ray are better represented than was the case for 2015, and while it is difficult to draw firm conclusions from a small number of examples, the increasing use of these techniques for screening is consistent with messages heard at recent FBDD conferences and from polls conducted on the Practical Fragments Web site.(45) Good quality structural data were central to the vast majority of examples: 25/28 used X-ray crystal structures at some point in the optimization, and 20/28 obtained an X-ray crystal structure of the fragment starting point or a closely related compound. New fragment screening techniques continue to be developed, and entry 24 is notable for featuring microscale thermophoresis among the assays used for fragment hit identification.

    Medicinal chemists have multiple strategies available for advancing fragment hits, such as fragment growing, merging, and linking. For a number of the examples in Table 1, primary fragment screening hits were obtained which bound to distinct regions of the main binding pocket. Fragment binding can be considered as a means of mapping available interaction hotspots on a protein surface, and therefore multiple fragment binding regions present an excellent opportunity to increase potency by using distal binding events to guide growth from the chosen fragment starting point, as demonstrated by entries 10, 11, 20, 21, and 26. Alternatively overlap between fragments binding to different regions of the binding site can suggest fragment merging as an appropriate strategy as shown by entry 28. Use of a second-site screen is a well-established method for identifying secondary binding hotspots for proteins.(46) Entry 6 (BACE1) is notable for employing 19F NMR screening in the presence of a blocking ligand to identify fragments binding in a secondary site, and these were subsequently elaborated into a sub-nM lead by a linking approach. Fragment linking of hits from a primary X-ray screen is well illustrated in the case of CK2Î± (entry 3). Second- or even third-site fragment screens can be of great value for identifying subsidiary hotspots as a means of improving affinity of advanced leads, with a notable example describing the discovery of picomolar Mcl-1 antagonists.(47)

    Synthetic accessibility is a key consideration for fragment elaboration. For the examples in Table 1, at least one new side chain is formally added to the starting fragment and the most common synthetic vector or growth point is an aromatic CH. Polar functional groups on the fragments are often key binding motifs (pharmacophores) and are therefore not generally used for synthetic modification. The application of organic synthesis to FBDD is assuming greater importance as evidenced by a number of publications during 2016, describing either de novo synthesis of fragments(48â50) or the synthetic challenges and opportunities presented by fragment elaboration.(51,52)

    For articles published in 2015, we provided analysis of physicochemical properties of fragments and leads. For molecular weight, the distribution of 2016 fragments and leads (Table 1 and Figure 2A) is similar to that reported in the previous article, as is the distribution of ClogP (Figure 2B). As was observed previously, the distribution of both parameters is right-shifted for leads in comparison with fragments. Ligand efficiency (LE) distribution is very similar between fragments and leads (Figure 2C), as was seen for 2015 examples. We also plotted the change in LE for each table entry (Figure 2D); interestingly and in contrast to anecdotal reports that LE tends to decline during the F2L process, LE decreased during optimization for only a minority of examples. However, this finding should be treated with caution because the data analyzed here are biased toward publishable F2L campaigns, and the examples in Table 1 only include those that pass predefined progression criteria.

    There has been much discussion in the FBDD community about the importance of three-dimensionality with respect to proteinâligand recognition and the perceived value of 3D fragments for particular classes of targets. We therefore plotted deviation from planarity (DFP),(53) a measure of three-dimensionality or shapeliness, for fragments and leads (Figure 3A). As would be expected, leads are right-shifted compared to fragments, given their greater size and complexity. When table entries are plotted in order of increasing fragment DFP (Figure 3B), the shapeliness of the fragment does not appear to correlate with that of the corresponding lead. However, in nearly all cases there is an expected increase in DFP value on progression from fragment to lead. A scatter plot of DFP for fragments and leads also demonstrates the lack of correlation (Figure 3C, R2 = 0.11) and, moreover, that there is no discernible clustering by target class. The data set presented here is small, and it is difficult to draw firm conclusions but is consistent with published analysis of Astex fragments and leads.(54) It is clear that even relatively planar fragments can furnish leads with extensive 3D shape, and the suggestion that certain types of target require more three-dimensional fragments is open to question; collection of further statistics in this area could be valuable.

    In conclusion, FBDD continues to thrive as a field as evidenced by successful F2L campaigns conducted in 2016. Possible trends to watch for the future are increasing use of SPR and other new biophysical techniques for detection of very weak binding fragments, more extensive use of X-ray screening,(55,56) further application of FBDD to allosteric sites, application of cutting-edge synthetic methodology (such as CH-activation) to enable efficient fragment growth, and more fragment-derived compounds progressing through clinical trials.
