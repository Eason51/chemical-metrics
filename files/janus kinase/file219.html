    Abstract

    Targeting signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) is a potential anticancer strategy. However, STAT3 inhibitors with good selectivity and bioavailability are rare. The aim of this study was to discover selective direct STAT3 inhibitors with good druglikeness. By the advanced multiple ligand simultaneous docking (AMLSD) method, compound 9 was designed as an orally bioavailable STAT3 inhibitor that presented superior druggability and selectivity compared with other representative STAT3 inhibitors. 9 directly and selectively inhibited the pY705 site of STAT3 with an affinity (Ki) of 440 nM. The IC50 of 9 for MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells was 184-fold lower than its IC50 for MCF-10A normal breast epithelial cells. 9 in vivo induced significant antitumor responses (better than gefitinib), and its therapeutic index should be over 100, indicating good safety of 9.

    Introduction

    Jump To
    Constitutive activated signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) protein is frequently detected in many malignancies,(1-3) representing one of the most promising anticancer targets.(4, 5) Activation of the JAK-STAT signaling pathway can be initiated by the binding of cytokines, hormones, and growth factors to the cell surface receptors. Activated receptors phosphorylate JAK kinases, which then phosphorylate STAT3 protein. Phosphorylated STAT3 (p-STAT3) proteins form dimers, which then translocate into the nucleus and bind to DNA, causing cell replication and cancers.(6, 7) p-STAT3 can be dimerized by the formation of salt bridges between the R609 residue in the SH2 domain and the pY705 residue of the native peptide. In the STAT3 SH2 domain, there are two âhot spotsâ, the pY705 (also named pY+0) site and a nearby pY+X site.(8) The pY705 site is related to the disruption of STAT3 phosphorylation and dimerization of inhibitors, while the nearby pY+X site is associated with the selectivity of STAT3 inhibitors. Therefore, compounds targeting the pY705 and pY+X sites of STAT3 can selectively inhibit both STAT3 phosphorylation and dimerization. In addition, phosphorylation of Ser727 (pS727) of STAT3 enhances transcriptional activation, and acetylation of Lys685 (Ac-K685) is crucial for methylation of tumor-suppressor gene promoters.(9, 10) Thus, proximal sites, pS727 and Ac-K685 sites, are also functional in tumor cell proliferation.
    The STAT family includes seven members, among which STAT3 and STAT5 are oncoproteins, but STAT1 plays an opposite role of STAT3 in carcinomas by triggering an antiproliferative effect in tumorigenesis.(3) STAT5 plays a critical role in lobuloalveolar proliferation, whereas STAT3 regulates lobuloalveolar apoptosis during pregnancy.(11) In addition, STAT3 is structurally most similar to STAT1 and STAT5. Hence, designing compounds with highly selective inhibitory activity toward STAT3 over STAT1 and STAT5 is difficult and critical for precision anticancer therapy.
    Strategies for direct deactivation of STAT3 mainly focus on the disruption of STAT3 dimerization and phosphorylation. Several series of direct STAT3 inhibitors have been discovered via both computational and experimental methods, represented by phosphor-peptide mimics,(12, 13) non-peptide small-molecule inhibitors,(7, 8, 14-16) metal-based inhibitors,(6) and natural-product-like inhibitors.(17, 18) Some of them have entered clinical trials.(19-23) However, there are no marketed STAT3 inhibitors because of poor selectivity, undesirable pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) properties, or other reasons. Therefore, the improvement of selectivity and druggability will substantially advance the discovery of STAT3 inhibitors as precision anticancer agents.
    Computer-aided drug design is widely utilized for the efficient identification and optimization of lead compounds.(24-27) With the aim of designing druggable STAT3 inhibitors, the multiple ligand simultaneous docking (MLSD) method was advanced in two aspects, i.e., fragment library design and linker design (Figure 1). Using advanced MLSD (AMLSD), we designed compounds that directly inhibit both phosphorylation and dimerization of STAT3 protein, offering an orally available, potent, and selective STAT3 inhibitor, 9, which presented superior druggability or selectivity compared with other representative non-peptide small STAT3 inhibitors (Figure 2 and Table 1).(7, 8, 14, 15, 19, 28, 29) For example, 9 has better druggability than 14,(7) which violates Lipinskiâs rules.

    Design by the AMLSD Method

    The first step was design of the fragment libraries. Drug scaffolds (MW < 300) were categorized into six fragment libraries according to their physical properties, including polarity, aromaticity, bulkiness, charge, and acidity/alkalinity. The second step was screening of fragment combinations (FCs). Naphthalene-5,8-dione-1-sulfonamide (fragment 1) was paired with each fragment from the polar fragment libraries, forming 963 FCs, which were simultaneously docked to the crystal structure of STAT3 SH2 domain (PDB entry 1BG1). In the resultant multifragment virtual screening, the FCs were ranked by their binding energies (Table S1 in the Supporting Information (SI)). The binding modes of the 25 top-ranked FCs are shown in Figure 3. The third step was linker design. Possible linear linkers for the selected FCs were designed by the measured distances. Since the measured distances between two fragments were in the range of 3â5 Ã (Table S1), the length of the linkers should be within the range of 3â5 Ã. All of the proper linear linkers are listed in Figure 4. Suitable linkers for the FCs were then designed on the basis of two criteria: the feasibility of the synthesis and the superposition of the docking modes of the linked molecules and the separate FCs. Finally, linker a1 could connect two fragments through a CâN coupling reaction while the linked compounds could reposition to their FCs. Thus, we chose a1 as an ideal linker to connect the FCs. The fourth step involved synergistic hit selection. FCs were linked and docked to the STAT3 protein. Their docking modes were repositioned to those of the prelinked FCs. If the linked compound could reposition to its FCs and, in addition, the original contacts between the FCs and protein could be maintained and even more good contacts could be formed after linkage, then we considered the FCs to be synergistically linked. Finally, 10 out of 25 compounds were chosen with synergistic effects (Figure 5 and Table S2). For example, the docking modes of the linked compound 9 could be repositioned to the docking modes of its FCs (no. 175) (Figure 6). Moreover, additional intermolecular hydrogen bonds were formed between the linker of 9 and the Ser636 residue, which enhanced the synergistic effects. Finally, the drug-like parameters of the hits were computed, including the ADME and toxicity properties, to predict all of the compounds with decent drug-like properties.
    In order to investigate the selectivities of the 10 compounds for STAT1/3/5 in silico, the compounds were also docked to the crystal structures of STAT1 (PDB entry 1BF5) and STAT5 (PDB entry 1Y1U).(30) Compound 1 was eliminated since it was also docked to the STAT1 SH2 domain with a free energy of â7.8 kcal/mol. Compounds 3, 4, 6, and 8 were excluded since they were weakly bound to the STAT5 SH2 domain. Limited by the availability of the starting materials, compound 10 was not synthesized. Finally, compounds 2, 5, 7, and 9 were synthesized for further biological tests.

    Synthesis of the Designed Compounds

    Reactions between fragment 1 and aromatic amines were catalyzed by Cu(OAc)2Â·H2O in an acidic environment. The reaction system was heated to 65 Â°C, and the reaction was terminated when 1 was consumed completely. The atom numbering of 1 is shown in Scheme 1. The temperature of the reaction was a key reason for the formation of the cyclized byproducts since the sulfonamide group can attack the nearby 8-ketone with loss of a water molecule to form the cyclized products. A higher temperature can cause a lower yield of the target compounds. For the synthesis of compound 9, to ensure selective reaction with the aromatic amine group of the reactant, the piperazine amine was protected with a Cbz group initially and then deprotected with trifluoroacetic acid (TFA).

    Cell Assays

    In a preliminary in vitro evaluation of the designed compounds, 9 exhibited better potency than the other compounds against a human basal-like breast cancer cell line, MDA-MB-231 (Figure 7A). However, its IC50 for MCF-10A normal breast epithelial cells was 128.9 Î¼M, which is 184-fold higher than that for MDA-MB-231 (0.7 Î¼M). Thus, 9 was highly selective in targeting of cancer cells. 14 was selected as the positive control since it is a representative orally available STAT3 inhibitor. Compound 9 was more potent than 14 in killing multiple types of cancer cells, i.e., the UW426, UW288-1, BKPC3, MDA-MB-231, and U2OS cell lines (Figure 7B).

    Compound 9 is a Direct STAT3 Inhibitor

    Compound 9 was predicted to be a direct STAT3 SH2 domain inhibitor by both docking and molecular dynamics (MD) simulations. The intermolecular interactions between compound 9 and STAT3 were revealed. Analysis of the simulations showed that the sulfamine group of fragment 1 formed hydrogen bonds with residues Arg609, Ser611, Ser612, and Ser613 in the pY705 site. In detail, the 8-ketone group of fragment 1 as a major pharmacophore interacted with residues Arg609 and Lys591. The linker amine group contacted with residues Ser636 and Glu638 in the loop. The piperazinyl fragment formed an ionic bond with residue Glu594 and a hydrogen bond with Gln635 in the pY+X site (Figure 8). The root-mean-square deviations (RMSDs) of the snapshots during the MD simulations also reconfirmed the stable binding between compound 9 and STAT3 (Figure S2 in the SI).
    The direct binding between 9 and STAT3 protein was demonstrated by both the fluorescence polarization (FP)-based competitive binding assay and the microscale thermophoresis (MST) assay. 9 was proven to preferentially bind to the pY705 site of STAT3, competitively disrupting the binding of the native high-affinity, fluorescence-labeled phosphorylated peptide 5-FAM-SpYLPQTV with a Ki value of 0.44 Î¼M (Figure 9A). In contrast, there was no direct binding between compound 9 and STAT1/5 proteins. Thus, 9 was determined to be a selective STAT3 inhibitor by the FP assay. MST is a recently broadly used technology for the analysis of the interactions of biomolecules, and it was used to confirm that 9 directly bound to the STAT3 protein with a Ki value of 2.42 Â± 0.26 Î¼M. The ligand:protein molar ratio was determined to be 1:1 (Figure 9B).

    Selectivity of Compound 9

    The selectivity of 9 was investigated in several ways. (1) 9 selectively docked to the pY site of STAT3 protein in silico, distant from the pY sites of STAT1/5 proteins (Figure 10). (2) 9 selectively inhibited p-STAT3 at the pY705 site without altering the S727 and K685 sites. The level of p-STAT3(Y705) was reduced in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 11A), while the levels of acetylated STAT3(K685) and p-STAT3(S727) were not dose-dependently influenced by 9 and the expression of total STAT3 was not changed. This indicates that 9 selectively decreased the level of pY705-STAT3 and that the decrease was not due to a constitutional decrease in total STAT3 expression (Figure 11B). (3) In Western blot (WB) assays, 9 selectively inhibited p-STAT3(Y705) (Figure 11A) and interleukin 6 (IL-6)-induced phosphorylation of STAT3 (Figure 12A), without any inhibition of p-STAT1(Y701) (Figure 11B) and interferon-Î³ (IFN-Î³)-induced phosphorylation of STAT1 (Figure 12B). (4) In the electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA), 9 preferentially inhibited STAT3 homodimer DNA-binding activity over STAT5âSTAT5 DNA-binding activity in vitro in a time-dependent manner, indicating that 9 is a selective STAT3 inhibitor. 9 weakly decreased the level of STAT1 dimer DNA-binding activity, but there was no observable inhibition of IFN-Î³-induced STAT1âSTAT1 DNA binding by compound 9 (Figure 12C). Quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) showed that compound 9 decreased the expression of the STAT3-specific oncogene MMP9, while the expression of the STAT1-specific gene CDK2 was not significantly influenced upon treatment with 9 (Figure 12D). (5) The stimulation of STAT3 upstream targets, such as EGFR, can induce not only the level of p-STAT3 but also the levels of p-AKT and p-ERK1/2 kinases. To investigate whether compound 9 is selective to the JAK-STAT signaling pathway, we also evaluated the levels of p-AKT and p-ERK1/2 kinases in bypass pathways. The phosphorylation levels of both kinases were not inhibited by 9 either (Figures 11C and S8). 9 showed better selectivity than 14 toward p-AKT, since the level of p-AKT was decreased by 14 but was not affected by 9 in the MDA-MB-231 cancer cell line (Figure 11C). In all, compound 9 selectively inhibited pY705-STAT3 protein.

    Compound 9 Induced the Expression of SHP-1

    Interestingly, 9 was found to induce the expression of STAT3 phosphatase (SHP-1). The mechanism is still unknown, but the effect may partially contribute to the low IC50 values of 9 (Figure 11B,D).

    Compound 9 Induced Apoptosis in Malignant Cancers

    The inhibition of STAT3 phosphorylation by 9 seemed to be consistent with the induction of apoptosis, as evidenced by the increase of cleaved caspase-3 in the MDA-MB-231 cancer cell line (Figure 11C).

    Compound 9 Inhibited the Nuclear Translocation of p-STAT3 to the Nucleus

    The IL-6-induced level of p-STAT3 in the cytoplasm and nucleus treated with 9 was lower than that upon treatment with 14 as determined by immunofluorescence staining, suggesting that 9 blocked STAT3 nuclear translocation with a better effect than 14 (Figure 13).

    In Vivo Study of Compound 9

    To investigate the safety profile of compound 9, the acute toxicity of 9 was tested in ICR mice at doses of 200, 400, 600, 800, and 1000 mg/kg (i.p., n = 10 per group). All of the mice were vital and lively even when treated at the highest dose (1000 mg/kg), and thus, the median lethal dose (LD50) could not be obtained. Since 70% tumor weight was shrunk at 10 mg/kg 9 (Figure S9), the therapeutic index of 9 should be over 100 (1000/10 = 100), indicating good safety of 9.
    First, we found that 9 orally inhibited the growth of cancer xenografts with better therapeutic effects than the first-line anticancer drug gefitinib and with less toxicity than doxorubicin. The nude mice tumor xenografts were injected subcutaneously with human breast cancer MDA-MB-231 cells. 9 orally shrank 53% tumor weight at 2.5 mg/kg, 60% tumor weight at 5 mg/kg, and 70% tumor weight at 10 mg/kg, whereas for gefitinib, a dosage of 40 mg/kg was needed to achieve similar therapeutic effects as 9 at 10 mg/kg, indicating that 9 is more active than gefitinib (Figures 14 and S9). The body weight after treatment with either gefitinib or 9 increased normally (Figures 14D and S9D), and no damage to the major organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung and kidney) was detected by hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining (Figure S9B).
    The IC50 of 9 was first evaluated as 0.42 Î¼M in the S180 cancer cell line. In the mouse sarcoma tumor xenografts, 73% tumor weight was significantly (p < 0.001) shrunk by 9 after a 21 day therapy (Figure 15A,C,E), with 9 at 5 mg/kg showing much lower side effects than doxorubicin at 1 mg/kg. Dramatic loss of body weight was observed from the 13th day upon treatment with doxorubicin, whereas for 9 the increase in body weight was normal (Figure 15D,F). Additionally, the sizes of the liver, spleen, and kidney upon treatment with doxorubicin were notably smaller than those upon treatment with 9 (Figure 15B).
    Second, we observed that 9 disabled STAT3 activity in vivo. The in vivo inhibitory activity of 9 against the overexpression level of p-STAT3 was studied by both immunohistochemical (IHC) analysis and WB. From the IHC results, 9 showed dose-dependent inhibitory effects on p-STAT3 (Figure 16). The results were confirmed by the WB results, which showed that 9 dose-dependently inhibited the level of p-STAT3 but had little influence on the level of total STAT3 (Figure 17).
    Third, the oral bioavailability of compound 9 was measured as 44.7 Â± 11.9%, which is comparable to that of gefitinib (47.4 Â± 0.9%), by a PK study. The PK parameters of 9 were well-comparable to those of gefitinib too (Table 2). The changes in plasma concentrations in rats from 2 min to 24 h upon treatment with 9 (i.v. and p.o.) are shown in Figure 18. These results indicate that 9 is an orally bioavailable candidate. The method was fully validated according to the FDA guidelines for selectivity, linearity, lower limits of quantification (LLOQ), accuracy, precision, recovery, matrix effect, and stability (Tables S8âS11).

    PAINS Analysis

    All of the designed compounds were filtered by the online filter for pan-assay interference compounds (PAINS) (http://zinc15.docking.org/patterns/home/). The quinone group was detected, which is a common structure in natural products. It is also a substructure for endogenous molecules, such as vitamin K, and some approved drugs, such as mitomycin, menadione, and idebenone. To test the potential reactivity of the Î±,Î²-unsaturated carbonyl group as a Michael acceptor site in the quinone group, compound 9 and cysteamine were mixed in DMSO or water solutions at 37 Â°C overnight. No reaction was found, as monitored by TLC (see the SI), indicating that 9 is not very reactive.
    The experimental results proved the good activity, selectivity, and safety of compound 9. The STAT3 siRNA transfection experiment demonstrated that the major antitumor target for compound 9 is STAT3 and that compound 9 is selective for STAT3 protein (see the SI). For the cell-based assays, both the MCF-10A normal breast epithelial cell line and the MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cell line were treated with compound 9. The IC50 for normal cells, 128.9 Î¼M, was 184-fold higher than that for cancer cells, 0.7 Î¼M, indicating that compound 9 selectively targets the tumor cells with good safety. For the acute toxicity study, mice were vital and lively, even upon oral treatment with 1 g/kg compound 9, indicating that its therapeutic index should be over 100. The growth of the body weight of mice was also not affected by compound 9, although the body weight of mice was significantly decreased by doxorubicin. No damage of the major organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney) upon treatment with 9 was detected by H&E staining (Figure 14B). The sizes of the liver, spleen, and kidney treated with doxorubicin were notably smaller than those upon treatment with 9 too (Figure 15B). All of the in vivo results indicated that compound 9 had better safety than the first-line anticancer drug doxorubicin. In addition, in the protein-based assays, compound 9 was proved to be a direct inhibitor of STAT3 protein. Therefore, both the in vitro and in vivo results point to compound 9 as a nonpromiscuous, selective STAT3 inhibitor.

    Discussion

    Jump To
    By the AMLSD method, multiple fragments are simultaneously docked to one target, which can cover the chemical spaces of binding sites better than hopping of one ligand. MLSD was advanced in fragment library design and the evolution process. The fragment library design is crucial for successful drug design. In our study, fragments were derived from the drug scaffolds aiming to design more druggable STAT3 inhibitors. Old drugs might ârepurposeâ for new uses.(27) The building blocks of clinical drugs share many interesting properties, and thus, there is a higher chance to find drug candidates by designing compounds derived from drug scaffolds.(27) In addition, AMLSD determines linkers in a manner reminiscent of the BRIDGE program in LUDI.(31) For example, different xylenes were determined by distance measurement in our case. Since the intermolecular interactions between fragments have been considered by AMLSD, there is a higher chance that the linked compounds could be repositioned to their fragment combinations. For previous applications of the MLSD method, the fragment library was designed according to the similarity of fragments to the scaffolds of known inhibitors. Then the selected fragments were grouped as FCs, resulting a very limited number of FCs before docking. The number of final linked compounds is small and determined by the variety of known inhibitors. If drug targets lack known inhibitors, then it would be hard to apply the MLSD method to design compounds. AMLSD can overcome the above problems. The selection of fragments no longer relies on the scaffolds of known inhibitors. Thus, AMLSD is more useful to design candidates for a target that is not so well-studied.
    Compound 9 was found to selectively bind to the p-STAT3(Y705) protein rather than STAT1/5 proteins, p-AKT and p-ERK kinases in bypass pathways, and other proximal sites. 9 also selectively targeted the MDA-MB-231 cancer cells by significantly disabling the p-STAT3 level. The IC50 of 9 for normal cells was 184-fold higher than that for breast cancer cells. For the animal study, the growth of the body weight of mice was not affected by compound 9, which had much less toxicity than the first-line anticancer drug doxorubicin. The therapeutic index of 9 should be over 100. In protein, cell, and animal levels, 9 was proved to exhibit good selectivity and safety.
    Compound 9 was found to be a direct small-molecule inhibitor (MW < 500) of STAT3 with a very low Ki value (440 nM). 9 also showed very low IC50 values in different cancer cell lines. 9 had excellent aqueous solubility. The oral bioavailability of 9 was 44.7% by PK study, comparable to that of the first-line drug gefitinib (47.4%). Thus, 9 is an orally bioavailable STAT3 inhibitor. All of its physicochemical properties follow Lipinskiâs rule of five. In a comprehensive assessment of 9 and other representative STAT3 inhibitors, 9 showed outstanding features among all. Further experiments are needed to evaluate the potential of 9 to undertake clinical trials.

    Conclusion

    AMLSD was used to design new STAT3 inhibitors by screening of FCs built from the library of drug scaffolds. Among these compounds, 9 was proved to directly and selectively bind to the pY705 site of STAT3 protein, which significantly decreased the level of STAT3 phosphorylation/dimerization as a dual-functional inhibitor. 9 also significantly shrank the tumor weight of both breast cancer and sarcoma cancer xenographs in vivo with better activity than the first-line drug gefitinib and less toxicity than doxorubicin. The therapeutic index of 9 should be over 100, indicating good safety of 9.

    Analysis of Binding Sites

    There are two âhot spotsâ in the STAT3 SH2 domain, the pY705 and pY+X sites. According to the crystal structure of the pY+X site of STAT3 (PDB entry 1BG1), several key polar residues were embedded inside and formed a polar binding region. On the basis of the structural analysis of the crystallized STAT1, STAT3, and STAT5, the pY705 site is highly conserved but the pY+X site varies within this family of proteins, which plays an important role in the selectivity of STAT inhibitors. We combined fragment 1 with each fragment of the polar fragment libraries to perform the hopping of FCs in the two adjacent pY705 and pY+X sites, aiming to design selective inhibitors of STAT3.

    Preparation of Fragment Libraries

    The fragment libraries in our study were generated from 1554 FDA-approved drugs, 4959 experimental drugs, 84 nutraceutical drugs, and 521 investigational drugs collected by the Drugbank database (https://www.drugbank.ca/). The complete database of all drugs was downloaded and prepared by LigPrep of SchrÃ¶dinger using the default parameters.(33) Fragments were extracted from the drugs using the scaffold decomposition program in Canvas of SchrÃ¶dinger with the default parameters.(22) Fragments were calculated with MW and Polar using physicochemical descriptors by Canvas of SchrÃ¶dinger.(34) All of the scaffolds were exported, and the scaffolds with molecular weight higher than 300 were deleted.     Screening of FCs and Evolution of FCs to Final Hits

    Two fragments as one fragment combination were grouped to probe the two âhot spotsâ of the STAT3 SH2 domain, the pY705 and pY+X sites. Fragment 1, which was the privileged structure of the two most potent STAT3 SH2 inhibitors, 16 and 17,(8, 35) formed two dipoleâionic interactions with residue R609 in the pY705 site, as analyzed by MD simulations (Figure S3). Thus, fragment 1 was chosen for the inhibition of the pY705 site while fragments fitting the pY+X site were screened from the newly designed fragment libraries. Since the pY+X site of STAT3 has a key polar binding region, fragments fitting to the pY+X site were screened from the four polar fragment libraries, giving a total of 963 fragments, including 280 polar uncharged nonaromatic fragments, 532 polar uncharged aromatic fragments, 61 polar charged basic fragments, and 90 polar charged acidic fragments. The PDB file of each fragment from the four polar fragment libraries was grouped together with the PDB file of fragment 1 and finally formatted as one PDB file, resulting in 963 fragment combinations. Each FC included two fragments.
    The FCs best fit for the pY705 and pY+X sites were docked to the crystal structure of STAT3 SH2 domain, scored by the PSO method,(32) and then ranked by their docking energies.
    The evolution of fragments to one compound was guided by the docking modes of the FCs, which were no longer determined by designersâ chemical intuition or experience. The shortest distances between C6 or C7 of fragment 1 and the heavy atoms of the other fragment were around 3â5 Ã (Table S1), as measured by Maestro of SchrÃ¶dinger. For example, for compound 9, the shortest distance between C6 of fragment 1 and the other 2-(piperazin-1-yl)aniline fragment was 3.671 Ã (Figure 5). Thus, the length of possible linear linkers should be within the range listed in Figure 4. Every linker was tried to link the FCs, and the linker a1 was finally chosen on the basis of synthetic feasibility and its repositioning ability. Linked compounds were repositioned to the SH2 domain, scored by the Lamarckian Genetic Algorithm (LGA) of Autodock4, and ranked by binding energy. Final hits were selected on the basis of both the docking energies and their repositioned docking modes. They were then synthesized and tested by all kinds of biological assays.
    The docking procedure involved the preparation of the ligand and macromolecule using the SchrÃ¶dinger software.(33, 36) AutoDockTools were used to assign Gasteiger charges to the ligands. After 10 million energy evaluations, all of the resulting conformations of the ligands in the binding pocket of the macromolecule were clustered into groups according to their conformations with a root-mean-square deviation threshold of 1.5 Ã. The most significant low-energy clusters were identified, and their binding energies were evaluated.(37)

        The stress response mediator ATF3 represses androgen signaling by binding the androgen receptor
        Wang, Hongbo; Jiang, Ming; Cui, Hongmei; Chen, Mengqian; Buttyan, Ralph; Hayward, Simon W.; Hai, Tsonwin; Wang, Zhengxin; Yan, Chunhong
        Molecular and Cellular Biology (2012), 32 (16), 3190-3202CODEN: MCEBD4; ISSN:0270-7306. (American Society for Microbiology)
        Activating transcription factor 3 (ATF3) is a common mediator of cellular stress response signaling and is often aberrantly expressed in prostate cancer. The authors report that ATF3 can directly bind the androgen receptor (AR) and consequently repress AR-mediated gene expression. The ATF3-AR interaction requires the leucine zipper domain of ATF3 that independently binds the DNA-binding and ligand-binding domains of AR, and the interaction prevents AR from binding to cis-acting elements required for expression of androgen-dependent genes while inhibiting the AR N- and C-terminal interaction. The functional consequences of the loss of ATF3 expression include increased transcription of androgen-dependent genes in prostate cancer cells that correlates with increased ability to grow in low-androgen-contg. medium and increased proliferative activity of the prostate epithelium in ATF3 knockout mice that is assocd. with prostatic hyperplasia. The authors' results thus demonstrate that ATF3 is a novel repressor of androgen signaling that can inhibit AR functions, allowing prostate cells to restore homeostasis and maintain integrity in the face of a broad spectrum of intrinsic and environmental insults.
