    Abstract

    In silico tools to investigate absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and pharmacokinetics (ADME-PK) properties of new chemical entities are an integral part of the current industrial drug discovery paradigm. While many companies are active in the field, scientists engaged in this area do not necessarily share the same background and have limited resources when seeking guidance on how to initiate and maintain an in silico ADME-PK infrastructure in an industrial setting. This work summarizes the views of a group of industrial in silico and experimental ADME scientists, participating in the In Silico ADME Working Group, a subgroup of the International Consortium for Innovation through Quality in Pharmaceutical Development (IQ) Drug Metabolism Leadership Group. This overview on the benefits, caveats, and impact of in silico ADME-PK should serve as a resource for medicinal chemists, computational chemists, and DMPK scientists working in drug design to increase their knowledge in the area.

    1 Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In silico ADME-PK (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and pharmacokinetics) is the use of computer modeling to understand structure–property relationships and to predict DMPK (drug metabolism and pharmacokinetics) properties from compound structure. This is related but distinct from physiologically based pharmacokinetic (PBPK) modeling, which strives to provide accurate predictions of the PK profile of drug candidates. The focus of in silico ADME-PK is to guide the design of novel compounds with superior ADME properties. Most often a QSPR (quantitative structure–property relationship) approach is used to relate a compound’s structure to the property in question (e.g., cell permeability or metabolic clearance) measured in an in vitro assay. As the acronym ADME-PK implies, in vivo properties can also be the target of such efforts.
    Appropriately applied during the discovery phase, in silico ADME-PK has the potential to enhance the probability of identifying compounds with favorable ADME properties. Importantly, these in silico tools are not always intended to be in lieu of experimental measurements but are intricately connected with the preclinical assay they attempt to predict and are ideally applied by chemists and DMPK scientists alike, presynthetically to guide the next round of synthesis. The intended result of such effort is to accelerate the drug optimization process by reducing the number of cycles of synthesis necessary to identify a quality drug candidate. Additionally in silico ADME tools can be used to prioritize in vitro testing after synthesis.
    Computational ADME modeling is quite a mature but still evolving field.(1-4)In silico ADME tools are routinely applied to drug design. However, scientists engaged in this arena often witness how the use of the in silico ADME tools is largely dependent on the perception of the individuals within the project teams. This skepticism is often the result of lack of clarity on the scope and limitations of the model and of the assay being modeled. For example, one cannot expect a reliable quantitative model of thermodynamic aqueous solubility when we are not aware of the solid form of the material used in generating the solubility data.(5)
    Models, by definition, will provide numbers, and numbers can be misinterpreted if used out of context. This is also true of experimental values, which have associated variability and experimental error and need to be interpreted with caution, especially for some high-throughput screens. In a recent perspective article, Kuhn and coauthors(6) highlight the value of qualitative assessments in their closing notes. These authors invite us to not use absolute values but rather consider a more qualitative approach within the context of the project. They also suggest to “stay close to the experiments”. This should be interpreted, in our view, as a coupling of the use of computed physicochemical, in vitro, and in vivo readouts with a solid understanding of the experimental assay and its limitations. For example, cLogP can easily be calculated by one of several commercially available software packages. The validity of this calculated property for a given chemotype of interest should be validated by submitting a sufficient number of compounds for experimental log P/log D determinations. In other words, “trust but check” to ensure the models are applicable to the structural space of interest; this is a critical consideration that will be further discussed later.
    This review discusses key lessons learned by scientists actively working in the in silico ADME-PK field within drug discovery. The present impact will be discussed as well as the importance of paying attention to the purpose and quality of the experimental assay being modeled and the resulting implications in model building, evaluation, and use. The review further suggests individual components necessary to implement a successful in silico ADME-PK modeling culture and infrastructure in industry. Complementary discussions focusing on the technical aspects of building an ADME QSAR infrastructure can be found elsewhere.(7) While the work described focuses mainly on QSPR modeling, many of the learning points are transferrable to other technologies such as molecular matched pairs (MMPs) and chemical data mining, which will be discussed briefly before the conclusion of the manuscript. The technologies discussed in this work require availability of hundreds of consistently measured data points for each property target of the modeling effort; this could be a practical limitation for scientists working in small organizations approaching this field.

    2 Impact of in Silico ADME-PK Modeling

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The aim of in silico ADME-PK modeling is to improve the design of candidate drugs. Experimental end points that are most commonly targeted include metabolic stability, permeability, solubility, CYP inhibition, and transporter-mediated efflux. For these properties most members of our group reported using a “global” model, i.e., including all the available internal data in the training set, as opposed to a “local” model focusing on a specific chemotype. While good models are needed, the mere existence of high quality models does not guarantee success. Models can only be truly impactful if the organization embraces and fosters an in silico culture; this requires a diversified approach involving in silico scientists, DMPK scientists (in vitro and in vivo), chemists, and to some extent management. Figure 1 reports real life examples where models influenced the quality of compound design organization-wide.(8, 9) An in silico solubility model at AstraZeneca and an in silico microsomal stability model at Genentech had been available for years. Nevertheless, true impact could only be seen once usage was enforced through departmental goals in 2004 at AstraZeneca and in 2010 at Genentech. This can be done by creating the expectation that projects facing a ADME challenges should factor in silico ADME models into decision making or provide explanations if they do not. At AstraZeneca this resulted in a 7-fold increase in the number of compounds with good solubility, whereas at Genentech the number of stable compounds increased by 2-fold. The process was slightly different for the third example in Figure 1: a high-throughput assay for assessing time-dependent inhibition (TDI) of CYP3A4 was made available at Eli Lilly in mid-2009. After proactive enrichment of the TDI data to capture a wide range of structural diversity, a global in silico model was introduced in 2011. Subsequently the ratio of compounds with low vs high potential for CYP3A4 TDI improved by 3-fold (1.4 before 2011 and 4.3 after 2011).
    While high quality of a model is an absolute prerequisite and management may help a model gain momentum, an equally important aspect is continued education and support promoted by a core team composed of the following:

    • In silico ADME-PK experts with the capability to develop robust models (usually computational chemists or cheminformaticians with a keen interest in understanding ADME properties)

    • In silico and in vitro (or in vivo) experts monitoring model performance over time as well as experimental data quality

    • IT and infrastructure experts ensuring model access and ease of use

    • Early adopters (or “champions”) of the models positioned within the project team that can lead by example

    • Educators creating awareness by presenting in different forums

    • Management encouragement of the use of the models

    Furthermore, usage and thus the impact of a model depend on the quality of the software infrastructure as well as the ability to promote the model’s interplay and synergy with available in vitro tools. The latter point is very important and is tied to understanding decision drivers during design. In companies adopting a tiered screening strategy, where only compounds that are stable in microsomes (tier 1) will be tested in hepatocytes (tier 2), a good in silico filter for bad actors in the liver microsomes assay can effectively act as a tier 0 assay. In contrast, a model for stability in hepatocytes may be less useful as guidance for the tier 2 assay, since in vitro metabolic stability would already be available during candidate selection for the hepatocytes stability assay. The opposite could be true in a different paradigm where metabolic stability in hepatocytes is used as a tier 1 assay, for example, in a project where phase II metabolism is recognized as important for the metabolic clearance. In essence, the success of the in silico tools is inherently linked to the local company environment.
    Scientists at AstraZeneca reported that their in silico ADME-Tox modeling infrastructure was utilized to predict at least one property during the design stage for ∼55% of the compounds synthesized between 2011 and 2012.(8) While this does not inform on the quality of the predictions, it is a clear indication of their importance in decision-making. Another example of the impact of in silico ADME models was described by scientists at Eli Lilly and Company, based on an integrated approach involving an iterative learning cycle through in silico–in vitro–in vivo assessments.(10)
    In an effort to provide an estimate of the impact of in silico ADME-PK modeling in the industry today, 11 members of the In Silico ADME IQ Working Group answered a survey (Figure 2). The scientists who responded to this survey are industry experts working in some of the major pharmaceutical companies in U.S., Europe, and Asia. The findings of the survey are summarized as follows:

    • In silico ADME is perceived as an impactful reality in industry; yet, approximately 3 out of 4 of the members think that there is still room for growth.

    • QSPR predictors are the most established tools. Complementary approaches focused on interpretation and idea generation, such as MMPs and data trend analysis, are also widely adopted.

    • Structure-based modeling of ADME targets (e.g., CYPs, P-gp, PXR) can be useful but should be considered as a tool to study specific cases.

    It is important to point out that in silico predictions of metabolic soft spots and computer aided metabolite identification may be regarded as components of the in silico ADME toolbox but will not be reviewed here. This is an extremely active and impactful area at the interface between in silico and in vitro science but is beyond the scope of this work.

    3 Model Building Considerations

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The necessary starting point for all good and useful models is an in-depth understanding of the property that is being modeled, how that property factors into decision making, and how it is related to the in vivo situation. In addition, model usage often depends on how the experimental data are used, which will also influence the approach. A categorical filter for bad actors will not necessarily need to capture 80% of the experimental data within 2-fold from the prediction, while a continuous model with increased accuracy may be preferred for uses such as establishing an in silico to in vitro correlation around clearance.
    Some experimental end points lend themselves to being easily modeled as in the case of fraction unbound in human plasma (h-fup) (Table 1). This parameter is experimentally highly reproducible, leading to well-defined data and thus facilitating robust in silico models. However, protein binding is not a property that warrants optimization(11) but requires an accurate value since it is a cornerstone for understanding pharmacokinetic–pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) relationships. Thus, the impact of such a model will depend very much on whether a confidence can be assigned to any predicted value; an overall high R2 will not be sufficient. Estimating confidence in prediction is not a trivial task; this will be addressed later on in this work.

The model was applied prospectively to predict P-gp efflux category along with “confidence” scores associated with each prediction. It was demonstrated that the use of confidence scores improved the accuracy of prediction from 73% to 81%.

    3.1 Experimental Data

    All modeling efforts require the availability of experimental values, usually measured at one or several occasions under specific, mostly defined conditions. Such data show variability and inherent errors. For example efflux ratio measurements in the MDCK-MDR1 cell line are aimed at understanding whether a compound is substrate of the key efflux transporter P-gp. However, interaction with P-gp may actually be underestimated for compounds with low permeability, which some define as <1 × 10–6 cm·s–1 and some define as <5 × 10–6 cm·s–1. In contrast, compounds may be trapped in the cells and their efflux potential underestimated under the static conditions of the assay.(10) Understanding these errors, limitations, and data variability is key to understand which data should (or should not) be used for modeling. The popular mantra, “garbage in, garbage out” is a catchy representation of the fact that in silico model can only be as good as the experimental data upon which it is based.
    In general, one can assume that data from one source only is more likely to be consistent. However, outside industry it may be difficult to obtain data sets from one source that are sufficiently large and diverse. Compilation of multiple literature data sets is a potential approach but is quite time-consuming if done properly. Additionally, one needs to consider that end points known for their experimental reproducibility, such as log P,(13) are more likely to benefit from summarizing literature data than end points with known inherent interlaboratory variability, such as Caco-2 permeability or efflux in transfected MDCK cell lines. Industry data sets, on the other hand, have the advantage of better consistency and higher numbers, and the covered chemical space is usually relevant for the project compounds to be investigated. Nevertheless, such data sets also need to be carefully scrutinized,(14) especially since they are often made up of single determinations, for obvious reasons of speed and cost. Downloading data to train models, without a thorough understanding of the experimental assay and a strong collaboration with the colleagues who have generated the data, should be discouraged.
    The simplest way to estimate the variability in the experimental data is to use the average standard deviation for the measurements of the set of well-known compounds used during assay validation. Ideally the compounds are rerun at regular intervals and the variability is re-established. An alternative, which additionally enables a continuous check of the variability over time, is to calculate the standard deviations of the control compounds measured during each assay run. Both approaches can be expected to deliver similar results.(15) More sophisticated approaches such as minimum discriminant ratios, accounting for standard deviation and number of repeats, can be derived from control compounds and can be used to understand if measurements for two different compounds are truly different.(15, 16)
    Also, in industry, data from assays run under slightly different conditions are often combined to expand the data volume. Wenlock and Carlsson(14) discussed which experimental conditions need to be considered when modeling eight DMPK end points and calculated typical standard deviations on the data sets available in the AstraZeneca internal database. Between 350 and 1700 data points were found with at least three repeat measurements for each of the end points, accounting for 5–25% of the total number of compounds. The resulting standard deviations (log scale) fall between 0.11 (human hepatocyte intrinsic clearance, experimental values range from 3 to 300 (μL/min)/106 cells) and 0.28 (thermodynamic solubility, measured from solid starting material, values between 0.1 and 1500 μM). Furthermore, the data showed that for some assays (e.g., intrinsic clearance in human liver microsomes, HLM CLint) the standard deviations were heteroscedastic,that is, dependent on the actual value. Other in vitro assays such as log D showed a similar standard deviation over the whole value range (Figure 4).
    For in vitro scientists, it may not be surprising that an assay has higher variability close to the assay limit. However, this has important implications in projects where team members may be compelled to push the assays toward these limits. Resulting values may still be of use for a given project but have to be considered with care.(17, 18)
    Wenlock and Carlsson(14) also analyzed how data variability affects the model performance by defining high- and low-quality data sets based on a standard deviation criterion for a compound measured at least twice. The high quality data set was randomly split into a test and training set, whereas all low quality data were used to compile a “low quality training set”. Considering a standard deviation of 0.1, an ideal model could at best result in a root-mean-square error of prediction (RMSEP) of 0.15, taking into account simple error propagation. For a very diverse set of compounds, with data from high-throughput screens and with a great number of compound having N = 1, a standard deviation of 0.11 is generally optimistic and 0.3 (log scale) is a more likely value. Thus, under the optimistic scenario of an ideal model that predicts all data correctly, the RMSEP can be expected to be rather close to 0.42. In reality the models reported by Wenlock and Carlsson achieved RMSEP values between 0.33 and 0.7, indicating that a large fraction of the error in prediction depends on the assay variability. Interestingly, the RMSEP values for the low quality models were not necessarily much higher. This was suggested to be due to the higher number of data points for the low quality data sets, highlighting that in silico ADME modelers need to find the optimal compromise between data quality and data quantity.

    3.2 Modeling Approach

    Modelers own the responsibility of deciding which combination of algorithm and chemical description best fits the purpose. They will have different preferences but will consider the following key parameters: accuracy, speed, interpretability, time of analysis, and risk of overfitting. Most modelers will agree that models need to be accurate in order to be useful. Additionally, usage of a linear model (as opposed to a nonlinear model) may enable easier model interpretation, while the use of an unsupervised learning algorithm could decrease the risk of building a biased model. Concerning the use of descriptors, less is more: if a property can be predicted by a low number of descriptors, these can become guidelines for optimization. Consequently, meaningful and interpretable descriptors are generally preferable.
    The experience of this working group is that both linear (e.g., principal component analysis, partial least-squares) and nonlinear (e.g., random forest, support vector machine) algorithms can be valuable. Furthermore, it has been observed that for models based on large data sets, as in the case of many in silico ADME models, nonlinear algorithms exploiting a huge number of noninterpretable descriptors often give more accurate predictions. This highlights a fundamental conflict between accuracy and interpretability or risk of model bias. For example, simple physicochemical descriptors such as cLogP and polar surface area (PSA) were useful in multivariate modeling approaches to predict permeability.(19-22) Similarly, most_basic_pKa and PSA were observed to be associated with P-gp efflux categorization.(10) There is power in relating these descriptors to a given biological effect, as they are generally ingrained in the minds of most medicinal chemists and pharmaceutical scientists. Other properties that are instead more dependent on direct structural interactions (e.g., binding to proteins) may require descriptors derived from the 3D structure, pharmacophoric information, and/or atomic properties. Ideally, the target of modeling efforts should be to understand how structural modifications affect a property. However, an accurate model able to generate a very rapid prediction (e.g., hERG or microsomal clearance) can be quite helpful to move chemistry into the right direction, even if the model results per se cannot be translated structurally. Depending on the intended use of the tool, both a black box “virtual assay” with superior accuracy and a simple set of rules to guide optimization can be valuable.(23) The preference will ultimately be determined by the local company culture and the ability of the modeler to build trust in the tool.
    Finally, important aspects of modeling and analyzing data are insights gained that enhance the mechanistic understanding of the property investigated. For example, the well-known Lipinski rule of 5,(24) which pioneered the in silico ADME field, and the solubility forecast index(25) are both routinely used in industry to drive decision making due to their simplicity, despite the lower perceived accuracy compared to QSPR models trained on large high-quality data sets. Simple models are especially appealing because of the resulting clear guidelines for design and, thus, for their potential to educate medicinal chemists. Nevertheless, simple models can only give general trends and may actually be misleading for the individual design problem. Interpretability of more complex models on the compound level has become achievable through, for example, descriptor sensitivity analysis, which enables guidance for specific design questions.

    3.3 Model Evaluation

    Stouch and coauthors discussed the question of why models fail in 2003.(26) Among the causes indicated, in the review of case studies of models developed by vendors, scrutiny is placed on the users who may not be sufficiently aware of caveats of the data used in terms of size and type of data set, and relevance to the target properties available in-house.
    The expectations from the models largely depend on the model developers’ ability to explain, document, and put the model into perspective for the users, which are the colleagues in medicinal chemistry and DMPK. It is not realistic to expect that every user is cross-trained in modeling and statistics and knows the caveats every model will have, so the human factor becomes of utmost importance in not pushing the model beyond the data it was built upon. Our experience is that the time spent in explaining the model performance and in making the user aware of the “domain of applicability” is as important as training and refinement of the models. If the latter activity is something that should be undertaken on a continuous basis, then so should education and guidance to the user.
    Furthermore, it is important to avoid predictions from an in silico model being directly compared to experimental end points under incorrect mechanistic assumptions. For example, one cannot expect that a compound’s enhanced permeability as a result of an interaction with an uptake transporter can be predicted from a passive permeability model. A case of potential misperception regarding “mispredictions” by a permeability model was presented by Larregieu and Benet(27) who discussed how the outliers in the training set of in silico models should be carefully scrutinized.
    In silico models are typically evaluated with statistical measures such as R2 and RMSEP for continuous models or accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity for categorical models, based on test sets or via cross-validation of the training set data. The latter should be interpreted with caution to avoid overfitting, and external sets are to be preferred.
    However, these statistical measures need to be put into the right context to be interpreted correctly.(28-30) Reports indicate that a truly independent test set, sometimes termed external evaluation set, which is not involved in model development, is essential to evaluate the true predictive power of a QSPR model. In fairly recent work Sheridan and coauthors(31, 32) point out that the most widely used cross-validation method is based on random selection, where one randomly extracts data (compounds) from the training set and uses them for internal validation. Additionally, an external validation is often done by selecting a test set from the whole data pool.(33-36) Sheridan points out(31) that the use of the “time-split” method, which is to build a model on data available at a certain time and test the model on data generated later, is the best way to simulate the process of prospective validation. Generally, model training in industry is done with an iterative approach, where the new data are used to test but subsequently also to train the models, in some cases with automated processes.
    In most cases such an iterative approach, where RMSEP values are determined from the latest new data, i.e., from a temporal test set, gives good confidence in the model. Still, if the end user evaluates model performance based on results for only a small subset of compounds from a given chemical scaffold of interest, the error may differ. At this stage the in silico ADME scientist can work with the project to select additional representative compounds to be tested to increase the validity of the model in the specific chemical space but also to increase confidence in the model. This exercise would result in establishing an in silico to in vitro correlation and could complement analogue efforts to establish in vitro to in vivo correlations resulting in a productive cycle covering all the steps from compound design to in vivo testing.
    Finally, the examination of outliers can be very revealing and bring out a powerful aspect of modeling. An outlier may highlight an experimental issue worthy of investigating because in reality experimental data may not always be right. When a good working relationship between the modelers and the scientists in the laboratory is established, this can result in a continuous feedback loop by which aberrant data can be highlighted and result in further testing. This helps to improve the quality of the experimental data set and is an additional quality check for the assay laboratories.
    The word “synergy” applied to computational and experimental work surfaces in these discussions, and it is recommended that it become a theme, if not yet adopted by scientists in the field. This is as important as, or perhaps even more important than, the choice of a testing scheme for which there is no universal answer. Once again, the models need to be used in context, i.e., for a specific purpose and within the remit of the project team, with the involvement of the computational and cheminformatics scientists, as well as the laboratory scientists who generate the data. If properly used, models will help with asking questions on the experiments, and they will be perhaps even more informative when significant discrepancies may be observed, prompting teams to ask questions.

    3.4 Applicability Domain and Confidence Metrics

    Confidence and/or applicability domain metrics are crucial elements for chemists and DMPK scientists using the models to understand the utility of a prediction. A confidence metric is usually associated with classification approaches; it addresses the question “What are the odds?” and is generally defined based on the distance from the class cutoff or the degree of agreement among the submodels used in ensemble modeling approaches.(37) For regression models this can be provided as a standard error in prediction estimated from the RMSEP. Applicability domain metrics inform on the reliability of the prediction based on the distance from the known chemical space. While different flavors of metrics for prediction confidence and applicability domain have been presented in the past, straightforward approaches (e.g., Tanimoto similarity to training set) may in some cases be considered sufficiently informative and can be easily communicated to teams. More informative algorithms to define applicability domain of QSPR models are based on the sample distance from the variable landscape, which in turn can be estimated using geometrical metrics or probability density distribution functions.(38) Depending on the applicability definition, this approach may suffer from shortcomings as the most common modeling techniques transform the variables, whereas the distance from model is calculated via untransformed variables. Hence, it is difficult to ensure that the prediction error is directly related to the distance from the original chemical space. In addition, there is no strict definition of how close to the chemical space an object must be to ensure an accurate prediction. Alternative methods such as the conformal predictor(39) are estimated based on “error models” depending on the conformity with the chemical space occupied by compounds in the training set and the characteristic of the model itself.(39-41) Once again the ability of the modeler to describe the use and significance of these parameters is of paramount importance to success.

    3.5 Modeling of in Vivo End Points

    The modeling of volume of distribution at steady state (Vss) and clearance, in human or animal, being targets of different complexity, deserve additional comments. Most authors engaged in this area have focused much more on human than on animal models,(33, 34, 36, 42, 43) seemingly seeking a higher return on their effort.
    No in silico, in vitro, or in vivo ADME model for predicting human pharmacokinetic properties offers assurance of high accuracy from candidate to candidate. As a corollary, a model that would attempt to predict, from structure only, a full PK profile is too lofty and unattainable at present and for the foreseeable future. Scaling methods based on in vivo data may fare better in most cases, but data analyses and extended modeling efforts(44-50) have shown that, depending on the nature of the compound (basic, neutral, acidic, or zwitterionic) and its properties, different animal scaling approaches will yield different results. A consensus among several approaches, including in silico methods, is probably a safer way to proceed from a risk assessment perspective when predicting properties such as volume of distribution and clearance. This is particularly true for investigators interested in early dose projection during lead optimization (not clinical candidate selection).(51)In vivo testing in higher species requires costly and fairly lengthy cycles that are not generally compatible with rapid design and testing cycles, aside from important considerations related to ethical aspects. For highly permeable compounds Vss is essentially based on passive diffusion and thus physicochemical determinants such as charge and lipophilicity,(36) which is part of the reason why in silico models (both QSPR and mechanistic) have shown accuracy comparable or superior to predictions based on rodent data.(33, 52) However, these models may be less accurate for poorly permeable compounds due to the high importance of active transporters in their disposition. Predicting total clearance in human solely based on molecular structure is a very significant challenge even though several authors have made headway.(34, 42)

    4.1 Model Availability: Infrastructural Requirements

    The quality of the computational infrastructure is a key feature that will inevitably determine the success of modeling in a company. Quite simply, computational models can be used by nonexperts if they are easy to access, quick to compute (few seconds), and well integrated with other informatics components. Medicinal chemists and DMPK scientists will need to be reminded of calculated DMPK properties through internal presentations but even more importantly through constant exposure. For example, some properties can automatically be calculated for each new compound that is added to the corporate database. Similarly calculations could also be embedded in the design tools, such as platforms for structure-based modeling and repositories for design ideas.(53)
    The most fertile ground for models to impact design is the microenvironment of a specific drug discovery project. Representatives involved in the design of new compounds, who are aware of past structure–activity relationship (SAR) and current challenges, are the customers and only suitable judges for new design ideas. The focus of scientists designing the computational infrastructure should be on enabling the use of the models during dedicated design meetings and through project workspaces. The latter are dynamic files including all the relevant project data, generally released and analyzed using data mining software such as Spotfire, MedChem Studio, or Vortex.(54-56) These tools can automatically generate dynamic plots that capture performance of the models for subseries over time and represent a great opportunity to attract chemists’ attention. As an example, Figure 5 shows visualization generated by a user-friendly Spotfire-based tool, ADMET_View, implemented at Eli Lilly. The tool is used by project teams to extract, visualize, and analyze in silico and in vitro data for the chemical series of interest. The only information required from the user to generate such a visualization is the list of compound identifiers for a given chemical series and the name of the potency assay for the project. As shown in Figure 5a, one of the visualizations provided by this tool helps to evaluate applicability of in silico models for a given series wherein the predicted properties are colored by measured data. For example, in the case of the MDCK permeability category, out of 115 compounds predicted to have “high” permeability 97 were measured to have “high” permeability (colored green) which translates to a positive predictive value of ∼85%. Having assessed the prospective performance of the in silico models for a given chemical series, project teams can then proceed to apply such models for the prioritization of virtual (designed) compounds based on predictions, as shown in Figure 5b. Such visualizations also help to assess whether the designed compounds are predicted to offer greater probability of finding compounds with desired properties in a multiparametric approach.
    Ultimately a model will become established only months, or even years, after its release. This is the time required for scientists to familiarize themselves with the new tool. Several members of this group reported that keeping track of all the prospective predictions since the original model release can contribute to success in this area. As an example, Desai et al. reported prospective assessment of successive versions of the P-gp global model to demonstrate a steady performance over several years which resulted in the use of this model as the first screen for P-gp, replacing a high-throughput version of the mechanistic assay.(10)

    4.2 Use of Calculated DMPK Properties in Drug Discovery Projects

    QSPR models can be a great resource for project teams that are in the process of optimizing chemical scaffolds of pharmaceutical interest. In silico DMPK scientists can use their knowledge of the project, of the model, and of the assay to advise team members on how and when the models should be used. Medicinal chemistry is a classical multiparameter optimization process. Yet, this does not imply that at each step of a campaign all the key parameters will concomitantly be optimized. During early stages, the focus is often on understanding potency SAR, but it is not uncommon for concurrent optimization of one or two ADME related issues, particularly if the initial aim of the project is to deliver a tool compound for efficacy or safety proof of concept. In some cases, however, additional tools can help the team temporarily overcome hurdles to establishing proof of concept. Poor solubility could be addressed by enabling formulations, poor permeability could be overcome by means of intraperitoneal dosing, while poor metabolic stability could be improved by predosing pan-CYP inhibitors such as 1-aminobenzotriazole. These ADME related issues may then be revisited at another stage of the program. It is important for modelers to be connected with the project or, at the very least, to understand what DMPK property is actually being optimized at a given moment. Some medicinal chemists may perceive the use of a calculated property as the most cost-effective way to maximize chances of success, while others may perceive it as a limitation to their ability to explore the chemical space of interest. A chemist may in fact argue that the use of in silico tools could result in missed opportunities. While this possibility cannot be excluded, this is a common theme for all models including in vitro and in vivo; for example, using in vitro MDCK permeability to predict intestinal absorption could result in false negatives if uptake transporters are involved. Nevertheless models are needed to support hypothesis driven research, as synthetizing and testing all the possible decorations of a given scaffold not only is practically impossible but would also pose a competitive disadvantage due to time required for completing the lead optimization process. This is of key importance in a landscape where different companies compete for the same target. It is important for in silico ADME scientists to promote models through a nondogmatic approach, since project team members will have different perspectives and past experiences that need to be understood and acknowledged. Claiming that many bad actors could have been avoided after the fact may not be a fruitful way to confront the team. On the contrary the same data points could be an opportunity to build confidence in the tool and to promote its future use. Overall, predictions should be considered as a way to enable informed design decisions, where the term “informed” refers to an objective probabilistic assessment. The use of hard filters and cut-offs in many cases may be in direct contrast with the intention to test hypotheses emerging from in vitro experiments such as metabolite identification and therefore should be disregarded. As the confidence in the in silico tools increases, chemists could be encouraged to synthesize at least a fraction of the new compounds that are predicted to have good DMPK properties, even if SAR and/or structure-based design suggests they may be more risky from a potency standpoint. In fact potency should not be the sole guiding parameter, and we are seeing in more recent times the adoption of multidimensional optimization with dose as the end “deliverable”.(51, 57-59)
    During early stages the priority is generally finding novel promising lead compounds rather than performing a detailed characterization of the space surrounding the front-runner molecules. In this context, models can guide chemistry toward the most promising portion of the accessible chemical space, since during early stages chemists are generally not too worried about losing good compounds due to false negative predictions. When the chemical matter is particularly synthetically tractable, teams might opt for a library enumeration approach, which poses a remarkable opportunity for QSPR DMPK models to influence the selection of the most promising candidates among the thousands that could be synthesized. As teams progress toward later stages, in silico DMPK models become more aware of the scaffold being optimized and therefore more predictive. However, this will not automatically result in increased model usage as the quality requirements for predictions may become much more stringent. Chemists could in fact be rightfully more concerned about losing a good near neighbor than wasting resources in synthesizing a compound with poor properties. Once again in this space there is not a perfect recipe for the use of the ADME models; team members will have to consider a number of problems including selectivity and safety profile, intellectual property coverage, intended modality of administration, and suboptimal DMPK properties.
    Genentech scientists(9) recently presented real life examples where in silico ADME QSPR models were used to help the team progress toward clinical candidate quality molecules. In each example computational approaches were customized based on the project phase and need and would not have been successful otherwise. It was shown how a JAK-1 project team was initially concerned about establishing potency SAR; therefore models were not extensively used. During later stages the in silico model for metabolic stability was incorporated into the design cycle, resulting in more rapid progression of the chemical series of interest.
    Models can encourage synthesis of compounds enriched with favorable ADME properties even if they cannot always explicitly point out a defined molecular feature (group, substituent, dipole moment, etc.) responsible for the improvement. Using such a “black-box” model on many compounds can enable the identification of “clusters” of structures with a higher probability of improving a given ADME property, which will aid prioritization of synthesis. The analysis of these results can provide useful structural hints to the trained eye of a medicinal chemist. The human factor is important here, as it is understandable that once time has been invested in designing a new compound, the person who has conceived the idea might want to pursue that path regardless of the in silico predictions. It is human nature to trust what is “understandable”, and QSPR predictions may not immediately satisfy these requirements.

    4.3 In Silico ADME: Positioning Relative Organizational Structure

    A further important aspect is where in silico ADME scientists most effectively reside within an organization. This will influence to a large extent the impact of in silico tools on the project. The initial temptation is to answer that these scientists fit within several parts of the organization, which may be true in principle, but that leaves much to be desired. Advice on the positioning of these groups should be a bit more detailed, if only to offer the reader a chance to debate and perhaps disagree. A prescriptive approach could be taken that cheminformatic/computational chemistry groups are ideally located close to, or within, medicinal chemistry departments while the ADME in silico scientists may be located in the DMPK department. We observe that regardless of the location of the in silico ADME expert, it is of key importance that advocates are present in each one of the departments where the customers for the models are located (chemistry, DMPK, and possibly drug safety evaluation).

    5 Knowledge Extraction and Idea Generation Tools

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Although predictive ADME models significantly guide the design of new molecules, they have the limitation that the structures of the compounds to be predicted have to be preconceived. This is typically done by members of the project team, usually a medicinal or computational chemist. These decisions are usually driven by prior experience, rules of thumb (i.e., decreasing lipophilicity leads to decreased clearance), or other SAR needs for the chemical series.
    This leads to the question of whether there is an opportunity to use ADME data to assist in the creation of new ideas for the optimization of ADME properties. One approach that has been used recently is the MMP analysis.(60-65) An MMP is a pair of compounds, typically within a series, where an observed end point difference is attributed to a single structural change. This change can be represented as a chemical transform. If all the pairs that undergo the same chemical transform are summarized, it may be possible to find transforms that lead to an overall improvement in a property. Hence, by utilizing information embedded in existing compounds, the transforms can generate new ideas for improvement of a particular property. MMP analysis is a complementary approach to in silico QSPR modeling in that it is entirely data driven and can generate new hypothetical structures whose properties can be predicted using models to further bolster or refute the hypothesis. Figure 6 depicts how MMP idea generation can complement predictive models in compound design.
    Figure 7a shows an example MMP. For a given MMP, all of the changes in biological or physical properties are a result of the structural change. Although there is only one structural change in an MMP, that change can be represented by multiple structural transforms as shown in Figure 7b.
    If we group all of the MMPs with a specific transform, we can summarize and analyze the changes in properties to determine if the changes are consistent across different series. Figure 8 shows Pfizer HLM CLint data for the 5-pyrimidyl → 2-pyrimidyl transform. For this transform, the mean change in log(HLM CLint) is −0.29 (50% reduction). 4% of the pairs show an increase in HLM, 45% show a decrease, and 51% show no change (<2-fold). These summaries and analyses can be used to rank-order different transforms to facilitate identification of those that are most likely to help improve a property of interest.
    Although the ability to generate ideas for the improvement of a property is valuable, drug design is often an exercise in the optimization of multiple properties. MMPs can also be useful in this situation by considering more than one end point at a time. For instance, transforms that simultaneously improve clearance, increase permeability, and reduce P-gp liability can be searched for and ranked.
    Another use for MMP databases is to identify general trends in common fragments that might be unexpected. An example would be the investigation of the changes in properties for all six-membered aromatic rings. Figure 9 shows a heat map representing the change in HLM clearance, passive permeability, and P-gp efflux for the phenyl → all other six-membered aromatic rings in Pfizer’s database. The data suggest that the best replacement of phenyl for HLM is a 4-pyridazyl, but that replacement may come at the cost of an increase in P-gp efflux.
    When using MMP databases and summary information for structural changes, there are a few considerations that one should keep in mind. First, the analysis of transforms makes the assumption that the end point changes are fragment additive, meaning that the change in an end point value will be the same for a given structural change regardless of the rest of the molecule. There are end points where this is largely true (i.e., log D), but this will not always be the case. Another issue is the potential importance of contextualization of a given structural change. This is the local environment of the changing group. For example, the observed change for a transform of a cyano group attached to a phenyl ring may be quite different compared to one attached to an alkyl group. Thus, while contextualization may matter, it is difficult to know a priori in any given situation. Lastly, the analysis of a large set of data via MMP may hide bias due to inclusion of compounds from a single series and may lead to inference that a specific effect to one (or a small number of) series is in fact general. The best advice is to study the underlying MMPs carefully to determine if the overall change is relevant in a given case. Finally, when using MMP transforms, note the statistical significance of the change. Kramer et al. discussed the types of statistical parameters that can be calculated for MMP transforms.(16) These measures of significance can be useful for ranking transforms and their likelihood of providing the desired outcome. However, in most MMP databases, there will be a high proportion of transforms that lack the underlying number of MMP examples to be significant. This does not mean that the observed changes of those transforms are not valid, rather that they do not have adequate statistical power. MMP tools and analysis can be very powerful additions to statistical modeling for compound design. From the computational ADME perspective, MMPs enable the move from a passive (model-based) evaluation of ideas to the actual creation of ideas to address a single liability or a combination thereof. These databases can also enable data mining to identify unexpected liabilities in virtual compounds as well as general trends. Future directions in the use of MMPs include transforms based on virtual data(64) and the evolution of matched molecular series (MMS) approaches.(65)
    Alternative methods that borrow elements from both QSPR and MMP technologies, but yet are different, can also be found in the recent literature. Figure 10 illustrates an implementation where MMP is embedded in R-group table with QSPR model.(66) In this approach, side chains and cores are sequentially fragmented in a way amenable to synthesis and organized into an SAR table. This presentation is intuitive and perhaps the most chemistry-friendly format and therefore could easily fit into the compound-design workflow. All blank cells correspond to virtual compounds generated by the analysis. Predictions for the properties of interest can be made using structural transforms based on MMP database analysis or any QSPR methods suitable for the selected properties. Users can evaluate different end points in parallel to enable a holistic characterization of the profile of the virtual compounds, which will likely be synthetically accessible and therefore aligned with the chemistry plan.
    Another approach to stimulate idea generation is to provide a visual representation of the QSPR prediction.(67, 68)Figure 11 shows a hERG model example where fingerprint-based descriptors are mapped back on the molecule with the degree of contributions. The visualization identifies the moieties that chemists need to focus on for the property optimization.

    6 Future Directions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In terms of new technologies, future opportunities lie with more integration of DMPK science, as well as in de novo design (Figure 12). In particular, beyond de novo design, the two most “voted” aspects in our cross-companies survey were early dose predictions and understanding the interplay between in silico, in vitro, and in vivo ADME data. We are of the opinion that robust models can and will assist in considering potential artifacts or lurking and previously unrecognized variables in the experiment. However, we should strive to utilize experimental protocols that (i) are generating consistent data over time, (ii) provide actual and not just boundary values, and (iii) overall generate data with modeling in mind, certainly a cost-related and management question but of the utmost importance and a fruit-bearing investment. Concerning de novo design, although a number of software tools are headed in the direction of suggesting structural modifications,(69, 70) there is still still room for improvement to account for a few practical considerations, such as reagent cost and availability, as well as the ability to provide synthetically tractable ideas in line with the current needs of the project on a weekly basis with minimal human intervention, which are barriers to their usage. The aim of this effort should not be replacing the human component from compound selection but rather suggesting minimal structural modifications that may be of interest.
    Drug discovery is a multiparameter optimization (MPO) process, which poses the problem of objectivity when evaluating progress. During lead optimization (or even earlier) it could be beneficial to evolve the optimization process from an arbitrary MPO score (or traffic-light system) to a “dose optimization system”, effectively an MPO approach based on mechanistic premises.(51, 57-59) Each experienced drug discovery scientist is aware of the challenges associated with dose projection, predicting human PK parameters and assessing drivers for the PK/PD relationship.(71) Nevertheless, it would be hard to argue that once in vitro and PK data are available, an arbitrary MPO score constitutes a better way to rank-order compounds in the same chemical series than mechanistic dose projections.(51) In this arena in silico and in vitro scientists and pharmacokineticists should work together to reach an appropriate balance between simple compartmental modeling and more advanced PBPK predictions. Such an effort should focus on creating a tool for rank-ordering similar compounds, not necessarily aiming at providing accurate dose projections. Lastly, as for other technologies discussed, dose projection approaches will only be successful if the effort also emphasizes translatability to the in vivo situation and accessibility and ease of understanding to medicinal chemists.

    7 Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    This work aims to bring together the wide experience of a group of scientists from several major pharmaceutical companies working in the in silico ADME-PK area. While in silico ADME-PK has been a reality in industry since the introduction of Lipinski’s rule of 5, this is often perceived as an extension of other disciplines such as DMPK or computational chemistry. As a consequence, scientists operating in this area are generally trained in computer science, chemistry, or DMPK science and did not receive formal training on in silico ADME-PK. On one hand, this has promoted an open environment creating an opportunity for people with diverse expertise (i.e., in vitro, in silico, statistics, analytical, PK, structural biology, synthetic chemistry, machine learning, etc.) to collaborate and innovate together. On the other hand, different from the more established PBPK and structure-based design fields, in silico ADME-PK is only now beginning to transform the experience and knowledge assembled through the years into best practices, moving the focus from creating technologies to using technologies, changing the objective from demonstrating potential to demonstrating impact. Implementing in silico tools for ADME-PK in drug discovery to support design leads to a tangible increase in knowledge and reduction in data generation. Collaboration between different departments and ease of access to data and models are key to success in this area. Several companies with well-integrated platforms of models incorporated into the screening cascades to improve the optimization flow for the properties of interest have greatly benefited from this approach.
    Drug discovery is an evolving landscape; regulatory agencies are currently promoting and rewarding novel breakthourgh therapies, leading to less explored biology and unfamilar chemical space. In this context companies capable of rapidly progressing initial HTS screening hits to druglike compounds amenable for testing efficacy and safety of novel targets could be the ones that thrive. This is the space where QSPR models, MMP, and other forms of chemical data mining are most effective. It will be important for in silico ADME scientists to consolidate best practices in this area and expand the pool of adopters.
