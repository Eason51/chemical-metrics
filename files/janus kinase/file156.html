    Abstract

    Recruitment of leukocytes is essential to fight infections or to heal injuries; however, excessive and/or prolonged responses favor the development of major inflammatory pathologies, such as cardiovascular or neurodegenerative diseases. Thus, it is of great interest to seek novel compounds that can regulate leukocyte recruitment depending on the degree of inflammation. We have isolated and characterized, by different chromatographic techniques, mass spectrometry, and Edman sequencing, a new hexapeptide (SSLSKL) from the Mexican frog Pachymedusa dacnicolor, which we named pachymodulin. In vitro, pachymodulin promotes the migration of leukocytes through the binding and activation of the human and mouse N-formyl peptide receptor 2 (huFPR2). In vivo, it exhibits opposite biological activities: under homeostatic conditions, pachymodulin induces the recruitment of leukocytes, whereas under inflammatory conditions, it inhibits this process. Therefore, pachymodulin represents an interesting template in the quest to design new immunomodulatory drugs in the therapy of immune-related diseases.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Although essential in early immune responses to fight infections and injuries, excessive and/or prolonged recruitment of leukocytes contribute to the development of numerous diseases and pathological conditions. A given inflammatory response represents the interplay between pro- and anti-inflammatory immunomodulators.(1) A strategy to prevent acute or chronic inflammation, without weakening the organism’s self-defense, would thus be to regulate the pool of immune cells, depending on the inflammatory state.
    In the search for new therapeutic agents, natural drug discovery exhibits a vast potential. In the past decades, more than 300 bioactive peptides with varied and remarkable pharmacological properties have been purified from the skin of frogs, representing a rich source of therapeutic compounds. Particularly, as they are part of the self-defense against predation or invading microorganisms and thereby are a key component of the amphibians’ innate immune system, most of these molecules exhibit antimicrobial properties.(2) In addition to killing a broad spectrum of invading microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, and protozoa), some of these multifunctional peptides selectively regulate different biological processes. Recently, a number of these antimicrobial host defense peptides have been identified to modulate inflammatory processes by promoting the migration of immune cells through the activation of chemotactic receptors and/or by stimulating the production of pro- or anti-inflammatory cytokines.(3-7)
    Among all the chemokine and chemoattractant receptors and their ligands that participate in leukocyte migration into inflamed, injured, or infected tissues, only the human N-formyl peptide receptor 2 (huFPR2) receptor, belonging to the formyl-peptide receptor (FPR)-related family of G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), has been shown to mediate the chemotactic effects of some frog antimicrobial peptides.(3) There are three functional types of FPRs in humans: huFPR1, huFPR2, and huFPR3. Whereas huFPR1 binds the prototypical N-formyl peptide fMet-Leu-Phe (fMLP) with high affinity, huFPR2 and its murine counterpart muFPR2 bind it with low affinity.(8) Apart from N-formyl peptides, FPR2 can bind numerous unrelated natural and synthetic ligands, such as host- and pathogen-derived peptides, lipids, and novel synthetic peptidic and nonpeptidic molecules that display pro- or anti-inflammatory signals, thereby suggesting a significant role for these receptors in immunoregulation. The interaction of FPR2 with derivates of lipoxin such as A4 (LXA4) or the aspirin-triggered lipoxins A4 leads to an anti-inflammatory response whereas that with the amyloid peptide Aβ1–42, the human antimicrobial peptide LL-37, the serum amyloid A, or the frog peptide temporin A leads to pro-inflammatory responses.(3, 9-13)
    Herein we report the isolation and characterization of a new small immunomodulatory peptide from the Mexican tree frog Pachymedusa dacnicolor, which promotes in vitro the migration of leukocytes and the calcium release by neutrophils and monocytes through the human huFPR2 and its murine counterpart muFPR2. Interestingly, this novel hexapeptide, which we named pachymodulin, exhibits a dual role in vivo: under homeostatic conditions, it induces the recruitment of leukocytes to the peritoneal cavity, whereas, under inflammatory conditions, it inhibits it. Therefore, pachymodulin could be of great interest in therapeutics and could serve as a template in the quest of designing new immunomodulatory drugs.

    Isolation and Characterization of Pachymodulin, a Novel Frog Peptide Extracted from Pachymedusa dacnicolor Skin Exudate

    Pachymodulin from P. dacnicolor skin exudates was purified to homogeneity by a two-step protocol. A 1.1 mL amount of secretion recovered by gently squeezing the parotoid glands of the skin of a single living frog was first fractionated on a Sephadex G-50 column. Three fractions were obtained, and each of them was tested for chemotactic activity on neutrophils (Figure 1a). Of the three fractions obtained, only fraction III exhibited chemotactic activity (Figure 1a, insert). This fraction was further fractionated with a reversed-phase high-pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC) semipreparative column (Figure 1b). Serial dilutions (1/10) of the peak exhibiting a retention time of 24.3 min (arrow) were tested on a neutrophil migration assay (bars1–7: ascending concentrations; bar 7: no dilution, bar 1:1/1 000 000). Neutrophils migrated in response to these diluted peaks with a typical bell-shaped dose–response curve (Figure 1b, insert), indicating that this peak contained a chemotactic peptide.
    As determined by tandem mass-spectrometry and Edman sequencing, the sequence of the peptide of this peak gave SSLSKL, with an experimental monoisotopic mass of the protonated peptide of 634.36. To confirm the sequence and to demonstrate that the biological activities of this purified natural peptide, that we named pachymodulin, reflected its intrinsic properties, it was synthesized by the solid-phase method. After HPLC purification with a semipreparative column, synthetic pachymodulin was indistinguishable from the natural product, eluting at the same retention time (24.3 min) as the natural product and giving the same mass to charge ratio (634.34) by matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization-time-of-flight (MALDI-TOF) mass spectrometry analysis (data not shown). Pachymodulin is a six-mer peptide that has an overall charge of +1, with only one positively charged residue (Lys); it is mainly hydrophilic due to the presence of serine and lysine. To characterize the conformational structure of this peptide, circular dichroism (CD) was performed. As would be expected for such a small peptide, CD spectra revealed that pachymodulin exhibited a random coil structure in the three media tested, i.e, phosphate buffer, in the presence of a mixture of phosphatidylcholine (PC) and phosphatidylglycerol (PG) or of PC alone liposomes (Figure 1c). Pachymodulin had no antibacterial potential, as it was found to be inactive against the tested Gram-negative (E. coli ATCC 11775) and -positive (S. aureus ATCC 25293) bacterial strains at concentrations of up to 100 μM (see Supporting Information Table S1). No hemolytic activity was detected within the same range of concentrations (see Supporting Information Figure S1a), consistent with its lack of membrane perturbation of eukaryotic membrane mimicking liposomes (see Supporting Information Figure S1b).

    Pachymodulin Promotes Directional Migration of Human and Mouse Leukocytes in Vitro through a GPCR Coupled to the ERK1/2 MAPK Pathway

    Similar to the natural peptide, synthetic pachymodulin induced the migration of human neutrophils, monocytes, and T cells (Figure 2a) as well as that of mouse neutrophils and monocytes (Figure 2b), with a typical bell-shaped dose–response curve. For all human and mouse cells tested, maximum migration was detected at 10 μM. Moreover, addition of the same concentration of pachymodulin in the upper and the lower wells of the Boyden chamber abolished the migration of neutrophils or monocytes (Figure 2a), suggesting that pachymodulin induced a directional migration (chemotaxis) rather than a random movement (chemokinesis).
    Most chemokines and chemoattractants induce migration through a Giα protein coupled receptor (GPCR).(14) To find out if pachymodulin-induced chemotaxis was also mediated by a GPCR, human neutrophils were pretreated with pertussis toxin (PTX),(15) a GPCR specific inhibitor, prior to the chemotactic assay. As observed in Figure 2c, pretreatment with PTX totally inhibited the motility of neutrophils induced by pachymodulin when compared with neutrophils stimulated with pachymodulin only. Pretreatment of neutrophils with 2-(2-amino-3-methoxyphenyl)-4H-1-benzopyran-4-one (PD98059),(16) a specific inhibitor of mitogen-activated kinase (MEK1/2) also inhibited up to 75% of the migration induced by pachymodulin, whereas pretreatment with 4-(4-fluorophenyl)-2-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-5-(4-pyridyl)-1H-imidazole (SB202190),(17) a specific inhibitor of p38 mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK), had no effect on migration (Figure 2c), consistent with the fact that most chemokine-sensing GPCR signaling leads to extracellular regulated kinase (ERK) activation.(18) fMLP (100 nM), known to activate neutrophil chemotaxis through huFPR1, induced directional motility through a pathway that recruited both ERK and p38 MAP kinases following FPR activation, as PTX, MEK1/2, and p38 inhibitors inhibited fMLP-induced motility by 100%, 75%, and 50%, respectively. These results indicate that both pachymodulin and fMLP induce cell migration through a GPCR via a MAPK pathway. To further confirm the activation of a MAPK pathway in neutrophils in response to pachymodulin stimulation, we evaluated the phosphorylation of ERK1/2. Incubation of neutrophils with pachymodulin for 5, 10, or 15 min induced the phosphorylation of ERK1/2 in a time-dependent manner (Figure 2d). A pretreatment with PTX or MEK1/2 inhibitor inhibited pachymodulin-induced ERK1/2 phosphorylation in neutrophils. Similarly, incubation of neutrophils with fMLP promoted ERK1/2 phosphorylation, which was decreased after preincubation with MEK1/2 inhibitor (Figure 2d). Therefore, downstream of a GPCR, pachymodulin induced chemotaxis through the activation of an ERK1/2 but not that of a p38 MAPK pathway.

    FPR2 is a Functional Receptor for Pachymodulin

    As mentioned previously, a number of chemoattractants, that are not CC or CXC chemokines, such as fMLP, LL-37 (the human antimicrobial cathelicidin), serum amyloid A (SAA) (an acute phase reactant of inflammation), Aβ1–42, T21 (an ectodomain peptide of HIV-1 gp41), or the frog antimicrobial peptide temporin A, bind to GPCRs, specifically to members of the FPR receptor family present on monocytes, neutrophils, and T cells in human as well as in mouse.(3, 9-11, 19) The huFPR1 receptor is a high affinity receptor for fMLP (KD = 3 nM), and huFPR2 and its murine orthologue, muFPR2, are low affinity receptors for fMLP (KD > 300 nM).(8) fMLP has been reported to induce calcium mobilization in cells transfected with FPR1 and FPR2, and chemotaxis in cells transfected with hu-FPR1 or muFPR2, but not in cells transfected with huFPR2, even at high concentrations.(8) In contrast, LL-37, SAA, Aβ1–42, and temporin A have been shown to induce chemotaxis, through huFPR2.(3, 9-13) We hypothesized that pachymodulin could mediate chemotaxis using one of these receptors.
    To test this possibility, neutrophils were pretreated with fMLP (0.1 or 10 μM) before a pachymodulin (10 μM)-induced chemotaxis assay. Preincubating the cells with 10 μM fMLP inhibited up to 50% the pachymodulin-induced migration while preincubating them with 0.1 μM fMLP did not affect the pachymodulin-induced motility (Figure 3a). However, the converse experiment, i.e., preincubation with 10 μM pachymodulin (Figure 3b), did not abolish the fMLP-induced migration, even with higher concentrations of pachymodulin (up to 1 mM) (Figure 3b). As a positive control, neutrophils were treated with fMLP (10 μM) before fMLP-induced migration, and as expected, pretreatment with fMLP abolished the fMLP-induced migration of neutrophils (Figure 3b).
    In addition to fMLP, FPR1, and FPR2, each have a series of common or unique agonists. However, overall, FPR2 is considered to be less discriminating, as it binds chemically unrelated molecules such as peptides (synthetic or pathogen- or host-derived) and lipids as well as a variety of small synthetic compounds.(8, 20) The hypothesis that pachymodulin could interact with FPR2 was tested by isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC), a technique that assesses the thermodynamic parameters of interactions. A recent study reported the use of whole cells, to measure the interactions of peptides at the cell surface.(21) Accordingly, we examined the heat produced in response to the addition of 150 nmol of pachymodulin (corresponding of 100 μM) to huFPR2 transfected-human embryonic kidney (HEK) cells. When adding pachymodulin to HEK-huFPR2, a heat release of −224 μJ could be detected (Figure 3c). This heat release was significantly higher (around 5-fold) than that observed after adding pachymodulin to nontransfected HEK cells (Figure 3c). To confirm that the interaction between pachymodulin and FPR2 detected by ITC activates cell migration, we performed chemotaxis assays with HEK-huFPR2 or HEK-muFPR2 cells. Pachymodulin induced the migration of both human and mouse FPR2 transfected HEK with a maximum chemotactic activity at 10 μM. No migration was observed with nontransfected HEK cells, suggesting that the binding of pachymodulin to FPR2 induces cell migration (Figure 3d). These data indicate that pachymodulin interacted with FPR2 and that this interaction mediated directional cell migration.
    As calcium release is associated with chemokine receptors stimulation,(22) we tested whether pachymodulin induced calcium fluxes on HEK-huFPR2 or HEK-muFPR2 cells. As shown in Figure 3e, pachymodulin induced a dose-dependent calcium flux in these cells, with a relatively low EC50 of 7.5 and 9 μM, respectively. On the contrary, no calcium release was observed with nontransfected HEK cells. Moreover, when HEK-huFPR2 cells were sequentially stimulated by fMLP (10 μM) or pachymodulin (10 μM) and then by pachymodulin (10 μM) or fMLP (10 μM), respectively, a calcium response of the cells was only observed after the first challenge, while after the second challenge, the calcium response was substantially reduced, suggesting a desensitization of the receptor and thus confirming that both pachymodulin and fMLP used FPR2 to induce calcium release (Figure 3f). When HEK-huFPR2 cells were sequentially stimulated by pachymodulin (10 μM) or by fMLP (10 μM) and then by ATP (30 μM), which induces calcium release through calcium-channel voltage-dependent 1.2 (Cav1.2) channels,(23) a calcium release to the first and the second challenge could be measured (Figure 3f), indicating that each molecule stimulated a different receptor and that the calcium response observed after pachymodulin or fMLP stimulation was likely to be specific to FPR2 activation. Collectively, these results indicate that pachymodulin bound the murine and human fMLP receptor FPR2, inducing calcium release and subsequent chemotaxis.

    Pachymodulin Modulates Leukocytes Recruitment in Vivo Depending on the Inflammatory State

    As pachymodulin induced the directional migration of mouse leukocytes in vitro, we investigated whether it could potentially attract mouse peritoneal leukocytes in vivo. To overcome the rapid degradation of pachymodulin in the presence of human or mouse serum (C. Lacombe, unpublished data), 120 μg of synthetic and sterile pachymodulin (40 μg, three times a day (tid)) in 1 mL of 150 mM NaCl was administered intraperitoneally, and cell recruitment to the peritoneal cavity was assessed shortly after the last injection. As a positive control, 1 mL of 3% thioglycollate, known to induce sterile inflammation by massively recruiting leukocytes to the periteonal cavity,(24) and sterile NaCl, as negative control, were injected into the peritoneal cavity. Mice were sacrificed 24 h after the first injection of pachymodulin, and the number of monocytes (CD11bhiLy6G–F4/80pos), macrophages (CD11bhiLy6G–F4/80hi), and neutrophils (PMN) (CD11bhiLy6G+) was evaluated in the peritoneal cavity and in the blood by flow cytometry (Figure 4a). Pachymodulin significantly increased the number of neutrophils and monocytes in the blood as well as in the peritoneal cavity as compared to the NaCl-treated control group, although to a lesser extent (50%) than did thioglycollate (Figure 4b). In contrast, as we assessed the effect of pachymodulin in a major inflammatory context, we found that mice that had been administered thioglycollate 4 h before treatment with pachymodulin (40 μg tid) showed a significant decrease in the number of neutrophils, monocytes, and macrophages recruited to the peritoneal cavity, as well as in the number of neutrophils and monocytes in the blood (Figure 4b).

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Autoimmune/chronic inflammatory and infectious diseases are a major burden for the society and represent national and World Health Organization strategic priorities. Finding novel immunomodulatory therapeutic drugs that regulate the pool of phagocytic cells at a given place, depending on the inflammatory state, is critical. Currently, most treatments aimed to reduce inflammation and fight infections are based on classical chemical drugs, frequently leading to numerous side effects. Peptidic compounds were seen as a promising alternative to these drugs, yet it is only recently that, based on the reduction of the costs of synthesis and improvement of peptide half-life, academic research and pharmacological companies have focused their interest in the development of peptidic compounds.
    In the search for peptidic therapeutic agents, frog skin is a rich source of host defense peptides that inhibit the growth of bacteria, fungi, and even protozoa.(25, 26) Some of these peptides, first identified on the basis of their antimicrobial potential, also exhibit immunomodulatory properties, acting as immunostimulators or immunosuppressors.(3, 5, 7, 26-28) Previous studies on the Mexican frog Pachymedusa dacnicolor led to the isolation and purification of several host defense peptides assigned to the dermaseptin family.(4, 28) While screening for immunomodulatory peptides, one of them, DRS-DA4, was found to be a potent chemoattractant for neutrophils, monocytes, and T lymphocytes.(4) In this study, we isolated and characterized a new immunomodulatory hexapeptide from Pachymedusa dacnicolor, pachymodulin (SSLSKL), that exhibits both anti- and pro-inflammatory activities on neutrophils, monocytes, and T cells, depending on the inflammatory context. In vivo, under homeostatic conditions, it promotes the recruitment of leukocytes, whereas, under inflammatory conditions, it inhibits it. Similar to peptides such as frenatin 2, isolated from the painted frogs belonging to the anuran family Alytidae, that stimulate the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, or the tigerinins, a family of cyclic peptides isolated from various frogs, that exhibit anti-inflammatory properties,(6, 27) pachymodulin has no antimicrobial activity. Regarding its mechanism of action, we evidenced that, similar to other host defense peptides, pachymodulin’s chemotactic activity depends on the recruitment of the PTX-sensitive G-protein-coupled receptor FPR2, which in turn results in calcium mobilization, ERK1/2 MAPK activation, and cell migration. Consistent with this, pachymodulin exerts its biological effects at concentrations that are similar to those of other FPR2- dependent immunomodulators (1–50 μM).(3-5, 9, 10, 29)
    A recent publication described a novel class of antimicrobial peptides, named medusins, that are synthesized by phyllomedusine frogs and which are effective against Gram-positive bacteria only.(30) Among other characteristics, each peptide of this family possesses in common a fully conserved C-terminal tetrapeptide amide sequence, SKLamide. Moreover, the C-terminal sequence of the Pachymedusa medusin (medusin-PD) is the exact sequence of pachymodulin isolated from Pachymedusa dacnicolor, yet amidated, i.e., SSLSKLa. Whether pachymodulin is a natural cleavage product of medusin-PD remains to be evaluated as does testing the whole medusin-PD peptide for the biological activities we detected for pachymodulin. Interestingly, a 21-amino acid peptide agonist for FPR2, TIPMFVPESTSKLQKFTSWFM (CGEN-855A),(29) that exhibits anti-inflammatory activity in the acute inflammatory model of the air pouch and provides cardioprotective effects in mice and rats, but has no effects under steady state, was recently discovered using a computational platform designed to predict novel GPCR peptide ligands.(29) Noteworthy, a motif sequence (STSKL) of this agonist for FPR2 is very close to the sequence of pachymodulin.
    Chemokines/chemoattractants and their receptors are key regulators of inflammation, as they control leukocyte migration. Among them, FPR2, also known as FPRL1 or CCR12, belongs to the FPR-related receptor family of GPCRs that are largely responsible for detecting invading bacteria and for fighting infection by activating degranulation or superoxide generation as well as by chemoattracting phagocytic cells. FPR2 is expressed on neutrophils, monocytes, and T lymphocytes and binds N-formyl peptides with low affinity (KD > 300 nM). In addition to N-formyl peptides, this receptor is activated by numerous structurally unrelated ligands from different origins such as host- and pathogen-derived peptides/proteins, lipids or synthetic peptidic and nonpeptidic molecules. The participation of FPR2 in a large number of physiological and pathological scenarios underscores the potential of FPR2-mediated signals for therapeutic uses, to positively or negatively regulate immune response and inflammation. On the basis of the nature and availability of the ligand, FPR2 mediates pro- or anti-inflammatory responses. Derivates of lipoxin such as A4 (LXA4) or the aspirin-triggered lipoxins A4, the glucocorticoid-regulated protein annexin 1 and its N-terminal-derived peptide AMVSEFLKQAWFIENEEQEYVQTVK or (Ac2-26)(19) trigger anti-inflammatory properties via FPR2, while the amyloid peptide Aβ1–42, the human antimicrobial peptide LL-37, SAA, or several frog-derived temporins trigger pro-inflammatory responses by interacting with FPR2 as well.(3, 9-13, 19) To the best of our knowledge, data we report here show that pachymodulin is the first natural FPR2 ligand that can by itself regulate the composition of the immune/inflammatory infiltrate recruited and, hence, the amplitude and possibly the nature of this infiltrate, depending on the timing where it interacts with FPR2, relative to an inflammatory insult such as intraperitoneal injection of thioglycollate. It is possible to think that the interaction of pachymodulin with FPR2 leads to heterologous desensitization of chemokines receptors, as described for the synthetic peptide WKYMVm.(31) Understanding the mechanisms inducing pro- or anti-inflammatory responses of human and mouse cells would be of importance for further pharmacological exploitation of this peptide for the development of novel therapeutics aimed at controlling the pool of phagocytic cells at a given site.
    Overall, our data point out that pachymodulin is a small peptide with Janus-like properties, as it has the capacity to positively and negatively regulate inflammation, thus offering unique potential therapeutic applications. This hexapeptide may serve as a template to further consider issues such as in vivo stability, potential toxicity, and routes of administration in the quest to design new immunomodulatory drugs.

    Experimental Section

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    Frogs

    Male and female specimens of Pachymedusa dacnicolor were captured in the state of Morelos (Mexico) and housed in a nonsterile environment, in a large plastic container covered by a fence. Phyllodendron, Potos, and Dracena were used as perches, and a water bowl was provided for nocturnal baths. Once a week, frogs were fed with crickets.

    Purification of the Peptide

    Fresh skin exudate was obtained by gently squeezing the laterodorsal portion of the frog’s skin, resuspended in deionized water, and centrifuged 15 min at 400g. The supernatant was first fractionated by size exclusion chromatography on a Sephadex G-50 fine column (60 × 0.75 cm) eluted with 10% acetic acid. Absorbance was monitored at 280 nm. Three main fractions were obtained and tested for their chemotactic activity, as described later in this section. Fraction III was further fractionated by reversed-phase HPLC (Amersham-Pharmacia Biotech, Piscataway, NJ) on a semipreparative column (Nucleosil 5 μm C18, 250 × 10 mm (Supelco, Sigma-Aldrich, Toluca, Mexico), using a solvent system composed of water containing 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) as solvent A and acetonitrile containing 0.07% TFA (Sigma-Aldrich, Toluca, Mexico) as solvent B. The column was eluted at 5 mL min–1 with a 0–60% linear gradient of solvent B for 65 min.

    Peptide Sequencing

    The lyophilized purified fraction was characterized by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry (MS) using a Voyager DE RP apparatus (Life Technologies, Saint-Aubin, France). Mass spectrometry analyses were performed with and without reducing pretreatment using solubilization in 50 mM carbonate buffer pH 8.5 and DTT (dithiothreitol) addition. Reduced sample was diluted in 0.2% TFA solution just before mass spectrometry analysis. Characterization was completed by amino acid composition and N-terminal sequence determination using the Edman method. Peptide sequencing was performed according to Edman chemistry on an automatic ABI 492HT/Procise peptide sequencer from Applied Biosystems (Foster City, CA). Samples were loaded onto a TFA-treated glass fiber precycled with Biobrene Plus. Nanoelectrospray mass spectrometry was performed offline on a quadrupole time-of-flight (Q-TOF) mass spectrometer (QSTAR Pulsar-i, Applied Biosystems) fitted with a Protana nanospray inlet system (Protana, Odense, Denmark). Spectra were recorded using the Analyst QS software (Applied Biosystems). Parameters were adjusted as follows: ion spray voltage (IS), 900 V; curtain gas (CUR), 25; declustering potential (DP), 10–45 V; focusing potential (FP), 265 V; declustering potential 2 (DP2), 15 V. Fragmentation experiments (CID) were performed in the collision cell using nitrogen gas on the doubly or triply charged ions detected, with a collision energy profile optimized individually (20–65 V). Before being placed in the source tip holder, capillaries (Protana) were loaded with the desalted samples as previously described.(32) Briefly, a 2 μL aliquot of chromatographically purified pachymodulin was loaded on Poros 20 R2 (Applied Biosystems) packed in a gel-loader pipet tip, washed with 1% formic acid (2 μL), and eluted with 2 μL of 50:50:1 methanol/water/formic acid.

    Solid-Phase Peptide Synthesis and Purification

    The synthesis of pachymodulin was carried out by a solid-phase Fastmoc chemistry procedure on an Applied Biosystems 433A Automated Peptide Synthesizer (Applera, Courtaboeuf, France) as previously described.(4) Resins and Fmoc-protected amino acids were purchased from Merck Chemicals (Novabiochem, UK) and solvents from SDS (Aix-en-Provence, France). Fmoc-Met-NovasynTGA (100–200 mesh) was used for pachymodulin. Briefly, synthesis products were cleaved from the resin by a mixture of TFA (94%), H2O (2.5%), ethanedithiol (2.5%), and triisopropylsilane (TIS) (1%), precipitated in ether, centrifuged, and lyophilized. Peptides were purified by a semipreparative HPLC (C18 reverse-phase column, PrepLC 25 mm module, 250 mm × 100 mm, 15 mm particle, Waters, Guyancourt, France) on a Waters 1252 Binary HPLC pump (flow rate 8 mL min–1). The purity of pachymodulin (>95%) was assessed by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry (Voyager DE P60, Applied Biosystems). A purity of >95% corresponds to only one peak detected in the analytical RP HPLC by UV (220 nm), with no other peak than the expected one in the MS spectrum. Pachymodulin concentration was assessed by dry weight method and by absorption measurement at λ = 205 nm as described.(33)

    Circular Dichroism (CD) Spectroscopy

    The CD spectra of the peptides were recorded on a Jasco 815 spectropolarimeter (Jasco Inc., Easton, MD) over the wavelength range of 190–270 nm, by using a 0.1 cm path length quartz cell (internal volume 200 μL) from Hellma GmbH. CD spectra were recorded at 20 °C, at 0.2 nm intervals and 10 nm min–1 scan speed. Typically, four scans were accumulated and averaged. Measurements were carried out in 10 mM phosphate buffer pH 7.4, in the presence of DMPC (1,2-dimyristoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphatidylcholine) or DMPG (1,2-dimyristoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphatidylglycerol)/DMPC (40/60) LUVs (Avanti Polar Lipids, Alabaster, AL) at a peptide/lipid molar ratio of 1/50. All dichroic spectra were corrected by subtracting the background obtained for each peptide-free solution. Analysis of CD spectra was conducted after curve smoothing, and conversion to molar ellipticity was calculated as follows: [θ = θobs/(10 × n × l)], where θobs is the observed ellipticity, n, the number of bonds, c, the peptide concentration in mol L–1, l, path length in cm.(34)

    Antimicrobial Assays and Hemolytic Activities

    The Gram-negative bacterial strains Escherichia coli ATCC ML35p, Escherichia coli ATCC 11775, Staphylococcus aureus ATCC 25923, and Staphylococcus aureus ST1065 (generous gift from M. Falord and T. Msadek, Biology of Gram Positive Pathogens, CNRS URA 2172, Institut Pasteur, Paris) were used. Strains were cultured as previously described.(5) The hemolytic activity of the peptide was determined using fresh rat erythrocytes. The blood was centrifuged, and erythrocytes were rinsed three times with PBS. The peptide was incubated with suspended erythrocytes (4% v/v in PBS) at 37 °C for 60 min, and the cells were then removed by centrifugation at 1500g for 5 min at 4 °C. Hemolysis was assessed by measuring the optical density of the supernatant at 550 nm (FLUOstar OPTIMA, BMG, Ortenberg, Germany). As a positive control, erythrocytes were lysed with 1% Triton X-100 to produce 100% hemolysis.

    Membrane Polarization Assay and Peptide-Induced Permeabilization of the Cytoplasmic Membrane of E. coli

    The cytoplasmic membrane depolarization activity was determined with the membrane potential-sensitive dye 3,3′-dipropylthiadicarbocyanine iodide (diS-C3(5)) (Molecular Probes-Invitrogen, Saint-Aubin, France) and E. coli 363 ATCC 11775. The permeabilization of the cytoplasmic membrane of E. coli ML35p by pachymodulin was assayed by measuring the β-galactosidase activity with the chromogenic substrate 2-nitrophenyl-β-d-galactopyrannoside (ONPG) as previously described.(4)

    Cell Preparation

    Human neutrophils, monocytes, and T lymphocytes were obtained as previously described.(5) Mouse bone marrow cells (MBMC) were obtained from C57BL/6 mice. Native and stably expressing muFPR2 or huFPR2 human embryonic kidney 293 (HEK293) cells(10, 35) (generous gift from P.M. Murphy, Laboratory of Molecular Immunology, NIH, Bethesda, MD) were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 2 mM l-glutamine, 1% nonessential amino acids, 2 mM sodium pyruvate, 10% FBS, penicillin (50 U/mL), and streptomycin (50 μg/mL) at 37 °C in 5% CO2. HEK-muFPR2 or -huFPR2 were grown in the presence of G418 (200 μg/mL).

    Chemotaxis Assays

    The chemotactic activity was tested on human or mouse leukocytes labeled or not with calcein (4′,5′-bis(N,N-bis(carboxymethyl)aminomethyl)fluorescein acetoxymethyl ester) using a 48-well microchemotaxis chamber (Neuro Probe, Gaithersburg, MD) as described previously.(5) Briefly, different concentrations of chemoattractants (pachymodulin or fMLP) diluted in chemotaxis medium were placed in the bottom wells of the chamber. The chamber was incubated for 45 min (neutrophils), 3 h (T cells), 4 h (monocytes), or 5 h (HEK, HEK-huFPR2, HEK-muFPR2) at 37 °C, 5% CO2, in a humidified atmosphere. Cell migration was assessed by measuring the fluorescence of the calcein on the lower face of the membranes with an Alpha Innotech image analyzer, using the Fluorchem 8800 software from Alpha Innotech (San Leandro, CA). Alternatively, cell migration was assessed by counting the cells by FACSCalibur flow cytometry with a predetermined number of beads (Polybead, carboxylate microsphere Polyscience, Inc., Warrington, PA) added to the tube or after DAPI staining under the microscope for HEK cells. Before counting the cells by flow cytometry, cells were immunophenotyped with a mix of fluorescent antibodies (antimouse Ly6G-PE, antimouse NK1.1-PE, antimouse CD11b-PerCP, antimouse 7/4-FITC, BD Biosciences, Le Pont-de-Claix, France). Data were analyzed with the FlowJo software. To differentiate between chemotaxis and chemokinesis, experiments were carried out in which the same concentrations of pachymodulin were added simultaneously to the upper and lower wells. For some experiments, prior to the evaluation of their migration toward pachymodulin or fMLP, neutrophils were preincubated for 30 min at 37 °C with PTX (Sigma-Aldrich, Toluca, Mexico), a specific GPCR inhibitor that ADP-ribosylates Gi proteins (200 ng mL1); fMLP (0.1 μM) or fMLP (10 μM) (Sigma-Aldrich, Toluca, Mexico); pachymodulin (10 μM); PD98059 (Calbiochem, USA) (30 μM), a selective ERK1/2 inhibitor, or SB202190 (Calbiochem) (10 μM), a p38 MAPK inhibitor.

    Cell Activation and Immunoblot

    Purified human neutrophils (2 × 106/sample) resuspended in RPMI 1640 were incubated with pachymodulin (10 μM), fMLP (100 nM), or chemotaxis medium for the indicated periods of time at 37 °C, 5% CO2. In some cases, neutrophils had been pretreated for 30 min at 37 °C with PTX, MEK1/2 inhibitor (30 μM), or p38 inhibitor (10 μM). The phosphorylation of the MAPK ERK1/ERK2 was detected with an antiphospho-ERK1/2 (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Dallas, TX) by chemiluminescence with ECL (Amersham, PA). Membranes were reprobed for ERK1/2 (Santa Cruz Biotechnology).

    Isothermal Titration Calorimetry (ITC)

    An amount of 1.5 × 106 native, huFPR2 stably expressing HEK cells resuspended in 1.5 mL of 50 mM Tris-HCl, 100 mM NaCl, and 6 mg/mL glucose was placed in the microcalorimeter cell compartment of a TA Instrument Nano ITC calorimeter (Waters, Guyancourt, France). After thermal equilibrium was reached, 50 μL of 3 mM pachymodulin, diluted in the same buffer, was injected into the compartment, leading to a 100 μM final extracellular concentration. Assays were performed at 37 °C, under constant stirring, to avoid cell deposition at the bottom of the compartment. After ITC measurements, a constant baseline was subtracted and the heat of the interaction after injection plotted versus time. As control experiments, 50 μL of buffer was injected into the compartment containing the cells and 50 μL of peptide or ligand was injected into the compartment containing only buffer. The total heat signal was determined from the area under the curve after subtracting the heat of the controls, using the NanoAnalyze software provided by TA Instruments (Waters).

    Calcium Assays

    Cytosolic-free calcium assay was measured by fluorescent detection with the HitHunter Calcium No WashPLUS (Ca NWPLUS) assay kit (DiscoveRex, Fremont, CA). Briefly, 5 × 104 HEK 293 cells expressing or not huFPR2 or muFPR2 were plated overnight at 37 °C, 5% CO2, before preloading with a calcium-sensitive dye (Ca NWPLUS working reagent) supplemented with probenecid (Sigma-Aldrich, L’Isle-d’Abeau, France) to avoid calcium dye leakage, for 1 h at 37 °C. Cells were then treated with fMLP or with pachymodulin. Signal was measured as a function of time on a fluorescent plate reader equipped with fluidic handling (FlexStation 3, Molecular Devices, St. Grégoire, France). Maximum change in fluorescence over baseline (peak signal), as quantified using SoftMax Pro software, was used to determine agonist response. All experiments were run in triplicate, and data shown are representative of at least three independent experiments.

    Animals

    Ten-week-old male or female C57BL/6 mice (Janvier, Le Genest-Saint-Isle, France) were kept in pathogen-free conditions, with food and water available ad libitum and housed in a 12 h light/12 h dark (100–500 lx) cycle. Animal experiments were approved by the local institutional Animal Care and Use Committee.

    In Vivo Migration Assay

    C57BL/6 mice were injected ip (intraperitoneal) either with 1 mL of 3% (wt/vol) thioglycollate (Sigma-Aldrich, l’Isle- d’Abeau, France) or with 1 mL of pachymodulin (tid; 40 μg/injection) dissolved in sterile NaCl. Where specified, 4, 8, or 12 h after injection of 3% thioglycollate, mice were treated with pachymodulin (40 μg/injection) or NaCl. Twenty-four hours after the first injection, mice were killed, blood was collected, and 3 mL of cold PBS was injected ip to harvest peritoneal cells which were then stained with antimouse Ly6G-PE, antimouse NK1.1-PE, antimouse CD11b-PerCP, antimouse 7/4-FITC, and anti F4/80-APC (BD Biosciences). Cells and beads were then counted by FACSCalibur flow cytometry, and data were analyzed with FlowJo. Monocytes were considered to be CD11bhiLy6G–NK1.1–F4/80int, macrophages CD11bhiLy6G–NK1.1– F4/80lo, and neutrophils CD11bhiLy6G+NK1.1– F4/80–. Statistical comparisons used unpaired two sample t-tests (p < 0.05) was considered statistically significant.
