    Abstract

    Celastrol is a natural pentacyclic triterpene used in traditional Chinese medicine with significant weight-lowering effects. Celastrol-administered mice at 100 μg/kg decrease food consumption and body weight via a leptin-dependent mechanism, yet its molecular targets in this pathway remain elusive. Here, we demonstrate in vivo that celastrol-induced weight loss is largely mediated by the inhibition of leptin negative regulators protein tyrosine phosphatase (PTP) 1B (PTP1B) and T-cell PTP (TCPTP) in the arcuate nucleus (ARC) of the hypothalamus. We show in vitro that celastrol binds reversibly and inhibits noncompetitively PTP1B and TCPTP. NMR data map the binding site to an allosteric site in the catalytic domain that is in proximity of the active site. By using a panel of PTPs implicated in hypothalamic leptin signaling, we show that celastrol additionally inhibited PTEN and SHP2 but had no activity toward other phosphatases of the PTP family. These results suggest that PTP1B and TCPTP in the ARC are essential for celastrol’s weight lowering effects in adult obese mice.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Celastrol is a triterpenoid isolated from different plants, namely from Celastrus scandens (bittersweet) and Tripterygium wilfordii (thunder god vine), with several therapeutic applications. In traditional Chinese medicine, celastrol-rich extracts of thunder god vine root are used to treat inflammation. Recent studies suggest that celastrol has beneficial properties in a wide spectrum of inflammatory diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, inflammatory bowel diseases, osteoarthritis, as well as cancer and neurodegenerative disorders.(1) Observations of celastrol as a weight- and glucose-lowering agent were first made in Lepdb (leptin receptor deficient) mice after treatment with 1 or 3 mg/kg celastrol.(2,3) Liu et al.(4) showed that celastrol at 100 μg/kg acts as strong leptin sensitizer, which inhibits food intake, reduces body weight, and improves glucose tolerance in diet-induced obese (DIO) but not in leptin deficient Lepob or Lepdb mice.
    Several mechanisms have been proposed for celastrol’s weight-lowering effects, including the increased expression of liver Sirt1 in mice,(5) an impaired adipocyte differentiation and increased lipolysis in vitro in 3T3-L1 adipocyte cells,(6) the inhibition of proteasome activity,(7) or an elevated heat-shock response.(8,9) Celastrol was further shown to induce the HSF1-PGC-1α transcriptional axis, leading to increased muscle and brown-adipose tissue (BAT) thermogenesis, and inguinal white adipose tissue (iWAT) browning.(10) This impact of celastrol on BAT and iWAT browning, i.e., an increase in uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1) levels, was later confirmed in our DIO mouse model, but the overall contribution to the weight loss efficacy of celastrol has been disputed.(11)
    To date, confirmed direct molecular targets of celastrol are mostly linked to inflammatory pathways and include the NF-κB kinases IKKα/β that are inhibited as consequence of celastrol binding to a Cys in the kinase activation loop.(12) Alternatively, binding of celastrol to the Hsp90 dimer interface,(13) Hsp90 Glu33,(14) or to a Cys residue in Cdc37(15,16) has been reported to destabilize the Hsp90-Cdc37-IKK complex and inhibit IKK signaling. Celastrol has also been reported to bind to Nur77, promoting its translocation to the mitochondria, interaction with TRAF2, Nur77 ubiquitination, and autophagy, ultimately alleviating inflammation.(17) However, these mechanisms do not explain the requirement for intact leptin signaling and therefore are unlikely to be directly involved in celastrol’s weight loss efficacy.
    In a screen for protein tyrosine phosphatase (PTP) inhibitors, celastrol was identified as an inhibitor of SHP2 and PTP1B,(18) phosphatases that have opposing effects on hypothalamic leptin signaling.(19,20) Indeed, celastrol has structural similarities to known natural inhibitors of PTP1B,(21) namely to trodusquemine (MSI-1436), a known allosteric inhibitor of PTP1B and a potent weight loss-promoting agent.(22,23) Therefore, we assessed whether the weight loss promoting effects of celastrol might be reliant on the inhibition of PTP1B. Moreover, due to a high catalytic domain sequence identity between PTP1B and T-Cell PTP (TCPTP)(24) and previous studies showing that TCPTP also negatively regulates hypothalamic leptin signaling,(25) we further determined whether the effects of celastrol might be at least in part dependent on the inhibition of TCPTP. PTP1B resides on the outer membrane of the endoplasmic reticulum, whereas TCPTP shuttles in and out of the nucleus and has access to nuclear and cytoplasmic substrates.(24) In the hypothalamus, PTP1B and TCPTP negatively regulate leptin signaling by dephosphorylating the downstream effectors Janus-activated kinase-2 (JAK-2)(26) and signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3),(25) respectively. Previous studies have shown that PTP1B and TCPTP can function in concert to regulate insulin signaling;(24) PTP1B inhibits insulin signaling by dephosphorylating insulin receptor (IR) and insulin receptor substrate 1 (IRS1)(27) to regulate the amplitude of signaling, while TCPTP dephosphorylates IR to regulate the duration of signaling.(28) TCPTP also has a fundamental role in hematopoietic development, inflammation, and the maintenance of T-cell tolerance through the regulation of Src family kinases and JAK-1/3 and STAT-1/3/5 signaling.(29−32) Mice with global deficiency in TCPTP develop hematopoietic defects and systemic inflammation and succumb soon after birth.(32,33) However, findings from mice that are heterozygous for TCPTP,(24,34) or from mice in which TCPTP has been conditionally deleted in neuronal and glial cells(25) or POMC-(35) or AgRP-neurons(36) suggest that targeting TCPTP may be beneficial for promoting insulin and leptin signaling to combat DIO and improve glucose metabolism. Therefore, we hypothesized that the weight loss promoting effects of celastrol in DIO might be reliant on the inhibition of PTP1B and TCPTP in the hypothalamus.

    Results

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    Celastrol-Induced Weight Loss Requires Hypothalamic PTP1B and TCPTP

    In adult mice, functional leptin signaling is necessary for celastrol’s catabolic action.(4,11) PTPs in the hypothalamus are important negative regulators of leptin and insulin signaling and have been long considered as potential antiobesity targets.(24,37) Deletion of PTP1B or TCPTP, or PTP1B and TCPTP, in the CNS combats DIO through the promotion of leptin sensitivity.(25) Although it has been shown that PTP1B is inhibited by celastrol in vitro,(18) it is not known whether hypothalamic PTP1B and TCPTP might be required for the weight-lowering activities of celastrol. We therefore aimed to assess whether celastrol could promote weight loss in obese mice in which PTP1B (encoded by Ptpn1), TCPTP (encoded by Ptpn2), or both PTP1B and TCPTP were deleted in the ARC. Recombinant adeno-associated viruses (rAAVs) expressing Cre and GFP (rAAV-CMV-Cre-GFP) or GFP alone (rAAV-CMV-GFP) were injected bilaterally into the ARC of high fat fed Ptpn1fl/fl, Ptpn2fl/fl, and Ptpn1fl/fl;Ptpn2fl/fl mice, and high fat feeding continued. Post-mortem analyses confirmed efficient targeting of the ARC (data not shown). Celastrol treatment induced the expected weight loss (Figure 1) and hypophagia (data not shown) in the individual and in the double-floxed mice administered control rAAV-CMV-GFP (Figure 1). By contrast, the administration of rAAV-CMV-Cre-GFP for the ARC deletion of PTP1B (Figure 1a) or TCPTP (Figure 1b) partially attenuated the weight-lowering effects of celastrol, whereas the combined targeting of PTP1B and TCPTP (Figure 1c) completely abolished the weight-lowering effects of celastrol. These results indicate that PTP1B and TCPTP in the ARC are indispensable for celastrol’s weight-lowering action in adult obese mice.

    Celastrol Is an Inhibitor of PTP1B and TCPTP Enzyme Activity

    After establishing the in vivo reliance on hypothalamic PTP1B and TCPTP for the weight lowering effects of celastrol, we next aimed to delineate the exact nature of celastrol binding and inhibition of PTP1B and TCPTP. In vitro enzyme kinetics assays using DiFMUP as a fluorescent substrate revealed that celastrol-mediated phosphatase activity inhibition of constructs containing the phosphatase domain of PTP1B or TCPTP (PTP1B1–298, TCPTP1–296; Figure 2a,b) and of longer constructs including the C-terminal tail but without endoplasmic reticulum and nuclear localization sequences (PTP1B1–393, TCPTP1–336; Figure 2c,d). The inhibition, with IC50 values of 2.1 μM for PTP1B1–393 and 18.8 μM for TCPTP1–336, is noncompetitive, as Vmax decreased at constant Km in celastrol-titration phosphatase assays (Figure 2e,f). For both phosphatases, the IC50 values do not change significantly between the constructs containing the phosphatase domain or including the C-terminal part, indicating that celastrol binds to the catalytic domain, and the C-terminal tail does not affect compound binding. Similar IC50 values were obtained using pNPP as a substrate (data not shown). Scott and colleagues(18) reported a celastrol inhibition IC50 value of 13.2 μM toward PTP1B1–435 using the DiFMUP assay in slightly different conditions (50 mM Bis-Tris, pH 7.0, 50 mM NaCl, 0.01% Triton X-100, 1 mM dithiothreitol, 1 mM EDTA, 40 μM DiFMUP, 100 nM PTP at room temperature).

    Celastrol Is a Reversible Inhibitor

    We also probed the reversibility of the inhibition with a jump-dilution experiment that assessed the recovery of the enzymatic activity upon a 100-fold dilution of the enzyme solution with celastrol at a concentration sufficient to inhibit the enzymatic activity 90% (IC90) (Figure 3a,b). Restoration of enzymatic activity after dilution to values comparable to the activity at IC10 celastrol concentration is indicative of reversible behavior. Mass spectrometry analyses of PTP1B1–298 with and without celastrol (Figure 3c,d) revealed that celastrol does not bind covalently to the protein, consistent again with a reversible inhibition. We further used switchSENSE technology(38) to confirm binding of celastrol to surface-immobilized PTP1B1–298 in vitro (Figure 3e,f). Celastrol binds to PTP1B1–298 with a KD of 6.1 μM, which is similar to the IC50 of 4.8 μM of PTP1–298, determined by enzymatic assays and confirming its noncompetitive nature (IC50 ∼ KD for a noncompetitive inhibitor).(39) Celastrol shows slow association (kon) and dissociation (koff) rates, suggesting that some conformational change or dynamics of the protein is associated with the interaction (PTP1B–celastrol complex half-life 31.91 min). The long half-life of the PTP1B–celastrol complex justifies why in the jump-dilution experiment, the PTP1B activity after dilution is not completely superimposable with the IC10 control curve (Figure 3a). Part of the PTP1B-celastrol complexes in the IC90 reaction were still bound after dilution and did not dissociate during the activity assay with DiFMUP.

    Celastrol Is a Noncompetitive Inhibitor

    Competition saturation transfer difference (STD) NMR experiments(40) performed with 4′-aminoxanilic acid (AOAC, PTP1B1–393 IC50 = 910 μM; TCPTP1–336 IC50 = 478 μM, data not shown), a derivative of the PTP1B active-site inhibitor 2-[4-(aminomethyl)anilino]-2-oxoacetic acid,(41) further verify the noncompetitive nature of the celastrol binding to PTP1B (Figure 4a,b). STD signals of AOAC (or celastrol) do not change in intensity upon addition of celastrol (or AOAC), while AOAC and celastrol STD signals are observed simultaneously in the presence of both inhibitors. This indicates that celastrol and AOAC can bind to PTP1B simultaneously and that celastrol does not compete with the active site inhibitor AOAC (Figure 4a,b). The STD signals from AOAC and celastrol are weak. This has to do, on one hand, with the weak binding of AOAC to PTP1B and, on the other hand, with the slow koff rate of the PTP1B–celastrol complex (Figure 3f).
    In addition, we observe in STD-NMR experiments that both the quinone methide and the aliphatic moiety containing the carboxylate group of celastrol are important for binding to PTP1B (Figure 4) and TCPTP (data not shown).
    To exclude that the inhibitory activity of celastrol is due to aggregation, we tested the effect of three different factors on the inhibitory ability of celastrol.(42,43) This included a 10-fold protein concentration increase and addition of detergent (0.05% Tween 20) or BSA (0.1 mg/mL) (Supporting Information, Figure S1). None of the three factors had a significant impact on the IC50 of celastrol to PTP1B and TCPTP (Supporting Information, Figure S1, Table S1), showing that celastrol does not act as an aggregator.

    NMR Spectroscopy Shows Direct Celastrol Binding to PTP1B and TCPTP

    We used NMR chemical shift perturbation (CSP) analysis to map the binding site of celastrol onto the crystal structure of PTP1B. CSPs were observed in 2D 1H,15N NMR correlation spectra upon titration of celastrol (Figure 5a–d). Significant CSPs and line broadening upon celastrol addition are observed for residues in strand β3 (I82 and L83), helix α2 and strand β4 (R105 and V107), WPD loop (D181), active site loop (V212, V213, and H214), and the Q loop (F256, G259, T263, and A264) (Figure 5b,c). These data indicate that celastrol binds in the vicinity of the catalytic site, extending from the N-terminal region (strand β3, helix α2, and strand β4) to the interior of the protein. This is consistent with noncompetitive allosteric inhibition (Figure 5c,d). Although TCPTP is not assigned, NMR titration experiments of celastrol with TCPTP exhibit comparable CSPs and line broadening (Supporting Information, Figure S2). By taking into account the high homology of the catalytic domain of the two proteins, we compared the PTP1B residues with CSPs higher than 2σ and line broadening with the corresponding TCPTP residues. We found that 10 out of 19 residues are identical (H54, I82, L83, V107, D181, V212, H214, T230, G259, and T263 in PTP1B), while six other residues are of the same type (R105/K107, I145/L146, R156/H157, V213/I214, F256/Y254, and H296/K294 in PTP1B/TCPTP), suggesting a similar binding of celastrol to TCPTP.

    Structure–Activity Relationship Studies on PTP1B and TCPTP with Other Triterpenoids

    The reduced form of celastrol, dihydrocelastrol, was synthesized (Supporting Information, Figure S3a) and tested to examine if the ring that contains the ketone group is indeed important for the binding. Supporting Information, Figure S3b shows that dihydrocelastrol, compared to celastrol (Figure 5a), induces only moderate CSPs and line broadening in 2D 1H–15N correlation spectra. We used the DiFMUP assay to determine the inhibitory ability of dihydrocelastrol on PTP1B1–393 and TCPTP1–336.. Dihydrocelastrol inhibits in the millimolar range (Supporting Information, Figure S3c), which stands in stark contrast to the low micromolar IC50 of celastrol (Figure 2c,d). These results indicate that quinone methide epitope in celastrol is important for protein binding and that its removal results in dramatic change in affinity.
    Withaferin A is another triterpenoid that was previously described as leptin sensitizer with weight-reducing properties similar to celastrol.(44) After testing withaferin A with the DiFMUP assay, we found that it had no inhibitory effect on full-length forms of PTP1B or TCPTP (Supporting Information, Figure S3d).

    Celastrol Selectivity on Hypothalamic PTPs

    We next measured the selectivity of celastrol toward other PTPs, namely SHP1, SHP2, PTPRE, PTPRJ, LMPTPA, LMPTPB, and PTEN (Figure 6 and Table 1). Scott et al.(18) reported inhibition of SHP1 and SHP2 by celastrol with IC50 values of 27.8 and 3.3 μM while we determined values of 3.1 and 4.1 μM, respectively. The determined IC50 for SHP2 is very similar, while the one for SHP1 is significantly lower, which may be attributed to the use of a different construct or different experimental conditions. Apart from SHP1 and SHP2 inhibition, celastrol inhibits PTEN with a comparable potency to TCPTP. All the other phosphatases are weakly or not inhibited by celastrol. These data indicate that celastrol is a partially selective inhibitor of PTPs. Interestingly, of the known PTPs involved in the leptin signaling pathway in the hypothalamus (PTP1B, TCPTP, PTEN, SHP2, PTPRJ, and PTPRE),(45) PTP1B, SHP2, PTEN, and TCPTP show low μM celastrol inhibition. However, PTP1B and TCPTP are negative leptin regulators while SHP2 is a positive regulator(46) and PTEN could be a positive or negative regulator.(47−49)

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Celastrol is a promising antiobesity drug that induces hypophagia due to in vivo leptin resensitization,(4) as well as iWAT browning and increasing BAT UCP1 levels.(10) We confirmed recently that, in adult mice, celastrol induced weight loss is largely leptin-dependent with a decrease in food consumption and a concomitant increase in UCP1 levels in iWAT and BAT.(11) However, we observed comparable body weight loss and hypophagia in UCP1 knockout and wild-type mice treated with celastrol, which suggests that UCP1-mediated thermogenesis is not a major driver for the body weight-lowering effects of celastrol. The lack of celastrol-mediated weight loss in adult Lepdb and Lepob mice indicates that it is a leptin-dependent mechanism and that the molecular target(s) responsible for this effect are components of this pathway. In fact, it was reported that celastrol targets PTP1B,(18) a key negative regulator of the leptin signaling pathway.(19,20) By taking into account the high-homology between the catalytic domains of PTP1B and TCPTP and their role as negative regulators in the hypothalamic leptin signaling,(20,25) we predicted PTP1B and TCPTP to be molecular targets of celastrol.
    Indeed, we found that in vivo genetic deletion of PTP1B, TCPTP, or their combined deletion in the ARC of adult mice largely abolished celastrol’s weight lowering effect, suggesting that these PTPs in the ARC are indispensable for celastrol-induced weight loss. However, the exact neuronal populations in the ARC that mediate celastrol’s catabolic actions and the individual roles of the two PTPs on celastrol-induced weight loss remain to be determined. It further remains unclear why global PTP1B deficient mice showed similar celastrol-induced weight loss as wild-type mice in our previous studies.(11) It appears possible that germline ablation of PTP1B can be fully or partially compensated by TCPTP or other structurally similar and coexpressed PTPs. The ablation of PTP1B in adult mice via virally induced Cre recombination does not allow such long-term adaptations during embryonal and subsequent development, which may explain the partial loss of celastrols’ weight loss efficacy in these mice. Future studies should aim at delineating this putative interplay and compensation. Such studies should further assess whether celastrol also acts on neurocircuitry outside the MBH or on peripheral tissues, and the extent to which this is reliant in PTP- or non-PTP-related mechanisms.
    As suggested by our in vivo findings, we show evidence that celastrol is a potent, noncompetitive inhibitor of PTP1B with IC50 values of 2.1 and 4.8 μM for PTP1B1–393 and PTP1B1–298, respectively, and a KD of 6.1 μM for PTP1B1–298. Inhibitory effects of celastrol are not mediated via compound aggregation or assay interference. Our NMR studies rather demonstrate that celastrol is an allosteric inhibitor that binds to PTP1B in the vicinity of the catalytic site but nonoverlapping with the binding site of an active site inhibitor. Celastrol binding to PTP1B is reversible as confirmed by activity assays, jump-dilution experiments, STD-competition experiments, switchSENSE, and mass spectrometry. SwitchSENSE kinetics data indicate additionally that celastrol association and dissociation is slow, indicative of a conformational change of PTP1B-associated ligand binding. This is consistent with studies by Loria et al., who showed that the active site loop in PTP1B is dynamic and that allosteric inhibitors can modulate the activity by altering the hydrogen bond patterns at the active site.(50,51) Celastrol also inhibits TCPTP noncompetitively but has almost 10-fold higher selectivity toward PTP1B, which may reflect the distinct inhibition potency of PTP1B and TCPTP in vivo. Nevertheless, coinhibition of TCPTP may expedite PTP1B-driven in vivo benefits of celastrol on body weight and glucose metabolism. Celastrol binds most probably with a similar binding mode to PTP1B and TCPTP, as the active site and the surrounding areas of the two PTPs are highly homologous.(52) We do not observe a significantly stronger celastrol inhibition using PTP1B and TCPTP constructs with longer C-terminal tails, indicating that the unstructured tails of the PTPs do not contribute to celastrol mediated inhibition. By contrast, using NMR spectroscopy, Tonks and collaborators observed that trodusquemine binds noncompetitively to two allosteric sites in PTP1B, one closer to the catalytic region and another on the disordered C-terminal tail.(22) This suggests that celastrol and trodusquemine inhibit PTPs using different molecular mechanisms, the former targeting an allosteric site close to the active site, the latter targeting allosteric site(s) in the C-terminal region. This effect appears to be specific to celastrol, as the two related compounds dihydrocelastrol or withaferin A have weak or no inhibitory activity toward PTP1B and TCPTP.
    By using a panel of PTPs we show that celastrol inhibits other phosphatases in vitro, namely SHP2 and PTEN, which are also relevant for hypothalamic leptin action. SHP2 is an enhancer of leptin signaling, and its genetic ablation leads to body weight gain in mice.(46) It has been shown that PTEN deletion in leptin-sensitive neurons results in lean mice,(48) while PTEN ablation in POMC neurons leads to leptin resistance and obesity in mice,(47) suggesting that PTEN has differential roles in hypothalamic leptin signaling.(49) Therefore, hypothalamic genetic ablation of PTEN could result in weight gain or loss in mice, depending on the neuronal populations affected. On the other hand mice with a neuronal genetic deletion of PTP1B(20) or TCPTP(25) are protected from diet-induced obesity. Moreover, deletion of PTP1B and TCPTP in POMC neurons enhances leptin and insulin signaling respectively and prevents diet-induced obesity by increasing WAT browning and energy expenditure.(35) Therefore, the weight lowering effects of celastrol might extend not only to the promotion of leptin sensitivity but also the alleviation of CNS insulin resistance.
    Although the precise mechanisms by which celastrol affects the hypothalamic PTP1B/TCPTP-mediated control of energy balance remain to be resolved, our studies indicate that the weight lowering effects of celastrol are absolutely reliant on PTP1B and TCPTP and suggest that any effects on other PTPs, such as SHP2 or PTEN, being of little relevance. Our studies indicate that celastrol can directly inhibit PTP1B and to a lesser extent TCPTP. However, it is also possible that celastrol may promote weight loss, at least in part, by inhibiting inflammatory signaling that promotes hypothalamic PTP1B expression in obesity.(53) Discerning between these possibilities will require further investigation.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, we have identified key molecular mechanisms and targets responsible for the unprecedented antiobesity action profile of the drug candidate celastrol. Our studies are consistent with celastrol driving weight loss via inhibition of PTP1B and TCPTP in the ARC. Celastrol inhibition of PTP1B and TCPTP is mediated by reversible noncompetitive binding to an allosteric pocket close to the active site. The binding kinetics are slow, indicative of associated conformational changes on the protein upon celastrol interaction. This discovery encourages reconsideration of PTP1B and TCPTP as drug targets against metabolic dysfunction and reinforces the concept that natural compounds derived from traditional medicine, even with multiple targets, are therapeutically and scientifically of considerable interest.

    Chemicals

    Chemical Synthesis of Dihydrocelastrol

    The compound dihydrocelastrol was synthesized as previously described by Klaić and colleagues(54) using celastrol as a starting material. In detail, in a 5 mL round flask 5 mg of celastrol (0.011 mmol) were dissolved in approximately 1 mL of MeOH and 4.2 mg (0.11 mmol) of NaBH4 were added to the solution. The mixture immediately turned from orange to a clear solution. After 10 min of stirring at room temperature, the reaction was quenched with 0.1 M HCl and the precipitants were extracted with 5 mL of CHCl3 and 10 mL of H2O in a 20 mL extraction flask. The aqueous layer was washed with CHCl3 three times, and the organic layer was collected and dried with Na2SO4 for a short period to prevent reoxidation of the product to celastrol. The Na2SO4 was removed from the organic layer by simple filtration. The solvent was removed in a Buchi Rotavapor R-200 with “V” assembly under vacuum. The purity of the compound was tested by HPLC-UV/MS and by NMR (Supporting Information, Figures S4–S5). Dihydrocelastrol was found to be 97% pure with 3% celastrol, most probably as reoxidation product.

CN80P100), [13C]D-d7-glucose (2 g/L) (97% D, 99% 13C, Sigma-Aldrich, no. 552151) supplemented with 0.012% (w/v) 13C, 15N-rich growth media Silantes (Silantes, no. 115604300) in 70% D2O (99.85% D Eurisotop, no. D216L). Uniformly 2H (∼70%), 15N (99%)-labeled protein was expressed at 37 °C using M9 minimal medium containing 15NH4Cl, [12C]D-d7-glucose(2 g/L) (97% D, Sigma-Aldrich, no. 55203) and 15N-rich growth media Silantes in 70% D2O. The next day, three precultures of 50 mL of M9 minimal medium in H2O were inoculated with 0.5, 1.0, or 2.0 mL of the overnight LB preculture and grown at 37 °C. Later on the same day, the preculture with optical density at 600 nm (OD600) closest to 0.6 was spun down for 10 min at 3202g.     In Vitro Phosphatase Activity Assays

    Phosphatase kinetic parameters were determined using a fluorescence assay with DiFMUP (Life Technologies GmbH, no. D6567) as substrate.(58) Experiments were performed in triplicate in black polystyrene 384-well plates with flat bottoms (Corning, no. 3575) at 37 °C. The fluorescence excitation was measured at 358 nm and fluorescence emission at 455 nm and was monitored continuously for 10 min using a PerkinElmer EnVision multilabel plate reader. The protein concentration was optimized for maximum sensitivity and linearity using minimal protein concentration. In short, each protein was prediluted in reaction buffer (25 mM Bis-Tris propane pH 7.5, 50 mM NaCl, 2 mM EDTA) to three different concentrations in a final volume of 45 μL. Reactions were initiated by adding 5 μL of DiFMUP to the reaction mixture to yield final concentrations of 2–100 μM. Enzyme kinetic parameters were determined using nonlinear regression and the Michaelis–Menten equation in GraphPad Prism program 5.03 (GraphPad Software, Inc. La Jolla, CA, USA).
    For IC50 determination, 1 nM purified PTP1B or TCPTP protein was incubated for 10 min with celastrol (5% DMSO final concentration) in 15 concentrations ranging from 0 to 500 μM in reaction buffer (25 mM Bis-Tris propane pH 7.5, 50 mM NaCl, 2 mM EDTA) in a total volume of 45 μL. The reaction was started by adding 5 μL of substrate buffer containing DiFMUP to a final concentration equivalent to the protein construct Km for DiFMUP (10 μM and 7 μΜ DiFMUP for PTP1B1–298 and PTP1B1–393, respectively; 10 μΜ and 9 μM DiFMUP for TCPTP1–296 and TCPTP1–336, respectively). IC50 values were defined as the concentration of the inhibitor that caused a 50% decrease in the phosphatase activity and it was determined using the equation fitting log(inhibitor) vs response from nonlinear regression analysis in the GraphPad Prism program. For the IC50 determination of compounds AOAC, withaferin A, and dihydrocelastrol, the assay was performed in the same way.
    The selectivity of celastrol was tested over other eight PTPs: SHP1, SHP2, PTPRE, PTPRJ, LMPTPA, LMPTPB, and PTEN. For proteins SHP1, SHP2, PTPRE, PTPRJ, LMPTPA, and LMPTPB, the DiFMUP assay was used as descrided above for PTP1B and TCPTP.(58) The conditions for each protein used for the IC50 determination are shown in Supporting Information, Table S2.
    For PTEN, the colorimetric malachite green assay using PI(3,4,5)P3 (PIP3) (Echelon Biosciences Inc., no. P-3916a) as substrate was used.(59) Experiments were performed in triplicate in half-area transparent 96-well plates (Greiner Bio-one no. 675101). The protein concentration was optimized for maximum sensitivity and linearity using minimal protein concentration. In short, PIP3 was diluted to various concentrations ranging from 10 to 120 μM in reaction buffer (25 mM Tris-HCl pH 7.4, 140 mM NaCl) and added to purified PTEN in a final volume of 25 μL. The reaction incubated for 60 min at 37 °C and was terminated by the addition of 100 μL of malachite green (Echelon Biosciences Inc., no. K-1501). Malachite green forms a colored complex with free phosphate, which can be quantified by reading the absorption at 620 nm. The resulting absorption was read after incubation at room temperature for 20 min in a PerkinElmer EnVision multilabel plate reader. Enzyme kinetic parameters were determined using nonlinear regression and the Michaelis–Menten equation in GraphPad Prism version 5.03 (GraphPad Software, Inc. La Jolla, CA, USA). For IC50 determination, 1 μM purified PTEN was incubated for 10 min with celastrol (5% DMSO final concentration) in 12 concentrations ranging from 0 to 500 μM in reaction buffer (Tris-HCl pH 7.4, 140 mM NaCl) in a total volume of 25 μL. The reaction was started by adding 3 μL of 25 μΜ final concentration of PIP3, which is equivalent to the protein Km for PIP3. The reaction was left for 60 min at 37 °C and stopped by the addition of 100 μL of malachite green, and the absorbance was read at 620 nm after 20 min of incubation at room temperature. The IC50 values were defined the same way as for the other phosphatases using GraphPad Prism version 5.03.

    Assay Interference

    Reversibility of the inhibition was assessed by jump-dilution experiment.(60) A 100× solution containing PTP1B or TCPTP (100 nM) was incubated at 37 °C for 30 min with celastrol at a concentration that exhibits 90% inhibition (IC90, 21 μM for PTP1B and 188 μM for TCPTP). The samples were rapidly diluted 100-fold in reaction buffer, resulting in a 1× solution of the protein and 0.21 or 1.88 μM of celastrol for PTP1B and TCPTP, respectively. At this concentration, the compound exhibits 10% inhibition (IC10). The reactions were initiated by adding immediately in the resulting enzyme solutions DiFMUP to a final concentration equivalent to each protein Km for DiFMUP. The reaction was monitored continuously for 60 min at 37 °C using a PerkinElmer EnVision multilabel plate reader.
    The DiFMUP assay was used to test if celastrol acts as a promiscuous inhibitor.(61) The effect of protein concentration, of a detergent, and of BSA on the inhibition of PTP1B and TCPTP was measured. If the IC50 is increased under these conditions, the compound acts as a potential aggregator. More specifically, the IC50 for each protein was measured again in three different conditions, first with a 10-fold protein concentration (10 nM), second in the presence of 0.05% Tween 20 in the buffer conditions, and third in the presence of 0.1 mg/mL BSA in the buffer conditions. The experiments were perfomed and analyzed as it is described above for the IC50 determination of 1 nM PTP1B and TCPTP.

PH010859) (1% DMSO-d6 final concentration). Reference STD experiments without protein were performed at the same conditions using the same irradiation regions.     SwitchSENSE PTP1B–Celastrol Kinetic Experiments

    The kinetic and affinity parameters (kon, koff, KD) of the interaction between PTP1B1–298 and celastrol were determined using switchSENSE technology on a DRX instrument (Dynamic Biosensors GmbH, Martinsried, Germany).(38) In this experimental assay setup, PTP1B1–298 was the immobilized ligand on the switchSENSE chip (MPC-48-2-Y1-S) biosensor surface, while celastrol was injected as the analyte in solution. For immobilization on the biosensor surface, PTP1B1–298 was covalently coupled to single-stranded 48mer DNA complementary in sequence to the single-stranded DNA functionalized on the biosensor surface using amine chemistry (amine coupling kit CK-NH2-1-B48). The PTP1B-DNA conjugate was hybridized to the covalently immobilized single-stranded surface DNA. All experiments were carried out in PE40 buffer (10 mM Na2HPO4/NaH2PO4, pH 7.4, 40 mM NaCl, 50 μM EGTA, 50 μM EDTA, 0.05% Tween 20) using the fluorescence proximity sensing (FPS) mode. After the protein immobilization, celastrol was injected at increasing concentrations up to 100 μM under a constant flow of 100 μL/min. During the dissociation phase, the flow channel was rinsed with running buffer at a flow rate of 100 μL/min. The dissociation kinetics were only recorded for the three highest concentration steps. The biosensor surface was regenerated and functionalized with fresh PTP1B–DNA conjugate for each association–dissociation measurement cycle. All used consumables were obtained from Dynamic Biosensors GmbH, Martinsried, Germany. Data analysis was performed by switchANALYSIS software (Dynamic Biosensors GmbH, Martinsried, Germany) using a monoexponential global fit model.
