    Abstract

    As one of the central tasks of modern medicinal chemistry, scaffold hopping is expected to lead to the discovery of structural novel biological active compounds and broaden the chemical space of known active compounds. Here, we report the computational bioactivity fingerprint (CBFP) for easier scaffold hopping, where the predicted activities in multiple quantitative structure–activity relationship models are integrated to characterize the biological space of a molecule. In retrospective benchmarks, the CBFP representation shows outstanding scaffold hopping potential relative to other chemical descriptors. In the prospective validation for the discovery of novel inhibitors of poly [ADP-ribose] polymerase 1, 35 predicted compounds with diverse structures are tested, 25 of which show detectable growth-inhibitory activity; beyond this, the most potent (compound 6) has an IC50 of 0.263 nM. These results support the use of CBFP representation as the bioactivity proxy of molecules to explore uncharted chemical space and discover novel compounds.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Despite the widespread and extensive applications of combinatorial and parallel synthesis in drug discovery, the discovered small druglike molecules only represent a small proportion of the chemical space and most regions remain unexplored.(1−3) The number of possible small molecules populating the chemical space has been estimated to exceed 1060; nevertheless, newly identified drug candidates are always close to known ligands in chemical space.(4,5) Some reasons may explain the importance of identifying bioactive compounds with novel chemotypes, including improving potency and drug-likeness, providing standby molecules to cope with potential attrition, and breaking the patent barriers of counterparts.(6,7) To set up the core competitiveness, many pharmaceutical companies are investing heavily in research for diverse bioactive molecules.
    Scaffold hopping, a concept derived from computational chemistry, is a widely used technique for the exploration of new chemotypes.(8) It is the detection of new compounds that share similar activity and molecular properties with known active compounds but possess different core structures. These novel structures can serve as starting points for lead optimization efforts. Many marketed drugs and drug candidates were identified by scaffold hopping methods. For example, the centrally acting analgesic agent, tramadol, was generated from the ring-opening operation of morphine;(9) a novel 5-lipoxygenase inhibitor with an entirely new core structure was found by topological pharmacophore searching.(10) In 2017, Bajorath et al. presented a mini-perspective on the recent advances in scaffold hopping,(11) where they summarized the exemplary computational methods of this field, including pharmacophore searching,(12) shape searching,(13) chemical similarity,(14) structure-based similarity searching (molecular docking)(15) methods, and machine learning (ML) methods.(16) Essentially, these methods follow the “similar property principle,” in which structure similarity is applied to infer biological activity.(17,18) It appears that the essence of these similarity-based methods is contrary to that of scaffold hopping, that is, they have intrinsic limitations in discovering novel structures. Does this mean that these methods are not suitable for scaffold hopping? It should be noted that chemical structures can be characterized by different descriptors with varying degrees of simplification, from simple binary topological fingerprints to complicated multipoint 3D pharmacophore fingerprints. With the decrease in the dependence on basic chemical structures, the abstract nature of these representations has increased significantly. Therefore, pharmacophore-based methods have gained preference due to relatively good scaffold hopping potentials.(12) However, they still more or less depend on structural information, thus limiting their practical capability in searching for new chemical scaffolds. There is therefore an urgent need to develop more straightforward and systematic approaches for identifying structurally novel compounds.
    In the context of scaffold hopping, moving from chemical structures to the bioactivity space seems blocked. Conversely, searching for novel chemotypes by using bioactivity space similarities may be more rational. Here, an important question is raised: how to characterize the bioactivity space of compounds. The expertise accumulated in chemical genomics provides a new idea wherein the activities against a panel of reference targets can be combined to represent the biological position of the ligand in the bioactivity space.(19,20) In practice, the compound activity profiles derived from multiple assay results are used to constitute the bioactivity signatures. Two ligands with similar biological effects on the targets of interest should share similar bioactivity signatures. Such bioactivity representation has been widely applied in many scenarios including mechanism-of-action elucidation,(21−24) virtual screening and potency prediction,(25−27) toxicity prediction,(28−30) etc. As for scaffold hopping, there are some meaningful explorations. Petrone et al. compared 1.5 million in-house compounds by using the biological “high-throughput screening fingerprints” (HTS-FPs) derived from 195 assays(31) and concluded that the HTS-FP was of an enormous value in virtual screening and scaffold hopping in particular. Recently, researchers of AstraZeneca combined the HTS-FP and the ECFP4 fingerprint for scaffold hopping,(32,33) and the results indicated that there was a synergistic action between the two fingerprints with the HTS-FP contributing the most to the overall performance.
    However, traditional assay-based biological representation has two inherent drawbacks which may hinder its application in the drug discovery domain.(20,34,35) One is data paucity. Although many large-scale biochemical and cellar assays are collected and serve as compound representation, a large proportion of molecules have only been tested in a few assays, resulting in a sparse bioactivity matrix. Moreover, due to inadequate sampling of biological space, some additional noise data may be introduced. Another challenge is the limited application range. Only molecules that have been tested in enough assays are qualified to generate their own bioactivity representations, thereby being predicted by the established models. Nevertheless, we require predictions to be made on unknown molecules rather than those that have been tested in multiple assays in most cases.
    A significant advancement that coped with these drawbacks described here was the computational bioactivity fingerprint (CBFP), where the predicted activities in multiple quantitative structure–activity relationship (QSAR) models were integrated to characterize the biological space of a molecule. Of particular note was that both tested and untested small molecules could obtain their own fixed-length biological representations. A retrospective scaffold hopping study was conducted to (1) evaluate the scaffold hopping potential of the CBFP through similarity searching, (2) compare the performance of the CBFP with those of other baseline chemical descriptors, and (3) determine the similarity value distribution of the CBFP to provide more guidance for virtual screening. Finally, the CBFP representation was used in a prospective experiment to find novel inhibitors of poly [ADP-ribose] polymerase 1 (PARP1) to confirm if it could be successfully employed in practical drug design applications.

These comparisons were based on the global scaffold hopping performance without distinguishing the targets. We used scaffold recall rata (SRR) to evaluate the scaffold hopping potential of different representations. Of note, the potential described here means the ability to discover novel chemotypes rather than hopping magnitudes. The results have been presented in Figure 1 and Table 1.

    Focusing on the top 100 molecules, it can be observed from Table 1 that most structural descriptors yielded mediocre performances across the screening datasets, suggesting that these descriptors exhibited a certain ability of scaffold hopping. The CBFP representation outperformed other chemical descriptors for three screening datasets (ChEMBL, Natural Products, and ASINEX), though a slightly less performant than the Gpi_DAPH3 and AtomPair descriptors for the ChemBlock and SPECS datasets, respectively. Improvements in the SRR results of the CBFP over those of the chemical descriptors were statistically significant (95% confidence interval, CI) according to the paired t-test, except for the ECFP4 descriptors. When the top 5000 compounds were taken into consideration, the SRR values for all representations improved remarkably. Particularly, the CBFP representation was superior to other chemical descriptors with its average SRR values above 50% across datasets, suggesting that the query molecules could recall more than 50% scaffolds on average within the top 5000 compounds. Another significant characteristic is the large SD across all the datasets. The significant differences among the query molecules of distinct scaffolds and targets may explain this dispersibility. Regarding the performance degradation on ChemBlock and SPECS, the high similarity between the screening compounds and the query molecules seems to make the higher ranking of some active molecules difficult. Moreover, with the enlargement of the screening data, simply employing the top 100/5000 as the threshold is more and more stringent. For example, the threshold of top 100 means the top 0.11% for the ChEMBL data set but the top 0.047% for the SPECS data set, with the former being more than twice the latter.
    Interestingly, the chemical descriptors, as illustrated in Figure 1, tended to show a similar trend across the screening datasets. The ECFP4 descriptors showed relatively good scaffold hopping potential in the four low-level representations (see the Experimental Section). Its average SRR values exceeded 25 and 50% in most datasets within the top 100 and 5000 compounds, respectively. This was consistent with the conclusions of previous studies that reported ECFP4 as the best chemical structure fingerprint in 2D fingerprints for performing scaffold hopping.(32,36) The least scaffold hopping potential was obtained by FP2. Even in the top 5000 compounds, its average SRR values rarely exceeded 25%. Additionally, it is difficult to overlook the unexpected performance of the MOE2D descriptors, especially within the top 100 compounds. Its median SRR values were usually high followed by those of the CBFP representation in most cases. The MOE2D representation, classified as a high-level representation, comprises 206 descriptors reflecting different properties of molecules, such as physical properties, drug-likeness, topologies, subdivided surface areas, etc. Such multiperspective, complementary, and comprehensive representation is less reliant on chemical details but focuses more on the whole molecule level. Therefore, we believe that MOE2D is a good choice in practical applications of scaffold hopping in the future. Lastly, it seems that pharmacophore fingerprints (median-level representation) have no significant advantage over the atom and structural fingerprints (low-level representation), which is not consistent with the original assumption that less dependence on chemical structures will lead to superior scaffold hopping performance, suggesting a significant gap in our understanding. However, it would be improper to deny their scaffold hopping potentials based on other approaches except simple similarity-based ranking. Anyway, the effectiveness of pharmacophore-based approaches has been proven in practice.
    The active molecule recall rate (AMRR) results were also systematically analyzed and the results are summarized in Table S1 and Figure S1. They are closely related to the SRR values, reflecting the virtual screening performance. Normally, AMRR values are smaller than the corresponding SRR values, as not all the compounds sharing the same scaffold can be recalled within a predefined threshold. For specific molecule representations, the AMRR and SRR results share highly similar trends. Therefore, we will not show more details herein and the AMRR results will not be included in our subsequent discussions.

    2.2. Target-Based Scaffold Hopping Potential Analysis

    There were many differences among the 17 query targets, reflected by the large SDs in all the molecular representations and screening datasets. Some target-specific characteristics may be concealed by the average-value-based statistics, though they could represent the average level of scaffold hopping potential. For this reason, a detailed scaffold hopping potential analysis across the targets is shown in this part.
    To compare the performance of the tested methods, SSR results within the top 100 compounds of the 17 query targets were plotted using the CBFP representation as a function of other chemical descriptors (Figure 2). The points above the diagonal are the specific query targets for which the CBFP representation outperformed the chemical descriptors (higher SRR value). The higher performance of the CBFP in scaffold hopping is evident in these comparisons. We applied the Wilcoxon matched-pairs signed-ranks test to critically evaluate these representations. At 95% CI, the CBFP representation significantly outperformed eight of the nine chemical descriptors. The CBFP representation may have slightly lower performance than that of ECFP4 in some cases, leading to no significant difference. Additionally, a heatmap reflecting the results of individual targets on different screening datasets was plotted to show the landscape of the scaffold hopping potential (Figure 3). To some targets, like DRD2, GRHR, MCR4, and SERT, CBFP representation exhibited strong scaffold hopping potential up to 90%. We also observed that the same molecular representation exhibited different scaffold hopping abilities across targets. Even the CBFP representation could not yield high SRR values for all protein targets. With the results for ChEMBL as an example, the SSR value for the melanocortin 4 receptor (MC4R) reached 86.2% but dropped to 11.1% for HIV-1 reverse transcriptase (HIVRT), showing a significant fluctuation. The results of the TGD and TDT descriptors were highly variable across the query targets; the AtomPair and ECFP4 descriptors yielded a relatively more stable performance. This indicates that it is critical and necessary to make a comprehensive assessment of molecular representations before implementing similarity searching on a given target. The results are not unexpected because, to some extent, such target-specific results run through the whole length of the drug discovery process. Despite the different performance across targets in general, the CBFP is considered a comprehensive and powerful biological representation suitable for scaffold hopping applications.
    Among the 17 query targets, DHFR and HIVRT seemed to be different from the others (Figure 3). The 10 molecular representations could consistently yield satisfactory scaffold hopping performances (blue) on DHFR but poor performances (red) on HIVRT. To understand this phenomenon, we closely scrutinized the active molecules of the two targets. The Tanimoto similarity calculation was implemented between each molecule and the rest of the molecules with distinct scaffolds. The molecule–molecule Tanimoto values are plotted in Figure 4A to visualize the chemical distance of scaffolds/molecules. As plotted here, the molecules toward DHFR had a higher degree of structural similarity compared to those toward HIVRT. Most similarity values for DHFR were distributed within the range of 0.18–0.28 while most intermolecular similarity values for HIVPR ranged from 0.08 to 0.15. At first glance, such similarity differences seem negligible, but they could indeed improve the rankings of active molecules and recall diverse scaffolds within the threshold. Moreover, the nearest neighbor Tanimoto similarity values of the query molecules are compared in Figure 4B. In terms of the median, HIVPR had higher data diversity compared to DHFR: more than 50% of the nearest neighbor similarity values for DHFR were higher than the worst 75% values for HIVPR, implying that the quality of the validation data could also affect scaffold recalling, especially for molecular representations that are heavily dependent on the structural details of molecules. However, under the same validation conditions, the comparison of molecular representations was reliable and meaningful.

    2.3. Guidelines for Virtual Screening: Successful Scaffold Hopping and Similarity Thresholds

    Both integral and separate analyses have confirmed that the CBFP performed well in searching for novel scaffolds. However, these qualitative analyses may not do much to guide real drug screening programs. Therefore, successful scaffold hopping and similarity thresholds will be discussed in this part.
    Generally, if the scaffold recall rate within the top 1% of compounds is greater than or equal to 25%, the trial is considered as a successful scaffold hopping. The average SRR values of the CBFP representations are illustrated in Figure 5. For comparison purposes, ECFP4 and MOE2D, two excellent baseline chemical descriptors, are also included. As shown in Figure 5A, all the three molecular representations could realize successful scaffold hopping for the five screening datasets. Particularly, the SRR results of the CBFP for ChEMBL, Natural Products, and ASINEX reached up to 50%, which was about twice the minimum of a successful scaffold hopping, suggesting that the CBFP had sufficient representation ability to navigate the discovery of novel scaffolds. Additionally, MOE2D could attain SRR values of more than 40% in four of the five screening datasets. Furthermore, the threshold was tightened to the top 0.5% for more rigorously evaluation (Figure 5B). As expected, the SRR values of the three representations decreased at different degrees, although their rankings remained unchanged. The average decrease of ECFP4 and MOE2D reached 12.5 and 10.2%, respectively, while the average decline for CBFP representation was 8.9%, implying that most recalled scaffolds of the top 1% compounds are mainly located in the top 0.5% region and that CBFP representation could improve the rankings of molecules with novel active chemotypes.
    We also calculated the similarity threshold for the CBFP representation. When 25% scaffolds were recalled, the mean Tanimoto similarity value was about 0.70. Meanwhile, under the same conditions, the similarity value for ECFP4 was about 0.38. Additionally, the similarity values of molecules near the top 0.5 and 1% were about 0.63 and 0.58, respectively. These data could be employed as a reference for virtual screening.

    2.4. Prospective Validation

    In addition to the retrospective comparison and validation of the 17 benchmarks and 5 screening datasets, it is essential to test the performance of the novel bioactivity representation in practice. Therefore, a prospective experiment aiming at searching for novel PARP1 inhibitors was designed and implemented. As a hot target in cancer therapy, PARP1 is tremendously important for a vast variety of cellular functions, such as DNA damage repair, gene transcription, and cell apoptosis.(37) Until now, four PARP1 inhibitors (olaparib, niraparib, rucaparib, and talazoparib, see Table S2) have been approved by FDA, mainly for the treatment of BRCA-mutated advanced ovarian and breast cancer, with their applications in other cancers in clinical evaluation.(37,38) From the perspective of chemotype, most inhibitors fall into three categories: phthalazinone derivatives, benzimidazole formamide derivatives, and tricyclic lactam indole derivatives. Considering the clinical importance and limited chemical space of anticancer drugs, searching for novel chemotypes may provide alternative starting points for lead optimization and improve adverse reactions commonly found in marketed drugs.
    Olaparib, the first approved PARP1 inhibitor launched by AstraZeneca, was chosen as the query molecular in the similarity searching with CBFP representation. A library containing 0.5 M commercially available synthetic small molecules downloaded from ChemBridge was used as the screening data set. Due to the computational complexity and storage space for these compounds, a two-step filter operation was carried out before similarity searching to retain only promising compounds that conformed to historic expert experience. First, we curated the whole screening data set and removed those compounds violating Lipinski’s rule of five. Then, the washed screening data set was docked to the prepared protein 4L6S using the standard precision (SP) mode of Glide.(39,40) The compounds were ranked according to their Glide docking scores. We retained a total of 12,763 molecules with docking scores lower than −8 and calculated their 831-bit CBFP (the PARP-1 protein was removed from the CBFP). The Tanimoto similarities between the query molecule and the screened molecules were calculated and used as the ranking criteria. A similarity value of 1.0 indicates that the molecule has an identical position in the bioactivity space as the query molecule, whereas a value of 0 means it is far from the query molecule in the bioactivity space. The 50 top-ranked compounds spanning 46 distinct molecular scaffolds were collected. Considering the potential redundancy of chemotypes, for the two compounds sharing the same Murcko scaffold, only the higher-ranked one was maintained to represent its scaffold. The same operation was carried out on analogues. All the compounds successfully passed the PAINS (pan assay interference compounds) prediction. Ultimately, 35 compounds (see the Supporting Information) were retained and purchased from the commercial supplier (ChemBridge) with a minimum of 95% purity as determined by HPLC.
    Guided by the diverse chemotypes and the high biological similarities with olaparib, these compounds were evaluated in cellular assays using MDA-MB-436 cancer cell lines (BRCA1-deficient breast cancer cells) and the antiproliferation activities of these hit compounds were demonstrated as shown in Figure 6A. Surprisingly, most compounds selected by the CBFP-based similarity searching exhibited antiproliferative activity against BRCA1-deficient cells: 25 of the 35 compounds attained statistical difference compared to dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) alone (P < 0.05, one-tailed t-test). Among them, eight compounds showed more than 50% inhibition, suggesting that their IC50 were lower than 10 μM. These potent compounds had diverse chemical scaffolds although they shared some fragments with approved drugs. Additionally, olaparib displayed strong antiproliferative activity with 84% inhibition, which is consistent with previous reports. Unexpectedly, hit compounds 6, 9, and 24 showed more potent antiproliferative activity than olaparib, and their dose–response curves are shown in Figure 6B. Compound 6 had an IC50 of 161.2 nM, far lower than those of compounds 24 and 9. For this promising growth-inhibition activity, it was submitted to the PARP1 inhibitory study using chemiluminescence assay to confirm our assumption that its antiproliferative activity was indeed caused by its PARP1 inhibitory capability. Compound 6 displayed excellent PARP1 inhibitory activity with an IC50 value of 0.263 nM, while that of olaparib was 1.719 nM (the value provided by the kit manufacturer was 1.8 nM). It is worth mentioning that the novel hit compound 6 possessed a distinct scaffold, which never appeared in the PARP-1 binders of ChEMBL27. The reverse docking simulation in the PARP1 active site showed that the quinazolin-4(3H)-one fragment formed two hydrogen bonds with Ser904 and Gly863, π–π stacking interactions with Tyr907, and the single benzene could form π–H stacking interactions with Asp766, reconfirming the favorable binding of compound 6 to PARP-1. Additionally, reverse target fishing was carried out using four different web services: Similarity Ensemble Approach (SEA),(41) SwissTargetPrediction,(42) TargetNet,(43) and PharmMapper.(44) The SMILES of hit compound 6 was submitted to these servers to search for the potential targets based on the similar property principle (Table S3). Among the three methods, SwissTargetPrediction and TargetNet could not find PARP1 in their top 10 predictive targets, and SEA could only predict PARP-1 as the tenth likely target. However, when olaparib was used as the query molecule, these methods could easily find PARP1 as its potential target. Regretfully, the 3D pharmacophore similarity-based PharmMapper could not find PARP-1 for both compound 6 and olaparib (Table S4). These results demonstrated that traditional similarity methods could not identify our hit compound 6 as a potential PARP1 inhibitor. In conclusion, utilizing bioactivity space similarity leads to the discovery of compound 6, demonstrating that it is completely feasible for use in finding novel active scaffolds by CBFP-based similarity searching.

    3. Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, we introduced the CBFP, an informative, high-dimensional, resource-conserving, and universal molecular representation that incorporates the predictive bioactivities of multiple QSAR models to characterize the bioactivity space of compounds for the discovery of novel scaffolds.
    The scaffold hopping abilities of CBFP representation and nine other chemical descriptors have been systematically benchmarked in 17 different query datasets paired with five screening datasets by ranking large databases in a decreasing order of Tanimoto similarity values with the query molecule. The CBFP representation could highly discriminate structural descriptors in terms of its efficacy for improving the rankings of molecules with novel scaffolds. In the prospective experiment, the identified eight hit compounds exhibit novel active scaffolds, and the most potent hit compound 6 could show more efficacious inhibitory activity against PARP-1 and breast cancer cells with respect to olaparib, suggesting it has the potential to show activity in subsequent in vivo and cellular mechanistic studies. Both compound 6 and the other two hits (Compounds 9 and 24) with good cellular inhibition could be regarded as starting points for medicinal chemistry optimization. These results support the use of the CBFP for discovering novel active scaffolds and broadening the chemical space of current drugs. It is worth reemphasizing that these hit compounds exhibit different chemical scaffolds from current active compounds, and very few of these compounds could be found in the final candidate lists of other chemical descriptors.
    Despite the superiority in generality and financial expenditure, there are two important concepts that require consideration in employing the predictions of QSAR models to characterize the bioactivity space of compounds. One is the reliability of models. In our CBFP construction, the accuracy of all the 832 × 9 classification models was in the range of 70.0–99.8%, providing adequate separation of high-active and low-active compounds. Even so, we understand that the reliability of predictive results of cheminformatics models is not equal to that of biochemical assays. However, it is demanding to require it to possess good generality and reliability simultaneously, as an increase in one quality would imply a decrease in the other. We can minimize the distance between predictive activity and real interactions by optimizing these classification models to the best of our ability. Another concept is the weak correlation with the chemical structure. CBFP representation is defined as a kind of bioactivity space characterization and the dependency on chemical structures is kept as low as possible. However, the generation of CBFP representation is essentially based on QSAR models and chemical descriptors (MOE2D, CATS, and MACCS), thereby having certain distant connections with structural information of compounds. In any case, the superiority of the CBFP relative to other pure chemical descriptors in scaffold hopping has been confirmed by our retrospective and prospective validations.
    Finally, it is necessary to stress the importance of prospective validation. Up to now, many strategies of ligand- or structure-based virtual screening have reported impressive performance in retrospective validation without proving useful beyond this. Once applied in practice, their results are usually not up to expectation. To determine the transferability of the performance in real practice and rule out inappropriate models, it is recommended that proper prospective validation be implemented alongside retrospective validation.(45,46)

    4.1. Definition of Molecular Scaffolds

    The term “molecular scaffold” is designed to depict the core structure of a molecule. In this study, the Bemis and Murcko scaffold(47) was used for scaffold hopping analysis. It is defined as pure ring systems and ring systems connected by linkers. The side-chains are usually excluded in scaffold calculation and chain compounds usually do not have Murcko scaffolds. From the perspective of the study endpoint, the Murcko scaffold is qualified to cover all types of scaffold hopping, including heterocycle replacement, ring opening and closure, pseudopeptides and peptidomimetics, and topological/shape-based scaffold hopping.(20) In this study, the Murcko scaffold calculation was implemented in the KNIME platform (version 4.1).(48,49)

    4.2. Data Set and Preparation

    The query data set was downloaded from the article reported by Vogt et al.(50) The whole data set consists of 1675 unique small molecules for 17 different targets collected from the ChEMBL database. A target subset contains a minimum of 10 distinct scaffolds and each of which has 5 corresponding molecules. All the active compounds satisfy three rules: (1) the potency (Ki or IC50 value) should be at least 1 μM; (2) the number of the total nonhydrogen atoms is not more than 50; and (3) the number of the nonhydrogen atoms of the side-chains should be less than that of the scaffold. We later removed molecules with unidentified SMILES, leading to a total of 1660 molecules belonging to 332 scaffolds. The information of the query data set is listed in Table S5.
    To better simulate a real scenario, five molecule datasets with different sizes and sources were collected as the screening datasets, including ChEMBL (85,644, from ChEMBL), Natural Products (120,686, from ZINC), ChemBlock (198,643, from Chemical Block), SPECS (212,863, from SPECS) and ASINEX (567,106, from ASINEX). All the screening molecules were washed to ensure that the screening datasets were truly nonredundant from the query data set. As a bias control, all the screened molecules follow the same ranges of heavy atoms of the query molecules.

    4.3. CBFP Construction

    The CBFP of a compound is designed as a binary encoding of the predicted bioactivities toward 832 human proteins. The workflow of the construction of the CBFP is illustrated in Figure S3 and elaborated below.

    4.3.1. Data Collection and Preparation

    The drug giant AstraZeneca has ever analyzed the in vitro bioactivity data extracted from the literature and patents, where 1736 human proteins were considered to be candidate drug targets.(51) In this study, these proteins were selected as the candidate targets for CBFP construction. Two publicly available databases, ChEMBL(52,53) and BindingDB,(54) were selected as the sources of small molecules for model training. Taking the names of proteins as the keywords, we collected both biochemical and cellular assay data of each target, which was the usual practice of QSAR modeling. Then, a series of preparations were applied: (1) the compounds without concrete bioassay annotations (IC50, EC50, Ki values) or structures were deleted; (2) duplicated molecules were identified and filtered off based on the molecular InChIkey format; (3) if a molecule was reported with two or more bioactivity entries, the arithmetic mean of these values was employed provided that the fluctuation was less than 0.3, otherwise discarded; and (4) target datasets with less than 100 compounds were removed from the candidate protein list due to limited chemical space. Finally, 0.81 M molecules spanning 832 protein targets were collected, with an average of 980 compounds per target data set. The total data set contained 0.54 M distinct compounds and 0.48 M Murcko scaffolds, indicating a diverse chemical space. The detailed information on these QSAR datasets has been provided in the Supporting Information.

    4.3.2. Model Training and Evaluation

    In this study, three different types of descriptors, CATS, MACCS, and MOE2D, were used to describe these compounds. The fingerprint calculation was implemented in Molecular Operating Environment software (MOE, version 2018.01).(55) Three popular ML algorithms, including random forest (RF),(56) Adaptive Boosting (AdaBoost),(57) and gradient boosting machine (GBM),(58) were used to construct the QSAR classification models with R packages, randomForest,(59) adabag,(60) and gbm.(61)
    For each target, three molecular representations and three ML algorithms were combined, thereby producing nine classification models. To ensure the balance between positive and negative samples and avoid potential data bias, for each subset, we employed the median bioactivity value as the cutoff point, dividing the compounds into two equal parts. The high bioactivity part was considered as the positive set (label = 1) and the low bioactivity part as the negative set (label = 0). Then the data set was split into training set:test set = 8:2. Hyperparameter tuning was performed via the grid search method to optimize the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) value. The modeling process was repeated 10 times and the final prediction of each compound was the arithmetic mean of the 10 predictions. Finally, a total of 832 × 9 SAR models were established and the accuracy values on the test sets were mainly in the range of 75.9–98.5% (see the Supporting Information). The R code implementing the CBFP and the default model.zip file has been deposited in GitHub (https://github.com/xiongguoli/Computational-Bioactivity-Fingreprint).

    4.3.3. CBFP Calculation

    Molecule descriptors (CATS, MACCS, and MOE2D) were generated for the whole query data set and five screening datasets using MOE. Then, these molecules were predicted by the 832 × 9 SAR models one by one. To achieve more accurate predictions, we employed the consensus score to represent the potential activity against the protein target by averaging the predicted values from nine individual models. The consensus score of all the proteins were assembled as an 832-bit vector and each bit is in a scale of 0∼1. Then, this feature vector was translated into a standard binary fingerprint by using a cutoff, where 1 means that this molecule has a strong possibility to be high active to this protein and 0 means low active. In this study, we employed 0.5 as the appropriate cutoff, which was derived from the comparison of many thresholds (Figure S2). This 832-bit fingerprint was named computational bioactivity fingerprint (CBFP) for the bioactivity characterization of small molecules.

    4.4. Baseline Descriptors

    The scaffold hopping potential of the CBFP representation was benchmarked against nine chemical descriptors, which were divided into three groups by focusing on their degrees of structure simplification. Basic structure recordings, including atoms, atom connections, and structural keys, were classified as low-level representations (AtomPairs, MACCS, FP2, and ECFP4). More extensive recordings, including pharmacophore, pharmacophore combinations, and distances, were classified as median-level representation (Gpi-DAPH3, TGD, TGT, and CATS). Finally, comprehensive molecular property descriptors, such as molecular weight and logP, are classified as high-level representations (MOE2D). These molecular descriptors were calculated by the RDKit package (version 2019.03.1, windows platform) and MOE (version 2018.1).

    4.5. Similarity Searching

    We used traditional similarity searching method to investigate the scaffold hopping ability of the CBFP and the other baseline chemical descriptors. As previously described, there were five different molecules of each scaffold. The five compounds were employed as the query molecules in turn and the remaining compounds of the other scaffolds within the same target were added to the screening data set. Therefore, a total of 1660 searching trials were carried out and analyzed. The similarities between a query molecule and the screened molecules were measured by the Tanimoto similarity coefficients paired with different molecular representations.
    Here, two statistical parameters, SRR and AMRR,(50) were utilized to evaluate the scaffold hopping potential and virtual screening performance, respectively. The SRR and AMRR values are defined as the ratio between the distinct scaffolds/active compounds retrieved within the threshold and the total active scaffolds/active compounds added to the screening data set. The thresholds were set to the top 100 and 5000 compounds for all the screening datasets. It should be clarified that the potential of scaffold hopping described in this work does not refer to the magnitude of hopping but the ability to discover novel chemotypes. The similarity searching and evaluation workflow were implemented in the KNIME platform (version 4.1).

    4.6. In Vitro Antiproliferation Assay and PARP-1 Inhibition Assays

    The human-cancer-cell line MDA-MB-436 was maintained in Leibovitz’s L-15 medium containing 10 μg/mL insulin, 16 μg/mL glutathione, and 10% (v/v) fetal bovine serum at 37 °C in 100% air incubator. Cell proliferation assay was determined by Cell Counting-Kit 8 (CCK8) assay. MDA-MB-436 cells were passaged the day before dosing into a 96-well plate, allowed to grow for 12 h, and treated with different concentrations for 11 days. After incubation, 10 μL of CCK8 was added to each well, which was incubated in an incubator for 3 h, and the absorbance at 450 nM was measured with a microplate reader. The inhibition rate (%) = [1–(OD drug – OD blank)]/(OD DMSO – OD blank) × 100%. The IC50 values were calculated using nonlinear regression with a normalized dose–response fit using the Prism GraphPad software.
    PARP1 inhibition assays were carried out according to chemiluminescence procedures. The PARP1 enzymatic activity of compounds in a cell-free system was determined by PARP1 chemiluminescence activity assay in 384-well plates. Each well was coated with histone (25 μL) diluted in 125 μL of PBS buffer and incubated at 4 °C overnight. After incubation, the plate was washed three times using 100 μL PBST buffer per well and blocked the wells by adding 100 μL of blocking buffer at room temperature for 60–90 min. The next step is the ribosylation reaction. The master mixture including 10× PARP buffer (1.25 μL), 10× PARP assay mixture (1.25 μL), activated DNA (2.5 μL), and distilled water (7.5 μL) should be added to every well followed by adding 2.5 μL of the compound at varying concentrations or the inhibitor buffer (no inhibitor). The reaction was initiated by adding 10 μL of 2 ng/μL PARP1 enzyme to the wells positive control and test inhibitor, and 10 μL of PARP buffer was added to the blank wells. The last step is detecting chemiluminescence. Diluted streptavidin-HRP (25 μL) was added to each well and incubated for 30 min at room temperature. Just before use, 25 μL HRP chemiluminescent substrate A and 25 μL HRP chemiluminescent substrate B were mixed on ice and added to each well. Immediately, the plate should be read in a luminometer or microtiter-plate reader capable of reading chemiluminescence. The inhibition rate of PARP1 enzymatic activity was calculated as (inhibitor – control)/(positive – control) × 100%. The concentration required for 50% inhibition of PARP1 enzymatic activity (IC50) was calculated using nonlinear regression with a normalized dose–response fit using Prism GraphPad software.
