    Abstract

    3,17β-Bis-sulfamoyloxy-2-methoxyestra-1,3,5(10)-triene (STX140), a bis-sulfamate derivative of the endogenous steroid 2-methoxyestradiol, has shown promising anticancer potency both in vitro and in vivo, with excellent bioavailability. Its activity against taxane-resistant xenografts makes it a potential drug candidate against triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC). These properties are linked to the ability of STX140 to act in a multitargeting fashion in vivo as a microtubule disruptor, leading to cell cycle arrest and with both proapoptotic and anti-angiogenic activities. Carbonic anhydrase IX (CA IX) is a well-established biomarker for aggressive cancers, including TNBC. This study reports, for the first time, the inhibitory activities of a series of steroidal and nonsteroidal sulfamate derivatives against CA IX in comparison to the ubiquitous CA II, with some compounds demonstrating 100–200-fold selectivity for CA IX over CA II. X-ray crystallographic studies of four of the most promising compounds reveal that isoform-specific residue interactions are responsible for the high specificity.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Despite advances in cancer diagnosis and treatment, breast cancer remains a serious health issue for women. Nearly 12% of women develop breast cancer during their lifetime, with over 40 000 fatalities each year in the United States (breastcancer.org). Although therapies for some selective breast cancers have been successful, patients with triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) have no currently available targeted therapies. This is because TNBC lacks the targeted receptors for estrogen, progesterone, and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2).(1,2) TNBC constitutes 15% of breast cancers diagnosed and is associated with a poor prognosis and higher relapse rates compared to luminal and HER2 breast cancers.(2) Hence, there is a need for new targets for TNBC therapies.
    Carbonic anhydrases (CAs) are zinc-containing metalloenzymes that catalyze the reversible hydration of carbon dioxide and water to bicarbonate and a proton.(3) There are 15 CA isoforms in humans which are involved in numerous physiological processes such as pH regulation, respiration, and blood homeostasis.(4) Recently, CA IX has been identified as a potential therapeutic target for TNBC.(2,5,6) Usually, the CA IX expression is limited to the GI tract to maintain an acidic pH. However, under hypoxic tumor conditions, CA IX is significantly overexpressed. Tumor hypoxia is caused by the lack of O2 supply because of the inability of the local vasculature to develop with the growing tumor.(7) Without oxygen present, the tumor cell shifts from the mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation pathway to utilizing anaerobic glycolysis, a process termed the Warburg effect.(7,8) This results in the production and export of lactic acid into the tumor’s extracellular environment. To compensate for this decrease in extracellular pH, cancer cells activate hypoxia-inducible factor 1 (HIF-1α) that induces its intracellular signaling pathway and causes the upregulation of numerous genes, including CA IX.(9)
    CA IX is a transmembrane multidomain enzyme consisting of a small intracellular domain, a helix transmembrane domain, a highly conserved CA catalytic domain, and an extracellular proteoglycan (PG) domain.(10) Under physiological conditions, CA IX exists as a homodimer, although its monomeric truncated catalytic domain is a functional CA. The active site comprises a 15 Å cavity, where zinc is coordinated by three histidines and a zinc-bound solvent (ZBS).(4,10) The active site cavity is divided into two distinct sides, a hydrophobic and a hydrophilic side. The hydrophobic side is lined by residues: 91L, 121V, 131F, 135V, 141L, 143V, 198L, 202P, 204A, 207V, and 209W (CA II numbering/sequence). These residues are important for the movement of CO2 in and out of the active site, whereas the hydrophilic side is lined by residues: 62N, 64H, 65S, 67Q, 69T, 92Q, 199T, and 200T (CA II numbering/sequence). These residues are responsible for stabilizing an ordered water network, involved in the movement of protons in and out of the active site.(3,10)
    The PG and intracellular tail domains make crystallization of the full-length CA IX difficult because of the large number of exposed hydrophobic residues. Therefore, a soluble CAIX mimic (CAIXmimic) has been developed for structural studies.(10,11) This CA IXmimic consists only of the catalytic domain, constructed from a CA II scaffold (a CA isoform that has been shown to be easily crystallized) where the active site residues have been replaced to those of CA IX. The amino acid substitutions are: Ala65Ser, Asn67Gln, Glu69Thr, Ile91Leu, Phe131Val, Lys170Glu, and Leu204Ala. These residues, although different between the CA II and IX isoforms, retain similar charge or hydrophobicity. One significant difference between the two isoforms is the more solvent “open” active site of CA IX because of the Phe131Val difference with CA II.(4,12) The CA IXmimic construct was used for all structural studies.
    Steroid sulfatase (STS) is an emerging therapeutic target for the endocrine therapy of hormone-dependent diseases and catalyzes hydrolysis of estrogen sulfate to estrogen.(13) Development of the aryl O-sulfamate pharmacophore as a key structural motif in drug discovery for STS has led to steroidal and nonsteroidal drugs entering many clinical trials, for example, STX64/Irosustat, with promising results in oncology and women’s health.(14,15) This “STX” sulfamoylated series of drugs, some of which are employed here, were designed as STS inhibitors and multitargeting agents and, in many cases, were derived initially from a classic four-ring steroid template; later, these were superseded for oncology by very potent and nonestrogenic nonsteroidal derivatives such as Irosustat, which has to date been evaluated clinically in hormone-dependent breast cancer, endometrial cancer, and prostate cancer. A steroid sulfamate is also currently in clinical trials in endometriosis.(14,15) Other nonsteroidal STS inhibitors are also currently being developed.(16) Another steroidal bis-sulfamate, the nonestrogenic STX140 (3,17β-bis-sulfamoyloxy-2-methoxyestra-1,3,5(10)-triene), has also shown great promise as a potential cancer therapy in the hormone-independent setting.(14,15,17,18) It competes inter alia at the colchicine binding site of tubulin, leading to microtubule depolymerization and thus cell cycle arrest and apoptosis.(19−21) It has also been proven to inhibit HIF-1α accumulation in the nucleus, causing down-regulation of CA IX mRNA expression.(19) STX140 outperformed the chemotherapy drug Paclitaxel, which also disrupts microtubule growth, reducing tumor size, and preventing metastasis in mouse models better than Paclitaxel. In addition, it was shown to have only mild side effects, when compared to clinically used antimitotic agents(22) and is active in xenografts derived from patients who failed on docetaxel therapy, showing promise for metastatic TNBC.(23) Because of the anti-angiogenic properties of STX140, it was hypothesized that these inhibitors may have a “double-hit” therapeutic effect, targeting both microtubule polymerization and inhibiting CA IX.(19)
    Some later nonsteroidal sulfamate derivatives were also developed using pharmacophore translation and good in vitro and in vivo activity was observed on cancer cell lines.(24−31) Recently, one member of a class of quinazolinone-based nonsteroidal agents with excellent activities was studied crystallographically in atomic detail in complex at the colchicine binding site of tubulin, the first example for such a sulfamate ester.(32) Steroid sulfamates and derivatives have been evaluated extensively against CA II and modeled to dock into the CA IX active site.(33,34) Actual complexes, however, have yet to be elucidated for CA IX, although some crystallographic studies have been performed on CA II with Irosustat and mainly steroidal agents and on CA II with nonsteroidal dual aromatase-sulfatase inhibitors.(35−37)
    Here, we report kinetic and docking studies for a series of compounds (Figure 1) against both CA II and CA IX, with multiple compounds exhibiting CA IX specificity. CA II is ubiquitously expressed throughout the body and is therefore used as an off-target comparison for determining CA IX specificity. Four inhibitors were then further studied for structure activity relationships (SARs) through X-ray crystallography and isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC). STX140 and three other derivatives were complexed with CA IX to determine the important sites of interaction that might promote specificity. Of these compounds, the parent STX140 is an estradiol bis-sulfamate with a four-membered ring system and an ortho-methoxy group adjacent to the phenyl 3-O-sulfamate. Two nonsteroidal inhibitors, STX2484 and STX2845, were subsequently developed to mimic STX140 without the rigid steroidal ring system. Instead, these compounds have a two-ring steroidal AB mimic motif connected to the phenolic sulfamate group, with a third ring attached via a methylene linker, thus allowing more rotational freedom. The related steroidal STX49 was also synthesized as a simple estradiol bis-sulfamate, mimicking STX140 but without the ortho-methoxy group.

    2.1. Kinetic Studies

    Stopped flow kinetic analysis of each STX ligand showed efficient inhibition of both CA IX and the off-target CA II at μM concentrations. Of the steroidal subset of STX molecules, STX738 exhibited the highest CA IX Ki value of 1.18 μM, whereas the others all showed Kis below 750 nM with STX641 being the next worse. This is most likely due to the lack of the aryl sulfamate motif. STX140 and STX243 are both bis-sulfamates, with a sulfamate group in the 3- and 17β position. STX641 lacks the cyclopentane ring sulfamate, which is replaced by a mimicking cyanomethyl group, but it still retains a 3-position aryl sulfamate. A bis-sulfamate scaffold seems to be the best candidate for CA IX inhibition among the steroidal class of molecules. It is also likely, perhaps not too surprisingly, that the ligands preferentially bind through the aryl sulfamate, as the Kis increase in its absence. The nonsteroidal subset of molecules also showed efficient inhibition of both CA isoforms with CA IX Kis ranging from 1.02 μM to 2 nM. By comparing the structures of the compounds with the range of Kis, it becomes apparent that the addition of more polar groups to the tail portion of the molecules results in higher Kis. STX2182 and STX3469 both introduce more polar groups to the flexible single ring. Likely, this pendant ring region of the molecule is interacting with the hydrophobic face of CA II and CA IX; thus, adding polar groups disfavors binding. Unsurprisingly, the nonsulfamate subset of ligands exhibited the least efficient inhibition of both CA isoforms. Traditional CA inhibitors utilize a sulfonamide or sulfamate group to bind to the catalytic zinc and block the active site. Without these high-affinity binding groups, inhibitor Kis greatly increase. Overall, STX140, STX2484, and STX2845 exhibited the highest inhibitory efficiency, with Ki values of 70, 80, and 2 nM, respectively (Table 1). Kinetic data in full can be seen in the Supporting Information (Figure S1).

    Although Kis are important when designing a CA IX-selective inhibitor, the most crucial kinetic constant is the selectivity ratio. This ratio is an inhibitor’s CA II Ki divided by its CA IX Ki. Although all inhibitors exhibited efficient Kis, only a few could be classified as CA IX-specific (Table 1). The steroidal subset of STX inhibitors all reported low selectivity ratios under 10 with STX243 being the most CA IX specific. The nonsulfamate class also showed poor selectivity, with ratios of 7 and 3 for STX2839 and STX2480, respectively. The nonsteroidal sulfamates, however, demonstrated impressive CA IX specificity, with STX2845 exhibiting a ratio of 202. By comparing such ratios to structure for each molecule, important motifs for selectivity are elucidated. The three ligands with the highest CA IX selectivity are STX2484, STX2848, and STX2845, with ratios of 104, 155, and 202, respectively (Table 1). These three molecules contain a trimethoxyphenyl group in the flexible tail portion. The CA II active site is crowded compared to the CA IX active site because of the F131 in CA II versus a V131 in CA IX. Likely, this trimethoxyphenyl motif clashes with the F131 in CA II, resulting in unfavorable steric interactions. In CA IX, however, the V131 provides more room for binding, resulting in stabilizing interactions with the group. Another important motif is the methoxy group located ortho to the zinc binding sulfamate. All of the nonsteroidal molecules have this ortho-methoxy motif, except for STX2845. STX2845 exhibited the lowest Ki and the best selectivity ratio of the inhibitors. This suggests that an ortho-methoxy motif might result in destabilizing interactions within the active site. It is likely that the methoxy group makes the inhibitor too bulky to bind well, causing more solvent displacement and thus decreasing inhibitory efficiency.
    Steroidal bis-sulfamate STX49, without the aryl 2-methoxy group of STX140, was subsequently synthesized to probe the effects of the ortho-methoxy group. Because STX2845 showed the most promising kinetic data, the ortho-methoxy group in the parent compound STX140 was deleted to generate a related ligand that potentially might outperform STX2845.

    2.2. In Silico Modeling

    All STX inhibitors except STX49 were docked into CA II and CA IX. Each inhibitor bound, as expected, with the sulfamate group interacting with the catalytic zinc (Figure 2A). Grid scores were used as a measurement of how well each molecule fits into the active site and as a prediction of the ΔG of binding, more negative numbers indicating better binding. Grid scores for each compound ranged from −90 to 0 for CA II, with STX2480 predicted to be the best binder, and STX140, STX2182, and STX2484 predicted not to bind to CA II at all. Grid scores for CA IX ranged from −100 with STX3209 to −45 with STX2480 (Figure 2). All compounds were shown to bind with moderate affinity to CA IX.
    To predict which compounds would be ideal for SAR studies, the docking grid scores and log Kis were plotted against each other, to generate an ideal inhibitor graph (Figure 2B). The ideal ligand would exhibit a negative ln(Ki) (indicative of a low Ki) and highly negative grid score (indicative of a high affinity inhibitor). Therefore, this plot should show the best inhibitors under the x-axis and to the left of the y-axis. The in silico docking scores predicted the nonsulfamate STX2480 to be the best CA II binder. This of course was proven false by the kinetic data, with the nonsulfamate class of inhibitors reporting the highest Kis. Although a powerful tool, docking software is still limited by solvation energies, metal interactions, and other unaccounted variables of binding. Docking was used in conjugation with Kis to get a prediction as to which inhibitors would be the most specific to CA IX. As shown in Figure 2B, the nonsteroidal sulfamate class of inhibitor was shown to be very specific to CA IX and bind with high affinity. The plot clearly shows a trend of nonsteroidal molecules being the best binders in CA IX and worst in CA II.

    2.3. CA IX Inhibitor Complexes

    After preliminary kinetics and docking data, four of the inhibitors were further studied using X-ray crystallography. STX140, STX2484, and STX2845 were chosen because of their low Kis, high selectivity ratios, and grid scores calculated from the in-silico docking (Figures 2 and 3). STX49 was also chosen because of the promising absence of the ortho-methoxy group, mimicking that in STX2845. The CA IX inhibitor complexes formed monoclinic P21 crystals that diffracted X-rays to maximal resolutions from 1.9 to 1.6 Å (Table 2). Difference electron density maps clearly showed that the ligands had bound in the active site (Figure S2A). As expected, all four inhibitors were tethered via their sulfamate group to the zinc, displacing the ZBS (Figure 4). The final refinement statistics for the structures determined were typical for their resolution (Table 2). Of note, previously reported CA IXmimic crystal structures have achieved higher ordered diffraction, better than 1.3 Å resolution. The lower resolutions for these studies are most likely a consequence of the high concentrations of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) used during co-crystallization, the DMSO affecting both the protein stability and crystal formation, resulting in higher mosaicity. Despite this, the structures are all within acceptable statistical ranges (Table 2). Occupancies for all inhibitors were determined to be 1.00 indicating binding in every unit cell. For CA-inhibitor complexes, β-factor, or thermal motion within the crystal lattice, was observed to be within acceptable values. β-factors for side chains ranged from 22 to 28 Å2, whereas the inhibitors ranged from 24 to 53 Å2. β-factors for ligands are similar to side-chain values, indicating tight inhibitor binding.

    Current X-ray crystallography usually takes advantage of cryo-cooling to protect crystals from radiation damage. As data are collected, the X-rays can lead to the formation of free radicals, which in turn causes decay in the lattice causing poor diffraction and therefore reduced quality. Synchrotrons and in-house X-ray sources take advantage of liquid nitrogen cooling to prevent heating within the lattice thus leading to higher quality diffraction over time. Cryo-cooling was initially attempted with the CA IX-STX complexes; however, crystals became brittle and cracked upon freezing resulting in poor data. Utilizing room-temperature diffraction with a low-energy in-house X-ray source, cocrystals of CA IX and each inhibitor showed high-resolution diffraction and high-quality statistics. Although the possibility of radiation damage is greater with room-temperature data collection, it is still a viable technique when cryo-cooling is not an option. Low β-factor values for main and side-chain atoms indicate high-quality data throughout collection and thus minimal radiation damage.

    2.4. STX49

    For STX49 (PDB: 6e8P, Figure 4A), the observed omit map electron density was weaker than that for the nonsteroidal inhibitors STX2845 and STX2484, possibly indicating lower binding occupancy and/or weaker binding (Figure S2A, map contoured at 0.7σ). This observation was reinforced further by the fact that only 66% of the inhibitor made contact with active site residues, meaning that 34% was solvent exposed (Table 3). The sulfamate group was stabilized via two hydrogen bonds with Thr199 and Thr200 (distances of 2.8 Å), whereas the fused rings exhibited van der Waals interactions with Val121, Val131, Leu198, and Pro202 (Figure 5A). On the basis of the crystal structure interactions, the estimated ΔG for the complex formation was determined to be −16.3 kcal/mol (Table 3).

    2.5. STX140

    STX140 (PDB: 6E8X, Figure 4B) is the parent compound of the series and inhibits microtubule polymerization. STX140 is chemically similar to STX49, with just the addition of an ortho-methoxy group on the phenyl sulfamate. This group is critical in the parent endogenous steroid 2-methoxyestradiol to abolish estrogenicity. The observed omit map electron density for STX140 was the least defined of the four inhibitors studied (Figure S2B, map contoured at 0.7σ). This is most likely due to the methoxy group causing steric hindrance within the active site, causing energetically unfavorable conformational geometry on STX140 and an unfavorable positioning (Figure 4B). However, with the addition of the methoxy group to STX49, an additional hydrogen bond was viable.
    STX140 had the same Thr199 and Thr200 hydrogen bonds as STX49 (distances of 2.7 Å) through the sulfamate group, an additional hydrogen bond created by the added methoxy moiety (distance of 2.8 Å), and it also exhibited extensive van der Waals interactions with Gln92, Val121, Val131, Val135, Leu141, Val143, and Leu198 (Figure 4B). The addition of the extra hydrogen bond would be estimated to stabilize the complex by ∼−1 kcal/mol. This matches the measured ΔG upon complex formation of STX140 to CA IX to be −17.2 kcal/mol, which is −0.9 kcal/mol better than STX49 (Table 2). This observation validates that the additional hydrogen bond and therefore the ortho-methoxy group improve binding affinity for the steroidal inhibitors.

    2.6. STX2484

    STX2484 (PDB: 6E91, Figure 4C) is a nonsteroidal derivative of STX140 with a rotatable carbon linker between the two ring systems. However, it retains an ortho-methoxy group, similar to STX140. The methylene linker resulted in more rotational freedom of the inhibitor, allowing it to sample multiple rotational configurations during binding to minimize its Gibbs free energy (Table 3 and Figure 5A). STX2484 exhibits a 90° twist compared to the steroidal STX49 and STX140 and the nonsteroidal derivative STX2845 (Figure 5A,B). As seen with STX140, it forms hydrogen bonds with Thr199 and Thr200 (distances of 2.9 and 2.7 Å, respectively) and a somewhat weaker hydrogen bound with the methoxy moiety (distance of 3.3 Å). STX2484 also exhibited van der Waals interactions with His64, Gln92, Val121, Val131, Gly132, Val135, Leu198, and Pro202 (Figure 4C). The observed omit map electron density for STX2484 was also the most defined of the four inhibitors (Figure S2C, map contoured at 1.0σ). The flexibility of the nonsteroidal scaffold allows the methoxy moiety to rotate and fit into the small cavity near the zinc, where residue Gln92 helps to stabilize it. It is worth noting that Gln92 may form an additional hydrogen bond with this group. This stabilization, paired with an added hydrogen bond from the Thr residues, results in high binding affinity.
    The predicted ΔG of STX2484 CA IX complex formation was calculated to be −18.4 kcal/mol, the best of the STX series subjected to SAR evaluation. Similarly to STX140, this is likely due to the addition of a hydrogen bond via the introduction of the methoxy group. STX2484 also had the highest interaction percentage of the solvent-buried surface of 71%, maximizing van der Waal interactions within the active site (Table 3). Although STX2484 showed the best calculated ΔG of complex formation and the highest area of interaction, it did not exhibit the lowest Ki and Kd (Tables 1 and 3). Although an efficient means of predicting binding, the estimated thermodynamics from ITC are still an estimation and should be accompanied by other experiments.

    2.7. STX2845

    STX2845 (PDB: 6E92, Figure 4D) has a structural formula similar to STX2484 being a nonsteroidal derivative, but lacking the ortho-methoxy group. Also, similarly to STX2484, STX2845 had well-defined omit map electron density (Figure S2D, map contoured at 1.0σ). As seen for the other STX inhibitors, STX2845 hydrogen bonded with Thr199 and Thr200, similarly to STX49 (distances of 2.7 and 2.8 Å, respectively), but it lacks the methoxy group and therefore the additional hydrogen bond as in STX140 and STX2484. STX2845 exhibited only hydrophobic van der Waals interactions with active site residues Leu91, Val121, Val131, Gly132, Val143, and Leu198 (Figure 4D).
    STX2845 had the poorest calculated ΔG for binding to CA IX of the four inhibitors, −16.2 kcal/mol, and this is probably a consequence of the low-buried surface area of 66% and the omission of the hydrogen bond of the methoxy moiety. The program used to calculate the ΔG of binding weighs heavily on the number of hydrogen bonds formed but largely ignores zinc in the active site. Binding to an active CA isoform is difficult to predict and measure because of the force fields induced by the zinc ion. As seen in Tables 1 and 2, STX2845 was shown to be the best inhibitor with the highest specificity, despite the predicted thermodynamic values.
    All of the drugs in the STX series showed preference for binding CA IX over CA II in both Ki and Kd (Tables 1 and 3). This is likely due to bulkier groups in the active site of CA II than that in CA IX. The CA II isoform has multiple differences compare to CA IX in active site residues; the most notable one being Phe131. This residue most likely hampers inhibitor access to the active site by inducing steric hindrance blocking binding. However, in CA IX, this residue is a valine opening up the active site. Of the four inhibitors studied in the SAR experiments, STX2484 showed the greatest CA isoform specificity with a sevenfold specificity for CA IX over CA II when comparing binding affinities. However, STX2845 has a twofold increase in binding affinity for CA IX, while also having the greatest CA IX specificity in Ki.
    The STX series of inhibitors explored the effect of inhibitor conformational flexibility and design aspects of CA inhibitors; a fused steroid ring versus linked ring structures and the effect of the addition of a methoxy group placement near the zinc.
    Conformational flexibility makes the nonsteroidal inhibitors better binders to CA IX. The steroidal class has Kds at least 20-fold higher than the more flexible nonsteroidal inhibitor STX2484 (Table 3), likely because of the flexibility that the methylene linker adds to STX2484 and STX2845. STX49 and STX140 possess a fused ring structure, making them rigid and bulky. These molecules are very limited in flexibility and thus can only sample a small amount of binding modes compared to the nonsteroidals. The result is a higher binding energy, leading to the steroid derivatives not binding as tightly. The more open system of the nonsteroidals allows them to rotate and sample more conformations upon binding. More energetically favorable confirmations are thus achieved, explaining the better binding affinities (Figure 5B, Table 3).
    The addition of the methoxy group seems to have different effects dependent on a given inhibitor. For the steroidal class, STX49 and STX140, it seems that the methoxy substituent allows tighter binding, providing an additional hydrogen bond and also increasing solubility. However, the opposite is observed for STX2484 and STX2845. Although the methoxy group does lead to an additional hydrogen bond in STX2484, its binding affinity is sevenfold lower than STX2845. Likely, the methoxy group aids the solubility of the steroidal inhibitors allowing more compounds to be in solution to bind to the active site. In turn, this may also mimic the effects of better binding as the availability of the inhibitor in solution increases. However, in the case of the nonsteroidal compounds that are already much more soluble, it seems that binding with the methoxy group results in a less energetically favorable conformation, despite the additional hydrogen bond, by forcing the compound into an unfavorable pocket. The compound must twist in conformation when the ortho-methoxy group is present, resulting in a decrease in affinity for STX2484 in CA IX.
    Because of differences in steric hindrance, each of the ligands occupies a different, slightly rotated position in the active site to maximize energy-stabilizing interactions (Figure 5A). The inhibitors bind similarly based on the presence of the methoxy group. STX49 and STX2845 bind planar to each other, with their first two rings superimposable. However, with the methoxy group present, the inhibitor has to “find” a conformation to bind to the zinc. Therefore, it samples multiple binding modes before arriving at a local energy minimum. STX140 rotates approximately 40° from STX49 and STX2845 (Figure 5B). This places the methoxy group in an area of charged residues that stabilize the alternate configuration. STX2484 binds similarly, twisting ∼90° away from the other three inhibitors to place its methoxy group in a polar cavity (Figure 5A,B).
    STX49 showed the lowest solubility of the inhibitors tested in the SAR studies, and, as such, a high DMSO concentration was required for experiments. Most proteins are unstable in DMSO, and CA has been reported to decompose at concentrations above 10%. Therefore, the limit of ITC was reached due to STX49 poor solubility and the Kd could not be calculated.

    3. Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A series of 11 sulfamate and 2 nonsulfamate phenolic ligands were evaluated for their binding and inhibition of CA II and CA IX. Named the “STX series”, these compounds are structural relatives to the compound STX140, which could potentially be a cancer therapy in a hormone-independent setting.(14,15,17,18) These molecules were classified under three subsets: steroidal, nonsteroidal, and nonsulfamates (Figure 1). All ligands studied were shown to inhibit CA II and CA IX through the use of a stopped flow kinetic apparatus monitoring the amount of 18O exchange between CO2 and water. The nonsteroidal subset in particular showed the best inhibition, with STX2845 possessing a Ki of 2 nM (Table 1).
    Molecular docking was then performed using the UCSF Dock 6 program. Each ligand was docked into the active site of CA II and CA IX and assigned a grid score based on how well it interacts with active site residues. Scores ranged from 0, indicative of no binding, to −100, meaning tight binding. By plotting Ki versus the grid score for every compound, the best candidate inhibitors were chosen for further SAR studies. STX140, STX2484, and STX2845 were explored in additional studies because of their low Kis, high selectivity ratios, and predicted grid scores. STX49 was designed to examine the effects of the ortho-methoxy motif (Figures 2 and 3).
    X-ray crystallography was performed on these four compounds in complex with CAIXmimic, followed by ITC. X-ray crystallography confirmed that all of these compounds bound through the aryl 3-O-sulfamate moiety, confirming observations in the kinetic studies. Val121, Val131, Thr199, and Thr200 were identified as important residues for inhibitor binding and Val 121 and 131 may be crucial for CA IX specificity (Figures 4 and 5).
    Of the STX inhibitors in the SAR studies, STX2845 and STX2484 were observed as the most effective and STX49 the poorest. Comparing Kis between CA II and CA IX, STX2484 showed a high CA IX specificity of ∼100-fold preference for CA IX. STX2845 showed a similar preference of ∼200, indicating its high specificity. Furthermore, ITC experiments confirmed tighter binding with CA IX for both of these inhibitors. STX2484 and STX2845 exhibit clogP values of 1.43 and 1.51, respectively. These values indicate high solubility and infer high bioavailability (Figure 3). These two nonsteroidal inhibitors are the lead compounds emerging from these experiments and should be further derivatized to lead to more specific compounds. Because of limited solubility, STX49 binding could only be measured via X-ray crystallography with high concentrations of DMSO. ITC was unable to detect complex formation between STX49 and CA IX and the stopped flow kinetic assay could not detect inhibition. The stopped-flow kinetic assay and ITC experiments are limited by organic solvent concentrations; therefore, low-solubility compounds cannot be measured accurately with these methods. STX49 exhibits a clogP value of 2.32, a significant decrease in solubility compared to the nonsteroidal compounds. This insolubility would translate to poor bioavailability in vivo, thus making STX49 a poor therapeutic. The other steroidal inhibitor STX140 in contrast performed much better in SAR experiments, showing μM binding affinities, a nM Ki, and a lower ΔG in binding energy. The added methoxy group decreased STX140 clogP value to 2.23, allowing higher solubility and therefore a higher bioavailability, something already established in vivo.(17,18)
    CA II is the main off-target destination for CA IX-targeted drug therapy, as it is expressed ubiquitously in a large concentration in the cytoplasm of most cells. CA IX, however, is expressed at relatively low concentrations on the extracellular surface of cancer cells. Traditionally, steroid molecules can efficiently transverse the cellular membrane because of their hydrophobic nature. Steroids have a cellular membrane permeability coefficient of 10–3 to 10–4 cm/s, similar to that of water, 3.4 × 10–3 cm/s.(38,39) However, molecules with clogP values higher than 5 are known to be cell membrane impermeable.(40) Hence, designing slightly insoluble inhibitors that have difficulty crossing the cellular membrane could prove beneficial for the STX series, as well as for any CA IX-targeted therapy in the future. Therefore, slightly insoluble nonsteroidal derivatives may prove to be even more selective for CA IX in vivo.
    STX140 and the other 12 inhibitors are intriguing for potential oncology applications as some of them have been shown to exhibit a “double-hit effect”, stopping both microtubule polymerization and being both proapoptotic and anti-angiogenic. Multiple targeting agents are currently growing in popularity with double and even triple targeting compounds in pharmaceutical pipelines.(41) Inhibiting CA IX activity in a targeted and selective fashion over CAII could thus add an additional advantage, linked through addressing pH dysregulation, an essential mechanism for cancer cell survival. Further derivatives should be synthesized to explore the effects of adding diverse chemical motifs to these compounds. The ortho-methoxy group in particular should be closely examined, as its effect on binding was different between the steroidal versus nonsteroidal compounds. Invasion and migration assays with CA IX-expressing cell types would also be a great way to determine the anti-angiogenic effects of these inhibitors. Overall, these drugs represent promising candidates targeted to TNBC.

    4.1. Compounds

    STX49 (3,17β-bis-sulfamoyloxyestra-1,3,5(10)-triene), STX140, STX243 (3,17β-bis-sulfamoyloxy-2-ethylestra-1,3,5(10)-triene), STX641 (17β-cyanomethyl-2-methoxy-3-sulfamoyloxyestra-1,3,5(10)-triene), STX738 (3-hydroxy-2-methoxy-17β-sulfamoyloxyestra-1,3,5(10)-triene), STX2182 (2-(3,4-dimethoxybenzoyl)-7-methoxy-6-sulfamoyloxy-3,4-dihydroisoquinolin-1(2H)-one), STX2480 (6-hydroxy-7-methoxy-2-(3,4,5-trimethoxybenzyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline), STX2484 (7-methoxy-6-sulfamoyloxy-2-(3,4,5-trimethoxybenzyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline), STX2839 (6-hydroxy-2-(3,4,5-trimethoxybenzyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline), STX2845 (6-sulfamoyloxy-2-(3,4,5-trimethoxybenzyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline), STX2848 ((±)-3-ethyl-7-methoxy-6-sulfamoyloxy-2-(3,4,5-trimethoxybenzyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline), STX3209 ((±)-7-methoxy-3-methyl-6-sulfamoyloxy-2-(2-chloro-5-methoxybenzyl)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline), and STX3469 (3-(2,3-dimethoxybenzyl)-7-sulfamoyloxy-6-methoxy-3,4-dihydroquinazolin-2(1H)-one) were synthesized as described.(17,24−29,32,42) The compounds exhibited spectroscopic and analytical data in accordance with their structures (Figure 1) and were pure (≥98%), as determined by high-performance liquid chromatography.

    4.2. Protein Expression and Purification

    CA IXmimic was expressed and purified according to the previously published protocols.(10,43) Briefly, the CA IX gene-containing plasmid was transformed into competent BL21 Escherichia coli cells via a standard BL21 transformation protocol that were then transferred to an overnight growth in nutrient-rich Luria broth. Cells were then allowed to grow to an optical density of 0.6 at 600 nm in a large-scale growth in the presence of selecting antibiotic. The cells were then induced by the addition of 0.5 mM isopropyl β-d-1-thiogalactoside (IPTG) and 1 mM zinc sulfate and incubated for an additional 3 h. The addition of zinc during induction aids expression and protein folding. Cells were then lysed using a microfluidizer, and CAIXmimic was purified using affinity chromatography with a gravity column and benzylsulfonamide resin. Purity was determined with SDS-PAGE, and protein concentration was determined by UV/vis spectroscopy at 280 nm.

    4.3. Kinetic Studies

    CA II and CA IX kinetics and inhibition were studied using a previously published protocol utilizing a stopped flow apparatus and membrane-inlet mass spectrometer to measure the exchange of 18O between CO2 and water.(44)

    4.4. Docking

    The STX series of compounds were docked both to CA II and CA IX using the DOCK 6 program found in the program suite of UCSF.(34,45,46) Docking was scored and compared between each isoform for 12 of the compounds.

    4.5. X-ray Crystallography

    Initial attempts at capturing the STX inhibitors in complex with CA IXmimic using cryogenic conditions were unsuccessful. It is possible that the rapid cooling procedure or the cryo-protectant hindered the inhibitor binding within the active site. CA IXmimic was successfully cocrystallized with each inhibitor using the hanging drop vapor diffusion method. Crystal trays were setup with 500 μL of mother liquor, containing 1.6 M sodium citrate and 50 mM Tris at a pH of 7.8. Each hanging drop consisted of a 1:1 ratio of 10 mg/mL protein to mother liquor. The inhibitors were dissolved in DMSO to ∼1 mM and added to the drops to a final concentration of ∼100 μM.
    CA IXmimic cocrystals formed after 3 weeks. A crystal of each inhibitor complex was wet mounted into quartz capillary tubes, with excess solvent removed, and the tubes sealed with wax. Diffraction data were collected using an in-house X-ray generator at room temperature using an RU-H3R rotating copper anode with a wavelength 1.5418 Å and the X-ray generator running at 50 kV and 22 mA. Diffraction images were collected on a Rigaku 0.3 mm R-Axis VI++ image plate detector. Data were collected with a detector distance of 120 mm, with 1.0° oscillations, and an exposure time set of 300 s and indexed, integrated, merged, and scaled to the P21 space group using the program HKL2000.(47) Molecular replacement phasing was performed using the software package PHENIX with the PDB entry 3KS3.(48) Phases, model refinements, and ligand restraints were also calculated using PHENIX. Further refinement and model building were performed using the interactive modeling program Coot.(49) Figures were generated in the molecular graphical software PyMol, and protein–ligand interactions and bond lengths were determined using LigPlot Plus.(50,51) Upon complex determination, PDB Pisa was used to determine surface interactions and estimated free binding energies.(52)

    4.6. Isothermal Titration Calorimetry

    ITC was performed with CA II and CA IX isoforms and each inhibitor to measure relative binding affinities. When preparing protein and inhibitor solutions, it is critical that each solution is identical to prevent heat of solvent dilution (except for drug and protein). To ensure this, the same concentrations of storage buffer and DMSO were used in the drug and protein solutions. A 500 μM solution of each inhibitor was made in 10% DMSO diluted in CA stabilization buffer (50 mM Tris, 100 mM NaCl, pH 7.8). CA IX was prepared to 50 μM diluted in the CA stabilization buffer with 10% DMSO. Experiments were performed on the MicroCal ITC 200 manufactured by Malvern. Degassed CA IX (300 μL) was loaded into the sample cell of the micro-ITC, and 60 μL of the degassed inhibitor solution was loaded into the syringe. The ITC program was set to one 0.2 μL injection with 18 subsequent 2 μL injections of drug, stirring at 700 rpm. All experiments were performed at 25 °C with a reference baseline heat of 5 μcal. Because of low heat of binding, the inhibitor concentration was increased if data did not indicate saturation. Heat peaks were generated, measured, integrated, and used to calculate binding affinities in the micro-ITC analysis software package provided.

        Krissinel, Evgeny; Henrick, Kim
        Journal of Molecular Biology (2007), 372 (3), 774-797CODEN: JMOBAK; ISSN:0022-2836. (Elsevier Ltd.)
        The authors discuss basic phys.-chem. principles underlying the formation of stable macromol. complexes, which in many cases are likely to be the biol. units performing a certain physiol. function. The authors also consider available theor. approaches to the calcn. of macromol. affinity and entropy of complexation. The latter is shown to play an important role and make a major effect on complex size and symmetry. The authors develop a new method, based on chem. thermodn., for automatic detection of macromol. assemblies in the Protein Data Bank (PDB) entries that are the results of x-ray diffraction expts. As found, biol. units may be recovered at 80-90% success rate, which makes x-ray crystallog. an important source of exptl. data on macromol. complexes and protein-protein interactions. The method is implemented as a public WWW service (http://www.ebi.ac.uk/msd-srv/prot_int/pistart.html).
