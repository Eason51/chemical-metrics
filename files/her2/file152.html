    Abstract

    Estrogen-related receptor α is a potential candidate target for therapeutic treatment of breast cancer. We describe the discovery and structure–activity relationship study of a series of 1-phenyl-4-benzoyl-1H-1,2,3-triazoles as novel suppressors of ERRα transcriptional functions. The most promising compound, 2-aminophenyl-(1-(3-isopropylphenyl)-1H-1,2,3-triazol-4-yl)methanone (14n), potently suppressed the transcriptional functions of ERRα with IC50 = 0.021 μM in a cell-based reporter gene assay and also decreased both the mRNA levels and the protein levels of ERRα and the downstream targets. This compound inhibited the proliferation and migration of breast cancer cells with high level of ERRα. Preliminary pharmacokinetic studies suggested that it possessed a good pharmacokinetic profile with an oral bioavailability of 71.8%. The compounds may serve as novel small molecule probes for further validation of ERRα as a molecular target for anticancer drug development.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Estrogen-related receptors (ERRs) are the orphan members of a nuclear receptor (NR) superfamily that contains three isoforms (ERRα, ERRβ, and ERRγ).(1) These isoforms share significant homology with the classical estrogen receptors (ERs), but they do not bind with endogenous estrogens or any known natural ligands. ERRα and ERRβ are the first two orphan nuclear receptors identified by a low stringency hybridization screen in 1988,(2) and ERRγ was discovered 10 years later.(3) ERRα is expressed predominately in metabolically active tissues such as intestine, brown adipose tissue, and skeletal muscles and functions as a key regulator of energy metabolism. ERRβ is important in early development, but its expression is highly restricted in adult stages and it can only be detected at low levels in adult eyes, inner ear, heart, and kidneys. The exact function of ERRγ remains elusive, but it is mainly expressed in the spinal cord and central nervous system.(3c, 4)
    Collectively, studies have demonstrated that ERRα plays a critical role in the regulation of the energy of metabolism by controlling the transcriptional functions and/or protein expression of key components involved in tricarboxylic acid (TCA) metabolism, oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS), and glycolysis. These key components include peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor γ coactivator 1α (PGC-1α),(5) medium-chain acyl-coenzyme A dehydrogenase (MCAD),(6) and pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase 4 (PDK4).(7) In addition, genetic and functional analyses have revealed the functional cross-talk between ERRα and ERα at multiple steps of the transcriptional process by which, through competition or cooperation, they interact with similar co-regulators.(8) Modulation of ERRα activity through small molecules may be seen as a novel strategy for clinical management of estrogen-linked diseases such as breast cancer.(1b)
    Breast cancer is one of the most common cancers diagnosed in the United States and after lung cancer, is the leading cause of cancer deaths in women. Hormonal therapy medications, which include aromatase inhibitors, selective estrogen receptor modulators, and estrogen receptor down-regulators, have achieved a significant advantage in the clinical treatment of most breast cancer patients, but about 10–20% of breast cancers are diagnosed as triple-negative [TNBC, negative for estrogen receptors (ER−), progesterone receptors (PR−), and HER2 (HER2−)] or basal-like (BLBC), for which the traditional hormone therapies are ineffective.(9) There is therefore an urgent need for alternative therapies for effective and comprehensive management of breast cancer with different mechanisms of action.
    There are several reports implying that ERRα might be a valid candidate target for therapeutic treatment of breast cancer, including ER-negative breast cancer.(8, 10, 11) For instance, Ariazi et al. reported that ERRα is overexpressed in ERα-negative breast cancer and could serve as an unfavorable biomarker in human breast cancer(11b) and Suzuki et al. also found that expression of ERRα is associated with an increased risk of recurrence and adverse clinical outcome.(12) Furthermore, small interfering RNA (siRNA) targeting ERRα in vitro was shown to substantially reduce the migration of the ERα-negative MDA-MB-231 cells, a BLBC cell line, and suppress tumor growth in vivo in an xenograft model.(13)
    Several classes of ERRα inverse agonists or agonists, which could suppress or improve ERRα’s transcriptional functions, have been discovered (Figure 1).(14) Some of the synthetic ERRα inverse agonists have been reported to inhibit tumor development and progression by disrupting the interaction of ERRs with their coactivators or their constitutive transcriptional activities, but despite these advances, no ERRα modulators have demonstrated efficacy in humans. As a part of our continuing program on ERR modulators,(14f) we report the discovery and structural optimization of a series of 1-phenyl-4-benzoyl-1H-1,2,3-triazoles derivatives as new suppressors of ERRα transcriptional functions, which may produce lead compounds for further development of novel antibreast cancer drugs targeting ERRα.

    The synthesis of new ERRα suppressors 14a–ab is shown in Scheme 1. The substituted benzaldehydes (7) used as starting materials were either first protected with 3,4-dihydro-2H-pyran (DHP) to give the derived benzaldehydes (8a–c) which were reacted with Grignard reagent ethynylmagnesium bromide to produce 9a–c or directly converted into intermediates 9d–h and 9j–k through a Grignard reaction.     Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    To identify new molecules capable of modulating the transcriptional functions of ERRα, we conducted the screen on a carbonyl-focused chemical library using a well-established mammalian two-hybrid reporter gene assay, where 293FT cells were transiently transfected with expression plasmids encoding fusion protein containing GAL4 DNA binding domain and human ERRα ligand binding domain, and PGC-1α, respectively, together with luciferase reporter constructs.(14f) Several compounds were identified that could potently modulate the transcriptional activity of ERRα, including 8-ethyl-2-phenyl-4H-pyrido[1,2-α]pyrimidin-4-one (6) which improved ERRα’s functions,(14f) while (4-hydroxyphenyl)(1-(4-isopropylphenyl)-1H-1,2,3-triazol-4-yl)methanone (14a) strongly suppressed its activity at 10 μM. Further evaluation showed the IC50 of compound 14a to be 1.59 μM, about 6 times more potent than the standard reference compound 5(14e) but approximately 4-fold less active than 2 (Table 1).(14b)
    Extensive structural optimization of the lead compound (14a) was conducted to improve its suppressive activity on the transcriptional functions of ERRα, as measured in this reporter assay, and the results are summarized in Table 1. We first investigated the potential impact of the hydroxyl group in ring A on the bioactivity by altering its position. The position of the hydroxyl group was found to strongly affect the inhibitory activity on ERRα’s transcriptional functions. Although the m-hydroxy derivative (14b) displayed potency similar to that of 14a, the suppressive effect on ERRα’s transcriptional functions was improved about 4-fold by moving the hydroxyl group from the para-position (14a) to the ortho-position (14c); 14c inhibited ERRα’s transcriptional functions with an IC50 of 0.39 μM. Further investigation suggested that the o-hydroxyl group in 14c could be replaced by an NO2 (14d) or a Me (14e) group with no significant effect on the bioactivity, but when the o-hydroxyl group in 14c was replaced by F (14f), CF3 (14g), or OMe (14h), the resulting compounds were obviously less potent. For instance, the o-CF3 substituted compound 14g failed totally to inhibit ERRα at concentrations as high as 50 μM. The potency was improved about 10-fold when the o-hydroxyl group in 14c was replaced with an amino group (14i), which strongly suppressed ERRα’s transcriptional functions with an IC50 of 0.041 μM, while the m-NH2 (14j) or p-NH2 (14k) analogues were clearly less potent. The investigation also suggested that the carbonyl group was necessary for the compounds to display strong suppression of ERRα. For instance, compound 14l, in which the carbonyl group was replaced by a methylene, was 4 times less potent than the corresponding carbonyl compound 14c.
    We further investigated the impact of the substituent in ring B on the ERRα suppressive activity by adopting compound 14i as a new starting point and found that the position of the substituent in this ring significantly affected the biological activity of the resulting compounds. For instance, when the p-isopropyl in 14i was moved to the ortho-position (14m), the potency was decreased about 100-fold. However, the compound bearing a m-isopropyl group (14n) was about twice as potent as the original compound 14i. The structure–activity relationship study also clearly revealed that an isopropyl at the meta position might be optimal for the compounds to suppress the transcriptional functions of ERRα. A smaller or larger hydrophobic group at this position was found to be detrimental to the ERRα suppressing activity. For instance, the compounds with H (14r), ethyl (14o), methyl (14p), or CF3 (14q) at the meta position in ring B displayed IC50 values of 0.43, 0.20, 2.17, 15.89 μM, respectively, numbers that are about 20-, 10-, 100-, and 800-fold greater than that of compound 14n. The compounds bearing larger hydrophobic groups such as tert-butyl (14s), phenyl (14t), or phenoxyl (14u) were also less potent than 14n, displaying IC50 values of 0.094, 0.65, 1.74 μM, respectively. When ring B was substituted with a m-benzoxyl group (14v), the ERRα suppressing potency was totally abolished. Further investigation showed that the m-isopropyl group in 14n could be replaced by ester substituents such as COOMe (14w), COOEt (14x), COOPr (14y) and still retained ERRα suppressing activity, but the potency was obviously decreased. However, when the m-isopropyl group in 14n was replaced with a hydrophilic group such as nitrile (14z), sulfonamide (14aa), or carboxyl (14ab), the resulting compounds totally failed to modulate ERRα’s transcriptional functions.

    Given its strong potency in the cell-based reporter gene assay, the ERRα suppressing activity of 14n was further validated by investigating its effect on the expression of ERRα and downstream target genes by using real-time PCR and Western blot analysis. It is known that ERRα is important in regulation of the transcriptional functions and/or protein expression of key compounds in tricarboxylic acid (TCA) metabolism, oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS), and glycolysis, such as MCAD(6) and PDK4.(7) Thus, the impact of compound 14n on these functions was investigated first at both the mRNA and protein levels. As shown in Figure 2, 14n dose-dependently and potently reduced the mRNA levels of PDK4 in a quantitative real-time PCR assay, whereas the mRNA levels of MCAD were not reduced significantly (Figure 2A). Interestingly, the protein levels of both PDK4 and MCAD decreased dose-dependently after a 48 h treatment by a Western blot analysis (Figure 2B). It was also observed that both the mRNA and protein levels of ERRα were also obviously decreased after the treatment with 14n, which might be caused by an autoregulatory loop of ERRα.(15)
    The antiproliferative effects of compounds were also investigated using ER-positive MCF-7 and ER-negative MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells, both of which positively express ERRα.(16) The results demonstrated that the antiproliferative potencies of compounds translated well from their activities in the heterologous reporter gene assay (Table 2 and Supporting Information). Compounds 14i, 14n, and 14s displayed the most potent ERRα suppressing activities in the reporter assay and also inhibited the growth of MCF-7 breast cancer cells with IC50 values of 6.7, 3.7, and 17.2 μM, respectively. The compounds also displayed comparable potencies in inhibiting the proliferation of ER-negative MDA-MB-231 breast cells, indicating that they might be used as lead compounds for development of new anticancer agents with which to treat triple-negative breast cancer.

    Studies have demonstrated that ERRα plays an important role in modulating the motility of cancer cells. Selective suppression of ERRα with siRNA has been shown to substantially reduce the migration of the ERRα-positive MDA-MB-231 and MCF-7 breast cancer cells.(13, 16) Therefore, we first investigated the effect of compound 14n on MCF-7 and MDA-MB-231 cell motility by using a Boyden chamber assay. It was shown that a 24 h treatment with compound 14n does-dependently reduced the numbers of both MCF-7 (Figure 3A,B) and MDA-MB-231 (Figure 3C,D) cells that migrated toward serum in the lower chamber, indicating its strong inhibitory effect on the motility of breast cancer cells. The migration inhibitory effect of 14n on MCF-7 cells was further validated by using a well-established wound healing assay. It was shown that treatment with compound 14n moderately inhibited the migrating process in MCF-7 cells, suppressing the wound closure by ∼32% and ∼40% at 5.0 and 10.0 μM (p < 0.01), respectively, compared with the untreated control (Figure 3E and Figure 3F).

    In summary, a series of 1-phenyl-4-benzoyl-1H-1,2,3-triazole analogues were identified as functional suppressors of the transcriptional functions of ERRα. ERRα suppressing functions of compound 14n were investigated by a cell-based reporter gene assay, real-time PCR, and a Western blot assay. Furthermore, the representative compound 14n demonstrated inhibitory potency against the growth of both ER-positive MCF-7 and ER-negative MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells with positive ERRα and a dose-dependent reduction in the migration of breast cancer cells in vitro. The results are further evidence that ERRα may be a useful target for treatment of breast cancer and also serve to identify compounds that could be used as leads in drug discovery efforts to discover compounds that could be used to treat breast cancer including ER-negative breast cancer. Further structural optimization to improve pharmacokinetic properties and pharmacodynamic evaluation in vivo of this class of compounds is in progress and will be reported in due course.

    All reagents and solvents were used as purchased from commercial sources. Flash chromatography was performed using silica gel (300 mesh). All reactions were monitored by TLC, using silica gel plates with fluorescence F254 and UV light visualization. 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker AV-400 spectrometer at 400 MHz and Bruker AV-500 spectrometer at 125 MHz.     Cell Lines and Cell Culture

    The human 293FT cell line was purchased from Invitrogen, and the MCF-7 cell line and the MDA-MB-231 cell line were purchased from ATCC. Cells were grown in the medium recommended by Invitrogen and ATCC, respectively. Unless otherwise indicated, cells were cultured in a complete medium containing 10% FBS in a 5% CO2 incubator at 37 °C.

    Transient Transfection Dual Luciferase Reporter Assay

    293FT cells were seeded in 96-well plate at a density of 10 000 cells/well in DMEM containing 10% FBS. After plating for 18–24 h, transit transfections were performed with Lipofectamine 2000 (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Total DNA for transfections included GAL4-DBD-ERRα-LBD vector (0.5 ng), pcDNA-PGC-1α vector (0.5 ng), pFR vector (25 ng), and internal control vector pRLTK renilla (3 ng). After cells were transfected for 6 h, compounds were then added for an additional 24 h. Luciferase activity was measured by dual luciferase reporter assay system (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s instruction on a Veritas microplate luminometer. Relative luciferase units were the ratio of the absolute activity of firefly luciferase to that of Renilla luciferase. The experiment was done in triplicate, and results are representatives of at least three independent experiments.

    Real-Time PCR Analysis

    RNA isolation and real-time analyses were performed as detailed in a previous report.(14f) Briefly, total RNA was isolated from MCF-7 breast cancer cells pretreated with 14n for 48 h using Trizol reagent (Invitrogen), and isolated total RNA was reverse-transcribed with Superscript III reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen). For real-time PCR analysis, cDNA samples were used in quantitative PCR reaction in the presence of fluorescent dye Cybergreen (Bio-Rad, Benicia, CA, U.S.). The following PCR conditions were applied: 3 min, 95 °C; 42× (10 s, 95 °C; 20 s, 60 °C; 1 s, 70 °C). After each elongation step, the reaction was quantified in a reading step and the product quality was tested by melting curve analysis. Relative abundance of mRNA was calculated after normalization to Gapdh mRNA. Sequences for the primers used in this study are shown in the Table S1 in Supporting Information.

    Antibodies and Western Blotting

    MCF-7 cells were plated in 6 cm dishes. After 24 h of growth, the cells were treated with the compounds under the indicated concentrations for 48 h. Cell lysates were collected with 1× lysis buffer (SDS) and were briefly sonicated. Western blot analyses were conducted after separation by SDS–PAGE electrophoresis and transfer to PVDF membranes. Membranes were blocked in 5% bovine serum albumin/TBST and probed with the indicated antibodies, followed by a peroxidase-conjugated anti-mouse or rabbit secondary antibody (Thermo). ERRα, MCAD, and PDK4 antibodies were obtained from Cell Signaling Technology Inc.

    Cell Proliferation and Growth Inhibition Assay

    Cell proliferation was assessed by MTT assay. Cells were exposed to treatment for 72 h, and the number of cells used per experiment for each cell line was adjusted to obtain an absorbance of 1.3–2.2 at 490 nm. Compounds were tested at five concentrations (0.016–50 μM), with each concentration duplicated six times. All experiments were repeated at least four times. The data were calculated using GraphPad Prism version 4.0. Dose–response curves were fitted using a nonlinear regression model with a sigmoidal dose–response.

    Transwell Migration Assay

    An in vitro migration assay was conducted using a 24-well Transwell unit (8 μm of pore size) placed in Transwell chambers according to manufacturer’s instructions (BD Bioscience, Bedford, MA, U.S.). The coated filters were rehydrated using serum-free medium for 2 h in the incubator at 37 °C before use. Medium containing 10% (v/v) FBS (0.5% FBS and 50 μg/mL collagen I for MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells) was applied to the lower chamber as chemoattractant. Cells (6 × 104 cells/well) in serum-free medium were seeded in the upper part of the transwell plate and incubated with various concentrations of compound 14n for 24 h at 37 °C. After incubation, the cells in the upper surface of the membrane were carefully removed with a cotton swab. The migrated cells that passed across the filters to the lower surface of the membrane filter were recorded with an Olympus CKX41 inverted fluorescence microscope and the Image-ProPlus image capture software, at magnification ×200, and the cell number was counted. The experiments were repeated three times.

    Wound Healing Assay

    MCF-7 breast cancer cells (5 × 105 cells/well) were seeded in a six-well tissue culture plate and grown to 80–90% confluence. After the medium was removed, a gap with constant width was created in the center of cells by scratching the monolayer with a sterile yellow micropipet tip. After that, cells were rinsed with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) twice to get rid of cellular debris, and then cells were exposed to various concentrations of compound 14n (5 and 10 μM for both cells). The wound closure was monitored and photographed at 0 and 24 h with the Olympus CKX41 phase contrast inverted fluorescence microscope and the Image-ProPlus image capture software (magnification ×40). For each well, at least eight different areas of the scratch were photographed.

    Pharmacokinetic Studies

    Male Sprague–Dawley rats (180–220 g) were dosed with test compounds intravenously at 5 mg/kg and by oral gavage at 25 mg/kg. Blood samples (0.3 mL) were then obtained via orbital sinus puncture at 2.0 min, 10.0 min, 30.0 min, 1.0 h, 2.0 h, 3.0 h, 4.0 h, 6.0 h, 8.0 h, 12.0 h, 21.0 h, 24.0 h, 30.0 h, 36.0 h, 48.0 h, and 72.0 h time points and collected into heparinized tubes. Blood samples were centrifuged for cell removal, and the plasma supernatant was then transferred to a clean vial and subsequently stored at 4 °C prior to analysis. Test sample concentrations were determined by LCMS using propranolol as internal standard. Pharmacokinetic parameters were calculated using DAS 2.0 software.

The SERM Raloxifene, approved for the treatment of osteoporosis, lacks tamoxifen's increased risk for endometrial cancer and is being evaluated for the prevention of breast cancer. Other SERMs approved or under development for use against breast cancer or osteoporosis include Toremifene, GW5638, GW7604 (the active metabolite of GW5638), Idoxifene, Lasofoxifene, Arzoxifene, Bazedoxifene, EM-800, and Acolbifene (the active metabolite of EM-800). The AIs include the nonsteroidal agents Letrozole and Anastrole, and the steroidal agent Exemestane. The pure antiestrogen Fulvestrant has proven to be just as effective as AIs. Other pure antiestrogens, ZK-703, ZK-253, RU 58668, and TAS-108, show great promise. Crosstalk among plasma membrane-localized ER, growth factor receptor signaling, and nuclear-localized ER provide further insights into antihormonal-resistant breast cancer.
