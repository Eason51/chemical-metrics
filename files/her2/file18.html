    Abstract

    A water-soluble fluorescent aza-BODIPY platform (Wazaby) was prepared and functionalized by a polyazamacrocycle agent and a bioconjugable arm. The resulting fluorescent derivative was characterized and bioconjugated onto a trastuzumab monoclonal antibody as a vector. After bioconjugation, the imaging agent appeared to be stable in serum (>72 h at 37 °C) and specifically labeled HER-2-positive breast tumors slices. The bioconjugate was radiolabeled with [111In] indium and studied in vivo. The developed monomolecular multimodal imaging probe (MOMIP) is water-soluble and chemically and photochemically stable, emits in the near infrared (NIR) region (734 nm in aqueous media), and displays a good quantum yield of fluorescence (around 15%). Single-photon emission-computed tomography and fluorescence imaging have been performed in nude mice bearing HER2-overexpressing HCC1954 human breast cancer xenografts and have evidenced the good tumor targeting of the [111In] In bimodal agent. Finally, the proof of concept of using it as a new tool for fluorescence-guided surgery has been shown.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Over the past two decades, the development of targeted therapies using monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) has improved the overall survival (OS), progression-free survival (PFS), and complete response (CR) of patients with tumors of different origins. For instance, the development of the human epidermal growth factor 2 (HER-2)-targeted mAbs (trastuzumab) has significantly improved patient survival, particularly in up to 20% of patients with breast cancer.(1) While cancer targeted therapies have become increasing treatment strategies, not all patients benefit from these therapies and serious toxicities can occur. The main cause of non-response to immunotherapies is a low or inexistent expression of the target within the tumor.(2) Therefore, strategies to improve patient selection based on identification of specific biomarkers are desperately needed to refine therapy efficacy. Currently, the expression of biomarkers is mainly evaluated by histology on tumor biopsies with the limitation that a single biopsy may not capture the heterogeneity across various tumor lesions in a patient and even within a single lesion.(3) To overcome these issues, in vivo molecular imaging can give additional information about the dynamic biodistribution of biomarkers within all tumor lesions of an individual patient and can give a picture of heterogeneity of target expression.(2)
    In the field of molecular imaging, bimodal probes have raised particular interest.(4) Especially, the combination of fluorescence with radioisotopic imaging techniques (positron emission tomography (PET) or single-photon emission-computed tomography (SPECT)) offers numerous advantages.(4−10) Indeed, the two imaging techniques are complementary and provide a highly synergistic diagnostic, both for preclinical and clinical use. More particularly, optical imaging (OI) is a very convenient and relatively cheap imaging technique. It is versatile and displays high spatial resolution in vitro and ex vivo for histology. This is complementary to PET or SPECT imaging, which are noninvasive, do not display penetration limitations, and are very sensitive techniques in vivo. In the clinical field, the combination of both imaging techniques recently emerged as a tool for, on the one hand, preoperative imaging with PET or SPECT for tumor identification and localization and, on the other hand, intraoperative fluorescence guided surgery (FGS) with optical imaging (OI).(11) FGS rose as a powerful tool to assist surgeons during the removal of the tumor to define the resection margins, permitting a better recovery of the patient and limiting relapses.(12) Moreover, it facilitates the identification of small tumors or positive nodes.
    Among the different possible strategies for performing OI/PET or SPECT bimodal imaging, an optimized approach consists of constructing beforehand a monomolecular multimodal imaging probe (MOMIP) and then attaching the resulting MOMIP to the antibody through a single conjugation step.(13) The elaboration of such a type of MOMIP represents a synthetic challenge, but the reported MOMIP systems, based on cyanines, rhodamine, and more recently BODIPYs (boron-dipyrromethene), showed the high potential of this approach for OI/PET or SPECT bimodal imaging.(8,14)
    The choice of the fluorophore is crucial for performing in vivo imaging and FGS: it should be resistant to photobleaching and non-toxic and if possible emit in the NIR region (700–1700 nm). Indeed, in this region of the light spectrum, autofluorescence, absorption by biological tissue, and scattering are lower than in UV and visible.(15) This permits the increase of the penetration depth of this modality and improvement of the quality of the images. Currently, one of the benchmark fluorophores for FGS is IRDye 800cw.(16) It has indeed ideal photophysical properties and can even be made water-soluble. However, the size, the lipophilic character, the charge, and the nature of the fluorophore can influence the pharmacokinetics of the resulting imaging agent and especially its affinity for its target.(17) Moreover, it is very challenging to use IRDye 800cw as a platform for grafting more than one group of interest, which is needed to perform targeted bimodal imaging (e.g., SPECT or PET/OI bimodal imaging). Most of the time, an additional platform has to be grafted to the fluorophore.(11,18)
    Therefore, there is a great need to propose new high-performance fluorophores, in order to propose the most suitable probe for the target. Ideally, this fluorophore should emit in the near infrared region, be chemically and photochemically stable, be synthesizable quickly and at reasonable scales, and be water-soluble. In addition, it must be easily functionalized in order to enable the grafting of a biovector and/or another imaging modality. Indeed, having a bimodal imaging agent, which targets selectively certain tumors, would make it possible to locate the tumor before the operation thanks to SPECT or PET imaging and to assist the surgeon during the operation through fluorescence imaging. However, currently, this type of MOMIP involves a long synthesis and offers little flexibility. The objective here is to solve this problem by designing a water-soluble fluorescence platform that can easily be functionalized by two groups of interest (e.g., a SPECT probe and a vector).
    For several years, we have been interested in the potential of aza-BODIPY fluorophores. Aza-BODIPYs present numerous advantages compared to reported fluorophores.(19) They display high chemical and photochemical stability and very good photophysical properties, which can be modulated from the NIR-I (700–900 nm) to the SWIR (Short-Wave Infrared 1000-1700 nm) region.(15,20) The potential of aza-BODIPY for in vivo imaging at the preclinical and even clinical stage was recently highlighted in several studies.(14,20) Their main limitation is their poor water solubility, but, recently, we succeeded in developing a strategy to water-solubilize them by substituting the fluorine atoms on the boron by ammonium groups.(20,21) We already proved that the resulting water-soluble aza-BODIPY, which we called Wazaby (for Water-soluble aza-BODIPY dyes), was chemically and photochemically stable and could be bioconjugated to an antibody,(21) to a metallodrug,(22) or to an MRI probe.(23) In order to investigate further the potential of the Wazabys, we aimed at constructing a SPECT/OI MOMIP, based on an aza-BODIPY platform, which presents a functionalization site for the introduction of a chelating agent for radiolabeling, and another one for the introduction of a bioconjugatable handle. This study describes therefore the synthesis and the stepwise validation of the MOMIP, including its photophysical properties, its bioconjugation to trastuzumab (Herceptin), a monoclonal antibody targeting HER-2, and the in vitro/in vivo studies of the resulting bioconjugate that were performed. In order to further highlight the potential of the probe for future clinical translation, in addition to a SPECT/optical bimodal imaging investigation, a preclinical proof of concept of fluorescence-assisted surgery was also successfully undertaken.

    Synthesis and Photophysical Characterization of the MOMIP

    As noticed above, recently, we developed a fluorescent water-soluble platform, Wazaby7 (Scheme 1), bearing two symmetric arms with pending carboxylic acid functions. It was proven that two substituents can be easily introduced on these functions if they are identical.(23) However, the key point of the synthetic strategy of the MOMIP (111In-Wazaby9-Trastu) is the disymmetrization of the platform Wazaby7 for the introduction of two different moieties on the carboxylic acid functions. After several unsuccessful strategies, the disymmetrization of Wazaby7 can be achieved with a very efficient one-pot reaction (Scheme 1). It consists of activating both carboxylic acid functions in the presence of HBTU and DIPEA, introducing the chelator (DO3A(tBu)3-N-(2-aminoethyl)ethanamide), then the TOTA (TOTA stands for 1-(tert-butyloxycarbonyl-amino)-4,7,10-trioxa-13-tridecanamine) linker, and finally deprotecting the three acetate groups of the macrocycle and the pending primary amine of the TOTA linker, thus leading to the formation of Wazaby8. All the steps were performed sequentially, without purification of the different intermediates. The completion of each step was monitored by HPLC. The targeted Wazaby8 was obtained in 27% overall yield over four steps and after purification via semipreparative HPLC. Wazaby8 was then reacted with diethyl squarate to give Wazaby9, which can be easily conjugated to “NH2”-bearing derivatives (e.g., amino residue of lysine-containing antibody).(21) It is worth noting that it is possible to graft other polyazamacrocycles but that it is necessary for the carboxylic acid arms to be protected in the ester form. The DO3A derivative was chosen because it provides an easy access to a DOTA derivative, which forms stable complexes with indium. A derivative of DOTAGA could have been taken to further improve the stability of the indium complex, but an easily accessible compound displaying sufficient complexing properties was favored for the present study.
    The non-radioactive indium complex In-Wazaby9 was prepared as a model to mimic the system after indium-111 radiolabeling as well as to study the influence of the metal on the photophysical properties of the aza-BODIPY dye (Scheme 1).
    The photophysical properties of Wazaby8, Wazaby9, and In-Wazaby9 were determined both in DMSO and in PBS (Figure S1). The three compounds present characteristic spectra of aza-BODIPY (Figure S1). In DMSO, absorption and emission maximal wavelengths are in the NIR-I (around 700 and 730 nm, respectively) region and the different compounds exhibit a high quantum yield of fluorescence (∼40%). In PBS, a limited hypsochromic shift (∼10 nm) can be observed and quantum yields remain good (∼10–15%). The decrease in quantum yield in PBS compared to that in DMSO is usual. Indeed, quantum yields are always lower in protic solvents due to an increase of the non-radiative deexcitation process (e.g., hydrogen interaction). It is worth noting, when comparing the fluorescence properties of Wazaby8, Wazaby9, and In-Wazaby9 with Wazaby7, that neither the functionalization of the Wazaby platform nor the metalation of the polyazamacrocycle significantly affects the photophysical properties (Table 1).

    Characterization of Wazaby9-Trastu

    These promising results prompted us to bioconjugate Wazaby9 to trastuzumab (anti-HER-2 antibody) to validate the potential of this bimodal probe. The Wazaby-DOTA derivative, Wazaby9, was also grafted on rituximab (anti-CD20 antibody) to serve as a negative control. Bioconjugation was optimized to get a degree of labeling (DOL) suitable for imaging purposes while avoiding risks of alteration of the antibodies. After bioconjugation and purification by FPLC, the labeled antibodies Wazaby9-Trastu and Wazaby9-Ritu were stored at 4 °C. The different DOLs were determined by both UV–visible and mass spectrometry analyses, which gave a dye/antibody ratio of 1.4 (Figure S2) and 1.6, respectively. The obtained values of DOL allowed us to be optimistic for the in vivo visualization of these antibodies since Rijpkema et al. stated that a DOL in a range of 1–1.5 is optimal for antibody-targeted dual-modality imaging applications.(25)
    The stability of the resulting bioconjugate was first investigated using the method previously described by Pliquett et al.(21)Wazaby9-Trastu was therefore incubated at 37 °C in human or mice sera for 24, 48, and 72 h. Denaturing polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) of the bioconjugate was performed in both non-reducing and reducing conditions and analyzed by fluorescence (Figure 1a). As expected, no fluorescence was detected with the non-labeled trastuzumab. Concerning Wazaby9-Trastu, under non-reducing conditions, a single band appeared around 150 kDa, which corresponds to the full-length antibody (Figure 1a, non-reducing conditions). No evolution—neither degradation nor decrease of fluorescence—was observed after 72 h of incubation at 37 °C in human or mice sera. Moreover, the fluorescence stability of Wazaby9-Trastu was demonstrated post-incubation at 37 °C in mice serum on an animal imaging system (Figure S3). This confirms the good stability of Wazaby9-Trastu. It is worth noting that Wazaby9 was conjugated randomly on both heavy and light chains (67 and 33% respectively) of trastuzumab (Figure 1b, reducing conditions), which may disturb the recognition of the epitope.
    The affinity of Wazaby9-Trastu for HER-2 was determined by a biolayer interferometry (BLI) approach (Octet Red96, Figure 2a,b).(26) Even if the KD is higher than the one measured with the unconjugated antibody (KD of 8 ± 2 nM, Figure 1b), Wazaby9-Trastu directly interacted with purified HER-2 with a good affinity KD of 35 ± 6 nM. Moreover, the interaction of Wazaby9-Trastu has been shown to be specific for the recognition of the epitope and independent of the presence of the bimodal probe, since the anti-CD20 antibody conjugated with the same probe (Wazaby9-Ritu) was unable to interact with human HER-2 (Figure 2a,c). In vitro, Wazaby9-Trastu was also able to recognize human mammary cancer cells overexpressing HER-2 (HCC1954 cells) (Figure 2c) with a good affinity (KD = 68.0 nM ±4.3 nM, Figure 2d).
    In addition, Wazaby9-Trastu was found to be specific for human HER-2. Indeed, substantial labeling could be detected on HCC1954 mammary tumor slices (human HER-2-positive mammary cancer), while no signal was observed on 4T1 mammary tumor slices (murine HER-2-negative mammary cancer) (Figure S4).
    Finally, before studying Wazaby9-Trastu in mice, the absence of cytotoxicity of the Wazaby-DOTA probe alone or conjugated to anti-HER2 (trastuzumab) or anti-CD20 (Rituximab) was verified on human liver cells, the liver being the main elimination pathway of the full antibody (Figure S5). No significant in vitro toxicity was observed up to 500 μg/mL (585.5 μM) for Wazaby9, 800 μg/mL (5.3 μM) for Wazaby9-Trastu, and 120 μg/mL (0.8 μM) for Wazaby9-Ritu. Moreover, no in vivo toxicity was also observed after i.v. injection of 100 μg of Wazaby9-Trastu (Table S3 and Figures S6 and S7).

    In Vivo SPECT Studies of Wazaby9-Trastu

    The ability of Wazaby9 to target HER-2+ expressing tumors was investigated in vivo in a model of HCC1954 tumor (human HER-2+ mammary cancer)-bearing mice with SPECT/CT imaging, in order to determine its biodistribution over time. For this aim, DOTA was radiolabeled with 111In. Radiolabeling of DOTA is usually performed in 1 h at 37 °C at pH = 5.(27,28) In our case, we had to perform the reaction overnight (16 h) in order to reach a specific activity of 520 MBq/mg (non-decay-corrected) (which corresponds to a radiolabeling yield of 93%, cf. Figure S16). This enabled us to inject a minimal amount of antibody with sufficient radioactivity in order to monitor the biodistribution of the radiopharmaceutical over time by SPECT/CT imaging.
    111In-Wazaby9-Trastu (25 μg) was intravenously injected in HCC1954 tumor grafted on BALB/c nude mice. SPECT/CT imaging was performed at 24, 48, and 72 h post-injection, highlighting a strong SPECT signal of 111In-Wazaby9-Trastu at the tumor site (% ID/g 34.1, 46.1, and 52.9 at 24, 48, and 72 h post-injection, respectively) and to a lesser extent in the liver and the spleen (% ID/g 29.6 and 35.2, respectively, Figure 3). Such liver uptake is common for lipophilic derivatives. Here, even if the MOMIP is water-soluble, the corresponding bioconjugated 111In-Wazaby9-Trastu will be more lipophilic than a DOTA-trastuzumab derivative. It will therefore accumulate more in the liver (for comparison, ref (29) corresponds to a study done by some of us where the bioconjugate compound did not contain any organic fluorophore). SPECT images also indicated that accumulation in tumor increases with time until 72 h (Figure 3b and Figure S8), while it decreases in other organs.
    The specificity of the imaging agent for HCC1954 tumors could be demonstrated by performing in parallel blocking studies, by co-injection of an excess of unlabeled trastuzumab (Figure 3). Animal receiving the co-injection of an excess of unlabeled trastuzumab showed a lower tumor uptake of 111In-Wazaby9-Trastu compared with controls.

    Near-Infrared Fluorescence (NIRF)/SPECT Bimodal Imaging

    Optical imaging presents very good sensitivity. However, SPECT imaging is even more sensitive. Thus, before performing bimodal imaging experiments, the amount of imaging agent needed to obtain a good optical image was determined. An injection of 25 μg led to a limited signal. Thus, different quantities of Wazaby9-Trastu (50, 75, and 100 μg) were injected and whole-body-NIRF images at different times (24, 48, and 72 h post-injection) were recorded (Figure S9). The probe could be traced by optical imaging at three concentrations with the highest signal for 100 μg. As no sign of toxicity was observed whatever the amount of Wazaby9-Trastu was injected (Table S3 and Figures S6 and S7), we decided to go further with this last concentration.
    Thus, a bimodal imaging study was performed 72 h after injection of 100 μg of 111In-Wazaby9-Trastu. The biodistribution was followed by NIR fluorescence and SPECT/CT imaging (Figure 4a,b). Intense fluorescent and SPECT signals were detected at the tumor site, highlighting that the radiolabeling did not affect the fluorescence properties of the bimodal probe (Figure 4a,b). These exciting preliminary results were confirmed by ex vivo imaging and organ uptake, which showed a stronger fluorescence signal in the tumor rather than in other organs, highlighting again a strong and specific accumulation of the radiolabeled bioconjugate at the tumor site (Figure 4c and Figure S8). As noticed at 25 μg with SPECT/CT imaging, some liver uptake was also observed. Very importantly, the blocking studies, which were quantified ex vivo by fluorescence and γ counting, were consistent with each other (Figure 4d).

    Fluorescence-Guided Surgery

    The encouraging results obtained in bimodal imaging and especially in optical imaging prompted us to use our imaging agent in a fluorescence-guided surgery experiment. Wazaby9-Trastu was used for a better visualization of the tumor. Indeed, in the case of HER-2+ breast cancers but also of other cancers, the use of neoadjuvant therapies makes surgery difficult because of the bad distinction between tumor cells and the fibrosis induced by these treatments.(30,31) Therefore, intraoperative detection of cancer cells overexpressing HER-2 may remain particularly challenging with direct targeting strategies using antibodies.
    75 μg of Wazaby9-Trastu was injected into BALB/c nude mice bearing HCC1954 tumor. 72 h after injection, surgery was carried out using a Kaer Labs camera, a device specifically designed for FGS. Intraoperative NIRF images clearly highlighted the tumor. The tumor—tumor 1—was removed and, thanks to optical imaging, a smaller one—tumor 2—could be detected and excised. The second tumor would have been very difficult to detect without the probe (Figure 5 and Video S1). It is worth noting that these experiments have been carried out using a set of filters (Ex: 640 nm, Em: > 665 nm), which is not optimal for Wazaby9-Trastu (λmax abs ≈ 690 nm, λmax em ≈ 730 nm in PBS).

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, we succeeded in developing an efficient strategy for the facile construction of the first aza-BODIPY-based bioconjugatable SPECT/OI MOMIP. This bimodal probe possesses all the properties required for performing bimodal in vivo imaging: a strong emission of fluorescence in the NIR-I region (which can be even more exploited using a more suitable set of filters), good water solubility, high chemical and photochemical stability, and no sign of toxicity. It could be stored for a prolonged period and easily be bioconjugated to different antibodies, such as trastuzumab. The resulting bioconjugate is very stable, and the high affinity of trastuzumab for the HER-2 receptors is preserved. The bimodal in vivo imaging experiments highlighted the potential of the compound and the specificity of the bimodal agent for the HER-2 expressing tumors. The study was also completed by a preliminary fluorescence-guided surgery proof of concept, proving the potential of the bimodal probe for future FGS experiments.
    More than just the design of a new bimodal probe, this work enables the development of a new tool, which opens the doors to the facile development of a new MOMIP through the developed strategy of “one pot” bifunctionalization of the Wazaby NIR-emitting platform. Indeed, this platform, which can be readily synthesized at a gram scale, can be conjugated to various antibodies or vectors, labeled with different radionuclides, and/or tethered to other biologically relevant moieties for imaging or even theranostic purposes.
    Thus, we believe that this new probe has real potential for the detection and surgical assistance of HER-2-expressing tumors and that by changing the vector, it can be adapted to do the same for other types of tumors. With regard to the Wazaby7 platform, the strategy developed in this article will allow its utilization for a whole range of uses in optical imaging, but it is likely to consider applications outside the health field.

    Synthesis of Antibody-Labeled Bimodal Imaging Probes

    The humanized anti-HER-2 (trastuzumab, HERCEPTIN) and chimeric anti-CD20 (rituximab, TRUXIMA directed against CD20, a receptor overexpressed in B lymphomas) antibodies were purchased from Roche, France. Antibodies were labeled thanks to the formation of an amide bond between the side chain of lysine amino acids and the ethyl squarate from the bimodal probe. In a first step, trastuzumab and rituximab were purified with HITRAP Desalting columns (GE Healthcare) and concentrated up to 10 mg/mL in PBS. Bioconjugation was optimized to get a degree of labeling (DOL) suitable for imaging purposes while avoiding risks of alteration of the antibodies. On this account, 3 equiv of probe at 5 mM (DMSO) was added to 10 mg of antibodies in 0.1 M NaHCO3/Na2CO3 pH 9 buffer, for a final concentration of antibodies of 2 mg/mL. The reaction mixture was then incubated for 2.5 h at 37 °C, protected from light. The free probe was removed by FPLC using an affinity column for protein A HITRAP MABSELECT (GE Healthcare). A solution of 25 mM acetic acid pH 3 (Sigma Aldrich) was used for elution, and 50 mM ammonium acetate pH 9 (Sigma Aldrich) was added in a final step to obtain a pH 6 solution suitable for the storage of antibodies at 4 °C. The degree of labeling (DOL) was calculated based on absorbance at 280 nm and AUC between 600 and 750 nm. MALDI-TOF analysis was carried out to confirm this result.

    Antibody Stability in Serum

    The Wazaby-DOTA-trastuzumab (Wazaby-9-Trastu) conjugate was analyzed by electrophoresis on a sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel (SDS-PAGE analysis 8% acrylamide gel) in non-reducing (without dithiotreitol (DTT)) or reducing conditions (with DTT and heating at 95 °C for 5 min). The fluorescence analysis of the gel was performed using the ChemiDoc MP imaging system (Bio-Rad) with an IRDye 680 nm filter and ImageLab software (version 6.0, Bio-Rad). To determine the stability of the Wazaby9-Trastu antibody, 2 μg of antibody was incubated in 25 μL of human or mice serum at 37 °C in the dark with gentle agitation. After 0, 24, 48, and 72 h, samples were separated on an SDS-PAGE (8% acrylamide gel) in non-reducing or reducing conditions before fluorescence analysis of the gel, as previously described.

    Biolayer Interferometry

    HER-2 affinity of Wazaby9-Trastu or uncoupled trastuzumab antibodies was determined by biolayer interferometry (BLI) on an OctetRed96e instrument (FortéBio). HER-2 was biotinylated with 5 equiv of biotin (biotin-PEG4-NHS EZ kit, Pierce) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In a second step, the streptavidin-coated biosensor was dipped into wells containing biotinylated HER-2. For the trastuzumab–HER-2 interaction, association and dissociation phases were determined after dipping of the functionalized biosensor (Accrobiosystem) into wells containing different concentrations of trastuzumab coupled or not with Wazaby-DOTA (Wazaby9) (6.25 to 400 nM) or wells containing PBS. Each KD was determined with a 1:1 stoichiometry model using the Octet software (version 3.1, FortéBio, Menlo Park, CA, USA).

    Flow Cytometry

    The Wazaby9-Trastu immunoreactivity was evaluated on HCC1954 cells via flow cytometry analysis. Cells were harvested and incubated for 30 min at 4 °C with 1 μg of Wazaby9-Trastu antibody or a range between 0.001 and 10 μg of this antibody. The staining was achieved after a saturation step of 15 min in FCSB (BD Biosciences, USA). The analysis was carried out on a BD FACSCanto (BD Biosciences, USA). The median fluorescence intensities were analyzed with FlowJo V10 BD software, and a standard curve was deduced with the results obtained for each concentration of labeled antibodies.

    Cytotoxicity assay(32)

    Human liver HepG2 cells were seeded in 96 well plates (5 × 103 cells in Falcon), cultivated for 48 h before treatments in RPMI 1640 medium (Corning) with 10% FBS (Dutcher) before treatment with Wazaby9, Wazaby9-Trastu, or Wazaby9-Ritu at different concentrations (0 to 3 mg/mL) for 36 h in RPMI1640 medium without FBS (200 μL per well). A viability assay was performed using an MTS colorimetric assay (Abcam) following the manufacturer recommendations. Absorbance at 490 nm was read using a microplate reader (Spark, Tecan) after 1 h of incubation at 37 °C and 5% CO2 in the dark.

    Radiolabeling

    111Indium-Radiometallation

    Wazaby9-Trastu in 0.1 M AcONH4 pH 5.8 was incubated with [111In]-InCl3 in 0.05 M HCl (Curium) (600 Mbq/mg of trastuzumab). The mixture was adjusted to pH 5 with 1 M AcONH4 and mixed at 37 °C for 16 h in a Thermomixer. Unincorporated 111In was scavenged by addition of a solution of 0.05 M EDTA in 0.1 M AcONH4 pH 5.6 (1/10th of the reaction volume) and incubated for 15 min. 111In-EDTA was removed by ultrafiltration using an Amicon Ultra 2 (MWCO 30 kDa, Merck Millipore), and the radiolabeled antibody was diluted in 0.9% NaCl prior to injection.
    The radiolabeling yield and the presence of free [111In]-InCl3 in the labeled antibody solution were determined using instant thin layer chromatography-silica gel (ITLC-SG) strips (Agilent). ITLCs were used before and after ultrafiltration to determine the radiolabeling yield and to assess the absence of free indium-111. 1 μL of each solution was deposited on the ITLC-SG strips at 2 cm from the bottom. The eluent (0.1 M sodium citrate pH 5) was allowed to rise to 10 cm from the bottom of the strips. Radiolabeled Wazaby9-Trastu remained at the application point while free indium-111 or 111In-chelates migrated with the solvent front. The strips were then analyzed using an AR-2000 γ-radiochromatograph (Eckert & Ziegler, Germany).
