    Abstract

    Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is a lethal malignancy without safe and effective therapeutic drugs. In this study, the anti-TNBC bioassay-guided isolation of the medicinal plant Croton kongensis followed by the structural modification led to the construction of a small ent-kaurane diterpenoid library (1–25). With subsequent biological screening, 20 highly potent compounds (IC50s < 3 μM) were identified. Among them, 8,9-seco-ent-kaurane 6 displayed comparable activity (IC50s ∼ 80 nM) to doxorubicin but with better selectivity. The analysis of structure–activity relationships suggested that the cleavage of the C8–C9 bond and the presence of α,β-unsaturated ketone moiety were essential for the activity. The mechanistic study revealed that 6 induced apoptosis, autophagy, and metastasis suppression in TNBC cells via inhibition of Akt. In vivo, 6 significantly suppressed the TNBC tumor growth without causing side effects. All these results suggested that 6 may serve as a promising lead for the development of novel anti-TNBC agents in the future.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Breast cancer (BC) is the most common female malignancy and represents the second leading cause of cancer death worldwide.(1) Although most BCs are curable in an early stage with the progress of diagnostic techniques and targeted treatments, a specific subtype of BC, triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), still remains high mortality due to its high metastatic and recurrent rates.(2) TNBC, accounting for 15–20% in diagnosed BCs, is characterized by being devoid of the expression of estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2).(3) Because it lacks the targetable receptors that are expressed in other types of BC and possesses complex pathological processes, treating this notoriously aggressive disease is challenging. Conventional chemotherapeutics, such as anthracyclines and taxanes, are still the primary option for TNBC in clinics.(4) Although these cytotoxic regimens may initially bring certain benefits to TNBC patients, their inevitable toxicity and easily decreased sensitivity (drug resistance) often result in poor clinical outcomes at later stages, with low survival rates and high recurrence rates.(5) Recent progress in TNBC drug discovery shows several promising targeted agents with clear mechanisms and fewer side effects, such as Akt inhibitor Capivasertib, PARP inhibitor Olaparib, and PD-L1 inhibitor Atezolizumab. However, these drugs are still under clinical trials.(6,7) Thus, the discovery of safe and effective therapeutic agents for TNBC is still compelling.
    Natural products have historically been of crucial importance in the identification and development of anticancer agents.(8−10) In the past few years, our group has endeavored to discover anticancer agents from medicinal plants and has proved that Euphorbiaceae plants are valuable sources of structurally intriguing and biologically significant diterpenoids.(11,12) In the current study, the anti-TNBC bioassay-guided isolation of a medicinal Euphorbiaceae plant, Croton kongensis, led to the isolation of a series of ent-kaurane diterpenoids. Subsequent structural modification and biological evaluation allowed the identification of 8,9-seco-ent-kaurane 6 as a promising anti-TNBC candidate. Herein, we report the details of the bioassay-guided isolation, structure elucidation, modification, and SARs of these diterpenoids, as well as the potential action mechanism of 6.

    Anti-TNBC Bioassay-Guided Isolation and Structure Elucidation

    The air-dried powder of the stems of C. kongensis was extracted with 95% EtOH at room temperature. The resulting crude extract was then suspended in H2O and successively partitioned with petroleum ether, EtOAc, and n-butyl alcohol. The EtOAc fraction that displayed promising cytotoxicity against human TNBC cell line MDA-MB-231 (IC50 = 6.8 μg/mL) was further subjected to a series of separations to afford compounds 1–9 (Figure 1). Among them, 1 and 2 are new compounds.
    Compound 1 was obtained as a colorless crystal. The molecular formula C20H30O5 was established by its high-resolution electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (HRESIMS) data at m/z 373.1971 [M + Na]+ (calcd: 373.1985). The 1H and 13C NMR spectra of 1 showed a high resemblance to those of shikoccidin, a known penta-oxygenated ent-kaurane diterpenoid, except for the absence of an acetyl group in shikoccidin.(13) Detailed two-dimensional (2D) NMR analyses (1H–1H COSY, HSQC, and HMBC) revealed that 1 bore a hydroxyl group at C-1 instead of an acetoxy at C-3 in shikoccidin (Figure S1). The relative configuration of 1 was established by the NOESY experiment as well as comparison of its one-dimensional (1D) NMR data with those of shikoccidin. Particularly, the β-orientation of 1-OH was assigned by the NOE correlations of H3-20/H-1, Hα-6, and H-14 (Figure S2). Finally, the structure including the absolute configuration of 1 was confirmed by a single-crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis [flack parameter, 0.05 (7)] (Figure 2). Compound 1 was given a trivial name crokongendin A.
    Compound 2 had the molecular formula C22H28O5, as established by HRESIMS (m/z 373.2001 [M + H]+, calcd 373.2010). The 1H and 13C NMR data (Table S1) of 2 were similar to those of taibaihenryiin C, a known ent-abietane diterpenoid, with the notable differences being the presence of an α,β-unsaturated ketone moiety and the shifted location of the acetoxy group in 2. A detailed 2D NMR analysis (Figure S1) further assigned the α,β-unsaturated ketone and acetoxy groups at rings B and C-1, respectively. The stereochemistry of 2 was found to be the same as that of taibaihenryiin C14 by the NOE experiment and comparison of their 1D NMR data. Therefore, compound 2 was assigned as depicted and was named crokongendin B.
    The known compounds, euphoranginone D (3),(15)ent-kaurane-3-oxo-16β,17-diol (4),(16)ent-16β-H-3-oxokauran-17-ol (5),(17) kongensin D (6),(18) kongeniod B (7),(14) kongensin A (8),(18) and kongensin F (9),(19) were identified by comparison of their spectroscopic data with those in the literature studies.

    Structural Modification

    As compounds 6–9 represent a rare 8,9-seco-ent-kaurane skeleton and 6 could be sustainably transformed from ent-kaurane 1 via the retro-aldol reaction, the structural modification was started from 6 and 8 with available amounts. Briefly, the α,β-unsaturated ketone moiety, the substituents on C-7 and C-1, and the formation of the C8–C9 bond were investigated to explore the influences of these kaurane-characterized functional groups on the activity. As shown in Scheme 1, the acylation of 7-OH in 6 with ethanoyl chloride, benzoyl chloride, chloroacetyl chloride, propionyl chloride, acrylyl chloride, and 3-butenoyl chloride in triethyl amine and freshly distilled CH2Cl2 afforded 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, and 18, respectively. The acylation of 8 with acryl chloride and chloroacetyl chloride under the same conditions yielded 16 and 17, respectively. The oxidation of 1-OH in 6 with the Dess–Martin reagent in CH2Cl2 gave ketone 19, which underwent acetylation, benzoylation, and chloroacetylation to afford 20, 21, and 22, respectively. The oxidation of the double bond in 6 with m-CPBA in CH2Cl2 afforded 8,14-epoxide 23. In addition, Pd/C-catalyzed hydrogenation of 6 in EtOAc and CH3OH resulted in the generation of 24 and 25, respectively.

    Effects of Diterpenoids on Breast Cancer Cell Lines

    The cytotoxicity of the obtained diterpenoids was measured on human TNBC cell lines MDA-MB-231 (mesenchymal-like subtype) and MDA-MB-468 (basal-like I subtype) and the estrogen receptor positive BC cell line MCF-7 by the MTT method. As shown in Table 1, most of the compounds (1, 2, and 6–23) exhibited potent cytotoxicity (IC50s < 3 μM) against all cell lines and were generally more effective on TNBC cells than on MCF-7. Among these diterpenoids, 8,9-seco-ent-kaurane 6 displayed the most pronounced activity on both TNBC cells (IC50s ∼ 80 nM), being comparable to the first-line drug doxorubicin (Dox).

    Structure–Activity Relationships

    In general, 8,9-seco-ent-kaurane diterpenoids showed better activity than the ent-kaurane ones, suggesting that the cleavage of the C8–C9 bond was beneficial to the activity. ent-Kaureane 1 was an exception, probably due to its easy transformation to 8,9-seco-ent-kaurane 6 in the solution state (Scheme 1). Previous studies showed that ent-kauranes with good anti-TNBC activities normally featured an oxygen bridge between C-20 and C-6 or C-7.(20,21) For 8,9-seco-ent-kaurane type, the structural optimization was deployed on four regions, 1-OH, 7-OH, Δ8,14, and Δ16. The epoxidation of Δ8,14 or hydrogenation of Δ16 led to a dramatic decrease of the activity, as shown by 6 vs 23 and 25, suggesting that the conjugated system in the exo-methylene cyclopentanone moiety was essential for the activity. The presence of 7-OH was favorable for the activity, as the acylation or etherification of this hydroxyl led to a decrease of the activity, as shown by 6 vs 7 and 10–15, 8 vs 9, 16 and 17, and 19 vs 20–22. Within the 7-OH modification group, the presence of an ether group showed a better activity than the ester ones, as shown by 7 vs 10–15, and 9 vs 16 and 17. Similarly, the presence of 1-OH was beneficial to the activity, as the oxidation or acylation of 1-OH led to ca. 3-fold decrease in activity, as shown by 6 vs 8 and 19. Overall, the free hydroxyls at C-1 and C-7 were considered to be positive factors for the activity, and the α,β-unsaturated ketone moiety was the key for cytotoxicity.

    As a promising anti-TNBC agent, the selectivity of 6 between the cancer cells and normal cells was evaluated. As shown in Table 2, the cytotoxicity of 6 on human normal breast epithelial cell line MCF-10A, human embryonic kidney cell line HEK-293, and human normal liver cell line LO2 indicated its good selectivity over the TNBC cell lines, with the selectivity index (SI) values being at 14, 26 or greater than 66, respectively. Comparison with that of Dox, the superior SI of 6 suggested its safer and more-tolerated potential.

    Compound 6 Suppressed Proliferation of TNBC Cells

    To evaluate the antiproliferative potential of 6 on TNBC cells, the colony formation assay was carried out. As shown in Figure 3, the colony numbers of TNBC cells were dose-dependently decreased with the treatment of 6 in 2 weeks, suggesting that 6 could suppress the long-term proliferative capacity of TNBC cells.

    Compound 6 Induced TNBC G2/M Cell Cycle Arrest and Regulated the Expression of G2/M-Related Proteins

    To explore the underlying mechanism of 6, the regulation of 6 on the cell cycle was first evaluated. The TNBC cells were treated with various concentrations of 6, and the cell cycle distribution was tested by flow cytometry. As illustrated in Figure 4A, 6 effectively arrested cells in the G2/M phase in a dose-dependent manner. Since cyclin B1/CDK1 complex plays a key role in promoting the progression of G2/M transition,(22) and it is mainly inhibited by the CDK inhibitor p21,(23) the effects of 6 on the expression of cyclin B1, CDK1, and p21 were further evaluated by western blot. As shown in Figure 4B, in 6-treated TNBC cells, the expression of p21 was significantly upregulated while the expression of cyclin B1 and CDK1 were downregulated, suggesting that 6 induced TNBC cell G2/M arrest via regulation of these cell-cycle-related proteins.

    Compound 6 Induced Apoptosis via Mitochondrial Depolarization and Regulation of Apoptosis-Related Proteins

    According to the above-mentioned cell cycle analysis, we supposed that 6 might induce the apoptosis of TNBC cells. To confirm this, TNBC cells were treated with various concentrations of 6 for 24 h, and then, the cells were stained with annexin V–FITC/ propidium iodide (PI) and analyzed by flow cytometry. As shown in Figure 5A, the percentages of early and late apoptotic cells were dose-dependently increased with the treatment of 6 in 24 h, suggesting that 6 induced the apoptosis of TNBC cells. As irreversible mitochondrial depolarization is the main pathway in apoptosis, which could be reflected by the decrease of the mitochondrial membrane potential (MMP, ΔΨm),(24) the changes of MMP were detected by JC-10 staining of mitochondria in TNBC cells.(25) As shown in Figure 5B, the cells in control groups had well-defined integral mitochondrial membranes, with aggregates (cells with high MMP) accounting for the predominant proportion (>90%) in JC-10 stained cells. When treated with various concentrations of 6, the mitochondrial membranes of TNBC cells were permeabilized and the proportion of monomers (cells with low MMP) were dose-dependently increased. These results suggested that 6 could induce a dramatic depletion of MMP, leading to apoptosis. Mitochondrial membrane permeabilization is a switchlike event regulated by the balance of anti-apoptotic protein bcl2 and pro-apoptotic protein bax, and the dynamic change of bcl2 and bax can lead to mitochondrial depolarization. When mitochondrial depolarization occurs, caspase 3 is cleaved into the activated form c-caspase 3, which further cleaves PARP to form c-PARP with the loss of DNA repairing function.(24) Thus, the influence of 6 on the upstream or downstream regulators of MMP was further investigated. As shown in Figure 5C, 6 decreased the ratios of bcl2/bax and significantly increased c-caspase 3 and c-PARP, suggesting that 6 could induce apoptosis via regulation of these cell death-related proteins.

    Compound 6 Induced Autophagy to Promote Apoptosis

    Accumulating evidence suggests that apoptosis is deeply involved with autophagy and their comprehensive crosstalk can lead to synergism or antagonism on cell death.(26) Therefore, the influence of 6 on autophagy was further investigated. In the autophagy process, ATG5, ATG7, and the cleaved form of LC3 (LC3-II) play important roles in the elongation and closure of the autophagosome membrane, and SQSTM1 (p62) is an adapter protein that recognizes ubiquitinated autophagic cargos and links them to the autophagic vesicles.(27,28) When autophagosomes are transported to lysosomes, the p62 and cargos are degraded and the autophagic flux is completed. Thus, the regulation of these autophagy-related proteins together could be used to estimate the process of autophagy. As shown in Figure 6A, 6 dose-independently increased ATG5, ATG7, and LC3-II expression and almost eliminated p62 in high dose group, suggesting that 6 induced autophagy in TNBC cells. To understand the interplay between apoptosis and autophagy, the apoptosis process was investigated under the blockage of autophagy by using chloroquine, a lysosomal inhibitor.(29,30) As shown in Figure 6B, when cells were treated with chloroquine alone, both LC3-II and p62 were increased because of the incomplete autophagic flux. When the cells were treated with the combination of 6 and chloroquine, the apoptotic related protein c-PARP was substantially decreased as compared to the 6-only group, indicating that the blockage of autophagy could attenuate the 6-induced apoptosis in TNBC cells. Taken together, 6 induced autophagy to promote apoptosis in TNBC cells.

    Compound 6 Inhibited the Migration and Invasion of MDA-MB-231 Cells

    One reason for the poor prognosis of TNBC is the high incidence of metastasis. To investigate whether 6 could inhibit the TNBC metastasis, the antimigration and anti-invasion potentials of 6 were evaluated by scratch assay and transwell invasion assay on the highly metastatic MDA-MB-231 cells. As shown in the scratch assay (Figure 7A), the wound width of the control group was gradually diminishing along with time, while the treatment of 6 significantly restrained the migration in a dose-dependent manner. Similarly, in the transwell invasion assay (Figure 7B), the number of invaded cells was dose-dependently decreased by the treatment of 6, with ca. 80% reduction in the high dose group (0.2 μM) as compared to the control group. As the matrix metalloproteinase family is considered as the main promoter in cancer cell metastasis, and it is mainly activated by β-catenin,(31) the regulation of 6 on the expression of β-catenin and the key matrix metalloproteinase members MMP2 and MMP9 were further evaluated by western blot. As shown in Figure 7C, the treatment of 6 dose-dependently reduced the expression of β-catenin, MMP2, and MMP9 in MDA-MB-231 cells, confirming the antimetastatic potential of 6 in TNBC cells.

    Compound 6 Inhibited Akt Signaling in TNBC Cells

    Accumulating evidence suggests that apoptosis, autophagy, and metastasis in TNBC are closely related to the aberrant activation of the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)/protein kinase B (Akt)/mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR) signaling.(32) Akt is the key regulator in this pathway with over a hundred substrates. Activated Akt can phosphorylate p21, leading to negative regulation of the cell-growth-inhibiting activity of p21. Activated Akt also phosphorylates the pro-apoptotic proteins in the bcl-2 family to inhibit their function. Meanwhile, the activation of Akt can suppress the autophagic flux and maintain the accumulation of β-catenin for cell migration and invasion.(33) Since 6 could affect the above-mentioned regulators involving cell cycle arrest, apoptosis, autophagy, and metastasis in TNBC, its possible influence on PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling was further explored.
    First, the expression levels of PI3K, phosphorylated PI3K (p-PI3K), Akt, phosphorylated Akt (p-Akt), mTOR, and phosphorylated mTOR (p-mTOR) in TNBC cells were evaluated by western blot analysis. As shown in Figure 8A, the levels of p-Akt and its downstream p-mTOR were significantly decreased with the treatment of 6 in a dose-dependent manner, while the level of its upstream p-PI3K was not changed. Meanwhile, 6 had no influence on the expression of PI3K, Akt, and mTOR. These results suggested that 6 might affect the p-PI3K downstream phosphorylation cascades.(33) Then, the kinase assay of PI3K was performed by ADP-Glo assay. The results showed that 6 did not affect the PI3K activities by competitively binding to the sites where p-PI3K interacts with its substrates (Figure S4). Therefore, we supposed that 6 might bind to the allosteric region of Akt to prevent the membrane interaction or inter-domain conformational changes necessary for Akt activation.(34,35) Akt is composed of three isoforms, named Akt1, Akt2, and Akt3, in which Akt1 is the main isoform responsible for cancer cell proliferation and survival.(36) Then the ligand–protein interaction between 6 and Akt1 was assessed in the native cellular environment using the cellular thermal shift assay (CETSA).(37) As shown in Figure 8B, 6 significantly shifted the melting curves of Akt1 in TNBC cells and led to thermal stabilization of Akt1, suggesting that 6 could directly bind to Akt1.
    To investigate whether 6 exerted its anti-TNBC effect mainly through Akt inhibition, Akt1 knockdown experiments were performed by siRNA in TNBC cells (Figure 8C). As shown in Figure 8D, the cytotoxicity of 6 was remarkably decreased in Akt1-silenced TNBC cells, with the cell viability being 30–40% greater than that of the CsiRNA group (negative control). These results suggested that 6 exerted the anti-TNBC effect via the inhibition of Akt.
    In addition, there is a crosstalk between PI3K/Akt signaling and the extracellular-regulated kinase (ERK) signaling. When Akt phosphorylation is suppressed, ERK phosphorylation might be provoked, resulting in the attenuation of therapeutic efficacy.(38,39) To investigate whether 6 could regulate the ERK signaling, the expression of ERK and p-ERK was evaluated by western blot. As shown in Figure 8A, 6 had little influence on both ERK and p-ERK, suggesting that 6 might bring a promising therapeutic benefit.

    Compound 6 Suppressed TNBC Xenograft Tumor Growth in NOD/SCID Mice

    To determine the in vivo antitumor efficacy of 6, TNBC xenograft mouse models were developed by subcutaneously injecting MDA-MB-468 cells into the female NOD/SCID mice. After the tumors were grown to 50 mm3 in 7 days, 32 mice were equally randomly divided into four groups: vehicle group, low-dose group (5 mg/kg 6 per day), high-dose group (10 mg/kg 6 per day), and positive control group (5 mg/kg doxorubicin per week). The tumor volume was monitored during the intraperitoneally injected treatments until 33 days, and the tumors and organ tissues were harvested for further analysis. As shown in Figure 9A,C,D, 6 dose-dependently suppressed the growth of TNBC xenograft tumors, with obvious reduction in the tumor weight (reduced by 58%) and volume (reduced by 44.8%) in the high-dose group as compared to the vehicle group. Meanwhile, there was no significant difference in the body weight between the vehicle group and the 6-treated groups (Figure 9B). The result of the positive control group was not obtained because the mice successively died from the 9th day to the 16th day with visible weight loss and sluggish behavior (Figure S5), probably being due to the intrinsic cytotoxicity of Dox. In addition, the potential cytotoxicity of 6 was evaluated by histological analysis of the heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney tissues of the high-dose group. As shown in Figure 9E, no abnormality was observed in these tissues, implying that 6 was more safe and tolerable than Dox with the same dosage.
    The inhibition of 6 on TNBC progression was further confirmed by immunohistochemical analysis of several biomarkers in tumor tissues. Ki67, a cell cycle nuclear nonhistone protein that regulates the cell circle, is a proliferative and prognostic marker in breast cancer.(40) CD31, the platelet-endothelial cell adhesion molecule involved in blood vessel formation, is used as a biomarker detecting the tumor microvessel density.(41) TdT-mediated dUTP nick end labeling (TUNEL) is used to mark DNA strand breaks generated during apoptosis, and LC3B acts as an autophagic indicator. As shown in Figure 10, compared with the control group, the treatment of 6 led to a large area of necrosis in tumor tissues, and the stains of Ki67 and CD31 were dose-dependently decreased, while the stains of TUNEL and LC3B were accordingly increased. Collectively, these findings demonstrated that 6 induced apoptosis and autophagy in TNBC tumor cells and inhibited tumor angiogenesis, eventually leading to tumor necrosis.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    TNBC is a highly malignant subtype of breast cancer without safe and effective therapeutic drugs. In the current study, a small diterpenoid library was constructed by bioassay-guided chemical investigation of the medicinal plant C. kongensis. A group of rare 8,9-seco-ent-kaurane diterpenoids were identified as potential anti-TNBC agents by subsequent biological screening. Among them, 6 showed promising anti-TNBC efficacy in vitro and in vivo, being comparable to Dox but with better tolerance. The mechanistic study revealed that 6 was an Akt inhibitor, which could induce apoptosis, autophagy, cell G2/M circle arrest, and inhibit cell migration and invasion.     Cytotoxicity Assay

    Cell cytotoxicity was measured using the MTT method. Briefly, cells were plated in 96-well plates (1 × 103 cells/well) for 24 h. Later, the attached cells were treated with different concentrations of compounds for 96 h. After the cells were incubated with 5 mg/mL MTT (Millipore) for another 4 h, the suspension was discarded and, subsequently, the dark blue crystals (formazan) were dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) (Millipore). Then, the absorbance at 570 nm was measured using a FlexStation 3 multifunction microplate reader (Molecular Devices). For the cells placed in 12-well plates and 24-well plates, cell viability was measured by survival cell counting. The cell viability was finally calculated as the percentage compared to the control group.

    Cell Cycle Assay

    Cells were plated in 12-well plates (1 × 105 cells/well) for 24 h and incubated with or without different concentrations of 6 for another 24 h. The cell cycle assay was performed according to the instruction of the Cell Cycle Detection Kit (KeyGenBioTech, China) and analyzed by Cytomics TM FC 500 flow cytometry (Beckman Coulter).

    Colony Formation Assay

    Cells were plated in 6-well plates (1000 cells/well) for 24 h and later treated with or without different concentrations of compound 6. After 2 weeks, cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde (Biosharp, China) for 10 min, and then stained with crystal violet solution (Beyotime, China). The number of colonies was counted.

    Annexin-V-FITC/PI Dual Staining

    Cells were plated in 12-well plates (1 × 105 cells/well) for 24 h and incubated with or without different concentrations of 6 for another 24 h.     Mitochondrial Membrane Potential Analysis

    Scratch Assay
