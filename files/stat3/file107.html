    Abstract

    Peptide modification with fatty acids is an effective method to improve peptide performance. We previously investigated the fatty acid modification of R-lycosin-I, a cytotoxic peptide derived from lycosin-I from the venom of the spider Lycosa singoriensis. In this study, we further investigated the position effects of fatty acid modification of lycosin-I. Dodecanoic acid was covalently coupled to the α/ε-amino group of one of the seven Lys residues of lycosin-I, generating eight different lipopeptides. Although all the lipopeptides had significantly improved cytotoxicity compared with lycosin-I, they displayed different cytotoxic potencies and profiles, which might be explained by multifactors including charge, size, helicity, hydrophobicity, and so forth. Of the eight lipopeptides, L-C12 demonstrated highest cytotoxicity and antimetastasis activity in two-dimensional cells, tumor spheroids, subcutaneous transplantation mouse models, and experimental melanoma metastasis mouse models. Collectively, our finding indicated that fatty acid modification position plays important roles in physiochemical parameters and biological activities of cytotoxic peptides.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In spite of tremendous advances in medical service and treatment modalities, cancer remains a major cause of mortality worldwide.(1) The prominent cause of cancer death is malignant cell metastasis to distant anatomical locations.(2) Therefore, it is urgent to exploit novel anticancer drugs which not only have powerful toxicity to cancer cells but also own highly tumor metastatic inhibition. Anticancer peptides (ACPs) and their analogues have been regarded as good candidates to treat cancer with potential cytotoxicity and antimetastasis activity. Melittin, a linear toxic peptide derived from the bee venom of Apis mellifera, prevents liver cancer cell proliferation and metastasis through inhibition of the Rac1-dependent pathway.(3) Furthermore, in breast cancer, melittin can suppress EGF-induced cell motility and invasion by inhibiting PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway.(4) Nevertheless, there are some drawbacks in ACPs, such as low activity to solid tumors and poor serum stability, which impede their application in the development of anticancer drugs.(5) To improve their prospect in therapeutic applications, many analogues have been designed and synthesized. One of the most important strategy is the conjugation of peptides with some promising materials including photothermal materials,(6,7) carbohydrates,(8) fatty acis,(9) etc. This strategy will aggregate the advantages of ACPs including high penetrability and targeting ability and of conjugation materials including strong stability, photothermal effect, etc. For example, conjugating the cytotoxic peptide lycosin-I with gold nanoparticles was found to improve selectivity against cancer cells and possess great potential in photothermal therapy.(10) In addition, by the rapid precipitation method, the transformable nanomaterials (1-NPs), which are composed of a hydrophobic bis-pyrene unit and two short linear targeting peptides, show high tumor invasion and metastasis inhibition.(11)
    Fatty acids, a group of critical nutrients, modulate many vital movement as well as many diseases such as cardiovascular disease, cancer, and diabetes.(12,13) Post-translational modification of proteins plays an important role in vivo, and acetylation is an important form in this modification. Acetylation of histone, cytoskeletal, and tumor suppressor proteins (e.g., P53) can regulate the processes of gene transcription, cell movement, tumorigenesis, etc.(14) Furthermore, fatty acid modification in vitro is also a promising strategy for the optimization of peptide function because of its strong hydrophobicity and stability. Fatty acid-modified peptides are mainly used for the following purposes. (1) Increasing peptide stability: N-terminal fatty acid-modified antimicrobial peptide analogues presented high stability in the presence of protease or serum.(15) In addition, myristic acid was conjugated with a lysine side chain in the α-chain of insulin to achieve the sufficient stability.(16) (2) Improving peptide-mediated molecule delivery.(17,18) Lehto et al. conjugated PepFect14 with acyl chain with lengths from 2 to 22 carbons to optimize oligonucleotide complexes for lowest toxicity and keep the delivery efficiency.(19) (3) Enhancing peptide cytotoxic activity. For instance, coupling the aliphatic acids with different lengths to B1 endows all conjugates with improved antitumor activity.(20)
    In our previous study, five fatty acids with different lengths were covalently coupled to the N-terminal of R-lycosin-I, which was derived from lycosin-I by amino acid substitution, and we investigated the effect of fatty acid lengths on the cytotoxicity and stability of R-lycosin-I. It was found that fatty acid conjugation actually improves the performance of the peptide. However, with increased fatty acid chain lengths, the peptide–fatty acid conjugates demonstrate gradually decreased cytotoxicity despite their increased hydrophobicity, and the highest cytotoxicity is resulted from dodecanoic acid modification. These data indicate that fatty acid modification not only changes the hydrophobicity of the peptide but also changes other physiochemical parameters, such as charge, size, helicity, etc., and these parameters affect the peptide activity in a synergistic way.(9)
    In the present study, we would further investigate the position effect of fatty acid modification on the performance of lycosin-I, as we supposed that the fatty acid modification position in the peptide might also be a crucial factor in the peptide optimization. According to our previous study, dodecanoic acid was used to covalently couple to different sites in lycosin-I through its α/ε-amino group of a Lys residue. Lycosin-I consists of seven Lys residues, and therefore, eight lipopeptides were obtained, which were named L-C12, L-C12-1, L-C12-2, L-C12-3, L-C12-4, L-C12-5, L-C12-6, and L-C12-7. We determined their physiochemical properties including particle size, zeta potential, secondary structure, and hydrophobicity, as well as their bioactivities including cytotoxicity, antimetastasis, etc. These data indicated that the dodecanoic acid modification at all the positions of lycosin-I was helpful for promoting the peptide cytotoxicity, but the eight lipopeptides showed different cytotoxic potencies because the modification at different positions might lead to distinct alterations of physiochemical properties. Furthermore, the most optimized lipopeptide L-C12 exhibited potential cytotoxicity and antimetastasis in vitro and in vivo, providing a good lead for anticancer drug development.

    Dodecanoic Acid Conjugation to Lycosin-I at Different Positions

    Lycosin-I was synthesized on a Rink amide-AM resin using the Fmoc solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) method.(11) As shown in Scheme 1, during the synthesis of peptide backbone, Fmoc-Lys (Dde)-OH was used for the fatty acid modification at certain positions. After synthesizing the peptide chain, the Dde group deprotection was selectively performed by using 5 mL of 2% hydrazine hydrate/dimethylformamide (DMF) (v/v), and the procedure was repeated twice for 15 min each time. Dodecanoic acid (0.5 mM) was dissolved in HATU (0.5 mM) and HOBT (0.5 mM) by using 5 mL of 5% NMM/DMF and then reacted with the Dde group-deprotected peptides in the reaction vessel for 1 h. Peptides were lysed by using trifluoroacetic acid (TFA)/1,2-ethanedithiol/thioanisole/anisole (90/5/3/2 (v/v)) and, after 2 h, precipitated in cold diethyl ether. As shown in Figure S1, the crude products were purified by using RP-HPLC (reversed-phase high-pressure liquid chromatography) (column, C18, 300 Å, 10 × 250 mm; Welch Materials, Inc.) on the basis of 0.1% TFA/acetonitrile (ACN) gradient from 0 to 80% in 48 min. The purified products were determined by using matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS) (AB, SCIEX). All the lipopeptides had the same average molecular mass of 2914.69 Da, which was consistent with the theoretical relative molecular mass and indicated that all the lipopeptides were successfully synthesized (Figure S1). The lipopeptides were named L-C12, L-C12-1, L-C12-2, L-C12-3, L-C12-4, L-C12-5, L-C12-6, and L-C12-7 based on the coupling position of dodecanoic acid (Scheme 1).

    Cytotoxicity of Lipopeptides

    Human lung carcinoma cells (A549) and noncancer cells (HEK-293T) were chosen to evaluate the cytotoxic activity of lycosin-I and eight lipopeptides by the CCK-8 assay. The potencies were calculated as IC50 values, which represent the concentration of the peptides that induce 50% growth inhibition. As shown in Figure 1A, all the lipopeptides exhibited greatly improved cytotoxic activity (about 4–15 times) on cancer cells compared to lycosin-I. Interestingly, conjugation of dodecanoic acid to different positions of lycosin-I resulted in varied cytotoxic activities of lipopeptides. The cytotoxicity of eight lipopeptides to HEK293T cells also indicated that all the lipopeptides showed greatly enhanced cytotoxicity but did not show selectivity (Figure S2). L-C12 showed highest cytotoxicity among the eight lipopeptides, and it was chosen in further experiments. As shown in Table 1, besides A549 and HEK-293T cells, for the other five cancer cell lines (U87, HeLa, MCF-7, SW620, and PC-3) and a noncancer cell line (L-02), the IC50 values of lycosin-I were approximately 11–26 and 42 μM, respectively, but the IC50 values of L-C12 were 1.7–2.8 μM for cancer or noncancer cell lines. These results further confirmed that dodecanoic acid conjugation enhanced the cytotoxic activity of lycosin-I. Accordingly, the eight lipopeptides could be classified into three groups: (1) L-C12, demonstrating the most cytotoxic activity (∼2 μM); (2) L-C12-1, L-C12-4, L-C12-5, L-C12-6, and L-C12-7, showing moderate activity (3–5 μM); and (3) L-C12-2 and L-C12-3, displaying the lowest activity (7–8 μM). These results indicated that the site of dodecanoic acid modification was important to evaluate the cytotoxic activity of lipopeptides. Trypan blue staining, which is often used to detect cell survival, was also conducted to validate the data obtained from the CCK-8 assay (Figure S3).

    L-C12 Induces Cell Apoptosis and Inhibits Cell Proliferation

    It was found that L-C12 could penetrate cell membrane because fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)-labeled L-C12 was observed in the cytoplasm and nucleus of A549 cells at a concentration of 2 μM (Figure 1B). The fluorescent experiment also showed that L-C12 exhibited stronger penetration ability than lycosin-I, which might be correlated with their cytotoxic potentials. When the temperature in which A549 cells were treated was lowered from 37 to 4 °C, a significant decrease in the cytotoxicity of L-C12 was found (Figure 1C), indicating that L-C12 might be internalized by endocytosis.(21) Next, we wondered if L-C12 triggered cell apoptosis in a way similar to lycosin-I.(22) As shown in Figure 1D, the annexin V-FITC and propidium iodide (PI) dual-staining assay indicated that 2 μM L-C12 resulted in approximately 47.07% apoptosis of A549 cells. However, as for 2 μM lycosin-I, no obvious apoptotic effect on A549 cells was observed. As compared with control and 2 μM lycosin-I, 2 μM L-C12 led to the activation of caspase-3 in A549 cells (Figure 1E), indicating that caspase-3 signaling pathway was involved in L-C12-induced cell apoptosis.
    In addition, L-C12 also had proliferation inhibition activity as revealed by colony formation assay (Figure 1F,G). L-C12 caused a strong inhibition of colony formation in A549 cells in a dose-dependent manner. Interestingly, a potent colony formation inhibition was observed in the treatment of L-C12 at a concentration of 0.25 μM, which was just 8 times lower than the IC50 value of L-C12 against A549 cells. However, lycosin-I-treated A549 cells demonstrated minor cell proliferation inhibition even at a concentration up to 10 μM. These data indicated that L-C12 exhibited dual effects on A549 cells. It could cause cell death at high concentrations (e.g., 2 μM) and inhibit cell proliferation at low concentrations (e.g., 0.25 μM).

    L-C12 Causes Direct Cell Membrane Disruption

    Many studies indicated that membrane disruption is the main mechanism of ACPs and their analogues inducing cell death.(23) Acridine orange (AO)/ethidium bromide (EB) double staining was employed to detect if lycosin-I and L-C12 had such an effect. As shown in Figure 2A, in the presence of 4 μM lycosin-I or L-C12, most of the A549 cells turned red in the 4 μM L-C12 treatment group, but they were still colored in green in the 4 μM lycosin-I or control group, which indicated that L-C12 treatment could directly disrupt cell membrane as EB can only stain permeabilized cells as red. Furthermore, the membrane-lytic effect of L-C12 was also visualized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) after A549 cells were treated with 4 μM L-C12 for 24 h. Plump spindle cell morphology was observed in untreated or lycosin-I-treated A549 cells (Figure 2B). In contrast, cells treated with 4 μM L-C12 demonstrated a shrunken morphology with membrane lysis. Cell permeabilization might lead to release of lactate dehydrogenase (LDH). Actually, LDH release assay has been widely used to measure membrane integrity.(24) As shown in Figure 2C, L-C12 led to obvious LDH release from A549 cells. For instance, 3.1 μM L-C12 caused 55% LDH release, but 25 μM lycosin-I leads to only 37% LDH release. Thus, these results corroborated that L-C12 could exert strong membrane-lytic activity. In addition, the membrane-lytic activities of the other seven lipopeptides were also examined by LDH release assay, indicating that these eight lipopeptides exhibited different LDH release abilities ranked as L-C12 ≈ L-C12-1 > L-C12-4 ≈ L-C12-5 ≈ L-C12-6 ≈ L-C12-7 > L-C12-2 ≈ L-C12-3 (Table 2). These data were further confirmed by erythrocyte hemolysis assay (Figure S4). The membrane-lytic abilities of the eight lipopeptides are positively correlated with their cytotoxic potencies, which also confirmed that besides cell apoptosis, direct membrane lysis might be the main action manner by which these lipopeptides induced cell death.

    L-C12 Enhances the Serum Stability

    It is accepted that fatty acid modification can improve peptide serum stability, which is an important factor for long-term efficacy of a drug.(25) In this study, we wondered if dodecanoic acid modification could increase the serum stability of lycosin-I. Mouse serum treatment and MALDI-TOF MS analysis(26,27) indicated that L-C12 remained detectable after serum incubation for 24 h, while lycosin-I was quickly degraded in mouse serum and no longer detected after 24 h of serum incubation (Figure 3A,B). To further verify this result, we used the 10% fetal bull serum (FBS) to imitate the hemato-microenvironment and performed the serum stability assay.(28,29) As expected, preincubation of L-C12 with 10% FBS for 48 h would not reduce the cytotoxicity of L-C12 on A549 cells, while lycosin-I had no such effect (Figure 3C,D), which indicated that L-C12 but not lycosin-I had the resistance to the enzyme degradation and remains intact in the serum.

    Physicochemical Characteristics of Lipopeptides

    In order to reveal the underlying reasons of different cytotoxic potencies of the eight lipopeptides, physicochemical characteristics such as size, charge, and hydrophobicity were determined because they play important roles in the cytotoxic activities of ACPs. As shown in Table 2, the overall order of hydrophobicity is L-C12 > L-C12-6 ≈ L-C12-1 > L-C12-7 ≈ L-C12-3 ≈ L-C12-2 > L-C12-5 ≈ L-C12-4 > lycosin-I. Note that the hydrophobicity of a certain peptide was represented by retention time (tR) by RP-HPLC at room temperature. The zeta potentials and hydrodynamic sizes were ranked as L-C12 > L-C12-3 ≈ L-C12-5 ≈ L-C12-7 > L-C12-4 > L-C12-1 ≈ L-C12-2 ≈ L-C12-6 > lycosin-I and lycosin-I > L-C12 ≈ L-C12-1 ≈ L-C12-2 ≈ L-C12-3 > L-C12-4 ≈ L-C12-5 ≈ L-C12-6 > L-C12-7, respectively. In addition, all the peptides adopted random coil conformation in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and α-helical conformation in 50% trifluoroethylene as determined by circular dichroism (CD) spectrum (Figure S5).
    ACPs with net positive charges possess high affinity and rapid binding to cancer cell membranes. Upon membrane binding, hydrophobic interactions between peptides and membrane lipids become dominant, and this process is strengthened by the conformational transition of ACPs (e.g., from disordered to helical secondary structures) and the consequent formation of amphipathic structures, finally resulting in membrane permeabilization.(30) Therefore, the process is affected by the following factors. (1) The electrostatic attraction between the negatively charged components of cancer cell membranes(31) and the cationic peptides plays an important role in peptide binding.(32) (2) Hydrophobicity is an essential factor for ACPs interacting with membranes because it controls the extent to which ACPs can partition into the membrane layer.(33) (3) The internalization capability of larger particles is much lower than that of smaller ones in general.(34) Furthermore, amphipathic helical conformation is often required to the insertion or penetration of ACPs into cell membranes.(35)
    Generally speaking, increased net positive charge, reduced hydrodynamic size, high hydrophobicity, and helicity would contribute to higher cytotoxic activities of ACPs, which might well explain that all the lipopeptides showed much stronger cytotoxic activities than lycosin-I. As described above, the eight lipopeptides could be classified into three groups based on their cytotoxic potencies, which might be partly explained by alteration of the physiochemical parameters caused by the conjugation of dodecanoic acid to the different positions of lycosin-I. L-C12 demonstrated the strongest cytotoxic activity (about 2 μM) because the dodecanoic acid conjugation to the N-terminal amino of lycosin-I actually endowed this lipopeptide the strongest hydrophobicity and zeta potential among the eight lipopeptides. Although the hydrodynamic size of L-C12 is the largest, considering direct membrane disruption was the main way of L-C12 inducing cell death, and therefore, net positive charges and hydrophobicity might contribute more to cytotoxicity than to hydrodynamic size. For the other lipopeptides, their increased extent of net positive charge or hydrophobicity was lower than that of L-C12, and therefore, they exhibited lower cytotoxic activities than L-C12. L-C12-2 had the lowest cytotoxicity because its net positive charge and hydrophobicity were relatively lower than those of other lipopeptides. Although its hydrophobicity seemed stronger than that of L-C12-4 or L-C12-5, its net positive charge was much smaller than that of the two lipopeptides, which might lead to reduced binding to cell membranes. L-C12-3 was an exception that it exhibited relatively strong hydrophobicity and zeta potential, but its cytotoxic activity is low. These data indicated that these physiochemical properties might be key factors affecting the cytotoxic activities of ACPs, but other factors might also be involved in the regulation of their activities and multiple parameters might act in a synergic way.

    L-C12 Inhibits the Migration and Invasion of A549 Cells

    Lycosin-I may inhibit cancer cell migration though inactivating STAT3 pathway.(36) We wondered if L-C12 could suppress cancer migration under noncytotoxic concentrations. Because 1 μM L-C12 had no obvious effect on the viability of A549 cells (Figure 1A), we choose 1 μM lycosin-I and L-C12 to conduct a series of experiments including transwell migration and invasion assays. As shown in Figure 4A,C, the antimigration effect of L-C12 was dose-dependent. Compared to control, a rare quantity of A549 cells was observed on the poly ethylene terephthalate membrane in the 1 μM L-C12 treatment group. Even at the concentration of 0.5 μM, L-C12 still significantly reduced the number of passing cells. On the contrary, lycosin-I exhibited no obvious effect in the transwell migration assay at the same concentrations. This result indicated that the antimigration potency of lycosin-I was highly improved after dodecanoic acid modification. Similarly, the inhibition of A549 cell invasion by L-C12 was also performed based on an ECM (Matrigel)-coated transwell chamber. The results indicated that L-C12 significantly attenuated the invasion of A549 cells (p < 0.001) in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 4B,D). No inhibitory effect on cell invasion was found in the lycosin-I treatment group. Taken together, L-C12 had multiple functions on A549 cells. It potently suppressed proliferation, migration, and invasion of cancer cells at low concentrations, while it induced cell death through apoptosis and membrane disruption at high concentrations.

    L-C12 Exhibits Cytotoxicity and Antimigration on a Three-Dimensional Spheroid

    The activity of L-C12 described above was assessed on cultured cell level. In order to better assess the cytotoxic and antimigration activity of L-C12, a three-dimensional (3D) tumor spheroid and animal model-based experiments were performed as described below. 3D tumor spheroids are heterogeneous cellular aggregates that are frequently characterized by hypoxic regions and necrotic centers,(37) which have been considered valid models to more accurately reflect the complex in vivo microenvironment than a simple two-dimensional cell monolayer.(38) A549 tumor spheroids were treated with lycosin-I (20 and 10 μM) and L-C12 (10 and 5 μM) on day 0, and the spheroid volumes were measured on day 5. In control and lycosin-I groups, the spheroids were gradually growing, but the growth of the spheroids was greatly suppressed with treatment of 5 μM L-C12, as well as physical shrinking and peripheral collapse of the spheroids occurred in the 10 μM L-C12 treatment group (Figure 5A,B). L-C12 also significantly reduced the cell viability of the spheroids as compared with the control and lycosin-I as determined by the CCK-8 assay (Figure 5C).
    A549 tumor spheroid-based migration was also performed as reported by Vinci et al.(39) As shown in Figure 5D,E, a rapid single-cell dispersal in control was observed, which resembles typical amoeboid migration as reported by some literature studies,(40) but with the treatment of L-C12 at the concentrations of 2.5, 5, and 10 μM, much slower and even no dispersal migration occurred. Lycosin-I exhibited antimigration effect on tumor spheroids at a concentration of 20 μM at which no cytotoxicity was detected.

    L-C12 Suppresses the Growth and Metastasis of B16–F10 Cells in Vivo

    In order to verify the in vivo tumor growth and migration inhibition by L-C12, subcutaneous transplantation model and experimental melanoma metastasis model were constructed by using murine melanoma B16–F10 cells because it is a highly metastatic cell line and not easily cleared by the BALB/C mice’s immune system. All the lipopeptides had strong cytotoxicity on B16–F10 cells, and the IC50 value of L-C12 was determined as about 2 μM (Figure 6A). For the subcutaneous transplantation model, lycosin-I or L-C12 at the dose of 10 mg/kg body weight was applied by the intraperitoneal injection every other day. After 10 d of treatment, L-C12 had a significant inhibition effect on B16–F10 tumor growth in mice (P < 0.05) as compared with lycosin-I and PBS control groups (Figure 6B,C). A significant tumor volume reduction in the L-C12 treatment group might be attributed to its highly improved cytotoxicity and serum stability. In addition, no significant body weight changes were observed in lycosin-I, L-C12, and PBS treatments, indicating that these peptides did not diminish their overall health (Figure 6D).
    Next, we further assessed the antimetastasis ability of L-C12. Lycosin-I or L-C12 at the dose of 10 mg/kg body weight was applied by tail intravenous injection every 2 days. As shown in Figure 6E, 10 days after treatment, PBS and lycosin-I treatment groups showed strong metastatic capability with a lot of visible metastatic nodules, but the L-C12-treated mice demonstrated low metastatic capability with visible metastatic nodules lower than the PBS and lycosin-I groups. H&E staining of lung sections also revealed remarkably fewer metastatic nodules in the L-C12 group (Figure 6E). Then, the metastatic nodules that formed on the surface of the lungs were counted, which indicated that there was a significant difference between the mice in the PBS and lycosin-I groups and the L-C12 group (p < 0.01). Again, L-C12 had no effect on the mouse body weight (Figure 6G). Taken together, these results showed that L-C12 strongly suppressed the growth and metastasis of B16–F10 tumors in vivo, indicating that L-C12 might be a potent and multiple functional agent to treat lung cancer.

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In the present study, we aimed to investigate the position effect of fatty acid modification of lycosin-I and to improve the cytotoxicity and antimetastasis potential of lycosin-I. Eight lipopeptides were obtained by coupling dodecanoic acid to the ε-amino group of a lysine residue or the α-amino group of the N-terminal of lycosin-I. Compared with lycosin-I, the eight lipopeptides demonstrated enhanced cytotoxicity, while their cytotoxic profiles were different, and L-C12 with dodecanoic acid coupling to the α-amino group had the strongest cytotoxicity. Further studies indicated that dodecanoic acid modification at different positions of lycosin-I altered the physiochemical parameters of lycosin-I, which could explain their distinct cytotoxic activities. Interestingly, L-C12 exhibited obvious cytotoxic and antimetastasis activities in vivo, suggesting that it might be a lead for ACP drug development.
    Our study revealed that fatty acid modification of lycosin-I led to obvious improvement of some physicochemical parameters (charge, size, and hydrophobicity) which are important to ACP activities. Considering dodecanoic acid is very hydrophobic, and it is certain that dodecanoic modification might enhance the hydrophobicity of lycosin-I. For the increment of zeta potential of the lipopeptides compared with lycosin-I, it may be explained as follows. The positive charges of lycosin-I were resulted from the protonation of the lysine residues. It has been accepted that the level of protonation can be affected by pKa values at a particular pH.(41−43) Lower pKa value causes occurrence of protonation more easily in a certain pH environment, indicating increased charges.(42,44) The pKa values of primary amines can be much lower in acylated peptides(19) because of the hydrophobic environment as is shown for folded proteins, where pKa values can shift up to 5 units, revealing that bases can become acids depending on the microenvironment.(45) As was expected, the eight lipopeptides might have such an effect; that is, dodecanoic acid modification not only increased the hydrophobicity of lycosin-I but also improved the zeta potential. The zeta potentials of the lipopeptides were also affected by buffer pH (Figure S6), with higher zeta potentials in lower pH values. However, it was interesting to know that the zeta potential of L-C12 did not show obvious differences in the three pH values (pH 4.0, 6.0, and 8.0). The hydrophobic fatty acid unit on the peptide could induce peptide self-assembly into a spherical structure.(46) High zeta potentials of the lipopeptides might result in increased charge repulsion among lipopeptide molecules; the dodecanoic acid modification also induced more α-helical conformation of the lipopeptides and consequently increased their colloidal stability, causing decreased aggregation among the lipopeptide molecules.(19) These might explain the much smaller dynamic sizes of the lipopeptides compared with lycosin-I.
    Our study also revealed the position effect of the dodecanoic acid modification of lycosin-I. The modification at the different sites of lycosin-I sequence resulted in distinct alterations of physiochemical parameters and consequently varied cytotoxic activities. There are seven lysine residues plus the N-terminal amine group, which is used for fatty acid modification. At the microscopic level, it is reasonable that these lysine residues and the N-terminal amine group might have different contributions to the structure and activities of lycosin-I, which was validated by our ongoing alanine scan analysis. Accordingly, modifications by dodecanoic acid at different positions would be bound to exert different influences on the assembly construction and the physiochemical parameters. In addition, fatty acids can increase the lipophilicity and membrane binding ability of the peptide.(47) Conjugation of dodecanoic acid to some positions of peptide may not conducive to the exposure of lipophilic dodecanoic acid, weakening the binding of fatty acids to the membrane. Accordingly, their modifications by dodecanoic acid would be bound to exert different influences on the assembly construction and the physiochemical parameters. Taken together, our data suggested that it should be necessary to explore the position effect of fatty modification so that the most optimized peptide analogues could be achieved.
    Our study demonstrated that L-C12, the most potent lipopeptide in this study, had multiple activities, including inhibiting cell proliferation, inducing cell death, and suppressing cell metastasis. Actually, these activities were not “new activities” created by the dodecanoic acid modification because lycosin-I already possessed these activities. Our previous studies indicated that lycosin-I upregulates p27 to inhibit cell proliferation,(22) activates caspase-dependent apoptosis pathway and directly disrupts cell membrane to induce cell death, and inactivates STAT3 pathway to attenuate cell migration.(36) These pathways should be involved in the action of L-C12 on cancer cells, but the dodecanoic acid modification rendered these activities more potency. The activities of L-C12 exhibited a dose-dependent manner. At the concentration above the “critical” concentration (2 μM), L-C12 caused rapid cell death by direct cell membrane lysis. Interestingly, L-C12 demonstrated potent cell proliferation inhibition even at the concentration of 0.25 μM in serum-containing medium for 15 days and powerful cell migration inhibition at 0.5 μM. Moreover, the dodecanoic acid modification also led to high serum stability of L-C12, which combining the potent activities contributed to the good efficacy of L-C12 on tumor growth and metastasis inhibition in vivo.
    In summary, in the present study, we attempted to modify lycosin-I by dodecanoic acid at the different sites on the sequence and drew the conclusions as follows. Coupling site on the sequence was important to optimize lycosin-I by fatty acid modification; it should be necessary to explore the position effect of fatty modification so that the optimum peptide analogues could be achieved. L-C12 with dodecanoic acid linked at the N-terminal of lycosin-I displayed the best performance among the eight lipopeptides, which provided a drug lead for the development of anticancer therapy. This study might lay a ground for optimizing fatty acid modification of such peptides.

    General Experimental Procedures for Synthesizing Lipopeptides

    Peptides were synthesized on a Rink amide-AM resin using the SPPS method(48) and purified by RP-HPLC. During the synthesis of peptide backbone, Fmoc-Lys (Dde)-OH was used at the fatty acid modification site. After the peptide is synthesized, the Dde group was selectively deprotected by using 5 mL of 2% hydrazine hydrate/DMF (v/v) and the procedure was repeated twice for 15 min each time. Dodecanoic acid (0.5 mmol) was dissolved in HATU (0.5 mmol) and HOBT (0.5 mmol) by using 5 mL of 5% NMM/DMF and then added into the reaction vessel for 1 h. Peptides were cleaved using TFA/1,2-ethanedithiol/thioanisole/anisole (90/5/3/2 (v/v)) and, after 2 h, precipitated in cold diethyl ether. All the lipopeptides evaluated in the biological assays were greater than 95% pure based on the HPLC methods described below. Purity, L-C12: 96.4%, L-C12-1: 95%, L-C12-2: 95.5%, L-C12-3: 96.1%. L-C12-4: 95.1% L-C12-5: 95.1%, L-C12-6: 95.4%, and L-C12-7: 95%.

    Chemicals and Reagents

    All Fmoc amino acids and the rink amide-AM resin were purchased from GL Biochem. (Shanghai, China). Crystal violet, dodecanoic acid, and gelatin were purchased from Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China). The cell counting kit-8 (CCK-8), LDH cytotoxicity assay kit, and AO/EB double staining kit were obtained from Beyotime (Shanghai, China).

    Cell Culture

    Human lung carcinoma cells (A549), human prostatic carcinoma cells (PC-3), human glioblastomas (U87), human cervical cancer cells (HeLa), human colorectal cancer cells (SW620), human breast cells (MDA-MB-231 and MCF-7), and noncancer cells (HEK-293T and L-02) were purchased from ATCC. Cell culture medium, FBS, 0.25% trypsin–EDTA, and antibiotic solution (penicillin and streptomycin) were purchased from Invitrogen (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). Cells were cultured in an F12K medium or DMEM containing penicillin/streptomycin, 10% FBS, and glutamine at 37 °C with 5% CO2.

    Cell Viability Assay

    The cells were seeded at a density of 2 × 103 cells per well in a 96-well plate. After 24 h, the culture medium in a well was sucked up, another 100 μL of DMEM or F12K medium containing various concentrations of lipopeptides was added into the well in a 96-well microplate. The efficacy of lipopeptides on cell viability was determined using the CCK-8 assay after the cells were incubated at 37 °C for 24 h.

    FITC-L-C12 Synthesis

    The synthesis of FITC-L-C12 of the lipopeptide was conducted as follows. After synthesis, L-C12, Fmoc-Lys (Dde)-OH was deprotected by reacting with 5 mL of 2% hydrazine hydrate/DMF (v/v), and the procedure was repeated twice for 15 min each time. FITC was connected to the ε-amino group of N-terminal first Lys residue at a 1.5-fold excess of FITC in a mixed pyridine/dichloromethane/DMF (12:7:5 v/v) system for 24 h. Note that the cytotoxicity of FITC-L-C12 (9 μM) was lower than that of L-C12 (2 μM).

    Flow Cytometry Assay

    A549 cells were seeded (2 to 3 × 105 cells/well) in six-well plates and cultured at 37 °C in 5% CO2 for 24 h. Then, the cells were washed twice with PBS, trypsinized, and resuspended with medium containing different lipopeptides and incubated for 4 h. After being centrifuged, the cell pellet was slightly washed three times with cold PBS and then stained by Annexin-V-FITC/PI apoptotic detection kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The fluorescence was measured using flow cytometry (BD, USA).

    Caspase 3 Activity Assay

    According to the manufacturer’s instructions of the Caspase 3 activity assay kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China), the activity of caspase 3 in A549 cells was assessed. In brief, after treating with 2 μM lycosin-I or L-C12 for 24 h, the cells were harvested, lysed, and centrifuged. Supernatants were collected and incubated with Ac-DEVD-pNA (2 mM), which is the substrate of caspase 3. The activity of caspase 3 was determined based on the absorbance at 405 nm by Absorbance Microplate Reader (BioTek Instruments, USA). The caspase 3 activity ratio was calculated as compared to the blank control.

    Colony Formation Assay

    For examining the proliferation of A549 cells treated with low concentration of lycosin-I and L-C12, cells were counted and seeded into a12-well plate in a range of 150 cells per plate. After 36 h, cells were treated with different concentrations of lipopeptides, which were refilled after 7 days and in total incubated for 15 days at 37 °C in a humidified 5% CO2 atmosphere. Colonies were stained with 2% crystal violet.

    AO/EB Double Staining

    A549 cells (1 × 103 cells/well) were seeded in a 24-well plate and incubated for 24 h. After treating with 4 μM lycosin-I and L-C12 for 1 h, cells were washed twice with PBS and stained with 200 μL of dye mixture, containing 80 μg/mL of AO and 80 μg/mL of EB in DMEM. After staining and washing, fluorescence was visualized immediately using a fluorescence microscope (Olympus FV1000).

    Scanning Electron Microscopy

    SEM was performed to investigate the cell morphological changes in A549 cells. The cells seeded at coverslips and placed into a six-well plate were treated with 4 μM lycosin-I and L-C12 for 1 h. Cells were fixed with 1 mL of 2.5% glutaraldehyde solution and dehydrated with a series of ethanol concentrations according to our previous protocol.(9) The samples were examined by using a scanning electron microscope (Hitachi Su8010, Japan).

    LDH Leakage Assay

    According to the manufacturer’s instructions, the LDH release assay was used to determine the membrane integrity. Briefly, after treating the cells with lycosin-I or lipopeptides, the reaction mixture (50 μL) was added to each well and reacted for 30 min at room temperature, followed by 50 μL of stop solution. No peptides were added, indicating no leakage. The cell treated with 1% Trition X-100 represented 100% leakage (total LDH release). The fluorescence was detected by a microplate reader at 490 nm.

    Serum Stability Assay

    According to the literature,(26) lycosin-I and L-C12 were added to normal mouse serum (50 μL) in a final concentration of 100 μM and incubated at 37 °C for 24 h. After 24 h, an equal volume of ACN was added to the mouse serum or mouse serum containing a drug to precipitate serum proteins. The cloudy mixture was centrifuged at 12 000 rpm for 3 min and then the supernatant was removed. After re-extracting the remaining peptides in the pellet and vacuum-drying, the supernatant was analyzed by MALDI-TOF MS. Another method was also performed to determine the serum stability of lycosin-I and L-C12 as previously described.(11) Lycosin-I and L-C12 were preincubated with 10% serum or PBS at 37 °C for 48 h. Then, the preincubated lycosin-I and L-C12 using 2-fold gradient dilution were added to A549 cells and incubated at 37 °C in 5% CO2. After 24 h, the cell viability of lycosin-I and L-C12 was detected by a microplate reader by using the CCK-8 assay.

    Measurement of Hydrodynamic Sizes and Zeta Potentials of Peptides

    Dynamic light scattering (DLS) and electrophoretic light scattering were used to determine the hydrodynamic sizes and zeta potentials of lipopeptides, respectively, through the Zeta sizer Nano ZSP (Malvern Instruments, UK) apparatus. The measurement concentration of lipopeptides was 100 μM with ultrapure water in a final volume of 1 mL. The measurement temperature was 25 °C, which was determined by averaging two repeated experiments. Each experiment consists of three repetitions.

    Transwell Migration Assay

    In the transwell migration experiment, the lower chambers were added with 600 μL of medium containing 10% FBS DMEM. A549 cells were suspended in a serum-free DMEM and plated to the top chambers of a transwell at a density of 5 × 104. Both serum-containing in lower chambers and serum-free medium in top chambers contain the same concentration as lycosin-I and L-C12. After incubating for 12 h, the cells that did not pass through the membrane were removed with a cotton swab. Cells on the other side of the membrane were fixed with 95% anhydrous methanol and stained with crystal violet. The transwell invasion experiment was carried out with a similar experimental protocol to transwell migration beside the top chambers of the transwell coated with Matrigel.

    3D Spheroid-Based Cytotoxicity Assays

    A549 spheroids were grown to study the cytotoxicity and migration of lycosin-I and L-C12 according to our previous procedure.(11) To test the effect of lycosin-I and L-C12 on the 3D tumor spheroid, the A549 spheroids were treated with 20, 10 μM lycosin-I, and 10, 5 μM L-C12 in the DMEM supplied with 10% FBS for 5 days. Images were taken on day 0 and day 5. Subsequently, the spheroids were washed three times, and the cell viability was measured using the CCK-8 assay.

    3D Spheroid-Based Antimetastasis Assays

    The 3D spheroid-based metastasis assay was performed according to the protocol described in the literature.(39) Briefly, gelatin was dissolved in sterile water at a final concentration 0.1%, and 48-well plates were coated with gelatin for 1 h at 37 °C. For antimetastasis studies, a total of 200 μL/well of culture medium was supplemented with 2% FBS, and different concentrations of lycosin-I and L-C12 were then added. Controls were treated with vehicle. Spheroids were allowed to adhere, and images were obtained using a microscope at t0 and 48 h. The effect of lycosin-I and L-C12 was analyzed by measuring the area covered by migrating cells. Data were normalized to the initial size of each spheroid at t0.

    In Vivo Functional Assays

    Six week-old healthy male BALB/c mice were purchased from Slac & Jingda Corporation of laboratory animals, Changsha, China. The mouse experiments were performed according to the ethical guidelines for laboratory animal use and approved by the Ethics Committee of Hunan Normal University. The in vivo antitumor growth ability of lycosin-I and L-C12 was performed in a xenograft mouse model. Approximately 5 × 104 of B16–F10 cells in 0.2 mL of hank’s solution were injected subcutaneously into dorsal regions of 5 week-old BALB/C mice (n = 3 mice/group). When tumors reached the size of 100–200 mm3, the mice were injected 200 μL of lycosin-I or L-C12 (10 mg/kg) by intraperitoneal injection every other day. The tumor volume was measured every 2 days, and mouse body weight was weighed every 5 days. After 10 days, mice were sacrificed and photographed. In vivo metastatic ability was estimated by tail vein injection. About 2.5 × 104 of B16–F10 cells in 0.2 mL of hank’s solution was applied through tail intravenous injection. After 2 days, mice were injected 200 μL of lycosin-I or L-C12 (8 mg/kg) by tail intravenous injection every 2 days. After 10 days, the mice were sacrificed and the nodules formed on the lung surfaces were counted. Lungs were excised and embedded in paraffin for conventional hematoxylin and eosin (HE) staining.
