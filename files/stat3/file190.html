    Abstract

    Antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) are key components of host immune defense of vertebrates against microbial invasions. Here, we report a new AMP (esculentin-1GN) characterized from the skin of the frog Hylarana guentheri. Esculentin-1GN (GLFSKKGGKGGKSWIKGVFKGIKGIGKEVGGDVIRTGIEIAACKIKGEC) with high amphipathic α-helical structure in membrane-mimetic environments has the microbial-killing activity by destruction of the cell membrane. Moreover, esculentin-1GN inhibits LPS-induced expression of proinflammatory nitric oxide, interleukin-1β, interleukin-6, and tumor necrosis factor while it enhances expression of interleukin-10. Furthermore, esculentin-1GN can bind to d-(+)-galacturonic acid and LPS. Meanwhile, esculentin-1GN suppresses the activation of inflammatory response pathway induced by LPS. In addition, esculentin-1GN significantly reduces acute inflammation in carrageenan-induced mice paw. Taken together, the novel LPS-binding esculentin-1GN with antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory activities will be an excellent temple for designing new antibiotic formulations.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Innate immune defenses like antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) play critical roles for amphibians with a poor cell-mediated adaptive immune resistance to the environmental microorganisms. As yet, about 1900 AMPs have been identified from 178 amphibians among which ranid frogs are the richest storehouses of AMPs.(1) According to structural similarities, AMPs from amphibians are classified into different families including temporins, magainins, ranatuerins, tigerinins, ranalexins, ranacyclins, nigrocins, brevinins-1 and -2, and esculentins-1 and -2. Esculentin-1 has been first characterized in the skin secretion of the frog, Rana esculenta, and afterward identified from the skin of 13 different ranids.(1,2) Besides possessing common characteristics of most linear AMPs like a large ratio of hydrophobic residues and a net positive charge at pH 7.0,(1) esculentins-1 consisting of 46 residues usually contain highly conserved a heptapeptide ring stabilized by a disulfide bond at the C-terminus and only change one or two amino acid residues at the N-terminus.(2,3) Many esculentin-1 peptides exhibit strong antimicrobial property and low cytotoxicity to mammalian cells.(1,3) In addition, antimicrobial peptide esculentins-1 and -1B have revealed insulin releasing activity.(4) Interestingly, the truncated peptide only containing the 19–46 residues of esculentin-1 has been reported to lack antimicrobial activity because of its loss of partial cationic residues. However, N-terminal portions from the molecules have been showed to retain this function.(2) Esc(1–18), the 1–18 fragment of esculentin-1b, has an antimicrobial activity comparable to that of the full-length parent peptide and displays anti-candida activity in in vitro and in vivo studies.(5,6) Another N-terminal fragment that only contains the first 20 residues of esculentin-1a before an amidated glycine and a higher net positive charge than Esc(1–18) at neutral pH, Esc(1–21), has shown many fascinating biological properties.(7−13) Esc(1–21) can inhibit production of TNF-α from P. aeruginosa LPS-induced immune cells, improve lung cell migration and removal of P. aeruginosa internalized in bronchial cells, promote re-epithelialization of the injured tissue, heal damaged lung tissue, disaggregate and detoxify the bacterial LPS, as well as enhance the mobility of human haCaT cells via the EGF receptor-dependent way at a faster rate than AMP LL-37.(9−11) Esc(1–21) also promotes the survival of mice with sepsis and pulmonary infection,(12) inhibits bovine mastitis caused by bacteria, and reduces murine keratitis in vivo.(7,13) These established abilities of esculentin-1 and their derivatives make them particularly attractive candidate pharmaceuticals against wound infection, type 2 diabetes, pseudomonas keratitis, epidermis injury.(3)
    Hylarana guentheri (H. guentheri) is a big-sized Rana frog and widely survives in pools, streams, rice fields, or other damp places at the altitudes of 50–1250 m in the south of China. It is necessary for H. guentheri to develop various mechanisms protecting themselves from pathogenic microbes from all kinds of sources under such conditions. To date, 14 AMPs belonging to the temporin, brevinin-1 and -2 families have been identified from H. guentheri.(14,15) However, no esculentin-1 peptide has been discovered from this species. To verify the widespread existence of esculentin-1 in Rana frog and identify novel potent esculentin-1 for peptide antibiotic design, we here characterized esculentin-1GN (Esc-1GN) from the frog skin of H. guentheri. A serials of structural analysis and pharmacological investigations indicate that LPS binding esculentin-1GN with novel structure features possesses strong antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory activities in vitro and in vivo.

    2.1. Identification and Characterization of Esculentin-1GN

    By PCR-based cDNA cloning method, a cDNA sequence encoding a new antimicrobial peptide was obtained from the skin-derived cDNA library of H. guentheri (GenBank accession number MH510231). As shown in Figure 1, the cDNA of 339 bp contained complete coding sequence and the deduced amino acid precursor comprised 87 amino acid residues. This sequence shared the typical primary structure feature of amphibian defensive peptides which generally consist of a signal peptide sequence, an N-terminal acidic interval domain ended with a well-known Lyse-Arg protease cleavage site, and the mature C-terminal AMP.(16) On the basis of the NCBI Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) analysis, the deduced mature AMP displayed high sequence similarities with AMPs grouped into esculentin-1 family (Figure S1); therefore, it was named as esculentin-1GN. The amino acid sequence of esculentin-1GN was GLFSKKGGKGGKSWIKGVFKGIKGIGKEVGGDVIRTGIEIAACKIKGEC. The physicochemical parameters of esculentin-1GN were shown in Table S1. Multisequence alignment of esculentin-1GN with other representative esculentin-1 peptides from ranid species belonging to different genera revealed that consistent with other esculentin-1, esculentin-1GN contained a highly conserved heptapeptide ring reinforced by a disulfide bond at the C-terminus. Noticeably, esculentins-1 was made up of 49 residues and contained 3 extra amino acids in the N-terminus in comparison with other esculentins-1 (Figure S1).

    2.2. Cladistic Analysis

    Multisequence alignments were performed, and the phylogenetic tree in NJ method was constructed with AMPs from the skins of ranid species classified into different genera (Figure S2). The esculentin-1 sequences clearly formed three distinguishable groups: one group comprising 9 AMPs identified from the frogs belonging to the genus Rana. The second group was divided into two clades and consisted of 10 AMPs from 3 different frog genera frog (Odorrana, Amolops, and Pelophylax). The third group was relatively complicated and contained 4 peptides. Esculentin-1GN was located in the top of them and was less closely related to the Hylarana frog peptides in phylogenetic relationship due to low sequence similarities with esculentin-1Lsa, -1LTa, and -1LTb.

    2.3. Secondary Structure Modeling of Esculentin-1GN

    BLAST search against PDB (Protein Data Bank) showed that esculentin-1GN shared the highest sequence identities with rantuerin-2CSa (PDB entry 2K10) and gaegurin-4 (PDB entry 2G9L). Meanwhile, their N-terminal segments of esculentin-1GN and esculentin-1a(1–21)NH2 (PDB entry 5XDJ) shared high similarities in amino acid sequences and biological functions. Therefore, they were used as temples predicting the esculentin-1GN structure by homology modeling. Figure 2A showed that the α-helix was a predominant feature in the predicted structure of esculentin-1GN, in which the residues 2–6, 9–20, and 45–48 formed two α-helices at the N-terminus and a short α-helix at the C-terminus, respectively. Besides, glycine 7–8 broke the extension of the N-terminal helix structures and a long random coil located in the middle of esculentin-1GN separating the two α-helix domains at the C-terminus and N-terminus. The N-terminal α-helical domain of esculentin-1GN showed the amphipathic character with the positively charged amino acids like lysine residues labeled in light blue which were concentrated on the same face and the hydrophobic residues which were segregated on the other (Figure 2A). Consistently, the helix-wheel analysis showed that the hydrophobic amino acids in gray and yellow were centered on one side of the helix while the hydrophilic or polar amino acids in purple and blue were on the opposite side. Such conformation was definitely an amphipathic structure commonly adopted by most of AMPs and usually acknowledged to be directly relevant to their antimicrobial function. Therefore, esculentin-1GN possibly is a new membrane-active peptide to exert its antimicrobial activity through membrane permeabilization or lysis mechanism. Consequently, the homology modeling results provide important clues to understand the antimicrobial mechanism of esculentin-1GN. Taken together, esculentin-1GN showed characteristics like the α-helical conformation and a cationic charge at pH 7.0, which are jointly possessed by a variety of AMPs.

    2.4. CD Determination

    To prove the accuracy of the simulated amphipathic α-helical structure and measure the effects of different solution environments on the structure of esculentin-1GN, the CD spectra of the esculentin-1GN in different solutions were determined. As shown in Figure 2C, the CD spectra of esculentin-1GN prepared in H2O revealed a big negative peak at 200 nm and a small negative peak at 211 nm, which are the spectral characteristics of random coil conformation. However, the CD spectra of esculentin-1GN dissolved in the membrane-like SDS solutions revealed a strong positive peak at 195 nm and dual negative peaks at 208 and 222 nm, indicating that the secondary structure components of peptide are mainly α-helix. Furthermore, the concentration difference of SDS in the range of 30–120 mM caused little changes in the secondary structure components of esculentin-1GN which contained about 91.5–96.0% α-helix, 1.9–2.0% β-sheet, 2.9–6.3% β-turn, and 0.2% random coil (Figure 2C). The secondary confirmations of esculentin-1GN in different salt and temperature environments were also assessed. Similarly, the CD spectra of esculentin-1GN with different temperature treatments also showed highly similar secondary structure (Figure 2D). When dissolved in SDS solutions with the indicated concentrations of NaCl, the strong positive peak at 195 nm shifted toward the right and the α-helix levels reduced somewhat by 6.2% while the β-turn content increased by 25.2% with the increasing concentrations of NaCl. Nevertheless, the predominant α-helix structure of esculentin-1GN was still retained even in 400 mM NaCl solutions (Figure 2E).
    To define whether the secondary structure of esculentin-1GN and its derivatives contribute to their difference of antimicrobial activity, the CD spectra of peptides in 50 μM LPS were investigated. As shown in Figure 2F, esculentin-1GN showed significant characteristic peaks from α-helices in a 50 μM LPS solution like that in the membrane-like SDS solutions. However, the CD spectra of four truncated peptides in the same buffer markedly changed and the peaks at three specific wavelengths shifted, lessened, and even disappeared, which indicated a decrease in α-helix and an increase in unordered structures. As listed in Table S2, esculentin-1GN presented a prominently ordered structure in a 50 μM LPS solution and contained about 89.8% α-helix, 1.3% β-sheet, 7.4% β-turn, and 4.2% random coil, which suggested that the LPS solution simulated to higher extent an actual membrane-like environment and could more efficiently induce the formation of α-helical structures of esculentin-1GN. However, truncation caused to some extent a decrease in α-helix but an increase in unordered structure of the corresponding peptide, which was in line with the results of their modeled structures in Figure S4.

    2.5. Antimicrobial Activities Assay

    By use of ampicillin as a positive control, the antimicrobial potency of esculentin-1GN and its derivatives against Gram-negative, Gram-positive bacteria, and fungi was investigated. As listed in Table S3, esculentin-1GN exhibited broad-spectrum antimicrobial activities against tested stains. Among the microorganisms tested, Propionibacterium acnes was the most sensitive to esculentin-1GN with a MIC value of 1.17 μM. Moreover, most Gram-positive bacteria with MICs ranging from 1.17 to 3.12 μM was more sensitive to esculentin-1GN than Gram-negative bacteria (MICs = 3.75 and 50.00 μM). Remarkably, esculentin-1GN had weak antimicrobial activity against methicillin-resistant microorganism (MIC = 39.96 μM). To measure whether the α-helix conformation and positive charge affected the antimicrobial ability of esculentin-1GN, four esculentin-1GN derivatives were designed and synthesized (Figures 3 and S3). As shown in Figure 4, compared with the original esculentin-1GN, the N-terminal or C-terminal truncation evidently decreased the activity of the peptide against most bacteria except P. aeruginosa. The antibacterial activity of esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 against P. aeruginosa was comparable with that of esculentin-1GN. Nevertheless, the MIC values of truncated fragments against other tested bacteria decreased with increasing positive charge and the antimicrobial abilities of four derivatives were as follows: esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 > esculentin-1GN(1–21)NH2 > esculentin-1GN(1–17)NH2 > esculentin-1GN(18–49). Actually, esculentin-1GN(18–49) lacked antimicrobial activity against all strains tested although it had high contents of α-helix, which was in line with results with C-terminal fragments derived from other esculentin-1.

    2.6. Bacteria Killing Kinetics Determination

    Killing kinetics of esculentin-1GN and esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 was investigated with a colony counting assay. As shown in Figure S5, esculentin-1GN and esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 rapidly exerted their antimicrobial function at a concentration of 2× and 4× MIC, respectively. It took about 180 min to completely eliminate all E. coli ATCC 25922 cells for both peptides, which was more rapid than that of ampicillin. More importantly, the antimicrobial effects of two peptides were bactericidal rather than bacteriostasic because E. coli ATCC 25922 was unable to resume growth on agar plates when being incubated with esculentin-1GN or esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 for 180 min or more time. It also was remarkable that esculentin-1GN had the more rapid bactericidal action and broader antimicrobial spectrum than ampicillin regardless of its lower potency than the latter against E. coli ATCC 25922, which indicated that esculentin-1GN still was a good antibiotic candidate.

    2.7. Stability Analysis

    The antimicrobial potency of many AMPs can be generally influenced by salt concentration and temperature conditions.(17,18) Therefore, the antimicrobial activity of esculentin-1GN was investigated under different NaCl concentrations and temperature conditions. Figure 5A showed that the MIC value of esculentin-1GN invariably stayed at 3.75 μM in the nonsalt solution. Moreover, after incubation with 150 mM NaCl, the antimicrobial activity of esculentin-1GN decreased gently and its MIC value was only increased to 7.5 μM. As NaCl concentration was further added up to 400 mM, the MIC value of esculentin-1GN raised to 13.33 μM, indicating that esculentin-1GN was unstable in high salt solution. However, esculentin-1GN was relatively stable and its MIC values remained in the range of 3.75–11.67 μM after incubation for 1 h at a series of temperatures below 90 °C (Figure 5B). The unstability in solutions definitely restricts the application of some traditional antibiotics like β-lactam antibiotics and tetracyclines. On the contrary, esculentin-1GN dissolved in water revealed high stability and its MIC value against E. coli ATCC 25922 increased 3.5-fold after incubation at 37 °C for 96 h (Figure 5C). Generally, the antimicrobial activity of AMPs was notably reduced by serum components due to protease degradation and binding interaction. Consistent with this, the antimicrobial ability of esculentin-1GN gradually declined with the extended incubation in human serum and its MIC value against E. coli ATCC 25922 improved from 3.75 to 40 μM. Nevertheless, the antimicrobial activity of esculentin-1GN continued to exist even after treatment with human serum for 6 h (Figure 5D), which displayed its far higher stability than the previously reported AMPs from frogs.(19,20)

    2.8. Effects on Bacterial Cell Membrane

    Generally, AMP-mediated cell initiated killing process immediately after their attraction and attachment to microbial surfaces.(21) Flow cytometry was performed to explore the binding interaction between esculentin-1GN and bacterial cells. As illustrated in Figure 6A, FITC-labeled esculentin-1GN could bind to E. coli ATCC 25922 and S. aureus ATCC 25923 cells, which was displayed obviously by different fluorescence intensity after 5 min incubation compared with the untreated bacteria. It has been reported that many antimicrobial peptides can permeabilize the cytoplasmic membrane, thereby disrupting microbial cell integrity, altering cell morphology, inducing effusion of intracellular inclusion and the death of cells. To better understand the possible mechanism of esculentin-1GN against bacteria, its effects on the integrity of the cytoplasmic membrane of bacterial cells were observed with confocal laser scanning microscopy and scanning electron microscopy. As shown in Figure 6B, E. coli and S. aureus cells with compromised membranes which were stained red by PI existed in both groups. However, by comparison with the nontreated control, the intensity ratio of PI/SYTO9 increased and more bacterial cells appear red after treatment with 1× MIC peptide for 90 min (Figure 6B, panels b, d), suggesting that esculentin-1GN apparently damaged the integrity of cell membrane and induced membrane permeabilization which led to the influx of PI into Gram-negative and -positive cells. In agreement with SYTO9 and PI staining experiments, the cellular morphology of esculentin-1GN-treated bacteria and the control was definitely different. E. coli and S. aureus cells in the control groups displayed a normal shape and smooth surface (Figure 6C, panels a, c). However, obvious cell swelling, shrinkage, and deformation and even a large amount of intracellular inclusions out of bacterial plasma membranes were observed in the groups treated by esculentin-1GN. Additionally, some obvious cell holes seemed to appear in the plasma membranes of esculentin-1GN-treated bacteria (panels b, d in Figure 6C; indicated by arrows). All these phenomena implied that esculentin-1GN like most AMPs exerted its antimicrobial activity through membrane permeabilization and cellular disruption mechanism.

    2.9. Suppression of Antimicrobial Activity by LPS

    To explore whether bacterial LPS accounts for the sensitive difference between Gram-negative and -positive bacteria to esculentin-1GN, the effect of LPS on the antimicrobial activity of esculentin-1GN against S. aureus lacking the LPS outer membrane was investigated in vitro. As Figure 7A showed, all lg values of CFUs without esculentin-1GN were almost unchangeable and about 6.83 after 60 min incubation at 37 °C. Obviously, LPS at all tested concentrations had no effect on the proliferation of bacteria. When different concentrations of esculentin-1GN were incubated with the same concentration of LPS, the viability of S. aureus significantly reduced with the increasing concentrations of esculentin-1GN. However, when the concentrations of LPS O55:B5 rose from 0 to 250 μM, the lg values of CFUs in the presence of 2× or 4× MIC esculentin-1GN increased from 4.90 to 6.80 and from 4.31 to 6.56, respectively. These results stated clearly that LPS could suppress the antimicrobial activity of esculentin-1GN against Gram-positive S. aureus in a concentration dependent manner, which was probably derived from the direct charge interaction between the negative LPS and positive esculentin-1GN.

    2.10. Bacteria Agglutination Tests

    E. coli ATCC 25922 was applied to examine the agglutination of esculentin-1GN toward the surface of microorganism. As shown by Figure 7D, agglutination in the BSA control group did not happen. However, mass agglutination formed after E. coli was incubated with esculentin-1GN for 1 h. Furthermore, metal cation addition to esculentin-1GN increased the agglutinating activity. However, this bacteria agglutination was completely abolished when TBS-EDTA, LPS, or d-(+)-galacturonic acid existed. In addition, no obvious hemagglutination was observed after esculentin-1GN was incubated with erythrocytes for 1 h (data not shown). These results indicated that esculentin-1GN agglutinated E. coli ATCC 25922 in a d-(+)-galacturonic acid-specific and metal cation-dependent manner.

    2.11. Direct Binding Reaction with LPS

    To better understand the reaction between esculentin-1GN and LPS, binding of the peptide to LPS was examined with ITC experiments which determined the binding affinity and thermodynamic parameters. As shown in Figure 7E and Table S4, both the binding-induced decline in enthalpy and the decreasing trends of the ITC profiles suggested that the binding reaction of esculentin-1GN with LPS caused to the release of heat. Binding saturation happened when the molar ratio of peptide to LPS was 4.0 at 50 min. The binding affinity, as measured by the equilibrium dissociation constant, Kd, between esculentin-1GN and LPS, was determined to be 15.00 ± 3.79 μM and indicated a strong interaction. The enthalpy change of association between esculentin-1GN and LPS was relatively low, and the value was determined to be −4.18 ± 0.486 kcal/mol. To identify the carbohydrate-binding specificity of esculentin-1GN, its reactions with 33 monomeric sugars were also evaluated using ITC experiments, and only d-(+)-galacturonic acid could bind esculentin-1GN (Figure 7F; Table S4). Like the interaction between LPS and peptides, the process of esculentin-1GN binding d-(+)-galacturonic acid also produced heat. However, binding saturation happened when the molar ratio of sugar to peptide was 7.0 at 6 min. In addition, the maximum enthalpy change in the binding process was measured to be −2.74 ± 0.036 kcal/mol with a Kd value of 3.35 ± 0.41 μM, which suggested that d-(+)-galacturonic acid and esculentin-1GN had much stronger interaction than LPS and esculentin-1GN. As shown in Figure S6 and Table S4, for the truncated peptide, esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2, its binding ability with LPS and d-(+)-galacturonic acid changed and their values of Kd were 1.69 ± 0.107 μM and 6.28 ± 0.445 μM, respectively.
    The real-time bimolecular interactions between esculentin-1GN and LPS/d-(+)-galacturonic acid were further characterized using SPRi experiments. As shown in Figure 7B and Figure 7C, strong SPRi signal responses were observed when increasing concentrations of LPS or d-(+)-galacturonic acid were flowed through esculentin-1GN immobilized on the chip. The resonance units derived from the reaction of LPS and d-(+)-galacturonic acid with esculentin-1GN were gradually increased in a dose-dependent manner, which suggested the direct interaction between LPS/d-(+)-galacturonic acid and esculentin-1GN. The Kd of LPS and d-(+)-galacturonic acid binding to esculentin-1GN were respectively calculated to be 13.86 μM and 3.53 μM, which agreed nicely with ITC results.

    2.12. Cytotoxicity of Esculentin-1GN

    Human red blood cells were applied to assess the hemolytic capability of esculentin-1GN. As shown in Table S5, esculentin-1GN showed a low hemolytic activity on human red cells. At concentrations of 40 uM, the hemolysis ratio of human red cells was only 6.2%. The effects of esculentin-1GN on the proliferation of eukaryotic cells were measured by the MTT assay with different types of mammalian cell lines: RAW 264.7, NCI-H460, PC12, MDCK, MDA-MB-231, A549, and HUVEC cells. As reported in Table S5, esculentin-1GN exhibited a variety of cytotoxicity toward the tested mammalian cells in a dose dependent manner. Remarkably, Raw 264.7 cells were more sensitive to esculentin-1GN (IC50 = 1.79 ± 0.27 μM) after 48 h of exposure. However, esculentin-1GN at concentrations less than or equal to 320 nM did not inhibit the proliferation of RAW 264.7 cells (Figure 8A).

    2.13. Inhibition of NO Production Induced by LPS

    The effects of esculentin-1GN on the NO production induced by LPS in RAW 264.7 cells were investigated as shown in Figure 8B,C. In comparison with that of cells without LPS addition, 100 ng/mL LPS led to 191.4-fold increase of the mRNA content of iNOS which is necessary to produce NO. Nevertheless, esculentin-1GN significantly caused a dose-dependent inhibition of the iNOS mRNA induction by 100 ng/mL LPS after 24 h incubation (Figure 8B). At a concentration of 80 nM, esculentin-1GN inhibited 73.5% of iNOS transcription. NO production is positively correlated with nitrite concentration in culture medium. Further, the NO production was determined by measuring the nitrite concentration in the supernatants of RAW 264.7 cells. As shown in Figure 8C, LPS at the concentration of 100 ng/mL stimulated 44.18 μM nitrite releases which were significantly suppressed by esculentin-1GN in a concentration-dependent way after 24 h incubation with RAW 264.7 cells. At the concentration of 20, 40, and 80 nM, esculentin-1GN inhibited about 10.2%, 13.7%, and 20.3% of the LPS-induced nitrite production, respectively.

    2.14. Suppression of LPS-Induced Inflammatory Cytokines Production

    To get insight into the effects of esculentin-1GN on the LPS-induced inflammatory cytokines secretion in RAW 264.7 murine macrophage cells, the gene expression and protein production of TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, and IL-10 were evaluated by qRT-PCR and ELISA as illustrated in Figure 8D–L. 100 ng/mL LPS markedly (p < 0.001) stimulated transcription and production of the above four inflammatory cytokines in the absence of esculentin-1GN. However, for three proinflammatory cytokines, the induction was significantly blocked by esculentin-1GN in a concentration-dependent manner. In detail, treatment with esculentin-1GN resulted in an approximately 19.0–78.2%, 22.1–65.0%, and 16.8–75.3% inhibition of TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6 gene expression in RAW 264.7 cells induced by LPS, respectively. In comparison with its effects on their mRNA expression, esculentin-1GN inhibited LPS-elicited proinflammatory cytokine protein production but to a much less extent, and approximately 6.8–19.9%, 2.7–28.4%, and 3.5–23.7% inhibition rates were observed for TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6, respectively. Astonishingly, esculentin-1GN at concentrations less than or equal to 20 nM enhanced the expression gene and protein production of IL-10. However, the increasing trend was reversed and IL-10 production was suppressed in a concentration-dependent manner when peptide concentration was more than 20 nM (Figure 8J–L).

    2.15. Suppression of LPS-Induced Inflammatory Response Pathways

    The binding effect of esculentin-1GN on RAW 264.7 cells was investigated by flow cytometry experiments. As illustrated in Figure 9A, esculentin-1GN concentration-dependently bound to RAW 264.7 cells after a 15 min preincubation. The sequential activation of intracellular signal-regulated protein kinase (ERK), c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK), p38 mitogen-activated protein kinase (p38), and the nucleus translocation of the transcription factor NF-κB play important roles in the expression of inflammatory cytokines.(22,23) Thus, we identified the effects of esculentin-1GN on LPS-induced inflammatory signaling pathways by Western blot. Compared with normal cells, 100 ng/mL LPS stimulation greatly upregulated the levels of phosphorylated ERK, JNK, p38, and the nuclear translocation of the NF-κB p65 subunit as illustrated in Figure 9B and Figure 9C. However, the upregulation was suppressed by esculentin-1GN treatment which did not affect the levels of total ERK, JNK, and p38. Furthermore, the inhibitory effect of esculentin-1GN on these signal molecules was dose-dependent and addition of 80 nM esculentin-1GN led to reduction of the levels of phosphorylated ERK, JNK by 80.4% and 66.2%, respectively. And even the LPS-induced phosphorylation of p38 and the nuclear translocation of p65 subunit were almost blocked by 80 nM esculentin-1GN (Figure 9B–D). These results indicated that esculentin-1GN executed its anti-inflammatory effect via suppressing p38MAPK and NF-κB signaling pathways in LPS-stimulated macrophage cells.

    2.16. Paw Edema Assay

    The role of esculentin-1GN in acute inflammation was investigated with the carrageenan-induced paw edema model. As shown in Figure 10A, the injection of carrageenan led to an edema which culminated at 4 h and slowly declined but continued to be visible until the end point of the assay at 24 h. However, the indicated concentrations of indomethacin, a standard antiphlogistic drug, and esculentin-1GN notably suppressed the carrageenan-induced increases in paw thickness and the edema at 4 h postinjection decreased about 45.9% and 34.6%, respectively. Moreover, at 24 h after injection, the edema induced by carrageenan was restrained by 54.9% and 35.3% (p < 0.001) (Figure 10B). In a further set of experiments, indomethacin and esculentin-1GN significantly reduced the neutrophil assembling induced by carrageenan in the foot pads by evaluating tissue myeloperoxidase activity at 4 h postinjection. The myeloperoxidase activities in the hind paw of indomethacin and esculentin-1GN treated mice were 12.6 ± 0.76 and 26.5 ± 1.95 units/mg protein, respectively, compared to 8.3 ± 0.48 units/mg protein in the control mice treated by saline and 43.1 ± 1.37 units/mg protein in the carrageenan controls (Figure 10C). Consistently, histological analysis displayed inflammatory cell infiltration with subcutaneous edema appearing in all paws injected with carrageenan. However, in the group treated with indomethacin and esculentin-1GN, the infiltrate was less intense and leukocyte infiltration was significantly reduced (Figure 10D, shown by arrow).

    3. Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The skin of amphibians produces extremely diverse defense molecules and plays important roles for their survival in different habitats such as lands, trees, streams, rocks, and so on. Over the past few decades, a large number of pharmacologically active substances from amphibian skin, especially those contributing to decrease the biotic and abiotic risk, have been extensively reported.(1) AMPs among them are a class of well-known innate immune molecules that protect amphibians from microbial infections. To date, 14 AMPs belonging to the temporin, brevinin-1 and -2 families have been identified from H. guentheri.(14,15,24) However, no esculentin-1 peptide has been discovered from this species thus far.
    In this study, a novel esculentin-1 family AMP with a unique structure, which we named esculentin-1GN, is successfully identified from the skin of H. guentheri. Esculentin-1GN precursor with 87 residues possesses the common feature of esculentin-1 which comprises a signal peptide sequence, an N-terminal acidic spacer domain, and the mature C-terminal AMP characterized by the presence of Cys-1 and Cys-7 numbered from the C-terminus (Figure S1).(1−3) Esculentin-1 GN shares sequence similarity with other esculentins-1 and the highest identity of 72% with esculentin-1LTa from H. latouchii.(25) However, the residues in the C-terminus are more conservative than those in N-terminus and show more identity. Furthermore, unlike most of esculentin-1 peptides with 46 residues, there are three extra amino acids in the N-terminus (Figure S1). All results indicate that esculentin-1 has distinctive primary and tertiary structures.
    The taxonomy and evolutionary history of the family Ranidae are still controversial. A maximum parsimony analysis on the basis of the amino acid sequences of the known esculentins-1 from different ranids could be taken as support for the nowadays revised taxonomy of ranid species.(26) The frogs taxonomically belonging to the genus Rana (R. sevosa, R. capito, R. areolate, R. palustris, R. pipiens, R. versabilis) construct a clear clade supporting the monophyly of the group. Likewise, P. nigromaculatus, P. esculentus, and P. ridibundus segregate together as do O. ishikawae, O. graham, and O. hosii. The deduced sister-group relationship between O. hosii and O. ishikawae is in line with conclusions derived from nucleotide sequence analysis of mitochondrial 12S and 16S rRNA genes although esculentin-1SIa from O. ishikawae seems to be more close to the peptide from O. graham.(27) Additionally, we observed that H. luctuosa was assigned a close sister-group relationship to H. latouchii and they comprised a separate cluster supported by a bootstrap value of 76. This result is consistent with the taxonomic assignments of Frost in which both species formerly grouped into the expanded genus Rana are now laid in the genus Hylarana.(26) Noteworthy, H. guentheri is less closely related to the other species in the data set (Figure S2), which disagrees with the current amphibian classification. So further endeavors should be made to identify and characterize more esculentins-1 from amphibians.
    It is becoming increasingly clear that most antimicrobial peptides identified from Ranidae show little organized structure in aqueous solution but adopt an ordered structure with α-helix in membrane-like environments such as 50% TFE or SDS micelles.(1,21) Accordingly, the CD spectra of esculentin-1GN dissolved in water show the characteristics of random coil conformations. However, the secondary structure of esculentin-1GN predicted by a bioinformatics method and confirmed by CD adopts an amphipathic α-helix structural feature with the hydrophilic cationic amino acids concentrating on one side of the helix and the hydrophobic residues segregating on the other (Figure 2A and Figure 2B), which allows esculentin-1GN to bind intensively and permeate into negatively charged bacterial membranes and finally cause perturbation of the cell membrane and the death of bacteria.(28,29) In agreement, FITC-labeled esculentin-1GN can bind to E. coli and S. aureus cells after 5 min incubation (Figure 6A). Both SEM analysis and bacterial staining experiments also imply that the bacteria-killing action of esculentin-1GN is due to the formation of transmembrane pores and lysis of bacterial cells (Figure 6B,C). In addition, the truncated derivatives of esculentin-1GN with less helix rate in the membrane-mimetic environments or lower positive charge at a neutral pH show weaker antibacterial activities, which indicates that the amino acid composition, positive charge, and amphipathicity importantly affected the antimicrobial potency of esculentin-1GN (Figure 4, Tables S1 and S2).
    LPS is the major cell surface component of Gram-negative bacteria and considered the first target of positively charged AMPs.(30,31) The net charge value of esculentin-1GN is +6.846 at pH 7.0, which possibly makes it interact with negatively charged bacterial membranes and LPS. As shown by the CD spectra analysis, esculentin-1GN shows an unordered conformation in water but exhibits predominantly α-helical structure in LPS micelles, indicating the interaction of the peptide and LPS happens synchronously with folding (Figure 2F). The addition of LPS also induces the decrease of the antimicrobial activity of esculentin-1GN against S. aureus (Figure 7A). Furthermore, esculentin-1GN induces mass agglutination of E. coli, while this bacteria agglutination is completely abolished after its incubation with LPS (Figure 7D). Overall, all these results clearly suggest that esculentin-1GN can bind LPS. To further understand these effects, ITC titration and SPRi experiments were performed to provide helpful information which is very important for identifying fundamental forces associated with the binding process. The binding affinity Kd value is about 15 μM for the exothermic process (Figure 7E). This value reveals the strong binding between the peptide and LPS. The O-polysaccharide, which consists of a great deal of oligosaccharide units, is a key component of LPS on the surface of Gram-negative bacteria. Although numerous different types of monosaccharides exist in the structure of O-polysaccharide, d-(+)-galacturonic acid has been reported in several E. coli serotypes such as O110, O48, and so on.(32,33) To identify whether the reaction between the positively charged esculentin-1GN and negatively charged LPS through either electrostatic interaction or specific binding to oligosaccharide in LPS, a series of monomeric sugars were used to screen the components in LPS responsible for the LPS-esculentin-1GN interaction and only d-(+)-galacturonic acid could inhibit its microbe-agglutinating activities (Figure 7D). What’s more, as we expected, d-(+)-galacturonic acid shows more potent ability to bind esculentin-1GN than LPS in the ITC and SPRi experiments (Figure 7B–F), which also suggests that d-(+)-galacturonic acid may exist in the structure of LPS from E. coli O55:B5.
    Markedly, the ability of esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2, the truncated peptide of esculentin-1GN, to bind LPS evidently increases while its antimicrobial activity against E. coli sharply decreases in comparison with the parent peptide. Besides the O-polysaccharide, the hydrophobic region termed lipid A is also an essential component of LPS on the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria.(30,31) The amphiphilic and anionic properties of lipid A provide it the ability to bind amphipathic and cationic molecules. Thus, it is reasonable that more positively charged esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 has stronger binding to LPS than the parent peptide because of different electrostatic interaction abilities of two peptides with the negatively charged lipid A. Generally, the electrostatic approach toward LPS is just the first step before the Gram-negative cytoplasmic membrane is disturbed by an AMP, and it is also necessary for AMPS to destroy the structure of LPS leaflet and traverse through the outer membrane into the bacterial phosphatidylglycerol-rich membrane leaflets.(34,35) Shang et al. reported that three Trp-containing antimicrobial peptides with high affinity for LPS display low bacteria-killing activity against Gram-negative bacteria due to their inability to destabilize LPS leaflets and pass across the tightly packed LPS molecules.(35) Presumably, the weak ability to disrupt the LPS leaflet and cross the LPS outer layer onto the bacterial cell membrane may be the reason why esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 is weaker against E. coli compared to esculentin-1GN. However, the N-terminal fragments of esculentin-1, Esc(1–18) and Esc(1–21), retain or increase antimicrobial spectrum and ability of the parent peptide.(5−7) The affinity of esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 to d-(+)-galacturonic acid is gently weaker while its antimicrobial activity against P. aeruginosa is almost similar when compared to that of the original esculentin-1GN. Therefore, we cannot rule out that the specific binding of peptide to d-(+)-galacturonic acid is responsible for its decline of antimicrobial activity against E. coli. Further experiments with spheroplasts and LPS molecules with lamellar structure will be helpful to clear this possibility.
    The engagement of TLR4 by LPS activates immune cells, causing production of proinflammatory cytokines, which contributes to the pathophysiology of inflammation, septic shock, and other immune diseases.(36) The capability of several AMPs to neutralize the toxic effect of LPS makes them modulate concomitantly both adaptive and innate immunity, a feature completely different with traditional antibiotics.(30,31,37) Considering esculentin-1GN as LPS binding antimicrobial peptide, we refer that esculentin-1GN has anti-inflammatory effects. As shown in Figure 8, esculentin-1GN is found to significantly inhibit the LPS-induced transcription and production of proinflammatory factors in RAW 264.7 murine macrophage cells, including NO, TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6. Furthermore, the anti-inflammatory mechanism involving in cytokine production was explored with Western blot and results shows that esculentin-1GN evidently inhibits the levels of phosphorylated ERK, JNK, p38, and the nuclear translocation of p65 subunit induced by LPS (Figure 9B–D), which obviously implies that the blockage of TLR4-mediated inflammatory signal transduction pathways by esculentin-1GN accounts for its anti-inflammatory effect in RAW 264.7 cells stimulated by LPS. On the basis of the facts esculentin-1GN has the same anti-inflammatory effects with some AMPs binding TLR4 and esculentin-1GN can bind directly to RAW 264.7 cells besides LPS, it is reasonable to speculate that esculentin-1GN simultaneously bind LPS and TLR4 on the each surface of Gram-negative bacteria and murine macrophage cells, blocking the reaction between LPS and TLR4 thus inactivating downstream signaling transduction mediated by TLR4, which finally reduces the secretion of proinflammatory cytokines.
    Remarkably, esculentin-1GN stimulates IL-10 secretion at all tested concentration while the increase trend of IL-10 production is dose-dependently suppressed when the peptide concentration is more than 20 nM (Figure 8J–L). It is generally accepted that IL-10, the most important anti-inflammatory mediator, regulates and suppresses production of proinflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-1β.(38) The LPS-induced IL-10 production relies essentially on the activation of p38MAPK/MK2 pathway and STAT3 as well as suppression of TTP-targeted degradation of the IL-10 transcripts. Additionally, the release of IFN-β and subsequent activation of IFNAR1 are necessary for LPS-stimulated secretion of IL-10 via another pathway.(39) However, some complicated negative feedbacks exist in the process of IL-10 production. For example, SOCS proteins induced by STAT-activating cytokines can limit STAT-mediated signal-transduction.(40) According to the facts that esculentin-1GN can inhibit the activation of p38MAPK pathway and upregulate IL-10 at all test concentrations while the increase trend of IL-10 production is suppressed at high concentrations, we guess that esculentin-1GN enhances IL-10 production at low concentration through at least two different pathways and the change of the increase trend is due to complete abolishment of the p38MAPK-phosphorylation at the concentration of more than 20 nM. Of course, it is impossible for us to eliminate the presumption that esculentin-1GN at high concentration activates some inverse feedback mechanism related with IL-10 production. Hence, more studies need to be carried out on the effects of esculentin-1GN on IL-10 production induced by LPS to understand the implied mechanism.
    It is generally accepted that cytokines and NO have an important role in formation of edema by inducing vasodilatation and increasing vascular permeability at the site of inflammation.(41) Esculentin-1GN displays suppressive effects on production of pro-inflammatory cytokines and NO in vitro. It thus is reasonable to infer that esculentin-1GN will have anti-inflammatory effects in vivo. Nevertheless, peptide activity usually down-regulates due to their easily clearance by host’s immune system and protease system in vivo. The carrageenan-induced paw edema model investigating new antiphlogistic drugs is used in the present study in order to better evaluate anti-inflammatory effects of esculentin-1GN in vivo. As shown by Figure 10A,B, esculentin-1GN effectively limits the increase of paw volume stimulated by carrageenan administration. Moreover, administration of esculentin-1GN also restrains infiltration of inflammatory cells boosted by carrageenan injection. In particular, administration of esculentin-1GN at 10 mg/kg significantly inhibits the increasing activity of MPO (a marker of neutrophil infiltration) induced by carrageenan injection in the paws (Figure 10C). The result also indicates that esculentin-1GN can suppress neutrophil infiltration into the inflammatory site, which is in line with those observations from histological examination (Figure 10D) and corroborates the rationality of the results about edema volume induced by carrageenan in mouse hind paws. Paw edema formation induced by carrageenan injection is a two-stage event, and the second phase (after 1 h) is primarily attributed to neutrophil infiltration into the inflammatory site and secretion of massive proinflammatory factors including PGE2 and diverse cytokines like IL-1β, IL-6, IL-10, and TNF-α.(41,42) By combination of these data from in vivo experiments with those from in vitro inflammatory cytokine expression analysis, the study fully demonstrates that esculentin-1GN significantly inhibits inflammation process of the late phase in the edema. We hypothesize that the potential suppressive mechanism of esculentin-1GN in edema formation is most likely associated with inhibition of IL-1β and TNF-α production by blockage of p38MAPK pathway in vivo. However, further studies are necessary to explicate the exact mechanism.
    In summary, the present study has reported the identification and characterization of a novel esculentin-1 (esculentin-1GN) with a single structure from the frog skin of H. guentheri. Esculentin-1GN exhibits potent antimicrobial activity against both the ATCC standard strain and multidrug-resistant clinical isolates with high stability. It can rapidly kill bacteria through destruction of the cell membrane and neutralize endotoxins by binding d-(+)-galacturonic acid and LPS. Furthermore, esculentin-1GN changes LPS-induced production of NO, TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, and IL-10 via blockage of p38MAPK and NF-κB inflammatory signaling pathways. Meanwhile, esculentin-1GN can significantly decrease acute inflammation in carrageenan-induced mice paw. All of these properties make esculentin-1GN a leading molecule for designing new potential anti-infective antibiotics.

    4.1. Animals and Ethics Statement

    Adult H. guentheri of both sexes (n = 4; weighing 25–40 g) which were not endangered or protected species were collected from the countryside of Guangzhou, Guangdong Province of China. The frogs were humanely euthanized by CO2 inhalation and sacrificed by decapitation. The frog was washed with deionized water, and the skin was removed immediately, sheared into pieces, and frozen in liquid nitrogen until used. All protocols and procedures involving live animals were approved by the Animal Care and Use Ethics of Southern Medical University and performed in strict accordance with the guidelines approved for Animal Care and Use for Southern Medical University.

    4.2. Molecular Cloning and Characterization of cDNA Encoding Esculentin-1GN

    Total RNA of the H. guentheri frog skin was extracted using TRIzol reagent (Life Technologies, Inc., Waltham, MA, USA) in accordance with the manufacturer’ s instructions. mRNAs were purified from the total RNA by using oligo (dT) cellulose chromatography (Life Technologies Inc., Waltham, MA, USA). RNA was quantified using a Merinton SMA1000 instrument (Merinton, Ann Arbor, MI, USA). The cDNA was synthesized using a SMART cDNA library construction kit (Takara Biotechnology, Dalian, China) according to the protocols. First-strand cDNA was synthesized using a SMART CDS III/3′ PCR primer, a SMART IV oligonucleotide, and SMART MMLV reverse transcriptase supplied by the kit. The second strand was amplified using Advantage Polymerase with a 5′ PCR primer and a 3′ PCR primer CDS III according to the manufacturer’s protocols. The synthesized double-strand cDNA was used as templates to amplify the cDNAs encoding AMPs of H. guentheri. Two oligonucleotide primers, HG (5′-AGATGTT(G/C)ACC(T/A)TGAAGAAATC-3′) which was designed according to the signal peptide of previously reported AMP precursors from H. guentheri frog in the sense direction and 3′ PCR primer CDS III (5′-ATTCTAGAGGCCGAGGCGGCCGACATG-3′) in the antisense direction, were used in PCR reactions.(14,15) PCR amplifications were performed in a reaction mixture containing Gene Tap polymerase (TianGen, Beijing, China) using a thermal cycler (Applied Biosystems, Foster, CA, USA). The PCR procedure was as follows: 5 min at 95 °C; 30 cycles of 20 s at 95 °C, 30 s at 48 °C, 30 s at 72 °C, and finally 72 °C, 10 min for extension. The PCR product (∼350 bp) was purified by gel electrophoresis and cloned into pMD18-T vector (Takara biotechnology, Dalian, China). DNA sequencing was performed by Applied Biosystems DNA analyzer, model 3730XL (Applied Biosystems, Foster, CA, USA).

    4.3. Peptide Synthesis

    Esculentin-1GN, its derivatives, and FITC-labeled esculentin-1GN (FITC conjugates to the N terminus of esculentin-1GN) were synthesized by GL Biochem Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Their physicochemical parameters were shown in Table S1. The crude synthetic peptides were purified with an Inertsil ODS-SP (C-18) reverse-phase HPLC column (Shimazu, Osumi, Japan). Once the purity of a peptide, which was calculated according to the ratio of different peak area, was higher than 95%, the peak was pooled, lyophilized, and further confirmed by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry.

    4.4. Circular Dichroism Analysis

    Circular dichroism (CD) experiments were performed to investigate the secondary structure and the stability of esculentin-1GN in solvent environments. CD spectra were recorded on a Jasco-810 spectropolarimeter (Jasco, Tokyo, Japan) with a 1 mm path-length cell (25 °C, 1 nm interval from 190 to 260 nm). The spectra at 190–260 nm were measured using 0.1 cm path-length cell with 1 nm bandwidth, 1 s response time, and a scan speed of 100 nm/min. Samples were prepared by dissolving peptide powder in different concentrations of SDS solutions (0, 30, 60, 90, and 120 mM) or 60 mM SDS solution with serial concentrations of NaCl (0, 100, 200, and 400 mM), respectively. For the temperature stability assay, esculentin-1GN was solubilized in 60 mM SDS and then was incubated at different temperatures (20, 50, 70, and 90 °C) for 1 h before the CD spectra were measured. The ultimate peptide concentration was 100 μM for all of the above CD experiments. For CD spectra of the peptides in the presence of LPS (L2880, Escherichia coli O55:B5, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) whose average molecular weight used for calculations is 4 kDa, an amount of 50 μM peptides was scanned in the presence of 50 μM LPS. Three consecutive scans per sample were performed and averaged, followed by subtraction of the solvent signal. CD data were expressed as the mean residue ellipticity (θ) in deg·cm2·dmol–1.

    4.5. Antimicrobial Activities Analysis

    Minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC) was determined in 96-well microtiter plates with a 2-fold broth microdilution method as described in our previous paper.(28) In brief, ATCC microorganism strains were obtained from Guangdong Institute of Microbiology and Staphylococcus aureus with drug resistance for ampicillin and benzylpencillin was collected from local hospitals. Microbes were grown in Mueller–Hinton broth (MH broth) at 37 °C to exponential phase and diluted with fresh MH broth to 106 CFU/mL. A serial dilution of peptides with MH broth was prepared in 96-well microtiter plates (50 μL) and added to equal volume of microbial inoculums. Plates were shaken at 37 °C for 10–12 h. The absorbance at 600 nm was determined to obtain the MIC values at which no visible growth of microbes occurred. Ampicillin was used as positive control, and all experiments were performed in quintuplicates.

    4.6. Stability Analysis and LPS Inhibition Assay

    The salt, thermal, and serum stability of esculentin-1GN was evaluated as described by Wei et al. with minor modifications.(31) Briefly, E. coli ATCC 25922 was grown in MH broth to exponential phase and diluted to 106 CFU/mL with fresh MH broth. Esculentin-1GN was treated with different concentration of NaCl solutions (0, 50, 100, 150, 200, and 400 mM) for 1 h at room temperature before gradient dilutions were prepared with MH broth containing normal concentration of NaCl and added to equal volume of microbial inoculums. The mixtures were cultured at 37 °C overnight, and the MIC values of esculentin-1GN against E. coli ATCC 25922 were measured to identify its salt stability. When measuring thermal stability, 500 μM esculentin-1GN in MH broth was incubated at different temperatures (20, 50, 70, and 90 °C) for 1 h or at 37 °C for 0–96 h before MICs of the peptide solutions against E. coli ATCC 25922 were measured. The serum stability assay was used to estimate the effect of serum on the antimicrobial activity of esculentin-1GN. In brief, 500 μM esculentin-1GN solubilized in sterile deionized water was added to human serum in a volume ratio of 1:4 and stood at 37 °C for 0–6 h. At each time point, aliquots were taken and the MICs against E. coli ATCC 25922 were determined, and all experiments were repeated four times.
    The inhibitory effect of LPS on the killing activity of esculentin-1GN was also investigated with the colony forming unit assay. Briefly, LPS was diluted to 0–250 μM with fresh MH broth and incubated with esculentin-1GN at the final concentration of 2- or 4-fold of MIC (3.75 or 7.5 μM) in microtiter wells at room temperature with shaking for 30 min. Then, these mixtures were individually mixed with 106 CFU/mL S. aureus ATCC 25923 at exponential phase. After 60 min incubation at 37 °C, the bacteria were serially diluted in MH broth and plated on MH agar plates. The plates were incubated overnight at 37 °C, and the viable colonies were counted. The control without peptide addition was run under the same conditions, and all experiments were performed in quintuplicate.

    4.7. Membrane Permeability and Morphology Alteration Analysis

    Confocal laser scanning microscopy and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) experiments were carried out to explore the possible action mechanisms of esculentin-1GN against several microorganisms. Membrane permeability assays were performed with the LIVE/DEAD BacLight kit (Invitrogen, Waltham, MA, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, E. coli and S. aureus at late exponential phase were washed three times with PBS and then incubated with the peptides at a final concentration of 1 × MIC for 1.5 h at 37 °C. SYTO9 and propidium iodine (PI) provided in the commercial kit were added to the treated cells and stained at room temperature for 30 min protected from the light. The stained cells were visualized using confocal laser scanning microscopy (Leica TCS SP5; Leica Microsystems, Wetzlar, Germany) with excitation/emission wavelengths of 480/500 nm for SYTO9 and 490/635 nm for PI. During imaging, the living microorganisms with intact membrane were stained by SYTO9 which emitted green fluorescence. However, the dead bacteria with damaged membrane were stained by PI which emitted red fluorescence. About 5–10 single plane images per coverslip were acquired.
    SEM experiment was done according to the method reported by Wei et al.(31) Briefly, log-phase culture of S. aureus (1 × 107 CFU/mL) and E. coli ATCC 25922 (1 × 106 CFU/mL) were incubated with esculentin-1GN (1× MIC) at 37 °C for 30 min. Cultures without peptides were used as the negative control. After the cultures were centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 10 min, the bacterial pellets were fixed with 6% glutaraldehyde solution for 4 h at 4 °C, washed with 0.1 M PBS, pH 7.2, and then again fixed with 1% osmium tetroxide in 0.1 M PBS, pH 7.2, for 1 h. After being rinsed with the same PBS and dehydrated in a graded series of ethanol, cells were frozen in liquid nitrogen cooled tert-butyl alcohol, vacuum-dried overnight, and mounted onto aluminum stubs. After sputter-coating with gold, the prepared samples were analyzed by S-4800 SEM (Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan) under standard operating conditions, and about 5–10 single plane images per sample were acquired.

    4.8. Isothermal Titration Calorimetry (ITC) Assay

    ITC was performed using a MicroCal ITC 200 (GE Healthcare; Chicago, IL, USA). Peptides, LPS, and d-(+)-galacturonic acid (48280, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) stock solutions were prepared by dissolving each component in a 50 mM PBS, pH 7.2, and were degassed under vacuum prior to use. Peptide aliquots (1 mM) were titrated in 1.5 μL aliquots every 2 min into the sample cell filled with 200 μL of 0.05 mM LPS under a constant stirring at 25 °C for 25 injections. For d-(+)-galacturonic acid binding, titration happened under the same conditions when 10 mM d-(+)-galacturonic acid in the syringe was titrated into 200 μL of 0.05 mM esculentin-1GN or esculentin-1GN(1–24)NH2 in the cell. High feedback mode of the instrument was chosen. The equilibrium dissociation constant (Kd) and the enthalpy change (ΔH) were analyzed by fitting to a single-site binding model, after subtraction of the heats of dilution, using the MicroCal Origin software. The Gibb’s free energy change (ΔG) and entropy change (ΔS) were determined from the basic thermodynamic equations, respectively. The experiment was carried out in triplicate.

    4.9. Surface Plasmon Resonance Imaging (SPRi) Assay

    Real time detection of esculentin-1GN binding to LPS and d-(+)-galacturonic acid was recorded and analyzed using a PlexArray HT A100 system (Plexera LLC, Bothell, WA, USA) with bare gold SPRi chip (Nanocapture gold chip, with a gold layer of 47.5 nm thickness). Peptides (2 mM in PBS) were spotted in multiplex (10 spots) onto the gold chip surface and then incubated at 4 °C overnight in a humid box according to the manufacturer’s specifications. The SPRi chip was washed and blocked with 5% (m/v) nonfat milk in PBS overnight before being mounted on the instrument. LPS and d-(+)-galacturonic acid as the analyte were diluted with PBS to concentrations of 12.5, 25, 50 μM and 10, 20, 40 mM, respectively. The SPRi analysis procedure was performed using the following cycle: stabilizing baseline with PBS, passing over three concentrations of LPS or d-(+)-galacturonic acid onto the chip, washing the chip with PBS, and regenerating the chip with 0.5% (v/v) H3PO4 in deionized water. The average results from 10 different spots were used to plot standard deviations, and the average kinetics of the complexes was obtained from a 1:1 Langmuir model of kinetics fitting from three different concentrations. The Kd was counted according to kinetic constants obtained by curve-fitting association and dissociation rates to real-time binding and washing data. All of the data analysis was done using the PLEXEA data analysis module and the ORIGINLab software (OriginLab, Hampton, MA, USA). The experiment was carried out in triplicate.

    4.10. NO and Inflammatory Cytokine Production Measurement

    RAW 264.7 murine macrophage cells were grown in RPMI-1640 with 10% FBS, 100 units/mL ampicillin plus 100 μg/mL streptomycin (Gibco, Waltham, MA, USA) and plated in a 96-well culture plate. After adhesion, the cells were co-incubated LPS (100 ng/mL, E. coli O55:B5, L6529, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) and different concentrations of esculentin-1GN (20, 40, and 80 nM) for 24 h. At the same time, the cells without peptide and/or LPS treatments served as the negative control. The cells were centrifuged and the culture supernatants were collected to measure nitrite level using Griess reagent (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) according to the manufacturer’s protocols. The absorbance at 540 nm was measured with a microplate reader (TECAN Company, Männedorf, Switzerland), and nitrite accumulation level which reflected NO production in medium was deduced from the standard curve obtained with NaNO2 (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA).
    RAW 264.7 murine macrophage cells (2× 105 cells/well) were plated to a 96-well culture plate and were treated with esculentin-1GN plus LPS as described under “NO Measurement” above for 24 h. The cell culture supernatants were collected for TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, and IL-10 analyses with ELISA kits (eBiosciences, San Diego, CA, USA). The absorbance at 450 nm was determined on a microplate reader (TECAN Company, Männedorf, Switzerland). All experiments were repeated four times.

    4.11. Quantitative Real-Time PCR

    RAW 264.7 murine macrophage cells (2 × 105 cells/well) were co-incubated with esculentin-1GN (20, 40, and 80 nM) plus LPS (100 ng/mL) as described under “NO Measurement” above. After treatment for 6 h, the cells were harvested for quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) to measure the mRNA levels of iNOS and inflammatory cytokines TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, and IL-10. Briefly, total RNA was extracted from cells using TRIzol reagent and quantified using a Merinton SMA1000 instrument.     RAW 264.7 murine macrophage cells (1 × 106 cells/well) were cultured in 6-well plate with RPMI 1640 containing 2% FBS. After treatment with esculentin-1GN (20, 40, and 80 nM), the cells were co-incubated with LPS (0, 100 ng/mL) for 30 min and then collected by centrifugation and washed twice with ice-cold PBS. All experiments were performed in triplicates.

    4.13. Paw Edema Assay

    The anti-inflammatory activity was investigated using the carrageenan-induced paw edema model according to the method of Kotsyfakis et al. with minor modifications.(44) In short, both male and female Kunming mice with weight range from 20 to 22 g were purchased from the Laboratory Animal Center of Southern Medical University at 6–8 weeks old and were randomly divided into four groups of six animals each. The mice were maintained under standard acclimatization conditions of 12 h light/dark cycle at 25 °C and fed standard rodent feed procedure. Prior to each injection, the mice pedal volume up to the ankle joint was recorded using plethysmometer (Taimeng PV-200 7500, Chengdu, China). Subsequently, mice were treated with esculentin-1GN (10 mg/kg, po), saline, or indomethacin (5 mg/kg) 1 h before 50 μL of 1% carrageenan suspended in saline or saline was administered into the plantar side of right hind paw. As an index of edema formation, the paw volume was then measured at 1, 2, 4, 8, and 24 h after injection using a plethysmometer. The swelling degree was examined by the Δvolume (a – b), where “a” is the volume of right hind paw after the chemical treatment and “b” is the volume before the treatment. In the secondary experiment, another set of mice were administered successively with esculentin-1GN, indomethacin, saline, and carrageenan as described above. The right hindpaws of all mice were surgically dissected at 4 h postinjection for the histological analysis and myeloperoxidase activity assay.

    4.14. Data Analysis

    qRT-PCR data were analyzed using the 2–ΔΔCT method.(45) Data were plotted and analyzed with Igor and GraphPad Prism 5.0 software (GraphPad Software Inc., La Jolla, CA, USA). One-way analysis of variance with a Bonferroni’s multiple comparison test was used to measure significance when comparing two or more groups with a control group and with a post hoc Tukey test when performing comparisons among three or more groups. The unpaired Student’s t test was used to determine significance between two experimental groups. Values were presented as mean ± SEM, and (∗) p < 0.05, (∗∗) p < 0.01, and (∗∗∗) p < 0.001 were considered statistically significant as compared to control.
