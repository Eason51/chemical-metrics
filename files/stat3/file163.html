    Abstract

    Fragment-based drug discovery is intimately linked to fragment extension approaches that can be accelerated using software for de novo design. Although computers allow for the facile generation of millions of suggestions, synthetic feasibility is however often neglected. In this study we computationally extended, chemically synthesized, and experimentally assayed new ligands for the β2-adrenergic receptor (β2AR) by growing fragment-sized ligands. In order to address the synthetic tractability issue, our in silico workflow aims at derivatized products based on robust organic reactions. The study started from the predicted binding modes of five fragments. We suggested a total of eight diverse extensions that were easily synthesized, and further assays showed that four products had an improved affinity (up to 40-fold) compared to their respective initial fragment. The described workflow, which we call “growing via merging” and for which the key tools are available online, can improve early fragment-based drug discovery projects, making it a useful creative tool for medicinal chemists during structure–activity relationship (SAR) studies.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In the past few years, fragment-based drug discovery (FBDD) has continuously gained in popularity and has become a dominant approach in order to explore novel chemical entities.(1-3) The year 2011 witnessed an important success for this field, with the first drug (vemurafenib) originating from a hit found in a fragment-based screening, reaching FDA approval.(4, 5)
    Fragments are commonly regarded as attractive alternatives compared to standard compounds contained in the libraries used for high-throughput screening campaigns. They are small molecules (MW < 300 Da) and usually offer better solubility, making them favorable in terms of ADMET properties.(6) Moreover, it is easier to cover chemical space with fragment-sized molecules: screening a set of 1000 fragments is claimed to be equivalent to probing the chemical space of 1 000 000 drug-like molecules.(7) Because of their simplicity and few polar interaction points, they also have a higher chance to bind to a given protein with optimal interactions.(8) However, because they are smaller, fragments usually bind with low affinity compared to drug-like molecules, resulting in the need for screening at high concentrations. Yet those that bind usually do so with high ligand efficiency (LE), defined as the ratio of Gibbs free energy of binding (ΔGbind) and the number of heavy atoms.(9) Fragments are thus often considered as advantageous starting points for exploratory synthesis campaigns.
    Several biophysical techniques can deal with the low affinity and therefore high concentration of fragments and allow detection of binding to a protein. Among them, surface plasmon resonance (SPR), isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC), thermal shift assays, and mass spectrometry (MS) have been successfully implemented in fragment-based drug discovery pipelines.(10, 11) However, since the interactions remain weak, structural methods such as X-ray crystallography(12) and NMR(13) might be preferable, as they lead to fewer false positives and negatives during screening campaigns.(7, 14) These two structural techniques can in principle determine the precise binding mode(s) of a fragment within a protein target, thereby also laying the foundation for subsequent structure-based design approaches.
    When no experimental data are available, the binding mode of a fragment within an active site can be predicted using in silico methods, most notably docking. It has to be noted that because of the propensity of fragments to bind in more than one mode, fragment binding mode prediction remains challenging. Moreover, most of the docking programs were parametrized and optimized for drug-like molecules, which might lead to a relative imbalance of the individual terms in a scoring function.(6) Consequently, several programs have been developed more specifically for fragment docking, including MCSS,(15) SuperStar,(16, 17) and SEED.(18, 19)
    Once the binding mode of a fragment has been determined, either experimentally or computationally, it can be used as starting point for an FBDD project, aiming at expanding the fragment into a bigger molecule while concomitantly increasing its affinity.
    Three main approaches are conceivable in order to extend fragments: growing, linking, and merging (Figure 1). Growing is based on one core fragment and aims at extending it within the binding site, looking for additional interactions that could improve affinity or selectivity. Growing is the most frequently used approach because it is considered more straightforward and successful than the other two techniques.(20, 21) The linking strategy describes the process of joining two noncompetitive fragments (i.e., fragments that bind in two different regions of the binding site) by means of a linker. Merging concatenates two fragments that contain a common portion known to bind at the same position in the binding site of a given target. Our approach takes advantage of both growing and merging procedures.
    Growing approaches have extensively been implemented in computer software, including LUDI,(22) SPROUT,(23) CONCERTS,(24) ReCore,(25) Caveat,(26) BREED,(27) GANDI,(28) BROOD,(29) and IADE.(30) Nonetheless, one of the main challenges of these softwares is to ensure the synthetic tractability of the designed molecules. Several rules can be implemented in order to assess whether or not a compound is synthetically feasible, among the most frequently used are BRICS,(31) RECAP,(32) or an estimation of the synthetic accessibility score.(33) These approaches are based on knowledge of chemical reactions in order to analyze the molecules.
    Proceeding the other way around is also possible: concatenating two available building blocks using a compatible chemical reaction. Hartenfeller et al. developed a library of 58 robust organic reactions,(34, 35) based on chemical reactions often-used in the pharmaceutical domain.(36) These reactions are used in the PINGUI (Python in silico de novo growing utilities) toolbox and define the first step of the “growing via merging” (GVM) workflow presented in this study: creating a focused library consisting only of those building blocks that are compatible with the core fragment in terms of further synthesis.
    The second constraint of our approach for fragment growing is the assumption that each individual building block should engage in favorable interactions with the protein by itself. In an alternative approach, where extensions are attached to core fragments and the resulting compounds are docked, slightly unfavorable extensions might be compensated by the interaction of the core fragment and thus masked. Within the GVM workflow, we generate maps composed of a large number of favorable poses for each extension (i.e., building block). On the basis of the crystallographic or computed binding mode of the core fragment, building blocks are then chosen such that reactive groups are within geometrically feasible distances and angles, therefore allowing bond formation between the core and an already optimally placed building block to occur. Basically, this step is equivalent to merging two fragments at their reactive centers, which will be described in more detail below. In this way, fragment chemical space and positional space are pruned at an early stage, and only optimally positioned fragments with favorable interactions are retained. After computationally generating the products from the building blocks, we ensure that the initial assumption (i.e., that each component of a molecule is, when regarded individually, located in an optimal spot) is still valid by re-docking the entire product. A large deviation from the original (predicted) binding mode would violate this assumption and lead to the removal of the product from our GVM workflow. Hence, growing is guided by both the synthetic tractability and the protein structure. Figure S1 in the Supporting Information shows a comprehensive scheme of our GVM workflow.
    In this manuscript, we describe the design of new ligands of the β2AR (Table S1) by employing the PINGUI toolbox and the semiautomated GVM workflow. Five different fragments were grown. They have in common that they are predicted to bind in the orthosteric site with a reaction-compatible reactive feature (herein an amine) close to the secondary binding pocket (SBP; Figure 2). Therefore, each GVM step was directed toward the SBP. Experimental results demonstrated that all eight suggested molecules could be successfully synthesized and half of them had a more favorable affinity than the initial fragment, with the best compound offering an almost 40-fold improvement.

    β2AR Binding Site

    The β2AR binding pocket sits buried in the extracellular part of the receptor with only a small fraction accessible to solvent. For the sake of this study, we divided the binding site into two smaller cavities: the orthosteric site and the SBP (Figure 2). While most of the orthosteric site is hydrophobic,(37) important polar residues shape its contour: Ser2035.42, Ser2045.43, and Ser2075.46 in transmembrane helix 5 (TM5) define the bottom of the orthosteric site (superscript indices refer to Ballesteros–Weinstein numbering(38)) and are involved in receptor activation.(39-41) Residues Asp1133.32 in TM3 and Asn3127.39 and Tyr3167.43 in TM7 offer strong electrostatic points that interact with the amine moieties of most known ligands. The SBP is defined by a small hydrophobic cavity (Trp1093.28, Phe1935.32, and Ile3097.36) and a number of more solvent exposed polar residues (Asp192ECL2 and His932.64) that agonists are known to interact with.(39-43)
    The difference in the orthosteric site between the basal and active receptor conformation is manifest mostly in His2966.58 and the size of the orthosteric pocket.(39, 42) In the active-like receptor conformation (PDB code 4LDE), His2966.58 is part of a large polar network involving the catechol moiety of the ligand, a water molecule, Tyr3087.35, Ser2045.43, and Asn2936.55.(42, 43) This polar network is located close to the orthosteric site, making it smaller in the active conformation (Figure 2). In the basal conformation of the receptor, the aforementioned polar network is not so extended, resulting in an inward shift of His2966.58.(39, 42) This shift results in a larger orthosteric site.

    Core Fragments

    Five fragment-sized ligands with experimentally determined affinity were used as core fragments (i.e., starting points) in this growing study. Compounds 1–4 (Figure 4) with favorable ligand efficiency (LE = 0.49, 0.39, 0.35, and 0.31, respectively, against the β2AR-Nb80) had emerged from an experimental screening with nanobody-stabilized receptors.(44) Compound 5 (Figure 4) had initially been discovered in a docking screen against the β2AR in a basal conformation with the same docking setup as used by Kolb et al.(37) Compound 5 also showed favorable LE (0.34) against the β2AR-Nb80. Compounds 1–5 had a known preference for the receptor conformation stabilized in an active state by Nb80 (Table 1). We will refer to such molecules as “agonist candidate” (AC) to clearly distinguish them from assay-confirmed (partial) agonists.

    Prediction of the Binding Mode of the Core Fragments

    We discuss compounds 1–4 and compound 5 separately, as they were docked to receptor structures in different activation states. Compounds 1–4 and compound 5 were placed in the binding site of the receptor active (PDB code 4DLE) and basal (PDB code 2RH1) conformations, respectively, using DOCK 3.6.(45) The predicted binding modes were kept for further growing and visually examined for plausibility.

    Compounds 1–4

    The growing procedure started from docked poses of compounds 1–4. We note that no direct interactions with the polar serines at the bottom of the orthosteric site were formed in the poses predicted in an active conformation of the β2AR for these compounds. Three of the core fragments contained an acceptor group, but they were too far away (>4 Å) and in unproductive geometries to form direct polar interactions with the serines in helix 5: the benzothiophene of compound 2, the thiazole of compound 3, and the pyrimidine moiety of compound 4. All the charged amines of the core fragments were found to interact with Asp1133.32 and Asn3127.39. The aromatic moieties of the core fragments made hydrophobic interactions with Phe2896.51. As shown in Figure 3, all of these fragments were predicted to bind to the orthosteric site.

    Compound 5

    Because no reliable binding mode was found in the active conformation of the β2AR by our docking methods, the second growing procedure started from the docked pose of compound 5 in the basal conformation. The docking solution is consistent with a pose expected for a binder of the β2AR: the charged amine moiety interacted with Asp1133.32 and Asn3127.39, the indole moiety made hydrophobic interactions with Phe2896.51, the methyl on the indole interacted with both Val1143.33 and Val1173.36, and the ether moiety engaged Asn2936.55 in a polar hydrogen bond. As shown in Figure 3e, we predicted compound 5 to bind to the orthosteric site with room to grow toward the SBP.

    Chemical Derivatization by Reductive Amination

    All the fragments of this study share a common chemical feature: an amine that will be positively charged at physiological pH and thus likely interacts with Asp1133.32, according to the standard pharmacophore for β2AR ligands. This reactive group can also be harnessed to grow the fragment toward the SBP. The most straightforward reaction that can be used with such a functional group is reductive amination because the resulting amine can still be charged, and thus the charge–charge interaction with Asp1133.32 will not be lost. This reaction involves a primary or secondary amine reacting with an aldehyde or a ketone in order to create a secondary or tertiary amine. The SMARTS code for the reaction was used as encoded by Hartenfeller et al.(34)

    Surrogate Creation

    During the first docking stage with SEED,(18, 19) all synthetically compatible building blocks (i.e., all fragments amenable to being used as extension of the core fragment) were docked individually in order to find the ones that are predicted to fit the SBP and form favorable interactions. However, in their unreacted forms, the building blocks will usually possess atoms or atom groups that will not be present in the final product. Thus, docking a building block in its unreacted form would not reflect its interaction options once attached to the core fragment. In order to mimic the behavior of the final product and minimize the occurrence of unlikely interactions, we converted all building blocks to “surrogates”. We define these surrogates as the final product without the unreactive part of the core fragment. In the specific case presented here, the reaction to be applied to all cores was reductive amination. Surrogates are thus the reactants minus the carbonyl groups but including the amine (Schemes 1 and 2). In this way, no additional interactions that could influence the placement of the reactant are available. 18 785 compatible building blocks (i.e., aldehydes or ketones) were extracted from the fragments now subset of the ZINC database(46) and converted to a new set of 26 892 surrogates (see Experimental Section). The increase in number resulted from the exhaustive enumeration of all possible stereoisomers.

    Surrogate Docking, Filtering, and Ranking

    Of the 26 892 surrogates, 15 702 were successfully docked with SEED to the SBP of the β2AR in an active conformation (PDB code 4LDE) yielding a total of 814 369 docking poses. The same docking procedure was applied to the basal conformation (PDB code 2RH1) and 16 297 surrogates were successfully docked, yielding 1 011 929 poses. These numbers intuitively make sense, since in the basal conformation, the entrance of the orthosteric site (included in the SBP) is wider than in the active conformation, and therefore more molecules can be docked in clash-free conformations. Only those surrogate poses that did not overlap with the core fragment were kept for further processing, as illustrated in Figure 5a. Then, a filter was applied based on the distance between the amine of the surrogate and the predicted position of the amine of the core fragment (i.e., the docking pose). This cutoff was set to 2 Å. Every pose that passed both criteria was ranked according to the score calculated by SEED. Only the top 500 surrogates (without duplicates) were kept for further processing (i.e., generation of the products).

    Derivative Product Creation and Docking

    For each core fragment, the top 500 surrogates were attached according to the reaction scheme for reductive amination which yielded equally many (500) derivative products. Then, the top 500 derivative products were docked into the binding site of the aforementioned β2AR conformation and visually inspected in order to narrow down the number of candidates for possible further synthesis. The selection of the best derivative products was based on several criteria: docking score, shape complementarity with the receptor, hydrogen bonds to the receptor, overlap of the products with the respective core fragment and surrogate (Figure 6), and chemical diversity. Eight ligands not violating these criteria were selected and synthesized (Figure 7).

    Radioligand Displacement Assay

    In lieu of performing cellular assays to determine the efficacy of compounds, we decided to use a “reverse pharmacology” assay as described in ref 44. Since this assay uses radioligand displacement, it allows the precise determination of affinity, which is the quantity estimated in our docking calculations and should therefore be used to assess the validity of our predictions. At the same time, because of the way the assay is set up, we can also make a statement about the likely efficacy of each compound. As shown in the original manuscript,(44) there is a high degree of correlation between this prediction and the actual cellular efficacy.
    The summary of the radioligand displacement assay for the eight products is illustrated in Table 1. The IC50 curves for compound 11, which showed improved affinity, are depicted in Figure 8, and the curves for remaining compounds are listed in the Supporting Information (Figure S5).

    Analysis of the Derivative Products’ Binding Modes and Activities

    In the following discussion, only the predicted interactions of the synthesized products in the SBP will be detailed, as the interactions in the orthosteric pocket remained constant in all cases, as required by our initial hypothesis. Compounds which are not detailed here are discussed in the Supporting Information (Figure S3).

    Compound 8

    Compound 8 (Figure 9) features a chiral center and was selected for synthesis because it contains a toluene moiety that can make favorable hydrophobic interactions with the apolar part of the SBP. The R-isomer was predicted to fit into the SBP, forming hydrophobic contacts with Trp1093.28 and Ile3097.36. On the other hand, the S-isomer was docked such that we observed steric clashes in the SBP, thereby forcing the amine out of range from the key Asp1133.32. Thus, the S-isomer is left with two unmatched hydrogen bond donors, thus making it harder to compensate for the entropic penalty incurred upon removal of the water shell. The docking score also predicts the R-isomer to be more favorable than the S-isomer, which is in line with the aforementioned reasoning. Compound 8 was tested as a racemic mixture and displayed an improved affinity (Ki = 4.39 μM, LE = 0.27) compared to its initial core fragment compound 4 (Ki = 78.1 μM, LE = 0.31) against β2AR-Nb80. The selectivity shift between the two conformations was still high (61; cf. Table 2), thus classifying compound 8 as an AC molecule. We note, however, that the affinity of one of the enantiomers, presumably the R-isomer, could actually be higher than the one measured.

    Compounds 11–13

    With this series based on compound 5, we wished to explore different substitutions on the benzene ring located in the SBP and the importance of the flexible chain between the two rigid scaffolds. Two of the compounds (compounds 11 and 12) contain a flexible chain with six rotatable bonds, while compound 13 has four rotatable bonds.
    Compound 11 showed the highest improvement in our elaborated molecules. It bound with a Ki of 520 nM against β2AR-Nb80, corresponding to a nearly 40-fold improvement of the initial compound 5 while maintaining a decent LE (from 0.34 to 0.30). Interestingly, it showed a Ki of 4 μM against β2AR-Nb69 (9-fold improvement only). Thus, the extended compound showed a stronger AC profile with shift fold of 0.2 (Table 2). One could speculate that Trp1093.28 and Ile3097.36, which are predicted to interact with the chlorobenzene moiety, might be involved in the activation of the receptor.
    Compound 12 was tested and also had a more favorable affinity (Ki = 10.4 μM, LE = 0.20) against β2AR-Nb80 compared to its initial core fragment (Ki = 20.4 μM, LE = 0.34), but the LE was not maintained. It contains a trifluoromethyl moiety, rendering it bulkier than compound 11, which only has a chlorine substituent. The docking pose suggests that compound 12 could furthermore entertain hydrophobic contacts with Trp1093.28 and Ile3097.36.
    Upon testing of compound 13, we found that it had a decreased affinity and LE (Ki = 80.4 μM, LE = 0.15) against β2AR-Nb80 compared to its initial core fragment (Ki = 20.4 μM, LE = 0.34). This small decrease in affinity (4-fold) can probably be explained by the size of the extension: it introduces more steric constraints in the SBP, thereby disrupting the hydrophobic interactions with Trp1093.28. Compound 13 has two rotatable bonds less than its two aforementioned analogs, and this could suggest that compound 13 has less flexibility for adapting to the SBP and therefore its interactions are less optimal.
    Taken together, we elaborated each fragment with at least one extension. Moreover, we created a small set of three and two derivatives, respectively, for core fragments 3 and 5. Four of the eight derivatives showed higher affinity, a 50% improvement rate.

    Conclusions

    Starting from a diverse set of fragments exhibiting affinity for the β2AR, we constructed extended derivatives guided by the protein structure. For 50% of the ligands, we were able to improve the initial affinities. The in silico workflow “growing via merging” we developed for this purpose tackles three challenges simultaneously. First, the fragments need to be grown toward a region offering enough space for the addition of another building block. Second, the suggested molecules should have a high likelihood of synthesizability. Third, the resulting molecules should have a high probability of binding to the target protein. The GVM workflow does this by focusing on a small number of candidates and pruning search space at an early stage.
    All the initial fragments in this study had two points in common: they were predicted to bind to the orthosteric site of the β2AR and they all contained a protonatable amine presumably interacting with Asp1133.32. Thus, an extension of the fragments in the direction of the SBP was feasible. In the interest of focusing on molecules that are likely to be synthesizable, we selected reductive amination from the library of 58 robust organic reactions.(34) Other approaches exist that can generate molecules with a high likelihood of synthesizability,(47) and it will depend on the specific protein and reaction which approach might be considered more appropriate by researchers who apply it. Importantly for usability, however, our tool is freely accessible online and does not require any software to be installed.
    The choice of reductive amination in this study was guided by the fact that the amine is the reaction center and is preserved in the reaction. Thus, all the generated products can extend past Asp1133.32 to the SBP while maintaining the salt bridge with this residue. All eight molecules that we suggested were easily synthesized, highlighting that reductive amination can indeed be regarded as a robust reaction. Of note, despite using this standard reaction, all of our final compounds are well below a Tanimoto coefficient of 0.4 when compared to known ligands (Table S1). Such compounds are commonly regarded as chemically different indeed. Furthermore, our compounds are distinct from the pharmacophore of β2AR agonists (phenylethylamine with a hydroxy group in β position) as they do not have the β-hydroxy moiety. This emphasizes the power of FBDD: growing from diverse fragments yields chemically novel compounds. When compared to known adrenoreceptor ligands,(48) compound 11 and labetolol share a similar extension past the amine (i.e., propylbenzene). This could suggest that this compound could prefer binding to the β2AR over the β3AR. Most importantly, however, this indicates that our workflow could also be applied to design selective ligands among receptor subtypes.
    In the interest of increasing the chance of our suggestions being active, our workflow prioritized extensions that entertain favorable interactions with the protein by themselves. We assume that the two-step docking of first the surrogate and then the resulting product leads to an increase in the robustness of our predictions. This has also recently been suggested in a different article focusing on the design of ligands for STAT3.(49) In our approach, whenever a promising product deviated too much from its initially predicted position, it was not pursued further. We are aware that there are retrosynthetic experimental analyses, including X-ray crystallography of the resulting fragments, that show that such binding mode faithfulness is not always the case.(50) However, one can afford to be more elitist in this in silico approach, as combinatorics works in our favor by generating such a large number of solutions that it needs to be pruned. Moreover, docking is notorious for producing many false positives,(51, 52) and applying stringent selection criteria often improves true positive rates. In our hands, these considerations allowed us to significantly improve 50% of the original fragment binders. It is difficult to assess whether this is better or worse than what a human medicinal chemist might have achieved. However, when one takes a look at current literature examples, an improvement rate of 50% seems higher than what is usually reported.
    The presented workflow is not strictly automatized, because it relies on spatial reasoning abilities during the evaluation of the poses. Yet as most of the growing steps rely on computational approaches, we decided to make them freely available to the public. Thus, we created PINGUI, a toolbox that we hope will help medicinal chemists as an idea generator in fragment-based ligand discovery project. PINGUI is available online at www.kolblab.org/scubidoo/pingui and is implemented alongside the SCUBIDOO database.(53) PINGUI features many options that were instrumental in this project, namely, displaying the compatible reaction for any given fragment, the creation of customized derivative libraries, a retrosynthesis module, or the option of filtering a library based on reactive chemical features. The addition of such functions should provide SCUBIDOO users with a full framework for virtual product generation. A description of the interface of PINGUI is available in the Support Information.
    As with SCUBIDOO, we do not see the utility of this approach so much in the probability that it will yield a highly potent compound during an initial round of optimization but rather in providing medicinal chemists with creative suggestions. Hence, we believe that PINGUI is best employed at the very early stages, when SAR needs to be generated quickly and with limited synthetic efforts. In contrast to SCUBIDOO, which is limited by the number of fragments it was built on, PINGUI can be regarded as a flexible and basically unlimited alternative when navigating into the immensity that is chemical space. Indeed, PINGUI generates a tailored chemical space defined by the user (the fragment to grow but also the library to react with) while respecting the constraints imposed by the binding site of the target protein. This tremendously reduces the number of products to virtually screen to merely hundreds or thousands. We also think that it will enable researchers who are not dyed-in-the-wool organic chemists (such as ourselves) to develop and synthesize comparatively simple substances, thereby speeding up research and potentiating the availability of pharmacologically active chemical matter.

    Docking calculations were performed with the basal conformation of the β2AR in complex with carazolol (PDB code 2RH1)(54, 55) and an active conformation in complex with the ligand BI167107 (PDB code 4LDE).(42) All ligands, solvent, lipid molecules as well as the T4-lysozyme insertion or the stabilizing nanobody Nb6B9 were removed. The hydrogens were placed and minimized using the HBUILD module in CHARMM.(56) CHARMm22(57) atom types and MPEOE(58, 59) partial charges were assigned using the program Witnotp [Novartis Pharma AG, unpublished].

    Surrogate Creation

    The fragments now data set was downloaded from the ZINC database(46) and contained 504 074 building blocks. Every compatible building block was then converted into the corresponding surrogate by means of a Python script written using the RDKit library.(60) Reaction centers (the nitrogen of the introduced amine group) of the surrogates were flagged in order to facilitate filtering later on. In the case of ketones, where condensation with an amine results in the introduction of a chiral center, both stereoisomers for the surrogate were generated using flipper.(61) In the context of docking, CHARMm22 atom types(57) and MPEOE partial charges(58, 59) were assigned using the program Witnotp (Novartis Pharma AG, unpublished). Up to 100 conformers were generated for each surrogate using OMEGA.(62)

    Docking the Surrogates with SEED

    SEED(18, 19) has been developed to exhaustively place small rigid fragments in the binding sites of proteins. The calculations also take into account the penalty that is incurred upon binding due to the removal of the water shells of the ligand and the protein, respectively. For each fragment, 106–107 individual poses are generated and subsequently clustered based on geometric and energetic criteria. This yields on the order of hundreds of poses, clustered into groups of a maximum of five. These maps were used to select poses with favorable energy and geometrically appropriate orientation with respect to bond formation.

    Docking the Derivative Products with DOCK

    For each fragment to be grown, the corresponding products were generated from the top 500 surrogates with favorable scores and appropriate geometry using PINGUI. Product conformations were generated using the dbgen pipeline described for ZINC.(63) Since DOCK does not evaluate intramolecular energy terms, some docked poses might show slightly unfavorable geometries. In order to ameliorate the clashes of such poses while keeping the overall binding mode found by DOCK, the poses were minimized using the force field including a Poisson–Boltzmann model for solvation as implemented in SZYBKI.(64) All products were then reranked according to the score calculated by SZYBKI and kept for subsequent visual inspection.

    Reductive amination of the respective aldehyde or ketone derived from the surrogate library with fragments 1–5 rendered the proposed modulators. All commercially available reagents and solvents were used without further purification.

    Radioligand Displacement Assay

    Compounds were examined for their ability to inhibit the binding of [3H]-dihydroalprenolol ([3H]-DHA; 2 nM final concentration) to membranes of Sf9 cells expressing the β2AR. An amount of 5 μg of total protein was mixed with each compound in concentrations ranging from 10–10 M to 10–3 M. The reaction mixtures were incubated for 2 h at rt, and free radioligand was removed by filtering over a Whatman GF/C filter. Filters were washed six times, then dried, and 40 μL of scintillation fluid (MicroScintTM-O, PerkinElmer) was added. Radioactivity (counts per minutes [cpm]) retained on the filters was determined in a Wallac MicroBeta TriLux scintillation counter. The half-maximal inhibitory concentrations (IC50) for these compounds were calculated from normalized dose–response curves obtained using a one-site competition binding model (nonlinear regression analyses) of the GraphPad Prism software program.(65) Each assay was performed in triplicates.
    For each compound to be tested, a comparative assay was performed. A first assay was with a β2-AR-nanobody fusion locked in an active state by a G-protein-mimicking nanobody called β2AR-Nb80.(40) A second screen was conducted relative to the basal state of the same receptor in a fusion with an irrelevant nanobody called β2AR-Nb69.(44) Doing so allowed one to classify the candidate efficacy of each hit (i.e., agonist (AC), antagonist (AntC), or inverse-agonist candidates (IAC)).(67) An AC molecule is defined by a shift in affinity between β2AR-Nb69 and β2AR-Nb80 higher than 1.1 (i.e., the compound is more selective toward β2AR-Nb69). An IAC molecule is defined by a shift in affinity between β2AR-Nb69 and β2AR-Nb80 below 0.9. An AntC molecule is defined when no obvious selectivity is observed between the two receptors (shift of 1 ± 0.1). IC50 values were transformed to Ki using the Cheng–Prusoff equation.(68)
