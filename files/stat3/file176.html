    Abstract

    In prostate cancer, two different types of histone lysine demethylases (KDM), LSD1/KDM1 and JMJD2/KDM4, are coexpressed and colocalize with the androgen receptor. We designed and synthesized hybrid LSD1/JmjC or “pan-KDM” inhibitors 1–6 by coupling the skeleton of tranylcypromine 7, a known LSD1 inhibitor, with 4-carboxy-4′-carbomethoxy-2,2′-bipyridine 8 or 5-carboxy-8-hydroxyquinoline 9, two 2-oxoglutarate competitive templates developed for JmjC inhibition. Hybrid compounds 1–6 are able to simultaneously target both KDM families and have been validated as potential antitumor agents in cells. Among them, 2 and 3 increase H3K4 and H3K9 methylation levels in cells and cause growth arrest and substantial apoptosis in LNCaP prostate and HCT116 colon cancer cells. When tested in noncancer mesenchymal progenitor (MePR) cells, 2 and 3 induced little and no apoptosis, respectively, thus showing cancer-selective inhibiting action.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The covalent modifications of histones, including acetylation, methylation, and phosphorylation, play central roles in DNA replication/repair and in the regulation of transcription.(1-3) In contrast to lysyl-acetylation, which normally correlates with transcriptional activation, lysyl-methylation correlates with both activation and repression depending on the context, including lysine residue location and the extent (me1, me2, or me3) of methylation. In general, methylation of histone H3 at Lys residues 9 or 27 (H3K9 or H3K27) or histone H4 at Lys 20 (H4K20) is associated with repression, whereas H3K4, H3K36, and H3K79 methylation correlates with enhanced transcription.(2) Lysine methylation was once thought to be irreversible; however, the identification first of the lysine-specific demethylases (LSDs) followed by the Jumonji C (JmjC) demethylases, has led to the proposal that it is dynamic.(3)
    The human LSDs (LSD1 and LSD2, KDM1) demethylate histone substrates via a flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD)-dependent amine oxidase type mechanism. They catalyze the overall transfer of hydride from the methyl amino group to FAD, thus generating a methyliminium ion, which is in turn hydrolyzed to yield a carbinolamine that spontaneously degrades to give formaldehyde and the demethylated amine (Scheme 1A).(4, 5) For mechanistic reasons, the LSDs demethylate only mono- and dimethylated lysines. LSD1 is specific for H3K4me1/me2 as a component of the CoREST corepressor complex,(4) but in the presence of the androgen receptor (AR), its specificity changes from H3K4 to H3K9.(6) Thus, LSD1 can act either as a transcriptional corepressor or coactivator depending on its binding partners and substrates. Pathologically, LSD1 was found to be oncogenic and/or overexpressed in several cancers ranging from leukemias to solid tumors.(5, 7-9) In addition, LSD1 also regulates gene transcription of viruses such as hepatitis B virus (HBV),(10) herpes simplex virus (HSV), and varicella zoster virus (VZV).(11, 12)
    The JmjC enzymes are Fe(II) and 2-oxoglutarate (2OG) -dependent oxygenases and represent the largest lysyl-demethylase (KDM) superfamily, comprising ∼20 human enzymes grouped into five subfamilies (KDM2/7, KDM3, KDM4, KDM5, and KDM6). Mechanistically, the reaction between Fe(II)/2OG complex and molecular oxygen converts 2OG into carbon dioxide, succinate, and an enzyme-bound Fe(IV)═O species. The Fe(IV)═O species reacts with the methyl group of the methylated histone lysine residue to produce an hemiaminal intermediate that spontaneously generates formaldehyde and demethylated lysine while regenerating the active Fe(II) center (Scheme 1B). In contrast to the LSDs, the JmjC KDMs can demethylate all three lysine-methylation states.(5, 13) JmjC demethylases are overexpressed in multiple types of cancer cells,(2, 3, 5, 13, 14) and some JmjC demethylases have been involved in neural development and/or function and are associated with noncancer diseases including X-linked mental retardation, autism, and midline defects.(15-17)
    In addition to histones, LSD1 and some JmjCs interact with nonhistone proteins that also contribute to transcriptional and/or nontranscriptional related processes. Specifically, LSD1 demethylates the tumor suppressor p53 at its K370me2 residue, repressing p53-mediated transcriptional activation and p53-induced apoptosis,(18, 19) and negatively regulates the signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) by demethylation of its K140me2 residue.(20) Demethylation of the transcription factor E2F1 in K185 by LSD1 regulates E2F1-induced cell death, but in this case opposing effects are reported(21, 22) and further studies are required. LSD1 is also able to demethylate myosin phosphatase MYPT1,(23) enhancing its ubiquitin-mediated degradation and increasing the amount of phosphorylated retinoblastoma protein RB1 with subsequent effects on the cell cycle. Moreover, K1096 in the DNA methyltransferase DNMT1 seems to be a substrate for LSD1,(24) thus establishing cross-talk between DNA and histone methylation. With respect to nonhistone JmjC substrates, mouse KDM6B interacts with p53 during neural stem cell differentiation and stabilizes p53 by demethylation, allowing its accumulation in the nucleus.(25) The K218 and K221 residues of the p65 subunit of the nuclear factor κB (NF-κB) may also be targets of KDM2A, that in this way negatively regulates NF-κB activity and transcription.(26)
    In human prostate cancers, LSD1 and JmjC KDM4A/C are coexpressed and colocalize with androgen receptor (AR).(27) Recently, KDM4A/C inhibitors were found not to inhibit prostate (LNCaP and PC3) and colon (HCT116) cancer cell growth in isolation, but they displayed antiproliferative effects in combination with NCL-2, an LSD1 inhibitor,(28) suggesting a potential for synergy in LSD and JmjC KDM inhibition.
    It is now clear that in multifactorial diseases such as cancer, central nervous system disorders, diabetes, or immunoinflammatory diseases, which involve multiple altered cellular pathways and signals, the use of single-target drugs (the “one drug, one target” concept) can result in unsatisfactory treatment outcomes.(29-31) Combination therapies employing two or more drugs can be more efficient in controlling such complex disease systems and less prone to resistance.(32, 33) An alternative strategy to combination therapy is to develop a single chemical entity that is able to modulate multiple targets simultaneously (designed multiple ligands, DMLs).(34-36) The overall goal of the DML approach is to enhance the efficacy and/or improve the safety of therapy with respect to drug combination. Additional advantages of DMLs are the reduction of pill burden for patients, due to the administration of a single compound, a lower risk of drug–drug interactions, and improvement in medication adherence.
    Building on previous works,(37-40) we describe here pan-demethylase inhibitors 1–6 (Figure 1) that simultaneously inhibit both LSD1 and JmjC KDMs. Compounds 1–6 were designed by coupling tranylcypromine (7), a known LSD1 inhibitor,(38) and 4-carboxy-4′-carbomethoxy-2,2′-bipyridine 8(41) or 5-carboxy-8-hydroxyquinoline (9, 5-carboxy-8HQ),(42) two 2OG competitive templates developed for JmjC inhibition (Figure 1). The molecular weight of these pan-demethylase inhibitors is acceptable because they result from combination of low molecular weight molecules.
    The compounds were selected on the basis of known inhibition results and crystallographic analyses of both the LSDs and JmjC KDMs. On the basis of manual docking studies (Figure 2), we proposed that conjugation of tranylcypromine to the carboxylate group of either 4-carboxy-4′-carbomethoxy-2,2′-bipyridine 8(41) (to form 1 and 2) or 5-carboxy-8-hydroxyquinoline 9(42) (to form 3–6), would give compounds that are capable of binding to both the LSD and KDM4 active sites (for description of docking, see Supporting Information). On the basis of the LSD1 structure (PDB 2XAJ),(38) the bipyridyl (for 1) and hydroxyquinoline (for 3) rings are predicted to protrude into the LSD1 substrate binding pocket, with the tranylcypromine moiety conjugating to FAD, as occurs during inhibition by tranylcypromine 7 alone (Figure 2A).(38) Analysis of the active site of KDM4A (PDB 3PDQ)(43) leads to the proposal that both 1 and 3 can chelate the protein-bound iron via their bipyridyl and hydroxyquinoline groups respectively, as observed for a known bipyridyl inhibitor(44) and for 5-carboxy-8HQ 9.(42) Moreover, the primary amines of 1 and 3 may be involved in hydrogen bonding and/or electrostatic interactions with KDM4A (Figure 2B).

    KDM Inhibitory Assays

    Compounds 1–6 were tested against LSD1 and a subfamily representative panel of JmjC KDMs including KDM4. In the LSD1 inhibition assays, the tested compounds all showed single-digit (1, 5, 6) or submicromolar (2–4) IC50 values, with similar or increased potencies with respect to the reference compound 7 (Table 1). Compounds 1–3 were also tested against MAO-A and MAO-B to assess their selectivity toward LSD1: at the tested conditions, 1 and 3 were definitively more effective in inhibiting LSD1 than MAOs, while 2 was less potent against MAO-B but showed similar inhibitory potency against MAO-A with respect to LSD1, analogously to what was observed with other tranylcypromine-based compounds.(38) Inhibition of JmjC KDMs by hybrid compounds 1–6 (Table 1) and 8HQ esters (21–23) and acids (24–26) (Table S2, Supporting Information) was then investigated, with 8 and 9 as reference compounds. Interestingly, both 8HQ (3–6) and bipyridine (1) hybrid inhibitors showed similar potencies against JmjC KDMs as the reference compounds 8 and 9, demonstrating that the conjugation of tranylcypromine does not adversely affect activity. Compound 1 displayed low or submicromolar activity against all the tested JmjCs, thus being the most potent of the series. Importantly, when tested against the hypoxia inducible factor hydroxylases FIH and PHD2, the former of which is closely related to the JmjC KDMs, 1 showed no/weak inhibition, revealing high KDM selectivity. Consistent with previous SAR for the bipyridine derivatives, the free acid is required for high potency against JmjC KDMs, as the ester “pro-drug” form (2) weakens the potency. Single-digit (9 out of 10) values were obtained for 2–6 against KDM4, the KDM subfamily that has been shown to work synergistically with LSD1 (Table 1).(28)

    Effects of 2 and 3 on Histone Methylation in HeLa Cells

    We then investigated whether the pan-demethylase inhibitors 1, 2, and 3 affect selected global histone methylation states in HeLa cells, using immunofluorescence-based methods (Figure 3). Compounds 7 for LSD1 and 8a(44) (a pro-drug form of 8,(13) Figure 1) and 9 for JmjC enzymes were used as references. The hybrid inhibitors 2 and 3 caused substantial, dose-dependent increases of methyl marks at lower concentrations than 9, whereas 7 caused essentially no changes in the overall methylation levels (including H3K4 methylations), in agreement with previous studies.(46-48) The lack of effects on H3K4me3 may reflect the weaker inhibition of H3K4 demethylases (JARID/KDM5) over H3K9me3 KDMs (KDM4), coupled to the simultaneous effect of inhibiting the H3K4me2-selective demethylase LSD1 (Table 1) or more complex effects due to inhibition of multiple targets. The 2,2′-bipyridine derivative 2 caused a H3K9me2/3-specific dose-dependent hypermethylation effect, whereas 8a and 7 alone had no effect. This observation suggests that the “hybridization” of bipyridyl and tranylcypromine motifs has a synergistic effect with respect to inhibition of LSD1 and KDM4/3 families. There was a general decrease in the immunofluorescence signal at higher doses where toxicity was observed (Figure 3I).

    Effects of Pan-KDM Inhibitors in Human Prostate LNCaP and Colon HCT116 Cancer Cells

    Compounds 1–6 (50 μM) were tested in human prostate LNCaP and colon HCT116 cancer cells to investigate their effects on cell cycle (after 30 h), apoptosis (after 30 h), and differentiation (after 48 h), using 7–9 as reference compounds. In LNCaP cells, 2, 3, and 6 caused a strong G1 phase arrest (≥90%) with a substantial fraction of the cells in pre-G1 stage consistent with apoptosis induction, especially for 2 and 3 (Figure 4A). In HCT116 cells, the pan-inhibitors 3 and 5 and the JmjC-selective inhibitor 9 displayed cell cycle alteration when compared to controls; however, as with the LNCaP cells, only 2 and 3 induced pre-G1 phase accumulation (Figure 4B). Differentiation data for 1–9 are reported in Figure S2, Supporting Information. Dose–response curves for apoptosis in LNCaP and HCT116 cells were detected after treatment of cells for 48 h with 2 and 3 at doses from 10 to 100 μM (Figure 4C). When compared in independent experiments to a combination of the single-family target inhibitors 7 and 8, 2 and 3 showed much more efficacy in inducing apoptosis in both LNCaP and HCT116 cells (at 50 μM for 24 h; Figure 5). To assess their differential toxicity, 2 and 3 were also tested (50 μM, 24 h) in mesenchymal progenitor (MePR) cells:(49) in this noncancer cell line, 2 increased the pre-G1 peak percentage from 8.4% to 12.8% with respect to the control, while with 3 the same value was under the control value (4.2%), suggesting a cancer-cell-selective apoptotic induction for the two pan-KDM inhibitors (Figure 5). The cell cycle phase effects of 2, 3, 7, 8 and 7 + 8 combination (50 μM, 24 h) in LNCaP, HCT116, and MePR cell lines are reported in Figure S2, Supporting Information.
    Despite differences in number of cells in pre-G1 phase in Figures 4 and 5, 2 and 3 induced cell death in a cancer-specific manner. These differences, however, could be due to cell populations (i.e., the percentage of pre-G1 for the untreated HCT116 cells was around 2% in Figure 4 and around 8% in Figure 5). Also times of induction (30 h in Figure 4 and 24 h in Figure 5) could justify the differences among percentages in data.
    Effects on cell proliferation and migration were detected by treating LNCaP and HCT116 cells with 50 μM 2 and 3 up to 72 h, using the histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid (SAHA) as a positive control. In LNCaP cells, 2 showed similar growth arrest as SAHA, while 3 was less effective (Figure 6A, left; in Figure S3, Supporting Information, the whole 72-h assay is shown). Nevertheless, differently from SAHA, neither 2 or 3 was able to stop migration in this cell line (Figure 6A, right). In HCT116 cells, strong antiproliferative effects for 2 and 3 were detected even after 6 h (Figure 6B, left), while the corresponding SAHA effect appeared only at 72 h (Figure S4, Supporting Information). In this cell line, 2 and SAHA strongly hampered migration, while 3 had no effect (Figure 6B, right).
    Western blot analysis using mark-specific antibodies supported the proposal that the effects of 2 and 3 in LNCaP and HCT116 cells are due to both LSD1 and JmjC inhibition. Figure 7 shows increased H3K4me2/3 and H3K9me3 methylation after treatment with 50 μM 2 and 3.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, we have shown that “pan-KDM” inhibitors can be obtained by coupling the chemical features of tranylcypromine, a known LSD1 inhibitor, with the 2,2′-bipyridine or 5-carboxy-8HQ scaffolds, two 2OG competitive moieties developed for JmjC inhibition. Such compounds are able to inhibit LSD1 as well as JmjC enzymes while having little or no effect against other tested 2OG enzymes lacking KDM activity. It is also interesting that the tranylcypromine moieties act as covalently binding inhibitors, whereas the JmjC inhibitor scaffolds bind reversibly (note the excellent safety record of covalent clinical candidates and marketed agents that has been recently described).(50) The application of such LSD/JmjC hybrid inhibitors to cells enables simultaneous increases in levels of H3K4me2/3 and H3K9me2/3 as well as high growth arrest and apoptosis (2 and 3) in LNCaP prostate and HCT116 colon cancer cells, whereas the related family-specific single-target inhibitors 7–9, as well as a combination of 7 + 8, were inactive. When tested in MePR noncancer cells to assess their differential toxicity, 2 and 3 showed very low (2) or no (3) ability to increase the pre-G1 accumulation. It should be noted that since a single KDM may target multiple substrates, different biological effects depending on the context could manifest.
    To the best of our knowledge, these are the first pan-KDM inhibitors to be described. More generally, the results demonstrate that hybrid molecules inhibiting different classes of histone-modifying enzymes have substantial potential as functional probes or histone methylation, and we suggest that combining other types of inhibitors targeting histone-modifying enzymes may be productive with respect to regulating the expression of specific (sets of) genes. Our pan-demethylase inhibitors may also be useful for medicinal chemistry efforts relating to cancer, in line with what happened with SAHA, which likely owes its anticancer activity to its pan-HDAC inhibitor profile.(51)

    Histone Extraction

    After stimulation with compounds, cells (HCT116 and LNCaP) were collected by centrifugation and washed two times with PBS. Then the samples were resuspended in Triton extraction buffer [TEB; PBS containing 0.5% Triton X-100 (v/v), 2 mM PMSF, and 0.02% (w/v) NaN3), and the lysis was performed for 10 min at 4 °C.     Histone extracts (10 μg) were denatured and boiled in buffer (0.25 M Tris-HCl, pH 6.8, 8% SDS, 40% glycerol, 5% 2-mercaptoethanol, and 0.05% bromophenol blue) for 3 min before electrophoresis.     Determining Cell Proliferation with the xCELLigence System

    Tumor cell proliferation was monitored with the xCELLigence system (Roche, Mannheim, Germany). LNCaP and HCT116 cells were suspended in DMEM and RPMI media, respectively, and added into a 96-well microtiter plate that is specifically designed to measure cellular impedance (E-Plate, Roche, Mannheim, Germany). The measured impedance, which is dependent on the level of confluence, was expressed as an arbitrary unit called cell index (CI) . The cell index at each time point is defined as (Rn – Rb)/(15X), where Rn is the cell-electrode impedance of the well when it contains cells and Rb is the background impedance of the well with medium alone. xCELLigence monitors cellular events in real time, measuring electrical impedance across interdigitated microelectrodes integrated on the bottom of tissue culture E-Plates. The impedance measurement provides quantitative information about the biological status of the cells, including cell number, viability, and morphology. For experiments, LNCaP and HCT116 cell lines were starved in DMEM/10% FBS and RPMI/10% FBS, respectively, overnight before being seeded on an E-Plate 96. Two hours after seeding, scalar cell concentrations were added in triplicate. Dynamic CI values were monitored in 30-min intervals from the time of plating until the end of the experiment. CI values were calculated and plotted on the graph. Standard deviation of tetraplicates of wells for the two types of cells with different treatments were analyzed with RTCA software.

    Cell Migration Assay

    Kinetic information about cell migration by dynamically recording the whole cell migration process in real time, without labeling cells, has been performed with the Roche xCELLigence real-time cell analyzer (RTCA) DP instrument. The RTCA DP instrument uses the CIM (cellular invasion/migration)-Plate 16 featuring microelectronic sensors integrated into the underside of the microporous poly(ethylene terephthalate) (PET) membrane of a Boyden-like chamber. In this way cells migrate from the upper chamber through the membrane into the bottom chamber in response to the chemoattractant (we used fetal bovine serum), thus contacting and adhering to the electronic sensors on the underside of the membrane, resulting in an increase of the impedance. The impedance increase is proportional to increasing numbers of migrated cells on the underside of the membrane. Moreover, cell-index values reflecting impedance changes are recorded by the RTCA DP instrument. The CIM-Plate was assembled by placing the top chamber into the bottom chamber and snapping the two together. Serum-free medium was placed in the top chamber to hydrate and preincubate the membrane for 1 h in the CO2 incubator at 37 °C before a background measurement was obtained. LNCaP and HCT116 cells were resuspended at the indicated concentration in serum-free medium. Once the CIM-Plate was equilibrated, it was placed in the RTCA DP station and the background cell index values were measured. The CIM-Plate was then removed from the RTCA DP station, and cells were added to the top chamber at the desired concentration. The CIM-Plate was placed in the RTCA DP station and migration was monitored every 2 min for several hours. Cells were analyzed in the absence or presence of 10% FBS in the bottom chamber. Cell migration was detected by automated real-time monitoring, and the low and high seeding densities were quantitatively monitored and reflected by the cell index values.
