    Abstract

    Lysine specific demethylase 1 KDM1A (LSD1) regulates histone methylation and it is increasingly recognized as a potential therapeutic target in oncology. We report on a high-throughput screening campaign performed on KDM1A/CoREST, using a time-resolved fluorescence resonance energy transfer (TR-FRET) technology, to identify reversible inhibitors. The screening led to 115 hits for which we determined biochemical IC50, thus identifying four chemical series. After data analysis, we have prioritized the chemical series of N-phenyl-4H-thieno[3, 2-b]pyrrole-5-carboxamide for which we obtained X-ray structures of the most potent hit (compound 19, IC50 = 2.9 μM) in complex with the enzyme. Initial expansion of this chemical class, both modifying core structure and decorating benzamide moiety, was directed toward the definition of the moieties responsible for the interaction with the enzyme. Preliminary optimization led to compound 90, which inhibited the enzyme with a submicromolar IC50 (0.162 μM), capable of inhibiting the target in cells.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Histone lysine demethylases are responsible for post-translational modifications of the histone tails,(1, 2) representing some of the most important partners in eukaryotic chromatin regulation.(3) Among them, KDM1A (LSD1), homologous to polyamine oxidase (PAO) and monoamine oxidase (MAO) A and B,(4) catalyzes mono- and dimethyl histone 3 lysine 4 (H3K4me1, H3K4me2) and H3K9 demethylation.(4) High expression of KDM1A has been reported in acute myeloid leukemia,(5) lymphoid neoplasm,(6) prostate cancer,(7) ER-negative breast cancer,(8) and neuroblastoma.(9) Moreover, its high expression correlates with poor prognosis in several tumor types, such as breast cancer,(7) prostate cancer,(8, 10) non-small cell lung cancer,(11) and hepatocellular carcinoma.(12) A role of KDM1A into conferring stem cell-like characteristics in breast cancer has also been proposed.(13)
    From the discovery of tranylcypromine (1 in Figure 1) as an irreversible inhibitor of KDM1A,(14, 15) a number of analogues bearing the cyclopropyl moiety has been described(16-20) and three clinical candidates are currently under investigation, two of them being disclosed, ORY1001/RG-6016(21) as compound 6 and GSK2879552(22, 23) as compound 7, whereas INCB059872 from Incyte is still structurally unknown.(24) We also have contributed to the field with the recent identification of a potent oral selective inhibitor [(1S,2R)-3, Figure 1](25) able to significantly prolong survival time in a murine promyelocytic leukemia model.
    Several examples of reversible KDM1A inhibitors have been reported in the literature (Figure 1),(16-20, 25-28) the development of which appears to be in the preclinical stage. A high-throughput screening (HTS) using time-resolved fluorescence energy transfer assay (TR-FRET) on KDM1A has also been described, although no inhibitors were reported.(27) Here we describe the identification of four chemical series that are active on KDM1A through the screening of a representative subset of our chemical collection (ca. 34000 commercially available compounds) and the process used to select the thieno[3,2-b]pyrrole-5-carboxamide series for further development. A very preliminary exploration allowed us to obtain submicromolar inhibitors. A subsequent extensive medicinal chemistry effort has led to a further optimization of the series, up to the identification of highly selective nanomolar inhibitors, endowed with high potency in cellular assays. This in depth exploration is the subject of the accompanying paper (DOI 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.6b01019).(28)

    The Chemical Collection

    The starting point for any HTS activity is a proper compound collection. To perform HTS for our projects, we built a structurally diverse collection, starting from five million commercially available compounds, filtering out molecules on the basis of their physicochemical properties, removal of undesired features,(29, 30) diversity, and chemotype representativeness, and we obtained a subset of 196000 compounds, which were purchased.
    This library was designed to represent each selected chemotype (scaffold-based) with a sufficient number of close analogues to obtain preliminary SARs for each hit identified during HTS. Although the chemical diversity is limited, the hit identification process is reinforced with the reduction of false positive rate. The analogues would help in deciding if positive (or negative) results are true values simply by looking at the whole activity range across the cluster. At the same time, the higher the number of cluster members (close analogues), the lower the “chemical diversity”, independently from chemical diversity space definition.
    To maximize signal over noise ratio(31) and to minimize the total number of assayed compounds still maintaining high number of confirmed hits, the collection has been divided into five subsets. Four of them, containing 8500 chemical diversity representatives each for a total of 34000, have been selected to be assayed in the first step of HTS. Close analogues of hits were pooled from the fifth set (Full Diversity Set), represented by the remaining 162000 compounds. These analogues were used during the hit expansion step. All compounds were plated into 384-well plates and were grouped by similarity, thus generating a sort of “compound cartridges” ready for screening and allowing hit expansions with a minimal effort.

    Quantitative High-Throughput Screening

    Step 1

    The screening was conducted on 34000 compounds, the representative subset of our chemical collection obtained from commercially available generic libraries, at the fixed concentration of 10 μM (see Figure 2). The KDM1A inhibiting activity was determined using a TR-FRET(time-resolved fluorescence resonance energy transfer) assay. The assay was performed at room temperature (RT) in 384-well plates in a final volume of 30 μL of assay buffer (Tris HCl 50 mM, pH 8.8, NaCl 50 mM, DTT 1 mM, Tween-20 0.01%, protease-free BSA 0.01% w/v) at the enzyme (KDM1A/CoREST) and substrate (histone H3K4 monomethylated) concentrations of 0.079 and 51 nM, respectively. After 60 min at RT, the reaction was stopped with tranylcypromine at the concentration of 300 μM. The primary screening campaign was completed in 5 days and the assays provided an overall Robust z prime factor (RZ′) of 0.85, demonstrating the robustness and the quality of the obtained data. The threshold for hit selection was set at 15% of activity, which corresponded to mean plus three times the standard deviation computed on neutral controls (maximal signal in absence of inhibitor). Compounds with high autofluorescence and therefore interfering with the assay were discarded. Applying these criteria, 320 out of 34000 compounds were identified as hits, corresponding to 0.95% hit rate. The 320 primary hits were then tested at 30, 10, and 3 μM with interplate triplicates. Out of 320 retested compounds, 113 were confirmed with a confirmation rate of 35% and with an overall RZ′ of 0.7. This result was consistent with the threshold applied in primary screening for hit selection. Indeed, the majority of compounds that did not confirm their activity were close to the primary screening threshold, whereas most of the compounds showing high activity in primary screening belonged to the group of confirmed hits. Using these compounds as templates, substructure and similarity searches against the fifth set of the collection was performed with ad hoc developed Pipeline Pilot(32) protocols. The protocols performed iteratively substructure and similarity searches (See Experimental Section for details) for each of the reconfirmed hits and then collected and merged all the results, thus producing a series of plates, giving us the opportunity to screen an enriched set of compounds similar to those obtained at 10 μM.

    Step 2

    Nineteen mother plates were selected from the Full Diversity Set to be tested in triplicate at 10 μM to identify hits from analogous compounds present in the library. An overall RZ′ of 0.81 was obtained, and 70 compounds among the 6080 tested were found to be active according to the same threshold of the primary screening, thus producing a hit rate of 1.15% based on all compounds and of 8% based on close analogues.

    Step 3

    Finally, a dose–response experiment was conducted on 115 selected compounds. The compounds were tested in dose–response at eight concentrations ranging from 30 to 0.1 μM with intraplate quadruplicate data points. The overall RZ′ was of 0.76, and 73 active compounds were identified. Among them, 27 compounds showed an IC50 higher than 30 μM, 32 compounds showed an IC50 between 10 and 30 μM, and 14 compounds showed an IC50 lower than 10 μM. Overall, four chemical series and 14 singletons were identified (Table 1).

    Structural Analysis

    The N-[3-(methoxymethyl)phenyl]-4-methyl-thieno[3,2-b]pyrrole-5-carboxamide (19) was successfully crystallized in complex with KDM1A/CoREST construct, see Experimental Section for details, and the X-ray diffraction quality allowed us to solve the complex at a resolution of 3.2 Å, adequate for structural analysis (see Figure 4A and Supporting Information, Table 1S). We also attempted to produce KDM1A/CoREST crystal in complex with compound 21, but all the trials failed. To our knowledge, 19 is the first small molecule in close contact with FAD, reversibly inhibiting KDM1A, for which the experimentally determined structure of the complex has been reported.

    Key Interactions

    The ligand is located within the catalytic site of the KDM1A enzyme, as seen in Figure 4A, with a relatively planar conformation due the electronic delocalization between the two ring systems bridged by the amide group. The inhibitor interaction with the target is mainly hydrophobic with no visible polar contacts. Its core, the thienopyrrole moiety, is caged between residues Val333, Thr335, Leu663, Tyr765, and Thr814. The flavin ring represents the back wall of such cage and the geometry of its contact with the bicyclic system is compatible with an aromatic–aromatic interaction. The second ring system, an aryl group, is packed between residues Val333, Phe542, and Trp699. The exit vector of the meta substituent on the second ring system points toward Gln358, suggesting a possible interaction region.
    Stability of the complex and its interactions have been evaluated using the molecular dynamics (MD) technique, propaedeutic to structure base drug design setup (see next paragraph).
    Molecular details of the noncompetitive inhibition mechanism for 19 are revealed by the comparison of this crystallographic structure with the one of KDM1A in complex with histone H3 peptide (2V1D)(34) depicted in Figure 4B. The superposition of the two structures shows a very partial overlap of the thienopyrrole molecule with the atoms of the peptide, only the oxygen of the methoxymethyl superimpose with the histone, in a noneffective manner. In particular, the ligand is proximal to the region where the dimethylated lysine interacts with the target to undergo the demethylation reaction.(35) Further comparison of the two KDM1A complexes (2V1D vs internal) highlights subtle differences of binding-site residue conformations. The two binding sites are quite identical with the exception of Trp699, which is present with different rotamers. The different conformation of this amino acid is due to the induced fit effect of thienopyrrole ligand binding to the target.

    Structure-Based Drug Design

    To support the development of this class of inhibitors, a specific molecular docking protocol was developed to better reproduce the binding mode of the thienopyrrole chemotype. A procedure was developed by combining docking programs, clustering techniques, and energy estimates (see Experimental Section for details). The KDM1A protein conformations used for the docking procedures were those obtained from internal X-ray trials, where the conformation of Trp699 has shifted with respect to its reported position. This effect is induced by the binding of the ligand and in particular is due to the phenyl ring position (Figure 4B). The use of these protein structures and the docking protocols gave satisfactory results to reproduce the X-ray crystallographic conformation of 19 and produced reasonable poses.
    The conformational space of the ligand inside the target was explored with more than one methodology: Autodock,(36) Plants,(37) and Glide(38) produced multiple docking poses. They were clustered with the ACIAP (Autonomus-hirerarchical clustering),(39) a method based on their distances as RMSD between conformations. Finally, one representative pose per family was submitted to binding estimation after refinement (BEAR)(40) evaluation.
    To identify binding site characteristics, such as hydrophobic-interaction pattern and hydrogen bonding propensity, we chose to explore the target protein with a grid-based approach. Autogrid software(36) was used to build different types of energetics maps by applying various atomic probes and the visualization of grid points below a certain threshold allowed us to scan the binding site for favorable interaction regions. Of all obtained maps, the hydrophobic pattern was quite informative and, associated with the electronegative areas, gave useful suggestions for the development of the thienopyrrole series. As seen in Figure 5, the ligand is buried into the hydrophobic region.
    The analysis of the electrostatics maps reveals two negatively charged regions, one in the upward direction with respect to the thienopyrrole, where the residues Asp379 and Glu383 are found, and one downward, corresponding to residues Asp559, Asp560, and Glu563. In particular, the former corresponds to the site where the guanidine group of the Arg8 of the histone substrate interacts with the enzyme, see Figure 5B. Thus, we decided to grow the chemical class elongating the substituent in position meta of the anilide in search of a favorable interaction regions along the upward direction. In addition, the distance between the benzene carbon in para position and the electronegative region is approximately 12 Å. Ultimately, these modeling suggestions prompted us to elongate the dimethylene chain of compound 19 into a chain ending with a positively charged group.

    A.2 Ring Substitution

    We approached a limited exploration on all the accessible positions of the bicyclic system (Table 6). None of the substitutions demonstrated the opportunity to decorate the core scaffold. Among the residues selected to substitute the pyrrolic nitrogen, only ethyl in compound 66 improved potency of almost three times when compared to the methyl analogue 23, while the other chains were all detrimental for the activity (see 2-aminoethyl, phenyl, phenylsulfonyl, and benzyl in compounds 68–71). The reason for this inactivity can be explained by steric hindrance of substituents in position 4 of thienopyrrole moiety: anything larger than the ethyl group in fact goes toward residues Leu663, Tyr765, as suggested in comments to Figure 6A. Analogously, the proximity of the enzyme wall to the ligand does not allow any atom replacing hydrogens in position 2, 3, and 6 of the thienopyrrole, neither small alkyls nor halogens of various types (44, 53–56, 58–65).

This cell line is characterized by the MLL-AF9 translocation and was selected in consideration of the experimental evidence, demonstrating the relevance of KDM1A in sustaining the oncogenic potential of MLL-AF9 leukemia stem cells.(43)
    The absence of evident effect on a global level of histone H3K4 methylation after genetic ablation of KDM1A, both in cancer cells(44) and hematopoietic cells,(45) let us to deprioritize this experimental read out for cellular KDM1A inhibition experiments.
    We evaluated instead the compound effects on the expression level of two differentiation markers: CD14 and CD11B (modulated by KDM1A down-regulation as reported in Supporting Information), considering that KDM1A inhibition induces differentiation of human AML cells.(5) As reported in Table 9, 90 and 91, as well as 6, induced an increase of mRNA expression of CD14 and CD11B selected genes, similarly to KDM1A down-regulation by short hairpin RNA(shRNA). These results demonstrate the compounds’ ability to block KDM1A activity in cells and to induce differentiation of THP-1 cells. To additionally characterize their effects on leukemia cells, cell cycle analysis and assessment of apoptosis induction were performed in THP-1 cells. As reported in Supporting Information, Figure S2, treatment for 72 h at the dose of 1 μM for 90 and 91 and at the dose of 0.5 μM for 6 did not induce apoptosis and cell death, consistentely with the expected cytostatic pro-differentiation mechanism, due to KDM1A inhibition in AML cells.(25, 43) Finally, the anticlonogenic potential of 90 was determined in the human MLL-AF9 leukemia cell line THP-1. As reported in the Table 10, at a dose of 5 μM, 90 was able to inhibit the colony formation in THP-1 cells. Moreover, 90, as well as 6, induces differentiation in THP-1 cells as demonstrated by expression increases of CD86 in the cells, recovered at the end of semisolid culture experiment.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We ran an HTS (TR-FRET assay) on 34000 compounds in search of reversible inhibitors of the demethylase KDM1A, identifying four chemical classes inhibiting the enzyme at a low micromolar concentration. We prioritized thieno[3,2-b]pyrrole-5-carboxamides and 3,8-diazaspiro[4.5]decanes, demonstrating the biochemical mechanism of action for each hit selected in both the series.
    The binding mode of a representative compound of thienopyrrole series was experimentally determined through X-ray crystallography and represents the first structural knowledge of KDM1A reversible inhibitors. With this notion, we expanded the class by modifying the scaffold, the amido linker, and the aryl on the benzamide moiety. We discovered that residues bearing a terminal basic moiety located at about 18 Å away from thienopyrrole centroid drastically improved the activity. Further SAR exploration of the chemical series is reported in part 2 (DOI 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.6b01019),(28) in which we approached the ambiguity of the binding poses.

    HTS Assay Conditions (KDM1A Enzyme Inhibition)

    To identify small molecule reversible inhibitor of KDM1A enzyme, TR-FRET technology was used to follow KDM1A enzymatic activity. In our assay, a biotinylated H3K4me1 peptide substrate underwent demethylation and the demethylated products were then captured by the Eu-labeled antibody (Eu-Ab) and U Light streptavidin (U Light-SA), thus bringing the Eu donor and U Light acceptor dye molecules into close proximity.
    Upon irradiation at 340 nm, the energy from the Eu donor was transferred to the U Light acceptor dye which, in turn, generated light at 665 nm. The intensity of the light emission was proportional to the level of biotinylated reaction product. Once identified, the best configuration of the assay in 384 format, optimizing the reaction and the detection conditions, assay adaptation to the automated screening-compatible conditions was performed, including the analysis of intraday variability and the spatial uniformity assessment. The overall RZ′ mean for the day 1 and day 2 plates respectively of 0.79 and 0.89 indicated the robustness of the identified conditions and protocol. Inter- and intraplate variability analysis identified no issues, the variability observed being below the threshold of 20%. RZ′ is defined asRSD = robust standard deviation; ⟨ ⟩ = median of the signal values for the indicate control wells of a plate (NC for neutral control, IC for inhibitor control).
    The KDM1A inhibiting activity was determined using a TR-FRET assay (time-resolved fluorescence resonance energy transfer, Lance Ultra Demethylase Technology, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA), which comprises a Europium chelate donor dye (TRF0404, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA), together with U-Light (TR0102, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA), a small molecular weight acceptor dye with a red-shifted fluorescent emission, and a biotinylated 21 amino acids histone H3-derived monomethylated peptide (H3K4me) [Lys(Me1)4]-histone H3 (1–21)-GGK(biotin), (64355, Anaspec, Fremont, CA, USA) as substrate. The intensity of the light emission is proportional to the level of biotinylated reaction product. The complex of human recombinant KDM1A/CoREST protein was produced in Escherichia coli as separate proteins and copurified as previously described.(34, 46)

    Demethylase Assay Conditions

    The enzymatic assay was performed in 384-well white plates (6007290 OptiPlate, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) in a final volume of 30 μL of assay buffer (Tris HCl 50 mM, pH 8.8, NaCl 50 mM, DTT 1 mM, Tween-20 0.01%, protease-free BSA 0.01% w/v) as follows: 10 μL of 237 nM KDM1A/CoREST protein were first incubated 10 min at RT with 10 μL of 3-fold concentrated compounds (prediluted in assay buffer at 1.5% DMSO). The reaction was then started adding with 10 μL of 150 nM histone H3K4 monomethylated substrate. After 60 min at RT, the reaction was stopped with 10 μL of 1.2 mM tranylcypromine (P8511-1G, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO 63103, USA).

    Detection Step Conditions

    Ten μL of detection mix containing 1 nM Eu-antibody and 4 nM U-Light–streptavidin in 1× Lance detection buffer (TRF0404, TR0102, CR97100, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) were dispensed on 40 μL stopped reaction. The resulting mixture was incubated in the dark for 1 h at RT. Then, TR-FRET signal was read by a fluorimeter (PHERAstarPlus, BMG LABTECH GmbH, Germany) (excitation 337 nm, emission 665 and 620 nm, delay time 100 μs, window time 200 μs). Compound activity and IC50 values in dose–response experiments were calculated on TR-FRET signal at 665 nm using Genedata Screener 10 (Genedata, Basel, Switzerland).

    Routine Assay Conditions (KDM1A Enzyme Inhibition)

    Demethylase Assay Conditions

    KDM1A/CoREST (0.25 nM) protein and compound in 100% DMSO were added in a final volume of 48 μL of assay buffer (Tris HCl 50 mM, pH 8.8, NaCl 50 mM, DTT 1 mM, Tween-20 0.01%) to each well of a 96-well half-area flat-bottom white plate (3693 Costar, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, M, USA).
    Demethylase reaction was started by the addition of 50 nM histone H3K4 monomethylated. After 20 min at RT, 300 μM tranylcypromine (P8511-1G, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO 63103) was added to stop the reaction.

    Detection Step Conditions

    Ten μL of the assay mixture was transferred from the original plate into a 384-well white plate (6007290 OptiPlate, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) and 10 μL of the detection mix containing 2 nM Eu-antibody and 10 nM U-Light–Streptavidin in 1× Lance detection buffer (TRF0404, TR0102, CR97100, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA). The resulting mixture was incubated in the dark for 1 h at RT. Then, TR-FRET signal was read by a fluorimeter (Infinite F200, Tecan, Männedorf, Swirzerland) (excitation 320 nm, emission 665 and 620 nm, delay time 50 μs, window time 100 μs).

    Reversibility and Michaelis–Menten Analysis

    Biochemical characterization of reversibility and Ki was performed using the KDM1A/CoREST assay horseradish peroxidase. The complex of human recombinant KDM1A/CoREST protein was produced in Escherichia coli as separate proteins and copurified. The experiments were performed in 96-well half-area white plates (catalogue 3693, Corning, Corning, NY) using a monomethylated H3-K4 peptide containing 21 amino acids (custom synthesis done by Thermo Scientific) as substrate and in a 40 μL volume of 50 mM TRIS-HCl, pH 8.0, and 0.05 mg/mL BSA buffer. The peptide purity was >95% as checked by analytical high-pressure liquid chromatography and mass spectrometry. The demethylase activity was estimated under aerobic conditions and at RT by measuring the release of H2O2 produced during the catalytic process by the Amplex UltraRed detection system coupled with horseradish peroxidase (HRP). Briefly, 20 nM of KDM1A/CoREST complex was incubated at RT for 15 min in the absence and/or the presence of various concentrations of the inhibitors, 50 μM Amplex UltraRed (Life Technologies), and 0.023 μM HRP (Sigma) in 50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 8.0, and 0.05 mg/mL BSA. The inhibitors were tested twice in duplicates at each concentration. Tranylcypromine (Sigma) was used as control. After preincubation of the enzyme with the inhibitor, the reaction was initiated by addition of 4.5 μM of monomethylated H3-K4 peptide. The conversion of the Amplex Ultra Red reagent to Amplex UltroxRed was monitored by fluorescence (excitation at 510 nm, emission at 595 nm) for 12 min and by using a microplate reader (Infinite 200, Tecan Group, Switzerland). The maximum demethylase activity of KDM1A/CoREST was obtained in the absence of inhibitors and corrected for background fluorescence in the absence of KDM1A/CoREST.     Reversibility was determined using jump dilution. Specifically KDM1A/CoRest complex was incubated at a concentration 100-fold over the concentration required for the enzymatic activity with saturating concentrations of the inhibitor (10-fold of its biochemical IC50). After 15 min of incubation, a 100-fold dilution into reaction buffer containing the H3K4me substrate was then performed to initiate the reaction. The reaction progress curves were followed and compared to a positive control (KDM1A/CoREST enzyme complex without inhibitor). The irreversible inhibitor 1 was used as a control of irreversible inhibition.

    Protein Preparation for Docking

    The crystallographic coordinates of 19 were prepared as previously reported.(50) In summary, the protein was added of hydrogen atoms in Chimera software, optimizing the H-bond networking and minimizing their position with 1000 steps of Steepest Descent. Then with Semiempirical Quantum Mechanics (SQM), first the hydrogen atoms were optimized until a gradient norm of 50 and then the whole protein was fully optimized SQM for 50 steps. The resulting structure was used to develop the inputs necessary for all modeling programs by means of the procedure presented (BASH scripted) in the work.(50)

    Favorable Interaction Regions

    Favorable interaction areas were derived from the exploration of proteins binding site with various atoms as probes. This procedure maps the interaction energy between a probe, which characterizes the type of interaction and the target. Examples of the probes are aliphatic carbon for the hydrophobic pattern or amine nitrogen for H-bond donor, etc. The Autogrid facility (from Autodock software) was used for this grid-based energy evaluation. Thus, the interaction was recorded placing the probe at each node of the grid in the binding site. Finally, selecting the appropriate energy cutoff and displaying only the grid’s points with energy below such value, the favorable interactions were visualized.
    The Autodock plugin(51) for PyMOL software (The PyMOL Molecular Graphics System, version 1.7.4; Schrödinger, LLC) read these Autogrid files and generated the surface representation on the graphical rendering of protein–ligand coordinates.

    Pose Production

    To predict the putative binding pose of ligands interacting with the target, a docking protocol was used. To explore a larger portion of the conformational space for these molecules, results from various docking programs were mixed together. The combination of various methodologies had been previously observed to increase the chances of finding ligand conformation close to the crystallographic structure and thus to enhance of docking performances.(39) In our study, three programs were used: Autodock,(36) Plants(37) and Glide,(38) with the settings described in our previous work.(50)

    Pose Clustering

    The docking predictions were processed as one ensemble undergoing cluster analysis to reduce data set dimensionality and to identify binding conformational families. The methodology used for this step is Autonomous Hierarchical Agglomerative Cluster Analysis Based Protocol (ACIAP).(39) In brief, this method consists on the construction of a full hierarchical tree of the predicted poses using the root mean square deviation (RMSD) between ligand conformations as metric and the average linkage as criterion to determine the distances. Finally, the clustering level to cut the tree was identified with the KGS penalty function, which was based on the average spread of clusters at each tree level. The global minimum of this function ensured the best compromise between the number of clusters and the mean distances between clusters.
    This methodology was implemented as automatic computational scripts by means of R software.(52) Once the procedure was performed, the medoid conformation for each cluster was kept as the representative for the family of binding. In addition, the number of cluster members is also used to judge the quality of a binding mode. The more a solution is found the more probable it is.

    Pose Scoring

    In our protocol, the surviving poses were then optimized and scored by means of binding estimate after refinement (BEAR).(40) With this method, each docking solution was minimized in complex with the target for 2000 steps, then a 100 ps molecular dynamics simulation of sole ligand was run, keeping the protein frozen. The resulting conformation of the complex was reminimized and scored (single point) with GBSA and PBSA. In addition to the interaction energy estimation, another value was used to judge the final pose: the RMSD value of the ligand between the initial conformation and the one after the MD. The higher the RMSD the less stable is the predicted conformation and thus the reliability of the binding pose.
    BEAR was implemented in house by means of the Amber software package(53) and Chimera.(54)

    Modeling Protocols Implementation

    Pose production, clustering, and scoring were implemented within the architecture described in our recent work.(50) The protocol was constructed in Pipeline Pilot(32) with its usual components plus the “CADDD components” that we developed to use the modeling software on a powerful GNU/Linux environment. This consists of PP sub protocols that invoke BASH scripts via SSH calls. The scripts are a combination of command lines, to interface with the wanted program, and scripting variables to automatize the process.

    General Procedure to Prepare Benzamido Derivatives via Acyl Chloride

    First, 1.5 mmol of SOCl2 and 2 drops of dry DMF were added at rt to a solution of 1 mmol of carboxylic acid in 5 mL of dry DCM/THF. Then the mixture was stirred for 2 h under reflux. The reaction mixture was then cooled down to rt and added to a solution of 1–1.5 mmol of aniline in 7 mL of pyridine. The mixture was stirred for 30 min at rt and for further 45 min at 60 °C. Then the mixture was diluted with DCM, washed with a saturated NaHCO3 solution and brine, dried over Na2SO4, filtered, and concentrated to give an oil which was purified by flash column chromatography.
