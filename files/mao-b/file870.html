    Abstract

    GPR52 is an orphan G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) that has been recently implicated as a potential drug target of Huntington’s disease (HD), an incurable monogenic neurodegenerative disorder. In this research, we found that striatal knockdown of GPR52 reduces mHTT levels in adult HdhQ140 mice, validating GPR52 as an HD target. In addition, we discovered a highly potent and specific GPR52 antagonist Comp-43 with an IC50 value of 0.63 μM by a structure–activity relationship (SAR) study. Further studies showed that Comp-43 reduces mHTT levels by targeting GPR52 and promotes survival of mouse primary striatal neurons. Moreover, in vivo study showed that Comp-43 not only reduces mHTT levels but also rescues HD-related phenotypes in HdhQ140 mice. Taken together, our study confirms that inhibition of GPR52 is a promising strategy for HD therapy, and the GPR52 antagonist Comp-43 might serve as a lead compound for further investigation.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Huntington’s disease (HD) is a monogenetic neurodegenerative disease caused by the mutation of the huntingtin gene, which encodes the mutant huntingtin protein (mHTT) with expanded polyglutamine tract (polyQ).(1) Multiple toxic mHTT species, from soluble monomeric/oligomeric to aggregates and inclusions, contribute to the neuronal death found predominantly in the striatum that leads to HD pathogenesis.(2−4) Numerous groups have also suggested that the reduction of mHTT through mitigated production or enhanced degradation rescues HD-associated phenotypes.(5−9)
    Current mHTT reduction approaches focus on direct delivery of Htt silencing modalities into the brain, including small interference RNAs (siRNAs), short hairpin RNAs (shRNAs), and antisense oligonucleotides (ASO).(10−14) Notably, both allele-selective and nonselective reduction of mHTT and wild-type HTT (wtHTT) show beneficial effects.(15) However, these gene therapies have limitations such as delivery efficiency. Recently, several signal pathways have been demonstrated to modulate HTT levels.(8,16,17) Among them, GPR52, an orphan G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) highly expressed in the striatum,(17) has been demonstrated to modulate mHTT levels in various models.(18,19) Embryonic knockdown of Gpr52 rescues disease-associated phenotypes in HD mice. In addition, a novel GPR52 antagonist E7 has been found to improve HD-related pathogenesis through the intracerebroventricular (ICV) delivery.(20) However, it remains unclear whether the regulatory role of GPR52 exists in adult HD. It would also be interesting to discover more potent and druggable GPR52 antagonists, which may cross the blood–brain barrier to reduce mHTT levels and rescue HD-related pathology in vivo.
    In this research, we found that knockdown of Gpr52 in adult HdhQ140 mice reduced mHTT levels. We also performed the high-throughput screening from libraries containing about 10 000 compounds and identified another GPR52 antagonist F11 ((E)-1,7-diphenylhept-4-en-3-one). Based on F11, we explored the structure–activity relationship (SAR) and found a highly potent compound 43 (Comp-43), which could reduce mHTT levels and promote neuronal survival in striatal cells. More importantly, Comp-43 reached the brain at a therapeutically relevant level after intraperitoneal treatment and reduced mHTT levels as well as the disease-related phenotypes in HdhQ140 mice.

    Striatal Knockdown of GPR52 Reduces mHTT Levels in Adult HdhQ140 Mice

    We have previously shown heterozygous or homozygous embryonic knockout of Gpr52 rescues disease-associated phenotypes in HD mice.(20) To further confirm the therapeutic potential of lowering GPR52 in adult HD, we investigated the effects of lowering GPR52 in the striatum of adult HD mice (Figure 1A). First, adeno-associated viruses (AAV) expressing either shRNAs targeting Gpr52 (shGpr52) or the nontargeting shRNA control (shNT) were generated and the efficiencies were evaluated in STHdhQ7/Q111 cells, a mouse striatal cell line expressing both wtHTT and mHTT with 7Q and 111Q mutations.(21) As shown in Figure 1B, Gpr52 mRNA levels were significantly reduced in STHdhQ7/Q111 cells expressing shRNAs targeting GPR52 (shG7 and shG10), compared to the shNT control. Then, we respectively injected shG7, shG10, and the shNT control into bilateral striata of 4-month-old HdhQ140 mice, a well-established knock-in HD mouse model expressing mHTT proteins with 140Q.(22) After 6 weeks, we observed that injection of shG7 and shG10, but not shNT, remarkably decreased GPR52 mRNA levels in the striatum, indicating the effective striatal downregulation of GPR52 (Figure 1C).
    Further, we applied the time-resolved fluorescence resonance energy transfer (TR-FRET) assay to examine the total HTT levels.(23) Two antibodies targeting HTT are labeled with terbium or D2 fluorophores, respectively, and the energy transfer between them occurs when they are in spatial proximity by binding to the same HTT molecular. As a result, the homogeneous time-resolved fluorescence (HTRF) efficiency is proportional to the HTT levels. However, considering the mHTT-specific antibody pair 2B7/MW1 is not suitable for tissue or cellular samples from mice because of nonspecific signals as reported previously,(8,23) we applied 2B7-terbium and 2166-D2 antibodies, which target the N-terminal 17 amino acids and 181–810 amino acids of HTT, respectively. Thus, the 2B7/2166 antibody pair examines the total HTT levels including mHTT and/or wtHTT depending on the genotypes in all TR-FRET assays. As shown in Figure 1D, in homozygous HdhQ140 mice, which express mHTT proteins with 140Q, we found that the expression of shG7 and shG10 significantly decreased mHTT levels compared to shNT in the striatum. We also observed a remarkable reduction of mHTT levels by western blot (WB) (Figure 1E,F). Together, our data demonstrate that striatal knockdown of GPR52 reduces mHTT levels in adult HD mice, further validating GPR52 as a therapeutic target.

    Design and Synthesis of Specific GPR52 Antagonists

    Our previous study identified a sesquiterpene lactone E7 as a novel GPR52 inhibitor, which rescued HD symptoms by the intracerebroventricular (ICV) delivery in vivo.(20) However, the naturally extracted E7 demonstrates a relatively low potency with multiple Michael acceptors, which might cause off-target effects.(24) To identify more potent GPR52 antagonists with structural diversity, we performed the high-throughput screening and identified compound F11 ((E)-1,7-diphenylhept-4-en-3-one), which displayed moderate potency with the half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of 5.45 μM. Compared to E7, compound F11 has relatively low molecular weight and extended chemical space containing an α,β-unsaturated carbonyl group, a linker, and terminal aryl-substitutes, which is more suitable for further optimization.
    First, we investigated the SAR on the α,β-unsaturated carbonyl moiety of F11. The broken α,β-unsaturated carbonyl system, by reducing double bond or carbonyl group and changing with other conjugated moiety, resulted in the loss of GPR52 inhibitory activity (Table S1, compounds 1–6). These results indicate that the α,β-unsaturated carbonyl group is critical and the flexible terminal aryl group at the side of double bond is required to keep the suitable direction. Shortening the linker length between α,β-unsaturated carbonyl group and two phenyl rings could maintain or even increase the inhibitory activity of GPR52, as shown in Table S2 (compounds 7–12). Notably, compound 8 (Comp-8) with ketone moiety significantly inhibited GPR52 activity with an IC50 value of 0.65 μM (Figure 2A,B). Although with the Michael system, Comp-8 exhibited the selectivity inhibitory activity targeting GPR52 but not other GPCRs (β-2 adrenergic receptor (β2AR), glucagon receptor (GCGR), glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor (GLP-1R)), as shown in Figure S1. However, when the left-hand-side linker in Comp-8 became too short or too long, the compound potency decreased (Table S2, compounds 9–12). We further modified the α,β-unsaturated carbonyl group system based on Comp-8 in Table S3 (compounds 13–21). All compounds including the added methyl group on the double bond (compound 14), and the replacement of the double bond with three-membered carbocyclic ring (compound 19) resulted in a dramatic loss of inhibitory activity targeting GPR52. These findings indicate that this key pharmacophore of α,β-unsaturated carbonyl group is preserved, which might fit the narrow binding pocket in GPR52.(25)
    Then, we investigated the terminal aryl tails based on Comp-8, as shown in Table 1 (compounds 22–48). After replacing the left phenyl ring with hydrogen, compound 22 lost the inhibitory activity completely. With different substitutions on the left aryl group, compounds 23 and 24 slightly decreased the activity. When changing phenyl with piperidine on the right-hand-side ring, compound 25 also lost the activity. These results indicate that the aryl substitutions on the two terminals might form the π–π stacking interactions with the residues of GPR52 protein. Then, the different aryl groups at the ketone substitutions with electronic and steric effects were synthesized. The substituted phenyl rings (compounds 26–38) and naphthyl rings (compounds 39 and 40) could maintain or lower inhibitory activity of GPR52 compared with Comp-8. Surprisingly, compound 35 with the 4-F substitution on the benzene ring lost the activity completely. After further screening for a variety of five- and six-membered heteroaromatic rings (compounds 41–48), we found compound 43 (Comp-43) bearing a thiophene ring exhibited high potency targeting GPR52 (IC50 = 0.63 μM), as shown in Figure 2A,B. In addition, as shown in Figures S1 and S2, Comp-43 showed no effects on the cAMP elevation or calcium response stimulated by other GPCRs (β2AR, GCGR, GLP-1R, C–C motif chemokine receptor 5 (CCR5), α-1D adrenergic receptor (α1DAR), and dopamine D2 receptor (DRD2)), except at a very high concentration.

    The current data based on the SAR study suggest that the active compounds might bind into the specific narrow pocket of GPR52,(25) which is probably mediated by the two hydrophobic π–π stacking interactions. In addition, compound 22 with the Michael system structure type (one terminal is H) is inactive, reflecting the importance of aromatic rings at both terminals. Further, we examined the reversibility of GPR52 antagonists F11, Comp-8, and Comp-43 in cells for targeting GPR52 (Figure 2C). After removing the antagonists, GPR52 could restore the protein function by treatment with its agonist WO-549 significantly. Together, by SAR study, we identified novel, specific, and highly potent GPR52 antagonists Comp-8 and Comp-43, which exhibit none of the properties of pan assay interference compounds (PAINS) and thus deserve further evaluation.

    SAR Analysis of Comp-43 by Docking with GPR52 Crystal Structure

    Recently, the crystal structure of GPR52 with an exogenous agonist was reported (PDB identification code: 6LI0).(26) To better understand the SAR of these novel GPR52 antagonists, computational investigations by docking with GPR52 crystal structure were conducted. First, the parent compound F11 was docked into the potential binding pocket of GPR52.(26) As shown in Figure 3A, the phenyl rings of F11 stretch into hydrophobic pockets and form key π–π interaction with Phe300 and Tyr34 in GPR52. The carbonyl oxygen atom of F11 forms a critical hydrogen bond with Ser299 in GPR52, which explains any modification of carbonyl group led to complete loss of activity (compounds 1–2). Also, the replacement of the double bond changes the phenyl ring conformation, leading to activity loss (compounds 3–6). Interestingly, as shown in Figure 3B, adjusting to the suitable distance of carbonyl group to benzene ring (Comp-8) resulted in a remarkable improvement of potency against GPR52 (IC50 = 0.65 μM). The docking result of Comp-8 with GPR52 suggests that this optimized molecular conformation with carbonyl oxygen provides two new hydrogen-bonding patterns to fit the binding pocket properly.
    On the basis of Comp-8, computational investigation of other derivatives suggests that either the shortened (compound 10, Figure S3A) or lengthened (compound 12, Figure S3B) chain is unfavorable for the terminal phenyl rings to form π–π interactions with the residues in GPR52. In addition, the linker in a relatively narrow pocket and the double bond are optimal for the unique steric and conformational profiles. Modifications of this double bond by introducing steric hindrance or changing conformation led to the total loss of activity (Table S3). A docking study of compound 14 with GPR52 shows that the steric methyl group disturbs the formation of the crucial hydrogen bond due to its steric repulsion against the side chains of Pro28 and Phe300 in GPR52 (Figure 3C). Moreover, π–π stacking interactions between terminal phenyl rings and GPR52 are disrupted by the spatial conformation changes induced by the modifications of double bond. As a result, compound 14 exhibited no potency against GPR52. Of note, in comparison to Comp-8, the binding mode of Comp-43 with GPR52 showed the thiophene ring with less steric resistance, the restored π–π stacking interaction with Phe300, and the specific electronic effects with His186 in GPR52 (Figure 3D). Thus, our data indicate that Comp-43 might be more favorable for binding with GPR52 and worthy for further study of biological function.

    GPR52-Specific Antagonist Comp-43 Decreases HTT Levels in Neuronal Cells

    As the representative Comp-43 shows specific and high potency targeting GPR52, we further examined the effect of Comp-43 on HTT levels in STHdhQ7/Q111 cells. First, we observed that Comp-43 showed little effects on the cell viability of STHdhQ7/Q111 at the concentration of 10 μM or less, which served as the recommended doses in further evaluations (Figure S4). As shown in Figure 4A, upon treatment for 48 h, Comp-43 significantly reduced the HTRF signals in a dose-dependent manner, which is proportional to total HTT levels. Similarly, by WB analysis, we observed a remarkable decrease on both mHTT and wtHTT levels by Comp-43, which is comparable to that by Rapamycin, a well-known autophagy enhancer (Figures 4B,C, and S5A).
    To confirm the regulatory roles of Comp-43 on HTT levels, we performed TR-FRET and WB analysis in mouse primary neurons obtained from HdhQ7/Q140 mice. As shown in Figures 4D–F and S5B, Comp-43 also remarkably reduced both mHTT and wtHTT levels in a dose-dependent manner, which is consistent with the observations in STHdhQ7/Q111 cells. Our data suggest that Comp-43 decreases HTT levels in a nonselective manner.
    To explore whether Comp-43 regulates HTT levels via GPR52, we transiently expressed either the siRNA targeting GPR52 (siGPR52) or the negative control (siNC), respectively, in STHdhQ7/Q111 cells followed by Comp-43 treatment. As shown in Figures 4G–I and S5C, the knockdown of GPR52 significantly reduced both mHTT and wtHTT levels compared to that of siNC control, as previously reported.(19) Furthermore, we observed that knockdown of GPR52 abolished the effect of Comp-43 on lowering HTT, suggesting that Comp-43 could decrease HTT levels via targeting GPR52.

    Comp-43 Protects Mouse Primary Striatal Neurons from mHTT Toxicity

    Numerous studies have shown that mHTT exerts toxic effects on neurons especially in the striatum, which leads to progressive HD phenotypes.(28−31) Therefore, we further investigated the effects of Comp-43 on neuronal survival. Primary P1 striatal neurons were cultured in vitro for 3 days and then challenged by removal of protective growth factors, which could induce HD-specific neuronal shrinkage and death.(32,33) The neuronal shrinkage and death could also be rescued by knockdown of mHTT (siHTT), confirming the mHTT-dependent toxicity.(20)
    Thus, we assessed this mHTT-dependent neuronal shrinkage and death by immunostaining for NeuN, a neuron-specific marker. As shown in Figures 5A–C and S6A, in comparison to primary striatal neurons from wild-type mice, primary striatal neurons from HdhQ7/Q140 mice showed remarkable neuronal loss, confirming the neuron deficits caused by mHTT as reported.(34) Furthermore, treatment with Comp-43 at 3 μM significantly rescued the neuronal loss in HdhQ7/Q140 striatal neurons, which is comparable to the expressing of siHTT. Similar results were observed in the neuronal apoptosis analysis by examining the time series of caspase 3 activity. As shown in Figures 5D and S6B, primary HdhQ7/Q140 striatal neurons showed significant caspase 3 activity compared to wild-type neurons. In addition, a remarkable reduction of caspase 3 activity was observed after treatment with Comp-43 as well as siHTT. Together, these data suggest that Comp-43 protects HD mouse primary striatal neurons from mHTT toxicity.

    Intracerebroventricular Treatment of Comp-43 Decreases mHTT Levels in HdhQ140 Mice

    To further explore the effects of Comp-43in vivo, we performed direct intracerebroventricular administration of Comp-43 into 9-month-old wild-type and HdhQ140 mice at a concentration of 0.5 mM, which is 5-fold lower than E7(20) considering the relatively high efficiency of Comp-43 antagonizing GPR52 (Figure 2B). After once-a-day 3 consecutive weeks of treatment, Comp-43 significantly reduced mHTT levels in the striatum of HdhQ140 mice, which express endogenous mHTT proteins with 140Q. Moreover, Comp-43 showed no detectable effect on mHTT levels in the cortex of HD mice (Figure 6A), possibly because of GPR52’s high expression specificity in the striatum and Comp-43-mediated mHTT reduction through GPR52 targeting, which further indicates on-target effects. In addition, Comp-43 showed no obvious influence on wtHTT levels in wild-type mice (Figure 6B). Hence, our data demonstrate that Comp-43 reduces mHTT levels in HD mice.
    To further examine the effect of Comp-43 on mHTT aggregates in HD mice, we performed immunostaining against S830, an antibody specific for the aggregated forms of mHTT.(35) As shown in Figure 6C, treatment with Comp-43 significantly reduced mHTT aggregates in the striatum of HdhQ140 mice, suggesting that Comp-43 decreased not only the soluble mHTT but also the aggregates in HD mice. In addition, mounting evidence has shown that microglial-mediated proinflammation plays critical roles in the pathogenesis of neurodegenerative diseases including HD.(36) As shown in Figure 6D, we noted that the staining intensity of Iba1-positive microglia was significantly decreased after treatment with Comp-43 in the striatum of HdhQ140 mice, suggesting that HD-associated microgliosis is ameliorated by Comp-43 treatment. Together, these results indicate that intracerebroventricular administration of Comp-43 attenuates disease-related pathology in HdhQ140 mice.

    Intraperitoneal Treatment of Comp-43 Attenuates HD-Associated Phenotypes in HdhQ140 Mice

    Considering the challenging and limited application of delivering Comp-43 intracerebroventricularly, we further explored whether Comp-43 crosses the blood–brain barrier. Adult wild-type mice were subjected to intravenous injection with Comp-43 at 5 mg/kg, and then the concentrations of Comp-43 in heart blood plasma and brain tissues were analyzed at the designated time. As shown in Figure S7, 0.5–6 h after injection, Comp-43 at the concentration of 5 mg/kg passed the blood–brain barrier and reached the wild-type brain at concentrations of approximately 20–60 nM, suggesting similar blood–brain barrier permeability in the more vulnerable HdhQ140 mice.(37)
    Then, we performed intraperitoneal injection of Comp-43 into 9-month-old HdhQ140 and the wild-type control mice at a dose of 5 mg/kg. After treatment once a day for continuous 4 weeks, we investigated the disease-associated motor performance in these mice, including rotarod and balance beam tests. As shown in Figure 7A, HdhQ140 mice exhibited obvious motor deficits quantified by the latency to fall in a rotarod test. Intraperitoneal treatment of Comp-43 significantly improved the rotarod performance in HdhQ140 mice, without influencing the wild-type mice. Similar results were observed in a balance beam test (Figure 7B). Compared to wild-type mice, evident motor deficiency determined by increased balance beam crossing times was observed in HdhQ140 mice. Comp-43 rescued this deficit in the balance beam test in HdhQ140 mice while exhibiting no obvious effect in wild-type mice. Thus, these data suggest that the treatment of Comp-43 alleviates the motor deficits in HdhQ140 mice.
    We further examined the effect of Comp-43 on mHTT levels in HdhQ140 mice, which express mHTT proteins with 140Q. In the TR-FRET assay, Comp-43 significantly reduced the soluble mHTT levels in the striatum of HdhQ140 mice without influencing cortex mHTT levels in HD mice (Figure 7C) or wtHTT levels in wild-type mice (Figure S8B).
    Furthermore, the aggregate fractions were resuspended with 2% SDS-contained buffer and subjected to TR-FRET test for mHTT aggregates measurement.(38) When mHTT forms insoluble aggregates, the epitope specific to 2166 is sterically blocked and can no longer bind with the antibody, resulting in loss of TR-FRET signal. Instead, by targeting the polyproline region in exon 1 of mHTT, a mixture of 4C9 antibody labeled with either terbium or D2 fluorophore, respectively, results in a TR-FRET signal for aggregated, but not soluble mHTT.(7,38) As shown in Figures 7D and S9C, a remarkable reduction of mHTT aggregation in the striatum of HD mice was observed after the treatment with Comp-43, whereas no obvious effects in the cortex of HD or wild-type mice were noted. These data demonstrate that intraperitoneal administration of Comp-43 reduces both soluble and aggregated mHTT levels in the striatum of HdhQ140 mice.
    In addition, we investigated the neuronal deficits by immunostaining of DAPRR32, a selective marker of medium spiny neurons in the striatum. As shown in Figures 7E and 6F, the neuronal loss in the striatum of HdhQ140 mice was rescued by the treatment of Comp-43. Moreover, the intensity of Iba1-positive microglia was reduced after treatment with Comp-43 in the striatum of HdhQ140 mice, further supporting that Comp-43 ameliorates HD-associated pathology.
    Taken together, our data demonstrate that intraperitoneal treatment of Comp-43 attenuates HD-associated phenotypes, including the motor performance, both soluble and aggregated mHTT levels, and neuronal deficits in HD mice (Figure 8).

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    GPR52 plays a critical role in HD pathogenesis and serves as a potential drug target for HD therapy. Previous studies showed that embryonic knockdown of Gpr52, which is highly expressed in the striatum, lowers HTT levels and rescues HD-associated phenotypes. This research further confirms the therapeutic potential of GPR52 in adult-onset HD, the most common form of this disease.(39,40) We found that AAV-mediated striatal knockdown of GPR52 reduces mHTT levels in adult HdhQ140 mice by TR-FRET and WB assays. While other in vivo phenotypes need to be further explored, our findings provide critical evidence for a GPR52 targeting HD therapy considering well-established mHTT reduction observed in HD phenotypic rescue.
    We have previously identified a novel GPR52 antagonist E7 and conducted the proof-of-concept study to demonstrate the potential of targeting GPR52.(20) In our current study, we performed high-throughput screening and identified F11, which showed moderate potency (IC50: 5.45 μM for F11 vs 16.59 μM for E7). The compound F11 contains an α,β-unsaturated carbonyl group, a linker, and terminal aryl-substitutes, with easier access to modify compared to E7. After the modification of F11 with the three parts, the SAR study indicates that the key pharmacophore of α,β-unsaturated carbonyl group is preserved and the aryl substitutions on the two terminals might form the π–π stacking interactions with the residues of GPR52 protein in the narrow binding pocket.(25) Thus, we identified Comp-43 with highly potent and specific GPR52 antagonist activity (IC50 = 0.63 μM), which does not exhibit the property of PAINS based on the cellular bioactive selectivity and SAR study. Further, by the molecular docking study of the GPR52 antagonists with GPR52 crystal structure,(25) the active GPR52 antagonists bind with the same binding pocket in GPR52 as the agonist, which might explain the results of the SAR study. Among them, Comp-43 shows an optimal binding mode with GPR52, which is worth for further functional evaluations in cellular and in vivo levels.
    In both STHdhQ7/Q111 cell lines and primary HdhQ7/Q140 neurons, we observed that the treatment with Comp-43 at a concentration of 0.3–3 μM significantly decreased mHTT levels, which is roughly consistent with the effective concentration antagonizing GPR52. Furthermore, knockdown of GPR52 almost abolished Comp-43-mediated HTT reduction, indicating that Comp-43 regulated mHTT levels via GPR52. While Comp-43 showed relatively mild effects on mHTT reduction, it is considered that ∼20% mHTT reduction was sufficient for phenotypic rescue in various HD mice models.(41) Notably, in addition to mHTT, a comparable reduction on wtHTT by Comp-43 was also observed. Multiple groups have also shown that ∼50% reduction of wtHTT is well tolerated in vivo.(13,42−44) Moreover, with individual strengths and limitations, both nonselective and allele-selective HTT reduction approaches have been investigated in clinical trials for HD.(15) In addition, we observed that Comp-43 rescued the neuronal loss and apoptosis in primary HdhQ7/Q140 striatal neurons. As neurodegeneration in HD occurs mainly in the striatum, especially in early stages of pathology, this neuroprotective effect might be sufficient to attenuate the disease-associated phenotypes in vivo.(14,15,45)
    By delivering this compound via intracerebroventricular administration into HdhQ140 mice, we observed that Comp-43 significantly reduced both soluble and aggregated mHTT, as well as the Iba1+ microglia in the striatum. Moreover, we found that Comp-43 showed favorable lipophilicity and could cross the blood–brain barrier. Further intraperitoneal injection of Comp-43 alleviated the motor deficits and disease-associated pathology in HdhQ140 mice, including the reduction of soluble and aggregated mHTT levels, neuronal loss, and neuroinflammation-related microgliosis. Considering both intraperitoneal injection and intracerebroventricular administration of Comp-43 exhibited consistent biological beneficial effects in HdhQ140 mice, our findings provide a potential lead compound and confirm the possibility of targeting GPR52 for HD therapy (Figure 8).

    TR-FRET Assay

    The time-resolved fluorescence resonance energy transfer (TR-FRET) assay was performed as previously described.(23) For measurement of the mouse soluble HTT levels, we applied 2B7-terbium and 2166-D2 antibodies, which target sterically different and unblocked epitopes in HTT. The monoclonal antibody 2B7 binds to the 17 amino acids, immediately followed by the polyQ repeat of HTT. When applied in combination with 2166, an antibody binds to 181–810 amino acids of HTT, and the 2B7/2166 antibody pair specifically detected both wild-type and mutant HTT levels.(9,23) For detection of mHTT aggregates, by targeting the polyproline region in exon 1 of mHTT, a mixture of 4C9 antibody labeled with either terbium or D2 fluorophore, respectively, results in a TR-FRET signal for aggregated, but not soluble mHTT.(7,38)
    Then, proteins from cells or tissues were diluted with original lysis buffer, and then incubated with indicated antibody pairs diluted with HTRF buffer at a final concentration of 1.4 ng/μL for D2-labeled acceptor antibody and 0.023 ng/μL for terbium-labeled donor antibody. The signal ratio between 665 and 615 nm was detected as the HTRF signal and the background was corrected by blank samples.

    cAMP Assay

    For the inhibitory activity of compound on GPCRs, cells expressing GPCR were harvested and resuspended in DMEM containing 500 μM IBMX at a density of 2 × 105 cells/mL. The cells were then plated onto 384-well assay plates at 1000 cells/5 μL/well. Another 5 μL of buffer containing compounds at various concentrations was added to the cells. After incubation at room temperature for 30 min, 5 μL of DMEM containing GPCR agonists was added, and the incubation was continued for another 30 min. Intracellular cAMP levels were tested by a LANCE Ultra cAMP kit (PerkinElmer, TRF0264) and EnVision multiplate reader according to the manufacturer’s instructions.
    To test the reversibility of compounds on GPR52, cells expressing GPR52 were seeded onto 96-well plates at a density of 3 × 104 cells/well and cultured overnight. Then, the medium was removed from the well. The control group was replaced with 50 μL of DMEM with GPR52 antagonist for 15 min. After that, another 50 μL of DMEM containing GPR52 agonist WO-459 (final 100 nM) and IBMX (500 μM) was added and the incubation was continued for another 30 min. The recovery group was replaced with 50 μL of DMEM with GPR52 antagonist for 15 min, washed, and replaced with 50 μL of fresh DMEM for 60 min. Then, another 50 μL of DMEM containing GPR52 agonist WO-459 (final 100 nM) and IBMX (500 μM) was added for further incubation of 30 min. Finally, the cells were lysed and the cAMP level was tested by a LANCE Ultra cAMP kit and EnVision multiplate reader.

    Compound Treatment in Animals

    For intracerebroventricular delivery of Comp-43, the 9-month-old HdhQ140 mice and the age-, weight-, and sex-matching wild-type controls were randomly divided into vehicle and Comp-43 groups. Intracerebroventricular injection was performed as previously described.(20) Briefly, first, the mice were anesthetized and located in a stereotactic instrument. Then, a guide cannula with a cap was implanted into the left lateral ventricle (0.6 mm posterior, 1.5 mm lateral, and 1.7 mm dorsal to Bregma) of each mouse, followed by the postoperative care for 1 week. In the following 3 consecutive weeks injection, an internal injector with extended 0.5 mm was inserted into the guide cannula, allowing direct delivery of 2 μL of Comp-43 at the concentration of 0.5 mM into ventricle at a rate of 0.25 μL/min (RWD Life Science) once a day. The final concentration of Comp-43 in the brain was approximately 0.25–0.5 μM given that the total cerebroventricular volume is 50–100 μL and the total brain volume is about 3 mL in mice.
    For intraperitoneal delivery of compound, the 9-month-old HdhQ140 mice and their matched wild-type controls were injected with 5 mg/kg vehicles or Comp-43 diluted in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) solution. The injection was performed once a day for a continuous 4 weeks, followed by further behavioral and bio-marker study.
