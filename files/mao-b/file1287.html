    Abstract

    The sirtuin SIRT1 is a NAD+-dependent histone deacetylase, a Sir2 family member, and one of seven human sirtuins. Sirtuins are conserved from archaea to mammals and regulate transcription, genome stability, longevity, and metabolism. SIRT1 regulates transcription via deacetylation of transcription factors such as PPARγ, NFκB, and the tumor suppressor protein p53. EX527 (27) is a nanomolar SIRT1 inhibitor and a micromolar SIRT2 inhibitor. To elucidate the mechanism of SIRT inhibition by 27, we determined the 2.5 Å crystal structure of the SIRT1 catalytic domain (residues 241–516) bound to NAD+ and the 27 analogue compound 35. 35 binds deep in the catalytic cleft, displacing the NAD+ nicotinamide and forcing the cofactor into an extended conformation. The extended NAD+ conformation sterically prevents substrate binding. The SIRT1/NAD+/35 crystal structure defines a novel mechanism of histone deacetylase inhibition and provides a basis for understanding, and rationally improving, inhibition of this therapeutically important target by drug-like molecules.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The sirtuin SIRT1 is a 747 amino acid 83 kDa (isoform a) class III (NAD+-dependent) histone deacetylase (HDAC), a member of the Silent information regulator 2 (Sir2) protein family and one of seven sirtuins in humans. Sirtuins influence a myriad of biological processes including inflammation, aging, metabolism, and oncogenesis. In yeast, increased activity of the homologous Sir2 protein results in chromatin silencing, suppression of recombination, and life span extension in vivo.(1, 2) Caloric restriction in mammals, which is associated with delayed onset of age-related diseases and increased life span, induces an increase in both SIRT1 expression and activity,(3, 4) and SIRT1 activation was demonstrated to protect against axonal degeneration.(5) Substrates for SIRT1 include histones, p53,(6) PGC1-α,(7) FOXOs,(8) PCAF,(9) NFκB,(10) PPARγ,(11) and others.(12) SIRT1 exerts its influence over cellular processes primarily through transcriptional and mitochondria-mediated mechanisms; however, a recent finding demonstrates that SIRT1 negatively regulates Notch signaling in endothelial cells by catalyzing the reversible deacylation of lysines within the Notch 1 intracellular domain.(13)
    Common to all sirtuins is a deacetylase domain of approximately 250 amino acids. The deacetylase domain of SIRT1 is located centrally between residues 250 and 500. The N- and C-terminal domains can be multiphosphorylated. Phosphorylation of SIRT1 increases its deacetylase activity,(14) and JNK2-dependent phosphorylation of serine 27 correlates with increased protein stability, a possible survival mechanism for cancer cells harboring increased levels of SIRT1 protein.(15) The crystal structure of Archaeoglobus fulgidis Sir2 revealed that the catalytic domain of class III HDACs consists of a Rossmann fold with an attendant small zinc binding segment and that NAD+ binds in a cleft formed at the junction of these two elements.(16) Additional structures determined the binding mode of peptide substrates: the acetylated lysine side chain is inserted along the side of the groove, placing it proximal to the hydroxyl of the NAD+ ribose moiety.(17, 18) Thus far, crystal structures have been determined for human SIRT2(19) (1J8F), SIRT3(20) (3GLS), SIRT5(21) (2FZQ), and SIRT6(22) (3K35). The deacetylase domain primary structure of SIRT1 is most similar to that of SIRT2, being 45% identical and 69% homologous, and least similar to SIRTs 6 and 7, being ∼22% identical and ∼40 homologous. Despite SIRT1’s similarity to known crystal structures and keen interest due to its important role in a multitude of critical cellular processes, the crystal structure of SIRT1 had not been determined. However, a plethora of crystal structures of wild-type and mutant sirtuin homologues bound to various combinations of ligands have been determined providing a wealth of information for the analysis of sirtuin structure and function. This considerable body of work provides an essential context for understanding and interpreting the crystal structure of the SIRT1 catalytic domain.
    Sir2 family proteins encode both deacetylase and ADP-ribosyl transferase activities. The formation of a ternary complex between the enzyme, substrate, and NAD+, where the binding of NAD+ is facilitated by the substrate, is the first step in the deacylation reaction. The reaction is initiated by the release of nicotinamide from NAD+, which is followed by transfer of the acetyl group from the substrate to ADP-ribose to form O-acetyl-ADP-ribose and the deacylated product.(23) Modulation of SIRT1 activity by small molecules, both activators and inhibitors, has been studied extensively. Siruin activators delay aging in metazoans.(24) The salutary effects of resveratrol, which protects against diet-induced obesity and insulin resistance, are mediated through the activation of SIRT1.(25) The mechanism by which small molecules activate SIRT1 is controversial: some studies conclude that activators bind directly to SIRT1,(26, 27) whereas others conclude that direct activator binding to SIRT1 is artifactual.(28)27 is a nanomolar inhibitor of SIRT1.(29) SIRT1/2 inhibitors induce cell death in the human breast cancer cell line MCF-7 and are proposed as potential therapeutics for the treatment of various cancers.(30) The SIRT1 inhibitor inauhzin has been shown to suppress tumor growth through p53 activation.(31) SIRT1 inhibition may also serve as a mechanism for the mitigation of allograft rejection.(32)27 acts synergistically with hydroxymate HDAC inhibitors to induce apoptosis in human leukemia cells in vitro.(33) To better understand the structure and function of SIRT1 as well as the mechanism of its inhibition by the indole 27, we determined its crystal structure bound to NAD+ and the 27 analogue(29) compound 35 to 2.5 Å. The crystal structure reveals that NAD+ and 35 bind cooperatively with the inhibitor buried deeply within the catalytic site. The extended conformation of NAD+, forced by the binding of the inhibitor, generates a conformation which sterically prevents productive binding of substrate.

    Overall Structure

    The SIRT1 catalytic domain (residues 241–516) was overexpressed in baculovirus-infected Sf9 cells and purified using conventional methods. Boundaries for the catalytic domain had been optimized by performing limited proteolysis mass spectrometry (LP/MS) on the full-length SIRT1 (data not shown). The binding of a panel of inhibitors was examined by thermal stability profiling (Supporting Information Figure 1a–c) and surface plasmon resonance (Supporting Information Figure 1d) to identify those molecules that imparted the greatest degree of thermal stabilization and exhibited the highest affinity. A series of compounds was identified that consisted of 27 and its relatives that bound strongly to the SIRT1 catalytic domain only in the presence of NAD+ (1.3 uM KD for 27). The previously measured IC50 of 27 for the full length SIRT1 in a fluorometric enzyme assay(29) was 0.098 μM. This same series of compounds significantly increased the melting temperature of the SIRT1 catalytic domain (∼10 °C) but, again, only in the presence of the cofactor. On the basis of these studies, we extensively screened, in crystallization trials, a variety of SIRT1 catalytic domain constructs in the presence of NAD+ and 27-like inhibitors, ultimately determining conditions for generating crystals of the ternary SIRT1/NAD+/35 complex that diffracted to high resolution.
    The SIRT1/NAD+/35 complex crystallized in the space group P32 with two copies per asymmetric unit. The structure was determined by molecular replacement using the program Phaser(34, 35) with Thermatoga maritime sir2 as the search model (2H2H).(36) After multiple rounds of model building and refinement using the programs COOT(37) and REFMAC,(35, 38) the 2.5 Å structure refined to an Rwork of 16.7% and an Rfree of 19.5% (Table 1). The final model consisted of two SIRT1 protein chains containing a total of 544 residues, (272 residues for each chain), two molecules of NAD+, two molecules of 35, two zinc atoms, and 165 water molecules (see Supporting Information Figure 2 for electron density). 97.6% of residues are in the most favored region of the Ramachandran plot, 2.4% are in the allowed region, and there are no residues in the disallowed region. The structure consists of a Rossmann fold, a six stranded parallel β-sheet (β1, β2, β6–9) with four α-helices (α1, α7, α8, α12) flanking the face distal to the active site and two α-helices (α10, α11) flanking the opposite face, into which is inserted a small zinc binding domain, a three-stranded antiparallel β-sheet (β3–5) neighboring a constellation of several short α-helices (Figure 1b). NAD+ and the inhibitor are bound in a cleft between these domains. There were no gross structural anomalies observed when comparing the SIRT1 catalytic domain structure to that of homologous deacetylases (Figure 1c). Superimposition of chain A onto chain B yields an rmsd of 0.07 Å for equivalent Cα carbons. The analysis of the structure refers to copy A of the complex except where specified. There are no significant structural differences between the two ternary complexes in the asymmetric unit.

    Inhibitor and NAD+ Binding

    In the SIRT1/NAD+/35 crystal structure, the inhibitor is deeply buried (Figure 1d) and NAD+ is bound lengthwise across the mouth of the binding site (Figures 1b, 2a). The adenine moiety, which is clamped between the R466 side chain and the single turn α-2 helix following the β-1 strand, forms two hydrogen bonds with SIRT1, one between the 6-amine and D483 Oδ1 and the other between the position 1 nitrogen and the backbone nitrogen of C482. The nucleotide ribose hydroxyl distal to the adenine forms a hydrogen bond with Nδ2 of N465. R274 forms a salt bridge with the first NAD+ phosphate and hydrogen bonds with the second phosphate through its side chain guanidinium group. Additional hydrogen bonds are found between the first phosphate and the F273 backbone nitrogen and Oγ of S442 and between the second phosphate and Oγ of S441 and the A262 backbone nitrogen. A bridging water molecule (W38) is found in the active site between NAD+ and the inhibitor. This water is triply coordinated by the second phosphate of NAD+, N346 Oδ1, and the indole nitrogen of the inhibitor. The NAD+ nicotinamide is tightly packed through van der Waals interactions between a trio of phenylalanine side chains contributed by residues F273, F297, and F414, while the NAD+ terminal amide hydrogen bonds to the Y280 side chain hydroxyl as well as the Q294 backbone carbonyl oxygen.
    The inhibitor interacts with SIRT1 primarily through hydrophobic interactions and is nestled deep within the active site behind the second ribose and nicotinamide of NAD+ (Figure 2b). Only the S-enantiomer is present, with no evidence of an alternate conformation of the ligand. The hydrophobic cage surrounding the inhibitor is defined by the side chains of the following residues: I347, I270, I279, F273, F297, I347, I411, and F413. Hydrogen bonds are formed between the amide substituent of the seven-membered ring of the inhibitor and the D348 side chain carboxylate and the backbone nitrogen of the same residue. A second triply coordinated bridging water (W12) is juxtaposed between the amide nitrogen of the seven-membered ring and the backbone carbonyl oxygens of A262 and I270. Additional van der Waals interactions are found between the chlorophenyl of the inhibitor and the NAD+ nicotinamide ring.

    Mechanism of Inhibition

    Superimposition of the SIRT1/NAD+/35 complex onto the Sir2 crystal structure bound to the H4 peptide and carbaNAD (1SZC)(39) clearly reveals the mechanism of inhibition by 35. The amide of the 35 seven-membered ring binds in the same mode as the amide of the NAD+ nicotinamide in its kinked active conformation (Figure 3a). In the active conformation, NAD+ wraps back around the α-2 helix to form hydrogen bonds with the D118 side chain carboxylate and the I117 backbone nitrogen. However, binding of 35 in the analogous mode supplants the nicotinamide moiety. Concomitantly, the F273 side chain rotates inward to form an edge-to-face π-stacking interaction with the indole of the inhibitor, forming an additional steric block to the binding of NAD+ in the kinked active conformation. These binding events force NAD+ into an extended conformation in which it is splayed diagonally across the outer edge of the binding groove, angling upward from the adenine to the right to the nicotinamide at the left. In this conformation, the nicotinamide extends to the extreme upper boundary of the binding groove where it is confined within a hydrophobic cage formed from three phenylalanine side chains (F273, F297, and F414). In the sir2/H4/carbaNAD crystal structure, the acetylated substrate lysine side chain insinuates deeply into the catalytic pocket. Coupled to NAD+ bound in the active conformation as observed in the 1SZC crystal structure, this places the acetyl group of the substrate lysine proximal to the flank of the kinked NAD+, exposing the nicotinamide ribose for nucleophilic attack by the acetyl-lysine substrate. However, the extended conformation of NAD+ blocks access to the channel infiltrated by the acetylated lysine substrate (Figure 3b). In addition, 35 extends far enough from the amide binding site such that the 5-chrorophenyl would also sterically inhibit catalytic placement of the acetylated lysine. Therefore, the mechanism of inhibition by 35 is 2-fold: first, binding of 35 mimics binding of the NAD+ amide and forces the cofactor into an alternate extended inactive conformation, and second, the forced extended conformation of the cofactor sterically blocks substrate binding by occluding the channel occupied by the acetylated lysine.

    Structure–Activity Relationships

    The crystal structure of SIRT1/NAD+/35 provides a rationale for explaining the structure–activity relationships (SAR) previously observed(29) (Figure 2b). Bulky substituents replacing the amide resulted in a >1000-fold decrease in the IC50. This can be explained by disruption of the critical hydrogen bonding network between the amide group and the protein (Figure 2b). Addition of an acetyl substituent at carbon 7 results in an ∼800-fold reduction in the IC50. This is explained by the placement of a substituent into a very tight hydrophobic pocket. More subtle aspects of the SAR are also explained by the structure. Replacement of carbon 7 with a nitrogen results in a >1000- fold reduction of the IC50. This is explained by the penalty associated with burying a hydrogen bond donor in a hydrophobic pocket. Only the geometry of (S)-35 allows the inhibitor amide to be positioned favorably for mimicking nicotinamide binding, explaining the previously observed enantiomer specific potency.

    Discussion and Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Despite the biological importance of SIRT1 and the wealth of structural information available describing members of the sirtuin family, a crystal structure of SIRT1 had yet to be determined. This is likely a result of the complex architecture of SIRT1 relative to other members of the family. To produce a crystallizable form of the catalytic domain of SIRT1, we used a combination of protein engineering based on LP/MS and biophysical analysis to identify the boundaries of the most stable domain as well as the combination of chemical entities which imparted the greatest thermal stability to the engineered domain.
    The results herein describe the novel crystal structure of the catalytic domain of human SIRT1 and the mechanism of its inhibition by the 27 class of indole inhibitors. The structure shows that the (S)-enantiomer of the inhibitor acts through functional mimicry to displace the nicotinamide of the cofactor. The resulting displacement of the NAD+ nicotinamide moiety induces the cofactor to adopt a novel inactive binding mode. The geometry of this novel configuration precludes binding of the acetylated lysine substrate through steric interference. Because racemic 35 was added to the protein for crystallization and there is no evidence of an alternate inhibitor conformation in the electron density maps, the crystal structure confirms preferential binding of the (S)-enantiomer. In addition to elucidating the mechanism of inhibition by 27-like molecules, the structure likely defines a temporal order for the pertinent binding events.
    Compound 35 is bound at the back of the catalytic site and is completely occluded from the solvent accessible surface by elements of the protein itself and by the cofactor which forms a seal over the binding pocket. Furthermore, 35 is clamped in place by the side chain of F273, which rotates inward to form an edge-to-face π-stacking interaction with the indole of the inhibitor. The second ribose and the nicotinamide of NAD+ pack alongside the outside face of the phenyl from the same side chain. This strongly implies that 35 must bind first, and this subsequently forms an alternative pocket that accommodates NAD+ binding. As noted previously, 35 also extends far enough into the substrate binding site to sterically impede peptide binding. From these observations, it can be inferred that the design of inhibitors which access the nicotinamide binding site could employ this interaction as an anchor and that extrapolation and branching of such rationally designed inhibitors from the anchor point could potentially result in high potency and dual functionality, i.e., direct inhibition of both cofactor and substrate binding simultaneously. While the structure provides insights into the mechanism of SIRT inhibition by 27 and its chemical homologues, the specific mechanisms of selectivity are difficult to construe. All of the direct inhibitor contacts are potentially conserved between SIRT1 and SIRT2. The SIRT1 crystal structure does, however, define an inhibitory mechanism involving binding of both the inhibitor and the cofactor. The observed inhibition is necessarily the culmination of the complex interplay between the binding of these two entities and the dynamic processes that underlie it may be too subtle to be derived from a static crystal structure.
    In conclusion, the novel crystal structure of the catalytic domain of human SIRT1 bound to a nanomolar 27 class inhibitor has been determined and clarifies the binding mode of indole SIRT1 inhihitors that had previously only been characterized using computational models.(30) The biochemical and structural data conclusively demonstrate that high affinity binding of 27-like inhibitors is dependent on cofactor binding and that these inhibitors act by forcing NAD+ into an extended inactive conformation. The elongated cofactor molecule thus forms a seal over the catalytic pocket, blocking access by the acetylated lysine substrate. In addition, the crystal structure reveals how novel approaches to inhibitor design might be implemented in the development of highly potent inhibito.rs of this clinically important protein.

    Protein Expression, Purification, and Crystallization

    A gene encoding the C-terminally his-tagged SIRT1 catalytic domain (amino acid residues 241–516 plus EGHHHHHH) was expressed in Sf9 cells using an internally generated baculovirus vector. Cell pellets were stored at −80 °C and subsequently lysed by incubation with stirring in cold (4 °C) hypertonic lysis buffer containing 0.02 M Tris-HCl pH 8.0, 0.5 M NaCl, 10% glycerol, 0.025 M imidazole, 5 mM BME, benzonase, and protease inhibitor (Roche complete EDTA-free, cat. no. 13317600). Cell lysates were clarified by centrifugation (JLA-16.25 at 16K RPM at 4 °C), and the his-tagged recombinant protein was purified using Ni-NTA beads (Qiagen) in batch mode. The beads were collected in a drip column after incubation with the decanted supernatant at 4 °C with stirring and then washed with 20 bed volumes of cold lysis buffer. Recombinant SIRT1 was eluted with lysis buffer containing 250 mM imidazole. The eluted sample was concentrated and applied to an S200 (GE Healthcare) gel filtration column equilibrated with 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, and 1 mM TCEP. SIRT1 containing fractions were pooled, diluted 5-fold with cold lysis buffer, and applied to a 5 mL His-TRAP Ni cartridge using an AKTA FPLC. Recombinant SIRT1 was eluted with a 0–0.25 M imidazole gradient, and the buffer was exchanged by concentration and subsequent dilution with 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, and 1 mM TCEP. The protein was concentrated to 15 mg/mL; 35 (racemic) and NAD+ were added to final concentrations of 1 mM and 2 mM, respectively, and crystallized as irregular cones by hanging drop vapor diffusion using a 1:1 drop ratio (1 ul) against a mother liquor containing 1.5 M sodium potassium tartrate and 0.1 M MES pH 5.6. The crystallization was optimized by streak seeding into hanging drops (1:1 ratio, 1 μL) equilibrated against a mother liquor of 1.5 M sodium potassium tartrate, 0.1 M MES, pH 5.4, and 0.3 M NDSB-195. Crystals (space group P32; a,b = 106.5 Å; c = 80.7 Å; α,β = 90°, γ = 120°) were cryocooled by immersion in N2(l) using 15% glycerol as the cryoprotectant.

    Surface Plasmon Resonance and Thermal Stability

    Surface plasmon resonance measurements were collected on a Biacore T100 instrument (GE Healthcare). Streptavidin (Thermo Scientific, cat. no. 21125) was immobilized to CM5 sensor chips (GE Healthcare, BR-1006–68) to ∼10000 RU using an amine coupling kit (GE Healthcare, BR-1000–50). SIRT1 protein and a reference protein were biotinylated with Sulfo-NHS-LC-LC-biotin (Thermo Scientific, cat. no. 21338). Unincorporated biotin was removed by desalting using a Sephadex G-25 Superfine (GE Healthcare, 17–0031–01) column. The protein fraction was then injected onto the streptavidin surface at a flow rate of 5 μL/min to generate a 6000–7000 RU SIRT1 surface in one flow cell and a similar density reference surface in second flow cell. Two buffers were used, no NAD+ and 1 mM NAD+. No NAD+ buffer contained 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1 mM DTT, 3% DMSO, and NAD+ buffer contained, in addition to the above, 1 mM NAD+. 27 (0, 0.12, 0.37, 1.11, 3.33, and 10 μM) was each injected (45 s; 50 μL/min flow rate) and allowed to dissociate for 120 s. Data analysis was performed using Scrubber 2 (BioLogic Software Pty Ltd., Campbell, Australia). Sensograms (n = 4) were double referenced (reference surface subtracted then blank subtracted) and fit to a 1:1 interaction model.
    The melting temperature of SIRT1 ligand complexes was determined by measuring the maximum fluorescence emitted by SYPRO orange when incubated in the presence of the protein or protein–ligand mixture to which a heat gradient was applied (25–95 °C over 5 min). The assay was conducted in triplicate in 96-well plates (Roche cat. no. 04729692001) in a Roche LC480 qPCR machine using a sample volume of 20 μL. The protein was diluted to 0.2 mg/mL in 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, and a 1:2000-fold dilution of SYPRO orange stock (Molecular Probes, Life Technologies, cat. no. S6650) was added. The inhibitor and cofactor concentration was 0.1 μM. The final DMSO concentration was 1%.

    Structure Determination and Refinement

    X-ray diffraction data (Table 1) were collected at the LRL-CAT beamline at the Advance Photon Source (Chicago, Illinois) (λ = 0.97929 Å). The data were processed using XDS. The crystal structure was determined by molecular replacement (two copies of the trimeric complex/a.u) using Thermatoga maritima Sir2 (2H2H) as the search model (PHASER) and refined (CCP4) at 2.5 Å resolution, after several rounds of model building (COOT), to an Rwork of 16.7% and an Rfree of 19.5% (Table 1) with an average B factor of 34.2 Å2. Because of incomplete or ambiguous electron density, C-terminal residues 513–516 from both chains were omitted from the final model. Similarly, side chains N241, K248, E252, K255, E300, K304, D401, E420, Y428, and E512 in chain A and K248, E252, K255, E300, E312, E402, and Y428 in chain B were truncated. Residues Q294 and L472 of chain A and L472 of chain B are represented with alternate side chain conformations. The conformation of the ribose proximal to the nicotinamide was assigned tentatively as the electron density map associated with one hydroxyl could not be reconciled with an allowable conformation of the sugar. This may be a result of the presence of multiple conformations of the second ribose. There are 162 water molecules modeled into the structure. There are no residues in the disallowed region of the Ramachandran plot. Chain B of the SIRT1 superimposes upon chain A with an rmsd of 0.9 Å for all equivalent Cα atoms. Molecular graphics were rendered, and atomic distances were measured using PYMOL (Schrödinger, LLC).
