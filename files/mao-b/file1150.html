    Abstract

    The increasing prevalence of antibacterial resistance globally underscores the urgent need for updated antimicrobial peptides (AMPs). Here, we describe a strategy for inducing the self-assembly of protegrin-1 (PG-1) into nanostructured antimicrobial agents with significantly improved pharmacological properties. Our strategy involves PEGylation in the terminals of PG-1 and subsequent self-assembly in aqueous media in the absence of exogenous excipients. Compared with the parent PG-1, the therapeutic index (TI) of NPG750(TIGram-negative bacteria = 17.07) and CPG2000(TIAll = 26.02) was increased. Importantly, NPG750 and CPG2000 offered higher stability toward trypsin degradation. Mechanistically, NPG750 and CPG2000 exerted their bactericidal activity by membrane-active mechanisms due to which microbes were not prone to develop resistance. Our findings proved PEGylation as a simple yet versatile strategy for generating AMP-derived bioactive drugs with excellent antitrypsin hydrolytic ability and lower cytotoxicity. This provides a theoretical basis for the further clinical application of AMPs.

    Introduction

    Jump To
    As the first defensive line for an organism’s immune system against the threat of antibiotic-resistant strains,(1−5) antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) have a broad antibacterial spectrum, fast bactericidal rate, and a lower likelihood of resistance development, which make them a favorable alternative to antibiotics. Protegrin-1 (PG-1) is a pig-derived AMP with four cysteine residues stabilized by two disulfide bonds (Figure 1D). These two disulfide bonds would promote PG-1 to form an antiparallel β-sheet and U-shaped rigid rotation domain (Figure S1), which endow PG-1 with the ability to create pores in the membrane.(6,7) Recently, the development and utilization of PG-1 as antimicrobials have made progress, but there are still some shortcomings, such as weak cell selectivity,(4,8) inactivation in serum, and protease degradation.(9−11) In order to solve these problems, in previous studies, the derivatives of PG-1 were synthesized by preserving the corner region of PG-1 and substituting low polar amino acids,(12) or hybridizing the fragments of Cecropin A with low toxicity and the PG-1 corner region, to reduce the cytotoxicity and improve the antibacterial activity.(13) However, the problem of protease degradation has not been solved.
    Self-assembly is considered an efficient strategy to improve the proteolytic resistance and cell selectivity of peptides.(14,15) Some polymeric matrix materials, such as aliphatic acids, palmitoyl groups, and cholesterol, have been introduced into peptides to facilitate the formation of diverse polyplex nanostructures.(16−19) In these peptide-conjugated polymers, the core peptides would be hidden inside the nanostructures, thus reducing degradation by proteolytic enzymes and recognition by the immune system.(19)
    The covalent modification of poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG), which consisted of repeated units of ethylene glycol (Figure 1A), is a successful self-assembly strategy for peptides and proteins.(20,21) For example, chitosan and peptide conjugated with PEG could self-assemble into a neutrally charged nanosphere structure with a size of ∼100 nm in aqueous solution, which is advantageous to combat Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa) biofilm formation.(22) Hong et al. conjugated the N-terminal of bacitracin A with PEGylated PLGA copolymers to construct PEGylated self-assembled Nano-BAs for the treatment of invasive infections.(23) However, in these previous studies, PEG played more of an adjuvant role for the self-aggregation of nanopeptides; few studies used PEG as an independent carrier to study its self-assembly driving force.
    Herein, we utilized PEG alone as a carrier to explore the ability of PEG to promote the self-assembly formation of PG-1 to resist protease hydrolysis. The length and attachment site of the PEG moiety play an important role in determining some properties of peptide drugs and maximizing the retention of biological functions in peptide drugs.(24) The low-molecular-mass polyethylene glycol monomethyl ether (mPEG) (Figure 1B,C) was coupled to the N-terminal of PG-1, named as NPGn. Moreover, Manhart et al. found that the N- and C-terminal modifications have different effects on the activation of the gastric inhibitory polypeptide (GIP) receptor, suggesting that an appropriate relative orientation of N- and C-terminal parts was essential for the bioactivity of peptides.(25) Thus, the corresponding derivatives of PG-1 with C-terminal PEGylation, named as CPGn, were also designed (Figure 1E,F).
    The antibacterial activities and cytotoxicities were first determined to assess the biological function of PEGylated peptides. Subsequently, the nanocharacterization using innovative PEGylated peptides was investigated. The behavior of peptides with trypsin was further analyzed to determine the stability of peptides and to elucidate the underlying mechanism of PEGylated peptides against Gram-negative bacteria. This study modified the end of PG-1 with PEG as a “booster” to construct self-assembled peptides to achieve the purpose of improving its proteolytic resistance and reducing its cytotoxicity while maintaining maximum biological activity, which may support further clinical applications of peptide-based supramolecular nanoparticles in the treatment of drug-resistant infections.

    Construction and Secondary Structure Analysis of the PEGylated Peptides

    To explore the effects of different molecular weight mPEG on the physicochemical properties of PG-1, we coupled different molecular weight (750, 1000, and 2000 Da) mPEG-COOH and mPEG-NH2 to its N- or C-terminal via an amide bond, named as NPGn and CPGn (n represents the molecular weight of mPEG), respectively (Table 1). All PEGylated peptides were purified to >95% purity by reversed-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC) and verified by matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS).

As shown in Figure 2, PG-1 showed a mixed structure of a disordered conformation and β-sheets in PBS; this result was the same as that reported by Lindsey(7) because there was a negative peak around 210 nm and a positive peak around 230 nm, indicating the emergence of a mixed structure. This same trend was reported in CPG1000. Notably, CPG750 and CPG2000 mainly presented disordered structures in PBS, and the CD spectra of the two peptides were characterized by a strong negative peak around 205 nm and no clear positive peak.
    Instead, each spectrum of NPGn was dominated by a minimum near 216–220 nm and a positive maximum near 202–205 nm in PBS, which displayed a typical β-sheet structure. However, in SDS, all the engineered peptides showed sheet-like structures with positive peaks around 200 and 205 nm and negative peaks around 214 and 222 nm. We concluded that although mPEG did not cause gross alterations in the secondary structure, but small changes in mPEG may affect the intrinsic stability of each peptide.

    In Vitro Biological Activity Determination of the PEGylated Peptides

    High biocompatibility is an important criterion for the clinical application of AMPs; as many previous studies have proved that PG-1 has high cytotoxicity against mammalian cells,(26,27) a new strategy is required to address this problem. Thus, we selected human red blood cells and human embryonic kidney (HEK) 293T cells as model cells to assess whether PEGylation would improve the cell selectivity of PG-1. As shown in Figure 3, all tested PEGylated peptides showed cytotoxic effects in a dose-dependent manner. Interestingly, at the highest concentration, the cytotoxicity of NPGn was significantly lower than that of PG-1, showing good biocompatibility. However, a totally different situation was observed for CPGn, PEG coupling to the C-terminal could not significantly reduce the cytotoxicity of the high concentrations of PG-1, or even worse.
    To quantify the cytotoxicity of PEGylated peptides, HC10 and IC50 values of the PEGylated peptides were calculated. As shown in Table 4, the HC10 values of all PEGylated peptides were improved compared to PG-1, indicating that PEG modification could reduce the hemolytic activity of PG-1, and the PEG modification at the N-terminal had a better effect. Similarly, the IC50 values of NPGn for HEK 293T cells were approximately twice higher than that of PG-1, but the IC50 values of CPGn were similar to that of PG-1(Figure S4). Overall, the PEG-coupled position had a great influence on the cytotoxicity of peptides, and the N-terminal modification could reduce their cytotoxicity. Subsequently, the antibacterial activity of the PEGylated peptides against Gram-negative, Gram-positive bacteria, and some drug-resistant strains was expressed with the minimum inhibitory/bactericidal concentrations (MICs/MBCs) (Tables 2 and 3 and Table S1), and the geometric mean (GM) values of the MICs of the PEGylated peptides across all the tested bacteria is summarized in Table 4, which represents the average antibacterial level of a peptide. As shown in the results, the PEG-coupled N-terminal obviously weakened the antimicrobial activity of PG-1 by 3.76–10.29-fold. Particularly for Gram-positive bacteria, NPGn showed almost no antibacterial activity. By contrast, CPGn were substantially more potent than either PG-1or NPGn against all tested strains. Among them, CPG2000 exhibited the highest average antibacterial activity (GMall = 3.88), and its GMall value was 2.88 times lower than that of PG-1.

    Maintaining high stability in a complex physiological environment is another prerequisite for the clinical application of AMPs. Thus, we evaluated the antibacterial activity of the PEGylated peptides against Escherichia coli (E. coli) in the presence of serum and salts to reflect their stability in a physiological environment. As shown in Table 5, compared with the respective controls, no or only twofold decrease in the antibacterial activities of the PEGylated peptides was observed in the presence of most of the cations, while Ca2+ obviously compromised the antibacterial activity of all PEGylated peptides by up to fourfold, indicating that except for Ca2+, the PEGylated peptides showed strong resistance to most salt ions similar to PG-1. Furthermore, it was worth noting that the addition of serum had a dose-dependent inhibitory effect on the antimicrobial activity of PEGylated peptides. With 50% serum, the MIC of all PEGylated peptides increased by more than 4 times, while 25 and 12.5% serum had relatively little effect on the antimicrobial activity of the PEGylated peptides. A comprehensive analysis of the GMMIC values of the PEGylated peptides was conducted across all tested serum concentrations, and we found that the GMMIC values of NPG750 and CPG750 had the lowest amount of variability, indicating that low-molecular-weight PEG coupling could improve the stability of PG-1 in serum. Comparatively, the GMMIC values of CPG2000 had the same amount of variability as PG-1 across all tested serum concentrations. However, among all tested PEGylated peptides, CPG2000 still had the highest antibacterial activity in the serum environment, which meant that CPG2000 possessed more clinical application potential.

    Screening of the PEGylated Peptides with High Clinical Therapeutic Potency

    To evaluate and quantify the therapeutic potency of the PEGylated peptides, the TI (GMHC10&IC50/GMMIC) of the PEGylated peptides was further calculated and is summarized in Table 4. The data showed that NPG750 (TIall = 7.87) and CPG2000 (TIall = 26.02) possessed the highest TI value within their respective series, which was1.21 and 4 times that of PG-1(TIall = 6.51), respectively. Considering the different advantages, both NPG750 (with high stability) and CPG2000 (with the best antibacterial activity) showed great potential for applications in the clinic.
    Furthermore, similar to PG-1, both NPG750 and CPG2000 eliminated more than 99.99% of E. coli cells within 30 s at 1× MIC, showing that NPG750 and CPG2000 had rapid sterilization ability in the clinic (Figure 4A).
    Currently, resistance to AMPs has evolved in some resistant microorganisms.(28) Thus, we also assessed the resistance development potential of E. coli to NPG750 and CPG2000 by conducting a drug resistance study. As the control, the ampicillin treatment began to develop resistance as early as passage 16, and its MIC value raised by 32-fold after 30 passages (Figure 4B). On the contrary, the antimicrobial potency of NPG750 and CPG2000 was unaltered for the duration of the test, which suggested that NPG750 and CPG2000 had a low likelihood of resistance development.

    Nanocharacterization and Trypsin Stability of NPG750 and CPG2000

    In order to verify whether the PEGylated peptides could form self-assembled nanostructures, the critical aggregation concentrations (CACs) of NPG750 and CPG2000 were first determined using the 1-anilino-8-naphthalene sulfonate (ANS) fluorescence assay, which is an anionic dye that produces high fluorescence intensity when it binds to biological macromolecules. As shown in Figure 5A, the ANS fluorescence intensity of NPG750 and CPG2000 increased with the increase of peptide concentrations and aggregation strength, and the CAC values of NPG750 and CPG2000 were 32.64 and 59.25 μg/mL, respectively, indicating that the PEGylated peptide molecules could aggregate to form a nanostructure. Instead, the fluorescence of PG-1 showed no significant increase in ultrapure water (Figure S5).
    The micromorphologies of nanostructured NPG750 and CPG2000 were further analyzed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The combined results showed that both NPG750 and CPG2000 could form spherical and fibrous nanostructures above the CAC. Intriguingly, NPG750 tended to form a dense nanofiber reticular structure, while CPG2000 tended to form spherical micelles with diameters of around 20 nm (Figure 5B). The dynamic light scattering (DLS) data confirmed the reliability of the above results.(29−31) As shown in Figure 5A, both NPG750 and CPG2000 exhibited two average hydrodynamic diameters, suggesting that NPG750 and CPG2000 presented two micromorphologies in aqueous solution. The above results confirmed that PEG coupling could facilitate the self-assembly of PG-1. Subsequently, we focused on the proteolytic resistance of the nanostructured NPG750 and CPG2000. To study the bactericidal activities, PEGylated peptides (512 μg/mL) were incubated with different concentrations of trypsin (the highest concentration up to 8 mg/mL) for a continuous time of 1–8 h and the bactericidal activities were determined to preliminarily reflect their antitrypsin hydrolytic ability. The results are shown in Figure 5C. PG-1 and melittin, which were chosen as controls, were completely inactivated after incubation with 1 mg/mL trypsin for 1 h. However, CPG2000 was not completely inactivated after incubation with high concentrations of trypsin (2–8 mg/mL), but its MIC value against E. coli increased, indicating that the antitrypsin hydrolytic ability of CPG2000 was stronger than that of PG-1. It is worth noting that NPG750 possessed extremely high proteolytic resistance and it still maintained the same antibacterial activity as the untreated NPG750 after 8 h of incubation with different concentrations of trypsin.
    In order to ensure that the high proteolytic resistance of NPG750 and CPG2000 was caused by self-aggregation rather than their own characteristics, we also determined the bactericidal activities of monomers NPG750 and CPG2000 after incubation with trypsin. The data showed that the antibacterial activity of monomers NPG750 and CPG2000 was lost (Figure S6), meaning that the antitrypsin hydrolytic ability of NPG750 and CPG2000 came from self-aggregation.
    The antitrypsin hydrolytic abilities of the PEGylated peptides were further verified using the 16.5% Tricine–SDS-PAGE assay. Figure 5D shows that NPG750 showed great resistance to hydrolysis of trypsin, even after 8 h of incubation, as similar bands to those of the controls were still present. However, CPG2000 could be degraded after incubation with trypsin, but the bands corresponding to CPG2000 were still visible, indicating that trypsin worked incompletely on CPG2000. In comparison, melittin and PG-1 were completely degraded after incubation with trypsin for 1 h. In a word, the above results confirmed that PEG-coupling modification could facilitate the self-aggregation of PG-1 to form nanostructures and improved the proteolytic resistance of PG-1.

    Membrane-Active Mechanism Analysis of NPG750 and CPG2000

    Many studies believe that the AMPs mainly exert antibacterial activity through inducing the formation of transmembrane pores.(13,32) Thus, NPG750 and CPG2000 were further studied for their membrane-active mechanism to kill the model Gram-negative bacteria E. coli.
    Lipopolysaccharides (LPS), as a major component of the outer membrane of almost all Gram-negative bacteria, are the initial site of interaction between AMPs and the bacterial outer membrane. Thus, the LPS binding activities of NPG750 and CPG2000 were tested and the results revealed that NPG750 and CPG2000 showed dose-dependent binding with LPS (Figure 6A). Subsequently, a competitive inhibition assay between LPS and the PEGylated peptides was conducted to further clarify the initial sites of action of the PEGylated peptides. Figure 6B shows that the coincubation of LPS with the PEGylated peptides had a dose-dependent inhibition effect on the bactericidal activity of the PEGylated peptides, and the antibacterial activities of the PEGylated peptides were completely blocked when the concentration of LPS was 1024 μg/mL, which further suggests that NPG750 and CPG2000 initially bind to LPS.
    The outer membrane (OM) of Gram-negative bacteria is the first barrier that AMPs need to penetrate. The hydrophobic fluorescent probe NPN was applied to evaluate the OM permeability of the PEGylated peptides. NPG750 and CPG2000 permeabilized the OM of E. coli in a concentration-dependent manner (Figure 6C). At high concentrations (64–128 μg/mL), the OM permeability induced by NPG750 was stronger than those of PG-1 and CPG2000. However, CPG2000 showed the strongest OM permeability when the concentration was below 32 μg/mL. Thus, the results demonstrate that NPG750 and CPG2000 could permeabilize the OM of Gram-negative bacteria.
    Destroying the cytoplasmic membrane (CM) of Gram-negative bacteria is the key step for AMPs to exert their antimicrobial activity. The CM depolarization assay results showed that NPG750 and CPG2000 caused a dose- and time-dependent fluorescence increase for E. coli within 2000 s, and even at low concentrations, NPG750 and CPG2000 also could cause rapid CM depolarization of E. coli (Figure 6D). The CM permeability of the PEGylated peptides was investigated using the probe of o-nitrophenyl-β-d-galactopyranoside (ONPG). As shown in Figure 6E and Figure S7, CPG2000 and melittin induced the rapid release of β-galactosidase at 4 μg/mL, while the release rates of PG-1 and NPG750 were slightly slower, but they also increased with the increase of the peptide concentration. We also utilized calcein-loaded large unilamellar vesicles (LUVs) (POPC/POPG = 3:1 (molar/molar)) as a model of the CM of Gram-negative bacteria to monitor the pore formation induced by NPG750 and CPG2000. The results showed that both NPG750 and CPG2000 could induce good leakage activity similar to PG-1 at 1× MIC, confirming the pore formation induced by NPG750 and CPG2000 (Figure 6F). Interestingly, with the increase of the concentration to 2× CAC, the leakage rate of calcein caused by NPG750 obviously decreased, suggesting that the membrane-active mechanism of NPG750 was reduced. For the LUVs composed of phosphatidylcholine (PC) with cholesterol that mimic the mammalian cell membrane, NPG750 showed similar results (Figure 6G). These results showed that above the CAC, the formation of nanostructures would reduce the ability of NPG750 to disrupt the membrane integrity, while reducing its cytotoxicity.
    To better understand the membrane-active mechanisms of NPG750 and CPG2000, TEM and SEM were used to visually investigate the surface and internal morphology changes of E. coli treated with the PEGylated peptides. After treatment with NPG750 and CPG2000, the SEM clearly demonstrated that the bacterial cells showed obvious morphological shrinkage, cytoplasmic leakage, and pore formation, while the formation of blank areas and the separation of the plasma membrane and cell wall were also observed by TEM. These micrograph observations further demonstrated that NPG750 and CPG2000 exert antibacterial activity through membrane-active mechanisms (Figure 7).

    A variety of self-assembled peptide nanodrugs has been widely used in biotechnology. These peptides are self-assembled to form well-defined nanostructures such as tubes, bands, fibers, spheres, and micelles in the past few decades. The self-assembly of peptides is essential for their clinical application with the advantages of good biocompatibility, versatility, and specific recognition.(33,34)
    In the present study, PG-1 was coupled with PEG to form nanostructures, reducing the toxicity and solving the problem of lower trypsin stability. To retain the effect of PG-1 on induced transmembrane migration and bacterial leakage, two types of PEG binding sites were set: terminal carboxyl group (CPGn) and an amino group (NPGn). A series of engineered peptides NPGn and CPGn was designed for developing new antibacterial nanodrugs, which have low cytotoxicity and high trypsin stability. The results displayed some differences between N-terminal and C-terminal functions. The CD analysis showed that PEG modification did not change the secondary structure of PG-1, but NPGn promoted the rapid formation of folded antiparallel β-sheets and an angular domain of PG-1. All peptides formed stable β-sheets in SDS. At the same time, NPGn mainly existed in the antiparallel β-sheet form in PBS solution, which may be related to the formation of a nanofiber network. The increase of free energy driven by the rise in the β-sheet content promotes the construction of fiber structures with intermolecular hydrogen bonding. NPG750, a kind of NPGn, was mainly present as β-sheets in PBS, which was induced by the signal of a higher aggregate tendency. However, CPG750 and CPG2000, two kinds of CPGn, showed a more disordered conformation in PBS, indicating that the C-terminal modification of PG-1 was flexible and tolerates structural and functional changes. Although the C-terminal conversion is flexible in theory, some of the functions obtained are persuasive.(35)
    The antibacterial effect played a major function in remolding PG-1. The antibacterial activity was significantly reduced in NPGn but improved with varying degrees in CPGn. As known to all, the antibacterial mechanism of AMPs is not well elucidated clearly. A widely accepted theory is that the positive charge and hydrophobic amino acids of AMPs bind to the lipid bilayer receptors on bacteria through electrostatic gravity and hydrophobic forces, resulting in bacterial death with membrane leakage.(36,37) PEG could break the delicate balance between hydrophilicity and hydrophobicity of AMPs itself. Furthermore, PEGylation has no effect on the physical properties of the membrane surface. For recognization, the N-terminal of PG-1 preferentially inserts into the phospholipid bilayer,(35) and PEG closed the positive charge of arginine and hydrophobicity leading to the decrease or even loss of their activity in NPGn. In contrast, the amino terminal of CPG2000 was not affected by the mPEG coupling at the C-terminal, which could also insert into the cell membrane. This transformation increased the surface area of CPG2000 acting on lipids, improving the antibacterial activity. It is worth noting that the MIC values of NPG750 and CPG2000 for E. coli K88 were 16 and 2 μg/mL, respectively, which were lower than their CAC values of 32.64 and 59.25 μg/mL; these results indicated that the killing of bacteria by NPG750 and CPG2000 was still by their monomer molecules.
    The toxicity of AMPs is an important risk factor in clinical applications. NPG750 and CPG2000 showed dose-dependent toxicity on human blood cells and HEK 293T cells. For NPG750, the charge shielding effect may be one of the reasons for reducing toxicity.(20,38) The fibrosis of N-terminal mPEG produces a sizeable steric hindrance to form a shell to wrap charged amino acids and hydrophobic amino acids, which reduces the binding force of NPG750 to the eukaryotic cell membrane. For PG-1, the C-terminal is flexible, and the N-terminal of AMPs preferentially inserts into the cell membrane,(35) which retains the spatial site of CPG2000 acting on the cell membrane. Because of this flexibility, the trend of CPG2000 aggregation is weak. The nanoaggregates do not encapsulate CPG2000 completely, and the positive charge exposes as a hydrophilic group. When the nanoparticles were close to the cell membrane, the larger sphere volume induced an increase of cytotoxicity with large-scale tearing of the cell membrane. The surface of bacterial cells and eukaryotic cells is composed of different lipid compositions. The OM of mammalian cells is mainly composed of PC, sphingomyelin, and some cholesterol molecules, which are electrically neutral under physiological pH. Furthermore, the extracellular lobules of bacteria are composed of phosphatidylglycerol (PG) and negatively charged phospholipids, which provides the relative specificity of peptides for cell selection.(39−41)
    Further study of the antibacterial mechanism revealed that all the engineered peptides retained the membrane dissolution effects as PG-1 in a concentration-dependent trend. The results showed that the OM permeability of NPG750 was more vital than its IM permeability and LPS binding ability compared with PG-1. The stability and membrane breaking ability of CPG2000 were improved. The creation of nanofibers reduced the ability of binding to negatively charged LPS by the encapsulated positively charged and hydrophobic amino acids with the PEG shell at high peptide concentrations.(23) The penetration of NPG750 may be accomplished by aggregation on the OM of bacteria. The covering of the fiber network increases the surface area of the engineered peptide contact membrane and causes instability to the OM, and a large number of fibers inserting into the membrane resulted in higher OM permeability of NPG750. For example, some surfactant-like self-assembly peptides form nanorods to complete the sterilization process by inserting into the OM to increase the surface tension of the membrane.(42) CPG2000 caused a significant increase of IM permeability and binding ability than PG-1 at low concentrations (4–16 μg/mL). This was not caused by spherical micelles because the MIC value of CPG2000 against E. coli K88 (2 μg/mL) was lower than that of PG-1 (8 μg/mL). This may be determined by the antibacterial ability of the peptide itself. In the LUVs of the simulated bacterial membrane (POPC/POPG = 3:1) and eukaryotic cell membrane (POPC/POPG = 10:1), the binding rate of engineered peptides to liposome membrane decreased after self-assembly. When the hydrophilic shell wraps the core peptide, the local density of PG-1 itself decreases. Therefore, it showed a low binding rate of the bacterial and eukaryotic cell membranes. This indicates that the toxicity of the self-assembled engineered peptides to bacteria and eukaryotic cells will be reduced. A schematic model of the antibacterial mechanism is shown in Figure 8.
    As shown in Figure 9, trypsin mainly acts on the peptide bond formed by lysine and arginine carboxyl. Arginine and lysine of melittin and PG-1 were cut off, making them lose their ability to adhere to the bacterial membrane.(27,43) The fiber network, the high aggregation trend of NPG750, composed of a hydrophilic shell to avoid the enzyme cutting site of trypsin, and mPEG acted as the carrier; CPG2000 showed an incomplete aggregation ability, and the active center was not encapsulated in the spherical micelle of CPG2000 aggregation. Because there were still a small number of fibers in CPG2000 at high concentrations, it was difficult for the spherical micelle structure to resist the cleavage of trypsin cleavage. The small number of fibers may be the reason for the higher antitrypsin ability of CPG2000 than PG-1.
    The previous studies had shown that the decrease and loss of activity were common with the introduction of PEG at the N-terminal of peptides or proteins,(19,44−46) but few studies reported the modification of mPEG at the C-terminal. A comparative report study had reported the N-terminal and C-terminal coupling mPEG of GIP. The N-terminal and C-terminal residues of GIP (1–30) were modified with PEG (long chain, 40 KDa), and the N-terminal modification of GIP directly abolished the functional activity. On the contrary, the C-terminal-modified GIP maintained the original mobility and potency of receptor binding and produced tolerance to dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPPIV). It was also found that the resistance of C-terminal-modified GIP to DPPIV was not complete. This resistance was stabilized by palmitoyl introduced into the N-terminal. It seemed that the functional change of the C-terminal was regulated by the N-terminal.(47) The present study showed a different result from the previous studies. First, mPEG has a low molecular weight (750–2000 Da), and this modification will not completely inactivate NPGn. Second, additional modifiers on the N-terminal of CPG2000 were not needed to stabilize the C-terminal function. The study indicates that the modification sites of PEG were different and a non-natural cysteine thiol group at the end of GIP for PEG binding. Both NPGn and CPGn showed that the length of low molecular weight mPEG had no significant correlation with the antibacterial activity, toxicity, and trypsin stability. Previous studies show a negative correlation between the molecular weight of mPEG and the activity of peptides.(19,46,48) Combined with our research, the molecular weight of mPEG was too small to reach the threshold.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, by introducing PEG-coupling modification, we successfully converted the porcine AMP PG-1 into an efficacious nanoantibacterial agent, which greatly improved the proteolytic resistance of PG-1. Meanwhile, we found that PEG coupled to the N- or C-terminal showed different effects on the biological activity of PG-1. PEG coupled to the N-terminal would weaken the antibacterial activity of PG-1 while reducing its cytotoxicity, which was the opposite of PEG coupled to the C-terminal. Among all designed PEGylated peptides, NPG750 and CPG2000 possessed high cell selectivity, great stability in the physiological environment, and good antitrypsin hydrolytic activity. Additionally, NPG750 and CPG2000 exerted their bactericidal activity by membrane-active mechanisms, which suggested that microbes were less likely to develop drug resistance against them. In a word, the construction of nanoparticles modified by PEG is an effective strategy to improve the clinical application potential of AMPs, and this also opened up a new route to the research and development of nanodrugs.

    Synthesis and Characterization of Peptides

    mPEG-COOH (750, 1000, and 2000 Da) and mPEG-NH2 (750, 1000, and 2000 Da) were purchased from Pengsheng Biochemistry Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Engineered peptides were synthesized by GL Biochem Corporation (Shanghai, China). The fidelity was measured by MALDI-TOF MS (Linear Science Co., Ltd.). The purity (>95%) and retention time of peptides were determined by RP-HPLC. The chromatographic column was GS-12 0-5-C18-BION 4.6 × 250 mm2, 220 nm, 10 μL, and the nonlinear water/acetonitrile gradient elution was used.

    Bacterial Strains

    The bacteria strains E. coli ATCC 25922, E. coli K88, E. coli K99, E. coli 987P, E. coli CVCC 1515, E. coli CICC 21530, E. coli 078, Salmonella pullorum C7913, and Salmonella typhimurium ATCC14028 and C7731 were obtained from the College of Veterinary Medicine, Northeast Agricultural University (Harbin, China), and E. coli UB1005 was kindly provided by the State Key Laboratory of Microbial Technology (Shandong University, China). Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus) ATCC 29213, S. aureus ATCC 25923, S. aureus 43300, Staphylococcus epidermidis ATCC 12228, Bacillus subtilis 63501, Enterococcus faecalis ATCC 29212, P. aeruginosa ATCC 27853, P. aeruginosa PAO1, and clinical isolates of Acinetobacter baumannii were provided by the 2nd Affiliated Hospital of Harbin Medical University (Harbin, China). Listeria monocytogenes CGMCC1.10753 were purchased from China General Microbiological Culture Collection Center (CGMCC).

    CD Spectroscopy

    The secondary structures of each peptide (25 μg/mL) were measured in 1.5 mM PBS (pH 7.4) and 1.5 mM SDS as previously described.(43)

    Biocompatibility Assays

    The biocompatibility of the engineered peptides was tested as previously described by assessing the cytotoxicity of the peptides to human red blood cells and HEK 293T cells.(49)

    Antimicrobial Activity Assays

    The MICs/MBCs of the engineered peptides against a series of bacterial strains were determined using the modified broth microdilution method of the National Committee for Clinical Laboratory Standards (NCCLS) as previously described.(50) The time–kill curve of the peptide was further plotted. A bacterial suspension (5 mL) (0.5–1 × 105 CFU/mL) was incubated with or without the peptide at 1× MIC, and at various exposure times, 100 μL of the samples was taken out and spread on Mueller–Hinton agar (MHA) plates to count the number of viable colonies and calculate the bacterial survival rate. The experiment was repeated three times independently.(51)

    Salt and Serum Sensitivity Assays

    The salt and serum sensitivity of a peptide was evaluated as previously described by determining the antibacterial activity of the peptide in the presence of physiological salts (150 mM NaCl, 4.5 mM KCl, 6 μM NH4Cl, 8 μM ZnCl2, 1 mM MgCl2, 2 mM CaCl2, and 4 μM FeCl3) or serum (12.5, 25, and 50%).(52) The microorganism was E. coli K88. The experiment was repeated three times independently.

    Drug Resistance Assay

    The drug resistance of E. coli K88 to the engineered peptides was evaluated using the sequential passaging method.(49) Ampicillin was employed as the model antibiotic for the drug resistance assay. First, the MICs of the peptide and ampicillin were determined as described previously. Next, each sub-MIC concentration of bacteria was diluted to 1 × 105 CFU/mL with Mueller–Hinton Broth (MHB, AOBOX) and was used for the next passage MIC determination. The above procedure was repeated for 30 passages.

    CAC Assay

    The CAC of the peptide was determined using the ANS fluorescence assay. ANS was dissolved in dimethylformamide to a concentration of 1 mM, and then 1 μL of this solution was added to 100 μL of different concentrations of peptide. The mixed solution was transferred to a quartz cuvette and tested using an F-4500 fluorescence spectrophotometer (Hitachi, Japan), and the fluorescence spectrum was collected from 420 to 550 nm with excitation at 360 nm. The CAC was determined from the matched curve, which was obtained by the ANS fluorescence intensities at 475 nm plotted against different concentrations of the peptide.

    DLS Assay

    The hydrodynamic diameter of the particles present in the peptide–ellipticine formulation was determined using a Zetasizer Nano Z90 (Malvern Instruments, Worcestershire, UK). Formulated solutions (1 mL) were placed in a disposable plastic cuvette. The reflective index and viscosity of pure water were used as the solvent settings, while the reflective index of the solute was set to be the same as that of the proteins. The temperature was maintained at 25 °C, and the solution was equilibrated for 2 min before data acquisition. Three measurements were performed for each sample. The data were automatically analyzed with the software to generate the intensity-based size distribution of the complexes.

    Trypsin Stability Assay

    The trypsin stability of the peptides was evaluated using the modified MIC method and 16.5% Tricine–SDS-PAGE method.(43,49) To determine the impact of trypsin concentration on the antimicrobial activities of the peptides, the engineered peptides were incubated with different concentrations of trypsin (1, 2, 4, and 8 mg/mL) for 1, 2, 4, and 8 h at 37 °C. E. coli K88 was used in the assays, and the subsequent method was the MIC test method. After the engineered peptides were incubated in the presence of 8 mg/mL trypsin for 1, 2, 4, and 8 h, the effect of incubation time on the engineered peptide was determined using the 16.5% Tricine–SDS-PAGE method. Finally, the engineered peptide was diluted to the concentration of MIC and incubated with trypsin, and then trypsin was removed in a water bath at 100 °C, and 20 μL was dropped into the MHA plate. The number of colonies was calculated every other day. To determine that the engineered peptide avoids the restriction site by formed a self-assembly structure.

    LPS Binding Assay

    The LPS binding of the peptides was determined using the BODIPY-TR-cadaverine (BC, Sigma, USA) displacement assay as previously described.(43) LPS came from E. coli O111:B4 (Sigma, USA).

    LPS Competitive Inhibition Assay

    LPS (50 μL) from E. coli O111:B4 and peptides (50 μL) were incubated for 1 h to achieve a final LPS concentration of 4–1024 μg/mL. Then, 100 μL of bacterial solution (0.5 × 105 CFU/mL) was added to the peptide–LPS mixture and incubated at 37 °C for 90 min. The final concentration of peptide was maintained at 1 × MIC. Then, a volume of 20 μL of the mixture was taken out from each well, diluted with an appropriate amount of PBS, spread on MHA agar plates, and cultured overnight. The number of colonies was counted. The experiment was repeated three times independently.

    OM Permeability Assays

    The OM permeabilization of the peptides against E. coli K88 was monitored using the fluorescent dye NPN uptake assay. The detailed protocol was consistent with the method reported by Wang et al.(43)

    CM Depolarization Assays

    The change of the CM potential of E. coli K88 with peptide treatment was monitored using a membrane potential-dependent probe DiSC3-5 as previously described.(3) The fluorescence density was recorded from 0 to 2000 s.

    IM Permeability Assay

    E. coli K88 cells were incubated to mid-log phase in MHB, which contained 2% lactose at 37 °C, and then harvested and diluted to an OD600 of 0.05 in 5 mM HEPES buffer (pH 7.4, containing 20 mM glucose and 1.5 mM ONPG). Then, cell suspensions and equal volumes of peptide solution (4–32 μg/mL) were added to 96-well plates and incubated at 37 °C. Images were recorded at time 0 and every 180 s until 5000 s at OD420.

    Liposome Leakage

    Calcein was dissolved in 4 mol/L NaOH solution, a dye buffer (30 mM Tris, 20 mM NaCl, and 0.1 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid, pH 7.4) was added until calcein was completely dissolved to 70 mM, and the solution was adjusted to 7.4 with HCl. Calcein-entrapped LUVs were made as described previously.(43) The unencapsulated calcein was eluted on a Sephadex G-50 column at a flow rate of 0.5 mL/min. The running buffer was Tris–HCl (50 mm, pH value 7.4). LUVs were diluted to 100 μM in the buffer. Then, 50 μL of liposome solution and 50 μL of peptide solution were incubated for15 min at room temperature. The calcein leakage rate was determined as mentioned earlier.(46)

    SEM Characterization of Bacterial Samples

    Mid-log phase E. coli K88 cells (OD600nm = 0.02) were incubated with the peptides at 1× MIC for 1 h and then harvested and washed three times with PBS by centrifugation at 5000 g for 5 min.(50) Peptide samples: NPG750 and CPG2000 were smeared on the cover slide (10 μL), dried naturally in an aseptic operating table, and observed on the sample. The final concentrations of peptides were 512 μg/mL and the CAC value.

    TEM Characterization

    Bacterial Samples

    Bacterial samples were prepared according to the method reported by Wang et al.(43) Peptide samples: To characterize the shape of peptides in neutral and acidic Milli-Q water, NPG750 and CPG2000 were visualized using a TEM (Hitachi HT7800 TEM). The peptide solution (10 μL) was plated on copper-coated grids (400 square mesh, Electron Microscopy Sciences) for 5 min, and the grids were stained with 0.1% phosphotungstic acid for 30 s and dried for 15 min before imaging, operating at 100 kV.

    Statistical Analysis

    The data were analyzed by ANOVA and Student’s t-test using Social Sciences (SPSS) version 20.0 (Chicago, Illinois, USA). Quantitative data were expressed as the mean ± SE/SD, and P < 0.01 was considered statistically significant.

        Highly Stabilized α-Helical Coiled Coils Kill Gram-Negative Bacteria by Multicomplementary Mechanisms under Acidic Condition
        Lai, Zhenheng; Tan, Peng; Zhu, Yongjie; Shao, Changxuan; Shan, Anshan; Li, Lu
        ACS Applied Materials & Interfaces (2019), 11 (25), 22113-22128CODEN: AAMICK; ISSN:1944-8244. (American Chemical Society)
        Although antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) hold tremendous promise in overcoming the threats of multidrug resistance, the main obstacle to successful therapeutic applications is their poor stability. Various synthetic strategies such as unnatural amino acids and chem. modifications have made advances for improving this problem. However, this complicated synthesis often greatly increases the cost of prodn. Here, a series of novel peptides, designed by combining an α-helical coiled coil model, knowledge of the specificity of proteolysis and major parameters of AMPs, exhibited efficient activity against all tested Gram-neg. bacteria under acidic condition and demonstrate low toxicity. Of these α-helical coiled coil peptides, 3IH3 displayed the highest av. therapeutic index (GMTI = 294.25) with high stability toward salts, serum, extreme pH, heat, and proteases. Electron microscopy and biol. anal. technique analyses showed that 3IH3 killed bacterial cells via a multicomplementary mechanism at pH 6.0, with phys. membrane disruption as the dominant bactericidal mechanism. These results suggest that 3IH3 shows great stability as an inexpensive and effective antimicrobial activity agent and has the potential for clin. application in the treatment of infections occurring in body sites with acidic pH.
