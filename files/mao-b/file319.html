    Abstract

    The traditional drug discovery strategy of pursuing “one compound–one target” has had difficulties delivering novel therapies for complex diseases currently lacking adequate treatments. An alternative and complementary approach is the design of multitargeted modulators simultaneously addressing multiple pathological mechanisms or overcoming pathway robustness. In this study, we propose a methodology to increase the probability of success for developing dual-acting modulators by systematically and rationally evaluating all dual-acting modulator opportunities within a specific disease area. This approach employs a combination of a five-step medicinal chemistry evaluation and a two-step biological analysis to help select the optimal target combination. It provides a novel methodology suitable for widespread application across disease areas. To exemplify the power of this approach, we focus on an analysis of the gastrointestinal (GI) disease area to identify opportunities supported by current literature data.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The discovery and development of new chemical entities is a high risk, high cost process. It is estimated that it can cost more than a billion U.S. dollars to bring a drug to the market.(1) This large cost is clearly unsustainable; thus, cost reduction is of the highest priority for everyone working within drug discovery and development. From an efficacy perspective, it is becoming increasingly apparent that it is often insufficient to modulate a single target in isolation to effectively address disease progression. To illustrate this way of thinking, the term network pharmacology has been established.(2) This approach highlights that targets and pathways rarely act in isolation; rather there exist numerous feedback loops and interconnections that might render a single molecule/single target approach unsuccessful because of pathway redundancies. By analyzing the Drugbank database,(3) Vidal et al. demonstrated that although successful drugs developed since the advent of the molecular revolution have been targeted against a single molecular entity, they do in fact demonstrate a level of promiscuity that could influence both efficacy and safety.(4) Indeed, by use of the same database, it was demonstrated that drugs developed to target a single molecular entity are on average active against 1.7 proteins. However, if the bioactivities reported in the literature as well as predicted bioactivities are taken into account, the number climbs to 6.3.(5, 6) For example, angiotensin II receptor type 1 is the primary target of telmisartan. However, telmisartan has also been shown to modulate the peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor γ (PPAR-γ) receptor,(7) which has been suggested to explain the improved insulin sensitivity with telmisartan treatment that is not evident in other more selective angiotensin receptor antagonists.(8)
    In many of the cited examples, the modulation of multiple targets has been unintentional, and clearly this type of approach presents challenges not only in effectively optimizing the activities for the selected targets but also in simultaneously optimizing all the ADME properties necessary to develop the dual acting molecule into a successful drug. However, despite these considerable challenges, several success stories have recently been reported in the literature.(9, 10) In fact, multitarget drugs are now common in the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases(11) and additional successful examples have also been published for depression, hypertension, oncology, inflammation, and diabetes.(9, 12, 13)
    In recent years, there has been tremendous progress in the availability of data in both the medicinal chemistry and biology disciplines that can be interrogated through increasingly sophisticated cheminformatics and bioinformatics tools. A variety of public and commercial databases exist that are suitable for data mining: PubChem,(14) a public repository of screening data; Chembl,(14, 15) a database of manually curated activity data extracted from the biomedical literature; BioPrint,(16) a commercial database of screening results for a set of drugs and other pharmacologically relevant substances against a panel of targets; Drugbank,(3) a public database combining detailed drug data with drug target information. There are also commercial databases with activity data extracted from journals and patents.(17) In combination, these resources provide access to an unprecedented amount of data associating compounds to biological activities and have made it feasible to develop a more rational approach to drug discovery than was previously possible. In the same way, more and more data, such as for protein–protein interactions or gene expression, are available to study the biology associated with proteins. Additionally, modern text mining approaches can add further value by extracting detailed compound–target relationships from the wealth of publically available biomedical literature. Recently, a number of studies have been published demonstrating the ability to connect all this information into a coherent system.(18-20)
    The combination of the knowledge that most drugs modulate more than one target and the increased availability of relevant chemical and biological information prompted us to develop a methodology to combine both medicinal chemistry and biological information in order to systematically evaluate all possible dual modulators within a specific disease area. A modulator in this context can be an inhibitor, a blocker, an agonist, an antagonist, or a positive/negative allosteric modulator. The methodology includes five medicinal chemistry workflows (MCWs) to help estimate the chemical feasibility of a dual modulator approach and two biology workflows (BWs) for a mechanistic evaluation of opportunities. The workflows, which are general, can be applied to any disease area and have been built to display chemical relationships and information extracted from internal and external data sets in an efficient manner allowing the rapid identification of dual-acting modulator opportunities. With the GI disease area as an example, the value of this methodology to support robust decision making is highlighted.

    Application of the MCWs To Explore Dual-Acting Modulator Opportunities within the GI Disease Area

    The five MCWs in our methodology are presented in Figure 1. They are generated separately and display complementary information to each other. However, when combined, they provide a thorough overview of the suitability for each target combination as a starting point for the discovery and development of a rationally designed dual-acting modulator. In this study, we illustrate the application of the proposed workflow to a set of targets linked to GI diseases and highlight specific opportunities for dual-acting modulators within this disease area. Examples of dual-target modulators (small molecules and peptides) identified through the workflow will be presented. However, it should be noted that examples presented here are all already known and the data available in the public domain. The targets used in the investigation of the GI disease area are given in Table 1, each of which has previously been associated with GI diseases in the literature. The Entrez Gene identifier(21) was used as a handle to extract the activity data from the GoStar(17) and BioPrint databases. In all the evaluations, three different activity cut-offs were used, 10 μM, 1 μM, and 100 nM (see Experimental Section).     Figure 2A presents the outcome from MCW 1 applied to the GI target set. Initially the GoStar database was used to identify target combinations for which there was pre-existing literature evidence for dual-acting modulators. The heat map generated highlights the existence of any previously published information across the range of the chosen dual-acting opportunities. It is not distinguished in the analysis whether the dual-acting modulator is designed to be dual-acting or whether the activity at one of the targets was considered to represent an off-target effect. As might be expected, the second case is quite common for two targets that have a high sequence similarity. For instance, there are a number of published structures that exhibit dual-target activity for highly similar sequences, for example, the cholecystokinin A receptor and cholecystokinin B receptor (CCK1 and CCK2) and the muscarinic M1 receptor and muscarinic M3 receptor (CHRM1 and CHRM3) target combinations (see Figure 2A,B and Figure 3).
    MCW 2 extends the initial analysis to include data where the activities for the different targets have been reported in different publications for a compound (Figure 2B). A general observation is that the heat map is much more populated in Figure 2B than in 2A. This is due to the same compound having been reported to have activity against more than one target relevant for the disease area. However, these occurrences may arise across a number of different sources and the dual-activity might therefore not necessarily be noticed. This is an important aspect in future prioritization of opportunities, and if more and additionally structurally diverse, dual-acting modulators are known for a target combination, it is assumed to be easier to develop a novel dual-acting modulator with high-quality druglike properties. Figure 2B also highlights situations where there is an absence of characterized dual modulators for a given target combination but where structurally similar compounds do exist for each of the individual targets. An example is the chemokine (C–C motif) receptor 9 (CCR9). In this case there are no known dual-acting modulators for CCR9 and any of the other targets analyzed for the GI disease area. However, identified compounds do exist that are active on CCR9 and that have similarity to compounds active on other targets in Table 1 (e.g., CCK2 and the purinergic receptor P2X, ligand-gated ion channel 3 (P2X3)). Furthermore this analysis highlights the existence of a number of compounds that modulate greater than two targets, highlighting the requirement to carefully examine the activity profile for each molecule individually. This can be achieved through drilling down of the displayed data via a set of Web interfaces to display all biological activities for each molecule. Finally, it is also sometimes useful to display the difference between the workflows 2 and 1 to emphasize the potential dual-acting modulator opportunities for which there is no previous publication describing molecules with activities against both targets in the same publication.
    The third MCW (MCW 3) is designed to identify opportunities to validate a target combination in vivo.For this task, only approved drugs and clinical candidates were extracted from BioPrint and GoStar databases. These compounds are most likely to have suitable ADME properties for preclinical in vivo experiments and therefore represent optimal tool compounds to effectively validate a given target combination. Figure 2C presents any such opportunities identified from within the GI disease area. A circle highlights target pairs suitable for testing in combination type in vivo experiments, whereas the availability of a dual-acting drug or clinical candidate for a target combination is indicated by a star. We have purposely designed Figure 2C to be asymmetrical in order to specifically highlight the number of compounds that are available for each of the chosen target pairs. The number of active compounds for each of the two targets is shown through the color and size of the dot. For targets on the horizontal axis, the number of active compounds is shown through the color, and for targets on the vertical axis the number of compounds available is shown through the size of the dot. The data displayed for an activity threshold of 100 nM are especially interesting, since these compounds have a high probability of demonstrating an effect in a relevant in vivo model. The availability of a dual-acting modulator is not necessary for the validation of an additive or synergistic effect for the target combination, as the hypothesis can be validated through a combination of two different compounds with good enough potency and selectivity, ADME, and pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) profile for one of the two targets each. It is important to investigate the selectivity profile of the compounds used in the in vivo experiment. Preferably the selected dual-acting modulator (or the two different modulators, one for each target) should have a good selectivity profile against other targets so that there is confidence that the observed in vivo effect is due to the modulation of the two targets of interest and not an off-target effect. Here, only data from the BioPrint and GoStar databases are displayed; however, these data can be supplemented by other internal and external sources.
    An analysis of bioactivity data indicates that multiple acting modulators tend to be more lipophilic and somewhat larger than more selective compounds.(22) This phenomenon has been referred to as the “physicochemical challenge” in designing dual-acting modulators. Therefore, our interest primarily focused on dual-acting modulator opportunities where the extent of molecular framework integration is maximized while avoiding compromises on physicochemical properties and oral bioavailability. Along these lines, MCW 4 was designed to indirectly estimate the similarity of the binding cavities through combined analysis of the physicochemical properties of known modulators for each of the targets. It will be more straightforward to develop a dual-acting modulator if the binding cavities are similar for the target combination for which there is an intention to develop the dual-acting modulator. The binding cavity similarity can be measured in two different ways, either directly by comparing the cavities to which the molecule binds or indirectly by comparing the properties of the molecules that bind to each cavity. The direct method would be preferred if the X-ray crystal structure is known for all the targets of interest. However, with the possible exception of the infection and oncology disease areas, it is unrealistic to expect crystal structures of all the targets under investigation to be available within a disease area. Therefore, as MCW 4, we have chosen to compare the binding cavities indirectly through comparison of the known modulators that bind to each of the targets. In a recent study, it was proposed that there are three fundamental properties of a molecule: size, lipophilicity, and ionization state.(23) Figure 2D presents the overlap of the distributions of the three physicochemical properties for the active molecules on each target as an indirect estimate of the similarity of the binding cavities. To aid visual inspection of the outcome of this analysis, the color of the dot reflects the overlap of the property that has the smallest overlap between the active compounds for the two targets. The shape indicates which property has the lowest overlap. By way of an illustration, the overlap between the active compounds for the ghrelin and motilin receptors (GHSR and MLNR) is shown in Figure 4. Inspection of the analysis presented for these targets in Figure 2D suggests only medium overlap for at least one of the physicochemical properties, and the shape indicates that the overlap is lowest for the size. Drilling down provides further evidence that the overlap of both the lipophilicity and the ionization state is high while it is only medium with respect to the size.
    The final MCW (MCW 5) presents an analysis of the number of privileged fragments in common for each target combination (Figure 2E). While it is considered beneficial if targets already possess robust molecules suitable for developing a dual-acting modulator, in many circumstances this may not be available. However, even in the absence of structurally similar molecules for a target combination it is of value to understand if the targets possess common privileged fragments. The presence of common privileged fragments identifies future opportunities to design chemical libraries with the intention of exploring novel chemical space and driving the synthesis of novel dual-acting modulators.(24-26) Moreover, an already known chemical equity might not be an optimal starting point for further exploitation. In this case, it might be very helpful to investigate if all the known active compounds on target A and all the known active compounds on target B have any privileged fragments in common. The number of privileged fragments for each target combination is displayed in Figure 2E. There exist privileged fragments for many of the target combinations, so at least for these cases there could be opportunities to design chemical libraries with the intention of exploring novel chemical space and identifying novel dual-acting modulators. Explicit examples of privileged fragments are given later in this section for the GHSR/MLNR combination.
    As a further addition to the medicinal chemistry analysis, we have we also included the potential to apply additional filtering criteria to the results: a “strict” option excluding data from patent(s) with no explicit reported activity data and a “normal” option including this data (see Experimental Section). A significant difference is observed between the two filtering methods. For example, by incorporation of an activity threshold of 10 μM, the “strict” way of filtering (excluding patents with no explicit activity data) extracted a total of 105 576 different compounds; whereas the “normal” filtering option retrieved 217 650 compounds. A consequence of these differences is the possibility of identifying increased numbers of target pairs with the “normal” way of filtering. Applying this approach to MCW 1 at 10 μM demonstrates the identification of 70 pairs of targets using the “normal” way of filtering compared with 61 through application of the “strict” filtering approach out of the 190 target pairs for the set of used GI targets. In the same way, at 1 μM, 64 pairs are identified as having dual modulators with the “normal” filtering (Figure 2A) whereas 48 are identified with the “strict” filtering. Given these findings, we advocated beginning with the “strict” filtering option which generates an output of increased confidence but with the caveat that some compounds might have been missed. As a second step, the results can subsequently be expanded using the “normal” filtering, where all available data are accessible but might contain more false positives.

    Application of the BWs To Explore Dual-Acting Modulator Opportunities with the GI Disease Area

    Not all information important for identifying new target combinations suitable for the development of dual-acting modulators resides in bioactive compound databases. The biology perspective of our approach focuses on both developing and strengthening the biological rationale for modulating two targets in conjunction, either through a dual-modulator approach or via coadministration of compounds selective for the individual targets combination (i.e., a combination treatment). There are two separate BWs: text mining and bioprocess mapping.
    The first BW (BW 1, text mining) is complementary with MCW 1: they both investigate the precedence of known literature studies on relevant target combinations. However, whereas MCW 1 identifies published dual-acting modulators, BW 1 focuses on applying text mining approaches to extract published information on combination therapies, previously identified functional synergies, and separate studies investigating both targets (see Experimental Section for more details). Figure 2F presents the output of BW 1 as applied to the GI target set and displays the numbers of documents identified through text mining defined as relevant for the specific evaluation. For the GI targets we found that there exist relevant documents for 83 of the 190 target pairs, with 71 pairs having greater than 10 text mining hits. This compares well to the 64 pairs of potential dual modulators identified through the first MCW using an activity threshold of 1 μM. A total of 33 overlapping target pairs were identified by both processes. Some examples from the analysis of the GHSR/MLNR combination are discussed in more detail below (see Example 1).
    The second BW (BW 2, bioprocess mapping) aims to provide a mechanistic overview of the impact of modulating the two targets in combination. This approach maps the impact of modulating each target individually or both targets in combination at the pathway level, the intracellular and macrocellular levels, the pathophysiology level, and finally the disease level. Both GeneGO MetaCore(27) and Ingenuity IPA (Ingenuity Systems, www.ingenuity.com) databases provide rich data for many targets at the first two levels, i.e., pathway and intracellular processes. However, to enable a full interpretation of the potential clinical impact of modulating key targets, literature mining is also used to further link the targets to the pathophysiology and primary disease processes. Any gaps in the network maps are filled in an iterative process in a close collaboration with the disease area biology experts. By taking a multilevel view, key similarities and differences between the two mechanisms of action can be readily highlighted. This detailed approach provides an opportunity to develop a comprehensive overview of the predicted role targets may play in disease mechanisms and the consequences of targeting them with dual-acting modulators. For example, the use of common intracellular pathways may indicate potential opportunities to overcome redundancy seen for single targets or the modulation of pathophysiology via two distinct biological processes (and in some cases present in distinct organs), which may further strengthen an argument for dual modulation.
    Figure 5 highlights an example of detailed bioprocess mapping for the GI target pair of NK1R and μ-opioid receptor (also discussed later in this section). The map highlights how both targets affect GI motility and abdominal pain. This task is manually intensive and highly time-consuming; therefore, it is only carried out for the most relevant target pairs identified using the five MCWs and the BW1. It is, however, considered that for key target combinations this is time well spent and the developed network maps have a number of benefits to projects and organizations from supporting decision-making to effective information and data-sharing.

    Example 1: Dual Ghrelin and Motilin Receptor Opportunities Identified from the Workflows

    With the help of the MCWs, a series of small molecule based opportunities was identified with GHSR being one of the components of a dual-acting modulator (Figures 2 and 6). It is perhaps not particularly surprising that MLNR is identified as one of the preferred combinations with GHSR, considering that the endogenous precursor peptides of GHSR and MLNR share almost 50% sequence similarity in their amino acid sequence.(28) The receptors also form part of the same subfamily of G-protein-coupled receptors and share 53% overall amino acid sequence identity. Furthermore, both peptides have been demonstrated to play an important role in GI motility regulating the interdigestive migrating contractions (IMC) resulting in prokinetic effects.(29) This is supported by the data generated from BW 1 in Figure 2F which clearly demonstrates that for the GHSR the combination with motilin presents the greatest level of biological validation. Approaching this question the other way, however, by starting with motilin also predicts the involvement of other key targets including 5-HT3, 5-HT4, and dopamine D2 receptors. By combination of this level of biological information to strengthen the biological rationale for identifying dual-acting molecules with the MCW, it is more straightforward to identify and prioritize future opportunities for dual-modulator approaches. A number of examples of dual-acting MLNR and GHSR modulators can be identified from the heat map shown in Figure 2B (see Figure 6 for examples).
    For example, the dual acting benzazepine sulfonamides were discovered by a high-throughput screen against the MLNR.(30) Further, structural modifications highlighted that the activity at both receptors (Table 2) can be comodulated as well as tuned toward a selective orally bioavailable MLNR agonist. While the developability toward selective dual-acting modulators still needs to be further evaluated, the available structure–activity relationship (SAR) data provide an early insight into the opportunities and risks aligned with the benzazepine series in this context.(30) Additionally, this series highlights the ease with which existing chemical equity in the literature can be scrutinized in the MCWs. Indeed and not surprisingly, as displayed in Figure 4, the active compounds of the GHSR and the MLNR share similar physicochemical properties (size, lipophilicity, and ionization state; MCW 4). The lipophilicity and ionization states of the active compounds are very similar for MLNR and GHSR, while the active compounds on the MLNR receptor are larger than the active molecules on the GHSR receptor. Therefore, from a physicochemical perspective the challenge to develop a dual-acting modulator will be mainly from a size perspective. Further analysis of the data sets highlights that many ligands acting on the GHSR and MLNR receptors share common substructural elements such as a benzazepine residue as displayed in Figure 6. The outlined workflow MCW 3 allows the identification of tool compounds suitable for in vivo/in vitro target validation work. Compounds such as MA-2029, GM-611, and ibutamoren were easily identified as tools for the GHSR and MLNR (Figure 7).

    Example 2: Dual NK1–Opioid Receptor Opportunities

    A second example identified through the application of this process within the GI disease area is dual-acting neurokinin type 1 (NK1) and μ-opioid receptor modulators. Opioids are the most widely used analgesics, and the effects of compounds acting directly at the G-protein-coupled μ-opioid receptor (MOR) have been well documented.(31) However, the clinical treatment of pain, and particularly chronic neuropathic pain, remains a major challenge due to tolerance, physical dependence, and the limited access of many compounds to the CNS. In contrast, the G-protein-coupled receptor NK1 preferentially binds substance P, a peptide belonging to the tachykinin family that was originally discovered based on its ability to regulate intestinal motility, secretion, and vascular function in the gut.(32) Recent evidence has emerged for the biological interaction between the opioid and neurokinin systems at a number of different levels that have been captured using a bioprocess mapping approach as demonstrated in Figure 5. For example, MORs are coexpressed with NK1 receptors in the CNS and initial observations demonstrated that chronic opioid treatment results in the release of substance P and up-regulation of its receptor NK1. Furthermore, NK1 receptor knockout mice were largely protected from opioid-induced tolerance, and these findings have been further extended through clinical studies combining aprepitant, a clinically marketed NK1 selective antagonist, with opioid agonists to modulate the response to oxycodone in prescription opioid abusers.(33) These data have led to the suggestion that compounds exhibiting both MOR modulatory efficacy and NK1 receptor antagonist efficacy may offer the opportunity to address some of the undesirable side effects of current opioid-based therapies.(34, 35)
    The MCWs highlighted a series of NK1 antagonists that where selective against a panel of 14 other GPCRs.(36) However, they also highlighted that the compounds share significant μ-opioid receptor activity (Table 3). The SAR studies outlined in the work point toward the potential of the development of high affinity dual-acting modulators. In general, a monosubstitution on the aniline phenyl ring was shown to be slightly favorable with respect to μ-opioid affinity, and additionally replacing the isopropylidene moiety by other small groups (cyclopropyl) also improved the affinity on the μ-opioid receptor.(35)

    Example 3: Dual Opioid–CCK Receptor Opportunities

    Finally, although the primary focus for this study was on the identification of dual-acting small molecule compounds, the design of bispecific peptides has seen significant attention during the past few years, especially in the area of diabetes. However, one needs to keep in mind that the data sets used for the analysis are less rich for peptides compared to small molecules. So to further demonstrate the utility of our approach, the MCWs were used to identify known bispecific peptides based on overlapping privileged substructures (Figure 2E). Two examples are illustrated below. Hruby et al. designed novel peptides based on overlapping pharmacophores of the opioid and the cholecystokinin (CCK) endogenous peptides.(37) The novel peptides incorporate the opioid pharmacophores in the N-terminus and the CCK tetrapeptide pharmacophores in the C-terminus of the novel peptides (Figure 8). The designed peptides interact with δ (and/or μ) opioid receptors as agonists and with CCK1 and CCK2 receptors as antagonists based on the concept that the pain-relieving actions of the opiates may be limited by the pronociceptive and antiopiate actions of CCK8. The pharmacological action of these peptides was not further evaluated in pain models; however, their results highlight the utility of designing overlapping pharmacophores of two peptides into a single peptide as a valid drug design approach. A similar strategy has been taken by Kream et al. for the design of tachykinin substance P and opioid dual acting peptides.(38) Overlapping functional NH2- and COOH-terminal functional domains of the endogenous opioid endomorphin-2 and the tachykinin SP formed the basis for the development of chimeric peptide ESP-7 (Figure 8) which demonstrated a unique non-tolerance-forming analgesic effect.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this article, a methodology combining medicinal chemistry and biology workflows to systematically explore dual-acting modulator opportunities within a disease area has been proposed. The workflows presented integrated information in a common framework and display the information in an efficient way for the end users. Additionally, they can help identify already available dual-acting tool modulators, which allows for rapid in vivo (preclinical proof-of-principle) concept testing. They are a first step toward a strategy that will be complementary to current, more target-centric views.
    The intention in this study is to identify, in a comprehensive way, opportunities in medicinal chemistry to provide compounds that will modulate the activity of pharmacological systems rather than focusing purely on a single target activity. Through evaluation of all target combinations and all known chemical equity, the most appropriate dual-acting scaffold type can be identified and progressed. The goal of the presented MCWs is to find molecular structures that satisfy the basic pharmacophoric requirements of each target within an acceptable physicochemical property space. In our opinion, this needs to be combined with a detailed understanding of the role of each target in pathophysiological mechanisms in order to help prioritize the rational design of dual-acting modulators. This medicinal chemistry evaluation is supported by the biological workflows that provide an early insight into target pathways and their relationships for a specific target combination. Consequently, the analysis can be viewed as a “chemical biology” approach for designing dual-acting modulators.
    To illustrate and validate the power of the developed workflows, we have drawn upon previously described small molecule and peptide examples from the GI disease area. The intention of this study is not only to illustrate the workflow applied to a set of targets linked to GI diseases and to highlight specific opportunities for dual-acting modulators within this disease area but also to demonstrate the general applicability of this approach suitable for any disease area. An interesting future model might be to combine fragment based screening with the design of dual-acting modulators.(39) In this context, the fifth chemistry workflow comparing privileged ligand substructures between targets could provide relevant seeds for fragment based screening.
    It is emphasized that there are many further considerations such as peripheral vs central acting molecules, PK/PD relationships, and route of administration that need to be part of the feasibility assessment of dual-acting modulators. However, in this study, discussions regarding PK/PD are considered to be out of scope. We believe that these potential concerns require addressing on a case-by-case basis depending on the time scale needed to for the therapeutic effect. The rational design of molecules that simultaneously modulate different proteins, either positively or negatively, at comparable concentrations for each of the targets remains a challenging task. In many instances, further experimental assessment will be required to develop a deeper insight into the developability of these individual dual-acting modulators. The proposed analysis provides additional insight in intragene and intergene relationships, which allows for lead generation activities utilizing the SOSA (selective optimization of side activities) concept or identification of potential risks of undesired side effects within specific indication areas due to lack of selectivity.(40) For example, the documented intragene relationship of monoaminergic and peptidergic G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) modulators is also observed in the analysis of the GI target list. This represents a previously known relationship that has been used for developing new selective modulators for peptidergic GPCRs.(41) In the same way, an issue raised in the literature concerns dual modulator molecules being too large to be orally available. This particularly can be the case if a specific target combination is selected as the first step in the project. In this case, the receptor cavities for the two targets might be dissimilar. This might lead to the design of a too large dual-acting modulator, which, because of a large polar surface area (PSA), would have limited adsorption into the bloodstream. The intention of the careful analysis of all target pair combination presented here is to avoid selecting a target combination that because of mismatches in the binding cavities will lead to too large a compound during the optimization stage. The proposed workflow is mainly applicable for the development of novel orally available drug candidates. If other administration routes are possible or even preferred, it might be easier to develop a dual-acting modulator by connecting the two molecules with the help of a linker. In the end, opportunities within a disease area for dual-acting modulators need to be assessed versus single molecule–single target options and the prospects for combining two molecules modulating two different targets into one formulation. However, the feasibility of designing dual-acting modulators has been demonstrated and the lists of examples are growing constantly.(9) Some very recent examples in the literature are the design of dual-acting melanin-concentrating hormone receptor (MCHR1) antagonists or G-protein-coupled receptor 119 (GPR119) agonists that also show dipeptidyl peptidase 4 (DPP-IV) inhibition.(42)

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A novel workflow to identify opportunities for rationally designing dual-acting modulators has been proposed. The workflow aims to exploit all possibilities within a disease area to identify opportunities that avoid designing dual-acting modulators with poor physicochemical properties. Historically, the design of multiple acting modulators has had issues with combining good physicochemical properties of the modulator with high potencies on both targets. It is hoped that a thorough evaluation of all possible target combinations will improve the odds of identifying highly potent dual-acting modulators with good physicochemical properties. Examples are given here for targets involved in GI diseases. The GI disease area is an area with high unmet medical needs, and our analysis indicates that rational design of dual-acting modulators might be a feasible approach to identify novel treatments. The workflows in themselves do not make novel predictions of suitable target combinations. Instead the workflows collect all available relevant data and display the data in such a way that a user can very efficiently view all options and decide which options that should be prioritized for further validation in either in vitro or in vivo disease models.
    A thorough investigation of all possible target combinations to identify the best options from both a chemical and a biological perspective is, to our knowledge, a novel way of exploiting existing knowledge to create new drug discovery projects. In conclusion, it is proposed that evaluating both medicinal chemistry and biology aspects systematically for all target combinations within a disease area can lead to more successful drug discovery and development projects.

    Medicinal Chemistry Data Resources

    All data were extracted and filtered with Pipeline Pilot.(43) The resulting heat maps from the data processing were displayed in TIBCO Spotfire Decision site.(44) Activity data were extracted from the GoStar(17) and the BioPrint(16) databases. The Entrez Gene identifier was used to extract all active compounds for a given set of targets relevant for the investigated disease area. The information was filtered, and only dose–response activity data were used from GoStar. The data were extracted and analyzed for three different activity cut-offs, 100 nM, 1 μM, and 10 μM. However, in many patents, activity data are not included. It was therefore decided to have a “strict” option alternative, where all the data from patents without activity data were excluded, and a “normal” option when the data were included. Thus, for all the MCWs there are six alternatives based on the three activity cut-offs and the “strict” option for displaying the data. In BioPrint, the assays were manually mapped to Entrez Gene identifiers and all active compounds for a set of Entrez Gene identifiers were extracted. The extracted data from the BioPrint database were also included in the analysis at the three activity cut-offs 100 nM, 1 μM, and 10 μM. Additional sources of bioactivity data like Chembl and PubChem can be easily incorporated.

    Medicinal Chemistry Workflows

    All the cheminformatics analyses were done within Pipeline Pilot. In order to integrate both BioPrint and GoStar databases, compounds sharing the same canonical SMILES were considered identical.
    In MCW 1, compounds are extracted from GoStar only. Compounds active on both targets according to the considered threshold are taken into account if the activities have been published in the same article or patent. In MCW 2, extended connectivity fingerprints with a length of 4 (ECFP_4) available in Pipeline Pilot(45) were used for the similarity estimation. The Tanimoto coefficient was used to evaluate the similarity between molecules. Two molecules with a Tanimoto similarity above 0.6 were considered to be similar enough to highlight the possibility of designing a dual-acting modulator for the target combination. In MCW 3, all compounds from BioPrint are taken into account. Compounds extracted from the GoStar database are only included if they originate from the Drug Database or the Clinical Candidate Database. In MCW 4, the heavy atom count was used as a measure of molecular size. AlogP as implemented in Pipeline Pilot was used as a measure for liphophilicity. The ionization state was also calculated with Pipeline Pilot. Finally, for MCW 5, the ECFP of length 6 (ECFP_6) in Pipeline Pilot was used to define the fragments. Each bin in the ECFP_6 fingerprint represents one fragment. The use of ECFP_6 fingerprints in this part of the analysis, compared ECFP_4 previously, is due to ECFP_6 representing larger fragments of a molecule than ECFP_4. ECFP_6 provided more useful fragment information when the restriction to only include fragments with at least two rings was applied. The restriction ensured that the identified fragments would be of a reasonable size and good starting points for library designs. A standard Bayesian analysis as implemented in Pipeline Pilot was thereafter done to calculate the probability for each fingerprint bin in the set of compounds active on a given target and in the whole data set from the GoStar database. A Laplacian corrected estimator was used to account for undersampled fingerprint bins in the Bayesian analysis.(46) Only fingerprint bins that had 5 times higher frequency among the active compounds for both of the targets in comparison to their frequency in the whole data set were considered to be privileged and accordingly displayed in the MCW 5.
    Web interfaces to drill down from each heat map were developed in Pipeline Pilot.

    Biology Data Resources and Tools

    In order to develop text mining queries, sets of synonyms were extracted and manually curated for the targets of interest, the marketed drugs, and/or the clinical candidates that are known to modulate them. The data source used for extracting the protein synonyms was an in-house repository containing synonyms from the HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee (HGNC),(47) the VEGA 35 gene set from the Sanger Institute,(48) Entrez Gene,(21) Uniprot,(49) Thomson Pharma,(50) Thomson Reuters Integrity,(51) and GoStar. Similarly, the drug synonyms were extracted from an in-house repository containing synonyms from Thomson Pharma, Thomson Reuters Integrity, Adis R&D Insight,(52) and Citeline Pipeline.(53) The linkage between the targets and the drugs/clinical candidates was extracted from Thomson Pharma and Thomson Reuters Integrity.
    The text mining queries were run against an in-house data repository containing abstracts from publications available in Medline(54) and Embase,(55) conferences from the Knowledge Discovery Platform (MIS KDP),(56) Clinical Trials from ClinicalTrials.gov(57) and TrialTrove,(58) patents from the European Patent Office (EPO, http://www.epo.org/), grants from the National Institutes of Health (NIH) Reporter (http://report.nih.gov/index.aspx), the Medical Research Council (MRC, http://www.mrc.ac.uk/index.htm), and the Biotechnology and Biological Sciences Research Council (BBSRC, http://www.bbsrc.ac.uk/home/home.aspx), and European Research Council (ERC) grants (http://erc.europa.eu/). This document repository was searched using the open source search engine Solr.(59) The search was then refined and annotated using Pipeline Pilot Text Analytics Collection and the open source Peregrine engine.(60)
    For the second BW (bioprocess mapping), the GeneGo MetaCore software and the Ingenuity Pathways Analysis platform (IPA) were used as data sources. The literature search was done against Medline.(54) The maps are visualized in an interactive software program developed in-house (Network Graph Viewer) where evidence supporting each edge is directly accessible.

    The Two BWs

    In order to identify combination studies, a set of phrases related to drug/target combinations were manually identified in the literature. A drug in this context can be either a small molecule or a biological drug. Examples of these phrases included are the following: combination therapy, combination of drugs, combined, coadministration, dual modulator, and synergistic effect (see Supporting Information Data 1 for the complete list of the terms used). In the same way, in order to annotate those studies as relevant or not relevant for the disease area of interest, a set of terms related to the disease area was gathered from the literature. For the GI disease area example, terms included are the following: gastrointestinal, bowel, vomiting, diarrhea, and gastroparesis (see Supporting Information Data 2 for the complete list of the terms used). Text mining queries were then run against the in-house data source repository described above. Because of the huge diversity of phrases authors employ for describing drug or target combination studies, a “greedy” algorithm was adopted to identify co-occurrences of synonyms for a given protein A (or synonyms of drugs active on A), synonyms for a given protein B, and optionally combination and disease relevant terms for all protein A–protein B combinations.
    The search for drug–drug combinations takes advantage of the data structure in Thomson Pharma, Adis R&D Insight, and TrialTrove databases and identified combination terms used to annotate the technology employed to develop drugs.
    The resulting document set is enriched with relevant literature. To provide the opportunity for the end users to effectively filter the generated outputs depending on the disease area and biological mechanisms on which they are focused, this set is then postprocessed to calculate the following:
    (1)

    Number of proteins appearing in a sentence containing the proteins of interest. If the proteins of interest occur among several other protein names, it is reasoned that the study is probably not focused on this specific protein.

    (2)

    Number of times disease terms occur in a sentence that contains the disease of specific interest. In the same way as above, this will be used to filter out studies that are not focused on the disease area of interest.

    (3)

    Average minimal distance in the text between the combinations terms and the synonyms for proteins A and B. The smaller the distance is, the more probable the combination term and the protein names are associated.

    (4)

    A relevance score based on the number of occurrences for each type of term.

    Moreover, in order to remove duplicated publications from Medline and Embase as well as very similar patents, grants, and congress abstracts submitted across multiple associations or conferences, the Pipeline Pilot text analytics module was used. The abstract and title of each document were represented with a normalized term frequency–inverse document frequency vector (UnitTF-IDF).(61) For each word present in the document collection, this vector measures the importance of this word for a specific document relative to its frequency within the collection. The similarity between documents is then calculated as the inner product of these UnitTF-IDF vectors. All documents with a similarity closer than a threshold were clustered together and considered as a single document. An empirical threshold of 0.85 was chosen based on previous experience with this algorithm.
    The resulting document sets are finally assembled in a single summary as a heat map. In this heat map, the results can be filtered according to all calculated properties described above in addition to data source, publication year, or author. Finally, a Web interface is directly accessible in order to allow the user to interrogate the data in more depth and provide access to the full text documents.
    The second BW consists of a pathway analysis integrated to a literature search about the targets mechanisms of actions. GeneGO Metacore, IPA, and literature sources such as Medline are mined and integrated to manually construct internal multiscale networks. The logical linkage between different biological levels is manually investigated (intracellular and macrocellular levels, pathophysiology level, disease level). GeneGO and IPA provide information about the pathways on which the targets have an impact. Complementary annotations in those data sources also provide information about the intracellular and macrocellular impacts of the modulation of the targets or of their pathways in different tissues and species. Linkages between targets and diseases are also accessible. Finally, the logical linkage between the macrocellular levels and the diseases via a pathophysiological level is more challenging to identify and often need manual intensive search of the literature using Medline.

        Thompson, Julie D.; Higgins, Desmond G.; Gibson, Toby J.
        Nucleic Acids Research (1994), 22 (22), 4673-80CODEN: NARHAD; ISSN:0305-1048. (Oxford University Press)
        The sensitivity of the commonly used progressive multiple sequence alignment method has been greatly improved for the alignment of divergent protein sequences. Firstly, individual wts. are assigned to each sequence in a partial alignment in order to down-wt. near-duplicate sequences and up-wt. the most divergent ones. Secondly, amino acid substitution matrixes are varied at different alignment stages according to the divergence of the sequences to be aligned. Thirdly, residue-specific gap penalties and locally reduced gap penalties in hydrophilic regions encourage new gaps in potential loop regions rather than regular secondary structure. Fourthly, positions in early alignments where gaps have been opened receive locally reduced gap penalties to encourage the opening of new gaps at these positions. These modifications are incorporated into a new program, CLUSTAL W which is freely available.
