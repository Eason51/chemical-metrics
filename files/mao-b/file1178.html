    Abstract

    Herein we detail the discovery of a series of parthenolide dimers as activators of PKM2 and evaluation of their anti-GBM activities. The most promising compound 5 showed high potency to activate PKM2 with an AC50 value of 15 nM, inhibited proliferation and metastasis, and induced apoptosis of GBM cells. Compound 5 could promote tetramer formation of PKM2 and reduce nucleus translocation of PKM2 in GBM cells without influence on the expression of total PKM2, thereby inhibiting the STAT3 signal pathway in vitro and in vivo. PKM2 knockdown assay demonstrated that the anti-GBM effect of 5 mainly depended on the expression of PKM2 in vitro and in vivo. Compound 16, a prodrug of 5, markedly suppressed U118 tumor xenograft growth and reduced the weight of tumor. On the basis of these investigations, we propose that 16 might be considered as a promising lead compound for discovery of anti-GBM drugs.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is an aggressive, fast-growing brain tumor and is the most common and most malignant type of primary brain tumor.(1â4) The average survival of patients with GBM is less than 12â15 months after diagnosis.(5,6) The widely accepted therapy for GBM is surgical resection, followed by radiotherapy and chemotherapy using alkylating agents.(7) Currently, temozolomide (approved 2005) and carmustine (approved 1996) are the only chemotherapeutics that are FDA approved for the treatment of newly diagnosed GBM.(8) However, the prognosis of patients remains poor, and the median survival rate is far from satisfactory.(9) Therefore, discovery of therapeutic agents with unique modes of actions is urgently needed to overcome current challenges in GBM treatment.
    Targeting cancer metabolism is an emerging avenue in the development of new tumor treatment.(10â13) Many cancer cells show up-regulated glucose uptake and lactate production, even under the condition of sufficient oxygen. The way to access energy is known as aerobic glycolysis or the Warburg effect.(14,15) Pyruvate kinase catalyzes the final rate-limiting step of glycolysis, converting the phosphate from phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) to ADP to produce pyruvate and ATP.(16) Pyruvate kinase M2 (PKM2), an isoform of pyruvate kinase, has emerged as a critical regulator in cancer cell metabolism.(17â21) PKM2 is predominantly expressed in tumor cells,(22) including glioma,(23,24) with elevated anabolic demands. PKM2 has a dynamic equilibrium between a dimeric state and a tetrameric state with low and high levels of pyruvate kinase activity, respectively. Thus, PKM2 acts as a key metabolic regulator for cancer cells in maintaining a balance of energy (tetramer) and biomolecule precursor requirements (dimer).(25â27) Dimeric PKM2 mainly exists in nucleus of cancer cells, where it plays a key role in tumor cell proliferation, invasion, and metastasis.(20,28) Activation of PKM2 can induce tetramerization of PKM2 and lead to a decrease in glycolytic intermediates that can be utilized as biosynthetic precursors and prevent nucleus translocation of dimeric PKM2. Therefore, activating PKM2 is supposed to be an effective strategy for treatment of cancer.(29)
    Recently, we screened an in-house collection of natural products for potential PKM2 activator, parthenolide (1, PTL, Figure 1) was identified as a moderate activator of PKM2. In view of its structure that is different from those of reported PKM2 activator, we were interested to synthesize its derivatives for discovery of more potent PKM2 activator. Thus, with compound 1 as the starting point, we designed and synthesized a series of parthenolide dimers and investigated their potency to activate PKM2, their mechanism, and their anti-GBM activity in vitro and in vivo.

    Chemistry

    PTL, a naturally occurring germacrane sesquiterpene lactone derived from feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium) in a yield of only 0.14â0.74%,(30) showed extensive biological activities.(31â34) Recently, we identified that PTL could be readily isolated from root bark of Magnolia delavayi without chromatography in a yield of approximately 5% (kilogram scale).(35) PKM2 has a dimer or tetramer state; accordingly, we speculated that PTL dimers would promote their binding to PKM2 and show higher activating activity. Previously, Crooks et al. demonstrated that dimers of melampomagnolide B showed elevated anticancer activity.(36) Structureâactivity relationship studies of PTL have indicated that derivatives modified on the 14-methyl group always maintained anticancer activity.(32,37) Accordingly, as shown in Scheme 1, we designed and synthesized a series of PTL dimers with different linkers. Oxidation of PTL with SeO2/t-BuOOH yielded melampomagnolide B (MMB, 2).(32,37) Treatment of 2 with DessâMartin periodinane in the presence of NaHCO3 followed by further oxidation of aldehyde 3 with NaClO2 produced acid 4.(38) Coupling of acid 4 with different lengths of polyethylene glycols afforded dimers 5â15. Compound 5 showed high anti-GBM activity in vitro, which prompted us to evaluate its anti-GBM activity in vivo. However, compound 5 showed low water solubility. We designed compound 16 as its prodrug.(39â41) Reaction of compound 5 with dimethylamine, followed by acidification with fumaric acid, provided compound 16.

    Parthenoide Dimers Activated the Pyruvate Kinase Activity of PKM2

    To identify the potent activator targeting PKM2, a percent activation assay was performed as a preliminary screening. In this assay, the activity of the rPKM2 was compared in the absence or presence of synthesized dimers 5â15, and PKM2 activator TEPP-46 was used as a positive control. PTL was also included for comparison. Eleven compounds showed >2-fold activation compared to PKM2 enzyme alone at a concentration of 10 Î¼M (Figure 2). Compounds 5, 6, and 10 were selected to further analyze their effects on PKM2 activity. We measured the pyruvate kinase activity of rPKM2 proteins in the presence of 5, 6, and 10 at a series of different concentrations. The results demonstrated that 5, 6, and 10 effectively promoted the pyruvate kinase activity of rPKM2 with a half-maximum activating concentration (AC50) value at 15 nM, 52 nM, and 65 nM, respectively (Figure 2). The most potent compound 5 was better than TEPP-46 (AC50 = 92 nM).(42)

    Compound 5 Inhibited Proliferation and Metastasis and Induced Apoptosis of GBM Cells

    PKM2 acts as a protein kinase and regulates the expression of cell proliferation, apoptosis, and metastasis-related genes. As potential PKM2 activators, parthenolide dimers 5â15 probably affect cell proliferation. To investigate the antiproliferation activity of these compounds, MTT assay was performed with PTL(43) and TEPP-46 for comparison and with ADR as a positive control. The antiproliferation of 5â15 against six brain tumor cell types, U87, U118, SF126, SHG44, U251, and C6, and a normal immortal line of fibroblast-like cells, 3T3, was evaluated. As shown in Figure 3 ant Table 1, compound 5 showed the most potent cytotoxicity against U87, U118, SF126, SHG44, U251, and C6 glioma cell lines with IC50 values of 3.03 Â± 0.54 Î¼M, 1.80 Â± 0.28 Î¼M, 7.49 Â± 0.64 Î¼M, 5.24 Â± 3.49 Î¼M, 7.14 Â± 0.08 Î¼M, and 1.66 Â± 0.01 Î¼M, respectively, which are more potent than that of the parent compound PTL, respectively. Compound 5 showed low toxicity against the normal immortal line of fibroblast-like 3T3 cells compared with its highly potent anti-GBM activity. Moreover, we further evaluated other kinds of cancer cell lines including cervical carcinoma (HeLa), liver cancer (HepG2), breast cancer (SUM159), and lung cancer (A549) compared with glioma (U118). As shown in Figure 4, compound 5 showed IC50 values for U118, HeLa, HepG2, SUM159, and A549 at 1.8 Â± 0.28 Î¼M, 5.05 Â± 1.17 Î¼M, 10.2 Â± 0.83 Î¼M, 2.82 Â± 0.39 Î¼M, and 11.9 Â± 0.23 Î¼M, respectively (Figure 4), which indicated that glioblastoma multiforme cells were more sensitive to compound 5 than other cancer cells. Taking account that compound 5 showed the most potent anti-GBM activity and did not significantly affect the viability of normal cells, compound 5 was selected for further investigation of its anti-GBM activity and mechanism of action.

    To further investigate the potential anti-GBM effect of 5, colony formation assay, cell apoptosis assay, and transwell assay were performed. As shown in Figure 5, compoud 5 significantly inhibited the colony formation of U87 and U118 cells and induced apoptosis of U87 and U118 cells in a dose-dependent manner. Moreover, the number of migration and invasion cells was strongly reduced after treatment with 5 for 24 h (Figure 6).

    Parthenolide Dimer 5 Could Directly Bind to PKM2

    To reveal whether 5 could directly bind to PKM2, the molecular docking assay and the thermal stability experiments were performed. As shown in Figure 7, the molecular docking assay proposed that 5 would directly bind to PKM2. The thermal stability experiments further illustrated that 5 increased the thermal stability of PKM2 by an increase in melting temperature at 42 Â°C, 49 Â°C, and 56 Â°C, which demonstrated that 5 could directly bind to PKM2. To further explore the possibility of compound 5 covalently binding to PKM2, we performed LC-MS/MS analysis of PKM2 after treatment with 5. As shown in Figures S1 and S2, we speculated that compound 5 might directly bind to PKM2 through the sulfhydryl group of C424.

    Compound 5 Promoted Tetramer PKM2 and Reduced Nucleus Translocation of Dimer PKM2 in GBM Cells

    To explain the mechanism of 5 in the activation of PKM2, we first analyzed the protein expression after treatment with 5. As shown in Figure 8A, after treatment with 5 at different concentrations, the protein expressions of total PKM2 were not evidently changed in U87 and U118 cells. The translocation of PKM2 from the cytoplasm to the nucleus played important roles for tumor proliferation. The tetramer form of PKM2 in the cytoplasm, which is the glycolytically active form (acts as pyruvate kinase), suppressed tumorigenesis. The dimer of PKM2 regulates gene transcription by acting as a protein kinase in the cancer cell nucleus and correlates with cancer cell proliferation. To determine whether 5 affected the nucleus translocation, PKM2 in the nucleus was analyzed. As shown in Figure 8B and Figure 8C, PKM2 in the nucleus was significantly decreased in U87 and U118 cells after treatment with 5 for 48 h. Tetrameric PKM2 is the active form of PKM2. To evaluate whether 5 promoted the tetramer formation of intracellular PKM2, a chemical cross-linking assay was performed. From the results of Figure 8D and Figure 8E, compound 5 enhanced the tetrameric PKM2 in U87 and U118 cells which was similar to the effect of TEPP-46 (a prominent PKM2 activator(42)). In addition, after the incubation of 5 and PKM2 protein, the size distributions of the particles were analyzed with a particle size analyzer (Figure 8F). The results showed that 5 and TEPP-46 increased the particle size, which indicated the increased formation of tetrameric PKM2. It is worth to note that compound 5 was more effective than TEPP-46 (Figure 8F).

    Parthenolide Dimer 5 Inhibited the STAT3 Signal Pathway

    PTL is a well characterized IKKÎ² and p65 inhibitor. At concentrations of 0.2 Î¼M and 0.5 Î¼M, compound 5 could significantly activate PKM2 (Figure 2) but did not evidently affect the level of IKKÎ² and p65 (Figure 9A). We further analyzed the IKKÎ² phosphorylation and nuclear translocation of p65 after treatment with compound 5 at concentrations of 0.2 Î¼M and 0.5 Î¼M with PTL as positive control. Figure 9B and 9C demonstrated that the dimer 5 did not significantly affect the IKKÎ² phosphorylation and nuclear translocation of p65 at concentrations of 0.2 Î¼M and 0.5 Î¼M where dimer 5 significantly activated PKM2 (Figure 2). These results indicated that dimer 5 did not affect the NF kB pathway at a concentration where dimer 5 significantly activated PKM2.
    Signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) is a member of the STAT family of transcription factors and is activated in several cancers. STAT3 tyrosine phosphorylation has been associated with cancer cell proliferation, apoptosis, and metastasis. It was reported that nuclear PKM2 regulated constitutive activation of STAT3 in colorectal carcinoma cells.(44) To determine whether 5 could inhibit STAT3 pathway by reducing nuclear translocation of PKM2 in GBM cells, the protein expression was detected. As shown in Figure 9D, the STAT3 expression and phosphorylation were clearly inhibited after treatment with 5 in U118 and U87 cells for 24 h. As an important transcription factor in cancer cells, STAT3 regulated the expression of the metastasis inhibition protein E-cadherin, the metastasis-inducing protein vimentin, the pro-apoptotic protein Bax, and the antiapoptotic proteins Bcl-2 and Bcl-xl. As shown in Figure 9D, E-cadherin was strongly increased, while vimentin was significantly decreased in U87 and U118 cells after treatment with 5 for 24 h. In addition, the pro-apoptotic protein Bax was clearly increased, while the antiapoptotic proteins Bcl-2 and Bcl-xl were significantly decreased. These results suggested that compound 5 inhibited GBM cell proliferation, induced cell apoptosis, and inhibited metastasis by inhibiting the STAT3 signal pathway.
    It was reported that PTL could inhibit the STAT3 signaling pathway(45,46) and activate pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase 4 (PDK4).(47) We evaluated and compared the effects of PTL and compound 5 on STAT3 and PDK4. The results in Figure 9E demonstrated that compound 5 could inhibit STAT3 and activate PDK4, and compound 5 showed more potent effects than PTL.

    Anticancer Activity of 5 Was Dependent on the Expression of PKM2

    To verify the influence of PKM2 on the effect of 5, we stably knocked down the expression of PKM2 in U87 and U118 GBM cells by lentiviral (Figure 10A). Then shPKM2 and shNC cells were treated with 5 at different concentrations for 24 h, and the cell viability was assayed. The results showed that the shPKM2 U118 and shPKM2 U87 cells are less sensitive to the inhibitory effect of 5 compared to shNC cells (Figure 10B and Figure 10C), which indicated that the antiproliferation effect of 5 on GBM cells was mainly dependent on the expression PKM2. To further identify the significance of PKM2 for anti-GBM activity of 5, the cell apoptosis assay was performed. The results shown in Figure 10DâF demonstrated that the 5-mediated apoptosis in shPKM2 U118 and shPKM2 U87 cells was clearly reduced compared to the corresponding shNC cells, respectively. Furthermore, the migration and invasion results indicated that the 5-mediated metastasis in the shPKM2 U118 and shPKM2 U87 cells was strongly reduced compared to the corresponding shNC cells, respectively (Figure 11). All these results demonstrated that the anti-GBM effects of 5 inhibiting proliferation and metastasis and inducing apoptosis mainly depended on the expression of PKM2.

    Compound 16, a Prodrug of Parthenolide Dimer 5, Inhibited U118 Tumor Xenograft Growth in Vivo

    Because 5 showed significant anti-GBM activity in vitro, we decided to determine the anti-GBM effect of 5 in vivo. However, compound 5 demonstrated low water solubility, so compound 5 was transformed to its prodrug 16 following our previously reported strategy.(48) Using a mouse xenograft tumor model, we investigated the anti-GBM activity of 16 in vivo. U118 cells (5 Ã 106) were injected into the subcutaneous right forelimb armpit. After the tumors had grown to an average size of about 100 mm3, either vehicle or 16 (50 mg/kg) was administered ip for 3 weeks (twice a week). The results indicated that 16 markedly suppressed tumor growth and reduced the weight of tumor. The body weight in the 16-treated group had no significant difference compared to vehicle group (Figure 12).
    The Western blot of the tumor tissue indicated that E-cadherin and Bax were significantly increased, while STAT3, vimentin, and Bcl-2 were decreased after administration of 16, which were in accordance with the results in vitro. Moreover, the gene expression also showed that E-cadherin was markedly up-regulated, while Bcl-2 and STAT3 were significantly down-regulated after administration of 16.
    To verify that the effect of 16 mainly depended on expression of PKM2 in vivo, we set a siPKM2 administration group with 0.3 pM siRNA was injected by intratumoral injection twice a week. The 16-mediated tumor growth inhibition was strongly reduced after the knockdown of PKM2, which indicated that PKM2 expression was important for the anti-GBM activity of 16. The xenograft tumors were then harvested for immunohistochemical (IHC) staining. The IHC staining implied that 16 administration treatment markedly up-regulated the expression levels of E-cadherin and Bax and down-regulated the expression level of vimentin, P-STAT3, and Bcl-2, while these changes disappeared in the 16-treated group with knockdown of PKM2 (Figure 13).

    Compound 16 Had the Potential To Penetrate the BBB

    The potency to penetrate the bloodâbrain barrier (BBB) is important for anti-GBM drugs. Therefore, we evaluated the potency of compound 16 for penetrating the BBB. Impedance analysis is commonly used to evaluate the integrity of the bloodâbrain barrier by detecting alterations in the transendothelial electric resistance measurements assay (TEER).(49) As shown in Figure 14, exposure of MadinâDarby canine kidney (MDCK II) cells to compound 16 transiently lowered the barrier integrity within 3 h. Temozolomide (TMZ), a clinically used drug for treatment of glioma, continuously lowered barrier integrity within 7 h. It is important to note that the TEER values of MDCK II cells were recovered within 7 h in the compound 16 treatment group. This result suggested that compound 16 transiently lowered the barrier integrity and had the potential to penetrate the BBB.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Targeting cancer-specific metabolism is an attractive strategy for cancer therapy. PKM2 is significantly up-regulated in many tumor cells, including GBM. PKM2 plays important roles in metabolism (tetrameric PKM2) and signals in cancer cells (dimeric PKM2). It was reported that genetic replacement of PKM2 with PKM1 greatly suppressed aerobic glycolysis and tumor growth of lung cancer in vitro and in vivo.(20) Therefore, PKM2 has been considered as an ideal therapeutic target for GBM treatment. PKM2 inhibitors showed anticancer activity because they could ablate the glycolytic phenotype that tumors are reliant upon.(50,51) However, PKM2 knockdown in U87 and U118 GBM cells did not affect the proliferation of cancer cells in vitro (Figure 10 and Figure S3) and the growth of xenograft tumors in vivo (Figure 13). Similarly, knockdown of PKM2 did not suppress the growth of xenograft colon tumors, breast tumors, and liver tumors.(52â54) These results suggested that inhibition of PKM2 is not sufficient to significantly reduce cellular proliferation of tumor cells. PKM2 exists in equilibrium between the dimeric form with low-glycolytic activity and the tetrameric form with high-glycolytic activity. Nucleic PKM2 exists as a dimer, which is essential for cancer proliferation and metastasis.(20,28) PKM2 activators reduce PKM2 translocation into the nucleus and thereby suppress tumorigenesis of nucleic PKM2. Thus, PKM2 activators provide a novel antitumor therapeutic strategy.
    We identified a series of parthenolide dimers as activators of PKM2 and evaluated their anti-GBM activities. The most promising dimer 5 exhibited significant potency to activate PKM2 with an AC50 value of 15 nM (Figure 2), which is more potent than that of the positive control TEPP-46 (AC50 = 92 nM).(42) Compound 5 greatly inhibited proliferation and metastasis and induced apoptosis of U87 and U118 GBM cells (Figures 3â6).
