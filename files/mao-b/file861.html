    Abstract

    The development of acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC) inhibitors for the treatment of metabolic disease has been pursued by the pharmaceutical industry for some time. A number of recent disclosures describing potent ACC inhibitors have been reported by multiple research groups. Unlike many prior publications in this area, more recent publications contain a significant amount of in vivo efficacy data generated by long-term experiments in rodent models of metabolic disease. Additionally, one compound has been advanced to human clinical studies. The results from these studies should allow researchers to better gauge the potential utility of ACC inhibition for the treatment of human disease.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The acetyl-CoA carboxylases (ACCs) are enzymes that are involved in the synthesis and oxidation of fatty acids and have been targeted as potential intervention points for the treatment of metabolic diseases such as type 2 diabetes and dyslipidemia. The ACC protein structure consists of three function domains: the biotin carboxylase domain (BC), the biotin carboxyl carrier protein domain (BCCP), and the carboxyl transferase domain (CT) (Figure 1).(1)
    There are two characterized isoforms of mammalian ACC, both of which catalyze the conversion of acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA.(2, 3) The first isoform, known as ACC1, is located in the cytosol and is primarily expressed in lipid rich tissue (liver, adipose). It is responsible for the rate limiting initiation of de novo fatty acid synthesis. In contrast, the second ACC isoform, ACC2, is imbedded in the mitochondrial membrane and is primarily found in oxidative tissue (muscle).(4, 5) ACC2 serves as a negative regulator of fatty acid uptake by the mitochondria via the inhibition of carnitine palmitoyltransferase I (CPT1), the protein that is responsible for the conjugation of free fatty acyls to carnitine for transfer across the mitochondrial membrane for subsequent Î²-oxidation. Thus, inhibition of the ACCs might be expected to reduce both fatty acid synthesis while simultaneously increasing fatty acid oxidation.

    Structural Considerations

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A number of crystallographic studies from the research group of Tong(6-11) have uncovered the structural basis for inhibition by a diverse array of ligands and have shown that potent ACC inhibition can occur via binding to either the CT or BC domains (Figure 2). The crystal structure of the CT domain of yeast ACC in complex with coenzyme A (CoA, 1) demonstrated that the active site resides at the dimeric interface of the N and C domains (wheat and green, respectively; Figure 3a).(6) Co-crystal structures of the herbicides 2 (haloxyfop) and 3 (diclofop) (orange and yellow, respectively, in Figure 2a) were also found to bind at the dimeric interface, from which the basis of ACC inhibition by these herbicides (partial occlusion of the CoA binding cleft) could be determined.(7) Compound 4 (CP-640186), an ACC1/ACC2 dual inhibitor developed by Pfizer that is noncompetitive with acetyl-CoA, has also been crystallized in the yeast ACC CT domain.(8) The anthracenyl moiety of 4 is sandwiched between Î±6 and Î±6â² helices of the N and C domains of ACC2 near the dimer interface, yet (in contrast to herbicide binding) does not overlap with the region corresponding to CoA binding, consistent with the competitive kinetic results. It was proposed that 4 may occupy the region utilized by biotin, the carboxylated form serving as the carboxyl source in the transformation of acetyl- to malonyl-CoA. Further cocrystal structures published by the Tong group of herbicides 5 (tepraloxydim) and 6 (pinoxaden) demonstrate a greater degree of occlusion of the CoA binding cleft (Figure 3b) compared to haloxyfop and diclofop, yet with less conformational change than that required for the latter herbicides.(9, 10) Finally, the structural basis of ACC inhibition by the potent polyketide natural product soraphen A1Î± (7) has been determined.(11) Genetic, biochemical, and ultimately structural studies have demonstrated that, in stark contrast to that of the binding of the previous described ACC inhibitors, 7 binds to the BC domain of ACC (Figure 4), likely disrupting BC dimerization. The crystallographically demonstrated existence of multiple ligand binding sites, involving more than one domain of the ACCs, underscores the availability of multiple potential avenues for activity inhibition by small molecules.

    In Vivo Pharmacology

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    ACC activity and function have been studied in a number of different mouse knockout models. The first such mouse model reported was an ACC2 knockout (ACC2â/â), described in a series of publications from Wakil and co-workers.(12-15) Compared to wild type (WT) mice, these ACC2â/â mice showed a significant reduction in malonyl-CoA levels in skeletal muscle and heart tissue, along with corresponding increases in fatty acid oxidation in these same tissues. Wakilâs ACC2â/â mice had increased whole body energy expenditure relative to WT mice and showed improved insulin and lipid profiles when placed on a high fat/high calorie diet. Subsequent to Wakilâs work, the groups of Cooney and Lowell reported ACC2â/â mice generated by an alternative methodology to that used by Wakilâs group.(16, 17) Conneyâs ACC2â/â mice exhibited reduced malonyl-CoA levels in heart (60%) and muscle (50%) tissues relative to WT mice, resulting in increased muscle and whole body fatty acid oxidation. Lowellâs ACC2â/â mice showed an even more modest reduction of malonyl-CoA levels in heart (30%) relative to WT mice, with no other significant metabolic changes observed. It is difficult to reconcile the significant differences in the observed phenotypes of these three ACC2â/â mouse strains. One possible explanation is that the cloning methods used by the different laboratories to generate the ACC2â/â mice were not identical. In Wakilâs case, the biotin binding domain of ACC2 was replaced with hypoxanthine phosphorylribosyltransferase (HPRT) expression cassette, whereas Cooney and Lowell ACCâ/â mice utilized LoxP methodology to delete the exon containing the biotin binding domain of ACC2 entirely. Lowell also postulates that Wakilâs ACC2â/â mice may contain mutant, catalytically inactive ACC2 protein that may exert a dominant negative effect on ACC1, resulting in a phenotype that is not entirely ACC2 dependent. Additionally, it is possible that mouse background strain (different for all three ACC2â/â models) may have had a confounding influence over the whole animal metabolic phenotype. Efforts to generate ACC1 knockout mice (ACC1â/â) met with initial failure, as the knockout proved to be embryonically lethal.(18) However, two liver specific ACC1 knockout mouse models (LACC1â/â) have been reported by the groups of Wakil and Kusunoki.(19, 20) Liver samples from the LACC1â/â mice in Wakilâs study showed significantly reduced malonyl-CoA levels and ACC activity relative to WT mice, along with a significant reduction in fatty acid synthesis. On the other hand, liver samples from the LACC1â/â mice generated by Kusunokiâs group showed no differences in malonyl-CoA levels or fatty acid synthesis relative to WT mice. Liver samples from Kusunokiâs LACC1â/â mice did, however, show increased levels of both ACC2 mRNA and protein expression, suggesting that ACC2 was being up-regulated to accommodate the deletion of ACC1. As was the case with the various ACC2â/â mice, there is no obvious explanation for the different phenotypes seen in Wakil and Kusunokiâs LACC1â/â mice, although the same sort of rationalizations used to offer possible explanations for the differences seen in the different ACC2â/â mouse strains could apply here as well. No attempts to produce ACC2â/â/ACC1â/â mice have been reported.
    In addition to mouse knockout studies, there is some human data to suggest that ACC inhibition may provide a therapeutic benefit. Healthy volunteers placed on a 3 month exercise program showed decreases in ACC2 mRNA levels and increases in fatty acid oxidation during the course of the study.(21) Obese and type 2 diabetic patients have been shown to have increased malonyl-CoA levels and ACC2 activity.(22) Thiazolidinedione treatment has been shown to reverse this effect. Additionally, it is thought that the efficacy of antidiabetic agent metformin may be due in part to its activation of adenosine monophosphate-activated protein kinase (AMPK), which serves to inhibit ACC2 activity in vivo.(23)
    Driven largely by the initially promising ACC2â/â mouse studies from Wakilâs lab,(12-15) there has been a significant amount of effort directed toward the development of ACC inhibitors for the treatment of metabolic diseases.(24-27) At the time this topic was last reviewed,(25) there had been a modest amount of long-term efficacy data reported related to the effect of ACC inhibition in improving metabolic parameters.(28) This review aims to cover subsequent relevant literature, including reports of several long-term efficacy studies with newly developed ACC inhibitors.
    Taisho has previously reported a series of ACC1/ACC2 dual inhibitors (Figure 5).(29) The most thoroughly profiled of these was compound 8, which inhibited ACC enzymatic activity [human ACC1 (hACC1) IC50 = 101 nM, human ACC2 (hACC2) IC50 = 23 nM]. Compound 8 also increased fatty acid oxidation (EC50 = 580 nM) and decreased fatty acid synthesis (IC50 = 340 nM) in HepG2 cells. A subsequent paper detailed efforts examining changes to the 2,6-diarylpyridine portion of the 8, focusing on increasing the size of one of the aryl groups.(30) This resulted in the synthesis of compounds such as indazole 9, which inhibited hACC1/2 enzymatic activity with an IC50 of 33 nM. (Note: For the bulk of these studies, Taisho utilized partially purified human hACC enzyme that contained a mixture of hACC1 and hACC2.) Further efforts to replace the 2,6-diarylpyridine functional group led to the discovery of a benzothiophene urea class of ACC inhibitors.(31) In particular, compound 10 inhibited hACC1/2 enzymatic activity with IC50 = 74 nM. It was then found that the bis-piperidine amide portion of 10 could be truncated, resulting in tert-butyl carbamate 11, a compound that showed improved hACC1/2 enzymatic inhibition (IC50 = 24 nM) and inhibited fatty acid synthesis in HepG2 cells (IC50 = 79 nM). Further work to improve the stability of the tert-butyl carbamate group to acidic conditions resulted in the synthesis of compound 12, which was profiled extensively. Compound 12 inhibited human and rat ACC1/2 (rACC1/2, partially purified enzyme containing a mixture of rACC1 and rACC2) enzymatic activity (hACC1 IC50 = 192 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 95 nM, rACC1/2 IC50 = 32 nM). Compound 12 also increased fatty acid oxidation (EC50 = 370 nM) and decreased fatty acid synthesis (IC50 = 60 nM) in HepG2 cells. Treatment of SpragueâDawley (SD) rats with compound 12 (10 mg/kg, po) resulted in a 74.6% decrease in liver fatty acid synthesis 1 h postdose. Encouraged by these results, compound 12 was then dosed for 12 days po (oral), b.i.d. (twice daily), in SD rats placed on a high fructose diet. Animals dosed with 12 had significantly reduced plasma and liver triglyceride levels relative to the control group, with a minimum effective dose of 3â10 mg/kg compound 12. In addition to the observed triglyceride effects, animals dosed with compound 12 showed significant skin irritation around the nose and toes. As lipids are an essential component of normal skin, the authors stated that they could not rule out the possibility that the skin irritation was a mechanism based effect of ACC inhibition of de novo fatty acid synthesis.
    Sanofi-Aventis has reported the development of a series of ACC1/ACC2 dual inhibitors (Figure 6).(32) Starting from 1,3-dioxane 13, a high throughput screening hit that was shown to inhibit hACC2 (IC50 = 630 nM) but was inactive on hACC1, rat ACC2 (rACC2), and rat ACC1 (rACC1), medicinal chemistry efforts led to the synthesis of compound 14, which showed enzymatic inhibition of all ACC isoforms tested (hACC2 IC50 = 30 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 190 nM, rACC2 IC50 = 400 nM, rACC1 IC50 = 170 nM). Compound 14 caused a dose dependent increase in fatty acid oxidation in human hepatocytes and possessed suitable in vivo pharmacokinetic (PK) properties for further profiling (plasma PK studies in Wistar rats: iv (3 mg/kg) CL = 1.6 L hâ1 kgâ1, Vss = 2.6 L/kg; po (10 mg/kg) CMax = 3.5 Î¼mol/L, TMax = 2 h, AUC = 19 Î¼molÂ·h/L, t1/2 = 1.8). Female Wistar rats dosed with 50 mg/kg compound 14 followed by the administration of triglycerides experienced a greater reduction in respiratory quotient (RQ) during the light phase, indicating that compound 14 was simulating fatty acid oxidation in vivo. Additionally, when obese female Zucker diabetic fatty (ZDF) rats were dosed orally with 30 mg/kg compound 7 for 3 days q.d. (once daily), a decrease in triglyceride levels was observed in the treated animals relative to the control group.
    A subsequent publication examined the effect of treatment with compound 14 over a longer dosing period.(33) Two sets of experiments were run using diet induced obese (DIO) female C57BL6 mice treated with compound 14. In the first study, the DIO mice were prepared by treatment with a high-fat diet for 24 weeks. Compound 14 was then dosed for 29 days q.d. while maintaining the high-fat diet. Animals treated with 14 showed no change in body weight or food consumption relative to the control group on a high-fat diet. Hepatic malonyl-CoA levels were decreased, and total ketone bodies, a marker of increased fatty acid oxidation, were increased in the mice treated with 14, indicating that there was an increase in fatty acid oxidation corresponding to malonyl-CoA reduction. Nonesterified fatty acid levels were also decreased in mice treated with 14 relative to the control group, but there were no changes observed in plasma triglycerides, hepatic triglycerides, or glucose tolerance observed between mice treated with compound 14 and the control group. The second study was designed to determine if treatment with compound 14 could prevent the development of metabolic changes associated with a high-fat diet. To this end, C57BL6 mice were fed a high-fat diet over a 6-week period with or without administration of compound 14. A control group of mice fed standard chow was also included in this study. Mice placed on the high-fat diet showed significant increases in body weight and body fat, as well as increased hepatic triglycerides, blood glucose, and cholesterol levels relative to mice on standard chow. The high-fat mice also showed impaired glucose tolerance. Mice on the high-fat diet that were treated with compound 14 showed increase plasma ketone bodies and increased liver weight relative to high-fat mice that were not administered 14; however, there were no differences observed in body weight gain, hepatic triglyceride accumulation, or glucose tolerance.
    In addition to the studies in DIO mice, an 8-day study in female ZDF rats dosed with compound 14 was reported. As seen in the previous study,(31) a decrease in hepatic triglyceride levels was observed at day 3 in animals treated with compound 14 relative to the control group. However, at day 8 of the study, no such differentiation was observed, suggesting that the triglyceride lowering was a transient effect. There was an increased serum level of ketone bodies and decreased serum level of nonesterified fatty acids in the compound 14 treated group on both day 3 and day 8, suggesting that fatty acid oxidation was elevated for the entire dosing period. The paper concludes that despite the ability of compound 14 to lower malonyl-CoA levels in rodent models of metabolic disease, there was no evidence of any improvement observed in hepatic lipid levels, glucose tolerance, or body weight relative to control animals in these studies.
    AstraZeneca has reported the development of a series of ACC1/ACC2 dual inhibitors that was initiated by the identification of screening hits 15 and 16 (15, hACC2 IC50 = 2.5 Î¼M; 16, hACC2 IC50 = 3.6 Î¼M) (Figure 7).(34) Examination of the structures of 15 and 16 suggested that these compounds might share an overlapping pharmacophore and that hybrid analogs combining features from both compounds might show increased ACC inhibitory activity. This resulted in the synthesis of compounds 17 and 18, which did in fact show improved enzyme inhibition (17, hACC2 IC50 = 609 nM; 18, hACC2 IC50 = 210 nM). Compound 18 was further profiled to see if it might be of use as an in vivo tool. Unfortunately, compound 18 demonstrated very high human plasma protein binding (fraction unbound of <0.1%) and was rapidly cleared in an iv rat PK study (CL = 60 mL minâ1 kgâ1). As compound 18 was highly lipophilic (logÂ D = 5.9), the team focused on optimizing ligand lipophilicity efficiency (LLE, calculated as pIC50 â logÂ D) to track new analogs.(35) Compound 18 possessed a more promising LLE than compound 17 (LLE = 0.5 vs LLE = â0.8), and thus subsequent work focused on analogs of compound 18. A key analog identified using this process was piperazine 19 (hACC2 IC50 = 1.3 Î¼M, hACC1 IC50 = 13 Î¼M; LLE = 3.0) which, despite demonstrating a reduced ACC enzymatic potency relative to 18, showed a significant improvement in LLE. Further derivatization of 19 resulted in the synthesis of piperidines 20 and 21, both of which showed improved ACC enzymatic inhibition relative to 19 while maintaining good LLE values (20, hACC2 IC50 = 110 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 1.2 Î¼M, LLE = 3.3; 21, hACC2 IC50 = 99 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 1.0 Î¼M, LLE = 3.6). Compounds 20 and 21 had significantly decreased protein binding (fraction unbound of 3.4 and 3.3, respectively) relative to 18, although rat iv clearance was still fairly high for both compounds (CL of 32 and 70 mL minâ1 kgâ1, respectively). Compounds 20 and 21 showed cross-species reactivity in rat ACC enzyme assays (20, rACC2 IC50 = 190, rACC1 IC50 = 460 nM; 21, rACC2 IC50 = 150, rACC1 IC50 = 250 nM) and were dosed in obese ZDF rats via iv infusion. There was an approximately 50% reduction in hepatic malonyl-CoA levels, observed in the rats treated with compounds 20 and 21 relative to the control group, corresponding to unbound plasma concentrations 2- to 3-fold above the in vitro IC50 for rat ACC2 inhibition. No other in vivo parameters were measured in this study.
    Takedaâs work to develop ACC inhibitors was initiated by the examination of a known inhibitor 4 published by Pfizer (4, hACC2 IC50 = 34 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 550 nM) (Figure 8).(36, 37) Compound 4 has been shown crystalographically to bind to the CT domain of ACC (Figure 3).(8) Modeling of 4 with hACC2 suggests that the two amide carbonyl oxygens make key interactions with ACC, the morpholine amide with Gly2162 and the anthracene amide with Glu2230.(38) The Takeda group hoped to improve upon compound 4 in two ways. First, by adjusting the orientation of the morpholine amide, they hoped to improve the efficiency of the hydrogen bond between the amide oxygen and Gly2162. Second, as the carboxylate of Glu2230 is in proximity to the anthracene ring system, replacement of the anthracene with a functional group that could also form an additional hydrogen bond to the Glu2230 carboxylate was expected to improve the affinity for ACC. To address the first goal, a series of bicyclic and spirocyclic morpholine amide replacements were synthesized, the most potent of which was spiroester 22 (hACC2 IC50 = 43 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 330 nM). Holding the spiroester constant, replacements for the anthracene ring system were then examined. Molecular modeling suggested that a 2-acylaminobenzothiophene or a 2-ureidobenzothiophene was an optimal replacement. Of the analogs prepared, urea 23 was the most potent enzyme inhibitor (hACC2 IC50 = 5.4 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 32 nM). Compound 24, the enantiomer of 23, had significantly reduced ACC activity (hACC2 IC50 = 1.6 Î¼M, hACC1 IC50 â¥ 10 Î¼M). X-ray analysis of compound 23 with hACC showed that, as planned, the two urea NH groups formed a tight hydrogen bond with the carboxylate of Glu2230.
    In a subsequent publication, Takeda disclosed that compound 23 suffered from high human liver microsomal clearance (HLM) (Clint = 230 mL minâ1 mgâ1), in addition to high lipophilicity (logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 3.01).(39) To address these concerns, efforts to modify the spirolactone and benzothiophene groups designed to reduce the logÂ D were then undertaken (Figure 9). It was found that the spirolactone could be replaced with a number of less lipophilic carbonyl containing rings, such as in lactam 25, without a significant loss in ACC enzymatic potency (hACC2 IC50 = 18 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 120 nM). The reduced lipophilicty correlated with an improvement in HLM stability (logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 2.61; HLM Clint = 66 mL minâ1 mgâ1). Similar improvements were realized by replacing the benzothiophene ring with a thieno[2,3-b]pyridine ring, resulting in compound 26 (hACC2 IC50 = 14 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 78 nM, logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 2.49; HLM Clint =79 mL minâ1 mgâ1). Combining changes to the benzothiophene and spirolactone rings resulted in compounds such as 27, which showed further improvement in HLM stability while retaining ACC potency (hACC2 IC50 = 8.5 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 160 nM, logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 2.51; HLM Clint = 33 mL minâ1 mgâ1). In vivo PK studies in male CD(SD)(IGS) rats showed 27 to possess a favorable profile (iv (1 mg/kg) CL = 0.95 L hâ1 kgâ1, Vss = 1.3 L/kg, MRT = 1.3 h; po (3 mg/kg) CMax = 440 mg/mL, TMax = 0.5 h, AUC = 1400 ngÂ·h/mL, F = 45%).
    Further work by Takeda focused on replacing the chiral spiro structure with a symmetrical group (Figure 10).(40) It was found that the spirolactone of 23 could be replaced with a spiropyrazolidinedione, resulting in compounds such as 28, which retained ACC inhibitory activity while demonstrating lower logÂ D and improved HLM values relative to 23 (hACC2 IC50 = 20 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 120 nM, logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 2.03; HLM Clint = 12 mL minâ1 mgâ1). Cyclization of the dialkylimidazolone was also found to be tolerated, with a four-carbon linker as shown in compound 29 possessing the optimal balance between ACC potency and microsomal stability (hACC2 IC50 = 23 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 130 nM, logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 2.45; HLM Clint = 18 mL minâ1 mgâ1). In an effort to further improve the potency of this series, the crystal structure of hACC2 was closely examined to identify additional hydrophobic interactions that might be capitalized on. It appeared that there was a small pocket near the 7-position of the benzothiophene ring formed by Glu2236, Lys1967, and Ala1964. The insertion of a methoxy group at the 7 position of the benzothiophene resulted in compound 30, which is the most potent compound reported by Takeda to date (hACC2 IC50 = 4.9 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 21 nM, logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 2.33; HLM Clint = 35mL minâ1 mgâ1). Compound 30 was advanced to in vivo PK studies in male CD(SD)(IGS) rats (iv (1 mg/kg) CL = 0.78 L hâ1 kgâ1, Vss = 0.97 L/kg, MRT = 1.3 h; po (3 mg/kg) CMax = 71 ng/mL, TMax = 0.25 h, AUC = 180 ngÂ·h/mL, F = 14%) and was found to be a potent inhibitor of rat ACC (rACC2 IC50 = 28 nM, rACC1 IC50 = 13 nM). Compound 30 was then dosed orally Wistar fatty rats, where it was found to cause a dose dependent decrease in RQ values, consistent with increased fatty acid oxidation.
    Pfizer has had a long-standing interest in the development of ACC inhibitors.(37, 41) A recent disclosure discussed efforts to improve the properties of a previously described series of spirochromanone ACC inhibitors (Figure 11) shown crystallographically to bind to the CT domain of ACC.(42) Compound 31 is a potent inhibitor of ACC (rACC1 IC50 = 25 nM) but suffered from relatively high microsomal turnover (CLint = 86 mL minâ1 kgâ1). It was postulated that the lipophilicity of 31 might be responsible for the high microsomal turnover (logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 3.8, LLE = 3.8). When the LLE value of 31 was compared to those of other compounds that had been prepared in the series, it was found that compound 32, while less active in the ACC biochemical assay than 31, had a more favorable LLE value (rACC1 IC50 = 370 nM, logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 1.1, LLE = 5.4). Follow-up work on 32 led to the synthesis of compound 33, which had greatly improved inhibitory activity in ACC enzymatic assays, as well as low microsomal turnover and high LLE (rACC1 IC50 = 17 nM, hACC2 IC50 = 11 nM, logÂ D (pH 7.4) = 1.8, LLE = 6.0, Clint â¤ 8 mL minâ1 kgâ1). Compound 33 was administered to SD rats in a pharmacodynamic (PD) study and was found to lower malonyl-CoA levels in both liver (ACC1 rich) and quadriceps (ACC2 rich) tissue, suggesting a robust PK/PD relationship. There was an apparent maximum inhibition level of 78% in quadriceps and 75% in liver at a 50 pg/kg dose. On the basis of these data, the apparent ED50 was then calculated to be 1.6 mg/kg in liver and 2.6 mg/kg in quadriceps.
    Compound 33 was advanced to preclinical development where the ketone of 33 was found to undergo a species-specific reduction to the corresponding alcohol.(43) Historic SAR studies on the spirochromanone core were examined, and the spirocyclic salicylamide derivative 34 was identified as a possible alternative scaffold that should be more stable to reduction (Figure 12). Compound 34 demonstrated only modest potency in ACC enzymatic assays and a nonoptimal LLE (hACC2 IC50 = 2.5 Î¼M, hACC1 IC50 = 2.5 Î¼M, LLE = 3.4). As the tert-butylpyrazole in 33 had been found to boost enzymatic potency compared to phenyl rings, attempts were made to replace the methylphenyl group of 34 with a tert-butylpyrazole. These attempts were unsuccessful because of synthetic chemistry challenges. However, the corresponding spirolactam 35 was prepared and demonstrated significantly improved ACC inhibitory activity and LLE (hACC2 IC50 = 76 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 118 nM, LLE = 6.4) relative to compound 34. It was then found that replacement of the indazole with a 2-methoxyquinoline (36) led to a further improvement in potency (hACC2 IC50 = 4 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 10 nM, LLE = 6.5). Compound 36 was advanced to in vivo PK studies in rats and was determined to have reasonable properties for further evaluation (iv (1 mg/kg) CL = 1.7 L hâ1 kgâ1, Vss = 1.7 L/kg; po (5 mg/kg) CMax = 403 ng/mL, AUC = 2070 ngÂ·h/mL, F = 71%). Compound 36 was then dosed orally to rats and was found to lower de novo lipogenesis in rats with an ED50 of 1.27 mg/kg.
    To the best of our knowledge, Pfizer is the only company that has initiated clinical studies with an ACC inhibitor.(44, 45) Their clinical candidate, 37 (PF-05175157, structure not disclosed), has been studied in nine phase 1 clinical trials. While the structure has not been disclosed, the lack of additional patent activity suggests that it comes from the one of their spirocyclic series. These trials have been conducted in both healthy volunteers and patients with type 2 diabetes and/or obesity. Initial trials were focused on safety and tolerability, and later trials also included monitoring of lipid and carbohydrate parameters in blood. Combination studies of compound 37 coadministered with the HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor simvastatin(46) and the benzodiazepine medazolam(47) (typically used as an indicator for cytochrome p450 3A4 inhibitors or inducers) were also performed. Four phase 2 clinical studies with compound 37 were planned. The first study was a 6-week treatment conducted in type 2 diabetes patients not currently taking any medication for their condition. An unspecified adverse event occurred, and the trial was discontinued, as were two additional planned trials in type 2 diabetic patients. Additionally, a study planning to examine treatment of patients with acne vulgaris with compound 37 was withdrawn prior to initiation. As far as we can determine, this is the first time it was has been suggested that ACC inhibition might potentially be used to treat acne vulgaris. The current status of Pfizerâs ACC program is unknown.
    Pfizer has also published several papers detailing the synthetic methodology developed for the chromanone and spirolactam series.(48-50)
    Boehringer Ingelheim has published several patent applications describing a series of ACC inhibitors.(51-55) The only biological data included in these patent applications are hACC2 enzymatic inhibition. Some of the most potent compounds reported are shown (Figure 13, 38 hACC2 IC50 = 60 nM, 39 hACC2 IC50 = 80 nM, 40 hACC2 IC50 = 50 nM, 41 hACC2 IC50 = 180 nM).
    Haselkorn and co-workers have described a screening assay designed to detect ACC inhibitors by using a yeast gene replacement strategy.(56) This screen resulted in the identification of compounds 42 and 43 (Figure 14), which possessed differential levels of ACC inhibitory activity (42, hACC2 IC50 = 2.8 Î¼M, hACC1 IC50 not determined; 43, hACC2 IC50 = 22 Î¼M, hACC1 IC50 = 23 Î¼M). No additional work with these compounds has been reported.
    The natural product soraphen A1Î± (7) has been reported to be an inhibitor of hACC1 and hACC2 in the 1â5 nM range (Figure 15).(57) Compound 7 has been shown to bind to the BC domain of ACC (Figure 4),(11) and the treatment of rodents placed on a high fat diet with 7 has been shown to lead to an improved insulin phenotype.(28) Micalizio and co-workers examined the crystal structure of soraphen A1Î± with ACC and postulated that the C11 methoxy group was not involved in a critical interaction with the protein and thus that the C11 des-methoxy analog 43 would better lend itself to efficient synthesis than soraphen A1Î±.(58) In practice, the total synthesis of 44 was completed in 14 steps (longest linear sequence) from commercial starting material. This was a significant improvement over the 25â40 step syntheses that have been reported for compound 7 itself.(59-61) Compound 44 was found to decrease fatty acid synthesis in SCR1163 and SCR1693 cells, as well as inhibit proliferation in SCR1163 cells with approximately 10Ã less potency that soraphen A1Î±.
    Nimbus has published a patent application containing series of thienopyrimidine inhibitors that bind to the soraphen A1Î± binding site in the BC domain of ACC.(62) In vitro and in vivo data for a subset compounds are included (Figure 16). Doseâresponse curves for compounds 45 (IC50 values not included, estimated to be 1â10 nM for hACC1 and hACC2) and 46 (IC50 = 2.6 nM hACC2, IC50= 1.7 nM hACC1) in ACC biochemical assays are reported. Compound 45 and related compounds were shown to cause a thermal shift upon binding with the BC domain of ACC, similar to the effect observed by the binding of soraphen A1Î±.(11) Compounds 45 and 46 both inhibit fatty acid synthesis in HepG2 cells with IC50 values of <100 nM. Cellular proliferation in LNCaP (prostate cancer) cells was inhibited by treatment with compound 45, and the effect of treatment with compound 47 on cell death in HepG2 and HepB3 (liver cancer) cells was also examined. Compound 46 was further profiled in a series of in vivo experiments, where it was shown to reduce fatty acid synthesis (ED50 < 0.3 mg/kg) and RQ in SpragueâDawley rats. In a 14-day study, DIO rats were treated with compound 46 at 3, 10, and 30 mg/kg po. Animals treated with 30 mg/kg of 46 showed a 5% reduction in body weight gain relative to the control group over the course of the study. Additionally, animals treated with 46 showed lower insulin, liver triglycerides, and plasma cholesterol levels relative to the control group, with no observed changes in alanine transaminase (ALT), aspartate transaminase (AST), or liver weight.
    In a subsequent poster presentation, Nimbus disclosed a significant amount of in vivo efficacy data for 48 (ND-630, structure not disclosed), their current lead ACC inhibitor.(63) Compound 48 demonstrated potent biochemical ACC inhibition (hACC1 and hACC2 IC50 = 2 nM) and was also shown to inhibit fatty acid synthesis in HepG2 cells (EC50 = 66 nM). Rats treated with 48 showed reductions in liver malonyl-CoA (ED50 = 1.5 mg/kg), reduced liver fatty acid synthesis (ED50 = 0.14 mg/kg), and reduced RQ. Compound 48 was then administered to DIO (high sucrose) rats treated to assess long-term efficacy. After 28 days of dosing, the rats treated with 48 showed reduced body fat and improved oral glucose tolerance relative to control rats. Additionally, the 48 treated group demonstrated reduced hepatic triglyceride and cholesterol levels as well as reduced plasma triglycerides, free fatty acids, and cholesterol after 28 days of dosing when compared to control. Thus, 48 is the first ACC inhibitor that has been shown to exhibit both a desired pharmacodymic effect (malonyl-CoA lowering) and efficacy in improving metabolic parameters in a rodent disease model.
    In addition to their work examining ACC inhibition for the treatment of metabolic disease, Nimbus has also recently presented two posters focusing on undisclosed ACC inhibitors that also inhibit proliferation of hepatocellular carcinoma and non-small-cell lung cancer cells.(64, 65)
    Amgen has published a series of potent piperazine oxadiazole ACC inhibitors (Figure 17).(66) Chemistry efforts began from screening hit 49, a biochemical inhibitor of ACC activity (hACC2 IC50 = 841 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 4280 nM) that was determined to be competitive with acetyl-CoA. It was found that phenylmethyl analog 50 offered an improvement in enzymatic potency (hACC2 IC50 = 126 nM, hACC1 IC50= 1460 nM) over compound 49. Attempts to reduce the aromaticity of 50 with an eye toward improving druglikeness(67) resulted in the replacement of the central phenyl ring with a number of substituted piperazines which, along with the truncation of the arylpropoxy group to an ethoxy group, led to compound 51, which was selected for further evaluation. Compound 51 showed an increased level of enzymatic selectivity for ACC2 vs ACC1 (hACC2 IC50 = 37 nM, hACC1 IC50 = 4570 nM); however, it suffered from relatively poor in vivo PK properties when dosed in SD rats (iv (2 mg/kg) CL = 2.6 L hâ1 kgâ1, Vss = 13 L/kg; po (5 mg/kg) CMax = 380 nM, AUC = 291 ngÂ·h/mL, F = 17%). The Amgen team then performed an analysis of the predicted sites of metabolism of compound 51 and synthesized analogs that modified these sites in an attempt to reduce metabolism. This work led to a series of oxadiazole amides exemplified by compound 52, an inhibitor of both ACC isoforms (hACC2 IC50 = 54 nM, hACC1 IC50= 193 nM) with good in vivo PK properties in rat and mouse models (SD rats, iv (2 mg/kg) CL = 0.68 L hâ1 kgâ1, Vss = 1.8 L/kg, po (5 mg/kg) CMax = 1200 nM, AUC = 2485 ngÂ·h/mL, F = 34%; C57BL6 mice, (2 mg/mL) CL = 0.21 L hâ1 kgâ1, Vss = 0.98 L/kg, po (5 mg/kg) CMax = 5800 nM, AUC = 8880 ngÂ·h/kg, F = 46%).     C57BL6 mice were dosed with 25, 50, and 100 mg/kg compound 52, and significant reduction of malonyl-CoA (70â85%) in both liver (ACC1 rich) and heart (ACC2 rich) tissue was observed in all three dosing groups. A time course PD study with compound 52 dosed at 25 mg/kg showed a â¼50% reduction in malonyl-CoA levels relative to control in both heart and liver tissue 12 h postdose. Given this evidence of target engagement, compound 52 was advanced to a 31-day efficacy study in DIO C57BL6 mice. The study included three dosing groups at 15, 35, and 75 mg/kg; however, because of severe weight loss, the 75 mg/kg group had to be dropped from the study at day 10. After 27 days of dosing, the dosing group receiving 35 mg/kg compound 52 showed a small but significant decrease in body weight gain relative to the control group. Surprisingly, however, both the 10 and 35 mg/kg dosing groups showed an increase in plasma glucose levels relative to control at day 21 and when subjected to an oral glucose tolerance test, the 35 mg/kg dosing group showed a worsening of glucose tolerance relative to control. Terminal PD conducted 25 h after the last dose of 52 in the 35 mg/kg dosing group showed malonyl-CoA reductions of 42% in liver and 67% in heart, with the unbound plasma concentration of 52 at 618 nM. A similar effect was seen at the 8 h time point from the time-course PD experiment (44% malonyl-CoA reduction in liver and 68% malonyl-CoA reduction in heart, unbound plasma concentration of 52 of 882 nM), implying a relatively consistent PK/PD relationship between drug concentration and malonyl-CoA lowering over the course of the study. There were no significant differences in insulin, triglyceride, free fatty acid, ALT, or AST levels in either the 10 or 35 mg/kg dosing group relative to the control group.

    Initial interest in ACC as a target for metabolic disease stemmed largely from work done by Wakil and co-workers describing ACC2â/â mice with a beneficial metabolic phenotype.(12-15) Two subsequent ACC2â/â mouse knockout models have been published that show a less beneficial metabolic phenotype.(16, 17) A number of publications describing potent ACC inhibitors have appeared in the recent literature, many of which include in vivo efficacy data. AstraZeneca and Pfizer report ACC inhibitors that reduce malonyl-CoA levels in vivo, suggesting target engagement, but long-term animal studies to examine the effect of compound administration on typical parameters (triglyceride levels, glucose tolerance, etc.) that are generally associated with improved metabolic function have not been reported. That being said, Pfizer advanced PF-05175157 to clinical studies, and whether or not Pfizerâs preclinical in vivo studies were entirely focused on monitoring of malonyl-CoA lowering is not known. Conversely, Taisho and Takeda have reported ACC inhibitors that have shown varying degrees of improvements in metabolic phenotypes in rodents, yet have not been demonstrated to lower malonyl-CoA levels in vivo, thus offering no direct evidence of target engagement. Sanofi-Aventis, Amgen, and Nimbus have reported ACC inhibitors that lower malonyl-CoA levels in vivo and have also been examined in long-term efficacy studies. The results in these studies are mixed. In the case of Sanofi-Aventis, lead compound 14 was shown to reduce hepatic malonyl-CoA levels in rodents. ZDF rats dosed with compound 14 also demonstrated a reduction in triglyceride levels in a 3-day study; however, the effect was apparently transient, as triglyceride lowering was not observed in a 8-day study in ZDF rats. In addition, in long-term studies performed in DIO C57BL6 mice, treatment with compound 14 showed no effects on triglyceride levels or glucose tolerance. Amgenâs compound 52 was also dosed in DIO C57BL6 mice in a long-term efficacy study. Despite an observed reduction in malonyl-CoA levels in heart and liver tissue samples that was consistent throughout the study, administration of 52 resulted in increased plasma glucose levels, as well as a worsening of oral glucose tolerance with no effects observed on triglyceride levels. On the other hand, Nimbusâ lead compound 48 was demonstrated to not only lower malonyl-CoA levels in vivo but also lead to a beneficial metabolic phenotype when dosed in a long-term efficacy study in DIO rats. Interestingly, unlike other ACC inhibitors whose binding sites have been characterized, 48 has been shown to bind to the soraphen binding site of the BC domain of ACC. Work by Tong suggests that rather than inhibiting ACC activity directly, soraphen serves to inhibit dimerization of ACC that may be required for catalytic activity.(11) This raises the question: Are all ACC inhibitors created equal? Are there potentially other uncharacterized functions of ACC inhibition that BC binders might impact that CT domain binders do not? Or could 48 possibly be interacting with other targets, complicating its pharmacological interpretation? One interesting experiment would be to dose 48 in one or more of the ACC2â/â mouse models that have been prepared, trying to conclusively link its in vivo activity to ACC inhibition.
    The prospects for success of ACC inhibition in the clinic is unclear. The early termination of Pfizerâs phase 2 clinical studies with compound 37 has reset the clock, so to speak, on testing this pathway in humans, and thus, we are left to speculate on what sorts of experiments would bring a âsatisfactoryâ answer to this question. Given the data set around 48, it would clearly be very interesting to compare the clinical effect of it or another BC binding ACC inhibitor with a presumably CT binding ACC inhibitor such as 37.(42) Another unanswered question is what the âbestâ indication for an ACC inhibitor would be. Pfizerâs clinical plan clearly focused on the expected type 2 diabetes population, yet they also had a trial planned for patients with acne vulgaris, which to the best of our knowledge is not an indication that had been previously postulated to benefit from ACC inhibition. Nimbusâ work suggests that they are interested in ACC inhibition as potential cancer therapeutic, providing another possible avenue for clinical entry. Lastly, a question that has dogged the area of ACC research since Wakilâs original publications is whether or not ACC1/2 dual inhibitors are necessary, or if an ACC2 selective inhibitor would be adequate to achieve a pharmacological response. Wakilâs ACC1â/â and ACC2â/â studies suggested that ACC2 inhibition itself might be sufficient, but subsequent work in this area seems to imply that ACC1 may be able to compensate for ACC2 deletion/inhibition, and thus, it would be expected that a dual ACC1/2 inhibitor would be necessary to achieve a pharmacological effect. Ultimately, the answers to these questions can only come from additional clinical studies.
