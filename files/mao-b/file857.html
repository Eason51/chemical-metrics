    Abstract

    Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is a leading pathogen of childhood and is associated with significant morbidity and mortality. To date, ribavirin is the only approved small molecule drug, which has limited use. The only other RSV drug is palivizumab, a monoclonal antibody, which is used for RSV prophylaxis. Clearly, there is an urgent need for small molecule RSV drugs. This article reports the design, synthesis, anti-RSV activity, metabolism, and pharmacokinetics of a series of 4′-substituted cytidine nucleosides. Among tested compounds 4′-chloromethyl-2′-deoxy-2′-fluorocytidine (2c) exhibited the most promising activity in the RSV replicon assay with an EC50 of 0.15 μM. The 5′-triphosphate of 2c (2c-TP) inhibited RSV polymerase with an IC50 of 0.02 μM without appreciable inhibition of human DNA and RNA polymerases at 100 μM. ALS-8176 (71), the 3′,5′-di-O-isobutyryl prodrug of 2c, demonstrated good oral bioavailability and a high level of 2c-TP in vivo. Compound 71 is a first-in-class nucleoside RSV polymerase inhibitor that demonstrated excellent anti-RSV efficacy and safety in a phase 2 clinical RSV challenge study.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Human respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is a negative-sense, single-stranded RNA virus and a member of the Paramyxoviridae family.(1) The 15.2-kb genomic RNA contains 10 genes, four of which code for intracellular proteins that are involved in genome transcription, replication, and particle budding and have orthologues in all members of the Mononegavirales, namely N (nucleoprotein), P (phosphoprotein), M (matrix protein), and L (“large” protein, containing the RNA polymerase catalytic motifs).(2) RSV is among the most important pathogens of childhood and is associated with significant morbidity and mortality. RSV causes common cold symptoms in most patients. In vulnerable children, RSV can progress to bronchiolitis and/or pneumonia with an increased chance of significant morbidity or death.(3, 4) By 2 years of age, most children have developed this infection at least once, which is associated with approximately 24 hospitalizations per 1000 infants and 1 million deaths worldwide per year.(4) Previous infections do not lead to persistent immunity, and reinfection is common. The primary therapy for RSV is supportive in nature and is comprised of measures to ensure adequate oxygenation and maintenance of appropriate fluid and nutritional requirements. No vaccine currently exists for active prophylaxis against RSV. To date, the only safe and efficacious approach to RSV prophylaxis is the humanized monoclonal antibody palivizumab, which is largely restricted to infants with an increased risk of hospitalization due to RSV infection. Ribavirin (1-β-d-ribofuranosyl-1,2,4-triazole-3-carboxamide) is the only small molecule drug approved for use against RSV infection. It has a restricted utility due to its controversial efficacy, toxicity, and requirement of special equipment for administration.(5, 6) Undoubtedly, there is an urgent, unmet medical need for new RSV therapeutics.
    In the last two decades, a number of highly potent RSV entry inhibitors targeting the F protein have been reported, with EC50 values in the nano- or picomolar range, suggesting considerable potential for further development as novel anti-RSV therapeutics.(7-9) Several of these compounds have successfully completed preclinical and early clinical studies, but they have not progressed to late stage human clinical trials.(10) However, recently, GS-5806 completed an RSV phase 2 challenge study. The results showed that GS-5806 significantly reduced viral load and clinical symptoms, thus demonstrating proof of concept for small molecule entry inhibitors in RSV treatment.(11) A siRNA agent (ALN-RSV01) administered as an inhaled drug was reported to have shown promising anti-RSV activity but subsequently failed to achieve its primary end point in a phase 2b clinical trial.(12) Another class of small molecule anti-RSV compounds are the inhibitors of transcription and replication of the viral genome. Liuzzi and co-workers reported a series of compounds that blocked cotranscriptional mRNA guanylylation by the viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase.(13) Sudo and co-workers reported another series of compounds represented by YM-53403 that inhibited the replication of RSV in plaque reduction assays.(14) Resistance studies showed that YM-53403 targeted the viral RNA polymerase L protein, which catalyzes the transcription and replication of the viral genome. Very recently, new analogues of YM-53403 were reported to be significantly more potent in vitro and investigated in preclinical studies.(15) To date, compounds in this class have not yet entered clinical studies.
    Ribavirin, the only approved small molecule RSV drug, is a broad-spectrum antiviral agent and active against a wide range of RNA viruses. Although the precise mechanism of action for ribavirin inhibiting viral replication has not been established, it is commonly accepted that the compound may have multiple mechanisms of action, of which viral mutagenesis and inhibition of IMPDH are well documented.(16-18) Although ribavirin 5′-triphosphate is the substrate of several viral polymerases, it is unlikely that inhibition of viral polymerases is a primary mechanism of action for antiviral activity as no significant viral polymerase inhibition by ribavirin triphosphate has been reported. In contrast, most antiviral nucleoside drugs in clinical use are known to target viral polymerases of DNA or retro viruses such as HIV, HBV, and herpes viruses or of RNA viruses such as HCV. Potent inhibition of a viral polymerase is well-known to efficiently shut down viral replication and thus represents a clinically validated approach to control viral infections.(19) A safe and efficacious antiviral nucleoside has to satisfy three main criteria: (a) be a potent inhibitor of the viral polymerase, (b) do not inhibit host DNA or RNA polymerases, including mitochondrial enzymes, and (c) be efficiently phosphorylated by host kinases to generate high levels of active nucleoside 5′-triphosphate in target tissues. The majority of currently approved antiviral nucleoside drugs inhibit viral polymerases by a chain termination mechanism where the nucleoside analogue is efficiently incorporated into a growing DNA (or RNA) chain and then prevents subsequent nucleotide incorporation, thus “chain terminating” viral genome replication. Structurally, the chain termination mechanism stems usually from unique sugar modification(s) of the terminating nucleoside analogue that is (are) typically highly specific for the particular class of viral polymerases.
    In search of effective nucleoside RSV polymerase inhibitors, we focused more on sugar-modified nucleosides that have the potential to inhibit RSV polymerase activity by chain termination. Thus, a large number (>100) of structurally diverse, novel sugar-modified nucleosides were designed and prepared for screening in cell-based RSV assays. In addition, we also converted the above nucleoside analogues to their respective 5′-triphosphates and screened the resulting NTP library in an inhibition assay of RNA synthesis by RSV RNA dependent RNA polymerase. In the initial phenotypic screening, only one compound, 4′-azido-2′-deoxy-2′,2′-difluorocytidine (1a), exhibited potent inhibition of RSV-induced cytopathic effect against the RSV Long strain, with an EC50 of 1 μM. Furthermore, the corresponding triphosphate was identified as a potent RSV polymerase inhibitor with an IC50 of 0.3 μM. Later, the nucleoside was shown to also inhibit the RSV A2 strain in an RSV replicon system, with an EC50 of 0.75 μM. We were encouraged by the initial anti-RSV results but also noted that a 4′-azido nucleoside could be a potential safety concern as RG-1626, a 4′-azidocytidine prodrug, caused severe adverse effects in phase 2 clinical trials that were halted.(20) In addition to the 4′-azido group, the 2′,2′-difluororibose sugar moiety of 1a was another potential concern as it exists in gemcitabine, a well-known cytotoxic compound. Our initial optimization was focused on the 2′-position of the sugar while the 4′-azido group remained intact. After testing a series of 2′-modified 4′-azidocytidines, 2-deoxy-2-fluororibofuranose was selected as a preferred sugar template because 4′-azido-2′-deoxy-2′-fluorocytidine (1d) exhibited potent anti-RSV activity with a good selectivity window. Subsequently, an intensive optimization was focused on replacement of the 4′-azido group, which resulted in discovery of 2c, a potent, selective RSV polymerase inhibitor, and its 3′,5′-di-O-isobutyryl derivative (71) as an oral prodrug. Compound 71 is a first-in-class nucleoside inhibitor of RSV infection in phase 2 clinical trials that demonstrated excellent anti-RSV efficacy and safety.(21) In this article, we report synthesis, in vitro activity, metabolism, and pharmacokinetics of compounds 1–3 as shown in Figure 1 and ester prodrugs of 2c (Schemes 7–9).

    Inhibition of RSV Polymerase

    The inhibitory effect of the nucleoside 5′-triphosphate (NTP) on RSV RNA synthesis was determined in a cell-free system using a crude RSV RNP complex (RSVpol) extracted from RSV infected cells.(32) This assay was used to measure changes in the incorporation of the radio-labeled 33P CMP or 33P GMP tracer into RNA in the presence of increasing concentration of an NTP analogue. In this assay, the first active NTP, 1a-TP, inhibited 33P CMP incorporation to the RNA chain elongated by RSVpol with an IC50 of 0.3 μM (Table 1). All NTPs with an azido group at the 4′-position and F or OH at the 2′-postion, either on α or β face (1a-TP, 1b-TP, 1c-TP and 1d-TP) exhibited potent inhibition. However, because of the potential safety concern of the 4′-azido group,(20) a series of nonazido, 4′-substituted nucleosides and their NTPs were investigated. Most of the TPs of 2a–l having α-F at the 2′-position showed potent inhibition of RSV polymerase activity (Table 1), with 2a-TP and 2c-TP having the 4′-methyl and 4′-chloromethyl being the most potent inhibitors (IC50 = 0.01 and 0.02 μM, respectively). While the high potency of 2a-TP might be ascribed to the smaller size of the 4′-Me, the three larger, closely related 4′-halomethyl NTPs (2b-TP, 2c-TP, and 2d-TP) also showed good inhibitory activity with an IC50 of 0.12, 0.02, and 0.15 μM, respectively. The NTPs having the 4′-vinyl, 4′-ethyl, 4′-ethynyl, and 4′-methoxy, respectively (2e-TP, 2f-TP, 2g-TP, and 2l-TP), also exhibited good inhibitory activity, with IC50 values in the 0.09–0.15 μM range. With a three-atom substituent at the 4′-position, the NTPs exhibited a varied inhibitory activity, with IC50 values ranging from 0.09 to 2.7 μM. While the 4′-allyl 2h-TP and the 4′-cyclopropyl 2j-TP still showed good inhibitory effect the 4′-n-propyl 2i-TP and the 4′-methylthiomethyl 2k-TP were >10-fold less potent. Obviously, the binding site on the polymerase had a good tolerance to the 4′-substituents, but certain size limitations are apparent. The TPs of 3a–d that have a 4′-chloromethyl and either F or OH at the 2′-position except for 2′-α-F showed much lower inhibition, which was in a sharp contrast to the TPs of 1a–d that were all active regardless of F or OH at the 2′-position. Thus, the 4′-azido group might serve as a predominant determinant, which could overcome certain changes at the 2′-position. It will be interesting to elucidate the role of the azido group at the 4′-position. The mechanism of RSV polymerase inhibition by 2c-TP was carefully investigated. It was concluded that nucleotide incorporation and subsequent chain termination was the mechanism of action for the inhibition of RNA synthesis by recombinant RSV polymerase.(33)

    Inhibition of Human DNA Polymerases α, β, γ, and RNA Polymerase II

    The potential inhibition by the NTP analogues of human DNA polymerases α, β, and γ as well as human RNA polymerase II was determined using in vitro DNA-dependent DNA polymerase (DdDp) assays for DNA polymerases α, β, and γ using recombinant enzymes and using a HeLa cell extract for the DNA dependent RNA polymerase (DdRp) activity of RNA polymerase II. The known polymerase inhibitors aphidicolin, dTTP and α-amanitin were used as control, respectively. When used at a concentration of 100 μM, 1a-TP, 2b-TP, 2c-TP, and 2f-TP all showed <50% inhibition of the DdDp activity of the purified human polymerases α, β, or γ and the DdRp activity of human RNA polymerase II. The results demonstrated that the NTPs in the study have high selectivity between the RSV polymerase and the host polymerases, which satisfies one of the prerequisites for a safe antiviral nucleoside analogue.

    Nucleotide Incorporation by Human Mitochondrial RNA Polymerase (HMRP)

    The substrate efficiency of a selection of CTP analogues was also tested against the DdRp activity of human mitochondrial RNA polymerase under conditions similar to those reported for other nucleotide analogues.(34) The natural CTP was used as the control to validate the assay. In the presence of 100 μM CTP, the majority of the 12-mer primer was converted to a 13-mer product (Figure 2). Natural UTP, ATP, and GTP were used as negative controls for base pair mismatch as they set the baseline level for mis-incorporation for this enzyme. Under these assay conditions, the ATP (A:G) and UTP (U:G) mismatch controls demonstrated low but significant levels of incorporation while the GTP (G:G) mismatch control displayed very low levels of incorporation, at the limit of detection for this assay. In comparison, three CTP analogues in this work, 1a-TP, 2c-TP, and 2f-TP, also showed undetectable to very low levels of incorporation, at a level below the mis-incorporation observed with UTP and ATP. In contrast, 2′-F-CTP, 2′-diF-CTP, 2′-ara-CTP, 1d-TP, and 2b-TP were recognized as efficient substrates for human mitochondrial RNA polymerase, at a level well above the level of mis-incorporation observed with UTP and ATP.
    On the basis of the RSV polymerase inhibitory potency and host polymerase inhibition results, clearly, 2c-TP demonstrated excellent activity and selectivity that satisfied the criteria for a safe and efficacious antiviral nucleoside analogue.

    In Vitro Antiviral Activity in an RSV Replicon Assay

    The RSV subgenomic replicon system in 395 HeLa cells was performed according to the protocols provided by Apath (Brooklyn, NY, USA). The system was originally developed by Peeples and co-workers.(35) To generate the subgenomic RSV replicon, three glycoprotein genes for SH, G, and F, were deleted from a full-length recombinant GFP-expressing (rg)RSV antigenomic cDNA. In their place, a blasticidin S deaminase (bsd) gene was inserted. Through multiple steps, the RSV replicon was established in the HeLa cells. All the nucleosides in this work were tested in the replicon system, and a number of them showed very potent anti-RSV activity with EC50 values as low as 150 nM as shown in Table 2. All the 4′-azido nucleosides 1a–d exhibited potent inhibition in the replicon assay, consistent with the RSV polymerase inhibitory results for their corresponding 5′-triphosphates. Compounds 2b, 2c, 2d, 2f, 2g, and 2j also exhibited good anti-RSV activity in the replicon assay, consistent with the potent inhibition of the RSV polymerase by their triphosphates. These cell-based anti-RSV results suggested that these nucleosides were well phosphorylated to their NTPs by cellular kinases and that the NTPs were potent RSV inhibitors in the cells. However, nucleosides 2a, 2e, 2h, and 2l were less active in the replicon with EC50 values ranging from 1.9 to 12.7 μM although their 5′-triphosphates were potent inhibitors of the RSV polymerase. In this case, cellular phosphorylation, metabolic stability, or other unknown reasons may have contributed to the lower activity in the replicon assay. Nucleosides 2i and 3a–d were less active or inactive in the replicon assay, consistent with the fact that their 5′-triphosphates were either less potent or inactive in the RSV polymerase assay. However, 2k exhibited good activity in the replicon with an EC50 of 0.51 μM despite 2k-TP not demonstrating potent activity in the RSV polymerase inhibition assay. Potentially, an oxidized form of 2k such as the sulfoxide or sulphone analogue could have been responsible for the cell-based activity. To clarify this assumption, the sulfoxide and sulphone versions of 2k-TP were prepared and tested. However, these nucleosides and their respective NTPs were even less active than 2k and 2k-TP (results not shown), which excludes the possibility that the activity resulted from these two possible metabolites. It is interesting to note that the 4′-azidocytidines 1a–c with either difluoro or OH at the 2′-position were all active in the replicon assay, while the 4′-chloromethylcytidines 3b–d with difluoro or OH at the 2′-position showed little activity. Clearly, the 4′-azido group was unique, overcoming the changes at the 2′-position and leading to good activity in both RSV polymerase and cell-based assays. In contrast, the 4′-chloromethyl resulted in potent anti-RSV inhibition only when it was combined with the 2′-α-F, as compound 2c showed. This, together with the fact that several 2′-α-F-4′-substituted cytidines were potent anti-RSV inhibitors, suggested that the 2′-α-F was a preferred modification for RSV polymerase inhibition. The active nucleosides in Table 2 demonstrated little cytotoxicity in the HeLa cells. Among the most potent compounds are 1d, 2b, and 2c, as demonstrated by their activity in the replicon assay and the RSV polymerase inhibitory effects of their 5′-triphosphates. However, the potent anti-RSV activity of 1d and 2b was confounded by their unfavorable incorporation into RNA by human mitochondrial RNA polymerase, as shown above. Compound 2c also demonstrated submicromolar activity against all RSV strains tested, including RSV B strains and RSV A clinical isolates.(33) On the basis of the RSV polymerase inhibition, lack of host polymerase inhibition, RSV replicon activity, and other drug like properties, compound 2c was selected for clinical development.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A series of 4′-substituted cytidine analogues have been synthesized and tested in anti-RSV assays. A number of 2′-deoxy-2′-fluoro-4′-susbstituted cytidine nucleosides demonstrated potent inhibition of the RSV replicon with a wide selectivity window, and their 5′-triphosphates effectively inhibited RSV polymerase with high selectivity with respect to host polymerases. These active nucleosides represent first-in-class antiviral agents against RSV infection. Compound 2c, which had an EC50 of 0.15 μM in the RSV replicon assay and an IC50 of 0.02 μM for its corresponding 5′-triphosphate in the RSV polymerase assay, was selected for clinical development. A series of 2c ester prodrugs were prepared and evaluated for bioavailability and other drug-like properties. Compound 71, the 3′,5′-di-O-isobutyryl prodrug of 2c, was selected for clinical development. Compound 71 given orally was efficacious against RSV infection in adult volunteers in a phase 2 RSV challenge study, with far superior virological outcomes when compared to the RSV fusion inhibitor GS-5806. Currently, compound 71 is under phase 2 clinical evaluation for the treatment of RSV infection in hospitalized infants aged 1–12 months.

    Synthesis of Nucleoside 5′-Triphosphates

    Dry nucleoside (0.05 mmol) was dissolved in dry PO(OMe)3 (0.7 mL). N-Methylimidazole (9 μL, 0.11 mmol) was added, followed by addition of POCl3 (9 μL, 0.11 mmol) at rt, and the mixture was kept for 20–40 min. The reaction was monitored on LCMS by appearance of the corresponding nucleoside 5′-monophosphate. After completion of the conversion, tetrabutylammonium salt of pyrophosphate (150 mg) was added, followed by DMF (0.5 mL) to get a homogeneous solution. After 1.5 h at rt, the reaction mixture was diluted with water (10 mL) and loaded on ÄKTApurifier equipped with HiLoad 16/10 with Q Sepharose High Performance. Separation was done in a linear gradient of NaCl from 0 to 1 M in 50 mM TRIS-buffer (pH 7.5). The fractions containing NTP were combined and concentrated. Desalting was done on RP HPLC on Synergy 4 μm Hydro-RP column (Phenominex). A linear gradient of methanol from 0 to 30% in 50 mM triethylammonium acetate buffer (pH 7.5) was used. The fractions containing NTP were combined, concentrated, and lyophilized three times to remove the TEAA buffer. Concentration of aqueous solutions of NTP was measured based on UV absorption on a GE NanoVue plus UV spectrometer. MS and 31P NMR data of the synthesized NTPs are listed in Table 7.

    RSV Polymerase Inhibition Assay

    Cell fractionation and extraction of RSV RNP complex was performed as described.(32) Unless otherwise specified, nucleoside triphosphate (NTP) concentrations were around their Km value: 0.1 μM CTP and 1 μM ATP, 2 μM UTP, and 500 μM GTP. The radioactive tracer was either 10 μCi [α-33P]rCTP (CTP competitive mode) or 15 μCi [α-33P]rGTP (non-CTP competitive mode) together with 0.1 μM GTP. The reaction was initiated with the addition of 1.5 μL of the RSV cell extract and incubated for 2 h at 30 °C in a final volume of 30 μL. The reaction was stopped by adding 25 μL of Tris-sodium-EDTA buffer containing 10 mM Tris-HCl pH 8, 150 mM NaCl, and 100 mM EDTA. Processing of samples included elution through a G-50 spin column (GE Healthcare), phenol–chloroform extraction, and ethanol precipitation. Air-dried RNA samples were reconstituted in 20 μL of TBE urea gel loading buffer (Invitrogen) and run through a 6% TBE urea PAGE gel for 1 h at 190 V. The gel was dried and exposed to a phosphor-screen that was scanned with a Storm 860 phosphorImager (Molecular Dynamics).

The concentrations of the NTPs were normalized by the cells numbers in each well and thus expressed as pmol/million cells.

    In Vivo Pharmacokinetic Studies

    The in vivo pharmacokinetic studies were conducted at Wuxi AppTec. Male Syrian Golden hamsters, male Sprague–Dawley rats, and non-naïve male cynomolgus monkeys were used in the in vivo pharmacokinetic studies. The animals (N = 3 for hamsters and rats and N = 2 for each of the monkey PK study) were fasted overnight before administered intravenously with 2f, 2c, or 2b or orally with 71, 81, or 73. The study compounds were formulated as solutions in PEG400-based vehicles. IV PK studies were conducted at 5 mg/kg and for oral PK studies the prodrugs were administered at 5 mg/kg parent nucleoside equivalent doses. Blood samples were typically collected at various time points up to 24 h post dose for hamster and rat studies and up to 12 h for monkey studies. K2EDTA was used as the anticoagulant. Following an immediate plasma protein precipitation treatment, the supernatant of the blood samples was analyzed for analyte concentrations using a LC/MS/MS method. Pharmacokinetic parameters of the test compounds were obtained by conducting a noncompartmental PK analysis of the blood concentration–time profiles of compounds using WinNonlin software (v5.2) by Pharsight (Mountain View, CA). For lung tissue collection, animals were anesthetized with either isoflurane or pentobarbital after the terminal blood collection. Lung tissue was removed immediately and flash-frozen into liquid nitrogen to prevent any ex vivo degradation of the phosphorylated metabolites. The frozen tissue was homogenized in an extraction solution in a sample tube set in a dry ice/ethanol bath to maintain cold temperature. The supernatant of the lung extracts was subjected to LC/MS/MS analysis for the concentrations NMP and NTP.

        A respiratory syncytial virus replicon that is noncytotoxic and capable of long-term foreign gene expression
        Malykhina, Olga; Yednak, Mark A.; Collins, Peter L.; Olivo, Paul D.; Peeples, Mark E.
        Journal of Virology (2011), 85 (10), 4792-4801CODEN: JOVIAM; ISSN:0022-538X. (American Society for Microbiology)
        Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) infection of most cultured cell lines causes cell-cell fusion and death. Cell fusion is caused by the fusion (F) glycoprotein and is clearly cytopathic, but other aspects of RSV infection may also contribute to cytopathol. To investigate this possibility, we generated an RSV replicon that lacks all three of its glycoprotein genes and so cannot cause cell-cell fusion or virus spread. This replicon includes a green fluorescent protein gene and an antibiotic resistance gene to enable detection and selection of replicon-contg. cells. Adaptive mutations in the RSV replicon were not required for replicon maintenance. Cells contg. the replicon could be cloned and passaged many times in the absence of antibiotic selection, with 99% or more of the cells retaining the replicon after each cell division. Transient expression of the F and G (attachment) glycoproteins supported the prodn. of virions that could transfer the replicon into most cell lines tested. Since the RSV replicon is not toxic to these cultured cells and does not affect their rate of cell division, none of the 8 internal viral proteins, the viral RNA transcripts, or the host response to these mols. or their activities is cytopathic. However, the level of replicon genome and gene expression is controlled in some manner well below that of complete virus and, as such, might avoid cytotoxicity. RSV replicons could be useful for cytoplasmic gene expression in vitro and in vivo and for screening for compds. active against the viral polymerase.
