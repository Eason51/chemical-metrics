    Abstract

    FK506-binding proteins (FKBPs) are evolutionarily conserved proteins that display peptidyl-prolyl isomerase activities and act as coreceptors for immunosuppressants. Microbial macrophage-infectivity-potentiator (Mip)-type FKBPs can enhance infectivity. However, developing druglike ligands for FKBPs or Mips has proven difficult, and many FKBPs and Mips still lack biologically useful ligands. To explore the scope and potential of C5-substituted [4.3.1]-aza-bicyclic sulfonamides as a broadly applicable class of FKBP inhibitors, we developed a new synthesis method for the bicyclic core scaffold and used it to prepare an FKBP- and Mip-focused library. This allowed us to perform a systematic structure–activity-relationship analysis across key human FKBPs and microbial Mips, yielding highly improved inhibitors for all the FKBPs studied. A cocrystal structure confirmed the molecular-binding mode of the core structure and explained the affinity gained as a result of the preferred substituents. The best FKBP and Mip ligands showed promising antimalarial, antileginonellal, and antichlamydial properties in cellular models of infectivity, suggesting that substituted [4.3.1]-aza-bicyclic sulfonamides could be a novel class of anti-infectives.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    FK506-binding proteins (FKBPs) are part of the immunophilin family and bind the natural products FK506 (Figure 1A) and rapamycin. They are best known for their ability to enable the immunosuppressive effects of these drugs by a remarkable gain-of-function mechanism.(1,2) Beyond ligand-induced immunosuppression, human FKBPs have been implicated in calcium signaling,(3,4) metabolic(5,6) and psychiatric disorders,(7−9) and chronic pain.(10,11) FKBPs are highly conserved and are present in all kingdoms of life, including in several human pathogens. Fungal FKBPs are thought to mediate the antifungal properties of FK506 and rapamycin by chemically inducing FKBP–protein complexes similar to those in immunosuppressive mechanisms in mammals.(12,13)Plasmodium falciparum, the causative agent of malaria, expresses an FKBP homologue (PfFKBP35) and is sensitive to FK506 and rapamycin. However, whether this sensitivity is due to the induction of protein complexes (like in other eukaryotes), due to the direct inhibition of PfFKBP35, or mediated by non-FKBP proteins is unclear.(14) In addition, many Gram-negative bacteria express FKBP homologues, some of which have been reported as being important in the virulence and replication of intra- and extracellular bacterial pathogens.(15,16) The prototypical member of these FKBPs was first described in Legionella pneumophila and named macrophage infectivity potentiator (Mip), as it supports the intracellular replication of this pathogen in phagocytic cells.(17,18)
    Taken together, FKBPs are considered promising drug targets. Traditionally, FKBPs have been studied pharmacologically using the prototypic ligands FK506 or rapamycin.(19−21) For most FKBPs, little is known regarding their recognition of ligands beyond these two natural products. FK506 potently inhibits calcineurin, whereas rapamycin blocks mTOR when bound by human FKBPs. This can severely confound or even obliterate studies in more complex biological systems. Moreover, the immunosuppression associated with these drugs is clearly counterproductive for putative anti-infective agents.
    In an effort to develop binders for FKBPs that do not interfere with immune responses, we recently identified bicycles, such as 1a, as highly efficient ligands of human FKBP12, FKBP51, and FKBP52 (Figure 1B).(22−24) In this scaffold, the [4.3.1]-bicycle mimics the pipecolate core of FK506 in its active conformation and presents three vectors that allow the various substituents positions for interacting with the rim of the FK506-binding site (Figure 1C).
    The FK506-binding pockets of human, bacterial, fungal, and plasmodium FKBPs are highly conserved (Figure 1D). Motivated by the high structural similarities of the FK506-binding sites across many FKBPs, we explored whether the (S)-C5-substituted [4.3.1]-aza-amide bicyclic core could be a general privileged scaffold for therapeutically relevant FKBP ligands.

    Synthesis of an FKBP- and Mip-Focused Substance Library

    We first aimed to explore the roles of R1 substituents in bicyclic ligands corresponding to scaffold 1. In order to generate an FKBP-focused library of R1-derivatized [4.3.1]-aza-amide bicycles, we first developed an efficient method of synthesizing key precursor 7, which harbored a PMB protection group in R1 (Figure 2A). The R2 substituent, 3,5,-dichlorphenylsulfonyl, was kept constant in this series, as this subgroup provided promising initial affinities for binding to human FKBP12, FKBP51, and FKBP52.(22)
    The synthesis commenced with allylation of phtalimide. The resulting allyl-phtalimide, 2, was transformed by metathesis with allylTMS and Grubbs first-generation catalyst and subsequently deprotected to afford the 4-TMS-but-2-en-1-amine, 4. An E-selective synthesis for this compound is known,(25) but in our case, the stereochemistry of the double bond does not influence the further course of the synthesis. Reductive amination of this fragment with paramethoxybenzaldehyde resulted in building block 5. This was reacted with commercially available (S)-6-oxo-2-piperidinecarboxylic acid and subsequently Boc-protected to furnish 6, the precursor for the key chemoselective reduction and asymmetric N-acyliminium cyclization.(24) Reduction with DIBAL-H and subsequent treatment with hydrofluoric acid in pyridine resulted in bicyclic building block 7 in an 8 g scale with a 29% yield over eight steps. A reaction with dichlorphenyl sulfonyl chloride afforded key intermediate 9.
    For the variation of the R1 substituent, compound 9 was deprotected with CAN. The resulting carboxamide, 10, was then alkylated with various halides to afford bicycles 11a–11n and 17a. To further enhance the diversity in the R1 position, compounds 11d and 11h were further derivatized to yield compounds 11o–11u (Figure 2B).
    Preliminary results showed that a pipecolyl group in the R1 position provided comparatively high binding to several FKBPs. We thus kept this subgroup constant in the subseries devised to explore the R2 group. We thus adopted the synthetic route developed above to synthesize key building block 14, which was obtained in a multigram scale with a 20% yield over eight steps (Figure 2C). Intermediate 14 was derivatized with various commercially available arylsulfonyl chlorides to yield 15a–15p and 16a.
    For the exploration of various R3 substituents in the C5 position, compounds 16a and 17a were chosen as high-affinity starting points (Figure 3). Taking advantage of the orthogonal reactivity of the vinyl group, alkenes 16a and 17a were first reduced to provide the saturated analogues 16b and 17b. Oxidative cleavage of the vinyl group furnished aldehydes 16c and 17c, which were further smoothly converted to the dimethylamine 16d, methylamine 17d, or carboxylic acid 17e. Standard transformations as follow-ups provided the amide 17f, the difluoromethyl group 16e, and the acetylene 16f. Reduction of the aldehydes with sodium borohydride gave alcohols 16h and 17g, which were subsequently transformed to the corresponding methyl ethers (16j and 17h), to the ethyl ether 16k, or to the fluoromethyl group 16i.

    Affinity Determination and Profiling of FKBP and Mip Proteins

    The 54 bicyclic sulfonamides were tested for their binding to six human FKBPs (FKBP12, FKBP12.6, FKBP13, FKBP25, FKBP51, and FKBP52) and five microbial FKBPs (from Candida albicans, Plasmodium falciparum, Chlamydia pneumonia, Chlamydia trachomatis, and Legionella pneumophila) using a competitive-fluorescence-polarization assay (Table 1). Gratifyingly, the microbial FKBPs bound to previously developed fluorescent tracers,(26) allowing rapid affinity measurements for these proteins (Table 1 and Supplementary Table 2). However, because several [4.3.1]-bicyclic sulfonamides bound very tightly to some of the human FKBPs (e.g., Ki < 0.5 nM for FKBP12), the exact affinities of these compounds could not be fully determined with the previously published assays.(26) We therefore developed a more sensitive fluorescent tracer, 16g, derived from the high-affinity ligand 16f (Figure 3). Tracer 16g tightly bound to all the FKBPs, including FKBP12 (Kd = 0.57 ± 0.03 nM) and FKBP12.6 (Kd = 0.53 ± 0.17 nM), allowing precise measurements of the high-affinity ligands for these proteins.

    For the human FKBPs, CaFKBP12, PfFKBP35, and LpMip, the affinities of the best [4.3.1] bicycles, such as that of 16j (Figure 4A), rivalled or exceeded the affinity of the prototypic ligand FK506 as well as the affinities of the best ligands published for these proteins so far.(27−38) For CpMip, which has no characterized ligand so far, compound 11j was found to bind substantially better than FK506.
    In general, the smaller human FKBPs bound tighter to the [4.3.1]-bicycles than their larger homologues (FKBP12 > FKBP12.6 > FKBP13 ≈ FKBP51 ≈ FKBP52 ≫ FKBP25). For the microbial FKBPs, the average affinities decreased in the following order: CaFKBP12 > PfFKBP35 ≫ CpMip ≈ LpMip > CtMip. This tendency closely correlated with the sequence homologies of the FKBPs to human FKBP12. Whether the enhanced ligand-binding propensity of FKBP12 is restricted to compounds of scaffold 1 or intrinsic to the individual binding pockets remains to be elucidated, possibly by an investigation of alternative scaffolds or by an unbiased screening of large substance libraries.(39)
    The high affinities for human FKBP12 suggest that microbial-FKBP inhibition in the absence of FKBP12 inhibition may be difficult. However, by using an elegant approach recently described by Nambu et al., the potent and selective inhibition of human FKBP12 observed here can actually be turned into an advantage in an antifungal therapy.(29) By blocking human FKBP12, but not fungal FKBP12, for example, with compound 16b (KiHsFKBP12/KiCaFKBP12 = 40), FK506 can be directed to inhibit fungal calcineurin only.

    Binding Mode of C5-Modified [4.3.1]-Bicyclic FKBP Ligands

    An exemplary cocrystal structure of the FK506-binding domain of FKBP51 in complex with ligand 16h was solved in order to clarify the molecular basis for the improved affinities of the optimized bicyclic ligands (Figure 4B and Supplementary Table 2). The key interactions were van der Waals contacts between the pipecolyl ring and a hydrophobic cage formed by Tyr57, Phe77, Val86, Ile87, and Trp90 as well as a hydrogen bond between the pipecolyl carbonyl oxygen and the amide of Ile87 (FKBP51 numbering). The high resolution of the cocrystal structure also unambiguously defined the very close contact (3.0 Å) between the pipecolyl carbonyl carbon and the phenyl oxygen of Tyr113, which likely reflected an orthogonal dipolar interaction.
    For the contacts of the R1–R3 substituents, we observed a hydrogen bond between the picolinyl in R1 and Tyr113 as well as a halogen bond between the R2 aryl chlorine and Ser118 (Figure 4B), which structurally explained the preferred binding of the compounds with these functional groups. The hydroxymethylene group in R3 did not engage in direct contacts with FKBP51, possibly explaining the shallow structure–activity relationship in this position. The hydroxyl group was able to adopt two possible conformations, and it interacted indirectly via water molecules with the backbone carbonyl of Gln85 or the side chains of Asp68 and Ser118 (Figure 4C).

    Structure–Activity-Relationship Analysis of the [4.3.1]-Bicyclic Derivatives

    The analysis of the R1 moieties revealed that the truncation of the 3-dimethoxy phenyl ether in 1a (R1 = C2H4OPh(OMe)2) to a simple methyl ether in 17a (R1 = C2H4OMe) only marginally compromised affinity (KiFKBP12 = 8.8 ± 0.5 nM → 18.8 ± 1.8 nM). Likewise, smaller or larger substituents on the γ-oxygen were well tolerated, such as in 11m (R1 = C2H4OH, KiFKBP12 = 11.8 ± 2.7 nM) or 11p (R1 = C2H4OEt, KiFKBP12 = 21.1 ± 1.3 nM). However, the γ-position of the ether was important because moving the oxygen to the δ-position, as in 11e (R1 = C3H6OMe, KiFKBP12 = 143 ± 16 nM), or the ε-position, as in 11f (R1 = C4H8OMe, KiFKBP12 = 249 ± 2 nM), substantially affected affinity. Removal of the ether group, as in 10, 11a, 11c, 11g, or 11q, slightly reduced affinity (KiFKBP12 = 49–80 nM), whereas replacing it with alternative hydrogen-bond acceptors, as in 11h or 11r–11u, retained the affinity (KiFKBP12 = 7–25 nM). Likewise, pyridine analogues, such as in 11k and 11l (with their hydrogen-bond acceptors in the ortho positions), were beneficial R1 moieties, whereas a phenyl moiety, such as in 9, or five-membered heterocycles, such as those in 11i and 11j, were not. This analysis, discussed here for HsFKBP12, generally held true for most of the FKBPs and Mips. A notable exception was 11j (with a furanylmethyl group), which bound comparatively well to Chlamydia Mips.
    In the R2 position, halogens in the meta position generally enhanced affinity compared with that of the unmodified phenyl sulfonyl group (15b). For Mips, a cyano group in this position, as in 15g, appeared to be comparatively favorable. Heterocycles instead of the phenyl ring, such as in 15a and 15c, were disadvantageous, but fused heterocycles, such as in 15o and 15p, tended to be favorable. Surprisingly, a pyrimidin in the para position, as in 15m, was particularly beneficial for CaFKBP12 binding (Ki = 52 nM). The R3 position was generally rather tolerant to modifications, with hydroxymethyl or methoxymethyl, as in 16j, conferring the highest affinities to FKBPs (Figure 4A). A carboxyl group was clearly not tolerated (cf. 17e to 17b or 17f).
    A general finding for all the FKBPs was the absence of cooperativity effects between the R1, R2, and R3 moieties. Productive interactions, such as having a hydrogen-bond acceptor in the γ-position of R1 or a halogen-bond donor in the meta position of R2, appear to operate independently. However, the beneficial effect of having a hydrogen-bond acceptor in R1 might not extend to compounds with a carboxamides(32,33,40,41) in R2, because in such a case the Tyr113 (Tyr89 in FKBP12) hydrogen donor would already be satisfied.

    Inhibition of Microbial Growth and Proliferation

    Given the promising affinities of several of the [4.3.1]-bicyclic sulfonamides for microbial FKBPs, we characterized the effects of the most advanced compounds on microbial growth. Compound 16b, as an exemplary high-affinity ligand for microbial FKBPs and Mips, dose-dependently reduced parasitemia in Plasmodium falciparum strain 3D7 with an IC50 of 2.3 μM (Figure 5A,B). Similar results were obtained with the weaker PfFKBP35 ligand, 17b (IC50 = 8.3 μM, Supplementary Figure 1B). Importantly, 16b maintained its antimalarial activity against multidrug-resistant strain Dd2 (Figure 5C).
    In the past, LpMip was shown to be important for the intracellular growth of Legionella pneumophila, the causative agent of Legionnaires’ disease.(17,42) Accordingly, we tested the potential of the [4.3.1]-bicyclic sulfonamides to inhibit the proliferation of this intracellular pathogen in human THP-1 monocytes that had been differentiated into macrophages. Compounds 16b and 17b abolished intracellular proliferation of the bacteria in a dose-dependent manner when they were added 2 h after infection following the removal of the extracellular bacteria. Antiproliferative activities could be observed for both compounds at concentrations ≥20 μM, resulting in the repression of intracellular replication within the first 24 h of infection to levels comparable to that of the avirulent dotA-negative mutant (Figure 5D and Supplementary Figure 1C).
    Next, we tested the effect of 11j on Chlamydia spp., because 11j displayed high affinities for CpMip and CtMip and discriminated at least against the large human FKBPs. Chlamydia spp. have a unique developmental cycle that alternates between two distinct forms: infectious elementary bodies (EBs) and more metabolically active reticulate bodies (RBs). After being internalized into host cells, EBs differentiate into RBs in intracellular membrane-bound compartments called the chlamydial inclusions where they replicate. To infect neighboring cells, RBs revert to infectious progeny EBs prior to cell lysis. Compound 11j led to slightly reduced inclusion sizes in host cells infected with Chlamydia pneumoniae (Figure 5E, bottom left). However, consistent with prior observations,(43) the generation of infectious EBs was reduced dramatically (Figure 5E, bottom right). Similar results were also observed for C. trachomatis (Supplementary Figure 1D), indicating that inhibition of Mip might principally block RB-to-EB conversion in Chlamydia, thereby suppressing infectivity. It is important to note that at this point, we cannot fully exclude the participation of human-host FKBPs in the anti-Chlamydia and anti-Legionella effects observed. More selective inhibitors will be needed to clarify this issue.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Taken together, our results show that (S)-C5-substituted [4.3.1]-aza-amide bicyclic compounds are a privileged, broad-ranged class of FKBP inhibitors. We present the first systematic SAR analysis for human FKBP12.6, FKBP13, and FKBP25 and CpMip as well as the first useful inhibitor for CpMip. Compounds such as 11j or 15m show that with carefully chosen substituents, selectivity profiles can be tuned (e.g., for CpMip, CtMip, or CaFKBP12). Our results suggest that the PPIase active site indeed plays a crucial role in the life cycle of P. falciparum, L. pneumophila, C. pneumoniae, and C. trachomatis. The presented compounds will be highly useful as probes to study FKBP and Mip function as well as starting points for the further development of FKBP- and Mip-directed drugs.

    1. Organic Synthesis

    General

    Reactions were performed in heatgun-dried flasks under an argon atmosphere. All reagents were purchased from commercial sources and were used directly without further purification.

    Nuclear Magnetic Resonance

    1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded at the Department of Chemistry and Pharmacy at the Ludwig-Maximilian University of Munich on a Bruker AC300, a Bruker XL400, or a Bruker AMX600 at room temperature. Chemicals shifts are reported in parts per million referenced with respect to residual solvent (1H: CDCl3 = 7.26 ppm, 13C: CDCl3 = 77.16 ± 0.06 ppm). The coupling constants (J) are given in Hertz (Hz), and the peak multiplicities are abbreviated as singlet (s), doublet (d), triplet (t), quartet (q), and multiplet (m).

    Mass Spectrometry

    ESI mass spectra (m/z) were recorded on a Thermo Finnigan LCQ DECA XP Plus mass spectrometer.

    High-Performance Liquid Chromatography

    Analytical reverse-phase HPLC was performed using a Beckman System Gold 125S Analytical Solvent Module, a Beckman 168 Diode Array Detector Module, a Beckman 508 Autosampler Module with a 50 μL sampling loop, and a Jupiter 4 μm Proteo 90 Å (250 × 4.6 mm) analytical column (Phenomenex) at a flow rate of 1 mL/min with buffer A, 0.1% TFA in water/acetonitrile (95:5, v/v), and buffer B, 0.1% TFA in acetonitrile/water (95:5, v/v).

    Flash Chromatography

    Chromatographic separations were performed either manually or with an Interchim Puriflash 430 automatic flash-chromatography system. Silica gel 60 (Merck 70–230 mesh) was used for manual column chromatography. Puriflash columns 50 Silica 50 μM (10, 25, and 40 g) were used for automated chromatography.

    Thin-Layer Chromatography

    Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) was performed using Merck-prepared aluminum plates (Silica 60 F254, 0.25 mm). The substances were detected with UV-light (λ = 254 or 366 nm). The thin-layer plates were also stained with the following solutions: Hanessian’s (5 g of CeSO4, 25 g of NH4MO7O24·4 H2O, 450 mL of H2O, and 50 mL of H2SO4) and potassium permanganate (1.5 g of KMnO4, 10 g of K2CO3, and 1.25 mL of 10% NaOH in 200 mL of H2O).

    Purity

    The purities of the compounds were verified by reverse-phase HPLC. All the final compounds synthesized and tested had purities of more than 95%.

After the solution was stirred for 3 h at room temperature, EtO2 (300 mL) was added to the reaction mixture, which was then washed with a saturated NaCl(aq) solution (3 × 75 mL). After the extraction, the solvent was removed under reduced pressure affording the title compound (19.0 g, 101 mmol, 99.0%) as a colorless solid, which was used for the next step without further purification.     Synthesis of 3, 2-(4-(Trimethylsilyl)but-2-en-1-yl)isoindoline-1,3-dione

The combined organic layers were dried over MgSO4 and the solvent was removed under reduced pressure.     Synthesis of 4, 4-(Trimethylsilyl)but-2-en-1-amine

    2. Competitive-Fluorescence-Polarization Assay for Binding to FKBP-Target Proteins

    The competitive-fluorescence-polarization assay was performed according to Kozany et al.(26) Briefly, the fluorescent tracer was diluted in the assay buffer (20 mM NaCl, 20 mM HEPES, pH 8.0, 0.002% Tween20) to a concentration 1.9× the final concentration. The competitive ligand was dissolved in DMSO to form a 100× diluted stock solution. This was used for a 1:1 serial dilution in DMSO. Every sample of this serial dilution was diluted by a factor of 50 in the assay buffer supplemented with the fluorescent tracer to achieve a 2× concentrated mixture of the fluorescent tracer and competing ligand. Protein aliquots (45 μL) at 1.11× the final concentration in the assay buffer were transferred into black 384-well assay plates (No. 3575, Corning Life Sciences). A mixture (5 μL) of the fluorescent tracer and competitive ligand were added to the protein. After a 30 min incubation at room temperature, which allowed the solutions to reach equilibrium, the fluorescence polarization was measured with a Tecan Genios Pro. The competition curves were analyzed by SigmaPlot 13.0.

    4. Analysis of P. falciparum Strains

    4a. Cultivation and Synchronization of P. falciparum Strains

    3D7 and Dd2 parasite cell lines were obtained from the Malaria Research and Reference Reagent Resource Center (MR4). The parasites were grown in vitro with human red blood cells obtained via the National University Hospital Blood Donation Centre, Singapore, and maintained in complete medium comprising of 16.2 g of RPMI (Gibco, Life Technologies), 5 g of Albumax II (Gibco, Life Technologies), 2.3 g of sodium bicarbonate (Sigma), 0.05 g of hypoxanthine (Sigma), and 10 mg of gentamicin (Gibco, Life Technologies) per liter. Late-stage parasites were obtained by purification with 68% Percoll in incomplete RPMI (MP Biomedicals) and density centrifugation, followed by synchronization with 5% sorbitol (Sigma) to obtain only the early postinvasion parasites.

    4b. Dose–Response Assays in P. falciparum Parasite Strains

    Parasitemia was determined by counting the percentage of infected erythrocytes in at least 1000 cells. For each assay, the parasitemia was adjusted to 1% using uninfected erythrocytes. In each well, 100 μL of a 2% hematocrit culture was treated with a serial dilution of the SP compounds dissolved in DMSO, and then the plates were incubated for 60 h. The DMSO content per well did not exceed 0.05%. Each dose treatment was done in triplicate.

    4c. Growth-Inhibition Assays of P. falciparum Parasite Strains

    Following the incubation, the culture supernatant was removed, and the cells were resuspended in 1× phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) containing 16.2 μM Hoechst 33342 (ThermoFisher Scientific). Staining was done for 20 min at room temperature and was stopped by adding 200 μL of 1× PBS. The parasitemia of the stained, treated cells was measured by flow cytometry using a BD Fortessa X20. The parasitemias of the treated cultures were used to plot the dose–response curves, and the efficacies in the parasite strains were estimated with the ICE estimator online software tool (http://www.antimalarial-icestimator.net/MethodIntro.htm).
