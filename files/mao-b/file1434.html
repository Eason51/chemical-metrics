    Abstract

    The present study investigates dose-dependent effects of trans-resveratrol on the membrane fluidity using planar lipid bilayer and liposome models. The complex admittance plots obtained for the lipid bilayer show that resveratrol below 60 μM preferentially interacts with the polar headgroups at the membrane–electrolyte interface, leading to enhanced membrane admittance and vice versa at higher concentrations (>60 μM). This was confirmed using solid-state 13C and 31P NMR studies and membrane fluidization studies. The localization of resveratrol in the membrane bilayer was found to alter the membrane rigidity, which resulted in a dose-dependent blebbing and lysis of erythrocytes. The protective effect of trans-resveratrol against DPPH also confirms that its localization in the hydrophobic region prevents lipid peroxidation. The cytotoxic effect of resveratrol on a breast cancer cell line also displays a progressive pattern, indicating possible correlation with its membrane rigidifying properties and localization in the lipid bilayer.

    1 Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    trans-Resveratrol is one of the most widely studied phytoalexins, naturally present in grapes, berries, peanuts, jackfruit, spruce, legumes, etc.(1) Resveratrol has been proven to be a potent antioxidant,(2, 3) anticancer agent,(4, 5) and antiinflammatory agent.(6) It is also known to exhibit antiplatelet activity, estrogen-like growth promoting effect, antiaging property, immunomodulation, chemosensitization, and chemoprevention effects.(1, 7) Moreover, the cardioprotective properties of resveratrol, well documented by several authors,(8-10) are partly responsible for the phenomenon known as “French paradox”, according to which consumption of wine is said to be beneficial to the heart owing to its rich polyphenol content though the alcohol in wine can produce adverse effects in other tissues.(11)
    Several diseases like cancer,(12, 13) inflammation,(14) cardiovascular disorders,(15) rheumatoid arthritis,(14) and neurodegeneration(16) have been shown to be related to excessive generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in the physiological system. Excessive ROS can cause damage to DNA, proteins, and cell membrane lipids and hence affect the basic functioning of the cell. Although there are inherent radical scavenging mechanisms comprising the glutathione system, uric acid, vitamins C and E, antioxidant enzymes like superoxide dismutase, catalase, DNA repair enzymes, etc., an antioxidant supplement can enhance the scavenging process.(16, 17)
    Resveratrol is a free radical scavenger and an antioxidant. It possesses three phenolic groups and acts as a free radical scavenger by transferring the proton from its phenolic group to the free radicals(18-20) (Figure 1). Resveratrol is known to scavenge OH• radicals produced as a result of Fenton’s reaction, hence preventing DNA damage. It has also been shown to scavenge O2•– (superoxide) radicals and other free radicals produced after exposure to Cr(VI) and also to inhibit lipid peroxidation.(2) In addition, it also maintains the levels of antioxidant enzymes like superoxide dismutase, catalase, and glutathione peroxidase.(21)
    The molecular mechanism of action of natural polyphenol antioxidants depends on their structure as well as their interaction with the lipid membranes.(22-30) The structure of the polyphenol influences its intercalation into the intermembrane space where it acts as a radical scavenger and protects the membrane from oxidative stress.(31, 32) Stilbenes preferentially interact with the polar headgroups of the lipids, while some of its derivatives are reported to penetrate the inner membrane regions.(33, 34) While one study showed that resveratrol interacts with the lipid headgroup region of the bilayer,(25) another reported that more than 90% of resveratrol permeated into the intermembrane region of the bilayer.(33) A concentration-dependent change was observed on the elasticity of the membrane due to the penetration of polyphenols into the lipid bilayer.(35) It has been hypothesized that the polar and nonpolar fragments of these compounds interact with the lipid headgroup and the nonpolar acyl chains, respectively.(36) The lipid acyl chains cooperatively bend to fill the free volumes created by the low concentrations of the intercalated molecules. On the other hand, higher concentrations of these molecules cause them to interdigitate resulting in the rigidity of the membrane.(27, 36)
    While some groups have reported the interaction of low concentrations of resveratrol, others have studied the effects of high concentrations of resveratrol on the lipid bilayers.(25, 27, 33, 34) However, the dose-dependent effects of resveratrol on the cell membrane fluidity, structure, and integrity have not been thoroughly investigated. The aim of the current work is to investigate the dose-dependent interactions of resveratrol on the electrochemical parameters of lipid bilayers and its localization within the bilayer membrane in an effort to understand its implications on its antioxidant properties. The “painted” lipid bilayer has been used as a model for the cell membrane. The conductance and capacitance were measured across the bilayer in the presence and absence of the stilbenoid, resulting in a clear description of the interaction of the polyphenol with the lipid membrane.

    2.1 Membrane Interaction Studies Using Planar Lipid Bilayers

    The cyclic voltammograms of the lipid bilayers were recorded in the absence and presence of resveratrol between −70 to +70 mV at a scan rate of 0.01 V/s. Figure 2 shows the cyclic voltammograms and admittance spectra for the lipid bilayer in the absence and presence of different concentrations of resveratrol. The characteristic hysteresis pattern in the cyclic voltammogram with low current flow and no redox peak indicates the capacitive nature for unmodified lipid bilayer (Figure 2A). The addition of resveratrol to the lipid bilayer increases both anodic and cathodic current in a dose-dependent manner without altering the capacitive nature of the lipid bilayer. This indicates that resveratrol may induce conducting pathways in the bilayer architecture with increasing concentrations.
    The observed simple admittance plot for unmodified bilayers exhibits a semicircle pattern throughout the frequency ranges studied (10 to 105 Hz) that is indicative of the capacitive nature of lipid bilayers (Figure 2B). It is also observed that the center of the semicircle admittance plot lies on the real axis, which indicates that the planar lipid bilayer possesses a dielectric component with slight leakage.(37) The admittance values obtained at high frequencies can be attributed to charge transfer dynamics, while the admittance data obtained at the lower frequencies represent kinetics-controlled current.(38) The diameter of the semicircle obtained for unmodified bilayers is high indicating the restricted charge transfer from one compartment to another through the bilayer. Any alteration in the semicircle plot on addition of resveratrol can be attributed to its interaction with the lipid bilayer components in either the surface or interior. In such a case, an increase in the diameter of the admittance plot on addition of resveratrol may be mainly attributed to an increase in membrane resistance.(37) Contribution to the admittance in planar lipid bilayers is due to four different domains in the lipid bilayer, namely, acyl chains, glycerol backbone, and carbonyl and phosphocholine regions in the phosphatidylcholine molecules forming the lipid bilayer. The total impedance for planar lipid bilayers with respect to an equivalent RC circuit is given by eq 1.(1)where Z = impedance, Rs = solution resistance, RCT = charge transfer resistance, ω = angular velocity, Cdl = double layer capacitance, j = ion flux. From the admittance plot, the charge transfer resistance (RCT) can be determined from the diameter of the semicircle. The RCT values for the unmodified bilayer were found to be in the range of 1–2 MΩ. The observed variation in the bilayer RCT for different individual experiments possibly arises because of the heterogeneity in the lipid composition, dynamic nature of the Plateau–Gibbs layer, and border leakage in the Perspex membrane support. Because of these technical difficulties, the data are presented in terms of relative alteration with respect to unmodified bilayers to attain uniformity in the results from the representative graph of four independent experiments.
    The slight distortion in the semicircle pattern observed in all cases indicates a strong surface interaction between the constituents in the bath medium with the polar headgroups of the lipid bilayer at the membrane–electrolyte interface. The addition of different concentrations of resveratrol alters the diameter and height of the semicircle pattern in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 2B). A reduction in the diameter of the semicircle up to the concentration of 60 μM resveratrol is observed, following which an increase in the diameter of semicircle was noted for further additions of resveratrol. Figure 3 shows the relative change in the charge transfer resistance of the bilayer on addition of different concentrations of resveratrol. It is evident that the RCT decreases until the addition of 60 μM resveratrol implies an increase in the bilayer permeability and the values start to increase beyond resveratrol concentrations of 60 μM.

    2.2 Membrane Fluidization Studies

    To understand the effect of resveratrol on the fluidity of the lipid bilayer, membrane fluidization studies were carried out using curcumin-encapsulated liposomes. Figure 4 shows the effect of different concentrations of resveratrol on bilayer fluidity using curcumin as a fluorescent probe. Curcumin is a well-known fluorophore that predominantly localizes in the hydrophobic region of the bilayer. It can be released from the hydrophobic region either because of an increase in the membrane fluidity or by displacement by a molecule competing for the same location inside the bilayer. It was observed that the addition of different concentrations (20–100 μM) of resveratrol gradually releases the curcumin from unilamellar vesicles in a dose-dependent manner. It was observed that about 17% of curcumin is released from unilamellar vesicles with respect to 100 μM resveratrol compared to other concentrations (p < 0.05). This suggests that resveratrol probably displaces curcumin from the lipid bilayer by preferentially localizing in the hydrophobic core of the bilayer. This is further confirmed using NMR spectroscopy (Figure 5).
    It was observed that the upfield shifts for the methylenic groups present in the acyl chains of egg phosphatidylcholine appear between 13 and 30 ppm in both blank liposomes and resveratrol loaded liposomes. However, the downfield shifts due to the olefinic carbons and the carbons near the polar ends of the phospholipids disappear in the resveratrol loaded liposomes (Figure 5). This suggests that resveratrol localizes in the hydrophobic acyl regions closer to the polar headgroups.
    The question of whether resveratrol remains associated with the polar headgroup or is present inside the hydrophobic region has been addressed using the 31P NMR spectrum (Figure 6). The peak at 1.2 ppm appears in the spectra for both blank and resveratrol-loaded liposomes in the 31P NMR, and the absence of any significant shift in this peak in the resveratrol-loaded liposomes implies that the resveratrol is localized in the hydrophobic region (Figure 6B). It is, however, observed that the peak at 1.191 ppm for egg phosphatidylcholine liposomes is broad and symmetric. This indicates that the phospholipids in the liposome exist in the lamellar phase. However, this peak appearing at 1.2 ppm in the case of resveratrol loaded liposomes exhibits a significant reduction in its width. This implies that resveratrol interacts with the egg phosphatidylcholine molecules in the liposome vesicles, leading to formation of an isotropic phase that eliminates any chemical shift anisotropy. It is also likely that the reduction in the width of the peak is the result of an additional wobbling motion of the polar headgroups of the lipid facilitated by the incorporation of the resveratrol molecules in the hydrophobic region.

    2.3 In Silico Studies

    Figure 7 shows the predicted interactions with a simulated lipid bilayer membrane composed of palmitoyloleoylphosphatidylcholine (POPC), palmitoyloleoylphosphatidylethanolamine (POPE), and cholesterol—the major constituents in egg phospholipids and membranes and resveratrol.(36) The interactions between resveratrol and the simulated bilayer components using docking studies show that resveratrol localizes preferentially near the outer bilayer leaflet. The docking studies also indicate an interaction of resveratrol with the acyl chains of the phospholipids, suggesting that resveratrol localizes in the region adjacent to the double bonds in the acyl chains. It is this region that is more susceptible to oxidative damage, and hence, this localization of resveratrol might contribute to its membrane protective action. This prediction is in agreement with the conclusions arrived at using the NMR data.

    2.4 Interactions of Resveratrol with Erythrocytes

    Red blood cells or erythrocytes have a characteristic morphology that is dependent upon its membrane composition and intracellular and extracellular ionic environment. Hence, introduction of a molecule that alters the membrane fluidity can induce morphological changes in the erythrocytes. Figure 8 shows the influence of different concentrations of resveratrol on the morphology of erythrocytes. The normal erythrocytes exhibit their characteristic biconcave morphology. However, addition of resveratrol is found to cause extensive blebbing, budding, and destruction of the erythrocytes. No distinct morphology of erythrocytes is discernible at concentrations of 60 μM and above, which implies a strong correlation between membrane localization of resveratrol and the destruction of erythrocytes.

    2.5 Antioxidant Activity of Resveratrol

    Figure 9 shows the percentage of inhibition of 1,1′-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) free radical by measuring the reduction in absorbance at 515 nm. It was observed that at doses below 100 μM, resveratrol exhibits lower antioxidant property when compared with vitamin C, a well-known antioxidant. However, beyond concentrations of 100 μM, the antioxidant property of resveratrol is significantly higher when compared with that of vitamin C (Figure 9).
    However, the DPPH assay gives only an indication of the antioxidant potential of a molecule, as it is carried out in the absence of a lipid bilayer. The aim of this work is to prove that the localization of the molecule within the bilayer and the membrane fluidity can influence its antioxidant property. Therefore, the ability of resveratrol to protect the lipid bilayer against DPPH-induced lysis was investigated for the first time using cyclic voltammetry. Figure 10 shows the admittance plot for the lipid bilayer membrane exposed to DPPH in the absence and presence of resveratrol. It was observed that the addition of DPPH dramatically reduces the diameter and height of the semicircle (inset of Figure 10). Thus, the addition of DPPH significantly increases the current flowing through the system. This can be attributed to the influence of DPPH on the membrane that induces packing defects by oxidizing fatty acids in the planar lipid bilayers. In the presence of resveratrol, the reduction in the diameter and height of the semicircle on addition of DPPH is inhibited. This indicates that resveratrol offers some protection against DPPH-mediated increase in current flow. The reduction in the diamater of the semicircle is even lesser in the presence of 100 μM resevratrol when compared with the addition of 50 μM resveratrol (Figure 10B). Also, higher concentrations of the free radical generator (DPPH) causes slightly higher reduction in the diameter and height of the semicircle in the presence of resveratrol. Interestingly, on addition of DPPH, the distortion in the semicircle observed for the lipid bilayer diasppeared indicating the creation of additional pathways for transport of charged species through the bilayer, thereby removing any adsorbed species at the membrane–electrolyte interface (inset of Figure 10).
    Figure 10 shows the relative change in the charge transfer resistance (RCT) of the membrane brought about by DPPH in the absence and presence of 50 and 100 μM resveratrol. The addition of 100 μM DPPH significantly reduces the RCT values of the lipid bilayer (by 95%) when compared to the control bilayer. This indicates that the free radicals cause lipid peroxidation leading to the lysis of the acyl chains. The compact packing of the lipid bilayer is affected causing many defects, which in turn act as low resistance pathways for transport of charges. Only 19.5% and 7.7% reduction in the RCT values was observed on addition of 50 μM DPPH in the presence of 50 and 100 μM resveratrol, respectively. The reduction slightly increased to 22% and 10.9%, respectively, for 50 and 100 μM resveratrol when the amount of DPPH added was doubled. This observation suggests that resveratrol protects the lipid bilayer by scavenging the free radicals and/or preventing direct access to the lipid bilayer for the DPPH radical (Figure 10).

    2.6 Cell Viability Assay

    Figure 11 shows the effects of different concentrations (20–100 μM) of resveratrol on MCF-7 breast cancer cells. It is observed that the cytotoxic effect of resveratrol on breast cancer cells gradually increases with concentration. The maximum cytotoxicity of about 50% was observed at 100 μM resveratrol compared to other concentrations tested (p < 0.05). This result is in accordance with LD50 values for resveratrol reported by earlier groups.(39)

    3 Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Plant polyphenols can be included in a wide range of therapeutic and dietary regimens owing to their pharmacological activities which have been attributed to their antioxidant properties determined by their structure.(40) These compounds, however, have also been found to alter the fluidity of cell membranes through nonspecific interactions,(41) which may influence their antioxidant and pharmacological effects in vivo.(42) In addition, the localization of the antioxidant inside the cell membrane is an important determinant of its free-radical scavenging efficiency.(32) Resveratrol has elicited much interest in recent years because of its antioxidant and anticancer properties.(1) However, the dose-dependent interaction of resveratrol with the lipid membrane and its implications on the antioxidant properties are not thoroughly understood and remain a topic of debate. While a few reports indicate that resveratrol localizes at the surface,(25) a few others have suggested that it penetrates deep into the bilayer.(44) One of the reasons for these contradictions may be due to the different concentrations of resveratrol and techniques employed in each study. Many studies have employed fluorescence-based techniques and thermal analyses to study membrane interactions of resveratrol.(25)
    The electrochemical parameters such as current flow, RCT, admittance, and capacitance not only indicate changes in membrane fluidity but also provide insights into the mode of interaction of the molecule with the membrane components.(43) The cyclic voltammetric experiments suggest that the addition of resveratrol increases the bilayer transmembrane current in a dose-dependent manner, indicating a progressive increase in the number of defects in the bilayer packing that provide low resistance pathways for the flow of charges (Figure 2A). The complex admittance plot shows an initial decrease in the charge-transfer resistance. This may be attributed to the surface interaction between the polar hydroxyl groups in resveratrol and the polar headgroups of the phospholipids forming the bilayer at the membrane–electrolyte interface (Figures 2B and 3). As a result, a change in the packing of the headgroups is effected, opening up small gaps facilitating charge transport. Similar observations had been reported earlier for resveratrol using fluorescent probes and differential scanning calorimetry.(25) However, as the concentration of resveratrol increases, the charge transfer resistance increases implying a decrease in the membrane permeability. This indicates that the access to the conducting pathways is blocked on addition of higher concentrations of resveratrol. Resveratrol has a hydrophobic stilbene backbone and hence can penetrate the lipid bilayer and associate with the acyl chains through hydrophobic forces. The combination of the rigidity of the aromatic rings and strong hydrophobic attractions between resveratrol and the fatty acyl chains tends to block the conducting pathways, leading to the observed increase in the charge transfer resistance. The presence of polar hydroxyl groups in resveratrol prevents further penetration of the resveratrol into the hydrophobic core of the lipid bilayer, thus localizing the molecule in the hydrophobic region closer to the surface.
    The release of the fluorescent curcumin from liposomal bilayers by resveratrol in a dose-dependent manner can be attributed to competitive displacement of curcumin by resveratrol (Figure 4). Both resveratrol and curcumin are lipophilic and hence compete for the same localization sites within the lipid bilayer. On addition of resveratrol to the bath medium, it tends to displace curcumin from the hydrophobic region. Interestingly, the maximum amount of curcumin released in the first 20 min occurs on addition of 100 μM resveratrol and is less than 20%, indicating that the released curcumin is localized near the outer leaflet. These conclusions are supported by the NMR studies where it is observed that the 31P signal observed for blank liposomes does not shift in the case of resveratrol-loaded liposomes, thus ruling out the possibility of any resveratrol localization on the surface. The reduction in the broadening of the peak at 1.2 ppm in the 31P NMR spectrum of resveratrol loaded liposome also confirms the interaction of resveratrol with the hydrophobic regions of the liposome, leading to reduction in chemical anisotropy. Similar reduction in the peak width in the 31P NMR spectrum of dimyrisitoylphosphatidic acid liposomes has been reported earlier by Picard et al. (1996) due to the interaction of cardiotoxin with liposomal bilayers.(45) The disappearance of the downfield signals in the 13C spectrum for the resveratrol-loaded liposomes correlates with the localization in the outer leaflet of the lipid bilayer (Figures 5 and 6). Such localizations have also been proposed for the tea catechins(46) using model membranes, and our conclusions also agree with those drawn by Fabris et al. and Brittes et al.(34, 44)
    Erythrocyte morphology is highly sensitive to changes in the membrane rigidity. Alterations in the membrane fluidity or increase in membrane stiffness can contribute to activation of signaling pathways leading to enhancement in the cytosolic calcium ion levels.(47, 48) This influx in turn activates the enzyme calpain that lyses the cytoskeleton network, causing appearance of blebs on the surface of erythrocytes.(49) Extensive blebbing causes complete degradation of the erythrocytes.(49) The conclusions drawn from the electrochemical changes in the membrane brought about from addition of resveratrol and also from the membrane fluidization assays and the NMR studies suggest that the resveratrol is completely localized in the outer leaflet of the membrane at concentrations of 60 μM and above, and this would alter the membrane rigidity. This is reflected in the observed disruption of the erythrocytes. Another possible reason for the pronounced degradation that could be explored in the future might be the effect of resveratrol on the calcium ion influx. Many reports have suggested that resveratrol increases the cytosolic calcium levels in some cell types.(50) This might also serve to augment the activation of calpain leading to eryptosis (erythrocyte apoptosis). Thus, higher concentrations of resveratrol can alter membrane fluidity, drastically leading to the observed effects on the erythrocyte morphology.
    The antioxidant assays such as the DPPH assay mainly concentrate on assessing the potency of a molecule toward free radical scavenging.(51) In the case of resveratrol, the presence of phenolic rings facilitates formation of a quinone moiety after donating a hydrogen radical to DPPH as per the mechanism shown in Scheme 1. However, membrane interactions and localization of the antioxidant and free radical generator exert a major influence on the antioxidant and pharmacological activities, a fact that has not been extensively explored for resveratrol. At the plasma membrane level, the rate of lipid oxidation is mainly correlated with membrane fluidity and membrane composition. A previous report on resveratrol indicates that the alteration in the membrane fluidity by resveratrol significantly contributes to its ability to block oxidative damage to the membrane.(34) Similarly, our electrophysiological data also suggest that the membrane stability conferred by resveratrol in the presence of DPPH is due to its localization adjacent to the double bond region. A similar inference is also arrived at from the RBC membrane stabilization and solid state NMR studies. (Figures 2, 4–6, and 8).
    The DPPH radical is a stable free radical that can cause membrane lipid lysis. This is evident from the dramatic reduction in the membrane resistance observed on addition of DPPH. However, in the presence of resveratrol, DPPH is unable to reduce the resistance to the same extent. This indicates that the presence of resveratrol in the lipid bilayer prevents access of the lipid to the free radical and scavenges it. Higher concentrations of resveratrol (100 μM) were able to inhibit the deleterious effects of DPPH to a greater extent than lower concentrations (50 μM). As it is observed that the complete localization of resveratrol in the outer leaflet of the lipid bilayer occurs at higher concentrations, it is evident that this is a major contributor to effective free radical scavenging activity. This conclusion is also concurrent with earlier reports on the preventive action of isoflavone and flavonols against butyl peroxide induced liposomal damage.(52) Similar reports are also available for rutin, quercetin, hesperetin, and naringenin using liposome vesicles.(23) The intercalation of resveratrol in the membrane may also contribute to a reduction in the close packing of lipids, which may facilitate easy contact with the free radical and its quenching (Figures 9 and 10).
    The membrane interaction and free radical scavenging ability of resveratrol also contribute to its anticancer property. The cell viability assay suggests that resveratrol is cytotoxic to cancer cells, and the magnitude of cytotoxicity is maximal at 100 μM (Figure 11). Though many factors influence cytotoxicity, a small but substantial role may also be attributed to the ability of resveratrol to alter the membrane fluidity, which in turn can alter cell signaling pathways as well as cell trafficking.

    4 Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Membrane localization of resveratrol and its effect on the antioxidant property have been elucidated using electrochemical measurements on a planar lipid bilayer model. It was confirmed that resveratrol at low doses interacts with the surface polar groups and at higher doses localizes in the outer leaflet of the lipid bilayer. In accordance with reports on other polyphenols, it has been shown that the antioxidant property of resveratrol is determined by its localization in the membrane. Resveratrol blocks access of the oxidizing species to the lipids and scavenges the free radicals before they can penetrate the membrane. It has been demonstrated that the fluidity of the membrane also influences the antioxidant property and cytotoxicity of a molecule. It is possible that the membrane localization and interactions with the membrane components can also play a major role in determining the other pharmacological properties of this stilbene derivative.

    5.1 Materials

    trans-Resveratrol (a kind gift from Orchid Chemicals and Pharmaceuticals Ltd., India), potassium chloride (Merck, India), n-decane, and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) (Merck, Germany) were used for the bilayer studies. Curcumin (Sigma Aldrich, India) and diphenylpicrylhydrazyl radical (DPPH) (Sigma Aldrich, U.S.) were used for the antioxidant assay. Phospholipids for electrophysiology work were extracted from egg yolk using the modified Singleton–Gray method.(53) Egg phosphatidylcholine for solid state NMR analysis was purchased from Avanti Polar Lipids, U.S. The breast cancer cell line (MCF-7, Michigan Cancer Foundation 7) was purchased from National Centre for Cell Science (NCCS), Pune, India. Dulbecco’s modified Eagle medium (DMEM), fetal bovine serum (FBS), antibiotic (penicillin–streptomycin) were purchased from Gibco, India.

    5.2 Methods

    5.2.1 Preparation of Planar Lipid Bilayer

    A 2% (w/v) dispersion of phospholipids in n-decane was painted on an aperture of area 0.007 93 cm2 in a Perspex septum bifurcating two chambers of volume 3.5 mL each. All experiments were carried out in 0.1 M KCl bath medium, and measurements were recorded at room temperature (25 °C). The bilayer setup was kept on a vibration-isolated platform and shielded from electrical noise using a Faraday cage. The bilayer formation and attainment of stability for the lipid bilayer were confirmed from the constancy in the electrical parameters. The electrical measurements were carried out using an electrochemical workstation (CH Instruments 604C, U.S.).

    5.2.2 Electrochemical Measurements

    The cyclic voltammetric (CV) studies and admittance data were recorded using an electrochemical workstation (model 604C, CH Instruments, U.S.). A three-electrode system consisting of a platinum working electrode, Ag/AgCl (3 M KCl) reference electrode, and a platinum wire as the counter electrode was used for the measurements. A 1 GΩ resistor was introduced in series to the bilayer capacitor. An interaction time ranging from 20 to 30 min was allowed before measurement of electrical parameters in all cases.

    5.2.3 Membrane Fluidization Studies

    Membrane fluidization effect of resveratrol was studied using curcumin-loaded liposomes as reported earlier.(54) Liposomes were prepared by the thin-film hydration method. Briefly, an amount of 10 mg of phospholipids was purged with nitrogen to remove traces of solvent, suspended in 0.1 mM Tris-HCl buffer (pH 7.4), and constantly stirred for 2 h. The suspension was extruded 10 times using an extruder (Liposofast Basic, Avestin, Canada) through 200 nm polycarbonate membrane to obtain unilamellar vesicles. Resveratrol and curcumin were separately dissolved in DMSO in order to obtain a final concentration of 3.5 mM each. Liposomes in Tris-HCl buffer were incubated with 100 μM curcumin for 20 min in the dark at room temperature and centrifuged at 15 000 rpm to pelletize liposomes. The pellet was resuspended in 3.5 mL of Tris-HCl buffer and incubated with different concentrations (20–100 μM) of resveratrol individually for 20 min at room temperature.     5.2.4 Interactions of Resveratrol with Erythrocytes

    An amount of 0.5 mL of blood was collected from a healthy individual in 0.5 mL of 0.1 M EDTA solution. The red blood cells were separated at 1000 rpm in a refrigerated centrifuge (Remi, India) and washed with phosphate buffered saline solution (PBS) thrice to remove proteins and other cells. The cells were then serially diluted with 500 μL of PBS and incubated with DMSO for control and with 20, 40, 60, 80, and 100 μM resveratrol for samples. Then the cells were incubated at 37 °C for an hour and were fixed with 2% (v/v) glutaraldehyde in 400 μL of distilled water and stored at 4 °C for 20 h. The glutaraldehyde was removed by centrifugation at 1000 rpm, dried, and transferred to a brass stub and coated with platinum for imaging using a cold field emission scanning electron microscope (JSM 6701F, JEOL, Japan).(55)

    5.2.5 Solid State 31P and 13C Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) Spectroscopy

    The interaction of trans-resveratrol with lipid membrane was analyzed using solid-state 31P and 13C NMR (DSS spectra, 300 MHz Bruker, Germany). The analysis was performed at 10 kHz spinning speed for both 31P and 13C. Phosphoric acid was used as a standard for 31P NMR, and l-glycine was used as a standard for 13C NMR. Liposome samples for analysis were prepared by thin film hydration in a trans-resveratrol to egg phosphatidylcholine ratio of 1:40 (w/w). Resveratrol and lipid were dissolved in methanol and chloroform, respectively, and dried in vacuum. The thin film was hydrated using 1 M phosphate buffered saline (PBS) and stirred for 30 min at a temperature higher than the phase transition temperature of the lipid. The liposomes were extruded using an extruder (Liposofast Basic, Avestin, Canada) through a 200 nm polycarbonate membrane. The unencapsulated drug was then separated from the liposomes by centrifugation, and the pellet containing liposomes was freeze-dried to obtain the powdered form.

    5.2.6 1,1′-Diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) Assay

    Free radical experiments were performed using stable free radicals (DPPH).(51) Briefly, the reaction mixture containing 3.5 mL of 1,1′-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) radical and different concentrations of trans-resveratrol was added to obtain a final concentration of 20, 40, 60, 80, and 100 μM and then incubated at room temperature for 45 min. The absorbance of the test solutions was measured at 515 nm against a blank sample containing DPPH and compared with the well-known antioxidant vitamin C using a UV–visible spectrophotometer.(54)

    5.2.7 In Silico Modeling

    The mode of interaction of resveratrol with the model membrane composed of palmitoyloleoylphosphatidylcholine (POPC), palmitoyloleoylphosphatidylethanolamine (POPE), and cholesterol was analyzed using Hexdock online server. The structure of membrane was simulated using CHARMM-GUI interface online server and converted to PDB format. The structure of resveratrol was developed using CHEMDRAW (version 11) by applying energy minimizing mode.

    5.2.8 Cell Viability Assay

    The viability of MCF-7 (Michigan Cancer Foundation 7) breast cancer cell lines in resveratrol was determined in the presence of different concentrations (20–100 μM) of resveratrol in DMEM medium. Cells were seeded at a density of 104 cells per well in 96-well plates with DMEM medium and incubated at 37 °C in 5% CO2 for 24 h. The cells were treated with different concentrations of resveratrol and incubated for 48 h in a humidified chamber (Nuaire, U.S.). Then the cell viability was assessed using standard MTS assay procedure.

    5.2.9 Statistical Analysis

    Cell viability data were expressed as the mean ± SD. The significance between the groups was calculated using one way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s test; p < 0.05.
