    Abstract

    The Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway plays a vital role in cell growth, the regulation, cell development, and the differentiation of normal stem cells. Constitutive activation of the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway is found in many human cancers, and thus, it is an attractive target for anticancer therapy. Specific inhibitors of this pathway have been keenly researched and developed. Cell based screening of compounds library, hit-to-lead optimization, computational and structure-based design strategies resulted in the design and synthesis of a series of anthracene-9,10-dione dioxime series of compounds demonstrated potent inhibition of β-catenin in vitro (IC50 < 10 nM, 14) and the growth of several cancer cell lines. This article discusses the potential of inhibiting the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway as a therapeutic approach for cancer along with an overview of the development of specific inhibitors.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    β-Catenin is a member of the armadillo family of dual function protein involved in coordination of cell–cell adhesion and gene transcription. In humans, β-catenin is encoded by the CTNNB1 gene,(1, 2) a subunit of the cadherin protein complex, and acts as an intracellular signal transducer in the Wnt signaling pathway.(3-5) Increased intracellular β-catenin Wnt pathway associated levels occur in greater than 90% of colorectal cancers, approximately 50% of breast, lung, ovarian, Wilms cancers and one-quarter to one-third of melanomas, prostate, and gastric cancers.(6) In addition, misregulated Wnt signaling with accumulation of β-catenin in the nucleus has been associated with a number of hematologic malignancies, including acute myelogenous leukemia multiple myeloma, chronic myelogenous leukemia, chronic lymphocytic leukemia, and T-cell non-Hodgkin lymphomas.
    Although the Wnt/β-catenin pathway is crucial to cancer initiation and promotion, to date, no standard drugs were discovered to provide effective treatments. A few druggable targets are known in the pathway (such as GSK3, CK1, and CDK8), but these have functions in other cellular pathways that are essential to the life of a normal cell.(7)
    Currently, the most active compounds targeting the Wnt-pathway work primarily by inhibition of Wnt/β-catenin signaling by a variety of mechanisms at the receptor level or by stimulating the activity of the β-catenin destruction complex. Recently a number of different compound families have been identified that block or down-regulate the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway (Figure 1).(8-14) Similarly, the newly identified targets, including tankyrase, porcupine, and CK1α, were screened for small molecules with available compound libraries using cell based screening strategies(15) and unmodified cancer cell lines containing an activation mutation in the Wnt/β-catenin pathway.(16)
    Further, hit-to-lead optimization, computational and structure-based design strategies resulted in the design and synthesis of a series of anthracene-9,10-dione dioxime series of compounds that demonstrated potent inhibition of β-catenin in vitro (IC50 < 10 nM, 14, BC-2059) and strongly inhibited the growth of several cancer cell lines. ELISA analysis coupled with cell cycle, apoptosis, and TopFlash assays validate the effect of 14 on β-catenin nuclear levels, resulting in the inhibition of Wnt-mediated gene expression. These data, together with xenograft studies, strongly promote 14 as a candidate for treatment for a wide spectrum of epithelial and hematological malignancies by inhibition of oncogenic β-catenin pathway signaling.

    Initial Leads

    Disruption of the β-catenin signaling pathway was detected using a novel biomarker profile based high-throughput transcriptional screening platform.(16) We screened a library of more than 200 000 small molecules in the HT-29 colon cancer cell line using a 9-gene biomarker signature and identified a spectrum of compounds that inhibited β-catenin pathway activity. Several compounds in the 9H-fluoren-9-one, 9H-fluoren-9-one oxime, and 2,7-disubstituted 9H-fluoren-9-one oxime chemotypes (compounds 1–4, Figure 2) were identified as potent and selective inhibitors of the β-catenin pathway.(17) Eleven compounds that contained isosteres of −C═O, an oxime functionality (compounds 2, 3, and 4), were identified in the screening library with activities ranging from 740 nM to 10 μM potency in growth inhibition assays in HT-29. Compounds containing a carbonyl group, such as 2,7-bis((4-methylpiperidin-1-yl)sulfonyl)-9H-fluoren-9-one (1), are inactive. We designed and synthesized over 100 analogues(17, 18) of compound 3 and screened these in our biomarker profile and in growth inhibition assays. Compounds were identified with activities between 0.5 and 10 μM. Although these early lead compounds show good cellular activity in inhibiting β-catenin pathway signaling and provided the mechanistic proof-of-concept in down-regulating β-catenin protein levels,(19) their properties were suboptimal because of poor solubility and stability.

    Structure–Activity Relationship of Initial Leads and Lead Optimization

    The optimization and structure–activity relationship (SAR) efforts on hits from the β-catenin primary screen provided 3 actives out of 11 from the screening library. The core scaffold 2,7-disubstituted-9H-fluoren-9-one oxime was undertaken for further evaluation because of the inactive (>10 μM in β-catenin depletion assay ) non-oxime 2,7-disubstituted 9H-fluoren-9-one series compound 1 (Figure 1), despite having the key sulfonyl group at the 2,7-position. Particularly, compounds 2 and 3 based lead optimization strategy led to the discovery of a new member of the 2,7-disubstituted anthracene-9,10-dione dioxime which is symmetrically substituted with heterocycloalkyl moieties at R1 and R2 positions (Figure 3). In the case of compound 4, from our SAR studies we noted that the presence of six-membered aryl ring either directly substituted to the sulfonyl functionality or having constrained carbon spacer (-C-methyl) lost its activity completely (IC50 > 10 μM), indicating the importance of a heterocycloalkyl moieties, size, and its hydrophobic binding pocket. We then chose to preserve the most important structural features of a tricyclic central core such as 9H-fluoren-9-one oxime (3), 9H-xanthen-9-one oxime (5), 10,11-dihydro-5H-dibenzo[a,d][7]annulen-5-one oxime (6), acridin-9(10H)-one oxime (7) (Figure 3), and solvent binding symmetrical sulfonyl functionality substituted by heterocycloalkyl moieties (compounds 2 and 3) while exploring the effects of alterations at the 2, 7 sites of the core scaffold molecule.(17, 18) Interestingly, the compound series 6 and 7 lost its activity completely, whereas 9H-xanthen-9-one oxime (5) series having six-, seven-, or eight-membered ring heterocycloalkyl groups at 2, 7 positions exhibited low micromolar IC50 in our HT-29 cancer cells and in β-catenin depletion assays (540 nM to 10 μM). These active mono-oxime series of compounds were not considered for further development because of their poor solubility and pharmacokinetic properties.

    Symmetrical Anthracene-9,10-dione Dioxime Series

    The preliminary structure–activity relationships based on three key pharmacophores we considered in our analogue design suggested a possible hypothetical model for these dioxime series of compounds and their interactions with a binding partner depicted in Figure 4. Since there is no physical or structural binding data available during our optimization process, the tight hydrophobic and electrostatic interactions were considered for the design of novel oxime series of compounds. As a strategy to lead optimization, we considered the original 2,7-disubstituted 9H-fluoren-9-one oxime and or 9H-xanthen-9-one oxime active hits. We then modified the central pyran ring oxygen atom of 9H-xanthen-9-one oxime 5 by substituting with a second oxime functionality, leading to the identification symmetrical anthracene-9,10-dione dioxime series of compounds. On the basis of these considerations, specifically compounds with the general structure of 8 were explored (Figure 3). To reduce molecular weight, we chose the  six- or seven-membered ring heterocycloalkyl group on 2,7-disubstituted anthracene-9,10-dione dioxime ring A and ring B with heteroatoms such as −N or −O to increase the solvent binding affinity. Since R1 and R2 groups project toward the solvent binding site, we used a variety of basic and polar groups to explore their effects on binding, solubility, and pharmacokinetic (PK) properties. Particularly the compounds having six-, seven-membered heterocycloalkyl rings substituted with methyl, tert-butyl, and dimethyl functional groups demonstrated potent activity both in β-catenin depletion assay with an IC50 between 60 and 190 nM in HT-29 cell lines (14, 23, 35, and 51). With an exception of compound 26, the compounds having bulkier 7–12 ring cycloalkyl groups exhibited low micromolar activity in HT-29 transcriptional inhibition. Additionally, our attempts to improve the solubility by replacing oxime with keto functionality and introducing second heteroatoms at the cycloalkyl rings resulted in complete loss of activity (17, 48). Incorporation of piperidine sulfonyl groups at position 6 of anthracene ring is well tolerated (54, 57); unfortunately these compounds suffered from poor solubility. On the basis of these considerations, 2,7-substituted dioxime series of compounds were explored. The symmetrical 3,5-dimethyl substituted piperidine analogs, particularly compound 14, have been profiled extensively in many cancer cell lines.
    Compound 14 showed potent growth inhibition in HT-29 treated cells with an IC50 of 90 nM. However, the inhibition was much less in its transcriptional inhibition of HT-29 cells (IC50, 630 nM). With its promising β-catenin depletion and cellular activities, compound 14 was profiled for its ADME-Tox experiments. The log D and solubility of 14 were found to be 3.3 and 1 μg/mL, and the in vitro hERG safety profile indicated no cardiovascular liability. The additional safety screening includes CYP2C9 (5.62 μM), and no P-gp activities were noted. Compound 14 possessed significant plasma protein binding with 99.8% and was further assessed for exposure in blood from single-dose mouse PK and in vivo efficacy experiments.
    The general syntheses exemplified by these compounds were shown in Figure 5 and Scheme 1. The 9,10-dioxo-9,10-dihydroanthracene-2,7-disulfonyl dichloride 9 was reacted with the substituted heterocycloalkyl 6–12 membered ring structure in the presence of basic or other solvents; triethylamine (TEA), diisopropylethylamine (DIPEA), pyridine in DCM, chloroform, THF, or acetonitrile solvents were used for 30 min to 4 h to give the series of anthracene-9,10-dione compounds of general structure 10 in quantitative yields. In the final step, the excess of hydroxylamine hydrochloride and pyridine at 95 °C for 16–36 h afforded the complete conversion of anthracene-9,10-dione compounds to racemic oxime 11.
    The racemic lead oxime 14 was then prepared by treating compound 11 with hydroxylamine hydrochloride in pyridine for 16 h at 115 °C, providing 99.6% as white solid. Compound 14 was isolated as enantiomerically pure (3R,5S)-3,5-dimethylpiperidine compound (Scheme 2). Because of the symmetric nature of oximes, it was not feasible to assign its E/Z, E/E isomerism at 9 and 10 positions. Representative compounds of 14 in this series and their biological data are reported in Table 1. To assess the potency of the new analogues as inhibitors of the β-catenin signaling pathway, we used two assays: growth inhibition in a β-catenin dependent colon cancer cell line (HT-29); a gene-expression-based biomarker profile signature that reports on β-catenin pathway activity.(15, 16, 19a, 19b, 20, 21) With the exception of compounds 23 and 24, the N-methylpiperazine, morpholin and piperazine substitutions at 2,7-disulfonamide site lost its cellular activity in both assays. The bulky hydrophobic cycloalkyl ring substitutions tolerated and were found to be very potent β-catenin pathway inhibitors with IC50 ranging from 0.02 to 3.90 μM (Table 1) against the HT-29 colon adenocarcinoma cell line. Both oxime and sulfonyl groups at the 2 and 7 positions of the anthracene ring are well tolerated with 7–12 ring hydrophobic moieties. As previously discussed,(17, 18) these oxime(22) series of compounds are stereospecific in their activity on β-catenin signaling. The absolute configuration is critical for potency, as the active enantiomer of compound 14 (3R, 5S) was about 100-fold more potent (IC50 of 7 nM) than the other enantiomers under the same assay conditions. Compound 14 was further characterized despite its low solubility of <1 μg/mL in PBS, due to its potent cellular activity to inhibit β-catenin signaling and cancer cell growth.

    In Vitro Inhibition Property of 14

    Initial tests were performed on the growth and viability of cultured cancer cells. We used a panel of 47 cell lines including colorectal, lung, ovarian, breast, and pancreatic, renal, and hematologic cancers.(22) Majority of the cell lines were sensitive to 14 at concentration of <0.2 μM, and others such as colorectal, HCT-15, HT-29, HCT-116, or adrenal carcinoma H296R cell lines were characterized by high levels of β-catenin expression, where 14 exhibited with an IC50 of 0.030, 0.09, 0.020, and 0.01 μM, respectively (Figure 6, Supplemental Figure 11B). FACS analysis found that the cancer cell lines were arrested in the G1 phase of the cell cycle(23) by 14. Continuous treatment with inhibitor resulted in the induction of apoptosis within 48–72 h. The percentage of cells arrested in the G1 phase and in apoptosis was increased in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 7). Secondary biological assays show that 14 potently inhibits β-catenin transcriptional activity in a promoter reporter assay, and that selectively down-regulates β-catenin protein levels (Figure 7, Figure 8A, and Figure 8B). No activity was found against other cellular pathways such as STAT3, thyroid receptor, RAR, and RXR using similar promoter reporter assays. Weak inhibition of NFkB reporter assays was found at 10–100 times lower potency than β-catenin pathway inhibition.(19b) The target specificity of 14 was further validated by using HEK-293 cells with Wnt/β-catenin activated pathway (STF3A). The cells transfected with Wnt3A show higher levels of apoptosis when compared to that of empty vector (Supporting Information Figure 10). To further validate these findings, we analyzed the effect of 14 on TCF/LEF activity in wild type STF cells transfected with luciferase reporter gene assay.(24) To stimulate Wnt signaling in wild type STF cell line, cells were treated for 12 h with Wnt 3a-conditioned medium (CM) collected from STF3A cells constitutively Wnt-activated, followed by treatment with 1 μM 14. As shown in Figure 8C, the effect of 14 on STF Wnt3a-CM-induced TopFlash activity is significant (P = 0.0001) while it does not reach statistical significance in wild type STF cells, suggesting that 14 induces preferential inhibitory effect on Wnt3a signaling.
    Both the binding and cellular assays support that the 14 as a preclinical lead exhibited potency and excellent selectivity in down-regulating the β-catenin protein levels in a proteasome dependent manner.

    Effect of 14 on Tumor Growth

    Our first findings shown that 14 promotes a strong growth inhibitory effect among all cell types, with the half maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) in the range of 10–50 nM. Since the Wnt signaling pathway is a key component of cell adhesion and invasion, we performed growth analysis in three-dimentional cultures using Matrigel.(25) Organoids generated from HCT-15 and H296R cell lines showed similar growth inhibition after 5–8 days of treatment with 14 (Figure 9A). Moreover, ex vivo Matrigel invasion assay using organoids generated from biopsies of colon, breast, and prostate cancer patients indicates that primary patient derived cancer cells are highly sensitive to 14 treatment (Figure 9A).
    Single dose pharmacokinetics studies show high levels of 14 exposure (up to 10 000 nM) with a terminal half-life of 4–6 h and exposure of excess of 1000 nM (concentration over time) for 12 h (Supporting Information Figure 12). These studies suggest that pharmacologically relevant concentrations can be maintained for greater than 12 h after a single dose of compound and that these levels are well tolerated in long-term exposure studies.
    Finally, to assess the therapeutic potential of 14 in vivo, we tested in subcutaneous colon cancer xenografts model using SW480 cells in nude mice. Transplant recipient animals with established tumors were treated with 35 mg/kg po 14 for 21 days, and on the terminal day, the mice were sacrificed for analysis. Our in vivo data demonstrated that the 14 treatment resulted in tumor growth inhibition compared to controls. Treatment was well tolerated as shown by stable body weights through the treatment (Figure 9B and Supporting Information Figure 13). As per the University of Utah and Huntsman Cancer institutional review assessing safety and humane usage, guidelines were followed for all animal studies.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we have synthesized a novel anthracene-9,10-dione dioxime series of compounds, particularly 14’s potential to be an effective agent for the treatment for a wide spectrum of epithelial and hematological malignancies through the inhibition mechanism of oncogenic Wnt/β-catenin pathway signaling. Our study showed that 14 efficiently modulates β-catenin transcriptional activity, β-catenin cellular levels, and cancer cell growth in vitro and in vivo. Further, by blocking the disrupted signaling that promotes both at the earliest steps in carcinogenesis as well as metastasis and the maintenance of the malignant phenotype, 14 may be effective at all stages of a treatment regime, including metastatic disease and prevention and reversal of premalignant, hyperproliferative lesions, such as colonic polyps.

    General Chemistry

    All reagents and solvents were purchased from commercial suppliers (Sigma-Aldrich) and used without further purification unless otherwise stated. Solvents were dried using standard procedures, and reactions requiring anhydrous conditions were performed under N2 atmosphere. Reactions were monitored by thin layer chromatography (TLC). 1H NMR spectra were recorded on a Varian Unity 400 instrument. Chemical shifts (δ) are reported in ppm downfield from solvent references. Mass spectra were obtained on a Finnigan LCQ Duo LCMS ion trap electrospray ionization (ESI) mass spectrometer. The purity of the newly synthesized compounds was determined by LC/MS and HPLC. All final compounds were purified to ≥95% purity (99.10% for 14) for all the biologically tested compounds as determined by Aq. Instrument LC1 high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) with UV detection at 250–500 nm using the following methods. Solvent methods A and B: A, 0.1% TFA; B, 0.1% TFA, CH3CN 80% in H2O with an injection volume 1 μL at 37 °C. Higgins Proto200 C18, 4.6 mm × 150 mm column was used with 1.0 μL/min flow rate at 25 min. Melting points were determined on a Buchi M-560 melting point apparatus and are uncorrected.

    General Methods for the Synthesis of Anthracene-9,10-dione Dioxime Analogues(17, 18)

    The compounds presented herein were prepared from the corresponding sulfonyl chloride derivatives according to the general synthetic pathway depicted in Scheme 1, and the specific reaction schemes for compounds 14–60 (Table 1) given in Figure 5 and Supporting Information.

    Synthetic Procedures. 2,7-Bis(((3R,5S)-3,5-dimethylpiperidin-1-yl)sulfonyl)anthracene-9,10-dione Dioxime (14, Scheme 2, Figure 5)

    9,10-Dioxo-9,10-dihydroanthracene-2,7-disulfonyl dichloride 9 (665 mg, 1.641 mmol) was dissolved in 80 mL of dichloromethane was cooled to −50 °C and was added (3S,5R)-3,5-dimethylpiperidine hydrochloride 12 (372 mg, 3.28 mmol), followed by the addition of 1.14 mL (8.21 mmol, 5 equiv) of triethylamine (TEA). The resulting reaction mixture was stirred for 30 min at room temperature for 1 h. After completion of the reaction (was monitored by TLC), the solvents were evaporated and the obtained residue was treated with 1 N HCl, filtered, washed with water, and dried. The crude material was crystallized from chloroform–hexane (4:6 ratio) and afforded the 2,7-bis-(((3R,5S)-3,5-dimethylpiperidin-1-yl)sulfonyl)anthracene-9,10-dione compound 13 as a yellow solid (725 mg, 79%). A mixture of compound 13 (725 mg, 1.302 mmol) in 5 mL of pyridine (4.92 mmol) and hydroxylamine hydrochloride (1.140 g, 16.41 mmol) was stirred at 115 °C for 16 h. Pyridine was evaporated, and the crude residue was stirred with 1 N HCl (25 mL) for 15 min. The obtained crude white product was collected by filtration, washed with water, and dried. The dried crude material was then crystallized from DCM–hexane to give 99.6% of title compound 14 as white solid (956 mg). 1H NMR (400 MHz, MeOH-d4): δ 9.21 (d, 1H), 8.92 (d, 1H), 8.41–8.23 (m, 2H), 7.88–7.71 (m, 2H), 3.81 (m, 4H), 2.12 (m, 12H), 0.98 (m, 12H). ESI-MS: m/z calcd for C28H36N4O6S2 588.74, found 589.26 [M + H]. Mp 287 (°C). HPLC purity 99.10%.

    Biology. Cells and Cell Viability Assay

    Human colon cancer cell lines HCT-15, HT-29, HCT-116, SW-480, lung cancer cell line A549, and breast cancer cell line MCF7 (ATCC) were cultured in RPMI (Invitrogen) containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (GIBCO) and antibiotics (penicillin and streptomycin) (Invitrogen) at 37 °C in a 5% CO2 condition. Human STF and STF3A (generously donated by Dr. David Virshup, Duke NUS Graduate Medical School) were maintained in DMEM (Invitrogen) supplemented with 10% FBS and antibiotics. Human adrenalcorticoid carcinoma H296R cells were cultured in DMEM–F12 (1:1) (Invitrogen) containing ITS (BD Biosciences). Cell viability was determined by Cell Titer Glo assay (Promega). Cells were seeded at 3 × 103 cells/well in a 96-well plate and allowed to attach overnight at 37 °C. The cells were incubated with increasing concentration of 14 (0 to 100 μM). After 72 h, CTG was added and the absorbance measured at 570 nm with an EnVision 2000 instrument (PerkinElmer). EC50 was calculated using GraphPad software. All experiments were conducted in triplicate.

    ELISA and Cell Cycle Assay. Matrigel Invasion and in Vivo Study

    Matrigel invasion assay was performed following ref 20. Organoids from cell lines were obtained seeding the cells in low affinity binding plates (Constar). 1 ×106 cells/mL were seeded in 24-well plates and incubated at 37 °C. Cancer and general uninvolved (GU) tissues from patients were treated with collagenase (Sigma) for 16 h at 4 °C. The cells were washed with MBGM medium and seeded in low affinity binding plate to form organoids at 1 × 106 cells/well. After 24 h, the cells aggregated to forms organoids that were separated from single cells by gravity. Approximately 400 organoids/well were seeded in a solution containing 80% Matrigel in 96-well plates in appropriate medium. After 24 h, organoids were incubated with increasing concentrations of 14 (0–100 μM). After 72 h, MTT was added and the absorbance measured at 570 nm with an EnVision 2000 instrument (PerkinElmer). EC50 was calculated using GraphPad software. All experiments were conducted in triplicate.

    ELISA, Cell Cycle Assay, and Apoptosis Assay

    Apoptosis assay was performed using the annexin V–FITC apoptosis detection kit (Abcam) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. HT-29 cells were seeded in a T25 flask at a final concentration of 1 × 106 cells. After 24 h at 37 °C, the cells were incubated with 14 for 48 h. The cells were harvested and resuspended in binding buffer. After addition of annexin V–FITC and propidium iodine, the samples were analyzed by flow cytometry (Ex/Em = 488/530 nm). Cell cycle assay was performed with the cell cycle assay kit, Red Fluorimetric (Abcam). Briefly, after treatment with 14 for 72 h, 1 × 106 cells/mL were fixed with 70% ethanol and incubated with 100× Nuclear Red CCS1 and RNase A, followed by flow cytometer analysis at Ex/Em = 490/620 nm. β-Catenin ELISA kit (Invitrogen Life Technologies) was used to quantify the level of β-catenin from lysates from HT-29 cells after a 72 h treatment with 14. Equal amounts of lysates from untreated and 14 treated cells were incubated in precoated 96-well plate with antibody specific for β-catenin, followed by a secondary antibody horseradish peroxidase conjugated. ELISA was developed by addition of TMB HRP substrate, and the optical density (OD) for each well was read with a microplate reader set to 450 nm.

    Localized Xenograft Models

    SW-480 cells were injected subcutaneously into the flank of 6- to 8-week-old female nu/nu mice at a concentration of 5 × 106 in 100 μL. When tumors were established and clearly palpable (50–100 mm3), mice were randomized into two groups to maximize uniformity. To test whether 14 was therapeutically active, mice were treated with 35 mg/kg po for 3 weeks. Treatment schedule for 14 was every 5 days with 2 days off for three cycles. Mice were monitored daily, and tumor growth was measured 2 times per week with a digital caliper using the following formula: volume = length × (width)2 × 0.5. Animals were euthanized when the tumor reached a volume greater than 2000 mm3.

    Luciferase Assay

    For T-cell factor (TCF) transcriptional activity assay, STF and STF3A cells were seeded at 70% confluency in 24-well plates (Constar). After 24 h, the cells were treated with 1 μM 14, 1 μM inactive analogue BC-9654B, and DMSO as control for 0, 4, 8, 12, and 24 h at 37 °C. After incubation, the cells were washed with PBS and 100 μL of 1× lysis reagent CCLR (PROMEGA) was dispensed in each well. The lysates were collected from the plate and transferred into microcentrifuge tubes. After a single freeze–thaw cycle, the samples were centrifuged at 12 000 rpm for 2 min at 4 °C. Supernatant protein concentrations were determined by Bradford assay, and an amount of 20 μL of equilibrated samples was seeded in 96-well OPtiPlates (PerkinElmer) and incubated with 100 μL of luciferase assay reagent (Promega). To examine the effect of 14 in Wnt3a treated STF cell line, cells were transfected with luciferase constructs and treated with Wnt3a conditioned medium (CM) prepared as described below, for 12 h, followed by treatment with 1 μM 14. The assay was evaluated with EnVision 2000 instrument (PerkinElmer). For transient transfection, 4 × 105 HCT-15 cells were seeded into 96-well plates and transfected using Attractene reagent according to the manufacturer’s protocol (Qiagen). 100 ng/well of NFkB reporter plasmid or the negative control luciferase (Promega) was added, and after 24 h the cells were treated with 100 nM 14 for 0, 3, 6, and 12 h. NFkB transcriptional activity was determined by the ratio NFkB/LUC negative luciferase activity, and each sample was normalized to the Renilla luciferase activity (co-transfected as internal control).

    Wnt3a Conditioned Medium Preparation

    STF3A cells were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum for 4 days (approximately to confluence). The medium was harvested and sterile filtered. A second batch of fresh culture medium was harvested after 3 days, and the cells were discarded because they were overgrown. The two medium batches were mixed 1:1 and stored at 4 °C.
