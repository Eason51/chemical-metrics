    Abstract

    The non-natural cyclic amino acids (1S,3R,4S)-1-amino-3-fluoro-4-(fluoro-18F)cyclopentane-1-carboxylic acid ([18F]9) and (1S,3S,4R)-1-amino-3-fluoro-4-(fluoro-18F)cyclopentane-1-carboxylic acid ([18F]28) have been prepared in 10 and 1.7% decay corrected radiochemical yield, respectively, and in greater than 99% radiochemical purity. Cell assays in rat 9L gliosarcoma, human U87 ΔEGFR glioblastoma, and human DU145 androgen-independent prostate carcinoma tumor cells indicated that both compounds are substrates for amino acid transport primarily by system L, with some transport taking place via system ASC. In rats with 9L gliosarcoma, [18F]9 and [18F]28 provided high tumor to normal brain tissue ratios, with maximal ratios of 3.5 and 4.1, respectively. Biodistribution studies in healthy rats confirmed that both compounds are BBB permeable and that bladder accumulation is low until at least 5 min post injection.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Over the past several decades, positron emission tomography (PET) has become an essential imaging modality in clinical oncology for the visualization of various cancers. The accurate visualization of neoplasia via PET is contingent upon the ability of a particular PET tracer to preferentially accumulate in tumor cells in the presence of healthy tissue. Among the strategies identified for achieving selective uptake in neoplasia, tracers that mimic metabolic intermediates have proven to be the most successful.(1) For example, 2-[18F]fluoro-2-deoxy-D-glucose ([18F]FDG), a substrate for glucose transporters, is the most widely used tracer for oncologic applications and has been applied to the visualization of a broad variety of neoplasia.(2,3) However, there remain applications for which [18F]FDG-PET is poorly suited, owing to its high physiological uptake in the normal tissue of certain organs, such as the brain and bladder, as well as its propensity to concentrate in inflammatory lesions.(4,5) Consequently, PET imaging of brain and prostate cancers is often problematic, and the ability to adequately visualize these types of neoplasia remains a critical unmet need.
    To address the shortcomings associated with [18F]FDG-PET, alternative metabolic tracers are of interest.(6) Like carbohydrates, amino acids (AAs) are involved in nearly all biological processes and are energy substrates necessary for cell growth, and most tumor cells display greater avidity for certain AAs relative to normal tissue, owing to the increased expression of amino acid transporters (AATs) in neoplastic cells.(7,8) Each of the ubiquitous AATs, which includes those from systems L (leucine preferring, sodium independent), A (alanine preferring, sodium dependent), and ASC (alanine, serine, cysteine, and threonine preferring, sodium dependent),(9) is generally upregulated to some degree in neoplasia, though only system L transporter LAT1 mediates significant amino acid transport into the brain.(10) Accordingly, radiolabeled substrates for system L offer potential as radiopharmaceuticals. For example, 11C-methionine, which is actively transported primarily by system L, has demonstrated efficacy over [18F]FDG in imaging glioma.(11−13) However, the short half-life of the 11C radionuclide (20 min) and the propensity of 11C-methionine and natural AAs in general to be metabolized or engage in protein synthesis preclude their broad clinical use.(14) By contrast, non-natural AAs offer a platform for the incorporation of the 18F radionuclide and can be engineered for metabolic stability. Several decades ago, Washburn and co-workers investigated a series of 14C labeled alicyclic alpha-amino acids as PET tracers and identified 1-aminocyclobutane carboxylic acid (ACBC)(15,16) and 1-aminocyclopentane carboxylic acid (ACPC)(17,18) as compounds with excellent in vivo stability and biological transport properties. Goodman and co-workers later described the synthesis and biological evaluation of several 18F radiolabeled ACBC derivatives.(19) The transport of each compound was assessed in 9L gliosarcoma cells and determined to proceed via system ASC, with some contribution from system L. In rats bearing intracranial 9L gliosarcoma, each 18F-FACBC derivative studied provided superior tumor to contralateral brain tissue uptake in comparison to [18F]FDG. Notably, anti-1-amino-3-(fluoro-18F)-cyclobutane carboxylic acid (anti-3-FACBC) received FDA approval for imaging nonprimary recurrent prostate cancer.(20) In 2011, the first fluorinated ACPC, anti-1-amino-2-fluoro-cyclopentane-1-carboxylic acid ((R, S)-anti-2-FACPC), was reported as a racemic mixture.(21) In contrast to FACBC analogs, (R, S)-anti-2-FACPC displayed a greater affinity for transport by system L, with some transport occurring via system ASC. In the 9L gliosarcoma rat model, (R, S)-anti-2-FACPC provided a twofold increase in the tumor to contralateral brain tissue ratio relative to anti-3-FACBC. Despite these promising preclinical data, a pilot study in humans with the enantiomer (1R, 2S)-anti-2-FACPC, obtained by chiral HPLC purification, demonstrated that it concentrates in the bladder at early time points, limiting its utility in the detection of prostate tumors.(22) We postulated that the inductive effect of the fluorine substituent beta to the amine and carboxylate moieties may contribute to the early bladder accumulation of (1R, 2S)-anti-2-FACPC. To alleviate this effect, we considered alternative fluorinated derivatives of FACPC wherein the fluorine atom is further removed from the amine and carboxylate moieties. While 1-amino-3-fluorocyclopentane carboxylic acid (3-FACPC) is a logical target based on the consideration presented above, we expected that the synthesis and isolation of a single stereoisomer would be challenging, given that there are two pairs of enantiomers (i.e., one for each of the 3-FACPC diastereomers). Conversely, the difluorinated ACPC derivative, 1-amino-3,4-difluorocyclopentanecarboxylic acid (3,4-DFACPC), exists as one set of trans-difluoro enantiomers and two discrete meso-cis-difluoro diastereomers, the latter of which would be synthetically accessible as single stereoisomers. Consequently, we aimed to prepare both cis-difluoro stereoisomers of 3,4-DFACPC and evaluate their utility as PET imaging agents, as described below.

    Chemistry

    From the outset, we envisioned that both of the cis diastereomers of 3,4-DFACPC would be synthetically accessible from certain N- and O-protected derivatives of 1-aminocyclopent-3-ene-1-carboxylic acid.(23−25) Our initial efforts toward the synthesis of a derivative of this olefin began from hippuric acid, which was converted to the N-benzamide derivative of the desired cyclopentene 4 in a four-step sequence culminating in a Grubbs ring-closing metathesis from diene 3.(23) Treatment of 4 with m-CPBA provided the syn-epoxide 5 (throughout the manuscript, ″syn″ is relative to the N-atom) in 78% yield, and after another four-step sequence involving ring-opening fluorination of the corresponding racemic fluorohydrin and acidic deprotection to free the amine and carboxylic acid functionalities, the cold standard 9 was obtained in 4% overall yield (Scheme 1). The assignment of this compound as the anti-cis-3,4-DFACPC stereoisomer was confirmed via X-ray crystallography (Figure 1).
    Though we were able to access difluorocyclopentane 9 using this route, we determined that the radiosynthesis of [18F]9 would be complicated by the slow cleavage of the benzamide moiety. Alternatively, benzamide 6 can be converted to the corresponding N-Boc derivative, though this protecting group manipulation requires another three steps and proceeds in only 50% yield (Scheme 2).
    Consequently, we employed an alternative route to cyclopentene 15, originally reported by Kurth,(24,25) which provided the N-Boc protected cyclopentene 16 in only four steps from glycine ethyl ester hydrochloride. Compound 16 was treated with m-CPBA to afford a 5:1 mixture of syn and anti-epoxides, which were readily separated by flash chromatography. Once separated, syn epoxide 17 was converted to racemic fluorohydrin 12 in the presence of triethylamine trihydrofluoride and then treated with triflic anhydride to afford racemic triflate 19, which is the radiolabeling precursor to anti-cis-3,4-DFACPC ([18F]9), in 28% yield over seven steps (Scheme 3).
    We next turned our attention to the synthesis of syn-cis-3,4-DFACPC. It was anticipated that this compound may be accessible from anti-epoxide 18, the minor diastereomer generated from the epoxidation of cyclopentene 15. However, we found that racemic triflate 21 was highly unstable, and our attempts to isolate it or use it in subsequent reactions without isolation met with failure (Scheme 4).
    Given that racemic triflate 19, in which the N-Boc and triflate moieties exist in a syn disposition, is chromatographically stable, we speculated that the anti stereochemical relationship between the N-Boc and triflate moieties in 21 might give rise to an intramolecular decay pathway stemming from the nucleophilicity of the Boc group. Consequently, as shown in Scheme 5, we targeted the synthesis of an analogous triflate with the less nucleophilic N-phthaloyl protecting group. To this end, cyclopentene 15 was treated with phthalic anhydride to give phthalimide 22 in 67% yield. Epoxidation of 22 was affected with m-CPBA, affording a 2.6:1 mixture of syn and anti diastereomers that were separable by flash chromatography, and the anti-epoxide 23 was isolated in 25% yield. After ring-opening fluorination of epoxide 23, the resultant racemic fluorohydrin 25 was treated with triflic anhydride to give a stable, crystalline racemic triflate (26), which is the precursor to [18F]syn-cis-DFACPC, in 85% yield. Fluorination of this triflate proved challenging, owing to the propensity of this compound to undergo elimination rather than substitution. Ultimately, CsF in tBuOH proved to be a viable fluorination system,(26) providing difluoride 27 in 18% yield. Acidic deprotection of difluoride 27 afforded syn-cis-3,4-DFACPC (28), although this reaction required several hours at elevated temperature to reach completion and therefore would not be suitable for the radiosynthesis of [18F]28. After surveying a variety of conditions, it was found that the phthalimide and ethyl ester protecting groups could be rapidly and efficiently cleaved by treating 27 with 2 M NaOH for 5 min at 140 °C followed by addition of an equal volume of conc. HCl for a further 15 min at 140 °C, affording 28 in 88% yield (Scheme 5). The stereochemical assignment of 28 was confirmed via X-ray crystallography (Figure 2).

    Radiochemistry

    The synthesis of anti-cis-3,4-[18F]-DFACPC ([18F]9) was accomplished under the conditions previously reported for the automated synthesis of [18F]FACBC.(27) Triflate precursor 19 (9 mg) was radiofluorinated by no-carrier-added nucleophilic substitution using dried K[18F]F, potassium carbonate, and Kryptofix 222 in acetonitrile followed by deprotection with 6 N HCl (Scheme 6). The crude mixture obtained from the labeling reaction was loaded directly onto a purification assembly consisting of an ion retard resin cartridge, alumina cartridge, and HLB Oasis reverse phase cartridge and eluted with saline solution into fractions. The fractions containing the highest concentrations of [18F]9 were pooled and used directly in subsequent in vivo and in vitro studies. The radiochemical decay-corrected yield (dcy) was 10% (50 mCi) based on a synthesis time of 70 min. Radiometric TLC of [18F]9 showed a single radiolabeled product in a radiochemical purity >99% (see the Supporting Information: Figure S35), with an Rf corresponding to compound 9. The specific activity of [18F]9 in the dose vial was not measured, although given that 9 mg of the triflate precursor was employed in the radiosynthesis, the maximum amount of unlabeled material in the final dose would not exceed 180 μg/mCi.
    Conversely, the synthesis of syn-cis-3,4-DFACPC ([18F]28) did not proceed under the established [18F]FACBC synthesis conditions, likely owing to the propensity of triflate precursor 26 to eliminate, as observed in the cold chemistry. However, the milder fluorination protocol described by Kim and coworkers,(26) employing CsF in a mixture of tBuOH and MeCN with Kryptofix 222, was effective in the radiolabeling of triflate 26 to provide [18F]27 (Scheme 7). Once generated, [18F]27, as a solution in acetonitrile, was passed through two alumina cartridges to remove residual [18F–] and then concentrated. The concentrate was treated with 2 M NaOH in a sealed vial at 140 °C and then with concentrated HCl, also at 140 °C. On completion, the mixture was evaporated under air flow and taken up in water and then passed through an HLB Oasis cartridge and two alumina cartridges. The eluent was collected in fractions, the most concentrated of which were pooled to provide a 1.7% dcy (8.4 mCi) based on a synthesis time of 135 min. Radiometric HPLC analysis indicated the presence of a single radiolabeled product with >99% radiochemical purity (see the Supporting Information: Figure S36). Again, the specific activity of the [18F]28 thus prepared was not measured, though given that 20 mg of triflate precursor 26 was used for the labeling reaction and a radiochemical yield of 8.4 mCi was obtained, the amount of unlabeled material remaining in the dose vial would not exceed 2.3 mg/mCi.

    Cellular Transport Assays

    The cellular transport of [18F]9 and [18F]28 in rat 9L gliosarcoma, human U87 ΔEGFR glioblastoma, and human DU145 androgen-independent prostate carcinoma tumor cells was evaluated in the presence and absence of transport inhibitors. Non-natural amino acid 2-aminobicyclo[2.2.1]heptane-2-carboxylic acid (BCH) was employed as a system L inhibitor, 2-(methylamino)-2-methylproprionic acid (MeAIB) was used to inhibit system A, and a 1:1:1 mixture of alanine, cysteine, and serine was used to inhibit system ASC. The uptake data were normalized and expressed as mean percent uptake relative to the condition without inhibitors (Table 1). Uptake data for anti-3-[18F]-FACBC were also collected under identical conditions to provide a control as well as to facilitate comparison to a clinically validated tracer. The uptake of [18F]9 in 9L and DU145 cells is consistent with a mode of transport facilitated chiefly by system L (80 and 81% uptake inhibition by BCH in 9L and DU145 cells, respectively), with some degree of transport taking place via system ASC (61 and 77% uptake inhibition by alanine, serine, and cysteine in 9L and DU145 cells, respectively). The selectivity of [18F]9 for system L was diminished in U87 ΔEGFR cells (50% uptake inhibition by BCH), while transport by system ASC was relatively greater (78% uptake inhibition by alanine, serine, and cysteine), indicating a preference for transport by ASC in this cell line (Table 2). Across all cell lines, [18F]28 demonstrated a preference for system L (80, 55, and 86% inhibition by BCH in 9L, U87 ΔEGFR, and DU145 cells, respectively), though some reduction of transport under ASC inhibition was also observed (43, 43, and 68% uptake inhibition by alanine, serine, and cysteine in 9L, U87 ΔEGFR, and DU145 cells, respectively). Uptake of anti-3-[18F]-FACBC indicated a strong preference for transport by system ASC, consistent with what has been reported previously.(28,29) The absolute uptake of [18F]9 was greater than that of [18F]28 in each of the cell lines studied, suggesting that the anti-cis, rather than syn-cis, difluoro stereochemical arrangement provides a superior substrate for facilitated amino acid transport in the cell lines studied. Notably, [18F]9 displayed absolute uptake values consistent with anti-3-[18F]-FACBC in each cell line, including in DU145 cells (human androgen-independent prostate carcinoma cells) (Table 3).

    Biodistribution Studies in Rats with Intracranial 9L Tumors

    The tissue distribution of [18F]9 and [18F]28 was evaluated by microPET following tail vein injection of each compound into Fischer rats bearing intracranial 9L gliosarcoma tumors (Figures 3 and 4). [18F]9 tumor tissue uptake reached equilibrium quickly and remained relatively steady over the course of the 60-min study, ranging from 2.05 ± 0.44 to 2.13 ± 0.62 %ID/g (Table 4). In contrast, maximal [18F]28 tumor uptake was observed within the first 4.5 min of the study (1.21 ± 0.22 %ID/g) and decreased to 0.78 ± 0.04 %ID/g by 60 min post injection (Table 5). In contra-lateral normal brain tissue, [18F]9 gave an uptake value of 0.59 ± 0.12 ID/g at 4.4 min post injection and continued to accumulate over the course of the study, reaching 0.95 ± 0.23 %ID/g at 60 min. [18F]28 uptake in contra-lateral normal brain tissue reached 0.37 ± 0.02 at 32.5 min post injection and remained steady through the end of the study. Consequently, despite the nearly twofold greater activity in tumor tissue of [18F]9 compared to [18F]28, the relatively low normal brain uptake of the latter compound results in a higher tumor (T) to normal brain tissue ratio (T/N) at each timepoint. Maximal T/N is observed at 4.5 min post-injection with both [18F]9 (T/N = 3.5) and [18F]28 (T/N = 4.1), though both compounds provide statistically significant differences (p < 0.05) in uptake between normal brain and tumor tissue with L/N > 2 at each timepoint.

    Biodistribution Studies in Normal Rats

    The integrity of the blood–brain barrier (BBB) is an important consideration in PET imaging studies where intracranial tumors are studied, since neoplasia can disrupt the function of the BBB and provide an alternative mode of entry into the brain.(30) Biodistribution studies in normal rats allow for the BBB permeability of a given tracer to be assessed in the absence of any potential obfuscations. The normal brain uptake of [18F]9 ranged from 0.59 ± 0.12 to 0.95 ± 0.23 %ID/g in tumor bearing rats and from 0.65 ± 0.19 to 0.87 ± 0.10 %ID/g in normal rats (Table 6). [18F]28 gave uptake values ranging from 0.28 ± 0.02 to 0.36 ± 0.01 %ID/g in tumor bearing rats and 0.37 ± 0.04 to 0.50 ± 0.04 %ID/g in normal rats (Table 7). These data demonstrate that [18F]9 and [18F]28 are transported into the brain to a similar extent in both tumor bearing and normal rats, indicating that these compounds are BBB permeable and that heterogeneity of the BBB was not a factor in the 9L bearing tumor rat studies. Another element to consider is the extent of tracer accumulation in the bladder, since high bladder activity can obscure the prostate, which is an important structure to accurately visualize due to the prevalence of primary prostate cancer. As was observed with anti-2-FACPC, [18F]9 and [18F]28 display considerable accumulation in the bladder at 32.5 min post injection. However, after 4.4 min post injection, [18F]9 bladder activity is 0.46 ± 0.18 %ID/g, which is comparable with anti-3-[18F]-FACBC bladder activity at the same timepoint (0.51 ± 0.13 %ID/g) (Table 8). Similarly, [18F]28 shows relatively low bladder activity at 4.4-min post injection (0.62 ± 0.64 %ID/g). Based on these data, it appears that at early timepoints, [18F]9 and [18F]28 accumulation in the bladder was sufficiently low to permit the imaging of tumors in the pelvic region.

    Conclusions

    The cold synthesis and [18F] radiolabeling of both diastereomers of cis-3,4-DFACPC have been reported. The radiochemical yields of the [18F]9 and [18F]28 were modest (10 and 1.7% decay corrected radiochemical yield, respectively), though both compounds were obtained in high radiochemical purity. In vitro inhibition experiments with 9L, U87 ΔEGFR, and DU145 cell lines suggested that cis-3,4-DFACPCs undergo transport primarily by system L, with some transport occurring through system ASC. In each cell line, [18F]9 showed a higher degree of uptake than [18F]28, though both compare well with FACBC. In vivo biodistribution studies demonstrated that both stereoisomers of cis-3,4-DFACPC show high uptake in 9L gliosarcoma intracranial tumors with relatively low normal tissue uptake. Furthermore, in contrast to anti-2-FACPC, both cis-3,4-DFACPC isomers showed low bladder uptake at early timepoints, suggesting that these compounds may also be viable for imaging prostate cancer.

The intermediate product was treated with 6 N HCl (0.5 mL) at 110 °C for 10 min and purified by passing through an IR column assembly consisting of a 7 mm × 120 mm bed of an AG 11A8 IR resin column, a neutral alumina SepPak (preconditioned with water), and an HLB Oasis reverse phase cartridge (preconditioned with water). [18F]9 eluted in series through the assembly with three successive portions of sterile saline (∼4.0 mL) into dose vials and was ready for in vitro and in vivo studies. Evidence of the identity of [18F]9 was achieved by comparing the Rf of the radioactive product visualized with radiometric TLC with the Rf of the authentic cold compound visualized with ninhydrin stain, using the solvent system MeCN/H2O/CH3OH = 2:1:1 (Rf = 0.6, Whatman silica gel plates). The only peak present on radiometric TLC analysis corresponded to [18F]9, and the radiochemical purity of the product exceeded 99% (see Figures S3–41). The pH of the final dose solution was tested with pH paper and found to be 6–7. The isolated radiochemical yield was 50 mCi in 12 mL of saline as determined using a dose calibrator, affording a 10% decay corrected radiochemical yield based on a synthesis time of approximately 70 min, which proceeded immediately upon the end of cyclotron bombardment.

    syn-cis-3,4-[18F]-DFACPC ([18F]28)

    The preparation of [18F]28 was based on the previously reported automated synthesis of anti-[18F]FACBC.(27) To a glass vessel containing a solution of Cryptand 222 in MeCN (22 mg/mL) (1.0 mL), was added 1460 mCi of no-carrier-added [18F]HF through a trap/release (T/R) cartridge by using a solution of Cs2CO3/H2O (20 mg mg/mL) (0.6 mL). The solvent was removed at 110 °C with a nitrogen flow, and additional MeCN (3.5 mL) was added followed by evaporation of the solvent with a nitrogen flow to remove residual H2O. Triflate precursor 26 (20 mg, 0.044 mmol) in dry tBuOH (0.5 mL) and MeCN (0.5 mL) was added to the vial, and the reaction mixture was heated at 110 °C for 10 min. The reaction mixture was diluted with 2 mL of acetonitrile, passed through two neutral alumina SepPaks (preconditioned with acetonitrile), and eluted with 10 mL of acetonitrile into a vented vial in a hot cell. The solvent was removed from the vial under a flow of nitrogen at 140 °C. 2 M NaOH (0.5 mL) was added to the vial, which was sealed with a Teflon septum capped with an aluminum crimp top, and the mixture was heated to 140 °C for 5 min. The vial was cooled, and 2 mL of concentrated HCl was added, and the sealed vial was again heated for 15 min at 140 °C. Then, vial was vented, and the solvent was removed under inert gas flow at 140 °C. The resultant solid was taken up in H2O (5 mL) and cannulated through a chain composed of one Waters HLB Oasis cartridge and two alumina SepPaks (both preconditioned with water). A second aliquot of water (5 mL) was passed through the chain, and the eluent containing [18F]28 was collected in a dose vial. The contents of the dose solution were assessed by analytical HPLC, (see Figures S3–43). HPLC analysis indicated that [18F]28 was obtained in >99% radiochemical purity. The pH of the final dose solution was tested with pH paper and found to be 6–7. The isolated radiochemical yield was 8.4 mCi in 5 mL of water as determined using a dose calibrator, affording a 1.7% decay corrected radiochemical yield based on a synthesis time of approximately 135 min, which proceeded 35 min after the end of cyclotron bombardment (total time from end of bombardment to measurement of radiochemical yield was 170 min).

    Cells and Culture

    The cancer cells used in the study include 9L gliosarcoma (rat), DU145 androgen-independent prostate carcinoma (human), and U87 glioblastoma tumor cell lines (human). Cells were cultured as described previously.(32) The tumor cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum, 100 U/mL penicillin, and 100 μg/mL streptomycin, maintained in T-150 tissue culture flasks under humidified incubator conditions (37 °C, 5% CO2/95% air) and were routinely passaged at confluence.(33) Cells thus prepared would be used in cell uptake and inhibition assays and in mice tumor implantations.

    Amino Acid Uptake and Inhibition Assays

    Amino acid uptake and inhibition experiments were performed as described previously.(32) At the time of the experiments, the medium was exchanged to amino-acid-free Hank’s balanced salt solution (HBSS) and cells were adjusted to a final concentration of 5 × 107 cells/mL. The following standard condition applied to each study (refer to supplementary data for optimization information). Approximately 5 × 105 cells were exposed to 5 μCi of [18F]9 and [18F]28, respectively, in 0.1 mL of amino acid/serum-free HBSS in the absence (control condition) or presence of transport inhibitors for 30 min under incubator conditions (37 °C, 5% CO2/95% air) in 1.5 mL conical tubes. Final concentrations (10 mM) of MeAIB were used to inhibit uptake mediated by system A AATs. Final concentrations (10 mM) of 2-amino-bicyclo[2.2.1]heptane-2-carboxylic acid (BCH) were used to inhibit uptake mediated by system L AATs. The combination of 10 mM alanine-cysteine-serine (ACS, 3.3 mM of each amino acid) was used for uptake inhibition of system ACS AATs. After incubation, cells were twice centrifuged (75 G for 5 min) and rinsed with ice-cold HBSS to remove residual activity in the supernatant. Each assay condition was performed in triplicate. The activity in tubes was counted in a Packard Cobra II Auto-Gamma counter, the raw counts were decay corrected, and the activity per cell number was determined from the average of the triplicate. The data from these studies were expressed and normalized as percent uptake of the initial dose per 0.5 million cells (% ID/5 × 105 cells) ± standard deviation (SD).

    Tumor Induction and Animal Preparation

    Rat 9L gliosarcoma cells for intracranial implantation experiments were cultured and prepared the same way as the uptake and inhibition assays and then were washed with phosphate buffer solution (PBS) and obtained a final concentration of 5 × 104/5 μL in PBS. Rat 9L gliosarcoma cells were implanted into the brains of male Fischer 344 rats (160–210 g) as described previously.(34) Briefly, following anesthesia with an intramuscular injection of ketamine (60 mg/mL) and xylazine (7.5 mg/mL) solution, rats were placed in a stereotactic head holder and were injected with 5 μL suspension of rat 9L gliosarcoma cells (5 × 104 cells per rat) in a location 3 mm right of midline and 1 mm anterior to the bregma at 4 mm deep to the outer table. The injection was performed over the course of 2 min, and the needle was withdrawn over the course of 1 min to minimize the backflow of tumor cells. The burr hole and scalp incision were closed, and the animals were returned to their original cages after recovering from the procedure. Intracranial tumors developed that produced weight loss, apathy, and hunched posture in the tumor-bearing rats. Typically, among 25 animals implanted with tumor cells, 20 would develop tumors visible to the naked eye upon dissection in approximately 10–12 days and were used in the study.(35)

    Anesthesia

    Anesthesis was carried out as described previously.(36) Rats were anesthetized using isoflurane gas. Anesthesia was initiated 10 min ahead of imaging experiments by placing the animal in a cage ventilated with oxygen containing 1–2% isoflurane. Body temperature was held at 37 °C using a temperature-controlled warm air convection system.

    Injection of the Radiotracer

    The tracer was administered as described previously.(36) A catheter placed in the tail vein prior to imaging experiments was filled with isotonic sodium chloride solution. The tracer was diluted with saline to a final volume of 0.4 mL and injected via the catheter.

    Rodent Biodistribution Studies

    The microPET imaging process was carried out as described previously.(36) After anesthesia and placement of the tail vein catheter, the animal was placed with its body located at the center of the field of view. The rats were injected through the tail vein catheter with 200–250 μCi of [18F]9 or [18F]28 in 0.4 mL of isotonic saline (pH = 6–7). PET Imaging MicroPET data were acquired with a Siemens Inveon PET/CT system (Siemens Medical Solutions, Knoxville, TN, USA). Radioactivity in the syringe was measured before and after the tracer was injected into the tail vein catheter using a Capintec CRC 15R (Capintec Inc., 6 Arrow Road Ramsey, NJ) dose calibrator. Data acquisition was performed for 60 min starting immediately following tracer injection. After PET imaging, all animals underwent computed tomography (CT) transmission imaging in the same position as the acquired PET data. The PET emission data were normalized and corrected for decay and dead time. The images were reconstructed with an ordered-subset expectation maximization algorithm, using attenuation correction from CT, into fifteen 1-min frames followed by nine 5-min frames. The image volume consisted of 128 × 128 × 159 voxels, each of a size of 0.78 × 0.78 × 0.80 mm. PET and CT images were co-registered using ASIPro and processed using software written by the authors. Regions-of-interest (ROIs) were placed on the fused PET/CT data including the tumor, in the right hemisphere of the brain, and contralateral region in the left hemisphere. The time-activity curves represent the mean activity in the regions-of-interest over time.

        Radiochemical Synthesis and Evaluation of 13N-Labeled 5-Aminolevulinic Acid for PET Imaging of Gliomas

        The endogenous amino acid, 5-aminolevulinic acid (5-ALA), has received significant attention as an imaging agent, including ongoing clin. trials for image-guided tumor resection due to its selective uptake and subsequent accumulation of the fluorescent protoporphyrin IX in tumor cells.
