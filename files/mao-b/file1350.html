    Abstract

    The metabolic syndrome (MetS) is a constellation of cardiovascular and metabolic symptoms involving insulin resistance, steatohepatitis, obesity, hypertension, and heart disease, and patients suffering from MetS often require polypharmaceutical treatment. PPARγ agonists are highly effective oral antidiabetics with great potential in MetS, which promote adipocyte browning and insulin sensitization. However, the application of PPARγ agonists in clinics is restricted by potential cardiovascular adverse events. We have previously demonstrated that the racemic dual sEH/PPARγ modulator RB394 (3) simultaneously improves all risk factors of MetS in vivo. In this study, we identify and characterize the eutomer of 3. We provide structural rationale for molecular recognition of the eutomer. Furthermore, we could show that the dual sEH/PPARγ modulator is able to promote adipocyte browning and simultaneously exhibits cardioprotective activity which underlines its exciting potential in treatment of MetS.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The metabolic syndrome (MetS) is a multifactorial pathological condition and defined as a constellation of cardiovascular and metabolic symptoms involving insulin resistance, steatohepatitis, obesity, hypertension, and heart disease. MetS or syndrome X has become a global epidemic, with an estimated prevalence of 20–27% in developing countries. MetS has its origin in central obesity and consists of four major risk factors: type II diabetes (T2D), dyslipidemia, heart disease, and hypertension.(1) Although safe therapeutics are available to treat each single risk factor, management of MetS often results in polypharmaceutical treatment regimes, with poor compliance, drug–drug interactions, and insufficient therapeutic success.(2) Therefore, multitarget therapeutic options which either treat multiple risk factors of the metabolic syndrome or increase the efficacy of the current therapeutic options are in the focus of current research.(3−6)
    The incidence of MetS often parallels the incidence of obesity and incidence of type 2 diabetes (one of the outcomes of MetS). In this metabolic derangement, peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPARs), i.e., nuclear receptors involved in the regulation of metabolic homeostasis, represent a valuable therapeutic target. PPAR activators have provided significant benefit in patients with primary hypertriglyceridemia (i.e., fibrates and omega-3 fatty acids, both PPARα agonists), as well as in cases of mixed hyperlipidemias with raised triglycerides (TGs) and low high density lipoprotein (HDL-C). Conversely, PPAR-γ activators have become drugs of choice in T2D. PPAR agonists are generally recognized as effective pharmacological tools for the management of MetS.(7) Activation of PPARγ leads to multimodal insulin sensitization, including increased glucose uptake by muscle cells, decreased lipolysis and hepatic glucose production, and improved insulin secretion.(8) Designed multitarget ligands that address PPARγ as one of the targets offer a valuable option to fully utilize the beneficial effects of PPARγ activation.(6) The combination of PPARγ modulation with inhibition of the soluble epoxide hydrolase (sEH) is a promising designed multitarget ligand (DML) approach to treat MetS. sEH, encoded by the EPHX2 gene, converts polyunsaturated fatty acid epoxides—such as the epoxy eicosatrienoic acids (EETs) to the corresponding dihydroxy eicosatrienoic acids (DHETs). Elevated EET levels resulting from sEH inhibition alleviate symptoms of MetS in vivo.(9) Furthermore, treatment with glitazones is restricted due to black box heart failure warning,(10) which is, however, controversially discussed in the literature.(11) Nevertheless, the underlying molecular mechanism for water retention and edema caused by glitazones is well-investigated and is caused by the stimulation of epithelial sodium channel (ENaC)-mediated renal salt reabsorption.(12) ENaC stimulation can be suppressed by sEH inhibition and subsequent accumulation of EETs.(13,14) Treatment of hypertensive and diabetic rats with 1 (TPPU, Figure 1), an sEH inhibitor, exhibited pronounced cardioprotective effects in hearts ex vivo(15) and reversed cardiac dysfunction in the high-fructose diet rat model of metabolic syndrome.(16) Taken together, a pronounced crosstalk between PPARγ and sEH signaling has been demonstrated in multiple disease models,(17−19) suggesting synergistic efficacy and safety of a DML targeting both proteins simultaneously. This assumption could be confirmed by the combination of the PPARγ agonist 2 (rosiglitazone, Figure 1) and sEH inhibitor in the spontaneously hypertensive obese rat (SHROB) model of MetS.(20)
    Inspired by the promising results of the combination therapy, we developed an N-benzyl benzamide based DML 3 (RB394, Figure 1), which is able to activate PPARγ and simultaneously inhibit sEH at submicromolar concentrations.(21) Application of 3 in SHROB and ZSF-1 rat models of MetS led to improvement of diabetes, hypertension, and dyslipidemia as well as kidney and liver fibrosis.(22) In this study, we provide evidence that incorporation of sEH inhibitory activity into a PPARγ agonist retains the beneficial metabolic effects while making the compound cardioprotective. Thus, DMLs targeting sEH and PPARγ simultaneously might be an interesting option for treatment of metabolic disorders in context of MetS.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    Chemistry

    The synthetic procedure to the enatiomers of 3 was inspired by Namura et al., who reported on efficient enantioselective synthesis of PPARα agonists using chiral Evans oxazolidinone auxiliar.(23) Synthesis commenced with 4-formylbenzoic acid 4, which was esterified to benzyl ester 5, followed by sodium borohydride mediated reduction of the formyl group to the corresponding alcohol 6. After conversion of 6 to benzyl bromide 7 with phosphorus tribromide, 7 was coupled to the butanoic acid Evans auxiliar adduct 9 in an asymmetric alkylation reaction, leading to 10. The benzyl protecting group of the intermediate 10 was subsequently cleaved by hydrogenolysis and the resulting free acid 11 was coupled to 4-methoxy-2-(trifluoromethyl) benzyl amine (Scheme 1). Alkaline/oxidative cleavage of the Evans auxiliar produced the (R)-3 and (S)-3 enantiomers, respectively.
    To determine the enantiomeric excess of the enantioselective synthesis, an enantioselective capillary electrophoresis method has been developed. Enantioseparation was achieved in a fused silica capillary using a 100 mM sodium acetate buffer, pH 5.0, containing 35 mg/mL hydroxypropyl-α-cyclodextrin as background electrolyte at an applied voltage of 20 kV. Enantiomeric excess of (R)-3 was determined as 98% ee, while (S)-3 could be obtained in 97% ee (Supporting Information). The CD spectra of both compounds are shown in Figure 2.

    In Vitro Identification of the Eutomer

    To identify the eutomer, we subjected both enantiomers of 3 to in vitro assays with both targets. In the case of PPARγ, we performed a homogeneous time resolved FRET (HTRF) cofactor recruitment assay, using a CBP (CREB-binding protein) derived peptide coupled to Tb cryptate as FRET donor and PPARγ LBD, expressed as a fusion protein with N-terminal superfolder GFP (sGFP).(24) Rosiglitazone 2 potently induced the recruitment of CBP peptide with an EC50 of 0.083 μM. (R)-3 also promoted cofactor recruitment with a slightly diminished upper plateau of the HTRF signal and an EC50 of 0.27 μM. (S)-3 exhibited markedly lower recruitment efficiency as well as a reduced EC50 of 5.2 μM (Figure 3A). The inhibitory activity toward sEH was investigated using a fluorescence-based activity assay. TPPU 1 potently inhibited sEH hydrolase activity with an IC50 of 0.013 μM, while both enantiomers of 3 exhibited almost equal potency ((S)-3, IC50 = 0.24 μM; (R)-3, IC50 = 0.2 μM; Figure 3B).

    Structural Analysis

    To rationalize the experimental observations on the enantiomers of 3, crystallographic analysis of (R)-3 in complex with both sEH and PPARγ was performed (Figure 4). The X-ray structure of (R)-3 in complex with the C-terminal hydrolase domain of sEH revealed binding to the catalytic center. Thereby, the amide moiety acts as an epoxide mimetic and exhibits directed hydrogen bond interactions toward Y383, Y466, and D335. The carboxylate moiety is engaged in one hydrogen bond interaction toward backbone NH. The stereogenic α-carbon of (R)-3 as well as the ethyl residue are pointing out into a wide hydrophobic tunnel. This observation fits to the equipotent inhibitory potency of both enantiomers toward sEH.
    In contrast, the X-ray structure of (R)-3 in complex with PPARγ LBD reveals tight engagement of the carboxylate moiety into directed hydrogen bond interactions toward S289, H323, Y473, and H449. These interactions can be considered as canonical for full agonists of PPARγ. The α-ethyl substituent is tightly enclosed in a small hydrophobic pocket (Figure 4C, orange). Thereby, (S)-3 would not be able to fit the hydrophobic pocket in a similar fashion, which explains its weaker activity and efficacy.
    Most interestingly, our observations on the enantiomeric preference of the R-enantiomer are in complete contrast to the studies on pan-PPAR agonists TIPP-703 by Oyama et al.(25) and Kuwabara et al.(26) Compound 13 (TIPP-703) and related compounds were more active in their S-configuration. The reason for this can be found in the superposition of the X-ray structures (Figure 5). The carboxylate moiety of 13 is further away from the interacting key residues S289, H323, Y473, and H449. The cocrystal structure of 13 reveals a movement of F363, which liberates more space in the lipophilic pocket adjacent to the α-position. The structural reason for the induced movement of F363 is speculative, one reason could be the interactions of the butoxy moiety which pull the acidic group of 13 away from the helix 12.

    Cellular Effects

    In vitro experiments indicated that (R)-3 is the eutomer of the racemate 3 considering PPARγ activation, while sEH inhibition can be achieved using almost equal concentrations of both enantiomers. We subjected both enantiomers as well as the racemate to the cell-based reporter gene assay using a Gal4-DBD-PPARγ-LBD chimera (Figure 6A). Indeed, the racemate 3 and (R)-3 potently promoted the induction of luciferase reporter gene with EC50 values of 0.39 and 0.40 μM, respectively. The distomer (S)-3 displayed a much weaker fold-activation with EC50 of 2.5 μM. The fact that maximum fold activation of the racemate is lower than (R)-3 supports the assumption that (S)-3 acts as a partial PPARγ agonist, which competes with the eutomer (R)-3.
    We also investigated the selectivity of (R)-3 toward selected nuclear receptors at a fixed concentration of 10 μM in a reporter gene assay panel (Figure 6B). (R)-3 weakly activated PPARα, and other tested receptors were not activated. Similar compounds developed by Nomura et al. were potent PPARα agonists, however, only in the (S)-configuration.(27) In agreement with our results, (R)-configured propionic acid derivatives displayed only weak PPARα activation.
    The next task was the analysis of both enantiomers in a cellular system with endogeneous expression of PPARγ. To this end, we used the human Simpson–Golabi–Behmel syndrome (SGBS) preadipocyte cell strain.(28) SGBS cells are a model system for human adipocyte biology and characterized by a high capacity to PPARγ-induced adipogenesis(29,30) and also express EPHX2 (data not shown).
    SGBS cells were subjected to adipogenic differentiation in the presence of increasing doses of the PPARγ agonist 2, (R)-3, and (S)-3. Upon morphological inspection, we observed the formation of intracellular lipid droplets after treatment with all three substances (Figure 7). A Nile red staining to quantify lipid accumulation revealed that (R)-3 potently activates PPARγ, while (S)-3 is less active. Comparable results underlining the divergent potency of (R)-3 and (S)-3 were obtained when measuring the mRNA expression of adipogenic marker genes, i.e., PPARγ itself but also perilipin, a lipid droplet-associated protein only expressed in mature adipocytes (Figure 7).
    Upon chronic cold exposure, white adipose tissue (WAT) can partially convert into thermogenic, beige adipose tissue, which shares main similarities with classical brown adipose tissue (BAT). This process is commonly referred to as browning and is associated with improved glucose and lipid metabolism in mice.(31) Thus, the induction of browning in white adipocytes might lead to metabolic benefits. PPARγ activation leads to browning of white adipocytes in vitro.(30) We thus investigated whether 3 could also induce a white-to-brown transition in SGBS cells (Figure 8). As expected, incubation of SGBS cells with rosiglitazone during the first 4 days of adipogenic differentiation resulted in an induction of uncoupling protein-1 (UCP1) expression in mature adipocytes. (R)-3 also induced UCP1 but at higher concentrations, while (S)-3 had a lower effect. Also expected, the mRNA expression of the mitochondrial marker UQCRC2 (Ubiquinol-cytochrome C reductase core protein 2), a subunit of the respiratory chain complex III showed a comparable expression pattern with a dose-dependent upregulation induced by rosiglitazone and to a weaker extent (R)-3 and again weaker, (S)-3.
    Next, we determined the respiratory activity in these cells to find out if the increased expression of UCP1 had a functional impact and uncoupled the electron transport chain. To induce the release of fatty acids, the physiological inducer of UCP1, cAMP was added to the cultures. (R)-3 increased the level of basal respiration as well as ATP-linked and proton leak respiration, although to a lesser extent than rosiglitazone but stronger than (S)-3. These data indicate that (R)-3 is able to induce browning in human adipocytes.

    Cardioprotective Effects in ex Vivo Model

    PPARγ agonism combined with soluble epoxide hydrolase (sEH) antagonism shows the potential for a beneficial outcome in terms of macrovascular events. Manipulation of sEH activity per se has been shown to exert cardioprotective functions. In particular, deletion of the sEH improved coronary reactive hyperemia (CRH),(32) while the elevated levels of EETs observed in the sEH depleted mice(32,33) have been linked to cardioprotective effects after myocardial ischemia/reperfusion injury.(34,35) Herein, we sought to investigate whether the combination of sEH inhibition and PPARγ activation affects cardiac function in response to reactive hyperemia and global no-flow ischemia induced in isolated murine hearts. To choose the appropriate dose of the novel dual sEH/PPARγ modulator (R)-3, we compared it with the widely used sEH inhibitor (trans-4-[4-(3-adamantan-1-ylureido)cyclohexyloxy]-benzoic acid (t-AUCB).(36,37) Initially, we performed a concentration response for the administration of (R)-3. Interestingly, bolus administration of either t-AUCB or (R)-3 at a concentration of 300 nM or 30 nM increased the beating rate of the isolated hearts. As such an effect was not observed at a dose of 3 nM further experiments used this concentration but as a continuous infusion (Figure 9A). The proposed concentration was able to alter the ratio of the 14,15 EET/14,15 DHET 15 min postapplication and achieved approximately 70% of the levels detected in the effluents of sEH–/– hearts (Figure 9B). Subsequently, we evaluated the effects of sEH inhibition in the coronary vascular function. As ex vivo hearts possess limited coronary vascular reserve and responses to vasodilators are poorly suited to study coronary function, we evaluated the effects of sEH inhibition on reactive hyperemia. During CRH, blood flow increases transiently in response to ischemia or interruption of blood supply,(38) and is associated with coronary vasodilation in response to cessation in coronary perfusion. The increase in blood flow associated with CRH increases repayment volume (RV) to prevent injury due to ischemia, therefore it is considered as a protective mechanism. sEH deletion or PPARγ agonism (with rosiglitazone) have been linked to increased repayment volume.(32) In addition, rosiglitazone enhances the CRH in the sEH depleted mice.(32) sEH deletion increased repayment volume by 28.57%. Inhibition of sEH with t-AUCB exerted similar effects. Simultaneous manipulation of sEH and PPARγ with (R)-3 was more effective at increasing repayment volume by 85.7% after 15 s of coronary occlusion. Addition of (R)-3 in sEH–/– hearts potentiated the effect by 64.4% (Figure 9C,D). Finally, we tested the capacity of (R)-3 to affect the outcome of acute myocardial infarction. Infarcted area was visualized following 20 min of global no-flow ischemia and 2 h of reperfusion. sEH inhibition exhibited cardioprotective effects which were further potentiated (by 4.2%) after dual inhibition of sEH and activation of PPARγ with (R)-3 (Figure 9E). Interestingly, although sEH inhibition improved left heart function, (R)-3 with its additional PPARγ activation elicited better results as indicated by the increase in left ventricular developed pressure (LVDP, Figure 9F) and decrease in left ventricular end diastolic pressure (LVEDP, Figure 9G), 20 min after global ischemia compared to the solvent treated isolated hearts. Taken together, these data indicate that single sEH inhibition or deletion suffice to increase the repayment volume and reduce the myocardial infarct size. However, (R)-3 potentiates the cardioprotective effects and in particular the left heart function achieved by the manipulation of PPARγ.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In the present study, we demonstrated that (R)-3 is the eutomer of compound 3 (RB394) considering PPARγ activation, while sEH is potently inhibited by both enantiomers of 3. Structural analysis provides the rationale for enantiomer preference. Our observations made in SGBS cells strongly support the well-established observation on the beneficial effects of PPARγ agonists promoting adipocyte browning. Direct comparison of the full agonist (R)-3 and partial agonist (S)-3 in SGBS cells suggest that the effects correlate with PPARγ activation, while sEH inhibition did not affect adipocyte browning. This seems to be in contrast to recent findings by Overby et al.(39) They showed that the sEH inhibitor t-TUBC increased in vitro adipogenesis in murine and human brown preadipocytes along with an upregulation of typical brown adipocyte marker genes and increased mitochondrial uncoupling. Treatment with t-TUBC in vivo resulted in reduced serum triglyceride levels in mice subjected to a high fat diet. Unfortunately, the authors did not provide data on white preadipocytes or WAT, therefore the data sets are not comparable. Given the high efficacy in improvement of diabetic markers and lipid profiles of compound 3 in rat models of MetS,(22) further comparative investigations of selective PPARγ agonists, sEH inhibitors, and dual sEH/PPARγ modulators in the context of adipocyte browning in vivo are required.
    Most interestingly, sEH inhibitors have already been attributed cardioprotective properties but (R)-3 was able to improve on the effects of targeted sEH inhibition, at least in the isolated buffer perfused heart. In the light of limited use of PPARγ agonists in clinics due to proposed adverse cardiovascular effects, sEH inhibitors as add-on therapy or dual sEH/PPARγ modulators could potentially protect the patients from the latter, especially, MetS patients relying on polypharmaceutical treatment could benefit from safe agents that promote PPARγ activation and reduce the number of drugs in treatment of different risk factors.(2)

    Synthesis

    Benzyl 4-Formylbenzoate (5)

    4-Formylbenzoic acid 4 (2.0 g, 12.9 mmol, 1.0 equiv) was solved in dry DMF (25 mL) and treated with Cs2CO3 (4.2 g, 12.9 mmol, 1.0 equiv). After 30 min, benzyl bromide (1.53 mL, 12.9 mmol, 1.0 equiv) was added and the suspension was stirred at room temperature for 3 h. The reaction mixture was filtered, and the filtrate was mixed with a solution of ethyl acetate and n-hexane (1:5). The organic phase was washed with water, followed by saturated NaHCO3 solution and brine. Afterward, organic phase was dried over MgSO4 and solvent was removed under reduced pressure to obtain desired product as colorless solid (2.83 g, 11.8 mmol, 91%), which could be used further without additional purification. 1H NMR (400 MHz, DMSO-d6) δ = 10.10 (s, 1H, CHO), 8.17 (d, 3JHH = 8.2 Hz, 2H, Har), 8.03 (d, 3JHH = 8.2 Hz, 2H, Car), 7.49 (d, 3JHH = 7.2 Hz, 2H, Har), 7.44–7.33 (m, 3H, Har), 5.38 (s, 2H, O-CH2) ppm. 13C NMR (101 MHz, DMSO-d6) δ = 192.9, 164.7, 139.2, 135.8, 134.3, 129.9 (2 × C), 129.7 (2 × C), 128.6 (2 × C), 128.2 (2 × C), 128.1, 66.7 ppm.     Langendorff Perfused Heart Preparation

    The Langendorff perfused heart was performed as previously described.(32) Mice were euthanatized with 5% isoflurane. Hearts were excised and immediately placed into heparinized (5 U/ml) ice-cold Krebs–Henseleit buffer containing (in mmol/L) 119.0 NaCl, 11.0 glucose, 22.0 NaHCO3, 4.7 KCl, 1.2 KH2PO4, 1.2 MgSO4, 2.5 CaCl2, 2.0 pyruvate, and 0.5 EDTA. After removal of the lung and surrounding tissue around the heart, the aorta was rapidly cannulated with a 20 gauge, blunt-ended needle and continuously perfused with 37 °C buffer continuously bubbled with ([95% O2]–[5% CO2]) at a constant perfusion pressure of 80 mmHg. The left atrium was excised, and a water-filled balloon was inserted into the left ventricle. The balloon was connected to a pressure transducer for continuous measurement of left ventricular developed pressure (LVDP) and heart rate (HR). The heart was then immersed in a water-jacketed perfusate bath (37 °C) and left to beat spontaneously. Left ventricular diastolic pressure was adjusted to 2–5 mmHg. A Power-Lab Chart data acquisition system (AD Instruments, Colorado Springs, CO) was used for data acquisition. Heart was allowed to stabilize for 30 min before initiation of CRH. Subsequently, hearts were treated with solvent or the mentioned compounds for 15 min in a circulating mode. Only hearts whose peak CF increased by more than 2-fold after a 15 s total occlusion were included in the analysis. For coronary reactive hyperemic response, hearts were subjected to 15 s of total occlusion by closing the valve directly above the cannulated heart. For global ischemia/reperfusion experiments, hearts were subjected to 20 min of no flow ischemia followed by 2 h of reperfusion. In the end of the experiment, hearts were washed with 5 mL of triphenyltetrazolium-chloride in PBS cut until 1 mm slices and placed for 15 min at 37 °C. Subsequently, the heart slices were compressed between two histological glass surfaces and scanned. The necrotic areas were visualized by decoloration. ImageJ was used to calculate the infarcted areas.
