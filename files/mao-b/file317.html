    Abstract

    New tritarget small molecules combining Ca2+ channels blockade, cholinesterase, and H3 receptor inhibition were obtained by multicomponent synthesis. Compound 3p has been identified as a very promising lead, showing good Ca2+ channels blockade activity (IC50 = 21 ± 1 μM), potent affinity against hH3R (Ki = 565 ± 62 nM), a moderate but selective hBuChE inhibition (IC50 = 7.83 ± 0.10 μM), strong antioxidant power (3.6 TE), and ability to restore cognitive impairment induced by lipopolysaccharide.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Alzheimer’s disease (AD), one of the most serious medical problems in modern society, remains the main cause of memory impairment and dementia in elderly people.(1) AD exhibits highly interconnected physiopathological signs, where the accumulation of abnormal extracellular deposits of amyloid-β (Aβ) peptide, intracellular neurofibrillary tangles (NFT), along with dramatic neuronal death and decreased levels of choline acetyltransferase are the typical hallmarks.(2) The increased oxidative stress (OS) and imbalances in the homeostasis of biometals such as Cu, Fe, or Zn (3−5) constitute also other prominent features of AD.
    Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) and butyrylcholinesterase (BuChE) are the catabolic enzymes responsible for acetylcholine (ACh) hydrolysis, and it is known that BuChE has a compensatory role in the hydrolysis of ACh in brains. Moreover, the levels of AChE decrease while those of BuChE increase, in aged AD patients.(6) Accordingly, three acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (AChEIs) donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine(7) are used for AD therapy but with limited therapeutic success. However, combined therapy of memantine a N-methyl-d-aspartate receptor antagonist(8) and AChEI may be associated with better benefits.(9)
    In view of this situation, new strategies have been promoted based on the multitarget-directed ligand (MTDL) approach(10,11) for the development of new drugs able to bind simultaneously at diverse enzymatic systems or receptors involved in AD pathology.(12−15) Following this paradigm, we have developed a number of new MTDL using multicomponent reactions (MCRs)(16−18) as the method of choice for introducing rapidly and efficiently chemical diversity in the search for new MTDL.
    Continuing with our contributions in this area, we report here the design, synthesis, and biological evaluation of diethyl 2,6-dimethyl-4-(4-(2-(amine-1-yl)alkoxy)phenyl)-1,4-dihydropyridine-3,5-dicarboxylates (3a–p) as new MTDLs resulting from the incorporation of a typical histamine 3 receptor (H3R) antagonist motif, such as the cycloalkylamine, present in pitolisant and contilisant, into a 1,4-dihydropyridine (1,4-DHP) I (Figure 1) through a convenient linker. Pitolisant is a H3R antagonist currently under investigation for diverse cognition impairments,(19,20) while contilisant is a recently developed MTDL by some of us, showing H3R antagonism, and a very attractive new lead-compound for AD therapy.(21,22) H3R is commonly recognized as a useful pharmacological tool in the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases, involved in the central regulation of histamine levels by feedback inhibition of histamine release(23) and the release of other neurotransmitters, such as acetylcholine, serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine.(24) However, we have to keep in mind that high affinity H3R inverse agonists may produce side effects as highly potent H3R ligands developed were rejected in preclinical studies because of unwanted effects(25) like phospholipidosis, high plasma protein binding, or poor blood–brain penetration.
    1,4-DHPs are privileged scaffolds in medicinal chemistry and the core fragment of well-known calcium channel antagonists such as nilvadipine, which is undergoing a European multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled, phase III trial targeted to mild-to-moderate AD.(26) It is well-known that increased cytosolic calcium levels, involved in the pathogenesis of AD, facilitate formation of Aβ peptides through calcium-mediated β-secretase activity(27−31) and also regulate glycogen synthase kinase, protein kinase C, and other kinases that hyperphosphorylate tau and potentiate NFT formation.(32) Moreover, calcium entry through voltage-gated L-type Ca2+ channels (Cav 1.1–1.4) causes calcium overload and mitochondrial disruption, which leads to the activation of the apoptotic cascade and cell death.(33) Thus, the calcium channel blockers (CCBs) of the type of DHP can prevent the formation of Aβ peptides, NFT formation providing neuroprotective effect, a fact that suggests that DHPs may prevent or slow the rate of progression of AD.(34)
    Thus, the new 1,4-DHPs 3a–p (Scheme 1, Table 1) were investigated for their calcium channels and their affinity toward H3R receptor and for their ChE inhibition and antioxidant power. From these studies, we have identified 1,4-DHP 3p (Scheme 1, Table 1) as a new and very promising hit-agent for the potential AD therapy combining activities against three biological targets, as 3p is a good Ca2+ channel blocker with potent H3R affinity and selective hBuChEI, showing in addition strong antioxidant properties.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The synthesis of the target 1,4-DHPs 3a–p (Scheme 1) has been carried out by a straightforward two-step synthesis with good overall yields, as depicted in Scheme 1. The reaction of commercial 4-hydroxybenzaldehydes 1a–e with the appropriate 1-bromo-n-chloroalkane in the presence of potassium carbonate in refluxing acetonitrile for 24 h followed by the reaction with the selected cycloalkylamines and potassium iodide afforded the (aminoalkoxy)benzaldehydes 2a–p. Next, the one-pot Hantzsch MCR of 2a–p, ethyl acetoacetate, and ammonium carbonate in ethanol–water under microwave irradiation (MWI) at 120 °C for 4 h gave the expected diethyl 2,6-dimethyl-4-(4-(2-(amine-1-yl)alkoxy)aryl)-1,4-dihydropyridine-3,5-dicarboxylates 3a–p. All new compounds showed analytical and spectral data in full agreement with their proposed structures (see Supporting Information).
    To verify the effectiveness of the rational design, first of all, we investigated the blocking effect of the depolarization-induced Ca2+ uptake in SH-SY5Y neuroblastoma cells stimulated with an extracellular concentration of K+ 70 mM, which induces the cell Ca2+ levels elevation by opening voltage-gated Ca2+ channels (VGCC), monitored with the Ca2+-sensitive fluorescent dye Fluo-4. As shown in Table 1, among the 16 tested compounds, nine displayed a Ca2+ channel blockade at 10 μM better than nifedipine used, as positive standard. These VGCC blockers were further assayed at different concentrations to assess their concentration-dependent blocking profile, and five of them showed IC50 data ranging from 1.5 to 15 μM and were therefore more potent than nifedipine (IC50 = 22 μM) in a significant manner. Not surprisingly, the most active compounds, having IC50 below 5 μM, were 1,4-DHPs 3c–f derivatives that bear halogen atoms at the phenyl ring, as one can find in amlodipine(35) or felodipine,(36) two well-known VGCC antagonists, largely used drugs in the clinics.
    The binding affinities for 3a–p on hH3R were evaluated taking methylhistamine as radioligand. As shown in Table 1, 3g, 3j, 3l–m, and 3o,p showed interesting affinities with Ki ranging from 40 nM for 3j and 1619 nM for 3l. The most active compounds, with Ki in the nanomolar range, were 3j and 3m. It is worth noting that among the active compounds, 3l,m and 3o,p were simultaneously good VGCC blockers (see above), with IC50 values comparable to that of nifedipine. Compound 3m still remains the most balanced compound, with an IC50 of 25 μM as VGCC blocker, showing an affinity for H3R with Ki = 60 nM. Regarding the structure–activity relationships (SARs), no activity was observed for 3a–f that bear a length in the linker of n = 2, irrespective of the nature of amine and the substituent on the aromatic ring. Nevertheless, when n = 3 and except compound 3l, which showed the less active and modest affinity, the activities were always observed for compounds without substituent on the aromatic ring. Finally and except hybrid 3n, the same trend was observed for 3m and 3o,p with the length of the linker n = 4 and 5.
    Next and to complete the multitarget biological analysis, we evaluated the hChE inhibition capacity of 3a–p following Ellman’s protocol;(37) see Table S1 (Supporting Information). No significant activity was observed toward hAChE. However and very interestingly, 1,4-DHPs 3n–p (n = 4 and n = 5) showed good hBuChE inhibition power in the micromolar range, with IC50 values equal to 7.22, 6.07, and 7.83 μM, respectively. Thus, 3o,p were identified as three-target direct ligand for AD combining VGCC blockade, H3R affinity with hBuChE inhibition. These two compounds were chosen to evaluate their selectivity against the other histamine receptor subtypes (see Tables S2–S4, Supporting Information). No activity was observed against H1. For H4, the structurally most homologous G-protein-coupled receptor, 3p showed a very low antagonist activity (IC50 = 1400 nM) compared to the value observed for thioperamide (IC50= 19 nM), while 3o showed a very weak antagonist activity. This moderate H3R affinity against H4 was previously observed in other compounds related to 3o and 3p bearing a pentyloxy linker connected to cyclic amine moiety.(21)
    Neuroprotective activity was then performed for 3o,p. First, we tested the neuroprotective profile of these two compounds at different concentrations (0.1, 0.3, 1, and 3 μM) against two toxic stimuli related to neurodegeneration: (a) a model of oxidative stress and ROS generation, such as a cocktail of the mitochondrial respiratory chain blockers rotenone and oligomycin A (R/O), and (b) hyperphosphorylation of tau protein using okadaic acid (OA), a well-known protein Ser/Thr phosphatase inhibitor. As shown in Table 2, the two compounds showed an interesting profile against R/O. At 0.3 and 1 μM, 3o produced a significant increase in cell viability against R/O (48.9% and 52.8%, respectively). Similarly, 3p at 1 and 3 μM showed a potent increase in cell viability (64.8% and 49.9%). However, only 3p showed a significant increase in cell viability against OA at the same concentrations of 1 and 3 μM (41.8% and 20.9%).

    Next, we explored the potential effect of 3o and 3p on memory behavior improvements in vivo, using the novel object recognition test (NOR) (Figure 2). Treatment of mice (250 μg/kg) with lipopolysaccharide (LPS) for 24 h resulted in a significant decrease of the discrimination index compared to control group, suggesting that LPS treatment significantly impaired memory behavior. Mice treated with 10 mg/kg of 3p after LPS showed significantly improved discrimination index, while at the same dose, 3o was not able to restore the cognitive impairment. These results show that recognition memory deficits caused by LPS (250 μg/kg) injection are restored at an enhanced level by 3p at 10 mg/kg, an effect that compares very favorably with the one provided by donepezil at 1 mg/kg
    To potentiate the therapeutic power of 1,4-DHPs 3a–p, the antioxidant activity was tested by the ORAC-FL method.(38,39) Results are expressed in Trolox equivalents (TE) unit, melatonin and ferulic acid being used as positive controls with ORAC values equal to 2.5 (16) and 3.7,(16) respectively. As shown in Table 1, all 1,4-DHPs presented strong radical scavenging properties with TE values ranging from 2.2 (3c) to 3.9 (3b). Compound 3b was a better antioxidant than ferulic acid, while seven of the remaining compounds were more active than melatonin. Regarding the SAR, no evident and clear trends could be observed. However, it should be noted that the two most balanced ligands 3o,p showed a strong antioxidant power with ORAC values equal to 2.8 and 3.6, respectively.
    Prediction of the blood–brain barrier (BBB) penetration of 3o,p has been addressed using the PAMPA method.(40) showing high probability for 3o,p to cross the BBB via passive diffusion; see Table S4 in Supporting Information.
    A few 1,4-DHPs reported in the literature show good selectivity between the brain and cardiovascular calcium channels(41,42) otherwise constituting a major drawback. Thus, the vasodilation effect of 3p was evaluated compared to nifedipine. As shown in Figure 3 and as expected,(43) nifedipine induced a robust dose-dependent vasorelaxant effect, with percentages of relaxation reaching 12, 37, and 81 at 0.1, 1, and 10 nM, respectively. By contrast, 3p did not vasodilate aortic rings except at 10 nM at which a small relaxant effect was observed (5%), significantly lower than nifedipine (p < 0.01).
    To obtain structural clues for selectivity against hH3R, we performed docking studies of 3o and 3p into the active sites of the X-ray crystal structure of the hH1R (PDB code 3RZE) of the homology-modeled hH3R and of the hH4R homology model. The modeling of the receptors 3D structures was performed using the automated homology-modeling program SWISS-MODEL,(44,45) and the program AutoDock Vina was used to perform the blind docking. The predicted docking poses for 3o and 3p in the hH3R were characterized by relatively high docking scores of −8.5 and −8.0 kcal/mol, respectively. Protein–ligand interactions include a salt bridge between the protonated amine of the pyrrolidine or piperidine groups and Asp114 (N···O distance, 3.71 and 5.44 Å) and a π–cation interaction with Phe398 (3o) or Tyr115 (3p) (Figures 4 and 5S (Supporting Information)). The first interaction is considered to be critical for receptor activation. The aliphatic linker is surrounding by Pro184, Ala190, Leu111, and Tyr374. Additionally, the oxygen ether was found to involve hydrogen bonding with the hydroxyl group of Tyr394 (O···O distance, 3.25 Å for 3o; 2.91 Å for 3p). Moreover, π–π stacking interaction could be observed between the phenyl ring and Tyr189.
    As shown in Figure 5a, 3o and 3p are located on the top of TM5, TM6, TM7 interacting with the extracellular loop 2 (ECL2) of hH1R. Both compounds are far from the crucial amino acids in the ligand-binding site; hence, no interaction between 3o, 3p and the binding site residues could be observed. The predicted binding modes in H1R are in agreement with the no activity observed in vitro.
    In the hH4R, both compounds are positioned at the same site, as shown in Figure 5b. The ligand-binding site is buried in an interior cavity, and it is located between TM3, TM5, TM6 and TM7. Compounds 3o and 3p are located at the entrance, blocking the access to the binding pocket by interaction with TM4, TM3, and ECL2. The weak inhibitory activities of these compounds support this interaction model

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The development of new drug candidates for an effective treatment of AD is a challenging task for medicinal chemists and a research area with potential huge impact in society. In the search for new MTDL able to bind simultaneously at diverse enzymatic systems or receptors involved in AD, 16 compounds were now designed, synthesized via Hantzsch MCR, and evaluated for their activities to modulate calcium dyshomeostasis, binding affinities of H3R, ChEs inhibition, and antioxidant power. From these results, we have identified diethyl 2,6-dimethyl-4-(4-((5-(piperidin-1-yl)pentyl)oxy)phenyl)-1,4-dihydropyridine-3,5-dicarboxylate (3p) as a suitable MTDL for further development. 1,4-DHP 3p showed good Ca2+ channels blockade activity (IC50 = 21 ± 1 μM), potent good affinities against H3R (Ki = 565 ± 62 nM), moderate and selective hBuChE (IC50 = 7.83 ± 0.10 μM), strong antioxidant power (3.6 TE), and ability to restore cognitive impairment induced by LPS. In addition, 3p did not show a significant vasorelaxing effect. It can therefore be hypothesized that, in vivo, unlike nifedipine 3p exerts its effects independently of vasodilatation. Work is now in progress in our laboratory to develop new analogues of the identified compounds, looking for a lead 1,4-DHP for AD therapy, and the results will be reported elsewhere in due course.

    Being aware that nitro bearing 1,4-DHP dihydropyridines, such as nifedipine or nicardipine, have been recognized as putative pan assay interference compounds (PAINS)(46) or might also have the potential to form aggregates and cause misleading results,(47) we did not use the nitro group in our design. Consequently, we are confident that our hit-compounds 3o and 3p are very reliable and the starting point of new family of neuroprotective agents.(48) However, and in spite of this, we have analyzed these compounds for their potential PAINS.
    First, the analysis conducted by using swissadme.ch proved that these compounds are not PAINS (see Supporting Information), a reasonable expectation for these compounds, since PAINS with dihydropyridine structure, reported in literature, have another chemical structure than our compounds (with a many conjugated double bonds, especially with double bond at C4 carbon atom of dihydropyridine). Besides this, we based our compounds on dihydropyridine drugs, which are not identified as PAINS.
    Our hit-compounds 3o and 3p were not recognized also as PAINS according to the Free ADME-Tox filtering tool (FAFDrugs4) program (http://fafdrugs4.mti.univ-paris-diderot.fr/) and to the “False Positive Remover” software (http://www.cbligand.org/PAINS/).
    Regarding the cholinesterase inhibition, no interference was expected with the assay, as the compounds were only active against BuChE but not against AChE.
    The compounds showed an absorbance (Supporting Information) at 343 nm, whose excitation at this wavelength gave a tiny emission at 421 nm, and therefore no possible interference was observed with the cytosolic Ca2+-sensitive fluorescent dye Fluo-4M in SH-SY5Y, shown at 485 nm (excitation wavelength) and 520 nm (emission wavelength).
    Finally, 3o and 3p were not recognized as aggregators according to the software “Aggregator Advisor” (http://advisor.bkslab.org/). However, and since the aggregators are nonspecific inhibitors, 3o and 3p were tested against an unrelated enzyme, such as MAO A, showing no inhibition.

    In a 10 mL microwave glass tube equipped with a septum cap, the appropriate aldehyde 2a–p (1 equiv), ammonium carbonate (2 equiv), and ethyl acetoacetate (3 equiv) were dissolved in a mixture EtOH/H2O (1:1, 1.5 mL). The mixture was then exposed to microwave irradiation at 120 °C during 4 h. After reaction was complete (monitored by TLC), the mixture was evaporated and the residue was dissolved in dichloromethane, washed with water, brine, dried over anhydrous sodium sulfate, and concentrated.
