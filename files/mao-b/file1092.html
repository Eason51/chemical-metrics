    Abstract

    New drugs that target Plasmodium species, the causative agents of malaria, are needed. The enzyme N-myristoyltransferase (NMT) is an essential protein, which catalyzes the myristoylation of protein substrates, often to mediate membrane targeting. We screened ∼1.8 million small molecules for activity against Plasmodium vivax (P. vivax) NMT. Hits were triaged based on potency and physicochemical properties and further tested against P. vivax and Plasmodium falciparum (P. falciparum) NMTs. We assessed the activity of hits against human NMT1 and NMT2 and discarded compounds with low selectivity indices. We identified 23 chemical classes specific for the inhibition of Plasmodium NMTs over human NMTs, including multiple novel scaffolds. Cocrystallization of P. vivax NMT with one compound revealed peptide binding pocket binding. Other compounds show a range of potential modes of action. Our data provide insight into the activity of a collection of selective inhibitors of Plasmodium NMT and serve as a starting point for subsequent medicinal chemistry efforts.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Despite recent progress, malaria remains the deadliest parasitic infection worldwide, killing approximately half a million people annually.(1) The causative agents of the disease are single-celled eukaryotes of the genus Plasmodium. Despite many recent calls for eradication,(2) the most advanced vaccine results in only limited protection,(3,4) suggesting elimination of malaria could rely heavily on drug-based intervention.(5,6) While several effective drug regimens have been introduced and have impacted disease burden,(1) parasites have developed some level of tolerance or resistance to all drugs in widespread use.(7−13) While most drugs target the parasite while it resides within the bloodstream, eradication will likely require targeting multiple stages of the malaria life cycle, including dormant forms of the parasite, called hypnozoites, which reside within the liver.
    Plasmodium parasites are transmitted to humans through the bite of female Anopheles mosquitoes. Soon after transmission, malaria parasites travel through the blood stream to the liver where they infect a single hepatocyte. During intracellular life, the parasite multiplies over 2–10 days during which each liver-stage parasite gives rise to tens of thousands of merozoites. Merozoites are then released back into the blood stream where they invade red blood cells and initiate cyclic asexual intraerythrocytic replication. While the liver-stage of infection is asymptomatic, blood-stage infection causes all symptoms associated with malaria. Moreover, blood parasites also differentiate into sexual stages, enter the bone marrow, and develop as gametocytes. Once mature, sexual forms return to the circulation and are transmitted. As such, successful control of malaria could occur at multiple stages of the life cycle. Targeting proteins involved in regulatory processes within the parasite that facilitate multiple stages of the complex life cycle might provide the most robust approach to eliminating malaria infection, disease, and transmission.
    The enzyme N-myristoyltransferase (NMT) has been demonstrated to be essential in multiple eukaryotic species. NMT catalyzes the transfer of myristate from myristoyl-coenzyme A (myrCoA) to the N-terminal glycine residue of substrate proteins following cleavage of the initiator methionine. Protein myristoylation can modulate protein stability and can serve to localize proteins to membranes (reviewed by Schlott et al.(14)). Myristolated proteins comprise ∼0.4% of the Trypanosoma cruzi proteome.(15) In Plasmodium species, although the myristome is less comprehensively described, more than 30 NMT substrate proteins have been identified through metabolic labeling for Plasmodium blood-stage parasites.(16) Given these expansive consequences, NMT has been recognized as an attractive drug target in multiple human pathogens including Leishmania, Plasmodium, and Trypanosoma. For example, Harrison and colleagues have identified a series of selective and brain-penetrant NMT inhibitors proposed for the treatment of human African trypanosomiasis (HAT) caused by Trypanosoma brucei.(17) NMT inhibitors were shown to kill Plasmodium falciparum blood-stage parasites and reduce parasite burden in a mouse model of Plasmodium berghei (P. berghei) infection.(18) Multiple small molecule inhibitors have been demonstrated to inhibit Plasmodium NMT,(19,20) and a series of selective NMT inhibitors have been shown to be effective against liver-stage Plasmodium parasites.(21,22) Moreover, while the breadth of the impact of NMT inhibition across the Plasmodium life cycle remains largely unexplored, chemical inhibition of NMT prevents completion of intraerythrocytic development in P. falciparum, in part due to inhibition of the inner membrane complex formation.(23) Thus, selective NMT inhibitors could provide both a tool for probing biological features of Plasmodium parasites and could result in the development of a drug that targets multiple life cycle stages.
    Plasmodium parasites express a single copy of NMT in their genome. Humans express two isoforms of NMT (NMT1 and NMT2), which have overlapping but also distinct substrate specificities (reviewed in ref (14)). For NMT to be a viable antimalarial target, it is critical to identify small molecules that exhibit differential inhibition of Plasmodium NMT and the two human NMTs. To date, obtaining selective compounds has been a challenge as Plasmodium and human NMTs share approximately 50% amino acid identity. Specifically, the proteins exhibit high conservation in the myrCoA binding site but are more divergent in the peptide binding site. While multiple inhibitors of Plasmodium NMT have been described, selectivity of inhibitors over human NMT remains a major challenge.(14,21) Here, we describe an industry-academia collaborative effort to identify selective inhibitors against P. falciparum and Plasmodium vivax (P. vivax) NMTs.

    High-Throughput Screening Identifies Plasmodium NMT Inhibitors

    To facilitate a broad approach to identifying novel scaffolds for targeting Plasmodium NMT, we screened the GlaxoSmithKline high-throughput screening (GSK HTS) collection for activity against P. vivax NMT. Approximately 1.8 million compounds were initially tested for activity at a single concentration of 10 μM. Specifically, we adapted a biochemical NMT activity assay(24) to the 1536-well format. P. vivax NMT was used for primary screening because of the higher stability of the recombinant enzyme compared to the P. falciparum homologue. NMT activity was indirectly measured through detection of free CoA by the thiol-reactive probe 7-diethylamino-3-(4′-maleimidylphenyl)-4-methylcoumarin (CPM). The reaction of CPM with the thiol group of CoA yields a highly fluorescent adduct (Supporting Information, Figure S1). Nonspecific plate patterns were detected and corrected with in-house developed algorithms.(25) We observed a signal-to-background ratio of ∼3.6. The average Z′ value, which is a statistical parameter that evaluates assay performance,(26) of all plates was ∼0.7 (Figure 1). Together, these metrics suggest that, despite a reasonably low signal-to-background metric, the assay is robust. An average statistical cutoff (calculated as average % inhibition + 3.5 standard deviation for each plate) of 28% inhibition at 10 μM was used for primary hit selection, resulting in 20245 hits (hit rate = 1.3%).
    These preliminary hits were retested in duplicate at a single concentration (10 μM). Plate patterns were corrected as described previously.(22) A cutoff of + 3SD of the uninhibited control values was used to mark hits. After this confirmatory round, 9402 compounds were active in at least one of the replicates (Figure 1). To focus on the most relevant hits, we raised the cutoff to 75% inhibition, excluded a subset of compounds based on lipophilicity (high clogP) and a predicted solubility forecast index(27) (Figure 1). Using these criteria, 3917 hits were selected and progressed to dose–response testing.
    In addition to the screening of naïve chemical diversity, we examined compounds that are part of the Tres Cantos antimalarial set (TCAMS).(28) These compounds have been previously demonstrated to be active against P. falciparum asexual blood stages(28) and, in some cases, also against P. berghei liver stages.(29) For this subset of compounds, we slightly relaxed our criteria and considered a compound a “hit” if its level of inhibition exceeded the mean + 2.5SD. This resulted in an additional 511 hits. Once these criteria were applied, 3917 confirmed hits plus the additional 511 hits from the TCAMS set were selected for dose–response testing.

    Hit Compounds Selected on the Basis of Potency and Selectivity

    Selected compounds were tested against P. falciparum NMT, P. vivax NMT, and human NMT1 and NMT2 to quantify the degree of selectively between plasmodial and human NMTs. They were tested in duplicate at 11 concentrations in threefold serial dilution starting at a maximum concentration of 100 μM. To eliminate false positives that arise due to their capacity to promote thiol-trapping or interfere in a nonspecific way with the screen assay, we tested all hit compounds in the enzymatic assay replacing the PvNMT enzyme in the reaction mixture with CoA (counter screen). Compounds with a pIC50 > 4 in the CoA counter screen were flagged as interfering and were removed from downstream analysis. Compounds that inhibited PfNMT with a pIC50 of <5 were deprioritized. Using these selection criteria, we identified 389 compounds for further characterization.

    Compounds Were Prioritized Based on Potency, Selectivity, and Chemical Properties

    We next analyzed what chemical scaffolds had been elucidated by our screening efforts. A complete-linkage clustering algorithm with a Tanimoto similarity threshold(30) of 0.55 identified 198 clusters. Ninety-three had more than one member in the cluster and 105 clusters were singletons. To triage this set of compounds, a composite score was developed (Table 1). The score included contributions from the potency in inhibiting the enzymatic activity of PfNMT and selectivity against human HsNMT1 and HsNMT2. Compounds with more than one order of magnitude difference between pIC50 values against PfNMT and HsNMT1 and HsNMT2 were the highest scored. The number of analogues contained in each cluster also contributed to the composite score. We reasoned that multiple analogues within a cluster supported the notion that the overall profile of the chemical series could be improved by chemical modification. Thus, clusters with more than five analogues scored for 3 points while singletons did not receive any points. We selected compounds scoring more than 20 points (Table 1 and Figure 2A) for follow-up studies. After applying this filter, we were left with a total of 39 prioritized compounds from 23 chemical classes (Figure 3 and Table S1). The most balanced compounds in terms of enzymatic potency, selectivity against HsNMT1 and HsNMT2, and cytotoxicity in HepG2 cells were selected for the mode of action studies (compounds 2, 11, 12, 16, and 23, Table S2). Interestingly, none of these compounds exhibited potent activity against P. falciparum blood stages (Table S2), despite reasonably favorable chemical properties for cell permeability. Therefore, subsequent work will need to optimize structures with the aim of obtaining compounds that are effective in cell-based assays and, ultimately, in vivo.

    Biochemical Competition Studies

    To assess the direct interaction between selected hits and Plasmodium NMT and to begin to elucidate their mode of action, compounds 2, 11, 12, 16, and 23 were evaluated for the capacity to compete with a peptide substrate of PfNMT. A shift in the inhibitory capacity of the compounds 2, 12, and 23 was observed when the concentration of peptide changed from 2 to 20 μM, suggesting that those compounds are competitive with a peptide substrate (Figure 4A and Figure S2). Since the peptide pocket is known to be less conserved than the Myr-CoA site,(31) we expected compounds with high selectivity for PfNMT and PvNMT over HsNMT to bind the peptide binding pocket rather than the Myr-CoA binding site.

    Thermal Stability Is Dependent on Binding of Myristoyl-CoA

    To further evaluate the mechanism of binding of the hit compounds and to assess if the binding was dependent on the presence of myristoyl-CoA, molecules were tested for the ability to stabilize PfNMT. We monitored the increase in the melting temperature of PfNMT using the differential scanning fluorimetry (DSF) assay. Compounds were incubated with PfNMT in the presence or absence of myristoyl-CoA. The mixture was incubated at room temperature and then increased in a stepwise manner from 27 to 75 °C by 0.5 °C/min, with fluorescence measurements collected every 1 °C using a SYPRO orange protein stain. Fluorescence corresponded to the denaturation of PfNMT. In the presence of 100 μM myristoyl-CoA, all five compounds shifted the melting temperature of PfNMT, indicating that each of these compounds alters the stability of PfNMT in its myristoyl-CoA bound form (Figure 4B and Table 2). Interestingly, in the absence of myristoyl-CoA, only two of the five compounds (compounds 11 and 16) significantly increased the melting temperature of PfNMT (ΔTm (°C)). Both compounds exhibited high inhibitory activity in enzymatic assays. The correlation between the ΔTm in the presence of myristoyl-CoA and the pIC50 of each of the compounds suggests that the capacity to stabilize the NMT-myristoyl-CoA complex is related to the inhibitory efficacy of the given molecule.

    PvNMT Structure Reveals Compound in Peptide Binding Pocket

    To underpin investigations into the binding mode of the hit compounds, we sought to interrogate the 3D structure of P. vivax NMT (PvNMT) bound to compound 2 and myristoyl-CoA. Of the residues within 5 Å of the ligand binding pocket of PvNMT, only 2 of 23 residues are not conserved with P. falciparum NMT. Specifically, the residues in the inhibitor binding pocket that differ are PvNMT(Tyr334) and PvNMT(Tyr212). In each case, the residue is replaced by a phenylalanine residue in PfNMT.(32) Due to this high-sequence identity, the enzymes have been used interchangeably for structural biology studies, with PvNMT offering the more robust platform for rapid crystallography.
    The ternary complex of PvNMT with compound 2 and myristoyl-CoA showed that 2 binds in the peptide binding pocket (Figure 5), consistent with our finding that compound 2 outcompetes a PfNMT-binding peptide in competition studies. A comparison to other PvNMT structures shows that, similar to other compounds, 2 occupies a hydrophobic pocket adjacent to the catalytic C-terminal residue composed of resides Val 96, Phe103, Phe105, Tyr211, and Leu388. In contrast to other high affinity inhibitors,(22,32−34) our structure suggests that 2 does not make direct interaction with the carboxylate of the C-terminal residue Leu410 that is at a distance of more than 6 Å away. Rather, a direct hydrogen-bonding interaction between the N atom of the amide group of Asn365 (1.9 Å) and the O atom of the morpholine moiety is observed (Figure 5). Asn365 is also involved in the stabilization of the inward conformational pose for the key residue His213, via an extra hydrogen-bonding interaction from the ligand via the Asn365 side-chain amide to the imidazole side chain (Figure 5), consistent with the described role for His213 in Saccharomyces cerevisiae NMT.(35)
    The high-resolution structure of 2 in the ternary complex with PvNMT allows visualization of multiple conformations of flexible residues. His213 is seen in two conformations suggesting mobility of the residue and flexibility of the binding pocket. A water-mediated interaction between the −NH2 group of Asp385 and the −CO group of the morpholine amide moiety was also observed. The −CO group favors the outward orientation of Tyr211 that also presents a stacking interaction with the biaryl region. A cluster of water molecules surrounds the pyridone moiety of 2 suggesting there is room for further medicinal chemistry efforts to improve affinity of the scaffold. The high resolution obtained for this structure will enable free-energy perturbation (FEP+) studies to evaluate the potential displacement of water molecules and enhance enzyme-ligand interaction.
    Despite high similarity of the drug binding pocket between HsNMT1, HsNMT2, and PvNMT, we observe a high level of selectivity of compound 2 (and other reported compounds) for PvNMT and PfNMT over the human proteins. Previously, selectivity for Plasmodium and Leishmania NMT inhibition over human NMTs has been described to result from a conformational change of Tyr211 within the parasite NMT.(32,36) Specifically, steric hindrance by the structurally equivalent Tyr296 in HsNMT1 contributes to selectivity if the ΔG value for the inward to outward transition in the tyrosine conformer is more positive than it is for Tyr211 in the Plasmodium NMTs. A nonhydrolyzable MyrCoA (nhMyrCoA) cofactor was previously used to obtain crystals of the PvNMT binary complex.(32) To explore the origin of selectivity of 2, we compared the ternary complex structure of PvNMT-MyrCoA-2 that we report here with the binary PvNMT-nhMyrCoA and HsNMT1-MyrCoA structures. Superimposition of the structures of PvNMT-myristoyl-CoA-2 and hNMT1-myristoyl-CoA reveals that the equivalent tyrosine (Tyr296) in the human enzyme occupies a position that would clash with 2 and confirms the similarity of the inhibitor binding pockets in the two enzymes and the close superposition of corresponding residues (Figure 5B). Our data support Tyr211 (HsNMT1 Tyr296) as a selectivity-conferring residue.(34,37) Specifically, Tyr211 adopts an alternate conformation in the complex PvNMT-MyrCoA-2 when compared to the conformation in the binary complex of PvNMT-nhMyrCoA. Tyr211 interacts via stacking with one of the aromatic rings of the biaryl region, and this conformation is favored by the hydrogen-bonding network extending from the ligand via the Asp385 carbonyl group to the Tyr211 hydroxyl one, binding to the enzyme to occlude the inwardly oriented conformation of the side chain of the tyrosine residue (Figure 5B). Comparison of the PvNMT-myristoyl-CoA-2 structure to that of drug-resistant PvNMT(G386E) bound to resistance breaking compound DDD85646 (PDB: 6MAZ)(37) shows that although 2 is bound to the same conformation of the Tyr211 residue as DDD85646, the resistance inducing the side chain of G386E is likely to make a steric clash with 2 and our expectation would be that PvNMT(G386) would be at least partially resistant to inhibition by compound 2.
    Taken together, our data present a cohesive framework for further investigation into the chemical origin of selectivity for Plasmodium NMT inhibition over human NMT inhibition. These data suggest that this series is suitable for further lead optimization activities with the purpose of identifying one or more compounds to progress to in vivo studies.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Plasmodium NMT is a validated drug target against Plasmodium and other parasite species. The optimal compound to target Plasmodium infection would include activity against P. falciparum and P. vivax, with very little cross reactivity with the related human enzyme(s). In this study, we performed a high-throughput screen that identified a range of compounds that inhibit PfNMT and PvNMT with high selectivity over HsNMT1 and HsNMT2. The top hits from our screen include compounds with at least five novel scaffolds. Compounds from each of these five scaffolds stabilize the NMT-myristoyl CoA complex, and at least a subset of these inhibitors bind in the NMT peptide binding pocket. Thus, our study reveals the capacity of a wide range of chemical scaffolds to bind the peptide binding pocket and retain selectivity for Plasmodium NMT over human NMTs, despite structural similarities between the Plasmodium and human NMT binding pockets. Our work thus further supports the notion that NMT is a good drug target for Plasmodium drug development. Moreover, the role of Plasmodium NMT across multiple stages of the Plasmodium life cycle may suggest that compounds that potently target Plasmodium NMTs could be multistage inhibitors and thus serve as a powerful tool for combating malaria worldwide.

    Compounds and Reagents

    The GSK HTS collection consists of ∼1.8 million small molecules of >90% purity, determined by LCMS. All compounds used in follow-up studies were evaluated to be >95% pure as determined by HPLC. Compounds tested in HTS and post-HTS assays were used from DMSO stock solutions stored at −20 °C, whereas compound samples for crystallography studies were prepared from solids. Myristoyl-coenzyme A ammonium salt of 99% purity was purchased from tebu-bio (synthesized by MedChem101). The peptide H-GLYVSRLFNRLFQKK-NH2 derived from the N-terminal sequence (aa2-16) of the P. falciparum ADP-ribosylation factor (PlasmoDB ID: PF3D7_1020900) was purchased from tebu-bio with >95% purity. The probe 7-diethylamino-3-(4′-maleimidylphenyl)-4-methylcoumarin was purchased from Invitrogen (cat. no: D346). Potassium phosphate, Triton X-100, ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), sodium acetate, and glacial acetic acid were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich.

    Expression and Purification of NMT

    The DNA sequence encoding aa27-410 of P. vivax NMT, aa 27-410 of P. falciparum NMT, aa2-496 of human NMT1, and aa2-498 of human NMT2 was fused to an N-terminal 6xHis tag followed by a 3C cleavage site, codon-optimized for expression in E. coli (GenScript), and cloned into the pET11a vector using NdeI and BamHI restriction sites. The identity of each clone was confirmed by DNA sequencing. Bacteria (Rosetta (DE3)) were grown in autoinduction media with ampicillin and chloramphenicol for 4 h at 37 °C followed by 22 h at 18 °C. Cells were harvested by centrifugation at 10,000 rpm for 30 min. The pellet was lysed in 180 mL of 20 mM HEPES, 5% glycerol, 30 mM imidazole, 0.5% CHAPS, 21 mM MgCl2, and 200 μL β-mercaptoethanol and five protease inhibitor cocktail tablets (Roche), sonicated (5 s on/ 10 s off for a total of 15 min), and incubated with 4 μL of benzonase for 40 min. The lysate was run over a nickel affinity chromatography column (HisTRAP FF, GE Healthcare), using a binding buffer composed of 0.3 M NaCl, 20 mM HEPES, 5% glycerol, 30 mM imidazole at pH 7. NMT was eluted with 0.3 M NaCl, 20 mM HEPES, 5% glycerol, and 0.5 M imidazole at pH 7. Fractions containing NMT were collected, pooled, and concentrated using a YM-30 Amicon concentrator. Fractions were then 100-fold diluted in anion exchange buffer (20 mM Tris–HCl, 1 mM TCEP; pH 8.9) and applied to HiTrap Q columns (GE Healthcare). Fractions were eluted with a linear gradient of anion exchange buffer with 500 mM NaCl over five column volumes. Fractions containing NMT were pooled, and N-terminal 6xHis tag removal was achieved by dialysis with His-MBP-3C (1:50 protease/protein) overnight at 4 °C in dialysis buffer composed of 25 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 500 mM NaCl, 5% glycerol, 1 mM TCEP, and 0.025% azide.(38) Cleaved NMT was recovered by applying the dialysate to a HisTRAP FF column (GE Healthcare) and collecting the flow-through and wash. The His-MBP-3C protease is removed from the target protein by binding to the column. Fractions containing NMT were pooled and concentrated and subjected to size exclusion chromatography Superdex 75 (GE Healthcare) and were isocratically eluted with size exclusion buffer 0.3 M NaCl, 20 mM HEPES, 5% glycerol, and 1 mM TCEP (pH 7). Fractions containing NMT were pooled, concentrated, and flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen. Purified NMT was stored at −80 °C. SDS-PAGE gels of purified PvNMT and PfNMT are presented in Figure S4.

    NMT Activity Assay

    The assay was adapted from Goncalves et al.(24) In the primary single-shot screen, compounds were tested against P. vivax NMT at a final compound concentration of 10 μM in a reaction volume of 4 μL. Briefly, 40 nL of 10 mM compound stock solution in 100% DMSO was dispensed into black 1536-well plates (Greiner, cat no. 782076) through acoustic energy using the Echo Liquid Handler (Labcyte). Reagent solutions were added to wells using a Thermo Scientific multidrop reagent dispenser with metal tip microcassettes (cat no. 24073295). Two microliters of P. vivax NMT (10 nM final concentration) in assay buffer (20 mM potassium phosphate, 0.5 mM EDTA, 0.01% (v/v) Triton X-100; pH 7.9–8.0) was added to the wells and incubated with the compounds for 10 min at RT. To start the reaction, 2 μL of assay buffer containing myristoyl-coenzyme A (10 μM final concentration), PfARF peptide substrate (10 μM final concentration), and the profluorescent probe 7-diethylamino-3-(4′-maleimidylphenyl)-4-methylcoumarin (CPM; Invitrogen) at a final concentration of 8 μM were added to the wells. NMT activity was indirectly monitored by measuring the byproduct of the myristoylation reaction, free CoA, using the thiol-reactive probe CPM that binds to CoA generating a fluorescent product (Supporting Information, Figure S1). Reagent concentrations were selected based on NMT Km values (Supporting Information, Figure S4). After 40 min of incubation at RT protected from light, the reaction was stopped by addition of 2 μL of 0.1 M sodium acetate buffer (pH 4.8). Fluorescent signals were measured using a PerkinElmer EnVision plate reader at Ex/Em = 380/485 nm (LANCE/DELFIA mirror bar code 412). Columns 11, 12, 35, and 36 of each plate did not contain compound but only DMSO and served as control for either the uninhibited reaction (columns 11 and 12) or for the fully inhibited reaction (columns 35 and 36, omission of NMT).
    The confirmation screen was run in duplicate using the above conditions. In dose–response experiments, compounds were tested in duplicate at 11 concentrations using threefold serial dilution with a maximum concentration of 100 μM under the above assay conditions. To exclude compounds that interfere with the assay either through fluorescence quenching or thiol-trapping mechanisms, a counter screen was run using the same assay conditions as above, but instead of NMT, the myristoylation byproduct CoA was added at a final concentration of 3 μM, which yields a similar signal as the uninhibited reaction.

    Artificial Membrane Permeability Assay

    The permeability rate is measured across a phospholipid bilayer system. The lipid is egg phosphatidyl choline (1.8%) and cholesterol (1%) dissolved in n-decane. This is applied to the bottom of the microfiltration filter inserts in a Transwell plate. Phosphate buffer (50 mM Na2HPO4 with 0.5% 2-hydroxypropyl-β-cyclodextrin) at pH 7.4 is added to the top and bottom of the plate. The lipids are allowed to form bilayers across the small holes in the filter. The permeation experiment is initiated by adding the compound to the bottom well and stopped at a predetermined elapsed time. The compound permeates through the membrane to enter the acceptor well. The compound concentration in both the donor and acceptor compartments is determined by liquid chromatography after 3 h of incubation at room temperature.(39,40) The permeability (logPapp) measuring how fast molecules pass through the black lipid membrane is expressed in nm/s.

    Data Analysis

    Data from the primary screen, confirmation screen, and dose–response experiments were analyzed with IDBS ActivityBase XE (IDBS, Guilford, U.K.). Plate patterns were detected and corrected with in-house developed algorithms.(29) For IC50 determination, dose response curves were fitted using a Michaelis–Menten equation, either with IDBS ActivityBase XE or GraFit software (Erithacus Software, Horley, U.K.).

    In Vitro Activity of Compounds against P. falciparum

    To assess the effect of compounds on the growth of asexual blood-stage parasites of P. falciparum 3D7A (MR4), a [3H] hypoxanthine incorporation assay was used.(41) Five hundred nanoliters of 200× compound stock solution in DMSO was predispensed into 96-well flat bottom plates (Costar, cat. no. 3599) at a single concentration of 2 μM. One hundred microliters of a culture of parasitized red blood cells (0.5% parasitemia and 2% hematocrit) in RPMI-1640, albumax, and 5 μM hypoxanthine was added to the compounds and incubated for 24 h at 37 °C, 5% CO2, 5% O2, and 95% N2 followed by addition of 3H-hypoxanthine. After another 24 h of incubation, parasites were harvested on a glass fiber filter using a TOMTEC Cell Harvester 96. Filters were dried and incorporation of 3H-hypoxanthine was determined using melt-on scintillator sheets and a MicroBeta counter. Data were normalized using the incorporation of the positive control (parasitized red blood cells without compound).

    Crystallization and Structure Determination

    The PfNMT protein (12.2 mg/mL in buffer) was incubated with 1 mM myristoyl-NMT and 1 mM compound for 20 min at room temperature prior to crystallization according to procedures described previously.(32) Crystals were prepared by using the sitting drop vapor diffusion method at 20 ° C. The droplets comprised 0.5 μL of protein and 0.5 μL of reservoir solution (crystallant condition). Sparse matrix crystallization screens were set up as well as optimization screens based on previous crystallization methods.(32) Crystals grew to a final size of 200 × 50 × 50 μm3 after 6 weeks. PfNMT/compound cocrystals were transferred to the cryo-protectant containing 25% PEG300 and flash-frozen by submerging in liquid nitrogen. Crystals were screened for X-ray diffraction at Beamlines ID21G Advanced Photon Source, Argonne National Laboratory, Chicago, IL. Only cocrystals containing compound 2 diffracted to resolution sufficient for X-ray data collection. A data set to 1.6 A resolution was collected using synchrotron radiation. Data were processed and reduced using the program XDS/XSCALE.(42) Crystal structure analysis was carried out by molecular replacement using coordinates of PfNMT deposited at the Protein Data Bank (PDB code: 2YND). The anisotropy of diffraction was corrected by TLS refinement using the program REFMAC5.(43) Conventional crystallographic rigid body, positional, and temperature factor refinements were calculated with REFMAC5. The model was completed using the interactive three-dimensional graphic program WinCoot. The atomic coordinates for PfNMT in complex with a compound and myristoyl-CoA have been deposited in the Protein Data Bank, Research Collaboratory for Structural Bioinformatics at Rutgers University, under the accession code 6NXG.
