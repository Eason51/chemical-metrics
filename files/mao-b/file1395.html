    Abstract

    The bisubstrate analogue strategy is a promising approach to develop potent and selective inhibitors for protein methyltransferases. Herein, the interactions of a series of bisubstrate analogues with protein N-terminal methyltransferase 1 (NTMT1) were examined to probe the molecular properties of the active site of NTMT1. Our results indicate that a 2-C to 4-C atom linker enables its respective bisubstrate analogue to occupy both substrate- and cofactor-binding sites of NTMT1, but the bisubstrate analogue with a 5-C atom linker only interacts with the substrate-binding site and functions as a substrate. Furthermore, the 4-C atom linker is the optimal and produces the most potent inhibitor (Ki,app = 130 ± 40 pM) for NTMT1 to date, displaying more than 3000-fold selectivity for other methyltransferases and even for its homologue NTMT2. This study reveals the molecular basis for the plasticity of the active site of NTMT1. Additionally, our study outlines general guidance on the development of bisubstrate inhibitors for any methyltransferases.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Protein methylation, a widespread epigenetic modification that is catalyzed by protein methyltransferases (PMTs), plays critical roles in many cellular processes such as gene expression, transcriptional regulation, DNA replication, and damage repair. Dysregulation of PMT expression has been implicated in a variety of diseases including cancer, cardiovascular, neurodegenerative, and other diseases.(1−5) Therefore, there has been emerging interest in the development of selective inhibitors for PMTs as potential therapeutic agents. Recently, several inhibitors of PMTs have been developed and have entered human clinical trials, such as DOT1-like protein inhibitor EPZ-5676 (phase I/II), protein arginine methyltransferase 5 inhibitor GSK3326595 (phase I/II), enhancer of zeste homologue 2 inhibitors EPZ-6438 (phase I/II), and CPI-1205 (phase I/II).(2)
    PMTs utilize the cofactor S-adenosyl-l-methionine (SAM) as a methyl donor to transfer the methyl group from SAM onto the substrate with concomitant generation of S-adenosine-l-homocysteine (SAH) and methylated substrate.(2) The PMT-catalyzed methylation reaction typically undergoes a Bi–Bi mechanism and involves the formation of a ternary complex.(6) Bisubstrate analogues that occupy both substrate- and cofactor-binding sites have the potential to achieve higher binding affinities than a substrate analogue.(7−10) Furthermore, bisubstrate analogues may offer superior selectivity for their targeted enzymes.(9,11,12) On account of a shared SAM cofactor-binding pocket across PMT families, it is rational to hypothesize that bisubstrate analogues can be a general approach to offer potent and specific inhibitors for each individual PMT. Recent progresses have been made in the development of bisubstrate analogues for a few PMTs, such as protein N-terminal methyltransferase 1 (NTMT1),(11,13,14) coactivator-associated arginine methyltransferase 1 (CARM1),(15) protein arginine methyltransferase 1 (PRMT1),(16) and PRMT6.(17) These bisubstrate analogues were created by connecting a SAM analogue to a substrate or a functional group of the corresponding substrate. However, the potency of those bisubstrate analogues for their respective targets varied from nanomolar to micromolar. This variable potency imposed the need for explicit instructions for the design of bisubstrate analogues for PMTs.
    The aforementioned need has prompted us to investigate how the linker length would affect the interaction of bisubstrate analogue to its target PMT. Here, we used NTMT1 as a model system to examine the optimal length of the linker, with the intention to provide a general guide to facilitate the design of optimal bisubstrate analogues for other PMTs. NTMT1 recognizes a unique X-P-K/R motif at the protein α-N-terminus.(18−20) It methylates centromere-specific histone H3 variant centromere protein A/B, the regulator of chromatin condensation 1, damaged DNA-binding protein 2, and poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase 3.(21−26) Hence, NTMT1 has been implicated in regulating chromosome segregation, mitosis, and DNA damage repair.(22,24,27) We have previously developed bisubstrate analogues for NTMT1. Among them, compound NAH-C3-PPKRIA showed an IC50 value of 35 ± 2 nM in a matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization-mass spectrometry (MALDI-MS)-based assay and displayed more than 100-fold selectivity for other methyltransferases (Figure 1A).(11) In addition, compound NAH-C3-GPRRRS showed an IC50 value of 0.94 ± 0.16 μM for NTMT1.(14) Both compounds have a propylene linker, which raises the question if a propylene linker is optimal. To address this question, we design and synthesize a series of NTMT1 bisubstrate analogues with variable linker lengths to probe the active site of NTMT1.

    Results and Discussion

    Design and Synthesis

    The linear distance between the sulfur atom of SAH and the nitrogen atom of substrate ranges 4.6–5.5 Å in the ternary structures of NTMT1.(18,19) A propyl linker was able to offer high potent inhibitor for NTMT1.(11) Herein, we proposed to vary the linker from 2 to 5 methylene groups to explore the optimal length to build bisubstrate analogues 1–7 (Figure 1B and Scheme 1). In addition, the peptide sequence was also altered to ensure that the effect of different linkers was not sequence-specific. We chose the peptide starting with Gly due to its synthetic versatility. Meanwhile, compounds 8–12 were designed to investigate the contribution of each part of the bisubstrate analogue to the inhibitory activity. The synthesis of compounds 1–7 and 10–12 was accomplished in a convergent manner by reacting the primary amines (16–19 and 24–26) with α-bromo peptide on resin, followed by acid treatment (Schemes 1 and 2).(14) Compounds 8 and 9 were obtained by acidic deprotection of 14 and 18, respectively (Scheme 1). The primary amines 18 and 19 were prepared through reductive amination of a common starting material 14 with the respective aldehydes followed by removal of the protection group (Scheme 1).(28,29) Commercially available amine 20 was subjected to reductive amination with aldehyde 21 to provide compound 22. The primary amine 24 was obtained after removal of the Fmoc group of compound 23 (Scheme 2).

    MALDI-MS Methylation Assay

    Compounds 1–4 were first subjected to the methylation assay catalyzed by NTMT1 in the presence of SAM to first check if they may act as possible substrates. Surprisingly, compound 4 containing the 5-C atom linker was able to be methylated by NTMT1 (Figure 2), indicating 4 as a substrate for NTMT1.

    Inhibition Evaluation

    The inhibitory activities of compounds 1–3 and 5–7 were evaluated in the SAH hydrolase (SAHH)-coupled fluorescence assay with 10 min of preincubation time.(30) Both SAM and the peptide substrate GPKRIA were at their respective Km values. To compare the inhibitory activity under the same condition, we resynthesized and purified 1, 2, and 6, respectively. Their IC50 values were lower than the previous values,(14) which may be caused by an improved S/N ratio with a different GPKRIA peptide substrate and fluorescent dye ThioGlo4. Among the NAH-GPKRIA series, compounds 3 (IC50 = 82 ± 17 nM) containing a 4-C atom length displayed the best inhibition for NTMT1 (Table 1). When the linker length decreased from a 4-C atom to a 3-C and 2-C, compounds 2 (IC50 = 130 ± 40 nM) and 1 (IC50 = 260 ± 70 nM) exhibited a 1.5- and 3-fold decrease compared to 3, respectively. Therefore, a 4-C atom length is optimal for the NAM-GPKRIA series. To further validate the optimal linker length, we synthesized three additional bisubstrate analogues that contained a tetrapeptide instead of hexapeptide in the substrate portion. For NAH-GPKR bisubstrate analogues, compounds 5 and 6 showed less than twofold activity decrease compared to their respective NAH-GPKRIA compounds 1 and 2, while compound 7 displayed comparable inhibition with compound 3 (Table 1). Furthermore, the 4-C atom linker is also the best inhibitor, as 7 (IC50 = 84 ± 12 nM) showed two- and fourfold better activity than 6 and 5, respectively. Therefore, the 4-C atom linker is the most favorable length for NTMT1 bisubstrate analogues in both the NAH-GPKRIA and NAH-GPKR series.

    The bisubstrate analogues contained three parts: a SAM analogue 8 (NAH), a linker, and a peptide substrate. To understand the contribution of each component of the bisubstrate analogue to the inhibitory activity for NTMT1, a deconstruction strategy was applied to design and synthesize only partial structures of the bisubstrate analogue (Figure 3): (1) compound 8 contained only the NAH part, (2) compound 9 with only the NAH and linker, (3) compound 10 without the adenine portion, (4) compound 11 without the NAH part, and (5) compound 12 without the aspartic acid moiety of the SAH. Compounds 10–12 were determined to be substrates for NTMT1 (Figure S1). Compound 9 displayed an IC50 value higher than 10 μM in the SAHH-coupling assay. Since 8 is a potent inhibitor (IC50 = 23 ± 2 nM) for SAHH, it does not exhibit significant inhibition against NTMT1 (IC50 > 10 μM) through an orthogonal MALDI-MS inhibition assay (Figure S2). These results suggested that each part of the bisubstrate inhibitor was critical for its inhibitory activity for NTMT1.

    Time-Dependent Inhibition Study

    The measured IC50 values for compounds 2, 3, and 7 converged to the enzyme concentration (100 nM) present in the assay (Table 1), suggesting tight-binding inhibition. Therefore, Morrison’s equation is a better method to analyze the concentration–response data for these tight-binding inhibitors to define their apparent Ki values.(31,32) The tight-binding inhibitors often exhibit slow binding traits, so compounds 1–3 were chosen to investigate the effects of preincubation time on the inhibitory activity of tight-binding inhibitors (Figure 4).(31,32) The Ki,app values of the three compounds were inversely proportional to the preincubation time (Figure 4B,D,F), indicating their slow binding feature. The results showed the interaction between the compounds and NTMT1 reached equilibrium after 30 min of incubation. Compound 3 containing a 4-C atom linker displayed the lowest Ki,app value (130 ± 40 pM) (Figure 4B) after 120 min, further substantiating its slow and tight binding feature ([E]/Ki,app > 100).(31,32) When the linker length decreased from a 4-C atom to a 3-C and 2-C, compounds 2 (Ki,app = 1.3 ± 0.6 nM) and 1 (Ki,app = 5.4 ± 2.6 nM) exhibited a 10- and 40-fold decrease in comparison with 3, respectively. The above results infer decent plasticity in the transition state of NTMT1 to accommodate a linker length ranging from 2-C to 4-C atom. Among them, a 4-C atom linker is the optimal length to achieve the best inhibition. Notably, a 5-C atom linker would convert a tight-binding bisubstrate analogue into a substrate for NTMT1 (Figure 2), indicating an upper limit for the linker length to achieve potent inhibition.

    Isothermal Titration Calorimetry (ITC)

    NTMT2 is a closely related homologue of NTMT1, which shares more than 50% sequence similarity and the same X-P-K recognition motif.(33) To assess the interactions of the bisubstrate analogues with both enzymes, the binding affinities of 1–4 for both NTMT1 and NTMT2 were determined by the isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC) experiment (Figure 5). All four compounds bound to NTMT1, especially compound 3 (Kd1/d2 = 0.31 μM/2.47 nM), showing the tightest binding affinity. Notably, the Kd values of bisubstrate analogues were higher than their Ki,app values, except for compound 4 (Kd1/d2 = 1.6/0.48 μM). This difference may be caused by endogenous amounts of SAM/SAH binding to recombinant NTMTs and slow binding characteristic of tight-binding inhibitors 1–3.(18,33) However, the ITC binding data was still informative since the bisubstrate analogues at 1 mM showed negligible interactions with NTMT2.(33) This delicate selectivity further corroborates the benefits of bisubstrate analogues on account of their high specificity.

    MALDI-MS Methylation Inhibition Assay

    To further confirm the inhibitory activity of bisubstrate inhibitor, the MALDI-MS methylation assay was performed as an orthogonal assay to monitor the production of methylated peptides with both substrate peptide GPKRIA and SAM at their Km values after 10 min of preincubation time (Figure 6A,B). At 50 nM compound 3, trimethylation of the GPKRIA peptide was substantially reduced. At 200 nM compound 3, trimethylation of the GPKRIA peptide was barely detected (Figure 6B).

    Selectivity Assessment

    The selectivity of 3 for NTMT1 was further examined against a panel of methyltransferases that shared a SAM-binding pocket, which also included the coupling enzyme SAHH that has a SAH-binding site and was used in the fluorescence assay (Figure 6C and Table S1). The compound barely showed inhibitory activity over euchromatic histone-lysine N-methyltransferase 2 (G9a), SET domain-containing protein 7 (SETD7), and nicotinamide N-methyltransferase (NNMT) at 100 μM. At 33 μM, 3 demonstrated less than 50% inhibition over protein arginine methyltransferase 1 (PRMT1), protein arginine methyltransferase 3 (PRMT3), Trypanosoma brucei protein arginine methyltransferase 7 (TbPRMT7), and SAHH. Therefore, compound 3 showed more than 3000-fold selectivity for NTMT1 over other methyltransferases.

    Cocrystal Structures of Compounds 1 and 4 with NTMT1

    To understand the molecular basis of the effect of different linkers, we obtained the cocrystal structures of bisubstrate analogues 1 and 4 with NTMT1 (Figure 7). The binary complex X-ray crystal structure of the NTMT1–1 complex (PDB: 6WJ7) illuminates the inhibition mechanism since compound 1 does occupy both cofactor- and substrate-binding sites of NTMT1. It also overlays well with previously reported bisubstrate inhibitor NAH-C3-PPKRIA in the X-ray crystal structure of NTMT1-NAH-C3-PPKRIA (PDB code: 6DTN) (Figure 7B). The SAM analogue moiety of 1 in the binary complex binds almost the same as NAH-C3-PPKRIA, which is nearly identical to SAH in the ternary complex of substrate peptide/SAH with NTMT1. When increasing the carbon linker to 5-C atom linker, the peptide part of the compound 4 retains its interaction with the substrate-binding pocket and overlays very well with the PPKRIA peptide portion of NAH-C3-PPKRIA (Figure 7D–F). However, the NAH moiety of compound 4 no longer occupied the SAM-binding pocket. Interestingly, the SAM analogue part inserted into a new pocket adjacent to the substrate-binding pocket, which resulted in the exposure of the secondary amine (α-N-terminal amine of the peptide portion) of compound 4 to receive the methyl group from SAM. Nevertheless, it infers that the length of the 5-C atom linker is too long to allow the bisubstrate analogue 4 to simultaneously occupy both SAM- and peptide-binding sites.

    Inhibition Mechanism Studies

    Despite additional efforts to screen and optimize the conditions, the cocrystal structure of NTMT1 in complex with 2 or 3 was not obtained. Hence, inhibition mechanism studies were performed to confirm the binding modes for 4-C atom linker compound. Since the cocrystal structures of NAH-C3-PPKRIA containing the 3-C linker revealed that the compound could simultaneously occupy SAM- and peptide-binding sites, it was reasonable to assume that compound 2 with 3-C linker could also interact with both SAM- and peptide-binding sites. So, inhibition mechanism studies for compound 3 were performed along with compounds 1 and 2 as the control under the condition of 2 h of preincubation (Figure 8). Even at both 6 and 12Km concentrations of the peptide substrate, there was slight competition for compound 3. Moreover, ∼10% of enzymatic activity was recovered in the presence of 0.3 μM or higher concentrations of compounds 2 and 3. When the competing peptide substrate concentration was increased to 50Km, a right shift of the IC50 curves was observed for 2 and 3. In addition, ∼20% enzymatic activity was recovered, further reflecting their tight and slow binding character. Upon increasing the concentration of SAM from 6 to 25Km, similar trends were observed. The catalytic activity of NTMT1 could be recovered with a higher concentration of SAM or peptide, suggesting that the compounds could be competitive with the peptide and SAM. These results indicated that all three bisubstrate inhibitors 1–3 occupied both SAM- and peptide substrate-binding sites in a reversible manner.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    This series of tight-binding and selective bisubstrate analogues to NTMT1 represent valuable tools to shed light on the plasticity in the transition state of NTMT1 since it can accommodate a linker length from 2-C to 4-C atom. Among them, a 4-C atom linker of compound 3 is the optimal length to achieve the best inhibition with a Ki,app value of 130 pM. Notably, a 5-C atom linker of compound 4 would convert a tight-binding bisubstrate analogue to a substrate for NTMT1, suggesting the upper limit of the aforementioned plasticity. The cocrystal structure of 4 in complex with NTMT1 (PDB code: 6PVB) implied that extending the linker to a 5-C atom linker may cause tension to prevent the bisubstrate analogue from engaging with both binding sites. Given the transient nature of the transition state, these bisubstrate analogues provide dynamic snapshots of the reorganization within the active site of NTMT1 during catalysis. Furthermore, top inhibitor 3 (NAH-C4-GPKRIA) containing a 4-C atom linker showed picomolar inhibition (Ki,app = 130 ± 40 pM) for NTMT1 and more than 100 000-fold selectivity for other MTases. In addition, all bisubstrate analogues 1–3 demonstrate no interaction with its close homologue NTMT2 despite similar catalytic sites of NTMT1/2.(33) This delicate selectivity further corroborates the benefits of bisubstrate analogues on account of their high specificity to offer tight and selective inhibitors for an individual target. However, increasing the carbon linker length to a 5-C atom resulted in a substrate instead of an inhibitor for NTMT1, which defined the scope or limit of plasticity. To our knowledge, this is the first case that delineates the optimal linker range of methyltransferase (MTase) bisubstrate analogue and provides the molecular basis for the obliterated activity for longer linkers.
    The linear distance between the sulfonium ion and the nitrogen atom that accept a methyl group is very similar for most PMTs, which ranges from 3.5 to 5.5 Å among the ternary complex of all examined PMTs including NTMT1, SET domain-containing protein 8, and euchromatic histone-lysine N-methyltransferase 1.(18,19,34−37) Therefore, we propose that a 3-C or 4-C atom linker would serve as a general and feasible starting point to build bisubstrate analogues for any MTase. The optimal linker length has also been corroborated by successful inhibitors for RNA MTase, as a 3-C atom linker showed better inhibition compared to a 2-C atom linker.(38) Small-molecule inhibitors have been reported targeting either SAM- or substrate-binding sites for MTase.(2,39) Future directions will focus on applying such linkers to conjugate small-molecule inhibitors that target the substrate-binding site with SAM mimic or SAM competitive small-molecule inhibitors to achieve higher selectivity and cellular potency for the respective methyltransferases.

    General Chemical Procedures

    The reagents and solvents were purchased from commercial sources (Fisher) and used directly. The intermediates and products were purified using an ISCO CombiFlash system and prepacked SiO2 cartridges. Final compounds were purified on preparative high-pressure liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC) performed on an Agilent 1260 Series system. Systems were run with 0–20% methanol/water gradient with 0.1% TFA. NMR spectra were acquired on a Bruker AV500 instrument (500 MHz for 1H NMR, 126 MHz for 13C NMR). Thin-layer chromatography–mass spectrometry (TLC–MS) was performed using an Advion CMS-L MS. Matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization-mass spectrometry (MALDI-MS) data were acquired in positive-ion mode using a Sciex 4800 MALDI TOF/TOF MS. The peptides were synthesized using a CEM Liberty Blue automated microwave peptide synthesizer with the manufacturers’ standard coupling cycles at a 0.1 mmol scale. The purity of final compounds was confirmed by a Waters LC–MS system and/or an Agilent 1260 Series HPLC system. Systems were run with 0–40% methanol/water gradient with 0.1% TFA. All target compounds showed purity >95% in RP-HPLC.

    NTMT1 Biochemical Assays and Enzyme Kinetics Studies

    A fluorescence-based SAHH-coupled assay was applied to study the IC50 values of compounds with both SAM and the peptide substrate at their Km values. After 10 min of preincubation, the methylation assay was performed under the following conditions in a final well volume of 40 μL: 25 mM Tris (pH = 7.5), 50 mM KCl, 0.01% Triton X-100, 5 μM SAHH, 0.1 μM NTMT1, 3 μM SAM, and 10 μM ThioGlo4. The inhibitors were added at concentrations ranging from 0.15 nM to 10 μM. After 10 min of incubation, reactions were initiated by the addition of 0.5 μM GPKRIA peptide. For 120 min preincubation, NTMT1 and compounds were preincubated for 120 min. Then, the preincubation mixture was added into the reaction mixture: 25 mM Tris (pH = 7.5), 50 mM KCl, 0.01% Triton X-100, 5 μM SAHH, 3 μM SAM, and 10 μM ThioGlo4. The reactions were initiated by the addition of 0.5 μM GPKRIA peptide. Fluorescence was monitored on a BMG CLARIOstar microplate reader with excitation at 400 nm and emission at 465 nm. Data were processed using GraphPad Prism software 7.0.
    For the inhibition mechanism studies, varying concentrations of SAM with a fixed concentration of GPKRIA (0.5 μM) or varying concentrations of GPKRIA with a fixed concentration of SAM (3 μM) were included in reactions at the concentration of inhibitors ranging from 0.15 nM to 10 μM. Fluorescence was monitored on a BMG CLARIOstar microplate reader with excitation at 400 nm and emission at 465 nm. All experiments were determined in duplicate. Data were processed using GraphPad Prism software 7.0.

    ITC

    Isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC) measurements were performed by a VP-ITC MicroCal calorimeter at 25 °C. Titration buffer contains 20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, and 0.5 mM TECP (tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine). Purified NTMT1 and NTMT2 were diluted with the ITC buffer to a final concentration of 30–50 μM, and the compounds were dissolved to 0.5–1 mM in the same buffer. The compound was titrated into the protein solution with 26 injections of 10 μL each. Injections were spaced 180 s with a reference power of 15 μcal/s. The ITC data were processed with MicroCal PEAQ-ITC analysis software using a one-site binding or two-site binding model.

    Time-Dependent Inhibition Study

    The assay was performed under the following conditions in a final well volume of 40 μL: 25 mM Tris (pH = 7.5), 50 mM KCl, 0.01% Triton X-100, 5 μM SAHH, 0.1 μM NTMT1, 3 μM SAM, 0.5 μM GPKRIA, and 10 μM ThioGlo4. The inhibitors were added at concentrations ranging from 0.15 nM to 10 μM. NTMT1 and compounds were preincubated for 30, 60, 90, and 120 min, Then, the preincubation mixture was added into the reaction mixture: 25 mM Tris (pH = 7.5), 50 mM KCl, 0.01% Triton X-100, 5 μM SAHH, 3 μM SAM, and 10 μM ThioGlo4. The reactions were initiated by the addition of 0.5 μM GPKRIA peptide. Fluorescence was monitored on a BMG CLARIOstar microplate reader with excitation at 400 nm and emission at 465 nm. Data were processed using GraphPad Prism software 7.0 with Morrison’s quadratic equation.

    Selectivity Assays

    The selectivity studies of G9a, SETD7, PRMT1, TbPRMT7, and NNMT were performed as previously described.(11,44)
    For PRMT3, the assay was also performed in a final well volume of 40 μL: 2.5 mM HEPES (pH = 7.0), 25 mM NaCl, 25 μM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), 50 μM tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine (TCEP), 0.01% Triton X-100, 5 μM SAHH, 1 μM PRMT3, 25 μM SAM, and 15 μM ThioGlo1.     MALDI-MS Methylation and Methylation Inhibition Assays

    MALDI-MS methylation and methylation inhibition assays were performed and analyzed via a Sciex 4800 MALDI TOF/TOF MS. Samples were analyzed by MALDI-MS with 2,5-dihydroxybenzoic acid matrix solution.
    The inhibition assay was performed in a final well volume of 40 μL: 0.1 μM NTMT1, 20 mM Tris (pH = 7.5), 50 mM KCl, 3 μM SAM, and various concentrations of compounds at 37 °C for 10 min before the addition of 0.5 μM GPKRIA peptide to initiate the reaction.     Cocrystallization and Structure Determination

    For cocrystallization, NTMT1 was mixed with 2 mM compounds incubated for 1 h at 4 °C, diluted sixfold in 1× tert-butyldimethylsilyl chloride (TBS), pH 7.6, with 1 mM TCEP, and then reconcentrated to 30 mg/mL. Broad matrix and optimization crystallization screening were performed using a Mosquito-LCP high-throughput crystallization robot (TTP LabTech) with a hanging-drop vapor diffusion method, incubated at 20 °C, and crystals harvested directly from the 96-well crystallization plates. Crystals containing 1 were grown in 32% (w/v) PEG 4000, 0.8 M lithium chloride, 0.1 M Tris–HCl (pH = 8.5), and crystals containing 4 were grown in 30% (w/v) PEG3350, 0.2 M Li2SO4, and 0.1 M bis–tris propane (pH = 7.0); all crystals were harvested directly from the drop and flash-cooled into liquid nitrogen.
    Data were collected on single crystals at 12.0 keV at the GM/CA-CAT ID-D beamline at the Advanced Photon Source, Argonne National Laboratory. The data were processed using the on-site automated pipeline with Fast DP, and molecular replacement was performed with Phaser-MR (PHENIX).(45,46) Whole model building was performed using COOT, and the subsequent refinement was done using phenix.refine (PHENIX).(46,47) Structure factors and model coordinates have been deposited in the Protein Data Bank with PDB IDs 6WJ7 (1) and 6PVB (4). Structure-related figures were made with PyMOL (Schrödinger) and annotated and finalized with Adobe Photoshop and Illustrator.

        Features and development of Coot
        Emsley, P.; Lohkamp, B.; Scott, W. G.; Cowtan, K.
        Acta Crystallographica, Section D: Biological Crystallography (2010), 66 (4), 486-501CODEN: ABCRE6; ISSN:0907-4449. (International Union of Crystallography)
        Coot is a mol.-graphics application for model building and validation of biol. macromols. The program displays electron-d. maps and at. models and allows model manipulations such as idealization, real-space refinement, manual rotation/translation, rigid-body fitting, ligand search, solvation, mutations, rotamers and Ramachandran idealization. Furthermore, tools are provided for model validation as well as interfaces to external programs for refinement, validation and graphics. The software is designed to be easy to learn for novice users, which is achieved by ensuring that tools for common tasks are 'discoverable' through familiar user-interface elements (menus and toolbars) or by intuitive behavior (mouse controls). Recent developments have focused on providing tools for expert users, with customisable key bindings, extensions and an extensive scripting interface. The software is under rapid development, but has already achieved very widespread use within the crystallog. community.
