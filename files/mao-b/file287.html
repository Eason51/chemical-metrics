    Abstract

    The development of small molecule inhibitors targeting GPVI has promising therapeutic role, as they inhibit arterial thrombosis with limited risk of bleeding. Among the compounds showing in vivo antithrombotic activity, the most active compound 6b (ED50 = 28.36 μmol/kg po in mice) showed improved inhibition for collagen (IC50 = 6.7 μM), CRP-XL (IC50 = 53.5 μM), and convulxin (CVX) (IC50 = 5.7 μM) mediated platelet aggregation as compared to losartan (LOS) (collagen, IC50 = 10.4 μM; CRP-XL, IC50 = 158 μM; CVX, IC50 = 11 μM) than any of its enantiomers S (6c) (collagen, IC50 = 25.3 μM; CRP-XL, IC50 = 181.4 μM; CVX, IC50 = 9 μM) and R (6d) (collagen, IC50 = 126.3 μM; CRP-XL, IC50 > 500 μM; CVX, IC50 = 86.8 μM). Compound 6b also inhibited platelet P-selectin expression and thus may diminish atherosclerosis. The molecular interactions of both enantiomers 6c and 6d at the GPVI receptor have been explained through docking studies.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The sequential activation of platelets on interaction with extracellular matrix proteins in artery wall is the key determinant leading to atherothrombotic complications culminating in coronary and cerebrovascular diseases.(1) GPVI, a type I transmembrane glycoprotein of the immunoreceptor family at platelet surface, has a decisive role in thrombus formation due to its participation in collagen mediated platelet activation and adhesion.(2) The clinical and experimental studies have established significant role of GPVI in the hemostasis. Regulation of its expression level has direct link in the prognosis of acute cardiovascular diseases, as enhanced expression of GPVI on circulating platelets is an early marker of myocardial infarction and ischemic stroke.(3, 4) The distinguishing role of GPVI in arterial thrombosis has invoked the development of competitive inhibitors targeting collagen–GPVI interaction having the potential to block GPVI-dependent thrombotic events to prevent cardiovascular complications. The current antiplatelet agents in the management of pathological thrombosis such as aspirin (cyclooxygenase inhibitor), clopidogrel and prasugrel (ADP receptor antagonists), cilostazol (phosphodiesterase inhibitor), and abciximab (fibrinogen receptor blocking antibodies) are associated with undesirable side effects like gastrointestinal bleeding, cerebral hemorrhage, and neutropenia.
    The extracellular domain of GPVI involved in binding of fibrous collagen is defined by two immunoglobin like domains D1 (N-terminal) and D2 (C-terminal) that share high level of sequence conservation with other leukocyte Ig-like receptor complex (LRC) such as FcαRI and the leukocyte Ig-like (LILR or LIR) and killer-cell Ig-like (KIR) receptor families.(5) Interestingly, triggering of platelet activation and downstream signaling through GPVI is not only restricted to physiological collagen but is extended up to the recognition of diverse ligands such as synthetic triple-helical collagen related peptides (CRP) that have a repeated GPO motif (glycine–proline–hydroxyproline) and CVX (CVX), a multimeric, C type lectin-like toxin from the venom of a rattlesnake having higher affinity toward GPVI as compared to collagen and CRP.(6-9) Monoclonal antibody binding assays have led to the determination of distinct sites with sharing of some common residues at the functional domain of GPVI for binding with collagen, CRP, and CVX.(9)
    A number of contemporary studies have strengthened the concept of blocking GPVI–collagen interactions as an attractive strategy for the treatment of thromboischemic diseases. The beneficial role of GPVI fusion (GPVI-Fc) proteins has been shown in the reduction of atheroprogression(10, 11) and infarct size with improvement in arterial remodeling(12) and regaining of myocardial function(13) in murine models. The GPVI receptor as a pharmacological target has a superlative aspect of minimal bleeding time that indicates that antiplatelet strategies targeting GPVI/collagen could be an attractive alternative for preventing pathological thrombosis without hemostatic negotiation.(14-16) Hence the development of small GPVI antagonists may have high therapeutic value.
    Losartan (I) (LOS) and its metabolite (EXP3179) (Figure 1) (concentration of both at 500 μM/L has significant effect on collagen (2 μg/mL) mediated platelet aggregation) were the first small molecules identified as GPVI inhibitors independent of angiotensin 1 (AT1) receptor antagonism.(17) In this study Grothusen et al. have reported that LOS prevents collagen I mediated platelet aggregation when stimulated by selective GPVI-receptor activating antibody 4C9 in human PRP (platelet-rich plasma), thus indicating that LOS prevents collagen mediated platelet aggregation. The first evidence about the direct interaction of LOS with GPVI protein was reported by Ono et al. where LOS has KD = 1.7 × 10–4 M for human GPVI receptor.(18) However, in a recent study it has been reported that LOS does not compete with collagen for binding to GPVI; instead it inhibits the collagen induced clustering of GPVI on platelets.(19) LOS is followed by other small molecule antagonists such as cinanserin (III) (IC50 = 35 μM and 40 μM for CRP (1 μg/mL) and collagen (2 μg/mL) mediated platelet aggregation, respectively; Figure 1)(20) discovered through structure based repurposing, S14161 (IV) (IC50 = 3.79 ± 1.17 μM for collagen (2 μg/mL) mediated platelet aggregation) a Pan-PI3K inhibitor(21) and other small molecules such as CompA(VI) (KD = (5.2 ± 0.4) × 10–5 M), CompB (VII) (no chemical shift perturbation in NMR based study), and CompC (VIII) (KD = (8.5 ± 0.5) × 10–4 M) derived through combination of losartan substructure and an active peptide molecule pep10L(YSDTDWLYFSTS) (KD = 5.7 × 10–5 M) (Figure 1). The binding site of LOS has been interpreted at the hydrophobic pocket of the GPVI receptor through NMR and docking based interaction studies.(18) This binding site has been also postulated to be the binding site for the peptide ligands (pep10L).(22) Although these compounds prevent GPVI mediated platelet activation, still there is a need for specific inhibitors that selectively block GPVI mediated platelet activation.
    We report here the development of a selective, racemic small-molecule antagonist with β-carboline core, initially screened from a series of potential antithrombotic candidates (based on in vivo activity). The design of these molecules was based on the observed antiaggregatory effect of 3S-1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-β-carboline-3-carboxylic acid moiety present in plant A. chinense G. Don.(23) There are ample evidence about β-carboline alkaloids preventing collagen-induced platelet aggregation.(24, 25) Further the synthesized molecules reported by us have a tryptophan substructure in the β-carboline core that is reported to be essential for the binding of the peptide antagonist pep10L at the GPVI active site.(22)
    The binding interactions for the most active compound in the series at the GPVI receptor were further determined with the help of docking studies. The insights gained in this study may provide opportunities for the development of small molecular therapeutics targeting GPVI.

    Chemistry

    Tryptophan was used as a precursor to synthesize the target 2,3-disubstituted pyridoindoles. Unlike our earlier reported(26) and successively used method(27) where the first step was the Pictet–Spengler cyclization of tryptophan with formaldehyde followed by the esterification of the 1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-9H-pyrido(3,4-b)indole carboxylic acid thus obtained, the procedure was changed where in the first step the methyl ester of tryptophan was synthesized using dry methanol and thionyl chloride (Figure 2). It was then followed by Pictet–Spengler cyclization reaction of this ester to give the hydrochloride of the methyl 1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-9H-pyrido(3,4-b)indole carboxylate hydrochloride salt which on treatment with sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3) yielded the free base (3). The free base (3) thus obtained was converted to respective sulfonamides (4a–l) by treatment with individual sulfonyl chlorides using a combination of DMF as solvent with triethylamine (TEA) at room temperature.
    The 1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-9H-pyrido(3,4-b)indole carboxamide (5) was synthesized by dissolving the ester (3) in methanolic ammonia under sealed conditions and kept overnight. The reaction of the 1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-9H-pyrido(3,4-b)indole carboxamide (5) with different sulfonyl chlorides yielded the corresponding sulfonamides (6a–i) under similar reaction conditions as described above for the conversion of esters to their sulfonamides. The amine substitutions at position 3 of the tetrahydropyridoindole were further explored by synthesizing compounds 9a–d. Compounds 9a–d were synthesized by de-esterification of the starting compounds 7a and 7b to yield their corresponding acids (8a, 8b) followed by the conversion of the acids to acid chlorides using oxalyl chloride. The reaction mixture containing the acid chlorides was concentrated under high vacuum conditions and without further isolation was treated with individual amines in the presence of dry THF and TEA to yield 9a–d.

    Structure–Activity Relationship

    In order to investigate the structural requirements involving the pyridoindole core, we have synthesized 25 derivatives with a substitution of diverse functionalities to rationalize the antithrombotic activity of these compounds in terms of structural modifications. The antithrombotic activity was examined in the mouse antithrombotic model employed primarily for the screening of antiplatelet agents. Through this screening method we have identified some promising antiplatelet agents (ED50 = 28–91 μmol/kg). Among these synthesized compounds the compound substituted by a methylsulfonyl and methyl carboxylate at 2 and 3 positions of 1,2,3,4-tetrahydropyridoindole (4a) showed good activity (ED50 = 54.09 μmol/kg). Since it has a asymmetric center at 3-position, its two enantiomers S (4b) and R (4c) were evaluated where the racemic 4a (50% protection; ED50 = 54.09 μmol/kg) showed better activity than the corresponding 4b (20% protection) and 4c (20% protection) enantiomers. However, the compounds having aryl/heteroaryl sulfonyl substituent at 2-position showed improved activity; viz., the compound with a quinonylsulfonyl, 4d (ED50 = 41.94 μmol/kg), and with a 2,5-dichlorophenylsulfonyl substitution 4g (ED50 = 36.1 μmol/kg). Thus, aromatic hydrophobic substituents at this position showed positive contribution toward activity. Similar to compound 4a, the enantiomers of the compounds 4d (4e and 4f) and 4g (4h and 4i) also exhibited lower activity than their corresponding racemates. The compound 4j with 5-dimethylaminonaphthylsulfonyl substitution showed promising antithrombotic activity, while 4k with 2,4,6-trimethylphenylsulfonyl substitution showed lower activity than 4j. The compound 4l with the 2-trifluoromethylphenylsulfonyl substitution showed improvement in activity. To explore further, compound 4g having a methyl ester at the 3-position of the 1,2,3,4- tetrahydropyridoindole moiety was modified to an amide functionality that resulted in compound 6a (ED50 = 68.34 μmol/kg). The other amide analogs such as compound 6b with a 4-methoxyphenylsulfonyl (ED50 = 28.36 μmol/kg), 6e (ED50 = 40.79 μmol/kg) with a 4-fluorophenylsulfonyl, and 6f with a 2-naphthylsulfonyl moiety showed better antithrombotic activity compared to 6a. Compound 6g with a 3-nitrophenylsulfonyl substitution at the N2 atom showed a moderate decrease in antiplatelet activity (ED50 = 80.68 μmol/kg). No significant loss in activity was observed for the amide 6h as compared to ester compound 4d, while improvement in activity was observed on amidation of the compound 4k to 6i. As the compound 6f with a naphthylsulfonyl and carboxamido groups at 2 and 3 positions, respectively, showed better activity, further modifications were performed at the 3-position where the carboxamide group was modified with propylamide (9a) and isopropylamide groups (9b). However, both these compounds 9a (ED50 = 66.54 μmol/kg) and 9b (ED50 = 91.19 μmol/kg) showed a decrease in activity; hence the 2-naphthylsulfonyl moiety was replaced with a 2-phenylsulfonyl group that resulted in improved activity. Compound 9c (ED50 = 42.64 μmol/kg) substituted by 3-propylamido group displayed better activity than compound 9d (ED50 = 63.62 μmol/kg) having a isopropylamido group.
    As 6b turned to be the most potent compound in the series, further investigations on its isomers 6c and 6d were performed, but none of them were found to be equipotent compared to 6b.

    The antithrombotic efficacy of compound 6b was further evaluated against collagen–epinephrine induced thrombosis in mice in a dose and time dependent study (Figure 3a, Figure 3b). Compound 6b (30 μmol/kg) displayed a significant protection against thrombotic challenge (60 ± 1%; 1 h, po dosing) which was sustained for up to 24 h (30%) and thus highlights its excellent bioavailability (Figure 3a). The standard antiplatelet drugs aspirin (170 μmol/kg) and clopidogrel (70 μmol/kg) were effective only up to 5 h after which their effect perishes and that too at a very high dose sufficient enough to cause bleeding complications. The bleeding time for compound 6b was found to be similar to aspirin for 4 h and clopidogrel for 6 h after which it was reduced (Figure 3c) gradually. However, the mice treated with losartan exhibited low bleeding time when compared to mice treated with 6b. Compound 6b also depicted better protection in ferric chloride induced arterial thrombosis in mice (Supporting Information Figure S1).

    Inhibition of Platelet Aggregation by Compound 6b and Its Isomers

    The effects of the active compound 6b and its isomers 6c (S isomer) and 6d (R isomer) were further assessed for their inhibitory potential against platelet aggregation as well as coagulation cascade, in order to identify their probable mechanism of antithrombotic action. The compounds did not display any alteration in the coagulation cascade, as evident from the assessment of thrombin time (TT) (Figure 3d), prothrombin time (PT) (Figure 3e), and activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) in human plasma (Figure 3f). Thus, the antithrombotic mechanism of compound 6b and its isomers may be platelet specific and thus was further evaluated for the identification of its target receptor over platelet surface.
    Compound 6b and its isomers 6c and 6d did not inhibit ADP, TRAP, and arachidonic acid induced platelet aggregation (Figure 3h). However, compound 6b and its isomer 6c significantly inhibited collagen-induced aggregation in a concentration-dependent manner, in contrast to the other isomer 6d, which showed no promise at lower concentrations but only at 300 μM (IC50 = 126.3 μM). Among the two active compounds, the racemic mixture 6b exhibited a relatively better platelet inhibitory profile (IC50 = 6.7 μM) than its enantiomer 6c (IC50 = 25.3 μM) (Figure 3i) and hence was further pursued for the identification of its target receptor (Table 2).

    Identification of the Target Receptor: Platelet Adhesion

    To further address whether compound 6b interferes with platelet adhesion by selectively binding to GPVI, it was further examined on human platelet adhesion to fibrillar collagen in the presence of Mg2+ (integrin mediated) and in the absence of Mg2+ (GPVI mediated). Compound 6b significantly inhibited GPVI mediated platelet adhesion over fibrillar collagen in a concentration dependent manner. On the contrary, the integrins mediated platelet adhesion to collagen in the presence of Mg2+ remained unaltered even at a concentration of 100 μM (Figure 3g). Thus, 6b effectively inhibited GPVI mediated platelet adhesion under static conditions.

    Identification of the Target Receptor: Platelet Aggregation

    In a platelet suspension ristocetin promotes vWF-GPIb-IX-V interaction and aggregates platelets. Compound 6b did not reduced ristocetin-induced platelet aggregation (Figure 3h), indicating that GPIb-IX-V is not a target for 6b.
    Two specific agonists were used to examine the probable interaction between 6b and GPVI: the synthetic, triple-helical, collagen-related peptide (CRP) based on a repeated GPO (glycine–proline–hydroxyproline) motif,(28) and convulxin (CVX), a multimeric C-type lectin-like toxin from the venom of a rattlesnake.(29, 30) Both molecules activate platelets selectively via GPVI. Compound 6b attenuated both CRP-XL (IC50 = 53.5 μM) and CVX (IC50 = 16.3 μM) induced platelet aggregation in a concentration-dependent manner (Figure 3l); however, the platelet inhibition was more pronounced against CVX and was almost similar to that observed against collagen induced platelet aggregation (Figure 3l). Compound 6c (S), however, better attenuated CRP-XL mediated platelet aggregation (Figure 3k) when compared to collagen (Figure 3i) and CVX (Figure 3j). The other isomer 6d (R) showed promising activity at higher concentrations. It should be noticed that the racemic mixture 6b showed better antithrombotic profile than compound 6c in collagen induced platelet aggregation (Figure 3i).

    Effect of Compound 6b on Collagen Induced P-Selectin Expression

    Collagen (30 μg/mL) stimulated P-selectin expression in platelets more than 10-fold in comparison to control. Compound 6b did not change basal platelet P-selectin expression. However, compound 6b exerted a concentration-dependent inhibition of collagen-stimulated platelet P-selectin expression, which was statistically significant at a concentration of 300 μM (Figure 4a and Figure 4b).

    Effect of Compound 6b on Collagen Induced ATP Release

    The results presented in Figure 4c depicts that compound 6b prevented ATP release from collagen stimulated platelets in a concentration dependent manner. The concentrations of 6b required to inhibit dense granule secretion were comparable to those required to inhibit platelet aggregation.

    Effect of Compound 6b on Collagen Induced Thromboxane A2 Generation

    Upon collagen stimulation, platelets released thromboxane A2 which was significantly inhibited by compound 6b in a concentration dependent manner (Figure 4d). Moreover, the COX inhibitor aspirin exhibited a significant inhibition in collagen stimulated platelets, thus confirming the importance of secondary mediators in collagen signaling.

    Effect of Compound 6b on Collagen Induced [Ca2+]i Mobilization

    The effect of compound 6b on [Ca2+]i mobilization was observed after 5 min of incubation with platelet before adding the collagen. Collagen induced a slow but stable increase of [Ca2+]i, which reached the peak level of 300 nM after 3 min. Pretreatment of platelet suspension with 6b attenuated the elevation of the [Ca2+]i in response to collagen in a concentration dependent manner (Figure 4e and Figure 4f).

    Effect of Compound 6b on Collagen Induced Tyrosine Phosphorylation

    The activation of platelets by collagen involving GPVI interaction causes tyrosine phosphorylation of various proteins in platelets, which peaked at about 1–1.5 min after activation and gradually declines after 5 min. Hence, platelets incubated with 6b were stimulated with collagen for 1 min and then subjected to Western blotting with an anti-phosphotyrosine antibody. It revealed that the phosphorylated level of few proteins was decreased at 3 and 10 μM, which was further reduced to its entirety at concentrations ranging from 30 to 100 μM (Figure 4g) compared to the vehicle control. It also attenuated the phosphorylation of PLC-γ2 at tyrosine residue, thereby implicating that the compound disrupts platelet function via modulation of collagen induced signaling cascade (Figure 4h).

    Docking Studies

    The docking studies for compounds 6c and 6d were performed using the induced fit docking (IFD) protocol to ascertain their binding priority for each of the racemates at the LOS binding site. The binding area for S1 was defined by the residues E40 to R60 which had been reported to be the binding site for LOS based on NMR study. We considered the results for the 10 best docked conformations for each of the enantiomers 6c and 6d giving priority for conformations showing the best induced fit XP docking score and having the tryptophan moiety at the base of the binding groove. Among the two enantiomers, the best docked conformation of 6c showed a better docking score (Glide XP score = −5.2) than the compound 6d (Glide XP score = −4.28) at this site. The mean docking score for all 10 conformers of compound 6c at the site (Glide XP score = −4.73) is also higher than the mean docking score of 6d (Glide XP score = −4.03), indicating the preference of S enantiomer 6c. The 10 best conformations of 6c (S-isomer) were found to harbor the β-carboline fragment in the D1 domain of GPVI in a similar fashion with slight differences in conformational orientation. The phenyl ring of the indole moiety resided in the hydrophobic pocket formed by the residues L53 and I55 (Figure 5a). The nitrogen atom of the indole fragment showed H-bond interaction with the hydroxyl group of Y66 at the base of the hydrophobic pocket. The sulfonyl moiety in 6c had hydrogen bond interactions with S44 and K41, and the attached 4-methoxyphenyl group, besides maintaining hydrophobic contacts with L62, formed H-bond contacts with S61 and K59 through the oxygen atom of the methoxy group. The nitrogen atom of the carboxamide fragment attached to the C3 carbon formed hydrogen bond contact with backbone carbonyl of R46. Unlike 6c the 10 best poses of 6d were not similar regarding the binding location of the β-carboline moiety at the site, as we got three different binding preferences for the β-carboline moiety. The best binding conformation of 6d has the phenyl methoxy moiety in the hydrophobic groove formed by the residues L39 and L53 (Figure 5b). The β-carboline fragment in the 6d remained planar with the indole nitrogen facing toward the hydrophobic groove formed by L53, I55, and L62. The carboxamide fragment in 6d maintained hydrogen bond contact with the side chain of S44 and backbone carbonyl of S61, respectively. The other H-bond contacts for this molecule included contact of the oxygen atom in the 4-methoxyphenyl moiety with the side chain of K59. These docking studies do explain the observed higher activity of 6c (S) enantiomer than the corresponding 6d (R) isomer. However, the higher activity of the racemic compound 6b than any of these enantiomers may be due to a second binding site that may exist at the interdomain region.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Collagen is the most reactive component of the extracellular matrix, and its interactions with the platelets play a pivotal role in the initiation of hemostasis and thrombosis in vivo. The GPVI has a central role in platelet activation by collagen and thus is considered as a potential pharmacological target for the treatment of ischemic cardiovascular diseases and stroke.(31) Thus, there is considerable interest in developing novel agents against collagen mediated platelet activation, such as peptide based receptor antagonists,(31) snake venom metalloproteinase (Kistomin),(32) human GPVI-specific monoclonal antibody (acts via cAMP-dependent endocytosis of the GPVI),(33) or GPVI-Fc fusion protein revacept(34) that directly blocks vascular collagen at atherosclerotic plaques or ruptured endothelium. With these approaches still under investigation, we took an initiative to develop a clinically useful synthetic molecule targeting platelet–collagen interaction at the sites of arterial injury or plaques. This compound 6b (CDRI-S002-333) is undergoing pharmacokinetic(35-40) and toxicological studies. From the pharmacokinetic studies it was observed that the isomer 6c or 6d does not interconvert in vivo.
    The present study, therefore, describes a novel synthetic platelet GPVI antagonist, 6b, that blocks platelet adhesion to collagen and subsequent platelet aggregation and signaling. Compound 6b thus might prevent a very early step in acute vascular thrombosis and might hold promise for a reduced risk of associated complications due to the exclusive presence of GPVI receptor on platelets and megakaryocytes.
    The mice treated with 6b were protected from the collagen–epinephrine induced pulmonary thromboembolism. Its antithrombotic efficacy was much better as compared to the standard antiplatelet drug aspirin, while it was almost similar to that of P2Y12 antagonist clopidogrel, when studied in the same model. Moreover, 6b exhibited its antithrombotic potential for almost 24 h and thus displayed an excellent bioavailability, in contrast to the standard antiplatelet drugs, aspirin and clopidogrel, which remained active for merely 4–6 h in the same study. Furthermore, our results are in agreement with the previous report by Nieswandt et al. which stated that mice depleted of GPVI were completely protected from lethal collagen-induced pulmonary thromboembolism.
    The current view of collagen-induced platelet activation is that collagen initially interacts with GPVI and integrin α2β1, and platelet activation signals are then conveyed by GPVI. Therefore, we confirmed the possibility of direct interactions between 6b and platelet GPVI by the following pieces of evidence: (1) the two GPVI receptor agonists CRP and CVX competed with 6b for platelet aggregation, while the other agonists (ADP, TRAP, arachidonic acid, and ristocetin) mediated platelet aggregation remained unaffected; (2) the platelets treated with 6b were prevented from GPVI dependent adhesion over fibrillar collagen, while the other adhesion mechanism via integrins remained unaltered. The compound did not modify the coagulation cascade and hence might be platelet specific pertaining to its mode of antithrombotic action
    Although compound 6b exhibited a profound platelet inhibition via GP VI antagonism, it displayed a disparity in the inhibitory profiles against the three GPVI agonists (IC50: CVX > collagen ≫ CRP-XL). Platelet GPVI belongs to the immunoglobulin superfamily of receptors with two Ig-like loops in their extracellular domain and hence binds to their ligands using either the first or the second loop or the hinge interdomain region. Studies involving GPVI chimeric proteins, anti-GPVI monoclonal antibodies, and mutant recombinant soluble GPVI proteins have postulated that although collagen, CVX, and CRP binding sites share various overlapping features, at the same time they also recognize some distinct residues required for the specific interaction of these ligands. Both collagen and CVX are complex multimeric proteins requiring the participation of both the GPVI extracellular loops, and thus their binding site appears to be widespread covering a larger surface on GPVI. CRP, in contrast, requires only the first Ig loop for binding, and the presence of other loop is indispensable for its binding to GPVI. These structural discrepancies regarding GPVI and ligand interaction might be responsible for the different pattern (IC50) of platelet inhibition by 6b following stimulation with different GPVI specific agonists.
    Compound 6b effectively blunted the release of ATP from dense granules as well as the cycloxygenase mediated thromboxane A2 production following collagen stimulation. These observations indicated that 6b blocked the GPVI receptor, thereby preventing platelet activation and interfered with intracellular signaling that further prevented the release of platelet granule components as well as COX activity. The inhibitory action of compound 6b on collagen mediated platelet signaling was further studied on various platelet nonreceptor tyrosine kinases. The compound reduced the phosphorylation phospholipase γ-2 and arrested the assembly of platelet signaling complex following collagen stimulation. Intraplatelet calcium mobilizations induced by collagen were severely attenuated in the presence of 6b, which was manifested not only as a reduction in maxima but also as a delay in its onset.
    These results clearly indicated that compound 6b inhibited platelet collagen interaction by blocking GPVI and thus interfered at the preliminary step of thrombus initiation. In addition to their pivotal role in thrombosis and wound repair, the growing literature regarding the proinflammatory capacity of platelets suggests that the potential drug targets directed at inhibiting platelet function may also provide alternative and powerful treatments for inflammatory diseases in the future. Boilard et al.(41) used both genetic and pharmacologic approaches to identify the collagen receptor GPVI as a key trigger for platelet microparticle generation in arthritis pathophysiology, and hence antagonism of this receptor represents a novel therapeutic approach. Therefore, besides its role as an antithrombotic agent, 6b might be an attractive compound to study the functional consequences of blocking platelet GPVI during chronic inflammatory diseases like rheumatoid arthritis and atherosclerosis. However, the present study warrants further studies to emphasize the therapeutic promise of the 6b initially characterized here.
    The effects of 6b were also studied on P-selectin expression. P-selectin, a transmembrane protein, is involved in inflammatory responses by regulating the leucocytes adherence onto the endothelium. Other strong function of P-selectin is seen in atherosclerosis because in the case of P-selectin deficiency a delay in formation of atherosclerotic lesion formation has been observed.(42, 43) P-selectin is mainly present in blood platelets and mediates platelet leukocyte interaction.(44) A decrease of P-selectin expression in blood platelets has been experimentally attributed to antiatherosclerotic activity of the compound. Compound 6b showed a decrease in P-selectin expression subsequently at increasing concentrations from 30 μM to 300 μM. Thereby, reducing the capability of platelets to interact with leukocytes by inhibition of P-selectin expression suggests that 6b may have anti-inflammatory activity.
    The pan assay interference compound (PAINS) filter identified the 3-alkylindoles substructure in the active compounds that may be problematic. However, in the publication on PAINS it is mentioned that this substructure is not well refined and requires further evaluation as PAINS.(45) Additionally the comprehensive antithrombotic assays performed for compound 6b as antithrombotic agent exclude it from being PAINS. The molecular modeling studies were used to investigate the binding pattern of the enantiomers 6c and 6d at the GPVI surface. The putative ligand binding pocket at the GPVI extracellular surface is situated at the D1 domain that is reported to bind LOS. Further this binding pocket also binds the tryptophan residue of pep10L; hence the synthesized compounds having a tryptophan substructure have high probability to bind at this site.

    Conclusion

    The development of highly specific GPVI inhibitors has the potential to result in effective antithrombotic agents with minimal bleeding tendency. In this study we report a new class of 2,3-disubstituted tetrahydropyridoindoles that prevent collagen mediated platelet aggregation. The most active compound 6b showed better activity than either of its enantiomers in collagen mediated platelet aggregation. It also displayed better activity than LOS in collagen, CRP-XL, and CVX mediated platelet aggregation. Compound 6b also inhibited platelet P-selectin expression, suggesting its antiatherosclerotic potential. The binding interactions for the enantiomers have been provided by docking the compounds at the LOS binding site at the GPVI surface that may provide additional insights to design better GPVI antagonists.

    Collagen–Epinephrine Induced Pulmonary Thromboembolism in Mice

    Pulmonary thromboembolism was induced by a method as described.(46) Briefly, the compounds to be tested, standard drugs, or the vehicles were administered by oral route 60 min prior to the thrombotic challenge. A mixture of collagen (150 μg/mL) and epinephrine (50 μg/mL) was injected into the tail vein to induce hind limb paralysis or death. Results have been reported as percentage protection, which represents protection against collagen and epinephrine induced thrombosis.(47)

    Bleeding Time

    The tail, approximately 2 mm from tip, of mice was incised with a sharp razor blade. The time elapsed from the tip incision to the stoppage of bleeding was determined as the bleeding time. The change in bleeding time was compared with that of vehicle treated mice, and results have been depicted as percent increase from control.(48)

    Preparation and Stimulation of Human Platelets

    Blood was drawn from healthy, drug-free volunteers after prior consent in acid citrate dextrose (ACD, 120 mM sodium citrate, 110 mM glucose, 80 mM citric acid, used at 1:7 v/v). Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) was prepared by centrifugation at 200g for 20 min, and the remaining blood was further centrifuged at 1000g for 15 min to obtain platelet poor plasma (PPP). Platelets were isolated by centrifugation for 10 min at 700g, in the presence of apyrase (0.002 U/mL) and prostaglandin I2 (400 ng/mL). The pellet was resuspended to a density of 2 × 108 platelets/mL in a Tyrode’s-HEPES buffer (145 mM NaCl, 2.9 mM KCl, 10 mM HEPES, 1 mM MgCl2, 5 mM glucose, pH 7.3).(49) Stimulation of platelets was performed in an aggregometer at 37 °C, with continuous stirring at 800 rpm.

    Platelet Aggregation and Coagulation Parameters

    A turbidimetric method was applied to measure platelet aggregation, using a dual channel aggregometer (Chronolog Corp., USA).(50) Platelet rich plasma (1 × 108 platelets/mL, 0.45 mL) was prewarmed to 37 °C for 2 min, then incubated with compound (3–300 μM) or an isovolumetric solvent control (0.4% DMSO) for 5 min before addition of the agonists (i.e., 2 μg/mL collagen, 5 μM ADP, 10 ng/mL convulxin, 25 μM TRAP, 1.5 mg/mL ristocetin, 0.3 μg/mL CRP). The reaction was allowed to proceed for at least 5 min, and the extent of aggregation was expressed as percentage which was calculated by using Aggrolink software.
    Coagulation parameters, prothrombin time (PT), activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT), and thrombin time (TT) were measured in the plasma within 2 h of sample collection by using commercially available kits as per manufacturer’s instructions (Stago, France) and were measured by using a semiautomated coagulometer (Start4, Young Instruments, Stago, France).(47)

    Platelet Adhesion Assay

    Washed platelets were pretreated with solvent or compound for 30 min. Ninety-six-well microtiter plates were coated with fibrillar type I collagen overnight, followed by blocking with 5 mg/mL BSA at room temperature for 1 h. Washed platelets were added to the wells (107/well) for 1 h at room temperature. After three washes in Tyrode’s buffer, the number of adherent platelets was evaluated colorimetrically (as described by Bellavite et al).(51) Briefly, 150 μL of 0.1 M citrate buffer (pH 5.4) containing 5 mM p-nitrophenyl phosphate and 0.1% TritonX-100 was added to the wells after washing. After incubation for 60 min at 25 °C in the absence of ambient light, color was developed by the addition of 100 μL of 2 N NaOH and the absorbance at 405 nm was read using a microplate reader (Powerware XS, Biotek, USA).

    P-Selectin Expression

    The inhibition of platelet P-selectin (CD62P) expression by compound 6b was determined by flow cytometry. Platelet rich plasma containing 50 μmol/L tirofiban was preincubated with 0, 30, 100, or 300 μmol/L of compound 6b for 10 min at 37 °C. Then, collagen (Horm, Nycomed, Linz, Austria) was added at a concentration of 30 μg/mL. Ten minutes later, 10 μL of PRP was incubated with 2 μL of anti-CD62P-PE (Beckman Coulter, Krefeld, Germany) for 10 min and analyzed on a Guava easyCyte 5 cytometer (Millipore, Schwalmbach, Germany).

    ATP Secretion

    ATP secretion was monitored in platelet rich plasma in parallel with platelet aggregation (Chrono log) by adding firefly luciferase and luciferin and comparing the luminescence generated by platelet ATP release with an ATP standard (Chrono-Lume), as per manufacturer’s instructions.(52)

    Thromboxane B2 Measurement

    Washed platelets were pretreated with compound or vehicle and stimulated with collagen under constant stirring at 37 °C. The reaction was terminated by the addition of an ice-cold EDTA solution (10 mM) containing 10 μM indomethacin, and the samples were centrifuged at 3000g for 5 min at 4 °C. Thromboxane B2, the stable metabolite of TxA2, was measured using a correlate-EIA thromboxane B2 enzyme immunoassay kit (Cayman Chemicals), according to the manufacturer’s instructions.(52)

    Measurement of Intracellular Calcium Concentration

    PRP was incubated with 5 μM Fura-2 AM (Sigma) for 60 min at 37 °C, followed by incubation with solvent or test compounds for 5 min and stimulation with collagen (5 μg/mL). The fluorescence was measured for 5 min at an excitation wavelength of 340/380 nm and emission wavelength of 500 nm (53) using a spectrofluorimeter (VARIAN, Cary Eclipse).

    Immunoprecipitation and Immunoblotting

    Washed platelets (2 × 108/mL) were preincubated with solvent or compound at 37 °C for 5 min, followed by the addition of collagen for 1 min. Platelet stimulation was terminated by the addition of an equal volume of ice-cold lysis buffer (2% (v/v) Nonidet P-40, 20 mM Tris, 300 mM NaCl, 2 mM EDTA, 2 mM EGTA, 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, 2 mM Na3VO4, 10 μg/mL leupeptin, 10 μg/mL aprotinin, and 1 μg/mL pepstatin A, pH 7.3). A total of 300 μg of platelet protein was incubated overnight with protein-A-Sepharose beads and 2 μg of anti PLCγ2 overnight at 4 °C. Beads were then washed before addition of 4× Laemmli sample buffer and boiled for 5 min. Proteins were resolved by electrophoresis in 8% SDS–PAGE gels and transferred to nitrocellulose membranes, blocked with 5% bovine serum albumin, and then probed with the following primary antibodies: anti-p-Tyr(4G10) (diluted 1:10,000 in TBST), β-actin (1:5000), anti PLC-γ2 (1:1000) for 2 h. Membranes were then washed before incubation with appropriate secondary antibody followed by thorough washing. Bound peroxidase activity was detected using enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL, Amersham Biosciences).

    Molecular Modeling Studies. Docking of Compounds 6c and 6d

    The docking studies were performed using 6c and 6d at the GPVI primary and secondary binding site. All the structures were prepared by using the Ligprep(54) module implemented in Schrodinger 9.0. The protein preparation was performed using Protein Preparation Wizard(55) in Schrodinger 9.0.(56) The induced fit docking protocol was used to dock the enantiomers 6c and 6d using the extra precision (XP) mode in GLIDE,(57) and the 10 best poses were analyzed. The crystal structure of GPVI (2GI7) was used for docking, and grid generation was performed by taking the amino acid residues Glu40-Arg60 for site 1.
