    Abstract

    The acetylcholinesterase (AChE) inhibitors remain key therapeutic drugs for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease (AD). However, the low-safety window limits their maximum therapeutic benefits. Here, a novel kinetics-driven drug design strategy was employed to discover new-generation AChE inhibitors that possess a longer drug-target residence time and exhibit a larger safety window. After detailed investigations, compound 12 was identified as a highly potent, highly selective, orally bioavailable, and brain preferentially distributed AChE inhibitor. Moreover, it significantly ameliorated cognitive impairments in different mouse models with a lower effective dose than donepezil. The X-ray structure of the cocrystal complex provided a precise binding mode between 12 and AChE. Besides, the data from the phase I trials demonstrated that 12 had good safety, tolerance, and pharmacokinetic profiles at all preset doses in healthy volunteers, providing a solid basis for its further investigation in phase II trials for the treatment of AD.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a complex and multifactorial, age-related, chronic neurodegenerative disease that often leads to cognitive dysfunction accompanied by severe behaviors such as irritability, anxiety, and depression.(1−7) An increasing number of people worldwide are living with dementia, but unfortunately, its pathogenesis is hitherto unknown. As different hypotheses are reported, such as the cholinergic hypothesis, amyloid hypothesis, tau hypothesis, neuroinflammation, and oxidative stress,(5−7) many potential drugs targeting these hypotheses have been tested for treating AD. However, acetylcholinesterase (AChE) is currently one of the only two well-validated molecular targets for AD. The AChE inhibitors reduce the hydrolysis of acetylcholine (ACh) into acetate and choline and consequently increase the ACh levels at the synaptic cleft which can stimulate cholinergic receptors and further promote memory function.(7) Therefore, AChE inhibitors such as donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine, and huperzine A remain the main choice for the treatment of AD today (Figure 1).
    Among the existing medications, donepezil was approved for the treatment of mild, moderate, and severe AD,(5,8) which is a blood–brain barrier permeable, reversible and noncompetitive AChE inhibitor. At one point, it was the world’s best-selling Alzheimer’s drug. However, its clinical benefits have been limited due to excessive side effects, including nausea, vomiting, and bradycardia.(2) Moreover, the incidence and severity of the side effects are augmented with an increase in the dose.(7) The poor tolerance renders difficulty in sustaining its efficacy and requires a constant dose-titration as the disease progresses.(9) This low-safety margin has largely limited its maximum therapeutic application for AD patients. Huperzine A (HupA), a sesquiterpene alkaloid extracted from Huperzia serrata, has been approved for use as a drug for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease in China and marketed in the USA as a dietary supplement.(10) It can increase the cortical ACh levels with an 8- and 2-fold higher molar potency as compared to donepezil and rivastigmine, respectively. Moreover, it also showed a longer duration of action and a lower oral dose.(11−13) Previous studies from our team showed that HupA could bind to the active site of AChE faster than its disassociation from it, which for the first time interpreted at the atomic level the experimental result that HupA equilibrated with free enzyme AChE in several minutes while HupA-inhibited AChE complex dissociated at a much lower rate (several hours) to restore enzyme activity.(14) This finding might be a valid interpretation for HupA having a longer duration of action.
    Inspired by these results, our current efforts were to design a new generation of AChE inhibitors by a binding kinetics-driven drug design strategy(15) and to overcome the unmet clinical needs of the therapy of AD, especially aiming to reduce the dose of administration and enlarge the safety margins of the current drugs. For this purpose, 44 new compounds were designed and synthesized by this strategy. Fortunately, a drug candidate 12 was discovered, and its detailed preclinical evaluations demonstrated it to be a highly potent, selective, orally bioavailable, and brain penetrant AChE inhibitor. Moreover, it significantly ameliorated the cognitive impairments in different mice models with a lower effective dose than donepezil. Also, the subsequent phase I trials in a single ascending dose study (CTR20181428) and a multiple ascending dose study (CTR20190664) demonstrated that drug candidate 12 exhibited good safety, tolerance, and pharmacokinetic profiles at all preset doses in healthy volunteers. These results motivated us to explore its further therapeutic values for AD patients in Phase II trials.

    2.1. Design of Novel AChE Inhibitors

    Compared with the well-known AChE inhibitor donepezil, HupA is a natural-origin AChE inhibitor and displays a lower oral dose and longer duration of AChE inhibitory action.(10−13) This was most probably because it can quickly equilibrate with the free enzyme AChE in several minutes, while the HupA-AChE complex dissociates at a much lower rate (maybe several hours) to restore enzyme activity.(14,16,17) Indeed, several reports have demonstrated that the kinetics of the drug–receptor binding process (i.e., the dissociation rate constant, koff) is more important than the binding affinity (i.e., Kd and IC50/EC50) in determining the drug efficacy,(18−21) and a longer drug-target residence time is helpful to reduce the dose of administration, enlarge the safety margins, and further alleviate the side effects.(14) It was reported that HupA was 8-fold more potent than donepezil in increasing the cortical ACh levels with a longer-lasting effect. Meanwhile, it has a higher efficacy than tacrine and donepezil if given orally.(12,13,22,23)
    Recently, selectively fluorinating bioactive molecules has become a well-established strategy in the design of new drugs because they improve their physicochemical characteristics, pharmaceutical effectiveness, and pharmacokinetic profiles.(24−29) On the basis of these findings, we attempted the introduction of fluorine atoms into the piperidine scaffold and/or phenyl group of donepezil and performed further structural optimization to design and synthesize 44 new compounds (1–44), expecting this modification to prolong their residence time on the target and give a larger safety margin to alleviate the undesirable side effects.

    2.2. Synthesis of All Target Compounds

    The synthetic routes of the target compounds 1–44 are depicted in Schemes 1 and 2. First, the key intermediate N-Boc-4-fluoropiperidine-4-carbaldehyde (48) was synthesized through a Corey–Chaykovsky reaction, a ring-opening reaction by HF in pyridine, and a classical Swern-oxidation reaction from N-Boc-piperidin-4-one (45). The resulting intermediate 48 was further coupled with 5,6-dimethoxy-inden-1-one (49) in the presence of CH3ONa in THF to provide the coupled product 50, which was further reduced into compound 51 with 10% Pd/C in the presence of H2. Next, the deprotection of the Boc group gave another key intermediate 52 with 50% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) in CH2Cl2. The resulting intermediate 52 was directly used to synthesize the target products 1–24 with diversified benzyl bromides and phenyl ethyl bromides under different reaction conditions, as shown in Scheme 1.
    As shown in Scheme 2, the target compounds 25–44 were synthesized by similar strategies as those of compounds 1–24 after using different indenones (56b–e, 57a–b, and 59a–b) as the key intermediates. Initially, after treatment of the commercially available substituted benzaldehydes (53a–e) with malonic acid in toluene in the presence of pyridine, further hydrogenation reduction easily prepared the substituted phenylpropanoic acids (54a–e). They were converted into substituted phenylpropionyl chlorides 55a–e with SOCl2 in CH2Cl2. The key indenone intermediates 56a–e were prepared by further Friedel–Crafts reaction from compounds 55a–e under the catalysis of AlCl3. Subsequently, the resulting intermediate 56a was converted into the other two indenones (57a and 57b), which may be further used to synthesize the target products 25–30. The 5,6-disubstituted indenones 59a and 59b were prepared via deprotection of the dimethyl group of compound 49 and further substitution reaction with propyl and n-butyl bromide under Na2CO3 in DMF. The resulting intermediates 59a and 59b were converted into the target products 31–34 by a similar strategy as that of compound 1. The other intermediates with different substituent groups on the R2 and R3 position (56b–e) were also converted into the target products 35–44 with different benzyl bromides by a similar synthetic strategy as for compound 1.

    According to the above design, we first introduced a fluorine atom into the piperidine scaffold of donepezil. As expected, the resulting compound 1 was endowed with a more potent inhibitory activity against AChE than donepezil; its IC50 was 0.96 nM (Table 1). Meanwhile, it also offered a smaller Koff value than donepezil (0.01764 vs. 0.02914, Figure 2A) in SPR binding determination, which means that compound 1 displayed a slower dissociation behavior from its target AChE.
    Encouraged by these results, we further introduced either a second fluorine atom or other groups containing fluorine atoms (-CF3 and -OCF3) into the para-position of the benzyl group (R1 position) of compound 1 to give compounds 2–4. However, only the 4-fluorine atom-substituted compound 2 displayed excellent inhibition against AChE with an IC50 of 2.67 nM. Further introduction of other groups such as the nitro group (5) or a tert-butyl group (6) into the para-position of the benzyl group appeared harmful for the inhibition against AChE. Subsequently, we explored the influence of different substituents on the meta-position of the benzyl group (compounds 7–11). We found that the 3-F group (7) and 3-Br group (9) could give strong inhibitory potencies against AChE with IC50 values of 3.0 nM and 4.33 nM, respectively. Likewise, the ortho-position of the benzyl group was explored to obtain compounds 12–15. The 2-fluoro-substituted compound 12 displayed the most potent inhibitory action against AChE with IC50 of 0.86 nM among all derivatives. These results indicated that the fluorine atom, irrespective of its location, could provide better activities for this scaffold than that provided by other groups. Inspired by these findings, we attempted to explore the influence of adding varying numbers of fluorine atoms on the benzyl group and designed derivatives 16–21. We found that the introduction of 2,4-difluoro (16) or 2,4,5-trifluoro (20) groups into the R1 position of the benzyl group yielded excellent inhibition against AChE. However, other substituted styles did not result in better biological activities as expected. These results indicated that more fluorine atoms might not be necessary for improving the inhibitory potential of this scaffold against AChE. Meanwhile, the solubility of these compounds became an obstacle as the number of fluorine atoms on the phenyl ring of R1 increased (21). Overall, a single fluorine atom on the phenyl ring of R1 was the best choice. Therefore, we attempted to maintain only one fluorine atom at the R1 position. We further prolonged the linker (n = 2) between the phenyl ring and piperidine ring from one carbon to two carbons and designed three new derivatives 22–24. Unfortunately, this dramatically decreased the inhibitory activities against AChE.
    The X-ray cocrystal structure of donepezil in the human acetylcholinesterase (PDB ID: 4EY7) displayed that the indanone ring stacks against the Trp 286 residue to form a π–π interaction, and its two methoxy groups reached the outside of the pocket (Figure S3a).(30) This phenomenon meant that more modifications could be launched on the indanone ring. Therefore, by positioning one fluorine atom or the 2,4-difluoro group on the R1 position, we further designed and synthesized a new class of derivatives 25–44 to explore the influence of different R2 and R3 groups on AChE inhibition. As shown in Table 2, we first replaced the methoxy group at the R2 position with the cyclopropylmethoxyl group or the propoxy group and screened the different fluoro substituted benzyl groups to obtain derivatives 25–30. As expected, most of them displayed excellent inhibition against AChE; the IC50s values of compounds 26, 27, and 28 were below 10 nM. However, it seemed that the introduction of the propoxy group at position R2 was detrimental to the improvement of inhibitory action (29 and 30). Similarly, simultaneous introduction of the propoxy group (31 and 32) and butoxy group (33 and 34) into the R2 and R3 groups proved unfavorable. Further investigations showed that removing the methoxy group of R2 (35 and 36) was more beneficial in improving the AChE inhibitory potency than removing the methoxy group of R3 (37 and 38); the IC50 values reached 6.29 nM (35) and 14.1 nM (36), respectively. Subsequently, we kept R3 or R2 as the methoxy group and attempted to introduce another fluorine atom into the R2 or R3 position to yield compounds 39–44. The results demonstrated that their activities against AChE could be maintained largely, indicating the small groups at the R2 and R3 positions are important for inhibition against AChE.

    2.4. Determination of the Dissociation Behavior of the Selected Compounds

    To explain the superior biological activities observed earlier, we further determined the kinetic (kon and koff) and thermodynamic (Kd) parameters of the highly active compounds (1, 2, 7, 12, 16, and 20) upon binding to their target, the recombinant human AChE (rhAChE), using the surface plasmon resonance (SPR) technology. As shown in Figure 2A, all selected compounds significantly reduced the Koff values and displayed Koff smaller than that of donepezil. This indicated that they had longer residence times (t = 1/Koff) on their target compared to donepezil, and almost comparable to that of HupA. These results indicate that introducing the fluorine atoms was very important for enhancing the binding affinities and prolonging the residence time of the target compound on its target; this may give a larger safety margin. It appeared that compound 12 offered a more desirable Kon and Koff behavior that was in agreement with its inhibitory potency against AChE (Table 1).
    We further explored the time-course of the AChE inhibitory action ex vivo for compound 12, and donepezil served as the control. Initially, cortical and hippocampal samples of ICR mice were collected at 0.5, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 h after administration of compound 12 (2 mg/kg, i.g.) or donepezil (12 mg/kg, i.g.). The peak inhibition was detected in the cortex and hippocampus at 30–60 min for both compounds. Compound 12 maintained a significant inhibition of AChE activity in the cortex and hippocampus from 30 to 240 min (Figure 2C,D). However, donepezil maintained its inhibition in the cortex from 30 to 180 min only. Moreover, its inhibitory potency seemed to decline rapidly after reaching the peak as compared to that of compound 12. Meanwhile, AChE activities were significantly inhibited in the mice treated with compound 12 as compared to those of donepezil-treated mice at 120, 180, and 240 min time point. These results indicated that compound 12 had a longer duration of drug efficacy and better inhibitory potency than donepezil as we expected, which is consistent with the results of SPR data shown in Figure 2A,B. All these results demonstrated that a slower dissociation behavior of a compound from its target might bring better efficacy and longer duration of action in vivo, further enlarging its safety margin.

    2.5. Crystal Structures of Compounds Bound to the Catalytic Domain of rhAChE

    To understand the binding mode of these new inhibitors with acetylcholinesterase, the crystal structures of compounds 2, 7, and 12 (with a single fluorine atom on different R1 positions) were determined in complex with rhAChE at resolutions of 2.45 Å, 2.66 Å, and 2.85 Å, respectively (Figure S3C–E, see Supporting Information). The complete diffraction data and refinement statistics are given in Table S1. All complexes belonged to space group P3121 and were isomorphous to each other; each contained two molecules in the asymmetric unit (Figure S3B). The overall structures of the complexes formed between each of these compounds and rhAChE were similar to each other; their RMSD values were less than 0.2 Å for all Cα atoms of rhAChE. All three structures also resembled the previous donepezil-bound rhAChE complex (Figure 3A).(30)
    Structurally, there is a deep and narrow active site gorge consisting of two separated ligand-binding sites: the peripheral anionic binding site (PAS) and the acylation (or active) site (AS).(31) The PAS is located close to the mouth of the active site gorge with an aromatic residue W286 as the important anionic site; the AS contains the AChE catalytic triad (S203/E334/H447) and the important aromatic residue W86 at the bottom of the gorge. Similar to donepezil, all three compounds were in the same configuration and oriented along the entire gorge composed of the PAS and AS sites.(30)
    Structural comparison of these structures with the donepezil-bound rhAChE structure revealed similar positions for the aromatic groups at the two ends: the indanone ring stacked against W286 in the PAS, while the fluorinated benzyl ring stacked against W86 in the AS. The π–π stacking of the fluorinated benzyl ring with W86 was enhanced because of the electron-withdrawing effect of the fluorine atom(32,33) and the interactions of the fluorine substituent with the rhAChE (Figure 3). The piperidine rings in the middle were flexile and adopted slightly different conformations, thus facilitating a favorable geometry of the fluorobenzene moiety to produce additional interactions (Figure 3A). For the fluorinated benzyl ring, the 4-fluoro substituent of compound 2 interacted with the side chains of Y133 and E202 (Figure 3B). The 3-fluoro substituent of compound 7 interacted with the side chain of Y133 (Figure 3C), while the 2-fluoro substituent of compound 12 formed an important multipolar fluorine–backbone interaction with the main chain C═O of the H447(34) (Figure 3D). The fluoro atom in the piperidine ring faced a relatively more open direction. It exhibited characteristics of a negatively charged and hydrophilic environment that was supported by the side chains of D74, Y124, S125, Y133, E202, and S203 (Figure 3B–D). It was speculated that additional interactions might be bridged by some potentially bound water molecules as found in the structure of the donepezil-bound rhAChE (PDB ID: 4EY7) at a higher resolution. These interactions, especially the formation of additional interactions between the C–F from the fluorinated benzyl ring and the residues from the rhAChE, give a plausible explanation for the much longer dissociation of these fluorinated donepezil derivatives from their target and their enhanced biological activities compared to that of donepezil.

    2.7. Preclinical Pharmacokinetic Evaluation of Compound 12

    The in vitro inhibitory activities against AChE demonstrated that compound 12 exhibited the desirable inhibitory potency and selectivity for AChE. We thus chose compound 12 for further exploration. First, the pharmacokinetics profiles of compound 12 were evaluated in Sprague–Dawley (SD) rats and beagle dogs (Table 4). Here, compound 12 displayed a good oral bioavailability in the rats (F = 45.3%) and dogs (F = 61.3%). In Sprague–Dawley rats, a Tmax of 0.6 ± 0.69 h, a Cmax of 35.7 ± 15.1 ng/mL, an AUC0-∞ of 179 ± 127 ng·h/mL, a t1/2 of 4.17 ± 2.32 h were obtained after an oral administration of 0.5 mg/kg dose of compound 12. And in dogs, a Tmax of 0.92 ± 0.2 h, a Cmax of 16.9 ± 3.46 ng/mL, an AUC0-∞ of 157 ± 34 ng·h/mL, a t1/2 of 23.2 ± 2.4 h were obtained after an oral administration of 0.25 mg/kg dose of compound 12. The same experiments were also performed after an intravenous administration of compound 12. The results showed that compound 12 had moderate clearance in rats and dogs; their clearance ratios were 23.7 ± 9.8 mL/min/kg and 17.5 ± 5.3 mL/min/kg, respectively.     2.8. Ex Vivo Inhibitory Activities against AChE

    On the basis of the superior in vitro inhibitory potencies as well as pharmacokinetic parameters, ICR mice were used to assess further the ex vivo inhibitory activities of compound 12 against AChE. For assessment of a dose-dependent effect, the AChE activities in the cortex and hippocampus were tested at 1 h following i.g. administration with compound 12 or donepezil. As illustrated in Figure 4, compound 12 (0.25, 0.5, 1, and 2 mg/kg) and donepezil (4, 8, 10, and 12 mg/kg) both inhibited the activities of AChE in a dose-dependent manner in the cortex (Figure 4A) and hippocampus (Figure 4B) of mice. The AChE inhibitory activity of compound 12 at 2 mg/kg was approximately equal to (or even more potent than) that of donepezil at 12 mg/kg, suggesting that the ex vivo central AChE inhibitory activity of compound 12 was about six times more potent than that of donepezil.

    2.9. Ameliorative Effect of Compound 12 on Scopolamine-Induced Cognitive Deficits in Mice

    Scopolamine, a muscarinic receptor antagonist, induces significant central cholinergic deficiency and consequent cognitive deficit. The scopolamine-induced amnestic mice model is widely used to assess the pharmacological effects of cholinergic agents, especially AChE inhibitors.(35−38) In the present study, a channel water maze (80 cm × 50 cm × 20 cm, Figure S1) was employed to assess the cognitive behavior of mice. Mice injected with scopolamine showed significantly increased escape latency (Figure 5A) and errors of entering no-exit areas (Figure 5B). In contrast, compound 12 (0.5, 1, and 2 mg/kg; P < 0.05, P < 0.05, and P < 0.001, respectively) and donepezil (8, 10, and 12 mg/kg; P < 0.01, P < 0.05, and P < 0.001, respectively) significantly reversed the increased escape latencies in the scopolamine-stimulated mice. Similarly, the number of errors in the scopolamine-treated mice was also reduced after administration of compound 12 at doses of 1 and 2 mg/kg (P < 0.05 and P < 0.01, respectively), or donepezil at 8 and 12 mg/kg (P < 0.05 and P < 0.01, respectively). Thus, compound 12 could ameliorate the scopolamine-induced cognitive impairment in mice at a lower dose (about one-eighth) than that achieved by donepezil.

    2.10. Ameliorative Effect of Compound 12 on Cognitive Impairments in APP/PS1 Mice

    Next, the performances in the Y-maze and Morris water maze were assessed in order to determine the ameliorative effects of compound 12 on the cognitive deficits in APP/PS1 mice, a rodent AD model widely used for evaluating pharmacological effects of anti-AD drug candidates.(38−41)Figure 6A illustrated that the levels of spontaneous alternation in the APP/PS1 mice were significantly reduced as compared to that of the wild type littermates (P < 0.05). Compound 12 rescued the spontaneous working memory impairment in the APP/PS1 mice in a dose-dependent manner. This resulted in significantly higher spontaneous alternations than those observed in the vehicle-treated APP/PS1 mice at 0.5 and 1 mg/kg (P < 0.01 and P < 0.001, respectively). The Morris water maze was further employed to assess the ameliorative effects of compound 12 on spatial learning and memory impairment in the APP/PS1 mice. In the probe trial, APP/PS1 mice showed statistically lesser time in the target quadrant compared with the age-matched control group (P < 0.05), while compound 12 significantly increased the time spent in the target quadrant (P values <0.05 vs the vehicle-treated APP/PS1 mice) at 0.5 mg/kg and 1 mg/kg (Figure 6B). In addition, we found that a 3-month oral administration with compound 12 at 0.5 mg/kg and 1 mg/kg significantly increased the number of platforms crossed in the probe trial (P values <0.05 vs the vehicle-treated APP/PS1 mice) (Figure S2). These results indicated that treatment with compound 12 significantly ameliorated the impairments of cognition in the APP/PS1 mice.

    2.11. Safety Evaluations for Compound 12

    Inhibition of the IkR channel encoded by the human Ether-A-Go-Go gene (hERG) is implicated in QT prolongation and can lead to cardiac arrhythmia and sudden death.(42) Hence, the role of compound 12 was evaluated, and an IC50 value of 9.23 μM was detected for the inhibition of the hERG channel (Table 6). This value was 10732-fold higher than the IC50 value for the in vitro inhibition of AChE and indicated that compound 12 was unable to cause QT prolongation, cardiac arrhythmia, and sudden death. We further assessed compound 12 in a multiple-dose cardiovascular study in beagle dogs. The results demonstrated that compound 12 did not affect the ECG (including H, PR, QRS, and QT), blood pressure, body temperature, and respiration after administration of 0.1, 0.3, and 1.0 mg/kg of compound 12. Besides, compound 12 showed negative results in the Ames, mutagenicity, and in vivo micronucleus assessments, thus indicating good safety (Table 6).
    The inhibition of human CYP isoforms (1A2, 2B6, 2C8, 2C9, 2C19, 2D6, and 3A4) by compound 12 was also assessed using human hepatocytes. The results displayed that compound 12 exhibited a moderate inhibition toward CYP2C9 (IC50 = 4.77 μM), while it did not inhibit other human CYP isoforms (IC50 > 30 μM); this indicated a low probability of drug–drug interactions. Moreover, compound 12 (up to 10.0 μM) did not induce the CYP1A2 or CYP3A4 expression in human hepatocytes at the tested concentrations.
    In a 4-week long-term toxicity study in the SD rats, no deaths were recorded after administering up to 30 mg/kg oral dose of compound 12. However, in a 13-week long-term toxicity study in the SD rats administered donepezil, deaths of 1 male and three females were reported in the 20 mg/kg group and of 4 females in the 10 mg/kg group.(43) Likewise, in a 4-week long-term toxicity study of compound 12 in beagle dogs, no deaths occurred after an oral administration of 1, 3, and 20 mg/kg, respectively. Five deaths were reported in the group administered 8 mg/kg of donepezil.(43) These results indicated that compound 12 may possess lower toxicity and a much larger safety margin than donepezil.

    2.12. Phase I Clinical Trials for Compound 12 in Healthy Volunteers

    The pharmacokinetics, safety, and tolerance of compound 12 were first evaluated in phase I clinical trials in healthy volunteers by a single ascending dose (SAD) study with doses of 1 mg, 2 mg, 4 mg, 8 mg, and 12 mg (CTR20181428), respectively.(44) These trials displayed that single oral doses of up to 12 mg of compound 12 were safe and well-tolerated; the compound could be rapidly absorbed, and the maximum plasma concentration (Cmax) was generally reached within 0.75–1.5 h. The area under the curve (AUC), as well as Cmax, appeared to increase in a dose-proportional manner, and the terminal plasma half-life was 14.2–32.1 h (Table 4). Further, another multiple ascending dose (MAD) study (CTR20190664) using oral doses of 2 mg and 6 mg once daily for 7 days was initiated, and the results showed good pharmacokinetic profiles and safety.(44) Overall, compound 12 displayed good safety, tolerance, and pharmacokinetic profiles at all preset doses in healthy volunteers. These results warrant further evaluation of its therapeutic potential for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease in phase II clinical trials.

    3. Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The small safety window of current AChE inhibitors has limited their maximum therapeutic application in the therapy of AD. Here, we have attempted to employ a new kinetics-driven drug design strategy to design desirable compounds to overcome this deficiency. By this new strategy, 44 novel derivatives containing an important fluorine atom were synthesized, and 14 of them displayed excellent inhibitory effects against AChE. As expected, their dissociation rate constant (koff) was much lower than that of donepezil, and the studies on the X-ray cocrystal complexes for three highly potent compounds (2, 7, and 12) provided a precise binding mode between the individual compounds and AChE. In multiple animal models, compound 12 demonstrated a much better efficacy and a lower effective dose than that of donepezil, indicating that slowing down the dissociation from its target may to improve its efficacy and safety. Actually, compound 12 exhibited excellent safety in hERG, Ames, mutagenicity, micronucleus (CHO cells) studies, as well as the 4-week long-term toxicity studies. Overall, compound 12 was a highly potent, selective, orally bioavailable, and brain-penetrating AChE inhibitor. It could significantly ameliorate the cognitive impairments in different mice models with a low effective dose. More importantly, the phase I trials of compound 12 in healthy volunteers with a single ascending dose study (CTR20181428) and a multiple ascending dose study (CTR20190664) also offered good safety, tolerance, and enhanced pharmacokinetic profiles. These favorable outcomes motivate us to explore its further therapeutic values in phase II clinical trials, more efforts for the therapy of AD patients are currently underway.
