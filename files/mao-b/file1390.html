    Abstract

    ClC-3 chloride (Cl–) channel has been shown to be involved in cell proliferation, cell cycle, and cell migration processes. Herein, we found that a series of bufadienolides isolated from toad venom were a novel class of ClC-3 Cl– channel activators with antitumor activities. Bufalin, which has the most potent antitumor activity, and 15β-acetyloxybufalin, which has no antitumor activity, were chosen as representative compounds to investigate the role of the ClC-3 Cl– channel. It was found that bufalin rapidly elicited activation of the ClC-3 Cl– channel and subsequently induced apoptosis through inhibition of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway. The PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway was attenuated by pretreatment with Cl– channel blockers [tamoxifen and 5-nitro-2-(3-phenylpropylamino)benzoic acid, NPPB] or ClC-3 small interfereing RNA. In summary, we discovered that activation of the ClC-3 Cl– channel, which subsequently induced inhibition of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway, was involved in the antitumor activities of bufadienolides.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Membrane ion channels, including chloride (Cl–) channels, play important roles in tumor development and metastasis.(1-3) It has been demonstrated that volume-activated Cl– channels are involved in cell proliferation, migration, and apoptosis in tumor cells.(3-9) Despite their ubiquitous expression and physiological/pathophysiological significance, the molecular identification of volume-activated Cl– channels is still deficient at present. Our previous work, as well as reports from other groups, supports the hypothesis that ClC-3, a member of the ClC family of voltage-gated Cl– channels, is one of the molecular candidates linked to the activation of volume-sensitive Cl– currents under hypotonic conditions.(10-13) Our research also demonstrates that ClC-3 is involved in cell proliferation, cell cycle progression, and migration in cancer cells.(9, 14, 15)
    Until now, only a few Cl– channel activators have been found. Bromotetramisole is a Cl– channel activator of cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR).(16) Avermectin B1a and its analogues are γ-aminobutyric acid (GABA)-gated and glutamate-gated Cl– channel activators.(17) Lubiprostone is a ClC-2 Cl– channel activator,(18) which is also a U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved prostaglandin E1 derivative for the treatment of chronic constipation and irritable bowel syndrome with constipation in adults. It induces intestinal secretion and acceleration of small intestinal and colonic transit. Recent studies have demonstrated that some antitumor agents, such as cisplatin(19) and doxorubicin,(20) can activate Cl– channels, but the evidence to consider them as chloride channel activators is still insufficient.
    Bufadienolides are typically C-24 steroids with a six-membered lactone (α-pyrone) ring at C-17β. They are isolated from animals and plants, including toads, Bufo spp. (Bufonidae); fireflies, Photinus spp. (Lampyridae); Cotyledon and Kalanchoe (Crassulaceae); Urginea/Scilla and Bowiea (Hyacinthaceae); and others.(21) It is widely accepted that bufadienolides are potent inhibitors of Na+/K+-ATPase, which can increase intracellular Ca2+ concentration through a backward-running Na+/Ca2+ exchanger, leading to activation of muscle contraction.(22) Therefore, bufadienolides have been used to treat congestive heart failure in traditional Chinese medicine.(23) Besides their classic use in cardiac diseases, preliminary epidemiological evaluations have indicated the use of bufadienolides in oncology.(24) Recent studies found that bufadienolides, such as bufalin, bufotalin, arenobufagin, and cinobufagin, possessed potent antineoplastic activity in several cancer cell lines.(25-29) The antitumor mechanisms include cell cycle arrest by increasing the expression of p21Cip1; inhibition of transcriptional factors such as NF-κB and AP-1; inhibition of PI3K/Akt and topoisomerases; affecting cell differentiation; inducing mitochondria-mediated and death receptor-mediated apoptosis; and decreasing antiapoptotic proteins, such as Bcl-2 and Bcl-xL.(24) Although Na+/K+-ATPase is considered as a target of bufadienolides, some antitumor mechanisms of bufadienolides have nothing to do with the inhibition of this enzyme,(24) indicating that the antineoplastic mechanisms of bufadienolides are complicated.
    In the present study, we found that bufadienolides, as a novel class of ClC-3 Cl– channel activators, can induce apoptotic cell death in cancer cells though activation of the ClC-3 Cl– channel, subsequently inducing inhibition of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway.

    Results and Discussion

    In Vitro Antiproliferative Activity of Bufadienolides in Human Carcinoma Cells and Their Structure–Activity Relationship Analysis

    The growth-inhibiting activities of bufadienolides, isolated from toad venom, in human nasopharyngeal carcinoma cell lines (CNE-1, CNE-2Z) and human hepatocellular carcinoma cell lines (HepG2, HepG2/ADM) were determined by MTT assay. The results were expressed as IC50 values shown in Table 1. Bufadienolides significantly inhibited the proliferation of CNE-1, CNE-2Z, HepG2, and HepG2/ADM, indicating their broad antitumor effects, which is in accordance with previous reports.(25-29) Furthermore, the sensitivity of HepG2/ADM cells to bufadienolides implied the potential of these compounds in the treatment of multidrug-resistant cancer. Although various cell lines showed selectivity to bufadienolides, in general, bufalin (1) had the most potent inhibitory effect with IC50 values of 0.279 ± 0.035 μM in CNE-1, 0.460 ± 0.071 μM in CNE-2Z, 0.589 ± 0.008 μM in HepG2, and 0.345 ± 0.015 μM in HepG2/ADM. Cinobufagin (2), 16β-hydroxylbufalin (3), and bufalin-3-hemisuberate methyl ester (4) showed moderate antitumor effects in all four cell lines. Bufalin-3-hemisuberate (5) and resibufagin (6) expressed weak antitumor activity. However, 15β-acetyloxybufalin (7) had no obvious bioactivity. Compared to bufalin, introduction of 16β-hydroxy substituents led to reduced activities, which was consistent with the previous report.(28) It was also found that oxidation of 3β-hydroxy group to 3-ketone or transfer to 3α-hydroxy group could decrease these effects; meanwhile, the activities of 3-suberate ester were stronger than that of 3-succinate derivative, suggesting that the influence of substituents at the C-3 position on the antitumor activity might be complicated.(28) However, in this study, we noticed that the activities of compounds 4 and 5 were weaker than those of bufalin. It could be concluded that large substituent groups at the C-3 position were not favorable to activity. Moreover, it is our first time to find that the replacement of 15-H by 15β-acetyloxy rendered the compound inactive, suggesting that the 15-H was necessary for maintaining the antitumor effects.

    Activation of Cl– Channel

    Ion channels and transporters seem to be the most successful therapeutic targets for pharmaceuticals because they can be easily reached and modified in the extracellular compartment. Some examples are amiloride, which is a Na+ transport/exchanger inhibitor for diuretics, and verapamil, which is a Ca2+ channel blocker for heart disease. Our previous research suggests that Cl– channels are associated with cancer due to their roles in cell migration regulation and cell growth control.(9, 15) The anticancer drug carboplatin was recently demonstrated to induce apoptosis via activation of Cl– channels in human lung adenocarcinoma cells.(30) These results suggest that Cl– channels are an attractive antitumor target. Therefore, we performed whole-cell patch-clamp recordings to analyze the effects of bufadienolides on Cl– channels. The results (Figure 1) showed that bufalin had the most potent effect, with almost a 17-fold increment in chloride current density compared to vehicle. Cinobufagin, 16β-hydroxylbufalin, and bufalin-3-hemisuberate methyl ester showed moderate activation effects, while 15β-acetyloxybufalin could not activate the chloride current.
    To further explore the activation of Cl– currents by bufadienolides in CNE-2Z cells, bufalin (1), one of the main components of toad venom with potent antitumor activity, and 15β-acetyloxybufalin (7), a bufadienolide without antiproliferative effect, were selected to stimulate the Cl– currents in CNE-2Z cells by whole-cell patch-clamp recordings. Cells were clamped at 0 mV and stepped to 0, ±40, and ±80 mV. The background current was small and stable in isotonic solution, with a density of 2.95 ± 0.80 pA/pF at +80 mV and −2.52 ± 0.28 pA/pF at −80 mV (n = 9). Stimulation of CNE-2Z cells with bufalin (1 μM) activated a Cl– current with outward rectification in 5–10 min. The bufalin-activated current displayed an almost linear current–voltage relationship with outward current of 33.72 ± 5.72 pA/pF at +80 mV and inward current of −18.67 ± 4.31 pA/pF at −80 mV (n = 9). There was no obvious time-dependent inactivation at the potentials tested (Figure 2 A–C). When the bufalin-activated current reached a peak, addition of the volume-sensitive Cl– channel blockers, NPPB (100 μM) or tamoxifen (20 μM), to the bath solution inhibited the current in both outward and inward directions to almost the same extent (Figure 3). However, 15β-acetyloxybufalin had no obvious effect on the activation of Cl– channels (Figure 2 D–F). These results strongly suggest that bufadienolides can activate Cl– channels.
    To further confirm the action of bufalin on activation of chloride channels, the effects of bufalin on chloride currents in a normal human nasopharyngeal epithelial cell line, NP69-SV40T, was tested. We previously proved that expression of ClC-3 chloride channels is lower in NP69-SV40T cells than in CNE-2Z cells.(15) In this study, it was shown that NP69-SV40T cells were insensitive to bufalin as reflected by the IC50 value of above 25 μM (data not shown). The concentration (1 μM) of bufalin that activated a large Cl– current in the cancerous cell CNE-2Z, as shown above, could only induce a weak Cl– current in the normal cell NP69-SV40T in Figure 4 (***P < 0.001 vs CNE-2 cells), suggesting the excellent selectivity of bufalin for cancer cells.

    Activation of ClC-3 Cl– Channel in CNE-2Z Cells

    ClC-3 is a ubiquitously expressed Cl– channel that is linked to cell cycle and cell proliferation in glial cells(31) and arterial smooth muscle cells.(32) It is also associated with migration of CNE-2Z cells(9) and apoptosis of PC12 cells.(33) Although the exact molecular protein of Cl– channels associated with the antineoplastic mechanisms has not yet been identified, much work has established ClC-3 as the most viable candidate at present. It is assumed that ClC-3 may mediate the antineoplastic effects of bufadienolides, and so whole-cell chloride currents were studied after CNE-2 cells had been treated with ClC-3 siRNA for 48 h. The treatment with ClC-3 siRNA depleted the activation of Cl– currents induced by 1 μM bufalin, suggesting that the bufalin-induced currents are mainly mediated by ClC-3 Cl– channels in CNE-2Z cells (Figure 5A,B). Results from Western blotting showed that the expression level of ClC-3 protein after bufalin treatment increased in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 5C), whereas the expression of ClC-3 protein was not significantly changed by 15β-acetyloxybufalin treatment (data not shown).

    Induction of Apoptosis

    The above data demonstrated that the abilities of bufadienolides to activate Cl– channels were in accordance with their antiproliferative activities, meaning that bufadienolides with antitumor effects could activate Cl– channels, whereas those without antiproliferative activity had no effect on the anion channels. Since the activation of Cl– channels is strongly suggested in the antitumor mechanism of bufadienolides, we explored the role of ClC-3 channel on the antitumor effects of bufadienolides. The change in cell cycle response as detected by propidium iodide (PI) staining assay demonstrated that bufalin treatment induced an obvious increase of sub-G1 peak in CNE-2Z cells (Figure 6A). Quantification of apoptotic cells by annexin V/PI double staining assay also showed that the percentage of apoptosis was markedly increased from 35.2% to 68.5% in bufalin-treated cells (Figure 6B). Moreover, bufalin induced poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) cleavage and activation of caspase 3 (Figure 6C). Caspase 3 is a critical component of apoptosis, which is responsible for the proteolytic cleavage of many key proteins, including PARP, which is a marker of apoptosis. All of these data suggest that bufalin induced apoptotic cell death of CNE-2Z cells through mitochondrial and death receptor apoptotic pathways (Figures S1 and S2, Supporting Information). However, similar phenomena were not observed in 15β-acetyloxybufalin-treated cells (Figures S1–S4, Supporting Information). Inhibition of the ClC-3 Cl– channel by either specific pharmacological inhibitors (20 μM tamoxifen and 50 μM NPPB) or ClC-3 gene silencing (ClC-3 siRNA) led to attenuation in bufalin-induced apoptosis. The population of apoptotic cells, specific cleavage of PARP, and expression of caspase 3 were all decreased when compared to treatment with bufalin alone (Figure 6C). It is likely that activation of the ClC-3 Cl– channel may mediate bufalin-induced apoptotic cell death. Given the fact that 15β-acetyloxybufalin could not induce apoptosis nor activate Cl– channel currents, it can be concluded that activation of the Cl– channel is required for bufadienolides to induce apoptosis.

    Inhibition of PI3K/Akt/mTOR Signaling Pathway

    The PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway plays critical roles in cell growth, survival, differentiation, and metabolism.(34) Inhibition of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway causes cell death associated with the mitochondrion- and receptor-mediated pathways of apoptosis.(34, 35) We thus investigated whether this downstream signaling pathway was linked to activation of the ClC-3 Cl– channel. Western blot analysis showed that the levels of PI3 kinase P110α, the catalytic subunit of PI3K, and Akt were decreased in CNE-2Z cells treated with bufalin. Concomitantly, levels of p-Akt (Ser473), p-Akt (Thr308), and its downstream regulators mTOR, p-mTOR (Ser2448), and p-mTOR (Ser2481) were also decreased (Figure 7). Bufalin seems to induce apoptosis via the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway, an idea that is consistent with previous works.(36, 37) Furthermore, inhibition of ClC-3 Cl– channel activation by ClC-3 siRNA pretreatment attenuated the inhibitory effect of bufalin on the activation of PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway, implying that the ClC-3 Cl– channel might mediate the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway. Recent studies have suggested that the PI3K was associated with the activation of volume-sensitive Cl– channel activity,(38) and Akt played a critical role in the homeostasis of the cardiomyocytes.(39) It was also found that DIDS, a volume-sensitive Cl– channel inhibitor, prevented staurosporine- or doxorubicin-induced apoptosis through activation of the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway.(40, 41) Other reports found that Cl– channel blockers (DIDS, NPPB and SITS) and ClC-3 siRNA could inhibit cell proliferation or arrest cell cycle by suppression of the PI3K/Akt pathway.(42-44) The present results were the first to demonstrate that the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway is involved in the pro-survival mechanism of ClC-3 Cl– channel blockade in bufalin-induced apoptosis. However, it is necessary to determine the exact regulating mechanisms between ClC-3 Cl– channel and the signaling pathway and how they cooperate to reduce apoptosis.

    In Vitro Antiproliferative Activity in Murine Carcinoma Cells

    It is well accepted that bufadienolides are inhibitors of Na+/K+-ATPase. Thus H22 and S180, two murine carcinoma cell lines with mutated α-1 Na+/K+-ATPase,(45, 46) were used to evaluate the cytotoxicity of bufadienolides. Our results showed that these murine cells were insensitive to bufadienolides, a concept consistent with previous research (Table S1, Supporting Information).(45, 46) Recent research has also demonstrated that cardiac glycosides are novel anticancer agents.(47-50) Therefore, we speculate that Na+/K+-ATPase is important in the antitumor effects of bufadienolides. Recent studies demonstrated that chloride channels could cooperate with Na+/K+-ATPase to regulate cell tubulogenesis(51) and secretion of the aqueous humor.(52) In addition, the Cl– channel inhibitor NPPB could completely block NaCl-induced upregulation of γ-subunit of Na/K-ATPase in mouse IMCD3 cells.(53) However, no obvious evidence so far shows how they interact to produce the antitumor effects. It remains to be clarified whether both chloride channels and Na+/K+-ATPase participate in bufadienolide-induced apoptotic cell death, and if so, how they collaborate with each other.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, this study indicates that bufadienolides are a novel class of ClC-3 Cl– channel activators with antitumor activities through inhibition of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report demonstrating the exclusive involvement of ClC-3 Cl– channel in bufadienolide-induced apoptotic cell death. This finding may support ClC-3 Cl– channel as a novel molecular target for drugs or gene manipulations aimed at controlling apoptotic processes in cancer. Our research provides a rationale for further structural modification to obtain more efficient antineoplastic bufadienolides targeting ClC-3 Cl– channel.

    General Experimental Procedures

    Melting points were measured on an X-5 melting point apparatus without correction. Optical rotations were recorded in CH3OH on a Jasco P-1020 polarimeter at room temperature. Ultraviolet spectra were determined in CH3OH on a Jasco V-550 UV/vis spectrophotometer. IR spectra were determined on a Jasco FT/IR-480 plus Fourier transform infrared spectrometer using KBr pellets. Electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) was performed on a Finnigan LCQ Advantage Max ion trap mass spectrometer. High-resolution (HR) ESI-MS data were obtained on an Agilent 6210 ESI/time-of-flight (TOF) mass spectrometer. NMR spectra were measured on a Bruker AV-400 or AV-600 spectrometer. Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) analyses were performed on precoated silica gel GF254 plates (Qingdao Marine Chemical Plant, Qingdao, China). Column chromatography was carried out on silica gel (200–400 mesh, Qingdao Marine Chemical Plant, Qingdao, China), reverse-phase C18 silica gel (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany), and Sephadex LH-20 (Pharmacia Biotec AB, Uppsala, Sweden). All solvents used in column chromatography and high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) were of analytical grade (Shanghai Chemical Plant, Shanghai, China) and chromatographic grade (Fisher Scientific), respectively.

    Materials

    The venom of Bufo bufo gargarizans was purchased from Baoying Toad Breeding Base in Jiangsu Province of China in August 2008 and was authenticated by Professor Guang-Xiong Zhou (Jinan University). A specimen (2008082001) was deposited in the Institute of Traditional Chinese Medicine and Natural Products, Jinan University, People’s Republic of China. Doxorubicin (Dox) was purchased from Merck Calbiochem (San Diego, CA). Annexin V–FITC/PI apoptosis detection kit was supplied by Biouniquer Tech (Nanjing, China). Primary antibody against PARP was purchased from Santa Cruz Biotechnology (Santa Cruz, CA). ClC-3 antibody was from Abcam (Cambridge, MA). Antibodies against caspase 3, PI3 kinase p110α, Akt, p-Akt (Thr308), p-Akt (Ser473), mTOR, p-mTOR (Ser2448), and p-mTOR (Ser2481) were supplied by Cell Signaling (Beverly, MA). ClC-3 siRNA kit was purchased from Genepharma (Shanghai, China). Protein inhibitor cocktail tablet was purchased from Roche Applied Science (Mannheim, Germany). All other chemicals were obtained from Sigma–Aldrich (St. Louis, MO).

    Extraction and Isolation

    The dried and roughly powdered venom (1.5 kg) was extracted by 95% ethanol under ultrasonic conditions (40 min, 40 °C). Then the extract was concentrated under reduced pressure to give a residue (900 g), which was subsequently partitioned between methylene dichloride (CH2Cl2) and water. The CH2Cl2 portion (321 g) was then subjected to silica gel (200–300 mesh) chromatography and eluted with cyclohexane–acetone (5:1, 3:1, and 1:1) to give 15 fractions. Fractions 3 and 6 were further chromatographed on reverse-phase C18 silica gel, eluted with methanol–water (30:70, 50:50, 70:30, and 90:10), to give subfractions 3a–3p and 6a–6k. Subfraction 3d was purified on Sephadex LH-20 to give 7 (11.0 mg). Subfractions 6c, 6e, and 6g were further purified by preparative HPLC, eluted with methanol–water (60:40, 65:35, and 82:18), to afford 4 (7.0 mg), 5 (3.3 mg), and 6 (8.3 mg), respectively. Fraction 5 was first separated on a silica gel column, eluted with cyclohexane–acetone (4:1), to give five subfractions (5a–5e), and subfraction 5b was recrystallized to afford 1 (2.4 g). Fraction 10 was subjected to silica gel chromatography, eluted with chloroform–methanol (100:2, 95:5, and 90:10), to give subfractions 10a–10k. Subfraction 10e was further purified by preparative HPLC, eluted with acetonitrile–water (23:77), to afford 3 (8.2 mg). Fraction 13 was subjected to reverse-phase C18 silica gel chromatography, eluted with methanol–water (50:50 and 80:20), to give five subfractions (13a–13e). Subfraction 13a was further separated by preparative HPLC, eluted with methanol–water (50:50), to give 2 (478.9 mg). The chemical structures of all isolated bufadienolides(54, 55) are shown in Table 1, and the physicochemical properties and spectral data are presented in Supporting Information. The purity of detected compounds was more than 95%, as determined by HPLC analysis.

    Cell Culture

    The well-differentiated human nasopharyngeal carcinoma cell line CNE-1 and the poorly differentiated line CNE-2Z were kindly supplied by Professor Wei-Ping Tang (Department of Pathology, Guangdong Medical College). The immortalized normal human nasopharyngeal epithelial cell line (NP69-SV40T) was obtained from Hunan Xiangya Type Culture Collection (Hunan, China).(15) The human hepatocellular carcinoma cell line, HepG2, was obtained from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Rockville, MD). The doxorubicin-resistant cancer cell line HepG2/ADM(56) was kindly provided by Professor Kwok-Pui Fung (School of Biomedical Sciences, The Chinese University of Hong Kong). All cell lines were incubated in RPMI-1640 medium (Invitrogen) with 10% (v/v) fetal bovine serum (FBS, Invitrogen) and 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin (PS, Invitrogen) in a 5% CO2 humidified atmosphere at 37 °C. Doxorubicin (1.2 μM) was added to the culture medium to maintain the multidrug-resistant characteristics of HepG2/ADM. For current recordings, cell suspension was plated onto 22-mm round coverslips and incubated at 37 °C for 30 min before recordings.

    Cell Viability Assay

    The cytotoxic effects of bufadienolides were detected by 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay as described previously.(36) Cells were seeded at a density of 5 × 103/well in a 96-well plate. After 24 h, cells were treated with different concentrations of bufadienolides for 48 h. Then 30 μL of MTT (5 mg/mL) was added into each well, and cells were incubated for another 4 h. The purple formazan crystals formed were dissolved in 100 μL of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), and the absorbance was detected at 570 nm by a microplate reader (Thermo MK3).

    Whole-Cell Current Recording(11)

    Whole-cell currents were recorded by the patch-clamp technique with 5–10 MΩ pipettes and a List EPC-7 patch-clamp amplifier (HEKA, Germany) at 20–24 °C. Membrane potential was held at the Cl– equilibrium potential (0 mV) and cycled through 200 ms pulses of 0, ±40, and ±80 mV repeatedly, with 4 s intervals between pulses. Voltages and currents were recorded by a computer with a laboratory interface (CED 1401, Cambridge, U.K.). The amplitude of currents was measured at 10 ms after onset of voltage steps. The isotonic bath solution contained 70 mM NaCl, 2 mM CaCl2, 0.5 mM MgCl2, 140 mM d-mannitol, and 10 mM HEPES. The pipet solution consisted of 70 mM N-methyl-d-glucamine chloride (NMDG-Cl), 10 mM HEPES, 1.2 mM MgCl2, 140 mM d-mannitol, 1 mM EGTA, and 2 mM ATP. The pH of bath and pipet solutions was adjusted to 7.40 and 7.25 with Tris, respectively.

    Cell Cycle Analysis(36)

    Cells were treated with bufadienolides (bufalin or 15β-acetyloxybufalin) in the presence or absence of tamoxifen (20 μM), NPPB (50 μM), or ClC-3 siRNA (100 nM) for 24 h, and fixed in 70% ethanol at 4 °C overnight. The fixed cells were centrifuged at 2500 rpm for 3 min and washed in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) to remove the excess ethanol. Then cells were resuspended in 500 μL of PBS containing 0.2 mg/mL propidium iodide (PI, Sigma) and 1 mg/mL RNase A (Sigma). The suspension was kept in darkness at 37 °C for 30 min and was measured by Epics XL flow cytometry at excitation wavelength 488 nm and emission wavelength 620 nm (Beckman Coulter, Brea, CA).

    Apoptosis Assay(36)

    Annexin V–FITC/PI double staining assay was performed to identify the quantity of apoptotic cells. After exposure to bufadienolides (bufalin or 15β-acetyloxybufalin) in the presence or absence of tamoxifen (20 μM), NPPB (50 μM), or ClC-3 siRNA (100 nM) for 24 h, CNE-2Z cells were harvested and resuspended in 500 μL of annexin V binding buffer containing 5 μL of annexin V–FITC and 5 μL of PI (annexin V–FITC/PI apoptosis detection kit, Biouniquer Tech). After gentle shaking and incubation of samples for 15 min in the dark, cells were analyzed by Epics XL flow cytometry at 488 nm excitation wavelength and either 525 nm emission wavelength for annexin V or 620 nm emission wavelength for PI (Beckman Coulter, Brea, CA).

    Transient Transfection(36)

    siRNA transfection was performed to assess the role of ClC-3 Cl– channel in bufadienolides (bufalin or 15β-acetyloxybufalin)-mediated cell death. CNE-2Z cells were seeded in 6-well plates and transfected at 60% confluence with 100 nM ClC-3 siRNA or 100 nM negative control siRNA by use of Lipofectamine 2000 (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. After 6 h of incubation, transfected cells were treated with bufadienolides (bufalin or 15β-acetyloxybufalin) for 24 h, which was followed by analysis of apoptosis by flow cytometry. Transfection efficiency was determined to be more than 75% by fluorescein-labeled nontargeted siRNA control (Ambion).
