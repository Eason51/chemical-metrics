    Abstract

    Myeloid cell leukemia 1 (Mcl-1), which belongs to the Bcl-2 family of prosurvival proteins, is a key regulator of cancer cell survival. To date, few drug-like Mcl-1 inhibitors have been reported. Herein, we report the preparation of 10 copper complexes with 9-substituted β-carboline ligands that act as metal-based Mcl-1 inhibitors. Complex 14 was identified as a potent and selective Mcl-1 inhibitor with strong in vitro antitumor activity. Mechanistic studies demonstrated that complex 14 disrupted Mcl-1-Bax/Bak heterodimerization and induced Bax/Bak-dependent apoptosis. In addition, complex 14 significantly (P < 0.001) inhibited tumor growth in vivo, induced tumor necrosis, and extended survival time in an NCI-H460 xenograft model. Furthermore, complex 14 showed no apparent toxicity in mice. Together, these findings indicate that complex 14 is a copper-based Mcl-1 inhibitor with high efficacy and low toxicity that could be developed for the treatment of Mcl-1-related cancers.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Apoptosis, a highly conserved process of cell death, plays key roles in organism development and homeostasis.(1,2) Apoptosis inducers can kill cells through extrinsic and intrinsic cell signaling pathways that lead to activation of caspases and irreversible cell death.(3,4) Members of the Bcl-2 family of proteins act as the key regulators of intrinsic apoptosis. These proteins can be classified into a prosurvival group (Bcl-2, A1/Bfl-1, Bcl-w, Bcl-xl, and Mcl-1) and two proapoptotic subgroups: the BH3-only protein group (Bim, Bid, Noxa, Puma, and Bad) and the multi-BH domain protein group (Bak, Box, and Bax).(5) Interactions among these subgroups involving binding of the BH3-only proteins to the prosurvival proteins release the multi-BH domain proteins Bak and Bax. Once activated, Bak and Bax cause mitochondrial membrane permeabilization, cytochrome c release, caspase activation, and apoptosis.(6)
    Mcl-1 is one of the most commonly amplified genes in cancers. It has been reported that Mcl-1 is critical for the development and growth of a variety of cancers, such as T-cell lymphoma,(7,8) acute myeloid leukemia,(9) multiple myeloma,(10) breast cancer,(11) and non-small-cell lung carcinoma.(12) Moreover, Mcl-1 overexpression is related to both intrinsic and acquired resistance to various antitumor agents, such as ABT-737 and ABT-263.(13−16) Therefore, inhibition of Mcl-1 is a valid therapeutic strategy for cancers.(17,18) However, Mcl-1 inhibitors are difficult to develop because the binding pocket of Mcl-1 is shallow.(19,20) Although some compounds show high binding affinity for Mcl-1, such as A-1210477,(21,22) S63845,(2) and AZD5991,(23) drug-like Mcl-1 inhibitors have rarely been reported.
    DNA is the primary target for most metal-based anticancer agents developed thus far; however, recent studies have sought to develop metal complexes that inhibit protein–protein interactions or enzymes.(24−31) Copper complexes compose one class of promising antitumor agents.(32) These compounds can target a variety of proteins, such as topoisomerases I and II, proteasome,(32) and NPL4.(33) We are interested in the development of novel copper complexes that inhibit Mcl-1.
    Herein, we describe the discovery of complex 14, a copper-based Mcl-1 inhibitor. Complex 14 showed potent cytotoxicity against multiple cancer cell lines in vitro and effectively inhibited tumor growth in a nude mouse model. Our results show that complex 14 could be a promising antitumor agent.

    2.1. Design, Synthesis, and Characterization of Copper Complexes

    In this study, copper complexes with β-carboline ligands were designed on the basis of several previous findings. First, β-carbolines are potential Mcl-1 inhibitors. Fesik’s group identified a β-carboline derivative that inhibits Mcl-1 through fragment-based screening.(20) Second, the β-carbolines are a class of promising anticancer agents.(34) In particular, the introduction of substituted groups at the 9-positions of β-carbolines affects their antitumor activities.(35) Third, Freeman-Cook et al. have reported that lipophilic efficiency plays a critical role in drug discovery. Compounds with relatively mild potency and high lipophilic efficiency can provide good starting points for drug discovery.(36) Fourth, copper complexes can exhibit multiple molecular geometries depending on the oxidation state of copper and the types of ligands. This molecular complexity allows copper complexes to access regions of chemical space within the binding pocket that are not accessible to other organic molecules. In many cases, copper complexes possessed higher binding affinity for their targets than the corresponding ligands.(32) Herein, a β-carboline scaffold was selected as a starting point to prepare Mcl-1-targeting metal complexes. Introduction of a pyridine ring at the 1-position of the β-carboline scaffold yielded 1-(2-pyridyl)-β-carboline, a bidentate ligand that could coordinate with metal atoms. To optimize the affinity of this compound for the hydrophobic binding site of Mcl-1, a series of hydrophobic substitutions were introduced at the 9-position of the β-carboline core.
    As shown in Scheme 1, a number of 9-substituted β-carboline ligands (2–11) were synthesized. The corresponding copper complexes (12–21) were obtained by reaction of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O with the 9-substituted β-carboline ligands (2–11) (Scheme 2). The structures of the ligands and copper complexes were determined by elemental analysis and electrospray ionization-mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) (Figures S1–S43). Moreover, complexes 12–17 and 19–21 were further characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis (Figure 1). All the copper complexes showed similar geometries. Complex 14, a representative compound, had a mononuclear structure. The metal center was four-coordinated with a coordination sphere filled by a 9-substituted β-carboline ligand and two nitrate anions. The structural refinement parameters and selected bond lengths (Å) and bond angles (deg) are shown in Tables S1–S10.

    2.2. Stability of Complexes 13 and 14 in Solution

    The stability of copper complexes 13 and 14 under simulated physiological conditions (Tris-HCl buffer solution at pH 7.4 containing 1% DMSO) was assessed by UV–vis spectroscopy (Figure S44) and ESI-MS (Figures S45 and S46). The results suggested that complexes 13 and 14 were stable under physiological conditions at room temperature for 72 h.

    2.3. Selective Binding of the Copper Complexes to Mcl-1

    To explore the ability of these copper complexes to bind to Mcl-1, a fluorescence polarization assay (FPA), enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA), and surface plasmon resonance (SPR)-based assay were performed.(37,38) As shown in Table 1, the copper complexes 12–21 exhibited significantly higher binding affinities than their ligands (2–11) or Cu(NO3)2·3H2O. Among these copper complexes, complex 14 (inhibitory constant (Ki) = 1.4 ± 0.3 nM in FPA; half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) = 7.8 ± 1.4 nM in ELISA; IC50 = 3.5 ± 0.7 nM in SPR) was more potent than the reference drug A-1210477 (Ki = 4.7 ± 0.7 nM in FPA; IC50 = 12.5 ± 3.6 nM in ELISA; IC50 = 8.8 ± 0.5 nM in SPR). The binding affinity of our copper complexes decreased in the following order: 14 (R = −CH2Ph(p-CH3)) > 15 (R = −(CH2)2Ph) > 18 (R = −CH2Naphth) > 12 (R = −CH2Ph) > 17 (R = −CH2C3H5) > 19(R = −(CH2)2OPh) > 20 (R = −(CH2)2OCH2Ph) > 16 (R = −CH2CH3) > 21 (R = −(CH2)2OCH2CH3) > 13 (R = −CH2Ph(p-F)). The structure–activity relationships (SARs) of our copper complexes were assessed. In general, aryl substitutions in the β-carboline ligands enhanced binding affinity more profoundly than alkyl substitutions.

    The binding mechanisms of complexes 12–14 and ligand 4 were further evaluated through analysis of docking of these entities with Mcl-1 [Protein Data Bank (PDB) ID code: 3WIX].(40) The structures of complexes 12–14 in solution were determined by ESI-MS (Figures S34–S36 and S46). No water molecules were predicted to bind to the copper center. According to the structural analysis, complex 14 had a docking score of −7.16. As shown in Figures 2 and S49, complex 14 was predicted to form a cation−π interaction with Arg263 and two H−π interactions with Ala227 and Leu267 of the Mcl-1 protein. The nitrate-Cu coordinate group faced outward, and the 4-methyl benzyl group lay inside the P2 pocket. The corresponding ligand 4 had a lower docking score (−6.18) than complex 14, suggesting a lower binding energy. These data confirm that copper complex 14 has a higher binding capacity than ligand 4. Complex 12 was predicted to form a cation−π interaction with Arg263 and a weak H bond with Ala227. The docking score of complex 12 (−6.91) was lower than that of complex 14. Complex 13 was predicted to form a cation−π interaction with Arg263. The docking score of complex 13 (−6.53) was lower than that of complex 12. These results suggested that the binding energies of our copper complexes decreased in the following order: complex 14 > complex 12 > complex 13. These findings were in agreement with the binding affinity data.
    We subsequently investigated the selectivity of all copper complexes for antiapoptotic Bcl-2 family members (Bcl-2, Bcl-xl, Mcl-1, Bcl-w, and A1/Bfl-1). FPA revealed that the copper complexes exhibited minimal affinity for Bcl-2, Bcl-xl, Bcl-w, and A1/Bfl-1. Overall, the selectivity of the copper complexes improved with increasing binding affinity (Table 3). The most potent Mcl-1 inhibitor, complex 14, showed a 558-fold greater binding with Mcl-1 than with Bcl-2, 661-fold greater binding with Mcl-1 than with Bcl-xl, 482-fold greater binding with Mcl-1 than with Bcl-w, and 360-fold greater binding with Mcl-1 than with A1/Bfl-1.
    Having demonstrated the targeting ability of complex 14 in biochemical assays, we confirmed the cellular target engagement of complex 14 by using a cellular thermal shift assay (CETSA). A CETSA is a direct binding assay that monitors ligand-mediated thermal stabilization of target proteins in cells.(41) NCI-H929 cells were treated with either complex 14 (40 μM) or DMSO for 1 h. The treated cells were then heated to the indicated temperature ranging from 45 to 63 °C. Finally, the cells were lysed, and the protein fractions were tested by immunoblotting. As shown in Figures 3A and S50, compared to the vehicle control, complex 14 increased the melting point of Mcl-1 by 4 °C, suggesting the occurrence of ligand-induced stabilization. A further isothermal dose–response experiment was performed at 57 °C. Complex 14 significantly enhanced the thermal stability of cellular Mcl-1 protein at low nanomolar concentrations [half-maximal effective concentration (EC50) = 6.3 nM], consistent with the biochemical data (Figures 3B and S50).

    2.4. Mechanism-Based Cellular Activity of the Copper Complexes

    Further studies were carried out to investigate the mechanism-based activity of our copper complexes. First, we assessed the growth inhibitory effects of complexes 12–21 and cisplatin on five validated Mcl-1-dependent cell lines (MOLP-8, L-363, K562, NCI-H929, and LP-1) and two Mcl-1-independent cell lines (MM.1S and KMS-12-PE) by Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK8) assay.(42,43) The results showed that copper complexes 12–21 selectively inhibited the growth of Mcl-1-dependent cell lines (Table 4). The most effective complex, complex 14, exhibited 18–49-fold greater selectivity for Mcl-1-dependent cell lines than for Mcl-1-independent cell lines. In contrast, the cytotoxic drug cisplatin exhibited no selectivity for Mcl-1-dependent cells or Mcl-1-independent cells.

    In addition, copper complexes 12–21 exerted more potent effects than their ligands (ligands 2–11) or Cu(NO3)2·3H2O on Mcl-1-dependent cells, suggesting that β-carboline ligands exhibit excellent antitumor synergism with copper(II) atoms. The growth inhibitory activities of our copper complexes against Mcl-1-dependent cells decreased in the following order: 14 (R = −CH2Ph(p-CH3)) > 15 (R = −(CH2)2Ph) > 18 (R = −CH2Naphth) > 12 (R = −CH2Ph) > 17 (R = −CH2C3H5) > 19 (R = −(CH2)2OPh) > 20 (R = −(CH2)2OCH2Ph) > 16 (R = −CH2CH3) > 21 (R = −(CH2)2OCH2CH3) > 13 (R = −CH2Ph(p-F)). The cytotoxic activity and the binding affinity values followed the same SARs, suggesting that these copper complexes exerted their cellular activity though inhibition of Mcl-1. The most potent complex, complex 14, had low IC50 values (0.4–0.9 μM) in Mcl-1-dependent cell lines and was 11–35-fold more potent than A-1210477.
    The cytotoxic activity of complexes 12–21 was also determined in four solid tumor cell lines (T-24, HepG2, MGC80-3, and NCI-H460) as well as the normal human hepatocyte line LO2 (Table S12). Similar synergistic effects were also observed in these solid tumor cell lines. In addition, copper complexes 12–14 were less cytotoxic toward LO2 cells than toward the tumor cell lines. The safety of these complexes was further investigated in a mouse model.
    Bax and Bak are important proapoptotic effector proteins that are crucial for Mcl-1-mediated apoptosis.(42) To further demonstrate that the observed activity of copper complex 14 was caused by inhibition of Mcl-1 in cells, we silenced the Bax and Bak genes and evaluated whether complex 14 killed tumor cells in a Bax/Bak-dependent manner. Specifically, K562 cells were treated with short interfering (si) RNA targeting Bax and Bak for 48 h. The efficacy of gene silencing was confirmed by immunoblot analysis (Figure 4A). Next, the ability of complex 14 to induce apoptosis in these Bax/Bak-deficient cells was tested by using Annexin V-PI staining and flow cytometry analysis. As shown in Figure 4B, complex 14 was less effective in Bax/Bak-deficient cells than in control cells, indicating that it killed tumor cells through a Bax/Bak-dependent process. For comparison, the cytotoxic agent staurosporine was also tested. Staurosporine showed no selectivity for Bax/Bak-deficient cells or K562 cells. These results suggested that complex 14 induced apoptosis through an on-target mechanism.
    To further confirm the on-target activity of complex 14 in solid tumor cells, a procedure similar to that performed on K562 cells was performed on NCI-H460 cells. The results showed that complex 14 also induced Bax/Bak-dependent apoptosis in NCI-H460 cells (Figure S51).
    Next, we investigated whether complex 14 induced apoptosis by disrupting Mcl-1-Bax and Mcl-1-Bak complexes. We exposed K562 cells to complex 14 for 4 h and detected Mcl-1 complexes by coimmunoprecipitation (Co-IP) and immunoblotting (Figure 5). The results suggested that complex 14 concentration-dependently induced release of Bax and Bak from Mcl-1 complexes. However, treatment with complex 14 did not affect the protein expression levels of Mcl-1, Bax, and Bak, suggesting that the decreases in Mcl-1-Bax and Mcl-1-Bak complex levels were not caused by changes in the expression of Mcl-1, Bax, or Bak. Thus, complex 14 disrupted the binding of Bax and Bak to Mcl-1 in K562 cells.
    Having demonstrated that complex 14 can disrupt Mcl-1 complexes and induce Bax/Bak-mediated apoptosis, we further investigated the impact of complex 14 on the mitochondrial apoptosis pathway. We exposed NCI-H460 cells to complex 14 (0, 1, 2, or 4 μM) and extracted proteins for immunoblot analysis. Treatment with complex 14 increased the level of cytochrome c in the cytosol and decreased the level in the mitochondria (Figure 6), suggesting that complex 14 induced cytochrome c release. In addition, the total levels of pro-PARP [poly (adenosine diphosphate–ribose) polymerase (PARP)] and cleaved PARP in NCI-H460 cells were detected by immunoblot analysis. Complex 14 upregulated cleaved PARP and downregulated pro-PARP in cells (Figure 6), suggesting that complex 14 induced PARP cleavage in NCI-H460 cells.
    Caspases are key apoptosis executors in the intrinsic apoptosis pathway. The abilities of complexes 13 and 14 to activate caspase-3 and caspase-9 in NCI-H460 cells were tested by using flow cytometry analysis. As shown in Figure 7, compared with the control treatment (0 μM complex 14), treatment with complex 14 (2 or 4 μM) for 6 h dose-dependently activated caspase-3/9 in NCI-H460 cells. The effects of complex 14 on caspase-3/9 activation were stronger than those of complex 13, consistent with the cellular IC50 data. To further verify that the observed cellular activity was caused by an on-target effect, the ability of complex 14 to induce apoptosis in either the absence or the presence of the caspase inhibitor QVD-OPh was tested. As shown in Figure 7C,D, the efficacy of complex 14 was attenuated by the addition of QVD-OPh, indicating that complex 14 rapidly induced caspase-dependent apoptosis in NCI-H460 cells.

    2.5. Acute Toxicity of Copper Complexes in Mice

    In cellular experiments, complex 14 showed modest selectivity for tumor cells over normal cells. However, cells and animals are different systems. Thus, we further investigated the in vivo safety of complex 14.
    The acute toxicity of copper complexes 13 and 14 was assessed to evaluate the safety profiles of these compounds. Kunming mice (n = 10, half male and half female) were randomized and dosed intraperitoneally with vehicle, complex 13, complex 14, or cisplatin. As shown in Figure 8 and Table S13, the acute toxicity of complexes 13 and 14 [median lethal dose (LD50) = 91.6 and 79.5 mg/kg, respectively] was much lower than that of cisplatin (LD50 = 11.1 mg/kg). In addition, blood biochemistry analysis was used to evaluate the renal toxicity and hepatotoxicity of complexes 13 and 14 in mice.     2.6. Pathological Examination of Mice Treated with Complexes 13 and 14

    To further evaluate the safety profiles of complexes 13 and 14 in mice, 4-week-old Kunming mice (n = 6, half male and half female) were divided randomly into three groups and treated with vehicle, complex 13 (10 mg/kg, once daily), or complex 14 (10 mg/kg, once daily) by intraperitoneal injection. As shown in Figure 9A, the complex 13- and complex 14-treated mice showed heart, liver, and kidney weights similar to those of the control mice (Figure 9B). Pathological examination showed that no drug-related side effects occurred in the hearts, livers, or kidneys of complex 13- or complex 14-treated mice (Figure 9C). These results indicated that complexes 13 and 14 were safe in mice.

    2.7. Pharmacokinetic Study

    The pharmacokinetic profiles of complexes 13 and 14 in Kunming mice were investigated. Complexes 13 and 14 were administered through intraperitoneal injection at doses of 10 mg/kg. After injection, blood was collected from the treated mice at 10, 20, 60, 120, 180, 320, and 640 min. The blood samples were tested by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP–MS).(44) As shown in Table 6 and Figure S52, the plasma concentration of complex 14 reached a peak of 8.4 μM at 0.33 h. This drug concentration was much higher than the cellular IC50 values, indicating that the dose of complex 14 used in the mouse model was adequate. The maximum plasma concentration (Cmax), area under the plasma concentration–time curve until time t (AUC0–t), and area under the plasma concentration–time curve until infinite time (AUC0–∞) values of complex 13 were lower than those of complex 14. These results suggested that complex 14 was absorbed more efficiently by mice than complex 13.

    2.8. In Vivo Antitumor Activity of Complexes 13 and 14 in NCI-H460 Xenograft Mice

    The in vivo antitumor activity of complexes 13 and 14 was tested in an NCI-H460 xenograft mouse model. NCI-H460 cells were selected because they express Mcl-1 protein and grow faster than other cell lines (K562, NCI-H929, and LP-1) in xenograft models.(45) The NCI-H460 tumor-bearing mice were divided randomly into five groups and intraperitoneally treated with vehicle, complex 13, or complex 14 (5 or 10 mg/kg, once daily). The tumor volumes and body weights of the mice were recorded every 2 days over 7 days of treatment. On the seventh day of treatment, the mice were euthanized, and the relative tumor growth rates [treatment group/control group (T/C) ratios] were calculated. As shown in Figure 10A, complexes 13 and 14 (10 mg/kg) effectively inhibited tumor growth with T/C values of 62.6 and 46.2%, respectively. However, complex 14 showed a higher tumor growth inhibition (TGI) value than complex 13 (53.5 vs 32.9%, Figure 10C), suggesting that complex 14 was more potent than complex 13 in this mouse model. This result is consistent with the SARs revealed in the cellular experiments. In addition, no body weight loss was observed in complex 13- or complex 14-treated mice (Figure 10B), suggesting that complexes 13 and 14 were well tolerated by the mice.

    2.9. Tissue Distribution and Tumor Pathology

    After 7 days of treatment, the mice were euthanized, and organs (hearts, livers and kidneys) and tumor tissues were collected for tissue distribution analysis via ICP–MS. As shown in Figure 10D, the concentrations of complexes 13 and 14 in tumor tissue were higher than those in heart, liver, and kidney tissue, suggesting that complexes 13 and 14 accumulate in tumors.(33) In addition, the drug concentration of complex 14 in tumor tissue was higher than the cellular IC50 values, indicating that the dose of complex 14 used was adequate for the treatment of tumor tissues.
    Tumor tissue was also excised for histologic examination. H&E staining (Figure 10E) indicated the presence of large areas of necrosis (homogeneous pink-stained areas) in tumor tissue after treatment with complexes 13 and 14. However, complex 14 showed higher tumor necrosis-inducing activity than complex 13, suggesting that complex 14 exerted stronger in vivo antitumor activity than complex 13. In addition, a TUNEL assay yielded similar results. As shown in Figure 10F, large areas of necrosis (homogeneous brown-stained areas) were present in tumor tissue after treatment with complexes 13 and 14. Consistent with the findings of H&E staining, this result suggested that complexes 13 and 14 induced tumor tissue necrosis.

    2.10. Survival Analysis

    After demonstrating that complex 14 significantly inhibited tumor growth and induced tumor necrosis, we further evaluated the impact of complex 14 on mouse survival in a xenograft mouse model. NCI-H460 xenograft mice (n = 6) were intraperitoneally treated with complex 14 (0, 5, 10, or 15 mg/kg) once daily. As shown in Figure 11, mice treated with 15 mg/kg complex 14 had 33% longer mean survival times (48 days, P < 0.001) than control mice (36 days). These results indicated that complex 14 extended the survival times of the NCI-H460 xenograft mice.

    3. Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, we identified a novel copper-based Mcl-1 inhibitor complex 14 that was effective in inhibiting cancer cell growth in cancer models. In vitro studies demonstrated that complex 14 selectively bound to Mcl-1 and inhibited the growth of Mcl-1-dependent cancer cells. In addition, complex 14 disrupted Mcl-1-Bax and Mcl-1-Bak complexes and induced Bax/Bak-dependent apoptosis in cells. In vivo, complex 14 significantly suppressed the growth of NCI-H460 xenograft tumors and induced tumor tissue necrosis. Furthermore, complex 14 extended the survival times of tumor-bearing mice and showed a good safety profile. In summary, complex 14 is a promising copper-based Mcl-1 inhibitor with high efficacy and low toxicity. Our findings indicate that the use of metal-based Mcl-1 inhibitors is an effective strategy for the treatment of Mcl-1-dependent cancers.
