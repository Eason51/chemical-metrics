    Structural Insights into the Competitive Binding of Diclofenac and Naproxen by Equine Serum Albumin

    Abstract

    The binding modes to equine serum albumin (ESA) of two nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), diclofenac (Dic) and naproxen (Nps), were studied by X-ray crystallography and isothermal titration calorimetry. On the basis of the crystal structure of ESA/Dic determined to a resolution of 1.92 Å and the structure of the previously described ESA/Nps complex (2.42 Å), it was found that both NSAIDs bind within drug site 2 (DS2) of ESA and both occupy secondary binding sites in separate cavities of domain II (Nps) and domain III (Dic). The two structures of the ternary complex ESA/Dic/Nps, obtained by competitive cocrystallization (2.19 Å) and through a displacement experiment (2.35 Å), were determined to investigate possible competition of these widely used pharmaceutical drugs in binding to ESA. In these complexes Nps occupies the DS2 pocket common for both drugs, whereas the other distinct binding sites of Dic and Nps remain unaffected. These results suggest that combined application of both drugs may result in increased concentration of free diclofenac in plasma.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are structurally diverse therapeutic agents, including derivatives of salicylates, phenylalkanoic acids, oxicams, anthranilic acids, sulfonamides, furanones, and propanamides.(1) Diclofenac (2-[2-(2,6-dichloroanilino)phenyl]acetic acid (Dic)) is an effective antipyretic and analgesic phenylalkanoic acid derivative, often applied against acute febrile sore throat and episodic tension-type headache.(2) The drug is also widely used against several rheumatic diseases, such as rheumatoid arthritis, osteoarthritis, and ankylosing spondylitis.(3) The most commonly used form of diclofenac is its sodium salt; however, potassium salt is used in immediate release tablets.(4) Naproxen ((S)-2-(6-methoxynaphthalen-2-yl)propanoic acid (Nps)), a popular antiphlogistic drug, is clinically applied in the pure (S)-enantiomeric form(5) and is used for the reduction of pain or fever.
    Both drugs prevent the conversion of arachidonic acid to prostaglandins by the inhibition of cyclooxygenase isoenzymes COX-1 and COX-2. The reduction of COX-2 activity caused by diclofenac is approximately twice as high as its inhibition of COX-1.(6) Moreover, high anti-inflammatory potency of diclofenac is also linked to the inhibition of phospholipase A2,(7) which results in decreased production of proinflammatory compounds.(8) Animal studies have shown that diclofenac may also exhibit antitumor and antiangiogenic activity.(9) Moreover, diclofenac decreases transthyretin (TTR) aggregation in vitro, which indicates its potential usefulness in the treatment of human amyloid diseases.(10) The inhibitory properties of diclofenac derivatives against COX(11) and amyloid fibril formation(12) were noted as well. The twist of the two rings is considered to be an essential prerequisite for high activity against COX;(11, 13) however, the activity against TTR fibrils is connected with the presence of a free carboxyl moiety.
    Within the circulatory system, more than 99% of diclofenac and naproxen are bound to serum albumin, with a low rate of tissue binding. Diclofenac is excreted by the organism much faster than naproxen, and its half-life is at least 6 times shorter.(5, 14) The primary route of diclofenac excretion is hepatic biotransformation, whereas only less than 1% of the drug is excreted in the unmodified form via kidneys. In rare cases, the adverse effect of diclofenac action, similar to other NSAIDs, is hepatic injury; the drug causes cell death in human hepatocytes by mitochondrial permeabilization linked with diclofenac concentration.(3, 15, 16) There are approaches to increase diclofenac action in some tissues, such as the central nervous system, by creating conjugates with ascorbic acid.(17)
    Serum albumin serves as a transporter of numerous substances in the circulatory system and possesses a number of binding sites.(18-20) Two very active binding regions are located within subdomains IIA and IIIA and are named drug sites DS1 and DS2 or Sudlow’s sites.(21, 22) The DS1 site consists of a central zone with three side subchambers, and it is often referred to as the warfarin site, able to bind bulky heterocyclic ligands.(23, 24) The DS2, or indolebonzodiazepine site, is a narrower pocket with only one subchamber, capable of interacting with negatively charged aromatic carboxylic acids of the ligands.(24, 25) The drug binding mode of serum albumin provides information about the biological distribution of the drug, including its release to the site of action or excretion, prevention from rapid drug metabolism, therapeutic activity, and toxicity of the drug.(26) Due to its capacity to bind many hydrophobic pharmaceuticals, serum albumin modulates their apparent solubility in body fluids and affects drug delivery to cells in vivo and in vitro.(27)
    The binding modes of a number of NSAIDs to human serum albumin (HSA) have been studied in structural terms,(24, 28, 29) but that has not been the case for diclofenac, for which no structural data for albumin binding are available. Other physicochemical techniques also did not yield consistent results about the number and location of diclofenac binding sites in serum albumin. Some reports indicate a single binding site in HSA(30-32) with a binding constant Ka between 7.6 × 103 M–1 and 5.5 × 105 M–1 and in bovine serum albumin (BSA)(2, 33) (Ka value from 2.2 × 104 M–1 to 6.7 × 104 M–1). Other reports point to two binding sites of diclofenac, with high and low affinity, which are recognized for HSA(15, 34-39) (Ka1 from 1.0 × 105 M–1 to 3.3 × 106 M–1 and Ka2 from 3.0 × 103 M–1 to 0.6 × 105 M–1) and for BSA(40) (Ka1 from 8.8 × 104 M–1 to 2.0 × 105 M–1 and Ka2 from 7.3 × 102 M–1 to 1.6 × 103 M–1). In this study we describe the binding mode of diclofenac to equine serum albumin (ESA) and its competition with another NSAID, naproxen, based on the crystal structures of the complexes with these drugs and on isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC) experiments.

    Binding Sites of Diclofenac in the ESA/Dic Complex

    The monomer of ESA binds two molecules of diclofenac (Dic) (Figure 1). The first Dic binding site (Dic1) is located within the DS2 region in subdomain IIIA, which was described in previous structural studies of ESA as the binding site of 3,5-diiodosalicylic acid (Dis) (PDB code 4J2V)(41) and as one of the naproxen (Nps) binding sites in the leporine serum albumin (PDB code 4OT2).(42) The second Dic binding site (Dic2) is located inside the cleft between two helices of subdomains IIIA and IIIB, in the same pocket where another Dis was found to be bound in the ESA/Dis complex.(41) Positive electron densities were present in the Fo – Fc difference maps for both Dic molecules from the beginning of structure refinement, and the Dic molecules were refined with full occupancy (Figures 2A and 3A). The average temperature factors for Dic1 and Dic2 are 52.8 Å2 and 60.1 Å2, respectively, only slightly higher than the average value of 52.6 Å2 for all protein atoms in the structure refined with anisotropic TLS contribution. Both the Dic1 and Dic2 binding sites are in close proximity to each other; however, they are located in distinct cavities, separated by the hydrogen bonded side chains of Arg409 and Asn390. During studies on binding of Dic to ESA we have verified that complexes obtained either by cocrystallization or by soaking methods show the same number, location, and conformation of the bound Dic molecules. Structure of ESA/Dic obtained by cocrystallization was determined at higher resolution and is described in this work (Table 1).

    Diclofenac Site 1

    One Dic molecule is bound within the main compartment of the DS2 region, with the carboxyl group facing toward the hydrophilic entrance where it creates two direct hydrogen bonds with the side chains of Ser488 (2.5 Å) and Arg409 (3.0 Å), as well as one water-mediated hydrogen bond with Tyr410 (2.3 Å) (Figure 2B). The interior of the pocket, where both aromatic rings of Dic1 are placed, includes many nonpolar residues (Figure 2C). The side chains of Leu386, Val387, and Leu452, located at the opposite sides of the phenylacetic moiety, stabilize the position of the drug. The other aromatic ring is immobilized mostly by hydrophobic contacts with the surrounding residues; however, the distances that are a little longer than the sum of the van der Waals radii between the chlorine atoms and the polar constituents of the protein (e.g., distance between Cl–OH of Ser448 is 3.6 Å) indicate that electrostatic interactions are involved in the Dic stabilization as well.

    Diclofenac Site 2

    The second Dic molecule is located in a rather hydrophobic environment, similar to Dic1. In particular, its dichlorophenyl ring is situated inside a hydrophobic compartment of the cleft, with its plane oriented parallel to the hydrophobic part of the side chain of Lys544 and Ala405 residue located on the other side of the ring plane. The phenylacetic ring is inserted between the side chains of two lysines (Lys540 and Lys544), and its carboxyl group creates a water-mediated 2.9 Å hydrogen bond with the carbonyl oxygen of Ala405 (Figure 3B). The same water molecule may electrostatically interact with one of the Cl atoms of Dic2. Moreover, both chlorines of Dic2 can be involved in electrostatic interactions with the protein main chain. Nps molecule is bound in an analogous pocket of leporine serum albumin (LSA) complex(42) (Figure 4C).

    Influence of Naproxen on Diclofenac Binding

    To investigate the effect of competitive binding of Dic and Nps to ESA, we obtained two ternary complexes of ESA/Dic/Nps: one by competitive cocrystallization with both drugs and the other one by consecutive soaking of unliganded ESA crystals first in Dic and then in Nps solutions. After the first cycle of refinement of the ternary complexes of ESA, the shape of the positive electron density maps in the DS2 site of both structures clearly showed that the molecule bound inside this pocket is Nps (Figure 2D). For the Dic molecule, positive electron density appeared only in the same crevice of domain III, where Dic2 is bound in the ESA/Dic complex. The conformation of Dic2 is nearly identical in all structures discussed here. In the ternary complex obtained by cocrystallization, the second Nps molecule is located between subdomains IIA and IIB, and this is the same location as in the ESA/Nps complex described in a previous work.(42) In the ESA/Dic/Nps-d complex obtained in a displacement experiment by soaking of the ESA/Dic complex with buffer containing Nps, the Nps2 site is not occupied by the drug molecule, probably due to the limited soaking time or the influence of ions present in the crystallization buffer. The lack of the Nps in the second binding pocket indicates that the affinity of this drug to the Nps2 pocket is much lower than to DS2. This observation is consistent with the results obtained in ITC experiments (see section below), in which only one association constant was determined. The binding sites of Dic and Nps identified in ESA are shown in Figure 1.
    Both the ESA/Dic and ESA/Dic/Nps complexes were obtained under very similar crystallization conditions, and their crystals were isomorphous in the space group P61. A superposition of the main chains of both structures yields the rmsd value of 0.25 Å. The placement of both drugs within the DS2 site is similar, with the nonpolar fragments of Nps and Dic buried inside the hydrophobic pocket. The naphthalene ring of Nps1 is placed in the same plane as the dichlorophenyl ring of Dic2, similarly reaching the bottom of the pocket. However, the resulting electrostatic contacts with the protein side chains appear to be different. The propionate moiety of Nps is shifted by about 2 Å with respect to the carboxyl moiety of Dic2 and is situated almost perpendicularly. As a result, Nps1 creates three direct electrostatic contacts with Arg409, Tyr410, and Ser488 (Figure 2E) and one water-mediated hydrogen bond with Lys413. The structures of ESA/Dic and ESA/Dic/Nps differ in the conformation of the side chains of Leu386 and Leu452, two nonpolar residues located in the DS2 pocket (Figure 2F). The side chains of these leucines in the ESA/Dic complex are rotated around the χ1 torsion angle to accommodate the second phenyl ring of Dic, which is twisted with respect to the dichlorophenyl ring. In the ESA/Dic/Nps ternary complex, the flat Nps molecule does not require as much space as Dic, and rotation of these two amino acid side chains does not occur. Additionally, slight movement of the fragments of two helices (Leu429 to Lys438 and Glu441 to Leu456), creating one wall of the DS2 pocket, reduces the volume and shape of this binding pocket, where Nps molecule fits better.

    Diclofenac and Naproxen Binding Parameters

    The stoichiometry (n), association constant (Ka), and the changes in the enthalpy (ΔH) and entropy (ΔS) were determined for the complexation reactions of ESA with both Dic and Nps. The titration diagrams and binding parameters are presented in Figure 5. Two association constants for Dic bound to ESA were determined (Ka1 = 5.13 × 106 M–1, Ka2 = 3.90 × 104 M–1) (Figure 5A), whereas only a single Ka was determined for Nps, with a value of 7.73 × 106 M–1 (Figure 5B). Subsequent titration of ESA/Nps complex with Dic did not indicate displacement of Nps by Dic, as was expected; however, the heat change showed the binding of Dic (Figure 5C) to its lower affinity pocket (Ka2 = 3.25 × 104 M–1). The result of this titration is in agreement with the lower affinity site of Dic from the first titration of ESA. The second titration of ESA/Dic complex with Nps showed low heat effect; binding parameters corresponding to the ligand displacement in DS2 pocket could not be determined. The association constant for Nps2, probably due to much lower affinity or slower binding kinetics, could not be determined in any of the performed titrations.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Our structural studies of the ESA complexes with Dic confirm the ability of albumin to bind Dic within two binding pockets. One of these is the DS2 of subdomain IIIA, a common drug binding place reported in most of previous works;(2, 15, 32-40) however, our findings show also the second binding location for Dic, which is not the DS1 pocket of subdomain IIA, as other authors suggested,(37, 38, 40) but the crevice at the interface between subdomains IIIA/IIIB.
    The structural competition analysis between Dic and Nps for ESA binding sites indicates that Nps binding in the DS2 is stronger than Dic. The consecutive soaking of an unliganded ESA crystal in much lower concentrations of Dic and Nps further confirms such a situation inside DS2, where Nps wins in competition with Dic. The soaked ESA/Dic and ESA/Dic/Nps showed that Dic binding in the Dic2 site is strong enough to preserve the ESA/Dic complex even with minimal Dic concentration in the environment. The situation is quite opposite when we look at the Nps2 site. In both cocrystallized structures (ESA/Nps(42) and ESA/Dic/Nps) Nps is bound under the lid made by an arginine side chain. However, a 1 h soaking in unsaturated Nps solution did not promote Nps binding within the Nps2 site of domain II in the complex ESA/Dic/Nps-d. This result suggests that the Nps2 pocket has a lower affinity and does not bind Nps as tightly as DS2. Probably the Nps2 site becomes active after a longer time or application of higher concentration of Nps. The structural findings are in the agreement with the ITC data, which suggest that for both drugs the primary and high affinity binding site is DS2. Combining the crystallographic and ITC data, we can assign the determined Ka values for both Dic sites: Ka1 to the higher affinity Dic1 site (DS2) and Ka2 to the lower affinity Dic2 pocket. In the case of Nps, the determined Ka is assigned to the primary Nps1 site (DS2).
    Many NSAIDs compete with each other and with other drugs for binding sites of plasma proteins, mainly serum albumin. The free concentration of a drug in plasma can be increased by application of other drugs occupying the same binding sites or in various pathophysiological conditions, e.g., secondary hypoalbuminaemia associated with the active rheumatoid arthritis.(5) However, depending on the patient’s state, in some cases too strong binding of a drug to albumin is unwanted, since it results in insufficient therapeutic effects. Thus, in the case of treatment with the use of Dic, inhibitors of serum albumin DS2 are applied(43) in order to increase the free Dic concentration in particular regions (e.g., aqueous humor). From the results of our work it can be postulated that despite similar affinity to DS2 pocket of Dic and Nps, the other can be used as an agent that increases the free Dic concentration by blocking DS2. To achieve similar effects, other drugs and druglike molecules that strongly bind to albumin between subdomains IIIA and IIIB in the Dic2 site could be administrated instead of DS2 inhibitors.

    Other Ligands’ Affinity to Diclofenac Binding Sites

    The structure of the previously determined complex of ESA with Dis(41) shows the binding of this ligand inside four pockets, two of which (Dis2 and Dis4) are also responsible for incorporation of Dic. The conformations of Dis2 and Dic1 bound in the DS2 pockets are quite similar. The carboxyl moieties of both ligands point toward the polar entrance of the pocket, and the plane of Dis2 is almost parallel to the phenylacetic ring of Dic1, with the iodine atoms situated in the upper and lower compartment of the pocket (Figure 4A). The situation is, however, quite different inside the Dic2 site. Dis4 is coplanar to the dichlorophenyl ring of Dic2 (Figure 4B); however, the carboxyl group of Dis4 points toward the compartment of the pocket where Cl2 of Dic2 is placed. Dis4 created more extensive electrostatic interactions with the protein than Dic2. An equivalent site was also identified as the Nps3 binding site in LSA.(42) In that case, the dichlorophenyl ring of Dic2 is also located in the same plane as the naphthalene ring of Nps3, but the carboxylic groups of both ligands are oriented in different directions (Figure 4C). Nps3 in LSA does not create direct electrostatic interactions with the polar amino acid side chains. Due to the conformational variability of the IIIB subdomain, this pocket is wider in LSA than it is in ESA.
    Among the ligands structurally analyzed in the studies of human serum albumin, the same binding site as Dic1 in ESA can also be occupied by other drugs, including NSAIDs(24) (diflunisal, PDB code 2BXE, and ibuprofen, PDB code 2BXG), anesthetics(44) (propofol, PDB code 1E7A, and halothane, PDB code 1E7B), diazepam(24) (PDB code 2BXF), an iodinated compound iophenoxic acid(45) (PDB code 2YDF), and different compounds such as uremic toxins(24) (CMPF, indoxyl sulfate), dansylated amino acids,(46) medium chain and long chain fatty acids,(47) thyroxine,(48) or a synthetic surfactant perfluorooctane.(49) In the studies of other serum albumin species, BSA and LSA, ligands such as naproxen(42) (PDB codes 4OR0, 4PO0) and Dis in BSA(41) (PDB code 4JK4) were found to bind in the DS2 pocket as well. The Dic2 site was identified for HSA as the binding site of oxyphenbuthazone(24) (PDB code 2BXO) and iophenoxic acid(45) (PDB code 2YDF), for ESA as Dis4 binding site(41) (PDB code 4J2V) and as the naproxen binding site 3 for LSA(42) (PDB code 4PO0).

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, structural studies of ESA complexes with diclofenac and naproxen confirmed their affinity to two albumin binding sites, albeit with different association constants. The primary binding place, common for both drugs, is the DS2, and this is the high affinity binding pocket for both diclofenac and naproxen. Secondary, lower affinity sites are unique for each of the described drugs. For Dic it is located in the crevice between subdomains IIIA and IIIB, whereas for Nps it is placed in the pocket of domain II. Moreover, in both ternary complexes ESA/Dic/Nps and ESA/Dic/Nps-d, Nps won the competition with Dic for DS2; thus, Dic presents lower affinity than Nps to this pocket. This is also confirmed by lower association constant for diclofenac in comparison to naproxen, obtained from ITC experiment.

    Experimental Section

    Jump To

    Materials

    Lyophilized equine serum albumin (96% purity) was obtained from Equitech-Bio Inc. (Kerrville, TX). Diclofenac sodium salt and S-naproxen, of highest available purity, were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO). 100% Tacsimate, pH 6.0, crystal screens, and plates were purchased from Hampton Research (Aliso Viejo, CA).

    Methods. Complex Formation and Crystallization

    Commercially obtained ESA was further purified by a two-step procedure,(50) which involved activated charcoal treatment and size exclusion chromatography. The purified protein was concentrated on VivaSpin 10 kDa cutoff microconcentrators to the 1 mM concentration. Purified protein was used in crystallization trials of unliganded form of ESA and for complex formation. Complexes of ESA/Dic and ESA/Dic/NPS were obtained by cocrystallization method. First, 1 mM ESA solution was mixed with the 10-fold excess of Dic to form ESA/Dic complex and with 5-fold molar excess of both Dic and Nps in the case of ESA/Dic/Nps complex. The mixtures were incubated overnight in 28 °C with continuous mixing. The second ternary complex of ESA/Dic/Nps-d was obtained in a displacement experiment by soaking of unliganded ESA crystals consecutively in Dic and Nps solutions. Different time of soaking and concentrations of drugs were tested to maintain stability of the crystals. Best results were obtained when ESA crystals were transferred to the drop with crystallization solution containing 500 μM Dic. After 1 h, crystals of ESA/Dic complex were moved to the drop containing 500 μM Nps in the same crystallization buffer for another hour. The best crystals of ESA/Dic/Nps-d were used in the diffraction experiment. Crystals of ESA without ligands and crystals of both complexes ESA/Dic and ESA/Dic/Nps were obtained by the vapor diffusion hanging drop method in crystallization solutions containing 1.6–2.0 M ammonium sulfate and 0.1 M acetate buffer, pH 4.5–5.0. Crystallization was carried out at 18 °C.

    Data Collection, Structure Determination, and Refinement

    X-ray diffraction data were collected at 100 K from single crystals of each complex using synchrotron radiation. Diffraction experiments were carried out at the X-13 beamline, DESY, Hamburg, BL.14.2, BESSY, Berlin,(51) and SER-CAT 22ID, APS, Argonne, for the ESA/Dic, ESA/Dic/Nps, and ESA/Dic/Nps-d complexes, respectively. Crystals were cryoprotected by the solution containing Tacsimate at pH 6.0.(52) The hexagonal crystals, space group P61, diffracted to resolutions of 1.98 Å (ESA/Dic), 2.22 Å (ESA/Dic/Nps), and 2.35 Å (ESA/Dic/Nps-d). Diffraction data were processed with XDS(53) and XSCALE.(54) Two data sets from separate parts of the same crystal for soaked ESA/Dic/Nps-d complex were collected and merged together to reduce the effect of radiation damage. The rigid body refinement procedure of Refmac(55) from the CCP4 package(56) was used to solve all the structures. The previously determined structure of the ESA/Nps complex (PDB code 4OT2)(42) was used as the starting model. Atomic coordinates and geometry libraries of the ligands were taken from the Protein Data Bank.(57) The ligands were manually fitted into the electron density using Coot,(58) and the models were refined with Refmac,(55) including anisotropic TLS refinement contribution(59) in the final cycles. Refinement progress and the quality of both structures were controlled by evaluation of R, Rfree,(60) and geometric parameters (see Table 1). The programs PROCHECK(61) and MolProbity(62) were used for evaluation of the quality of the final models. A summary of data collection and the refinement statistics are given in Table 1.

    Isothermal Titration Calorimetry Experiments

    Isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC) measurements were carried out at 25 °C using a VP-ITC microcalorimeter (Micro Cal Inc., Northampton, MA). All solutions, either protein or drugs, were prepared in 50 mM citrate buffer at pH 6.2 and were degassed prior to usage. Despite stereochemical restraints, it is possible to overfit or 'misfit' the diffraction data: an incorrect model can be refined to fairly good R values as several recent examples have shown. Here I propose a reliable and unbiased indicator of the accuracy of such models. By analogy with the cross-validation method of testing statistical models I define a statistical quantity (R(free) (T) that measures the agreement between observed and computed structure factor amplitudes for a 'test' set of reflections that is omitted in the modelling and refinement process. As examples show, there is a high correlation between R(free) (T) and the accuracy of the atomic model phases. This is useful because experimental phase information is usually inaccurate, incomplete or unavailable. I expect that R(free) (T) will provide a measure of the information content of recently proposed models of thermal motion and disorder, time-averaging and bulk solvent.
