    Abstract

    Selective inhibitors of hyaluronan (HA) binding to the cell surface receptor CD44 will have value as probes of CD44-mediated signaling and have potential as therapeutic agents in chronic inflammation, cardiovascular disease, and cancer. Using biophysical binding assays, fragment screening, and crystallographic characterization of complexes with the CD44 HA binding domain, we have discovered an inducible pocket adjacent to the HA binding groove into which small molecules may bind. Iterations of fragment combination and structure-driven design have allowed identification of a series of 1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinolines as the first nonglycosidic inhibitors of the CD44–HA interaction. The affinity of these molecules for the CD44 HA binding domain parallels their ability to interfere with CD44 binding to polymeric HA in vitro. X-ray crystallographic complexes of lead compounds are described and compared to a new complex with a short HA tetrasaccharide, to establish the tetrahydroisoquinoline pharmacophore as an attractive starting point for lead optimization.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The importance of the extracellular matrix (ECM) to cell migration,(1) inflammation,(2) and tumor metastasis(3) is well established. The polysaccharide hyaluronan (HA), a high molecular weight copolymer of N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) and glucuronic acid (GlcUA), is an integral component of the ECM needed to maintain tissue integrity and homeostasis(4) and serves as a common thread to bring adhesion receptors into close proximity to amplify biological effects.(5) HA modulates cell adhesion(5) and proliferation(6) important in inflammation and the progression of many vascular diseases(7) and cancer.(8)
    HA serves as both a structural framework for a well-developed matrix and as a product of tissue injury; cells must be able to recognize changes in HA composition to shape the environment in which they live.(9, 10) Vascular injury or pathogen invasion can raise the proportion of smaller HA fragments that are interpreted as danger signals by cells of the innate immune system.(11) HA fragmentation has been linked to cell differentiation,(12) angiogenesis,(13, 14) tumorigenesis,(15, 16) and in resistance to cancer chemotherapy.(9, 17)
    CD44 is a major cell surface receptor of both polymeric HA (p-HA) and its fragments. It is widely accepted that CD44–HA interactions are important to both immune stimulation and cell adhesion, but CD44 responds differently to HA of varying lengths.(18) The binding to native p-HA promotes epithelial structural integrity and is generally considered to be anti-inflammatory.(4, 19) CD44 binding by HA fragments (<500 kDa) can trigger signaling cascades that result in stimulation of the innate immune response,(13) the recruitment of immune cells,(19) tumor growth,(15, 16, 20-22) HA synthesis,(23) and the disruption of cell surface receptor clustering(24) that supports tissue homeostasis. HA oligosaccharides (o-HA) as short as HA4 or HA6 can also influence a variety of biological processes,(25) presumably because they can disrupt binding to endogenous p-HA.(20, 26, 27)
    The study of the molecular details of CD44-mediated signaling has been hampered, however, by the existence of multiple mechanisms by which cells respond to changing HA populations.(18, 28, 29) Other cell surface receptors such as HMMR (also known as RHAMM) or Toll-like Receptors 2 and 4 preferentially recognize very small HA-derived oligosaccharides.(10, 18, 30) CD44 apparently partners with RHAMM, integrins, and other receptors to regulate activation of pathways that stimulate growth and motility and, in the case of malignant tumors, promote therapeutic resistance.(9, 17) There are limits to what can be learned about CD44 signaling mechanisms by study with oligosaccharide fragments. If one were known, a selective small molecule inhibitor of HA binding to CD44 would provide a means to study signaling and cell localization effects in HA-mediated responses, without the interfering consequences of off-target effects involving other HA receptors.
    It has been proposed that selective antagonists of HA binding by CD44 may be useful in the treatment of inflammatory diseases(6, 31, 32) and certain types of cancer.(8) Monoclonal antibodies to CD44 that block binding to HA have been used to investigate the role of CD44 in immune cell recruitment,(33) where they reduce tissue swelling and inflammation by inhibiting immune cell infiltration.(34) A more recent study has also shown that anti-CD44 antibodies reduce HA-induced vascular smooth muscle cell migration and promote the production of inflammatory cytokines and reactive oxygen species in vascular injured mice.(32) However, a clinical trial initiated to study the utility of CD44 antibodies in cancer chemotherapy was halted due to toxicity.(35, 36) Limited work toward the development of small molecule inhibitors of HA binding has been reported. A natural product isolated from fungi reportedly possesses potent activity as an HA antagonist (24 μM),(37) but the molecule – a fusion of a long fatty acid and a trisaccharide – has not been synthetically prepared and does not have a structure that would be appealing as a drug candidate.
    CD44 is a large membrane-associated glycoprotein of the hyaladherin or “Link” family that is widely expressed in different cell types in a variety of different splice variants.(38) While variant forms of CD44 exist, all include a consensus functional ectodomain at the N-terminus;(38) the hyaluronan-binding domain (HABD). A great deal is known about the structure of the HABD and the interaction it makes with oligosaccharides of HA from structural biology.(39, 40) A recombinantly expressed and refolded HABD is able to bind immobilized high-molecular weight HA with an affinity similar to that of the glycosylated CD44 purified from eukaryotic cells.(41) Crystallographic research has provided a description of the structure of the human HABD,(39) and a complex of the murine HABD with a bound HA octasaccharide (HA8).(40)
    From the structural data, one might conclude that CD44 is not an easily druggable target. The murine HABD–HA complex reveals an extended HA binding site with surface area exceeding 800 Å2 and molecular binding stabilized by a large number of weak interactions involving at least seven consecutive saccharide units of HA. The HABD has no well-formed or deep pockets that would serve as attractive binding sites for small molecule inhibitors and is known to undergo small but important conformational changes upon binding HA.(40) In many respects, the protein–polysaccharide complex resembles protein–protein interactions that are difficult to disrupt effectively with small molecules.
    Given the strong precedent for CD44 HABD structural biology, we have chosen a fragment-based approach to identify possible sites for small molecules.(42) Fragment screening, when coupled with the crystallographic characterization of low affinity hits and structure-based design, permits a direct empirical observation of conformational adjustments needed to accommodate specific bound small molecules and has proven applicable in the search for inhibitors of protein–protein interactions.(43, 44) Knowledge of the structure of complexes with bound molecules can be exploited to combine individual molecular features of different molecular fragments using a well-characterized spatial arrangement that best compliments the altered geometry of a larger protein-binding site, leading to improved ligand design.
    In this report, we describe fragment screening that has led to the identification of a small inducible binding site on CD44 that can be targeted in the search for selective small molecule inhibitors of HA binding. Through a combination of molecular features illuminated by cocrystal structures, a substituted 1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline pharmacophore is identified as a promising lead compound. Biophysical binding and competition studies confirm that binding inhibits CD44 association with p-HA in vitro, establishing this scaffold as the first nonglycosidic inhibitors of this important receptor.

    In Vitro Assays for Inhibitor Characterization

    To effectively quantify the activity of small molecule inhibitors of HA binding to CD44, a sensitive in vitro assay of CD44 binding has been implemented (Figure 1A). The immobilized-HA SPR assay can be used to access the affinity of soluble proteins for a p-HA-coated surface that resembles the extracellular matrix. It is comparable to that employed by others to assess affinity of various CD44 HABD mutants for HA.(40, 45, 46) With this assay, we have measured human CD4420–178 (hHABD) binding with a dissociation constant (KD) of 36.6 ± 4.0 μM (Figure 2A). This KD is higher than the ∼20 μM KD reported previously(40, 46) but may differ due to differences in the density of HA immobilized on a given chip surface or because DMSO is included in the vehicle buffer. Inhibitors of CD44 binding can be recognized by their ability to cause a dose-dependent reduction in binding response observed from a fixed concentration of hHABD (Figure 2B). Because inhibitor activity is expressed as the concentration of 50% inhibition (IC50) relative to a full response of hHABD induced concurrently with vehicle (5% DMSO) on the same chip, the apparent binding affinity to any particular prepared surface may vary without consequence.
    To validate this assay, we quantified the p-HA binding interference from short HA oligosaccharides (o-HA) varying in length from 4 to 12 saccharide units that are known to impact CD44-mediated cell adhesion,(27) presumably by direct competition with matrix HA binding. Resulting IC50 values are summarized in Table 1. (Full dose–response curves underlying these values are shown in Figure 6A). Smaller HA fragments inhibit HABD adsorption less effectively (higher IC50), but there is a clear trend indicating that the limit in potency is nearly reached with HA12. Even HA4 gives rise to quantifiable inhibition from a reasonable dose–response curve in this assay, although the IC50 is only in the millimolar range (1.3 mM, Table 1). The assay can be used to quantify the p-HA binding interference of even very weak inhibitors.

    A second assay was implemented to enable direct measurement of equilibrium dissociation constants (KD) for small molecule binding to CD44 hyaluronan binding domains (Figure 1B) and for use in fragment library screening. Recombinant hHABD or mHABD were immobilized on dextrin-coated gold film chips by amine coupling, where small molecule binding can be monitored using SPR. The binding affinity of oligosaccharides (HA4 to HA12) to murine and human CD44 hyaluronan binding domains was assessed using this assay (Table 1) to confirm that the observed interference activity correlates directly with HABD binding in vitro. Dissociation constants (KD) calculated upon analysis of dose–response curves (shown in Figure 3) are given in Table 1. Oligosaccharide fragments exhibit progressively higher binding affinity with increasing oligosaccharide size. Our measurements reflect affinities that are 3- to 4-fold more potent than previously determined by isothermal titration calorimetry for HA8 and HA10(40) but which preserve the same relative ranking. The sharply lower affinity of HA4 for either human or murine HABD (KD is 245 μM and 428 μM, respectively) compared to larger oligomers is consistent with monovalent competition analyses conducted in whole cells to evaluate oligosaccharide influence on cell adhesion.(27) The sensitivity of this assay for small molecules with low binding affinity validates this assay format for use in finding other molecules that bind to the HABD in vitro.

    Murine HABD–HA4 Binary Complex

    In an attempt to better define an appropriate foci on CD44 for the binding of HA antagonists, we determined the crystal structure of a complex of murine HABD complex with HA4 at 2.55 Å resolution. HA4 was soaked into preformed monoclinic apo crystals, and the structure was solved by molecular replacement. These crystals are of the same form previously reported in apo and HA8-bound structures(40) (Table S1, Supporting Information). Overall, the mHABD structure differs little from the previously reported complex with HA8 (PDB-ID 2JCR). The protein structure can be superimposed upon the HA8-bound atomic coordinates with a root-mean-square deviation of 0.18 Å for 129 paired Cα atoms. HA4 appears to exclusively occupy saccharide subsites 5–8 of the larger HA8 binding groove (Figure 4A). There is no density suggesting even low occupancy in subsites 2–4. Glucuronic acid residues occupy subsites 5 and 7, while N-acetylglucosamine units occupy subsites 6 and 8. There is a small 0.6 Å shift of HA4 toward lower numbered subsites, but key hydrogen bond interactions utilized by comparable residues in the HA8 complex are maintained. The smaller o-HA appears sufficient to induce the “O conformation”(46) or the “high affinity” ligand-bound state previously described only in relation to HA binding of HA8.(40) The loop centered on Arg-45 adjusts in concerted movement with Glu-41, Asn-43, Gly-44, and Arg-82 to create more intimate contacts with the polysaccharide in the HA4 complex (Figure 4B).

    Fragment Screening and the Characterization of Hits

    The Maybridge Ro3 Diversity Library of “fragment” compounds was screened to identify compounds that bind to surface-immobilized hHABD at a fixed initial concentration (5 mM). The SPR assay permitted efficient screening; only two sensor chips and less than 10 μg of hHABD were consumed in the screening of 1000 compounds. A cutoff response threshold (9.85 RU) was selected to achieve an initial 4% hit rate. Compounds that appeared to bind above this threshold in duplicate measurements were examined for dose-dependent responses that fit a 1:1 binding isotherm. Twenty-one compounds listed in Table S3, Supporting Information, met these criteria and were subjected to more detailed characterization, including cocrystallization. Five compounds identified through this initial screening were successfully cocrystallized with murine HABD.
    Two compounds (compounds 1 and 2; Table 2) were deemed worthy of additional investigation due to the observed binding location. Dissociation constants determined for compounds 1 and 2 to hHABD are 8.5 and 6.9 mM, respectively (Table 2). By high-resolution crystallography (1.6 Å) with murine HABD, the two compounds are shown to occupy the same subsite adjacent to the HA binding groove (Figure 5A,B). Both molecules induce alternate conformations of Arg-155, and Asn-29 to create π-stacking above and below the aromatic ring of the small molecule. One exocyclic amine of aniline 1 makes a hydrogen bond with the main chain oxygen of Val-30 and two bound water molecules, while the other amine interacts with another buried water (Figure 5A). The 1,2,3,4-tetrahydroisoquinoline (THIQ) of compound 2 (Table 2) binds with the aromatic ring in the same location beneath Arg-155. This structure reveals a preferred half-chair conformation for the saturated six-membered ring that positions the endocyclic amine directly above the carboxylate of Glu-41 to which it may donate a hydrogen bond (Figure 5B). The induced and apo conformations of Arg-155 and Glu-41 are occupied in roughly equal proportions in this complex, suggesting that compound 2 occupancy is also less than 100%.

    Preliminary Optimization of Fragment Hits

    A direct comparison of the structures of the protein complexes with these two initial hits led us to evaluate compound 3, which combines the tetrahydroisoquinoline of 2 with an exocyclic aryl amine placed on carbon 5 to emulate compound 1. This compound displayed a large 7-fold improvement in affinity for hHABD (KD = 0.9 mM) in relation to compound 2. A similar improvement is seen for mHABD (Table 2). A cocrystal structure at 1.7 Å resolution has been determined to verify that this compound binds at the same site as the fragment precursors (Figure 5C). The improved affinity can be attributed to the presence of hydrogen bonding to both Val-30 and Glu-41, as well as effective π-stacking beneath Arg-155 (Figure 5D). In this complex, the binding site appears to be fully occupied by compound 3 (Figure S2, Supporting Information); there is no evidence of the partial occupancy of the apo conformation. Perhaps most importantly, compound 3 also shows an improved capacity to compete with polymeric HA for binding of CD44 (IC50 = 6.7 mM). This is a 4-fold improvement over the minimal activity of the original THIQ fragment (compound 2).
    It should be noted that HABD binding alone is not sufficient to inhibit binding to immobilized HA. One molecule initially identified by fragment screening (compound 4) binds hHABD with affinity higher than that of either compounds 1 or 2 (Table 2). Cocrystallography shows that it binds on the back side of the HABD far from the HA binding groove in a shallow subsite formed adjacent to Arg-33, Phe-38, Phe-60, Asn-125, Ser-136, and Val-137, flanking the loop between the strand of β7 to β9 (Figure S4, Supporting Information). This amphiphilic molecule interacts with a relatively hydrophobic core of the pocket via a chlorine and a methyl group and forms a hydrogen bond with a carbonyl oxygen of the more solvent-accessible Ser-136. Binding occurs without any significant changes to the apo mHABD conformation. Nevertheless, compound 4 has no effect on HABD binding to immobilized HA (Table 2).
    Next we examined a number of commercially available analogues of 2 bearing substitutions on the THIQ scaffold (Table S5, Supporting Information). Our purpose was to identify analogues that extend far enough into the HA binding groove when bound to compete directly for HA binding. A number of these compounds possess affinity for the HABD higher than that of the initial screening hit, and crystal structures of several of these complexes have also been determined (Table S5, Supporting Information; not all data is shown). Compound 5 is distinctive in the way that it binds with a well-ordered methylimidazole extending across the CD44 surface toward the HA binding groove (Figure 5E). A torsional preference conferred by the ortho methyl group brings about a desirable 90° twist of the imidazole from the plane of the THIQ, allowing for the protonated NH of the imidazole to donate a hydrogen bond to Glu-41 below, while the methyl group points upward to lie within 3.2 Å of GlcUA residue atoms occupying saccharide subsite 5 in the HA4-bound complex. The affinity of compound 5 for hHABD is only marginally improved over that seen for compound 2 (KD = 4.6 mM), but the binding mode positions the exocyclic methyl group adjacent to HA saccharide-binding subsite 4; it is easy to imagine how this molecule could be further modified to improve p-HA antagonism.
    Surprisingly, the crystal structure with compound 5 reveals that the THIQ is flipped over in the binding site, so that the endocyclic nitrogen rests in a position comparable to that occupied by C2 in previous complexes. (A comparison of binding modes observed for compounds 2 and 5 is provided in Figure S6A, Supporting Information). Small conformational changes to Glu-41, coupled with an inversion of the saturated ring pucker and a small rotation of the THIQ scaffold within the binding pocket, make it possible for molecules with the endocyclic nitrogen at either position to bind and still preserve similar hydrogen bonding (Figure S6B, Supporting Information). The two THIQ orientations likely have comparable binding energies. The small difference in binding affinity of the 5-amino THIQ (0.9 mM) (compound 3) and the 8-amino THIQ (1.2 mM) (compound 6 in Table S5, Supporting Information) supports this assertion. In the course of study of analogues with only different alkyl extensions of either the 2 or 3-position of the THIQ core (Table S5, Supporting Information), we have observed binding of molecules in either orientation (not all data shown). The preferred binding mode of any particular compound is likely driven by a need to optimize other interactions.
    Given the binding mode observed for compound 5, we hypothesized that addition of an exocyclic amine to compound 5 at the 8 position (compound 5a; Table 2) would lead to further potency gains. This proved to be true. A crystal structure with 5a (Figure 5F) confirms the expectation of H-bonding to both carboxylate oxygen atoms of Glu-41 as seen in the complex with compound 5, and the additional H-bonds involving the exocyclic amine, Val-30, and bound water previously observed in complexes with compounds 1, 3, and 5. The affinity for hHABD improves significantly, (KD = 0.42 mM) while the p-HA inteference activity also improves (IC50 = 3.5 mM). These results confirm that a combination of compound design elements can achieve some measure of additivity desirable for future potency optimization.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    To discover possible binding sites for small molecules that might interfere with HA binding to CD44, we conducted fragment screening with label-free biophysical assays utilizing SPR. A surface onto which the extracellular hyaluronan binding domain of CD44 has been immobilized was used to screen a compound library efficiently and to measure binding affinity of small molecules for the hHABD. This initial screen identified compounds that bind (Table S3, Supporting Information) but not necessarily compounds that interfere with HA binding. Structural studies led to the identification of relevant analogues with both higher binding affinity and interference of p-HA binding in vitro. The barely detectable interference activity of compound 2 (IC50 = 27 mM) would not have attracted much interest were it not for the coincidental binding of other molecules (e.g., compound 1) that bind at the same site. The juxtaposition of this binding site and the site occupied by HA8 (Figure 7A) heightened interest and motivated a search for more potent compounds that combined the structural features responsible for the stabilization of low affinity complexes with both molecules. In this case, fragment screening accomplished exactly what was hoped: it led to the empirical identification of sites for small molecule binding that would not have otherwise been known. It also provided a means to empirically observe protein conformational adaptations associated with small molecule binding, and insights into the rational design of more potent antagonists.
    We show with oligosaccharides that there is good correlation between binding affinity as measured with immobilized HABD on SPR in vitro and binding to CD44 measured by other means.(41) Others have shown that small oligosaccharide HA fragments that compete directly for HA also modulate CD44-mediated adhesion and motility in cells (see below). Banerji et al. have shown that HA binding occurs across a shallow groove that spans the width of the HABD.(40) Our HA4 complex structure further isolates subsites 5–8 as those preferred for smaller o-HA fragments that bind CD44 (Figure 4). Because our structural data establishes that the THIQ pharmacophore also binds within the foci of the larger HA binding site and we can show a direct competition for HA binding in vitro, we anticipate that these molecules should also have biological activity. Experimental proof of this hypothesis must await direct measurements of cell motility with cells that lie beyond of the scope of this report. While the affinity of the lead compounds is modest and additional potency may be needed to see effects, it can be noted that very short oligosaccharides with only marginally higher affinity have an effect on CD44-mediated cell adhesion.(27)
    The presence or absence of HA-binding inhibition by nonglycosidic molecules can be rationalized based on a structural understanding of how these molecules bind in relation to known HA binding. Any molecule that binds within this groove (including o-HA fragments), or that has the capacity to stabilize a protein conformation that is not consistent with this binding mode, can potentially act as an antagonist that interferes with CD44 binding to polymeric HA or the HA-coated surface. Some molecules, such as compound 1, likely have insufficient potency to show detectable inhibition, but these molecules also may not be large enough to prevent simultaneous binding by HA. Our attempt to prepare a ternary complex of compound 1, HA4, and mHABD by soaking generated only another HA4 cocrystal (data not shown). Molecules that include the intact tetrahydroisoquinoline (such as compound 5a) may be large enough to prevent simultaneous binding of HA by steric exclusion at the GlcNAc saccharide that would occupy subsite 4 (Figure 7B). It is possible to imagine derivatives incorporating the THIQ pharmacophore that extend farther into the HA binding groove, particularly into the HA subsites 4 or 5 (Figure 7B), but this may not be a requirement for antagonist activity. Further optimization of these molecules will be needed to improve binding affinity and HA binding inhibition and to study the effect of antagonism of the CD44–HA interaction in vivo.
    The binding site of the THIQ inhibitors is one we hoped to target with fragment screening. Prior to screening, a computational method(47) was applied to the apo hHABD structure (PDB-ID 1UUH)(39) to detect and rank possible small molecule binding sites along the HA binding groove. The method identified a cavity between Thr-31 and Arg-155 (equivalent to Thr-27 and Arg-150 in human) based on surface curvature, surface area, and residue hydrophilicity, but it could not have predicted the concerted changes in side-chain conformations of Asn-29, Arg-155, and Glu-41 required to establish a favorable site for THIQ binding. An R155A mutation had previously been shown to reduce the affinity of HABD for HA but not to abolish binding.(40, 48) In murine mutation studies, greater importance has been attributed to the conformation of Arg-45 because the R45A mutant has much smaller affinity for HA.(40) A shift in Arg-45 has been proposed as the principle difference between the open and the closed state of CD44 induced upon HA binding(40) (illustrated in Figure 4B). Both solution structural studies(40, 45) and molecular dynamics calculations(49) have supported this hypothesis. The inhibition by the THIQ molecules may be enhanced because they can induce local conformational changes that are inconsistent with those induced by HA binding. All of these molecules induce distinct conformational changes involving residues (Asn-29, Glu-41, and Arg-82) that are in direct contact with the loop of Arg-45 that must respond to create the high-affinity HA-bound form of the HABD. Notably, the installation of an extended group such as the N-linked imidazole (compound 5 and 5a) appears to constrain the Arg-82 conformation and prevent the shift in Arg-45 from making intimate contacts with polysaccharides. A detailed comparison of this conformation to the HA-bound state is presented in Figure 7B.
    The scaffold of tetrahydroisoquinolines (THIQ) constitutes a promising lead for further structure-driven optimization. Unlike the oligosaccharides that lie across large and exposed binding sites, the THIQ molecules induce formation of a small pocket between Arg-155 and Asn-29 that allows them to bind with ligand efficiency much higher than that of oligosaccharide fragments. It should be possible to create molecules that combine this scaffold with fragments of natural saccharide ligands to create potent antagonists of HA binding. Compound 5a is particularly compelling as a starting point for additional optimization; this imidazole adds little to the compound binding affinity, but the complex crystal structure shows that it binds positioned for further extension into the HA binding groove. We have already shown that a combination of compound design elements can achieve some measure of additivity desirable for future potency optimization.
    In the course of this study, human CD4420–178 (hHABD) and murine CD4425–174 (mHABD) have been used interchangeably as convenient. The two CD44 HABD constructs share 86.2% sequence identity across 159 residues, and previous structural studies have confirmed that only small differences occur near the HA binding site.(39, 40) The yield from E. coli expression of hHABD is higher than mHABD, so it has been used in the competition studies with immobilized-HA that require more protein. All cocrystallography has utilized mHABD. The monoclinic crystal form previously described(40) provides open access to the binding site upon the soaking of small molecules, even molecules as large as HA4. While we have been able to reproduce orthorhombic crystals of hHABD(39) and have also discovered a novel hexagonal form (data not shown), we, as others,(40) have not been able to prepare cocrystals with hHABD. We have consistently measured lower binding affinities of oligosaccharides to immobilized mHABD than to hHABD (Table 1), even as the same relative affinities of different o-HA molecules are preserved. The THIQ inhibitors, however, have comparable affinities for either HABD (Table 2). A possible explanation for this lies in the substitution of Asn-101 in hHABD for histidine in mHABD. This conservative difference exists at the extreme end of the binding groove; in the mHABD complex with HA8, His-105 contacts the GlcNAc residue in subsite 8. This difference could account for the lower affinity of any of the oligosaccharides we have studied for mHABD, because they all occupy this subsite upon binding. The THIQ antagonists do not.
    The binding of CD44 to the extracellular matrix promotes cell adhesion and tissue structural integrity.(9, 31) Small o-HA fragments can disrupt these interactions.(20, 26, 27) It is likely that many of the biological effects on cells attributed to o-HAs are simply due to the dissociation of receptors bound to the ECM that can occur upon o-HA binding. The ability of o-HA preparations of different lengths to alter cell avidity for fluorescein-labeled HA has been studied by flow cytometry.(27) The monovalent avidity of cells depends on the amount of cell surface CD44 present and is diminished upon exposure to HA6 or HA8 and, to a significantly lesser extent, HA4. Our measurements of o-HA binding provide the quantitative assessment of the specific binding affinity of oligosaccharides to CD44 HABD (Table 1), and these results are entirely consistent with measurements made with competition analyses using soluble p-HA.(26, 27, 39) The SPR surface with immobilized polymeric HA with concentrations fixed at ∼1 mg/mL (115 RU) may better represent a compact matrix-like environment for use in competition assays than soluble p-HA.
    Unlike o-HA, we expect THIQ compounds to possess high specificity for CD44. There is no reason to believe there would be any cross-reactivity with unrelated HA receptors such as RHAMM or Toll-Like Receptors, that employ distinct HA recognition mechanisms, given the structure-driven approach used for lead discovery in this report. There are, however, a number of other receptors that include a “Link” module with homology to CD44.(10, 29, 50) Structural studies with TSG-6 show that His-39 and Glu-41 of CD44 are conserved amino acids but not Arg-155 or Thr-31.(51) On the basis of a comparison of structures (PDB-ID 2PF5), the THIQ scaffold is not expected to bind TSG-6 with any observable affinity. LYVE-1 Link domain homology to CD44 is high (51%), but among residues primarily responsible for the THIQ binding site, only Glu-41 is conserved.(29) N29S, H39L, R155Q sequence differences likely mean that THIQ compounds are also ineffective inhibiting that binding of HA by LYVE-1.
    A variety of different studies have shown that o-HAs can influence cell-adhesion processes through CD44. Oligosaccharide administration affects wound healing,(13, 19) tumorigenesis,(15, 20-22, 52) tissue integrity,(4, 19) CD44 receptor clustering in lipid rafts,(24) and the colocalization of CD44 with other receptors that also bind HA.(53) Measurements are complicated, however, by potential interference posed by other receptors that also bind HA, or other receptors that might compete for the binding of o-HA fragments. A potent small molecule HA-binding antagonist may have the capacity to mimic o-HA effects in any and all of these biological contexts if the biology is truly mediated by the CD44–HA interaction.
    There are, however, many other CD44-associated events that may arise from more specific molecular signaling. CD44 has been implicated in Fas-mediated apoptosis,(54) in the phosphoinositide 3-kinase/Akt tumor cell survival pathway,(20) in activation of the NF-κB pathway involving Ras and protein kinase C,(14, 55) and in ERK signaling and transcriptional activation.(56-58) CD44 is also apparently involved in initiating signaling through the Hippo(59) and RhoATPase(60) pathways and has been linked to the changes in the catabolism of HA,(61) lipogenesis in cancer cell survival,(62) and the emergence of resistance to cancer chemotherapy.(38, 63) The molecular mechanism of signal initiation is largely unknown. It would be interesting to see whether these molecules that disrupt HA binding have the capacity to initiate these signaling cascades or whether they have no effect whatsoever. These agents therefore have considerable potential value as tools for further research.

    Preparation of CD44 HABDs

    Human and murine CD44 HABDs were cloned, expressed, and purified to homogeneity as described for human and murine CD44.(40, 41) For bacterial expression of HABDs, the ectodomain constructs of human CD4420–178 (hCD44) and murine CD4425–174 (mCD44) were prepared from full-length cDNA clones using appropriate primers. These segments were amplified using standard forms of human and murine CD44 cDNA as a template prior to being cloned into the pMCSG7 expression vector digested with NdeI and XhoI. The resulting plasmids were transformed into the E. coli strain Rosetta 2(DE3)pLysS for bacterial expression.
    Soluble HABDs were expressed, refolded, and purified from insoluble inclusion bodies as previously described.(41) In brief, denatured HABDs were purified from washed inclusion bodies and refolded in a buffer containing 250 mM l-arginine and 2 mM reduced and 1 mM oxidized glutathione. The solution was slowly stirred at 4 °C for 24 h followed by ultrafiltration. Monomeric HABDs were separated from the oligomeric forms by a single pass through Sephacryl S-100 and analyzed on SDS/PAGE. This results in yields of 30 mg and 10 mg each from 1 L of culture for the human and murine construct, respectively. The sequence and formation of three disulfides in the two constructs were confirmed by mass spectroscopy and crystallography. The molecular mass of the refolded human and murine HABD were 17580.9 and 16947.6 Da by electrospray ionization mass spectroscopy, consistent with the expected sizes for fully reduced proteins 17586.5 and 16952.9 Da, respectively.

    Protein Crystallization and Data Collection

    Murine HABD apo crystals have been reproduced as described(40) for cocrystallization experiments. Briefly, protein was concentrated to 8 mg/mL prior to mixing in a 1:1 ratio with a well solution containing 30% PEG MME 5000, 200 mM Na2SO4, and 100 mM MES buffer pH 6.5 to obtain a monoclinic form in space group P21 in a few days. Separate plate-shaped crystals form after two to three rounds of microseeding with crushed polymorphic plates that form initially. HA tetrasaccharide complex cocrystals were prepared by soaking apo crystals in a droplet of well solution containing HA oligosaccharide 4-mer (HA4) in a final concentration of 32.5 mM for 30 min. Crystals were flash frozen in liquid nitrogen after quick transfer into the well solution with an additional 10% glycerol and stored until required for data collection.

    Immobilized-HA SPR Assay

    Competitive binding analyses were performed on a polymeric HA-coated surface (Figure 1A).(40, 45, 46) To prepare this surface, biotinylated HA was synthesized from hyaluronan sodium salt and hydrazide biotin as described(64) and immobilized onto a streptavidin-coated chip (Biacore, GE Healthcare) as progress was monitored in a Biacore 2000. Biotinylated-HA in HEPES-EP (10 mM HEPES pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl, 3 mM EDTA, 0.005% (v/v) surfactant P-20) was passed over the chip at a flow rate of 20 μL/min until response units stabilized at 115 RU. An equilibrium binding constant (KD) for wild-type hHABD in the presence of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) as solvent vehicle was determined for use in competition binding studies. Two-fold serial dilutions of the protein solution (0–181 μM) in HEPES-EP containing 5% DMSO were injected over the surface at 20 μL/min for 1 min and dissociated in the same buffer composition. When hHABD is premixed with small molecule ligands, changes in the apparent KD identifies inhibitors of HA binding. For the competition binding experiments, seven or eight solutions containing a fixed concentration of hHABD (typically, 17 μM) were flowed over a working surface at a constant rate (20 μL/min) for 1 min after being pre-equilibrated with various concentrations of ligands in HEPES-EP containing 5% DMSO at 25 °C. The same method was applied to measure the interference of hHABD binding to p-HA by oligosaccharides and non-HA derived small molecule ligands. Their activity can be expressed as IC50, the concentration resulting in a 50% decrease in hHABD binding to HA on the chip surface. Each ligand series includes a blank solution containing only the protein that provides a reference full response of hHABD binding to the immobilized HA. A “blank” reference surface was coated with only streptavidin. All of the samples, along with blanks from buffer and solvent calibration solutions,(65) were centrifuged and measured on a 96-well microplate (Biacore, GE Healthcare) at 25 °C.
    Reflectivity response data points were extracted from response curves in the 5 s prior to the end of the injection to determine steady-state binding (Figure 1A). All the data were solvent calibrated and double referenced with blanks using standard procedures(65) with BIAevaluation software v4.0.1. The equilibrium binding constant KD of hHABD was measured by fitting a curve to the calibrated data with nonlinear regression of one-site saturation binding. IC50 was calculated after the calibrated data were experimentally fit to a four-parameter dose–response curve from plots of the binding signal as a function of transformed ligand concentrations using Graphpad Prism 5.04.

    Immobilized-HABD SPR Assay

    Recombinantly expressed human or murine HABD was immobilized on the CM5 sensor chip (Biacore, GE Healthcare) via amine coupling for use in HABD binding assays utilizing SPR (Figure 1B). Briefly, the dextran surface was activated with a 1:1 mixture of 0.4 M 1-ethyl-3-(3-dimethylaminopropyl)carbodiimide hydrochloride and 0.1 M N-hydroxysuccinimide. Human HABD (150 μg/mL) in 10 mM sodium acetate at pH 4.4 was flowed past a working surface for 7 min before blocking the remaining activated carboxymethyl groups with 1 M ethanolamine at pH 8.5 to achieve a level of 3500 RU suitable for binding analysis. The reference surface was activated and reacted with only ethanolamine.

    Fragment Screening

    The Maybridge Ro3 Diversity Library of 1000 small molecular “fragments” was screened using the immobilized-HABD SPR assay on a Biacore 3000. Each compound dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide at 100 mM was diluted in 1.05X PBS buffer with P-20, to create 5 mM fragment solutions in 5% DMSO. Fragments plated on 96-well plates were centrifuged, replated with supernatants, and screened in duplicate at this concentration for 30-s association followed by 60-s dissociation to increase throughput of the assay at 25 °C. Any compounds not soluble in this concentration were excluded from the screen. This was pumped with PBS buffer containing 0.005% P-20 and 5% DMSO at a flow rate of 30 μL/min. A positive control composed of crude HA8 at 500 μM was included at 10 sample intervals throughout screening to calibrate changes of protein activity. Primary hits were selected above the calculated detection cutoff (9.85 RU) determined as described.(66) When appropriate, binding was validated with dose–response curves for human or murine HABD using 2-fold serial dilutions of compounds for a 60-s association and dissociation period in triplicate. Murine HABD can be immobilized as performed with human HABD. The dose-dependent binding of different lengths of HA oligosaccharides was measured similarly in HEPES-EP. Data were analyzed in a way similar to that for the immobilized-HA assay and fit into a 1:1 binding isotherm.

    Small Molecule Protein Crystallization

    Small molecule cocomplexes can be prepared by soaking apo crystals in a well solution including 50 mM to 300 mM of compounds for 3 to 12 h. Crystals were flash frozen after brief transfer to 10% glycerol in the presence of equal concentrations of compounds.

    Structural Determination and Refinement

    Diffraction data from murine HABD cocrystals were collected from single crystals at IMCA-CAT (Advanced Photon Source, Argonne, IL) or 4.2.2 (Advanced Light Source, Berkeley, CA). These data were processed using XDS(67) and scaled with SCALA.(68) The structures of the murine HABD complexes in the monoclinic space group P21 were solved by molecular replacement with the program PHASER,(69) using the apo monomer structure as a search model (PDB entry 2JCP). To avoid phase bias, a common Rfree reflection set was used for all structures. Iterative rounds of model building and restrained refinement were carried out with COOT(70) and REFMAC5.(71) Ligand coordinates and stereochemical restraints were generated in JLigand(72) or PRODRG.(73) Refined structures were validated with MolProbity.(74) Atomic coordinates and reflection data have been deposited in the Protein Data Bank.(75) Crystallographic data collection, refinement statistics, and PDB accession codes are provided in Table S1, Supporting Information.
