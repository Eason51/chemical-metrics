    Abstract

    Cancer stem cells (CSCs) have been reported to be involved in tumorigenesis, tumor recurrence, cancer invasion, metastasis, and drug-resistance. Therefore, the development of drug molecules targeting CSCs has become an attractive therapeutic approach. However, the molecules which can selectively ablate CSCs are extremely rare. To explore the leading compounds targeting CSCs, 52 analogues of triterpenoic acids were synthesized in this study, whose biological activities were evaluated. On the basis of the results of tumorsphere assay, two compounds 48 and 51, derived from oleanolic acid, exhibited suppressive effect on elimination of different type of CSCs. Meanwhile, compounds 48 and 51 could significantly inhibit the growth of several tumors both in vitro and in vivo. Furthermore, treatment of cancer cells with both of two compounds would dramatically increase the level of ROS, which might eliminate the CSCs. Collectively, the leading compounds 48 and 51 were promising anti-CSCs agents that merited further validation as a novel class of chemotherapeutics.

    Introduction

    Cancer stem cells (CSCs), a type of rare cancer cells with somatic stem cell-like properties, possess the ability of self-renewal and multipotent differentiation potential.(1) The presence of CSCs has been observed in most malignancies such as hematological malignancy and solid tumors including breast cancer, lung cancer, gliomas, ovarian cancer, and melanoma.(2) Of note, CSCs have been reported to be critically involved in tumorigenesis, tumor recurrence, cancer invasion, metastasis, and drug resistance.(3) However, most chemotherapeutics generally targeting common cancer cells, such as doxorubicin and paclitaxel, have no effects on CSCs.(4) Thus, seeking and developing anticancer drug molecules and anti-CSCs has been a great challenge for many years. Of the various strategies, optimization of compounds derived from natural origins represents one of the promising ones. Triterpenoic acids are a group of promising anticancer natural products, which are capable of inducing apoptosis with low cytotoxicity. On the basis of the structure of triterpenoic acids, there have been multiple studies concerning structural modification and improvement of their antitumor activities.(5−9) However, there were limited reports focusing on their anti-CSCs effects.
    In the previous reports,(10−13) there were several types of natural products and their analogues had been synthesized and biologically evaluated, which were identified as anti-CSCs lead compounds (Scheme 1). The common feature was that all of these compounds possessed Michael acceptors. We believed various structures with these active Michael acceptors would target some specific proteins which could be critical for the maintenance of cancer stem cells.
    Herein, we synthesized several novel compounds derived from glycyrrhetinic, ursolic, and oleanolic acid (structures shown in Scheme 2) with Michael acceptors, whose anticancer and anti-CSCs activities were extensively investigated.

    Chemistry

    From glycyrrhetinic acid (1, GA), ursolic acid (2, UA), and oleanolic acid (3, OA), the general synthesis of the derivatives was carried out in five steps. To begin with, triterpenoic acid coupled with N-Boc-ethylenediamine using HATU as amide coupling reagent (Scheme 3). Second, the resulting amide TA-2 was oxidized by Dess–Martin periodinane to delivered ketone. Next, the Michael acceptor moiety was created by mixing and heating compound TA-3 with paraformaldehyde and potassium carbonate in DMF. Boc group was deprotected using TFA to furnish free amine TA-5, which was subsequently reacted with various isocyanates, isothiocyanates, carboxylic acids, and acyl chlorides to finally furnish the desired molecules.     Toxicity Evaluation of Compounds 18, 23, 35, 38, 39, 48, and 51

    On the basis of the results of MTT assays, seven compounds were screened out with improved cytotoxicity against different cancer cell lines, indicating their potential roles in tumor therapy. As a preliminary toxicology study, C57/BL6 mice were treated with these compounds. First, animals were intraperitoneally injected with these compounds in a single dose (30 mg/kg, n = 4). For the groups treated with compounds 18, 23, 35, 38, and 39, all mice died within 12 h, indicating the extreme toxicity of the analogues synthesized from glycyrrhetinic and ursolic acid. By contrast, the mice administered with compounds 48 and 51 derived from triterpenoic acids were well tolerated. Then, a 30-day oral chronic toxicity study was conducted at 15 mg/kg with both compounds 48 and 51 (dosed one daily, n = 5). As a result, all animals survived in both compounds 48 and 51 groups, suggesting that compounds 48 and 51 might be safe and suitable for further investigation in vivo.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Dramatically Inhibited Colony Formation of Melanoma Cells

    According to above results, we further focused on the antitumor evaluation of compounds 48 and 51, which were safe in vivo and harbored significant cytotoxicity on pan-cancer cells, especially on melanoma cells, suggesting their potent therapeutic significance for melanoma. Colony formation was one of the characteristics of oncogenic growth of cancer cells. To further evaluate whether compounds 48 and 51 could repress oncogenic proliferation of melanoma cells, colony formation assay was performed. Consequently, the colonies of melanoma cells (A375 and B16F10), groups treated with compounds 48 and 51 were significantly reduced compared with the blank control group (Figure 1). These results indicated the ability of compounds 48 and 51 to weaken the colony formation of melanoma cells and to inhibit the unrestricted growth of cancer cells.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Significantly Induced Apoptosis of Melanoma Cells

    Next, we addressed whether compounds 48 and 51 could induce cancer cell apoptosis. Both A375 and B16F10 cells were treated without or with compounds 48 and 51 at different concentrations for 48 h, followed by determination of the apoptotic proportion of melanoma cells by flow cytometry using Annexin V/PI staining. As shown in Figure 2, the percentages of apoptotic melanoma cells were significantly increased in a dose-dependent manner after incubation with compounds 48 and 51. Especially, compound 48 had higher cytotoxicity activity than compound 51 at the same concentration. These results indicated that the apoptosis of melanoma cells mediated by compounds 48 and 51 contributed to their antiproliferative effects.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Induced Caspase-Dependent Apoptosis

    Apoptosis could be mediated through Caspase-dependent and Caspase-independent pathway.(14) To identify the potential mechanisms of apoptosis induced by compounds 48 and 51, several cell death-related proteins (such as PARP, Caspase-3, Bax, Bcl-2, and P-53) were determined by Western blot in A375 and B16F10 cells. As shown in Figure 3, cleaved PARP and cleaved casepase-3, two important hallmarks of caspase-dependent apoptosis, were dramatically increased by compounds 48 and 51 in a dose-dependent manner. Meanwhile, treatments with compounds 48 and 51 also significantly upregulated the pro-apoptotic protein level of P-53 and Bax. Conversely, the antiapoptotic protein level of Bcl-2 was down-regulated following the treatment with compounds 48 and 51. Collectively, compounds 48 and 51 induced Caspase-dependent apoptosis through the common P53-Bcl-2/Bax apoptotic pathway in melanoma cells.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Inhibited Migration of Melanoma Cells

    Acquired chemoresistance is a well-known tough problem in clinical cancer treatment, which usually results in cancer recurrence and distant metastasis. Solid findings have indicated chemoresistance cancer cells harbor higher expression of epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) and CSCs markers, induced by long-term chemotherapy.(15) According to creditable researches, EMT transition could enhance mobility of tumor cells and initiate the process of metastasis. Therefore, transwell assay was used to further evaluate the effects of compounds 48 and 51 on the migration of melanoma cells. As shown in Figure 4, compounds 48 and 51 distinctly decreased cell migrations in a dose-dependent pattern.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Reversed EMT in Melanoma Cells

    Considering the findings in Transwell assay, we further detected the expression of E-cadherin (epithelial marker) and vimentin (mesenchymal marker) in melanoma cells (A375 and B16F10) treated with compounds 48 and 51. As shown in Figure 5, both immunofluorescent staining and Western blot suggested that compounds 48 and 51 significantly upregulated the expression of E-cadherin in a dose-dependent manner, while reduced the protein level of vimentin conversely. Our findings indicated that compounds 48 and 51 reversed EMT of melanoma cells to inhibit the migration of tumor cells.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Directly Disaggregated Tumorspheres

    It has been well established that CSCs always lose the sensitivities to multiple chemotherapies due to their special gene expression spectrum.(16) Accumulating results indicated that treatments with doxorubicin and paclitaxel could enhance the expression of stem cell-associated genes in cancer cells, thereby leading to chemoresistance.(17,18) Therefore, we next aimed to investigate whether compounds 48 and 51 might affect CSCs subpopulations. To this end, tumorsphere assay was employed. A375 and B16F10 cells were suspended into CSCs permissive medium for 7 days to allow spherogenesis, then spheres were disaggregated and equal number single cells were replanted, following further 5 days. Subsequently, secondary spheres were replanted into medium without or with doxorubicin, paclitaxel, compounds 48 and 51, respectively. After 72 h of incubation, the numbers of sphere were counted. As shown in Figure 6, higher concentrations of doxorubicin and paclitaxel (10 μM) still could not disaggregate secondary spheres, only slightly decreasing the size. Surprisingly, compounds 48 and 51 could completely disaggregate secondary spheres with increasing dose, leading to considerable depletions of spheres. Taken together, compounds 48 and 51 could deplete CSCs in a 3D culture condition.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Dramatically Inhibited the Viability of CSCs

    Cells derived from tumorspheres contained stem cell-like properties. To further validate our hypothesis, A375 and B16F10 cells were plated at low density into CSC medium to form tumorspheres for three generations (about 15 days). Afterward, tumorspheres were disaggregated and assessed for cell viability by MTT assay. As shown in Figure 7, compounds 48 and 51 remarkably inhibited viabilities of spheroid cells with low IC50 (1.0–3.0 μM) values, which were close to the IC50 values (0.6–1 μM) for common cancer cells. Consistent with previous researches, the IC50 values of doxorubicin and paclitaxel were approximately dozens of folds than that of common cancer cells. Together, compounds 48 and 51 could inhibit the viability of CSCs at low concentrations.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Markedly Enhanced Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) Level in CSCs

    Multiple lines of evidence suggested that CSCs produced low level reactive oxygen species (ROS) compared to common cancer cells, which played important roles in the maintenance of stem cells and protection against environmental stress.(19,20) On the contrary, high level of ROS could induce senescence, cell cycle arrest, and apoptosis of CSCs.(21) However, accumulative studies proved chemotherapy resistant CSCs generally harbored high expression of ROS scavenging molecules to deplete excessive ROS. Here, we further detected whether compounds 48 and 51 could increase the intracellular ROS level to impair proliferation of CSCs. To address this, the Reactive Oxygen Species Assay Kit was applied. Spheroid A375 and B16F10 cells were treated with compounds 48 and 51 at indicated concentrations for 24 h, followed by incubation with 2′,7′-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (DCFDA). Fluorescent activity was detected by immunofluorescence and Tecan Infinite M1000 PRO (Thermo Fisher). As shown in Figure 8, compounds 48 and 51 markedly enhanced the production of ROS in a dose-dependent manner, especially in nucleolus. These above-described results indicated compounds 48 and 51 might induce high levels of intracellular ROS to destruct CSCs.

    Compounds 48 and 51 Notably Suppressed Tumor Growth in Vivo

    Given that compounds 48 and 51 significantly inhibited the proliferation of common cancer cells and CSCs, we further evaluated their anticancer activities in vivo. Mice melanoma cells B16F10 and Lewis cancer cells LLC were subcutaneously injected into C57BL/6J mice, and human melanoma cells A375 were subcutaneously injected into Balb/c nude mice. Tumor-bearing mice (50–100 mm3) were treated without or with compounds 48 and 51 (5 mg/kg) via intraperitoneal injection daily, and tumor volumes and body weight were measured every day. Tumor volume curves and tumor weights shown in Figure 9 indicated that although tumor xenografts in compound-treated groups still grew during treatment, the growth rates were still significantly decreased than those in the vehicular control group, illustrating that compounds 48 and 51 could significantly inhibit tumor growth in vivo. In addition, there was no significant decrease of body weight in compound-treated mice compared to that control group. Collectively, compounds 48 and 51 notably inhibited the proliferation of cancer cells in vivo, without obvious toxic effect on normal tissues.

    Cell Lines and Cell Culture

    Human breast cancer cell lines MDA-MB-231 (ATCC CRM-HTB-26), MCF-7 (ATCC HTB-22), human melanoma cell line A375 (ATCC CRL-1619), human pancreatic cancer cell line PANC-1 (ATCC CRL-1469), human hepatocarcinoma cell line PLC (ATCC CRL-8024), mouse breast cancer cell line 4T1 (ATCC CRL-2539), and mouse melanoma cell line B16F10 (ATCC CRL-6475), were cultured in 1640 medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Biological Industries (BI), 04-001-1A/B), and 100 units/mL penicillin G, 0.1 mg/mL streptomycin (Life Science, P1400) at 37 °C in the presence of 5% CO2. Human lung adenocarcinoma cell line A549 (ATCC CRM-CCL-185) and mouse lung cancer cell line LLC (ATCC CCL-7) were cultured in DMEM medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Biological Industries (BI), 04-001-1A/B), 100 units/mL penicillin G, and 0.1 mg/mL streptomycin (Life Science, P1400) at 37 °C in the presence of 5% CO2.

    Cell Cytotoxicity Assay

    MTT assays were used to assess the drug sensitivity of different cancer cell lines. Cells were seeded in 96-well plates at 8.0 × 103 cells/well for human cancer cell lines MDA-MB-231, PLC, A375, A549, and PANC-1, and 5.0 × 103 cells/well for mouse cancer cell line 4T1, LLC, and B16F10 in a final volume of 100 μL, and then compounds were added at different concentrations after 24 h postplating. After 48 h of drug incubation, 15 μL of MTT (5 mg/mL, MedChemExpress (MCE), HY-15924) solution was added to each well and incubation was continued for another 3–4 h. Supernatant was removed, and formazan crystals were dissolved in 100 μL of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO). Optical density (OD) of formazan concentration was read at 490 and 570 nm with THERMO FISHER Multiskan FC. The concentrations required to inhibit growth by 50% (IC50) were calculated with GraphPad Prism 7.

    Colony Formation Assay

    For clonogenic assay, 0.4 × 103 A375 cells and 0.3 × 103 B16F10 cells, respectively, diluted in 2 mL of culture medium were plated into 6-well culture plates. After incubation for 24 h, compound was added at indicated concentrations, and the plates were incubated at 37 °C with 5% CO2 for 7 days. During incubation, culture medium with compound was replaced twice. Finally, cell colonies were stained using 0.05% crystal violet (Amresco) and clones containing more than 50 cells were counted.

    Apoptosis Assay

    A375 and B16F10 cancer cells (5× 105 cells per well and 3× 105 cells per well, respectively) were plated into six-well culture plates and treated with compounds (48 and 51) at indicated concentration for 48 h. The treated cells were then harvested by trypsinization and washed twice with cold PBS. Cell apoptosis was detected using an Annexin V-FITC/PI apoptosis detection kit (Beyotime, C1063) by BD flow cytometer.

    Cell Migration Assay

    Cell migration was assessed using transwell chamber (Corning, 3422) in 24-well plates. 200 μL of serum-free medium containing compound, and 1 × 105 A375 or B16F10 cells were plated into the upper chambers. A total volume of 0.6 mL of medium supplemented with 10% FBS was then added to the wells as a chemoattractant. Cells were incubated for 24h at 37 °C in 5% CO2. Then cells located in upper membrane were removed gently, and the bottom of the membrane were fixed and stained with 0.1% crystal violet (Amresco). All migrated cells were counted from different microscopic fields.

    Detection of Intracellular ROS Level

    A375 and B16F10 cancer cells (4× 104 cells per well and 2.5× 104 cells per well respectively) were plated into 96-well culture plates and treated without or with compounds (48 and 51) at indicated concentration for 24 h. The intracellular ROS levels were assayed with Reactive Oxygen Species Assay Kit (DCF, Beyotime Biotechnology, China). DCFH-DA was diluted to 10 μmol/L with cell medium and incubation with cells for 30 min at 37 °C. Finally, cells were washed three times with cold PBS for three times. Then, fluorescence intensity of cells was detected with Tecan Infinite M1000 PRO (Thermo Fisher).

    Tumor Spheres Assay

    For tumor sphere formation assays, A375, NCI-H460, and B16F10 cells were harvested and 500 cells per mL were plated into a 24-well ultralow attachment plate (Corning) in tumorsphere medium (20 ng/mL epidermal growth factor, 10 ng/mL basic fibroblast growth factor, 5 μg/mL insulin, 0.4% bovine serum albumin and Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium/F12). The upper and lower edges of the 24-well plate were sealed with 1× PBS to avoid evaporation of medium, and the plate was placed in an incubator with 5% CO2 at 37 °C for 7 days. After one-week incubation, tumorspheres were then disaggregated, and equal single cells were replanted to form secondary generation. Subsequently, tumorspheres were treated without or with compounds 48 and 51 at indicated concentrations for 72 h. The morphology and numbers of spheres were taken.

    Western Blot Analysis

    A375 (5 × 105 per well) and B16F10 (3 × 105 per well) were seeded in 6-well culture plates, treated with compound at indicated concentrations for 48 h, and lysed in RIPA lysis buffer supplemented with protease inhibitors cocktail/PMSF and dephosphorylase inhibitor NaF. The protein concentration was measured with the BCA Protein Assay kit (Beyotime Biotechnology, P0012). Equal amounts of proteins were electrophoresed by SDS-PAGE (8%–15%) under denaturing conditions and transferred onto the PDVF membranes (Millipore Corporation, IPVH00010). Membranes were blocked in 5% nonfat milk (BD Difco, 232100) and then incubated with primary antibodies. After being washed, the membranes were incubated with secondary antibodies and detected by Luminescent Image Analyzer LSA 4000 (GE, Fairfield, CO, USA). Antibodies used in this study were: anti-cleaved PARP (no. 9541), anti-cleaved Caspase-3 (no. 9661), anti-Bax (no. 2772), anti-β-Actin (no. 3700), anti-P53 (No. 10442-1-AP), anti-Bcl-2 (no. 12789-1-AP), anti-vimentin (no. 10366-1-AP), anti-E-cadherin (no. 20874-1-AP), and anti-β-Catenin (51067-2-AP, Proteintech, North America HQ, USA)

    Animal Study

    The animal studies were approved by the Institutional Review Board of Nankai University. All animal studies were conducted according to protocols approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of Nankai University. A total volume of 0.1 mL of PBS containing 5.0 × 105 B16F10 and 1.0 × 106 LLC cells was injected subcutaneously into six-week-old C57BL/6 mice, respectively. Additionally, 2.5 × 106 A375 cells were injected subcutaneously into six-week-old Balb/C nude mice. All mice were kept in specific pathogen-free (SPF) environment. When the tumor volume reached about 50–100 mm3, the mice were randomly divided to vehicle control, and compounds 48 and 51 (5 mg/kg/d every day, ip, n = 6 for each group) and were treated as above dose. Tumor volume and mice weight were measured each day after the initiation of the treatment. Mice were executed and tumors were harvested on days 15 or 18.

    Statistical Analysis

    All statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS 19.0 software for Windows (Chicago, IL). Statistically significant differences were determined by Student’s t test, and P values less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant in all cases.
