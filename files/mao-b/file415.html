    Abstract

    The novel iridium(III) complex 1 was verified as a potent inhibitor of the TNF-α–TNFR protein–protein interaction in vitro and in cellulo. The iridium(III) center plays a critical role in organizing the structure of the bioactive metal complex, as the isolated ligands were found to be completely inactive. Both iridium enantiomers inhibited TNF-α-induced NF-κB activity and TNF-α–TNFR binding. 1 represents a promising scaffold for the further development of more potent organometallic TNF-α inhibitors.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Metal complexes have been studied to treat a wide range of human ailments, including cancers, infectious diseases, anti-inflammatory disorders, and neurological disorders.(1, 2) Additionally, group 9 complexes have recently attracted attention in biological, therapeutic, and environmental fields.(3-14) For example, cyclometalated iridium(III) and rhodium(III) organometallic compounds have been reported to bind to the minor groove of DNA through electrostatic interactions or with proteins via binding to the hydrophobic residues.(15) Meggers and co-workers have successfully pioneered the application of kinetically inert metal complexes as molecularly targeted inhibitors of enzyme activity with nanomolar or even picomolar potency.(16, 17) Our group has recently reported kinetically inert iridium(III) and rhodium(III) complexes as enzyme and protein–protein interaction inhibitors.(18, 19)
    The biologically active tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) complex consists of a trimer of identical submits, which can be recognized by the TNF-α receptor (TNFR).(20) Dysfunction in TNF-α activity can lead to the development of various diseases,(21, 22) such as rheumatoid arthritis, Crohn’s disease,(23) sarcoidosis,(24, 25) Behcet’s disease, noninfectious uveitis, and relapsing polychondritis.(26) Clinically approved TNF-α inhibitors include synthetic antibodies such as etanercept, infliximab, and adalimumab. However, disadvantages of using these synthetic antibodies include their ability to elicit an autoimmune response and the potential weakening of the immune defense to opportunistic infection. In 2005, He et al. reported 6,7-dimethyl-3-[[methyl[2-[methyl[[1-[3-(trifluoromethyl)phenyl]-1H-indol-3-yl]methyl]amino]ethyl]amino]methyl]-(4H-1-benzopyran-4-one dihydrochloride (SPD304)(27) as the first small molecule TNF-α inhibitor. 23 (SPD304) was able to bind TNF-α and accelerate subunit dissociation to inactivate the cytokine. Over the past 10 years, a number of small-molecule TNF-α inhibitors have been discovered using a variety of approaches.(28-31)
    In our ongoing interest in bioactive metal complexes, we have recently discovered an iridium(III) complex targeting the protein–protein interface of TNF-α.(32) In this work, we endeavored to develop superior TNF-α inhibitors by the synthesis and screening of 21 other iridium(III) complexes (1, 3–22) containing a variety of ligands (Figure 1) using the previous compound as a lead scaffold. The novel iridium(III) complex 1 [Ir(pbt)2(tm-phen)](PF6) (where pbt = phenylbenzo[d]thiazole and tm-phen = 3,4,7,8-tetramethyl-1,10-phenanthroline) was identified as a high-affinity inhibitor of the TNF-α–TNFR interaction. Notably, 1 showed higher potency against TNF-α activity compared to our previously reported iridium(III) complex 2 [Ir(ppy)2(biq)](PF6) (where ppy = 2-phenylpyridine and biq = 2,2′-biquinoline).(33)

    Synthesis and Characterization of Iridium(III)-Based Metal Complexes

    To identify a promising iridium(III)-based inhibitor of TNF-α, 22 kinetically inert organometallic iridium(III)-based metal complexes 1–22 (Figure 1) with the general structure [Ir(C∧N)2(N∧N)]PF6 (N trans to N) were designed and synthesized. Structurally, the N atoms of the C∧N ligands are presumed to be mutually trans to each other based on analogy with similar cyclometalated [Ir(C∧N)2(N∧N)]PF6 complexes for which X-ray crystal structures have been reported.(34) The complexes were prepared by the reaction of the corresponding C∧N ligands with IrCl3·3H2O to give the dimeric iridium intermediates [(C∧N)2Ir(μ-Cl)]2. The dimers were then reacted with the corresponding N∧N ligands and were isolated as the hexafluorophosphate salts by the addition of NH4PF6. The crude products were purified by column chromatography on silica to afford stable solids. All of the complexes were synthesized as racemates and characterized by 1H nuclear magnetic resonance, 13C NMR, high-resolution mass spectrometry, and elemental analysis.
    Iridium(III) complexes 1, 7–9 contain various C∧N and N∧N ligands and were based on the structure of our previously reported complex 2. Complexes 3 [Ir(2,9-dpphen)(ppy)2](PF6) (2,9-dpphen = 2,9-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline) and 4 [Ir(5,6-dmphen)(ppy)2](PF6) (5,6-dmphen = 5,6-dimethyl-1,10-phenanthroline)(35) bear the same ppy C∧N ligands as 2, while complex 5 [Ir(debpy)(dfppy)2](PF6) (debpy = 4,4′-dinonyl-2,2′-bipyridine, dfppy = 2-(2,4-difluorophenyl)pyridine) carries ppy ligands substituted with fluoro groups.(36) Complexes 6 [Ir(debpy)(ppz)2](PF6) (phpy = 1-phenyl-1H-pyrazole)(37) and 7 [Ir(tBrphen)(ppz)2](PF6) (tBrphen = 3,5,6,8-tetrabromo-1,10-phenanthroline) bear ppz C∧N ligands, while complex 8 [Ir(2,9-dmphen)(phpy)2](PF6) (2,9-dmphen = 2,9-dimethyl-1,10-phenanthroline, phpy = 1-phenyl-1H-pyrazole-4-carbaldehyde)(38) bears ppz ligands with an aldehyde substituent. Complexes 1 and 9 [Ir(piphen)(phisoq)2](PF6) (piphen = 2-phenyl-1H-imidazo[4,5-f][1,10]phenanthroline, phisoq = 1-phenylisoquinoline)(39) possess pbt and phisoq C∧N ligands, respectively. With regard to the N∧N ligand, complexes 5 and 6 carry the debpy moiety. Complexes 1, 4, and 8 bear a phen N∧N ligand substituted with two or four methyl groups at different positions, while complex 7 possesses a phen ligand substituted with four bromine groups. Moreover, complexes 3 and 9 carry larger N∧N ligands with additional fused aromatic rings. The iridium(III) complexes (10–22) were designed as structural analogues of complex 1. Eight complexes (10–17)(37, 40-42) contain the same pbt C∧N ligand as 1 but vary in the nature of the N∧N ligand, while five other complexes (18–22)(7, 19, 43, 44) carry the tm-phen N∧N ligand of 1 but possess different C∧N ligands.

    Inhibition of Cellular TNF-α-Induced Nuclear Factor κ Light-Chain-Enhancer of Activated B Cells (NF-κB) Luciferase Activity

    We first tested the effect of the complexes at inhibiting TNF-α-induced NF-κB activity in a human hepatocellular carcinoma cell line (HepG2) using the luciferase assay. HepG2 cells were stably transfected with the NF-κB-luciferase gene and were stimulated with TNF-α after preincubation with 10 μM racemic 1–22 or 23. The results showed that 1 exhibited the highest ability to inhibit TNF-α induced NF-κB luciferase activity, with 61.5% inhibition at 10 μM (Figure 2). In contrast, 23 showed 45.7% inhibitory activity at the same concentration. Significantly, 1 showed higher potency at inhibiting TNF-α-induced NF-κB activity compared to 2 that we reported previously. Moreover, 3–22 were found to be less active than the parent 1. To determine the inhibitory potency of 1, a dose–response experiment was performed. Complex 1 showed a half maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of 5.1 μM at inhibiting TNF-α-driven NF-κB luciferase activity, while 23 exhibited an IC50 of 13.8 μM under the same conditions (Table 1). Therefore, 1 was identified as a promising inhibitor of TNF-α for further study. In addition, the stability of 1 was further investigated by UV–vis and 1H NMR experiments (Figures S1 and S2 in Supporting Information), which showed that the complex was stable in 90% DMSO-d6/10% D2O or 80% acetonitrile/20% Tris-HCl buffer for at least 7 days.

    Inhibition of NF-κB Cell Signaling Pathway

    TNF-α-induced phosphorylation of nuclear factor of κ light polypeptide gene enhancer in B-cells inhibitor α (IκBα) at critical serine residues is an essential requirement for phosphorylated IκBα (pIκBα) degradation and NF-κB activation.(31) Therefore, we investigated the effect of 1 on IκBα serine phosphorylation in cells. Human cervical cancer cell line HeLa cells were treated with the indicated concentrations of 1 and 25 ng/mL TNF-α for 30 and 60 min. Protein lysates were subjected to Western blotting with anti-pSer32-IκBα or IκBα antibodies. In the absence of compounds, TNF-α induces the phosphorylation of IκBα and subsequent degradation of pIκBα, which could be observed after 30 min of TNF-α stimulation (Figure 3).
    Encouragingly, co-treatment of cells with 1 resulted in a dose-dependent decrease of IκBα phosphorylation at 30 min (Figure 3) and at 1 h (Figure S3). At 30 min, the inhibition of IκBα phosphorylation by 1 at 5 μM was comparable with that for the positive control 23 at the same concentration (Figure 3). These observations are also consistent with the result of luciferase assay described previously, which showed that 1 could inhibit TNF-α-induced NF-κB activity in transfected HepG2 cells. We presume that the decrease of IκBα phosphorylation is due, at least in part, to the inhibition of TNF-α activity by complex 1.

    Inhibition of TNF-α–TNFR Binding in Vitro

    To verify whether 1 was targeting TNF-α–TNFR binding in the cellular NF-κB signaling assay, an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) was performed. At 50 μM, 1 decreased TNF-α–TNFR binding by 63%, while 23 reduced binding by 72% under the same conditions (Figure S4A). Notably, the isolated pbt and tm-phen ligands showed negligible TNF-α–TNFR inhibitory activity at 50 μM, indicating that the assembly of the ligands into an intact complex is essential for biological activity. These data suggest that the observed in cellulo effects exhibited by 1 could be attributed, at least in part, to its inhibition of the TNF-α–TNFR protein–protein interaction.
    In previous experiments, the cyclometalated iridium(III) complexes were tested as racemic mixtures of the two enantiomers Λ and Δ. To investigate the effect of stereochemistry on biological potency, we synthesized enantiopure Λ-1 and Δ-1 according to a method reported by Meggers’ group (Scheme S1),(45) and characterized these by circular dichroism spectroscopy (Figures S5 and S6). A comparison of the two enantiomers of 1 showed that Λ-1 (IC50 ≈ 30 μM) was more potent against the TNF-α–TNFR binding interaction than Δ-1 (IC50 ≈ 57 μM) (Figure 4A). The in vitro data were further corroborated with an AlphaScreen experiment, which is a bead-based, amplified luminescent proximity homogeneous assay that can measure the distance between tagged proteins. Thus, biotinylated TNF-α and His-tagged TNFR were incubated with varying concentrations of Λ-1 or Δ-1 for 1 h. Streptavidin donor beads and nickel chelate acceptors were added, and the effect of the compounds on the TNF-α–TNFR interaction was evaluated by measuring the energy transfer between the two beads. As shown in Figure 4B, both enantiomers dose-dependently inhibited the TNF-α–TNFR interaction, with Λ-1 again being more potent than Δ-1. In addition, a competitive ELISA assay was performed to understand the nature of inhibition of TNF-α and TNFR binding by two enantiomers (Figure 4c). The double-reciprocal plot showed that both enantiomers were the competitive inhibitors of TNF-α and TNFR binding with Ki values of 7.4 and 6.3 μM for Λ-1 and Δ-1, respectively.

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Protein–protein interactions are known to be relatively large and featureless in nature, lacking specific contact points that can be exploited for small molecular recognition. Indeed, 23(27) and natural product-like inhibitors(32) of TNF-α have been reported to interact with the binding surface of TNF-α primarily through hydrophobic interactions. Thus, we envisioned in this study that by incorporating aromatic C∧N and N∧N moieties bearing substituents with various shapes and sizes, we could effectively explore the region of chemical space that is required to inhibit TNF-α activity. On the basis of lead iridium(III) structure 2, a total of 21 other iridium(III) complexes (1, 3–22) containing a variety of C∧N and N∧N ligands were designed and tested for their potency against TNF-α activity.
    On the basis of a cellular TNF-α luciferase assay, the iridium(III) complex 1 [Ir(pbt)2(biq)](PF6) emerged as the top candidate, with 61.5% inhibition at 10 μM. Notably, 1 was more potent compared to the positive control compound 23 and the lead structure 2. On the basis of consideration of the entire data set, structure–activity relationships regarding the TNF-α inhibitory activity of 1–22 can be deduced. Among 1–9, only the bioactive 1 contains the pbt ligand, suggesting that this C∧N ligand is desirable for TNF-α inhibitory activity. Moreover, 1 showed higher TNF-α inhibitory activity than 4 and 8, which bear only two methyl groups on their phen scaffold compared to four methyl groups for 1, suggesting that the number and arrangement of methyl groups on this N∧N ligand could be important for biological activity. When comparing 1 with 10–14, which all contain the pbt ligand but vary in the nature of the N∧N ligand, it can be seen that tm-phen ligand of 1 plays a critical role in biological potency. Interestingly, 11, carrying the 5,5′-dimethyl-2,2′-bipyridine, showed moderate TNF-α inhibitory activity (27.3% at 10 μM), which could be possibly due to the fact that its methyl groups are located in the same relative position as two of the methyl substituents of the tm-phen ligand of 1. Excessive bulkiness seems to detract from biological activity, as 12 and 14, which bear tert-butyl and phenyl groups on their N∧N ligands, respectively, showed significantly reduced activity. A similar comparison of 1 with 15–22, which differ only in their C∧N ligands, reinforces the notion that the pbt ligand of 1 is most desirable for biological activity. Complex 15, the second-most potent compound in this group (21.4% at 10 μM), contains the ppy ligand that is also found in the previously reported complex 2, indicating that this C∧N ligand imparts some degree of TNF-α inhibitory activity, though its combination with the tm-phen N∧N coligand resulted in lower activity for complex 15 compared to 1 and 2.
    The cellular activity of 1 was further confirmed in a NF-κB signaling activity assay, where it was found to inhibit IκBα phosphorylation with comparable potency to positive control compound 23 at the same concentration. We also performed a direct TNF-α–TNFR binding in vitro assay to confirm that 1 could disrupt the interaction between TNF-α and its cognate receptor.
    To gain additional mechanistic understanding into the activity of 1, we further synthesized enantiopure Λ-1 and Δ-1 and discovered that while both complexes could inhibit TNF-α–TNFR binding, Λ-1 (IC50 ≈ 30 μM) was more potent than Δ-1 (IC50 ≈ 57 μM). This result was corroborated by an AlphaScreen assay measuring the strength of the TNF-α–TNFR interaction in the presence of compounds. Finally, ELISA data were used to generate a double reciprocal plot that suggested that both enantiomers were competitive inhibitors of TNF-α and TNFR binding. These results are in contrast to our previous study,(43) where we found that Λ and Δ forms of 2 showed similar potency.(32) The difference in potency between the two enantiomers in the present study may indicate that Λ-1 is able to exploit a tighter binding interaction within the binding pocket of TNF-α compared to Δ-1.
    In summary, we have reported a novel iridium(III) complex 1 as a direct inhibitor of TNF-α. In a cell-based luciferase assay, 1 showed superior ability to inhibit TNF-α induced NF-κB activity in cellulo compared to the positive control compound 23 and the previously reported iridium(III) complex 2. Complex 1 inhibited the TNF-α–TNFR protein–protein interaction as revealed by an ELISA test. Moreover, 1 inhibited IκBα phosphorylation in a dose-dependent fashion in cells. Structure–activity relationship analysis indicated that the presence of the pbt C∧N ligand and the arrangement of methyl groups on the tm-phen N∧N ligand were important for biological potency. Moreover, the iridium(III) center plays a critical role in organizing the structure of the bioactive metal complex, as the isolated ligands were found to be completely inactive. The discovery of 1 could further the development of more potent organometallic complexes as protein–protein interaction inhibitors.

    Preparation of the Dimer Complexes [(C∧N)2Ir(μ-Cl)]2

    A mixture of iridium(III) chloride (150 mg, 0.43 mmol) and corresponding C∧N ligands (0.95 mmol, 2.2 equiv) in methoxyethanol/deionized H2O (3:1, 12 mL) was allowed to heat to reflux overnight under nitrogen protection. The mixture was cooled to ambient temperature and the resultant solid was collected by filtration, washed with deionized H2O (2 × 50 mL) and diethyl ether (2 × 30 mL), and dried to afford the corresponding dimer complexes.

    Preparation of the Final Complexes

    A suspension of [(C∧N)2Ir(μ-Cl)]2 (0.2 mmol) and corresponding N∧N ligands (0.24 mmol, 2.2 equiv) in DCM/MeOH (1:1, 16 mL) was heated to reflux for 6 h. The resulting solution was cooled to ambient temperature and filtered to remove unreacted cyclometalated dimer. Afterward an aqueous solution of ammonium hexafluorophosphate was added to the filtrate, and the solvent was removed in vacuum until the solid occurred. The precipiate was collected and washed with deionized H2O (2 × 50 mL) followed by diethyl ether (2 × 30 mL). The mixture was purified by silica gel column dichloromethane as the eluent to afford the titled complex. The purity of all complexes was validated by combustion using automatic analyzers (Atlantic Microlab Inc., Norcross, GA) to confirm the purity is ≥95%. The presence of PF6 ion in all complexes was demonstrated by electrospray ionization mass spectrometry that the peak correspond to complex + PF6 + H was observed (Figure S7).

    Preparation of Enantiomer of Complex 1

The solvent was removed under reduced pressure, and the residue was purified by silica gel column chromatography. The diastereomers could be separated by using hexane/ethyl acetate (4:1 to 3:1) as eluent. Two orange fractions, each containing a single diastereomer, were concentrated to dryness to yield Δ-(S)-S3 (20.5 mg, 25%) and Δ-(S)-S3 (19.2 mg, 23%) with a dr of at least 100:1 for both diastereomers.

        Asymmetric Catalysis with an Inert Chiral-at-Metal Iridium Complex
        Chen, Liang-An; Xu, Weici; Huang, Biao; Ma, Jiajia; Wang, Lun; Xi, Jianwei; Harms, Klaus; Gong, Lei; Meggers, Eric

        The development of a chiral-at-metal iridium(III) complex for the highly efficient catalytic asym. transfer hydrogenation of β,β'-disubstituted nitroalkenes is reported. Catalysis by this inert, rigid metal complex does not involve any direct metal coordination but operates exclusively through weak interactions with functional groups properly arranged in the ligand sphere of the iridium complex. Although the iridium complex relies only on the formation of three hydrogen bonds, it exceeds the performance of most organocatalysts with respect to enantiomeric excess (up to 99% ee) and catalyst loading (down to 0.1 mol %). This work hints at an advantage of structurally complicated rigid scaffolds for non-covalent catalysis, which esp. relies on conformationally constrained cooperative interactions between the catalyst and substrates.
