    Abstract

    To better understand the difficulties surrounding the identification of novel antibacterial compounds from corporate screening collections, physical properties of ∼3200 antibacterial project compounds with whole cell activity against Gram-negative or Gram-positive pathogens were profiled and compared to actives found from high throughput (HTS) screens conducted on both biochemical and phenotypic bacterial targets. The output from 23 antibacterial HTS screens illustrated that when compared to the properties of the antibacterial project compounds, the HTS actives were significantly more hydrophobic than antibacterial project compounds (typically 2–4 log units higher), and furthermore, for 14/23 HTS screens, the average clogD was higher than the screening collection average (screening collection clogD = 2.45). It was found that the consequences of this were the following: (a) lead identification programs often further gained hydrophobic character with increased biochemical potency, making the separation even larger between the physicochemical properties of known antibacterial agents and the HTS active starting point, (b) the probability of plasma protein binding and cytotoxicity are often increased, and (c) cell-based activity in Gram-negative bacteria was severely limited or, if present, demonstrated significant efflux. Our analysis illustrated that compounds least susceptible to efflux were those which were highly polar and small in MW or very large and typically zwitterionic. Hydrophobicity was often the dominant driver for HTS actives but, more often than not, precluded whole cell antibacterial activity. However, simply designing polar compounds was not sufficient for antibacterial activity and pointed to a lack of understanding of complex and specific bacterial penetration mechanisms.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The continual evolution of multidrug-resistant bacteria represents a significant health threat, with many of the last-line treatment options losing their effectiveness. As an example, a recent 2014 World Health Organization report described the emergence of “alarming rates of carbapenem resistance exceeding 50%...reported in K. pneumonia in some patient groups, for which few if any alternative treatment options are available”.(1) Despite this rising concern, investments in pharmaceutical antibacterial research over the past decade have declined, summarized in a review by Butler et al.(2) In this review, the authors have described only 22 new antibiotics which were launched worldwide between 2000 and 2013. Of these, only five are considered first-in-class: linezolid 1, retapamulin 2, fidaxomicin 3, daptomycin 4, and bedaquiline 5 (Figure 1). However, none of these first-in-class treatments are indicated for serious multidrug-resistant Gram-negative infections. The drugs launched between 2000 and 2013 to treat Gram-negative infections (n = 16) come from four legacy classes of antibiotics (β-lactams, fluoroquinolones, macrolides, and tetracyclines). For drugs targeting Gram-negative pathogens, there has not been a compound approved with a novel mechanism in nearly half a century. For this ever-increasing medical need, polymyxins remains the last line of defense for life-threatening infections, a molecule which is over 40 years old and has a narrow safety margin to toxicity (Figure 1, colistin 6 or polymyxin E).(3) The need for new Gram-negative agents has never been higher, especially with emerging polymyxin resistance.
    The physical properties which define antibacterial drugs are very different from those of oral drugs used to treat noninfectious human diseases. O’Shea and Moser have compared marketed antibacterial agents with typical oral drugs and concluded that the physical properties of antibacterial drugs are biased toward more polar chemistry space.(4) The average calculated log D (clogD) of marketed Gram-negative agents is −2.8, and the average polar surface area (PSA) is 165 Å2, compared to marketed Gram-positive agents which have an average clogD of −0.2 and PSA of 243 Å2. This compares to compounds from the Comprehensive Medicinal Chemistry (CMC) database, representative of compounds active in other therapeutic areas, which have an average clogD of 1.6 and PSA of 70 Å2. The polarity shift for antibacterial drugs and especially Gram-negative drugs may be explained by two potential reasons. The first is that most severely ill patients will require intravenous treatment under physician care in the hospital, which requires compounds optimized for high solubility. Second, the polar nature of the outer membranes of Gram-negative pathogens results in poor passive permeability of hydrophobic molecules.(5) To further exclude molecules which do penetrate the outer membrane, Gram-negative bacteria have multiple promiscuous efflux pumps capable of removing many types of foreign molecules.(6)
    The identification of new antibacterial leads has been discussed in previous publications, in particular the difficulties in high throughput screening (HTS) screening. Payne et al. at GSK reported on a lack of success of HTS in finding new leads for antibacterial targets.(7) From a chemistry point of view, they attribute this to the lack of chemical diversity within corporate screening collections and the significant chemistry required to convert biochemical actives into cell-penetrating actives. They suggest that greater molecular diversity as well as a better understanding of the physical properties of antibacterial agents will greatly assist the discovery of new therapies. In a follow-up article, they also recommend potential strategies such as prefractionated natural product libraries, DNA-encoded libraries (DEL), and fragment-based lead generation (FBLG) approaches as potentially useful areas for expansion in antibacterial research.(8)
    Our analysis of physical properties would suggest that many biochemical and phenotypic screening strategies are biased toward lipophilic compounds. This bias leads to a significant gap between lead structure and whole cell activity in relevant pathogens, which is largely attributed to the polar nature of the Gram-negative outer membrane, promiscuous efflux pumps, and small substrate specific porins in pathogens such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa.(9) Here we report our analysis on antibacterial project compounds designed across multiple projects and target classes and their activity across a diverse panel of Gram-positive and Gram-negative pathogens and how that relates to finding new actives within this chemical space.

    Analysis of Antibacterial Property Space from Whole Cell Actives

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A set of ∼3200 antibacterial project compounds from ongoing projects against Gram-positive and Gram-negative pathogens from the AstraZeneca collection was analyzed and formed the basis of the physical property distribution of “antibacterial chemical space” for the purposes of this investigation. We defined antibacterial chemical space as those properties such as log D, MW, and charge which were most typically associated with compounds having a defined minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) against Gram-positive or Gram-negative pathogens. The set of compounds was composed of diverse target classes such as inhibitors of LpxC, penicillin binding protein, DNA gyrase, and the ribosome as examples. These examples were chosen, as they represent the dominant targets of antibacterial projects in our collection which have shown the ability to lead to compounds with whole cell activity against either Gram-positive or Gram-negative pathogens (or both). The graph in Figure 2 illustrates eight of the pathogens studied, along with two pump mutants and two in the presence of 2% human serum albumin (HSA). The number of actives identified in this antibacterial panel screening is further binned into two categories, which are those with a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) of ≤1 μg/mL and those with a MIC of ≤8 μg/mL. These binning criteria were chosen to both provide context to the activity distribution across species as well as to understand if there were any differences between the more potent compounds (≤1 μg/mL) and less potent examples (≤8 μg/mL) with respect to physical property distribution. Upon further analysis, the physical property distributions were similar to both bins, and subsequently the MIC cutoff of ≤8 μg/mL was chosen for all physical property correlations to provide the largest meaningful sample size possible. This should not be misunderstood as a hit rate from random screening because these are compounds designed specifically for existing antibacterial drug programs. From the analysis, it can be seen that the most frequent occurrences of activity are found at the efflux pump-deficient E. coli mutant (tolC–), with greater than 30% of antibacterial project compounds showing activity at ≤8 μg/mL. Wild-type Haemophilus influzenzae was the next most frequent, followed by the E. coli (tolC–) mutant in the presence of 2% HSA. On the opposite end of the scale, A. baumannii and S. aureus in the presence of 2% HSA demonstrated the least number of actives from this MIC testing.
    The active compounds from Figure 2 were then analyzed for various physical property distributions to observe any trends and exceptions which may help characterize antibacterial property space. The first analysis was to understand the role of ionic charge (e.g., acidic, basic, neutral, or zwitterionic) versus whole cell activity across the various pathogens. Each active compound (≤8 μg/mL) was binned into one of four charge classes consisting of basic, acidic, neutral, and zwitterionic charge types (Figure 3). Included in this analysis was also the typical distribution of charge classes found in the AstraZeneca collection from a random sample of compounds (45K). One interesting observation here is the large difference in the proportion of neutral compounds from the typical AZ collection compared to those present in Gram-positive versus Gram-negative actives in Figure 3. For example, neutral actives are more common for S. aureus and S. pneumoniae than for K. pneumoniae and A. baummanii, but for all species they are much reduced compared to the frequency of the corporate screening collection. Another interesting observation with this analysis was the number of acidic molecules active against the notoriously difficult Gram-negative pathogens such as P. aeruginosa, K. pneumoniae and A. baumanni. A closer examination of these structures indicated that they were primarily from the β-lactam class of antibiotics.
    Another analysis was performed to understand the hydrophobic character of these antibacterial active compounds across the various pathogens in Figure 2. The calculated log D (clogD pH 7.4) was determined for all actives within each pathogen bin and the average plotted along with the 99% confidence interval (± vertical bars) of the mean (Figure 4). Also included in this analysis was the clogD average of random AZ collection screening compounds (n = 45 000) as well as the average clogD for compounds exhibiting cytotoxicity in human A549 cell lines at similar concentration to the MIC actives (≤8 μg/mL). The AZ collection average is included to compare the properties of general HTS screening compounds from the AZ collection alongside those properties found among Gram-negative and Gram-positive active compounds. The pathogens are rank-ordered in Figure 4 by average clogD and separated by Gram-positive versus Gram-negative classification. It can be seen in Figure 4 that most of the Gram-positive species have average clogD values >0 for whole cell actives, whereas most of the Gram-negative species have clogD values <0 for whole cell actives. The two exceptions to this trend are E. coli (tolC–) and H. influenzae which have slightly positive clogD averages. It should also be noted that these two species were also the most frequent to have whole cell actives in Figure 2.
    One concern which may arise from this analysis is the relevance and predictability of clogD compared to measured log D. Out of the 3200 compounds considered in this analysis, we were able to compare measured log D vs clogD for 1251 compounds as illustrated in Figure 5. The relationship is generally correlative, with an R2 = 0.836. Most of outliers are indicated in the graph and were a subset of aliphatic polyamines which could not be measured below of value of −1 due to technical issues with the compounds. These molecules presented issues of apparent “compound stickiness” with possible adherence to plastic vials and other apparatus, which may explain the lack of correlation to the measured values. However, when calculated, the values fell between the range from −1 to −8. Even with these outliers, given the generally good correlation, we concluded that clogD is a useful as predictable substitute for measured logD on these diverse antibacterial compounds.

    Analysis of HTS Actives and Hits

    Jump To
    With this baseline in place, we next analyzed the physical properties associated with representative actives from biochemical and phenotypic HTS screens as summarized in Table 1. The primary screen actives (typically at 10 μM) were chosen for this analysis, as they represent the first step of HTS triage and were the compounds selected for IC50 determination. The point at which to evaluate and compare HTS screening output with this type of analysis can be a controversial decision because the definition of active, hit, and lead may vary across companies and even within a company by individual project teams. Furthermore, each assay may have different hit rates and different susceptibility to false positives and assay artifacts. The hit triage on the actives listed in Table 1 was multifaceted and eliminated many active compounds based on criteria such as assay interference, lack of robust dose–response, lack of broad-based isozyme activity, activity in mammalian cell lines, inferior physical properties, etc., and is discussed later in the text. We felt that the most objective place to analyze compounds for physical property space was at the HTS active stage, even if it also included assay artifacts and compounds that did not reproduce in an orthogonal assay. The analysis at this stage represents the first and most severe triage of compounds in the process (e.g., ∼600–800K screening compounds resulting in typically 1–30K actives). To understand if analysis of primary actives provided too much emphasis on screening artifacts, we examined how the physical properties changed on going from active to hit to lead based on our internal criteria of such categories. An “active” was defined as a compound which demonstrated activity above the noise threshold in single concentration (run in triplicate) of the HTS. A “hit” was defined as a compound which demonstrated a dose–response curve with an IC50 but also passed multiple and sometimes project specific criteria to rule out artifacts and nonspecific inhibitors (e.g., nephelometry measurements > IC50, Hill slope >0.5 and <2, activity against other bacterial isozymes, chemical property filters; with FBLG projects X-ray structure or NMR confirmation, etc.). Of the examples we studied, the properties did not change much in going from active to hit (average Δlog D = −0.26 from active to hit, discussed in more detail below, Table 2).
    With that context, the first section of Table 1 illustrates 15 representative biochemical screens, which is further broken down to 14 full HTS campaigns (∼600–800K screened) and one fragment-based lead generation program (FBLG) (TrmD, ∼14K fragments screened). Of those screens, eight demonstrated lower average clogD than the random sample of the AZ collection. TrmD had the lowest (avg = 1.27), but this was biased because the screening set consisted of FBLG library containing a generally lower average clogD relative the generic HTS collection. When comparing “actives” for TrmD versus the profile of the FBLG set, the screen actually demonstrated a higher average clogD when compared to the screening set used (e.g., average FBLG set = 0.93 clogD compared to 1.27 for the average clogD of TrmD actives). When compared to the average for P. aeruginosa wild-type actives (average = −0.47, last entry in Table 1), even the low clogD average for the FBLG screen was still ∼0.8 log units higher. Of the biochemical screens listed in Table 1, none of these programs advanced into lead optimization.
    Phenotypic screens are listed in the bottom panel of Table 1. Growth inhibition of a P. aeruginosa efflux mutant (MexABCDXY–) demonstrated the lowest average clogD for actives out of any of the phenotypic screens listed in Table 1 (clogD avg = 1.73). However, the only actives identified in this screen were either known antibacterial compounds or suspected general cytotoxins based on similar potency of these compounds in human A549 cell line. An E. coli cell wall mutant (waaP–) was also screened and identified compounds with an average clogD lower than the typical screening collection. As with the P. aeruginosa screen, many of the actives were known antibacterial compounds which were in the AZ collection. However, a novel series (Figure 6, compound 8) was identified from this effort and found to be a LolCDE pathway inhibitor (localization of lipoproteins).(11) However, the clogD of this inhibitor series was on the high end of the spectrum and did not demonstrate potent wild-type activity in relevant Gram-negative pathogens (e.g., E. coli, P. aeruginosa, A. baumannii). These compounds also suffered from high plasma protein binding and serum-shifted MIC’s which is likely due to the high hydrophobicity (Figure 6). The phenotypic screens of SOS reporter gene assay (SOS), H. influenzae (−acrB), S. pneumoniae (AntiB,G-pos) and a coupled transcription translation assay (TT) did not reveal any novel chemical starting points with MIC’s in wild-type pathogens. The AntiB (G-pos) and cell-wall reporter (CWR) assays demonstrated the highest average clogDs of any screen (biochemical or phenotypic). However, from the CWR screen a novel chemical series was discovered, again another LolCDE inhibitor (Figure 6, compound 7).(10)

    We also examined the process by which the actives from the HTS output were further triaged into hits. The process of active-to-hit involves a significant cascade to narrow down the list, but it is also more subjective than the initial HTS active generation. For example, the decision to define a compound as an active resides only on criteria set by assay performance and background noise. The definition of a hit involved not only a cascade of other on-target (e.g., orthogonal assay background) and off-target assays (e.g., ruling out assay artifacts) but also involves chemists’ decisions based on drug-like filters or previous experience. This decision-making process could be different for any given assay based on known issues (e.g., ruling out metal chelators) or past experiences (e.g., “I don’t like those structures”).(12) The results of this active-to-hit analysis are listed in Table 2 based on the active output from screens listed in Table 1. One observation of this data is the steep and significant attrition in going from active to hit. When these projects were examined, there was no one single reason for this attrition in any given assay, or across assays. Typical reasons that led to the steep attrition were the following: lack of dose–response, suspected interference with assay read-out, lack of activity in orthogonal assay, lack of activity in other bacterial isozymes, and presence of activity in mammalian cell line. Some projects also required that bacterial activity was also present, even if only determined in outer membrane or efflux mutant strains. With such a high attrition rate, a legitimate question was raised as to whether the physical property analysis of the active output would provide meaningful insight into the future of the project. In other words, if assay artifacts are giving rise to the high lipophilicities of the actives of some HTS screens, and furthermore, if chemists are removing real actives which have poor physical properties, would the output of the hits be much more in-line with antibacterial property space? This is a more difficult analysis to interpret, because the number of hits for each screen was relatively low as compared to the number of actives. Table 2 illustrates that for most screens, the average clogD changed very little during this triage process. The average change for all screens listed in Table 2 was −0.26 log units. The program with the highest drop in clogD was TilS (ΔclogD = −1.02), and on the other end of the spectrum, CoaD (ΔclogD = 0.42) had the highest increase. We concluded that even with a significant triage cascade and chemistry input on selection of compounds moving from active to hit, the overall hydrophobicities of hits are not that much different from those of the actives. Thus, the analysis of the physical properties on actives is an important first glimpse on how far apart the properties are likely to be from typical antibacterial property space.

    For six screens in Table 1, the hit rates were further analyzed to probe whether the hit rate observed varied by clogD bins. Six screens were chosen to represent the types of distributions seen in this type of analysis, as follows: biochemical screens with differing distributions of clogD (Figure 7b and 7c), phenotypic screens with very similar profiles of hydrophobic distributions (Figure 7d and 7e), and phenotypic screens which have a hydrophilic distribution (Figure 7f and 7g). Actives were placed into clogD bins from <0 to >4, and then a hit rate was calculated for each bin by dividing the number of actives in each clogD bin by the number of compounds which were screened corresponding to bin. A normalized line is indicated (point “A” on each graph) and represents the expected distribution if there was no bias of hydrophobic molecules in screening output. These graphs can also be compared to Figure 7a, where the AZ screening collection distribution of clogD is illustrated. A few interesting observations can be distilled from these graphs. Figure 7b and 7c is a representative example from the biochemical HTS screens, MurC (UDP-N-acetylmuramic acid: l-alanine ligase) and MAP (methionine aminopeptidase). Figure 7b deviates from the expected distribution because fewer hydrophilic compounds were identified in HTS screening than what was expected based on the collection distribution (point “B” on the graph). This could be due to a strong hydrophobic interactions in the binding site which carry a substantial portion of the overall ligand binding and hence the biased distribution toward hydrophobic molecules. In contrast, the hits for methionine aminopeptidase (MAP) program in Figure 7c contained many more hydrophilic compounds, but the graph is U-shaped and has an increased number of hydrophobic compounds >4 when compared to the normalized line. An analysis of this data revealed that many of the compounds with clogD <0 are likely metal chelating moieties (point “C”), consistent with this target being a metallopeptidase. No distinction was made at this stage as to whether these were indeed metal chelators binding to the enzyme itself or simply indirectly inhibiting the enzyme by titrating away the metal. Nonetheless, many of these were present in the output and exhibited a more polar compound distribution than most typical screens we examined with this analysis. In Figure 7d and 7e, two phenotypic screens are shown which illustrate a very large bias toward hydrophobic compounds and away from hydrophilic compounds (point “B” on graphs). In Figure 7d, the cell-wall reporter assay (CWR) in E. coli (tolC–) was designed to identify any compounds which interfered with cell-wall biosynthesis, but it resulted in a high proportion of hydrophobic molecules. Unfortunately, after triage and testing in whole cell pathogens, only a few molecules exhibited weak wild-type activity in E. coli, but none had broad-spectrum activity across other pathogens (as indicated previously, one novel series did emerge from this effort and is exemplified by 7 in Figure 6).(10) Figure 7e depicts a screen against a pump-deficient form of H. influenzae (acrB–) which also illustrated a similar distribution of many hydrophobic actives (clogD 3–4) and few hydrophilic actives (clogD <0). Interestingly, most screens with this pattern of increased incidence of highly hydrophobic actives show a drop-off above clogD of 4. This is possibly due to physical property limitations (e.g., solubility) which then work against the pronounced hydrophobic energetic drive of some of these targets. Similar to the cell-wall assay outcome, no actives were identified in the H. infleunzae (acrB–) screen which exhibited wild-type activity (except for previously known antibacterial project compounds). Figure 7f and 7g illustrates entirely different distributions. Figure 7f is a waaP-deficient strain of E. coli. This strain is lacking a polar component of the lipopolysaccharide outer membrane and was assumed to provide greater permeability which in turn would help identify compounds which might have been missed in other wild type whole cell screens. The distribution is skewed to the polar set of compounds from the screening collection (point “D” on graphs). However, after inspection, most of the active compounds fell into two categories: known antibacterial project compounds, or general cytotoxins based on activity in A549 mammalian cell line. As mentioned previously, a novel series was identified as exemplified by 8 in Figure 6.(11) Although 7 and 8 served to identify novel chemical equity operating via a unique mechanism, the high hydrophobicity appeared to severally restrict potent Gram-negative activity in important wild type pathogens such as E. coli and P. aeruginosa. The final project (P. aeruginosa efflux mutant (MexABCDXY–), Figure 7g illustrated a similar trend to the E. coli (waaP–) screen, as only known antibacterial compounds and general cytotoxins were identified.
    Taken together, these examples provide useful insight into screening for novel antibacterial starting points. Many HTS targets (both biochemical and phenotypic) are biased toward hydrophobic molecules. In some cases the bias is quite large, resulting in significant selection of compounds from the screening collection at the higher end of clogD (e.g., CWR assay). The real complicating factor in this phenomena of hydrophobic bias is observed when comparing screens such as CWR (Figure 7d) or H. influenzae (acrB–) (Figure 7e) against the collection distribution (Figure 7a). This comparison illustrates that a low hit rate is observed at the lower clogD bins for many screens, and this is further exacerbated by the fact that these compounds represent a smaller component of the AZ corporate archive. The opposite is true for the hydrophobic compounds (high hit rate, highly represented in the archive). The net result can be a significant oversampling of hydrophobic compounds in the HTS active output.

    Target Class Analysis of Antibacterial Actives

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The analysis of the phenotypic and biochemical screens indicated that many of the HTS actives exhibited higher hydrophobic character than is represented in antibacterial chemical space, as well as the screening collection. It was important then to understand the known classes of antibacterials which made up the analysis in Figures 1 and 2 because these targets appear to be amenable from a chemistry point of view to providing combined biochemical activity and bacterial permeability. The class distribution of the antibacterial actives in P. aeruginosa (n = 344) is illustrated in Figure 8. As stated previously, these compounds were designed toward existing antibacterial programs and had MIC values ≤8 μg/mL against wild-type P. aeruginosa. In Figure 8a, the distribution against the target class is shown for all actives against P. aeruginosa < 8 μg/mL, and in Figure 8b the distribution is limited to those compounds with clogD values >1. The majority of these actives in Figure 8a (clogD <1) belong to the β-lactam class, which was further subdivided into siderophore-containing and nonsiderophore-containing molecules. The siderophore is thought to be mediated by an active uptake mechanism in which the bacteria sequester iron from the host for its own survival. Although this mechanism presents a viable opportunity for a Trojan-horse penetration strategy, it was recently disclosed that this mechanism of uptake was found to have significant adaptation-based resistance in vivo.(13) It should be noted that the β-lactams (siderophore or nonsiderophore) need to only penetrate the outer membrane because the penicillin-binding proteins and β-lactamases reside in the periplasmic layer in between the outer membrane and inner membrane of Gram-negative bacteria. The covalent mechanism of β-lactams as well as their periplasmic location is thought to be a strong rationale as to why this class demonstrates excellent whole cell activity and continues to be a strong area of interest for Gram-negative drug discovery research. Although the covalent nature would not be expected to influence the permeability, this may affect accumulation, as the compounds become covalently attached to targets inside the periplasm and may take longer to dissociate than noncovalent compounds.
    The second largest class in Figure 8a are the polymyxins. These are highly polar compounds which bind to the charged lipopolysaccharide units on the cell surface of the bacteria.(14) Hence, with these molecules, bacterial cell penetration as a first step of their mechanism is presumed not to be required. When the distribution of the target class is shown in Figure 8b where the actives are restricted to compounds with clogD >1 (>1.75 clogD units above the average, Figure 4), one large class of compounds emerges which proves to be an exception to the trend of primarily hydrophilic compounds such as P. aeruginosa wild type actives. This class belongs to inhibitors of UDP-(3-O-(R-hydroxymyristoyl))-N-acetylglucosamine deacetylase (LpxC), the first committed step in lipid A biosynthesis. An exemplar compound is shown by the hydroxamic acid 9 in Figure 9.(15) This compound has excellent wild-type P. aeruginosa activity but has a clogD of 3.1 and measured log D of 2.3, which is >3 log units above the typical log D of P. aeruginosa actives. The compound does have an efflux issue (at least 4-fold efflux ratio from wild-type to efflux mutant), although the true value is perhaps masked somewhat due to the exquisite potency of the compound at wild-type (0.25 μg/mL) and the efflux mutant (<0.063 μg/mL) species. The exact activity in E. coli is also masked given that it was at the lower limits of detection for the assay at <0.063 μg/mL for both strains. It is not clear why this class of molecules deviates from the trend that few hydrophobic molecules are found with potent P. aeruginosa and E. coli activity. Perhaps there is an unknown mode of entry which leads to high levels of drug concentration, or perhaps the target itself leads to a weakening of the cell wall, allowing for better penetration. The mechanism by which these molecules exhibit potent wild-type activity when compared to most other compounds with high hydrophobicity remains poorly understood.
    A second class of molecules which tends to lie outside of the trend for wild-type P. aeruginosa are the LolA/MreB dual inhibitor 10 illustrated in Figure 9.(16) This class of molecules is speculated to work through two different pathways, one of which is inhibition of MreB, a bacterial actin-like protein. The other pathway is by inhibition of LolA, another component of the localization of protein pathway mentioned in Figure 6. These compounds lie above the typical clogD average for P. aeruginosa wild-type active compounds but have good wild-type activity (MIC = 4 μg/mL).
    When actives and inactives were compared in the same series, it became clear that the difference in clogD of these was not significantly large. Two extreme cases are highlighted in Figure 10 which illustrate this observation. In Figure 10a, LpxC actives have a generally higher clogD than inactives, although the values are quite close to one another. LpxC is a cytoplasmic target,(17) and antibacterial activity is anticipated to require both permeation of the polar outer membrane as well as the inner more hydrophobic cytoplasmic membrane. It may be possible that some hydrophobicity is important for this process. Of the target classes we examined (∼10), this was the only example where the average clogD for actives was higher than that of the inactives. In contrast, the β-lactams in Figure 10b illustrate an opposite trend. For all species, the average clogD of the inactives are less polar than those of the actives by 0.5–2.0 log units. However, these inactives are still quite polar and serve to reinforce the theme that polar compounds may provide better starting points for novel antibacterial agents, but polarity by itself is not sufficient for antibacterial activity.

    Effect of Hydrophobicity on Plasma Protein Binding and Plasma-Shifted MIC’s

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Another analysis involves MIC shifts in the presence of plasma protein as it affected antibacterial compounds. This is an important property to monitor, because not only the MIC is affected by addition of protein; efficacy and PK/PD models often rely on the unbound or free fraction of the drug. The exact free fraction can be estimated by measuring the amount bound to plasma protein in an equilibrium dialysis experiment, for example, and in vitro effect of bound compound can also be qualitatively assessed by measuring the MIC in the absence or presence of HSA. Compounds with high hydrophobic character were often found to suffer from high plasma protein binding and subsequent shifting of the MIC’s. In some cases the shift is high enough that the effect of adding 2% HSA rendered the compound inactive against the pathogen of interest. The role of hydrophobicity and plasma protein binding has been discussed in a recent paper by a group at Vertex, with a conclusion that keeping clogP low (<2.1) and PSA high (>120) resulted in shifts <16-fold within a series of topoisomerase inhibitors.(18) We extended this analysis to all classes of antibacterial compounds presented earlier in the text in Figure 1. Our results indicated that clogD was a very good predictor of serum shifting with some exceptions (Figure 11a). Compounds with <4-fold shift in the presence of plasma protein had an average clogD = 0.1 for the compounds studied in S. aureus (±2% HSA) and an average clogD = −0.5 for the compounds studied in E. coli (tolC–, ±2% HSA). In contrast, those molecules with clogD values more in line with typical screening actives or typical collection compounds (e.g., >2) were observed to have high or sometimes significant serum shifts. It was not possible to predict the extent of the shift simply based on clogD alone as can be seen from the broad distributions of the two bins of “intermediate shift” and “high shift”. As can be appreciated, the range for the “intermediate” shift from 4 to 32 is quite large and compounds in this set would be viewed differently (e.g., low risk vs high risk) depending on which side of this bin the compound fell on and the intrinsic potency of the compound. Figure 11b illustrates the analysis of serum shift as a category of molecular charge type. There was no correlation in molecular charge type across the serum-shifted bins, except for zwitterions, which were more represented in the low shift bin (but not exclusively found in the low bin). Within these classes, there are compounds which do not follow the trend of increased hydrophobicity with increased serum shifting. Three examples are shown below in Figure 12 which illustrates the types of molecules which appeared as exceptions. Example 11 is the third generation cephalosporin cefpiramide which is quite polar and would be expected to have a low serum shift but instead is quite high (ratio of 128 in E. coli). One possible explanation of this discrepancy may be that the molecule has specific polar binding interactions with HSA which are not captured in the simple clogD prediction shown in Figure 11. Kamimura et al. have reported that cephalosporins tend not to follow the trend of increased hydrophobicity leading to increased plasma protein binding but rather associate the high plasma protein binding to polar changes at C-3 which may interact with sites on HSA in the vicinity of the warfarin binding site.(19) Another possible explanation could be that the electrophilic nature of the compound results in covalent and irreversible binding to plasma protein. It should be noted that covalent binding is typically time dependent, and the correlation of assays such as the MIC and the equilibrium dialysis protein binding experiment may not be relevant for compounds which covalently bind to HSA. For example, the MIC assay is a 24-h assay (time necessary to measure bacterial proliferation and inhibition), compared to standard equilibrium dialysis plasma protein binding experiments which are shorter in duration (∼5 h). Covalent binding to plasma protein is reported in the literature to covalently modify lysine residues of HSA and is seen across several classes of β-lactam antibiotics.(20) Based on our analysis, most of the exceptions in this category (e.g., low logD and high serum shift) are in the β-lactam class of molecules. Another confounding phenomenon may be variability in species differences. In a recent paper by MacFaul et al., the authors report that cefpiramide shows significantly different plasma protein binding in other species (1.6% free in human vs 60% in dog).(21) Typically the serum-shifted MIC’s are only run with human plasma, which indeed is the more relevant one, but if extreme differences are seen in species, the PK/PD predictions may be unreliable if based on human-shifted MIC’s in rat models of efficacy. The other class of exceptions is those which have high hydrophobic character but yet have low serum shifts when MIC’s are measured in the presence of HSA. One exception in this category is the natural product prodigiosin 12, which is a general cytotoxin and kills not only most bacterial strains but also mammalian cell lines.(22) The mechanism for mammalian toxicity may be associated with anion transport across lipid bilayers, although the antibacterial mechanism is not well understood. Why a compound is not well predicted to have a low serum shift (e.g., E. coli shift = 2-fold with clogD of 3.71) may lie in its mechanism of interaction and perhaps competition between strong affinity for an extracellular ion or target versus plasma protein (e.g., coordination with metal ions creates a much more polar compound which has lower affinity to plasma protein). Furthermore, the shift was low in E. coli (2-fold) and intermediate in S. aureus (8-fold), which highlights for some compounds that there may be species-specific effects which influence the plasma protein binding and hence the MIC serum shifting.
    Exceptions to the cases where the unbound compound and pharmacological action (e.g., the “free-drug” principle) do not correlate can be at times associated with non-steady-state behavior or energy-driven transport phenomena as possible explanations recently cited in a review by Trainor.(23) Another compound in this class of exceptions is azamulin 13, a member of the pleuromutilin class of compounds which inhibit protein synthesis via interaction of bacterial ribosomes.(24) On the basis of our analysis, a compound such as this should have a higher serum shift than what was observed. Overall, there are some exceptions noted to the trend of log D and high serum shifting, but the bias is still quite strong that most compounds with clogD > 2 experience some level of serum shifting, whereas most compounds with clogD <−1 HSA have a low impact on the MIC’s. The likelihood of an HTS biochemical or phenotypic screen identifying very hydrophobic compounds is high and, along with that probability, includes a high probability that such compounds are going to suffer from significant serum shifting.

    Effect of Lipophilicity on Cytotoxicity

    Another important property which was examined for a connection between typical physical properties of HTS actives and lipophilicity was that of cytotoxicity. Mammalian cytotoxicity was routinely measured as part of the HTS triage effort for two principal reasons. The first reason was to exclude compounds which may be acting via a general cytotoxic mechanism (e.g., cell lysis, etc.). The second reason was to gauge the risks of the series and to understand if the cytotoxicity happens to be coincidental and unrelated to the MIC after SAR expansion. A set of 5958 compounds, which were identified from multiple HTS programs and other antibacterial projects, were screened for cytotoxicity against A549 cell line.
    Figure 13 demonstrates the relationship between cytotoxicity as measured in A549 cell lines for range of clogD, further categorized by activity >100 μM (defined as inactive) and <100 μM (defined as active). The correlation between lipophilicity and toxicity has been described previously in the transformed human liver epithelial cell lines (THLE) by Greene et al., where they reported that compounds with clogP >3 and total polar surface area (TPSA) of <75 Å2 were more likely to be positive at <10 μM activity.(25) The A549 off-target screen was particularly important for phenotypic screens for antibacterial programs, where we wished to eliminate compounds with general and nonspecific cytotoxic activity but at a much higher threshold than 10 μM given the typically high doses associated with antibiotic therapy. The graph illustrates that the percentages of cytotoxic compounds increased with increasing hydrophobicity. The link to hydrophobicity and promiscuity against ancillary targets has also been established and linked to potential attrition of compounds in further development.(26) Upon investigation of the cytotoxic molecules, the predominant class were kinase inhibitors (Figure 14, ∼40% of the compounds were made for kinase programs). This analysis suggests that at least for some portion of HTS antibacterial actives which are flagged as “cytotoxic”, a plausible hypothesis for their cytotoxicity may be rationalized and tested (e.g., remove kinase activity while improving biochemical activity against the bacterial target). For others, the mechanism of toxicity remained unknown and presented the challenge of understanding whether the general cytotoxic mechanism was the cause of the antibacterial activity.

    Physical Property Analysis and Efflux

    Jump To
    We also examined the role of physical properties on bacterial efflux. Gram-negative bacteria not only have highly polar outer membranes to restrict penetration but also have promiscuous efflux pumps which serve to remove potentially harmful foreign agents.(6) To understand the properties which may be associated with efflux, we examined the two extreme categories of efflux. The first category consisted of compounds which were active in wild type P. aeruginosa with at least an MIC <8 μg/mL and demonstrated no more than an 8-fold improvement in activity in the P. aeruginosa strain with several efflux pumps inactivated (MexABCDXY–). This category was defined as “low efflux”. The other extreme category consisted of compounds which had measurable wild type activity in P. aeruginosa but possessed no less than a 50-fold improvement in activity against P. aeruginosa (MexABCDXY–). This category was defined as “high efflux”. This same analysis was performed on E. coli using data collected on E. coli (wild-type) and E. coli (tolC–). We initially examined clogD, similar to the process used against the HTS actives and antibacterial project compounds. The results of the clogD analysis are shown below in Figure 15a–d.
    A simple analysis of the average and median clogD of the two sets does not highlight a huge difference between them (e.g., average clogD for low efflux compounds = 1.50 vs 1.54 for high efflux). However, examination of the bar graph distribution by bin in Figure 15 does illustrate some interesting similarities and differences. When comparing the low and high efflux categories, the majority of clogD bins in the graphs for P. aeruginosa (Figure 15a and 15b) appear very similar to each other. One exception to this might be clogD <0, where the distribution of compounds in the low efflux category is greater when compared to the high efflux category. The same trend is seen for E. coli (Figure 15 c and 15d), except that there are fewer overall examples in the low efflux category (n = 78 vs n = 128 for P. aeruginosa). Although efflux appears to disfavor clogD < 0 compounds for P. aeruginosa and E. coli, it is not a guarantee; there are many compounds with clogD <0 in the high efflux category for both species. Neither set has many molecules with clogD > 4. One explanation for this might be that the highly polar outer membrane excludes these hydrophobic compounds; even these cannot penetrate the pump deficient mutant (and perhaps they are not found as frequently in the collection). It should also be noted that hydrophobic molecules (e.g., >3) show up in both categories of low and high. Thus, hydrophobic compounds may be susceptible to efflux, but there are compounds which do not follow that trend.
    Figure 16 illustrates the analysis of molecular weight (MW) and efflux as designated by the two categories of low efflux (Figure 16a and 16c) and high efflux (Figure 16b and 16d). The overall interpretation of this graph would suggest that very small MW compounds are more populated in the low category. Further examination revealed that all of these low molecular weight compounds are also all highly charged molecules. Examples of compounds in this set are shown in Figure 17. Perhaps these can transit through the small sized porins, and if they are effluxed, this efflux rate may match the influx rate. Another observation is that some large polar compounds have low efflux (examples in Figure 18), and these are most typically zwitterionic in character. Perhaps this can be rationalized by these molecules permeating through passive transport in very low concentrations but once in are not easily effluxed out due to change in pH and ion trapping inside the cell.(27) Compounds in this set are often very weak (e.g., 19 is a gyrase inhibitor(28) with wild-type P. aeruginosa activity of MIC ∼25 μM and pump mutant MIC of 3.13 μM), which may be supportive of this notion that they have low cellular concentrations but are not effluxed. Of course, these arguments do not take into consideration the target or potential off-rates at that target. For example, the oxaborole 14 is known to form a covalent complex with bacterial leucyl-tRNA synthetase.(29) It could be that the low efflux is a consequence of a long-off rate and has little to do with translocation based on size and charge. Alternatively, it could be that all of these factors contribute to the low efflux. The surprising characteristic of the oxaboroles is that while they are relatively weak in the biochemical assay, they are sufficiently active to elicit whole cell activity. This is a very unusual phenomenon in antibacterial research, where the dogma has often been that a requirement to achieve measurable MIC’s in Gram-negative pathogens such as E. coli or P. aeruginosa, the biochemical potency should be in the low nanomolar range. The reported data for 14 for E. coli LeuRS is IC50 = 1.0 μM with an MIC range of ∼1 μg/mL against four strains of E. coli (1 μg/mL ∼ 6 μM).(29) In contrast compounds 21 and 23 in Figure 19 show much more potent biochemical activity (21 <0.0005 μM) and submicromolar potency for 23 (0.29 μM) but did not achieve any wild-type activity in E. coli. Only 21 showed activity in E. coli (tolC–) at 2 μg/mL. These results suggest that the oxaborole is unique in that it maintains excellent whole cell activity in wild-type species, with no significant efflux and with a biochemical potency not normally expected to achieve such results. There have been no reported explanations for this observation.

    Physical Property Changes in Hit-to-Lead Programs

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In a report by Manchester et al. on the physical property determinants of AcrB-mediated efflux in H. influenzae, the authors point to fractional polar surface area (FPSA) of ∼25% and MW ∼500 for the mean of compounds which are highly effluxed in this system.(27) The authors further reiterate that targeting physical properties alone are not sufficient to introduce antibacterial activity. Many classes of known antibiotics operate through specific uptake mechanisms (e.g., porin-mediated uptake, self-promoted uptake, ion trapping, etc.), and simply being in the physical property space of compounds known to operate via these types of mechanisms will not ensure activity. Three examples of hit-to-lead programs which illustrate some of the extremes of physical property changes over the course of an antibacterial drug discovery program are highlighted below and help to reinforce the conclusions by Manchester et al. These examples further illustrate that simply relying on confines of physical property space may be helpful but not sufficient to have whole cell activity against Gram-negative pathogens. Figure 19a highlights the progression of clogD vs time in the MurC program, which utilized a P. aeruginosa enzyme as the biochemical assay.(30) In this depiction, the X-axis contains the consecutive compounds which were registered for the project, and the vertical scale is the clogD of each compound. In this graph it is apparent that over time the lipophilicity increased from the initial starting point to the final compounds made. The red dashed lines on the graph indicate the average clogD against all of the biochemical actives (top), average logD for any antibacterial project compounds active against P. aeruginosa pump mutant (MexABCDXY–, middle line), and the average clogD for wild-type P. aeruginosa (bottom). From these dashed lines, it becomes evident that as the project progressed, the separation from the average lipophilicity of P. aeuruginosa-active compounds became larger over time. Figure 19b shows the increase of biochemical potency over time. Taken together, these two plots allow one to hypothesize that the nature of the target is likely very hydrophobic, which leads to optimization based on increasing clogD, opposite of that which is likely required for bacterial penetration. This project resulted in very potent biochemical starting actives but without antibacterial activity in relevant wild-type pathogens. Examples of hit-to-lead progress and clogD values are illustrated in Figure 20, with examples 20 advancing to 21. A second example in hit-to-lead progression is shown with TrmD (e.g., hit compound 22 advancing to 23).(31) It is important to note that this project began with a fragment-based lead generation effort, which in principle might be easier to control the addition of hydrophobicity. In comparison to MurC, the starting points were less lipophilic, and over time, as biochemical potency began to improve, the lipophilicity decreased from the original hit. However, two different chemotypes were explored during the project. At the ∼300 compound mark in Figure 19c, the compounds hit the highest level of potency, but the clogD was still low. A shift in chemical strategy to try and further access parts of the binding site and improve potency led to compounds with much higher hydrophobicity but with little improvement in potency (compounds 400–500 in Figure 19c). At the end of the program, the most potent compounds had IC50 values ∼100 nM, but antibacterial activity was not observed in either E. coli or H. influenzae. This example is useful, because it stresses that just having molecules in the “right” antibacterial property space which engage the target is not sufficient to achieve antibacterial activity. There are many factors which may preclude cell-based antibacterial activity, including cell permeation, target vulnerability, active efflux mechanisms, etc.
    Another example of this is illustrated by GlmU in Figure 19e and 19f.(32) In this case, the program routinely made compounds in the low clogD range but were only able to observe weak activity in H. influenzae. The lead compound is illustrated by 24 (Figure 20) which was further improved to 25 with excellent biochemical potency and a decrease in clogD. The lead compound contained a carboxylic acid which contributed strongly to the low clogD. In Figure 3, acids were observed in the antibacterial project actives to a large extent (20–40%). However, as mentioned previously, these acids are mostly connected to β-lactams such as cephalosporins and carbapenems. It may be that the shape and charge of the β-lactam is finely tuned for bacterial penetration through porins but that general carboxylic acids may have a much more difficult time. Also as mentioned above, the target for β-lactams is perisplasmic and does not require cytoplasmic penetration as would be the case for all three examples illustrated in Figures 19 and 20. These last two examples strengthen the view that there are biochemical targets which are amenable to reduction in clogD, but this does not guarantee antibacterial activity.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We have examined a large series of antibacterial drug discovery projects that have been explored in the recent past at AstraZeneca. The projects were an evolution of drug discovery strategies and a changing commercial landscape (e.g., more focus on Gram-negative indications). Early adoption of HTS biochemical screens led to identification of many actives and hits for most targets; however, the properties of those starting points were severely disconnected from the property space typically seen in antibacterial space. In fact, many of the HTS actives were more hydrophobic than the collection average, suggesting that many of these targets have hydrophobic binding pockets which dominate the binding energetics. Attempts to maintain biochemical potency and reduce lipophilicity often failed for these targets and led to project closures based on lack of whole cell activity. In some cases, the biochemical potency was improved with reduction of hydrophobicity, but in those cases, antibacterial activity was still elusive. This led us to conclude that although antibacterial property space is quite polar, it is not sufficient just to have chemical scaffolds with these properties. Even though most antibacterial compounds reside in low clogD space, there are exceptions to this trend. For example, the LpxC inhibitors are quite hydrophobic but still have antibacterial activity in P. aeruginosa. Furthermore, the novel LolCDE inhibitors 7 and 8 were identified through phenotypic screening and are also very hydrophobic. These hydrophobic exceptions typically come with significant other issues which render optimization quite difficult (e.g., high plasma protein binding, cytotoxicity). Our observations on the lack of success of biochemical HTS screening prompted a shift toward more phenotypic targets with the hopes that compounds with intrinsic properties for antibacterial activity may be selected from other compounds. However, many of these phenotypic screens performed worse than the biochemical screens in identifying actives and hits with high hydrophobic character. A few screens were able to identify all of the known antibacterial compounds in the collection, which is evidence that if novel compounds with antibacterial activity were in the collection, they would have been identified. A final conclusion on HTS screening led us to realize that many targets have low hit rates against the hydrophilic portion of the screening collection, which is further complicated by the fact that there are relatively few of these molecules to screen. Looked at a different way, the lists of HTS actives are often populated by hydrophobic molecules due to both higher hit rates for hydrophobic molecules combined with higher proportion of these molecules in the screening collection.
    Finally, we performed an analysis on the properties which define high and low effluxed compounds. In this analysis, it appeared that most compounds suffered from some level of efflux when measured against wild-type species and pump-deficient mutants. The few compounds which demonstrated low efflux fell into two large categories: (a) small and polar and (b) large and zwitterionic. We rationalize that the small and polar molecules may have easier access to small porins. Even though they may be effluxed, the rate of influx may perhaps overwhelm the efflux. For the large and zwitterionic molecules, which are likely too large to transit through the small porins of P. aeruginosa, perhaps a very small level of passive or an unknown active transport then results in ion-trapping once inside the cell.
    There is a large debate ongoing in the antibacterial drug discovery community on how to address the lack of novel antibacterial compounds from corporate screening collections. One possibility is a return to natural product screening, which would likely put the screening libraries back in conventional antibacterial space and rediscovery of well-known compounds plus their targets. Another possibility is for reactive covalent libraries to be screened to find the “new β-lactam” type inhibitors. Further ideas include diversity-oriented synthesis libraries, peptide libraries, or rationally designed antibacterial compound libraries. It seems that if any of these ideas are to reach their full maximal potential, what is clearly needed is greater insight into medicinal chemistry strategies which optimize transport through porins and decrease efflux through the prolific efflux pumps. Antibacterial medicinal chemistry still remains quite empirical, and the need for more sophisticated tools to enable medicinal chemistry is very high.
