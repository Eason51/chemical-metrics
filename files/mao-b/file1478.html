    Abstract

    Aberrant PRC2 activity produces gene repressive epigenetic marks in multiple diseases and led to identification of Ezh2 as a drug target. Recent studies have shown that the epigenetic reader protein EED, associated with Ezh2 in PRC2, has an additional function to stimulate the PRC2 activity after binding to H3K27me3. Optimizing a compound known to block the H3K27me3 site in EED discovered by in-house screening, Novartis scientists have now produced a compound that shows durable tumor regression in a lymphoma xenograft model.

    How can a drug developer disable the function of a multicomponent molecular machine that has been crafted by evolution? The rational choice of target would be the critical member in the complex. In human diseases attributed to gene repression mediated by dysfunctional PRC2, enhancer of zeste homolog 2 (Ezh2) is thought to be responsible(1) because it catalyzes di- and trimethylation of lysine on histone tails to generate epigenetic marks, such as trimethylated H3K27 (H3K27me3), for gene silencing.(2) Selective inhibitors of Ezh2 (GSK126, N-[(1,2-dihydro-4,6-dimethyl-2-oxo-3-pyridinyl)methyl]-3-methyl-1-[(1S)-1-methylpropyl]-6-[6-(1-piperazinyl)-3-pyridinyl]-1H-indole-4-carboxamide; and EZP-6438, N-[(4,6-dimethyl-2-oxo-1H-pyridin-3-yl)methyl]-3-[ethyl(oxan-4-yl)amino]-2-methyl-5-[4-(morpholin-4-ylmethyl)phenyl]benzamide) are now in clinical trials,(1) and the drug development story to target PRC2 may be ending. In a paper in this issue, Novartis scientists however prove otherwise and so write a new chapter in drug discovery.(3)

    The core of the mammalian PRC2 complex is composed of four proteins, Ezh2 or Ezh1, EED, suppressor of zeste 12 (Suz12), and either RbAp46 or RbAp48.(2a) It had been puzzling that the PRC2 complex has a 1000-fold enhanced methylation activity compared to Ezh2 alone. A previous model proposed that noncatalytic core component proteins maintain the integrity of PRC2 and function as epigenetic readers to interact with nucleosomes to facilitate the efficiency. A cocrystal structure further revealed a segment of Ezh2 bound to the opposite side of the H3K27me3 binding site in EED.(4a) The submicromolar affinity of the Ezh2 peptide with EED and the cocrystal structure inspired a targeting strategy to block the association between EED and Ezh2. Indeed, a stapled Ezh2 peptide was shown to disrupt EED-Ezh2 association and causes growth suppression of PRC2-dependent cancer cells.(4b)

    Besides serving as an adaptor and epigenetic reader protein, recent studies uncovered an additional function of EED in PRC2 by showing that binding of EED to H3K27me3 enables efficient methylation activity of PRC2(2d, 2e) (Figure 1). The lesson is a reminder that in the evolution-optimized biological machine even an adaptor protein can assist in the quest for high functional efficiency. A canonical trimethylated lysine recognition motif in epigenetic reader proteins is a small and shallow cage pocket formed by the side chains of Trp, Tyr, and Phe. The cage pocket in EED (Figure 2A) was deemed to be a challenging druggable site. Furthermore, whether the H3K27me3 binding site in EED can be inhibited to show therapeutic benefits had been unknown. It has now been demonstrated that a compound 43 (EED226), developed by optimization of 7 (EED162)(5a) which was discovered by high throughput screening and targeting EED, can indeed decrease PRC2 activity and yield durable tumor regression in a lymphoma xenograft model.

    Novartis scientists started the development of 43 with the in-house high-throughput screening of 1.4 million compounds using an optimized time-resolved fluorescence assay based on five component proteins (Ezh2, EED, Suz12, AEBP2, and RbAp46/48).(3) The goal was to identify compounds that decrease the PRC2 activity in the methylation of the H3K27 peptide. They discovered two classes of compounds that are either competitive or noncompetitive with the cofactor SAM used by Ezh2.(5) They also observed that the H3K27me3 peptide can stimulate the PRC2 activity through an allosteric mechanism(2d, 2e) with a Kcat value of 1.0 Î¼M. Among 1000 hits of the compounds noncompetitive with SAM, they have reported five representative hits {7; 210 (EED210), (3R,4aS,10aS)-6-methoxy-3-[(3-methoxyphenyl)methyl]-1-methyl-3,4,4a,5,10,10a-hexahydro-2H-benzo[g]quinolone; EED396, 4-azanylidene-2-(3-methoxy-4-propan-2-yloxyphenyl)-6,7-dihydro[1,3]benzodioxolo[6,5-a]quinolizine-3-carbonitrile; EED666, 2-[3-(3,5-dimethylpyrazol-1-yl)-4-nitrophenyl]-3,4-dihydro-1H-isoquinoline; EED709, (3R,4S)-1-[(2-methoxyphenyl)methyl]-N,N-dimethyl- 4-(1-methylindol-3-yl)pyrrolidin-3-amine}(5a) that bind to the H3K27me3 binding site in EED (Figure 1). Validation of these hits and their binding site in EED was established with additional biochemical assays and cocrystal structures.(5a)

    Although optimization of 210 to obtain compound 19, 6-(2-fluoro-5-methoxybenzyl)-1-isopropyl-5,6,7,8-tetrahydroimidazo[1,5-a]pyridin-3-amine (EC50 = 4.4 Î¼M in the cell proliferation assay),(5b) has been reported, no in vivo data were provided. In the article by Huang et al.,(3) the optimization of another hit, 7, leading to 43 is described. The cocrystal structure of 7 with EED revealed that concomitant changes of Y365 and W364 rotamer states (Figure 2B) open a deeper pocket inaccessible by H3K27me3 (Figure 2A) to accommodate the furan group in 7. Deconstruction of the interaction between 7 and EED identified two scaffolds for structureâactivity relationship (SAR) exploration and compound optimization.

    Through compound optimization, three candidates, 41â43, were advanced to pharmacokinetic evaluation. Though least potent of the three, 43 has the best pharmokinetics in mice with a half-life of 2.2 h and oral bioavailability of 109%. A histone methyltransferase panel study showed 43 has >10â¯000 selectivity with respect to other histone methyltransferases. The in vivo efficacy of 43 was subsequently evaluated in a Karpas-422-derived diffuse large B-cells lymphoma (DLBCL) model in Balb/C nude mice. A 300 mg/kg dose of 43 orally administered twice daily for 34 days gave complete tumor regression after day 30 in all 10 mice tested. A recurrence of small tumors was detected in one mouse at day 130 but became unobservable after day 160 until the end point at day 180. In an accompanied study, they further showed that 43 inhibits H3K27 trimethylation in the Karpas-422 cell line and is effective against the Ezh2 mutated, EPZ-6438 insensitive diffuse large cell lymphoma cell line (EI1-resistant WSU-DLCL2).(5c) At the same time, AbbVie disclosed a different class of EED inhibitor, A-395, (3R,4S)-1-(7-fluoro-2,3-dihydro-1H-inden-1-yl)-N,N-dimethyl-4-(4-(4-(methylsulfonyl)piperazin-1-yl)phenyl)pyrrolidin-3-amine, that is potent in in vitro and in vivo tumor models and retains activity against cell lines resistant to the catalytic domain Ezh2 inhibitors.(6)

    New and strong evidence provided by this study argues that the H3K27me3 binding site in EED is druggable and blockade of the pocket can compromise the methylation activity of PRC2. This work together with accompanied studies will spawn many novel ideas from different research groups for the design of EED inhibitors. The findings will also inspire medicinal chemists that seek to develop inhibitors targeting other proteins sharing the same WD40 repeat architecture as EED including RbAp46/48.

    When considering the therapeutic applications of 43 to broader disease indications, some questions arise. EED also binds to other trimethylated lysines in H1, H3, H4, JARID2 and can be incorporated into PRC1; thus, it remains unclear how diminished PRC2 activity resulting from blocking the H3K27me3 site in EED will affect epigenetic reprogramming. Further, 43 inhibits EED indiscriminately in both PRC2-Ezh2 and PRC2-Ezh1 and its on-target toxicity, compared with selective Ezh2 inhibitors, remains unknown. Several isoforms and mutations of EED in cells have also been reported. How they affect the ultimate clinical use of EED inhibitors remains to be determined.

    In summary, the discovery and preclinical studies of 43 by Huang et al.(3) and the accompanied study,(5c) as well as the study by AbbVie(6) provide convincing evidence that targeting EED is a novel effective strategy to inactivate the PRC2 complex. While we await the clinical trial data of Ezh2 inhibitors, the works on EED inhibitors show a new path to development of another class of agents to overcome gene repression mediated by PRC2 activity in human diseases. For clinicians fighting diseases, more weapons are always welcome.
