    Abstract

    Dysregulation of histone H3 lysine 4 (H3K4) methylation is implicated in the pathogenesis of neurodevelopmental disorders. Lysine-specific demethylase 1 (LSD1) determines the methylation status of H3K4 through flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD)-mediated histone demethylation. Therefore, LSD1 inhibition in the brain can be a novel therapeutic option for treating these disorders. Positron emission tomography (PET) imaging of LSD1 allows for investigating LSD1 expression levels under normal and disease conditions and validating target engagement of therapeutic LSD1 inhibitors. This study designed and synthesized (2-aminocyclopropyl)phenyl derivatives with irreversible binding to LSD1 as PET imaging agents for LSD1 in the brain. We optimized lipophilicity of the lead compound to minimize the risk of nonspecific binding and identified 1e with high selectivity over monoamine oxidase A and B, which are a family of FAD-dependent enzymes homologous to LSD1. PET imaging in a monkey showed a high uptake of [18F]1e to regions enriched with LSD1, indicating its specific binding to LSD1.

    Introduction

    Epigenetic control by chromatin remodeling through the modifications of histones (e.g., acetylation, methylation, and phosphorylation) is essential in the regulation of gene transcription.(1â3) Specifically, the methylation of histone H3 lysine 4 (H3K4) is involved in various neural functions (e.g., learning and memory) through the regulation of synaptic plasticity.(4) Recent studies have revealed that the reduced methylation of H3K4 and the consequent reduction of gene transcription via the alteration of the chromatin structure is implicated in the pathogenesis of various psychiatric and neurological disorders including schizophrenia,(5,6) autism spectrum disorders (ASD),(7,8) intellectual disability disorders,(9) and Huntingtonâs disease.(10) Therefore, the recovery of H3K4 methylation levels can be a promising approach to treat patients with epigenetic dysfunction.
    Lysine-specific demethylase 1 (LSD1, also known as KDM1A) determines the methylation status of H3K4 by catalyzing the removal of methyl groups from mono- and di-methylated H3K4 through flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD)-dependent enzymatic oxidation.(11,12) Therefore, the recovery of H3K4 methylation levels by the inhibition of LSD1 enzyme activity in the brain may offer novel therapeutic options for treating central nervous system diseases with epigenetic dysregulation. For example, it is reported that some of the brain-penetrating LSD inhibitors increased H3K4 methylation levels and improved the learning function in a mouse model of schizophrenia and ASD.(13â15)
    Positron emission tomography (PET) is a non-invasive imaging technology that allows us to quantify physiological and pathological processes in living subjects. PET imaging may potentially allow us to determine the occupancy of therapeutic LSD1 inhibitors in LSD1; this technology will help to assess the target engagement of LSD1-targeting drugs. In addition, the expression levels of LSD1 and their alterations in homeostatic and disease conditions can be assessed by in vivo PET scans. However, no PET imaging agents for LSD1 have been published thus far.
    A number of LSD1 inhibitors as antitumor agents like (Â±)1 have been reported thus far.(16â18) X-ray crystal analyses revealed that 1 (eutomer) produces a 1-FAD adduct in the binding pocket of LSD1 (Figure 1).(19) However, our group identified irreversible-type selective LSD1 inhibitors such as T-448 which uniquely form a compact formylated FAD adduct and do not show steric hindrance with LSD1 cofactors such as GFI1B, which is a critical regulator of hematopoiesis (Figure 2).(19) Hence, for the design of LSD1 PET imaging agents, it is essential to carefully consider the structures of resultant FAD adducts. Radiolabeling positions in the LSD1 inhibitors which generate formylated FAD adducts are very limited; thus, we selected (Â±)1 as a lead compound for the development of LSD1 PET imaging agents (Figure 3). In this report, we describe the design and synthesis of novel PET imaging agents for LSD1 as well as their in vivo characterization by a monkey brain PET scan.

    Designed Compounds and in Vitro Characterization

    Central nervous system PET imaging agents not only require high affinity and target selectivity for sufficient in vivo binding potential but also a desirable chemical structure, physicochemical and pharmacokinetic properties that are appropriate for radiolabeling, efficient bloodâbrain barrier (BBB) penetration, and low levels of undesirable nonspecific binding (NSB).(23â25) (Â±)1 irreversibly inhibits LSD1 enzyme activity with 29 nM concentration for 50% inhibition (IC50). This compound is unlikely to be a substrate for P-glycoprotein [P-gp, also known as multidrug resistance 1 (MDR1)] because its efflux BA/AB ratio is 0.8, which indicates its good brain permeability.(26) However, (Â±)1 lacks a site for 11C and 18F radiolabeling, and its NSB may not be sufficiently low, judging from its low unbound fraction (Fu) in rat plasma (<1%).(27,28) This will be attributed to the relatively high lipophilicity of the compound (log D = 3.29).(29,30) Thus, to overcome these concerns, we decided to modify the biphenyl amide moiety of (Â±)1 to reduce the lipophilicity by introducing a chemical group that can be labeled with 18F.(31)
    The synthesized compounds were first evaluated by the in vitro parameters of LSD1, IC50, log D, MDR1 efflux ratio, and Fu, in rat plasma.
    Our initial efforts focused on reducing the lipophilicity of (Â±)1 by removing the distal benzene ring. The N-(4-(2-Aminocyclopropyl)phenyl)amide derivative 1a and 4-(2-aminocyclopropyl)benzamide derivative 1b exhibit lower log D values (1a:1.88 and 1b:2.26) and higher Fu values (1a:24% and 1b:40%) than those of (Â±)1. This is in line with our hypothetical view that the NSB of a compound can be reduced by lowering its lipophilicity. These compounds also show increased inhibitory activity compared with that of (Â±)1. Specifically, the 4-(2-aminocyclopropyl)benzamide derivative 1b exhibits an approximately sevenfold improvement in the activity (IC50 = 4.1 nM). Because 1b also exhibits an MDR1 efflux ratio (0.5) that is suitable for BBB penetration, which is similar to (Â±)1, we further modified 1b to introduce a chemical group that can be labeled with 18F. The 3-Cyano-4-fluorophenyl derivative 1c and 6-fluoropyridine derivative 1d, which are designed to introduce a fluorine atom as the labeling part, exhibit a single-digit nanomolar activity for the inhibition of LSD1. On the basis of these findings and the measured log D values, we selected the pyridine derivative 1d with a relatively lower lipophilicity compared with 1c as the first candidate for radiolabeling (Table 1).

    1e-FAD Adduct

    To study whether the radiolabeled positions can remain in the FAD adduct after the inactivation of FAD in LSD1, we performed the LC/MS analysis using human recombinant LSD1 reacted with 1e. 1e generated a peak with m/z 534.6246, which corresponded to the molecular weight of the 1e-FAD adduct (Figure 6). This result confirmed that 1e irreversibly inhibited LSD1, and the radiolabeled position remained in the resultant FAD adduct.

    LSD1 Distribution in the Brain

    LSD1 protein biodistribution in the brain has not been reported so far, although the information is useful to assess the specific binding of [18F]1e to LSD1. Hence, precise distribution patterns of LSD1 in the human brain, monkey brain, and rat brain were evaluated by the western blot (Figure 7). In the human brain, the LSD1 expression level in the cerebellum was the highest, and that in the medulla was the lowest among the tested regions. Similar distribution patterns were observed in monkeys and rats; the expression levels were relatively higher in the cerebellum, and lower in the brain stem regions (pons and medulla). This distribution pattern of LSD1 was consistent with the radioactivity uptake of [18F]1e, which suggested the specificity of [18F]1e for LSD1.

    In Vitro Binding of 1e and Its Selectivity for MAOA and MAOB

    Given that the in vivo PET results indicate that [18F]1e may be a potential PET imaging agent that reflects LSD1 expression in the brain, the binding properties of 1e were further assessed by in vitro measurements. The specificity constant of irreversible inhibition (kinact/KI) for 1e was determined as 5.5 Ã 105 by a peroxidase-coupled assay. The specificity of compound 1e for LSD1 was also assessed using an in vitro enzyme assay for MAOA and MAOB, and with FAD-dependent enzymes with a high amino acid sequence homology for LSD1. Compound 1e showed a very weak inhibitory activity for MAOA/B with 19,000 nM and >100,000 nM IC50, respectively. These results support the high selectivity of 1e for LSD1 (Table 3).

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Starting from the irreversible LSD1 inhibitor (Â±)1, we designed and synthesized a series of novel (2-aminocyclopropyl)phenyl derivatives to identify PET imaging agents for LSD1. By tailoring the lipophilicity of the molecule to optimize NSB and BBB permeability, we successfully identified 1e with a potent inhibitory activity for LSD1, an MDR1 efflux ratio suitable for BBB penetration, and a high Fu value. The in vivo PET analysis of [18F]1e in a monkey brain and the supplemental in vitro evaluation indicated the specific uptake of [18F]1e in regions with high LSD1 expression. Further validation, including blocking studies with pretreatment of LSD1 inhibitors and detailed radiometabolite analysis, is underway to assess the suitability of [18F]1e as a potential lead for clinical translation.
