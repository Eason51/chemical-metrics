    Abstract

    The structural diversity of metal scaffolds makes them a viable alternative to traditional organic scaffolds for drug design. Combinatorial chemistry and multicomponent reactions, coupled with high-throughput screening, are useful techniques in drug discovery, but they are rarely used in metal-based drug design. We report the optimization and validation of a new combinatorial, metal-based, three-component assembly reaction for the synthesis of a library of 442 Ru–arene Schiff-base (RAS) complexes. These RAS complexes were synthesized in a one-pot, on-a-plate format using commercially available starting materials under aqueous conditions. The library was screened for their anticancer activity, and several cytotoxic lead compounds were identified. In particular, [(η6-1,3,5-triisopropylbenzene)RuCl(4-methoxy-N-(2-quinolinylmethylene)aniline)]Cl (4) displayed low micromolar IC50 values in ovarian cancers (A2780, A2780cisR), breast cancer (MCF7), and colorectal cancer (HCT116, SW480). The absence of p53 activation or changes in IC50 value between p53+/+ and p53–/– cells suggests that 4 and possibly the other lead compounds may act independently of the p53 tumor suppressor gene frequently mutated in cancer.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Traditionally, drug discovery research has focused on the development of organic molecules as pharmacophores. However, their limited structural diversity makes it difficult for them to access other scaffolds that span the entirety of the biologically relevant chemical space,(1-3) some of which may contain promising drug candidates. Out of the 24 million organic molecules registered under the Chemical Abstract Service (CAS) Registry, half could be classified into 143 unique motifs, a small number compared to the vast quantities of molecules being classified.(4) In addition, of the 5000 compounds in the Comprehensive Medicine Chemistry (CMC) database that were analyzed for diversity in topological shapes, half of them belong to only 32 shape classifications,(5) leaving large areas of this chemical space containing structurally and chemically unique motifs unexplored. In order to tap into this substantial and potentially valuable unexplored chemical space, scaffolds that are not accessible with organic chemistry need to be investigated.
    Transition-metal scaffolds could therefore be uniquely suited for the development of potential drug candidates. They can readily accommodate higher coordination numbers and thus access different molecular geometries not possible with a purely organic framework.(6) Transition-metal complexes are also endowed with tunable properties, such as variable oxidation states that are stable under room conditions and predictable reactivity based on metal–ligand bonding interactions. Since the first successful platinum-containing drug cisplatin was discovered and approved by Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of testicular and ovarian cancer in 1978,(7) much research has been directed at uncovering new metallodrugs that are more effective than cisplatin.(8, 9) This research is beginning to bear fruit, with Ru-containing NAMI-A receiving favorable evaluations in clinical trials and KP1019 showing promising results in preclinical studies.(10, 11) More recently, metal-based compounds have been designed and tested against biological therapeutic targets, which are known to regulate processes involved in the development of cancer.(12) One class of Ru compound in particular, namely, half-sandwiched Ru–arene complexes, has been garnering much interest, with a large body of evidence demonstrating their therapeutic properties against cancer(13-15) or their inhibitory activity toward enzyme targets linked to cancer.(16-19) A few examples include the antimetastatic RAPTA complexes(20) and antitumor [(η6-biphenyl)Ru(en)Cl]+ (RM175) scaffold,(21) as well as organoruthenium-based paullone complexes(22) (Figure 1). These studies pave the way for the development of Ru–arene complexes as the next generation of metal-based anticancer drugs.
    Yet, discovery of new organoruthenium complexes with the necessary prerequisites for therapy is a laborious process involving iterative steps of synthesis, characterization, functionalization, and testing. For example, good aqueous stability and solubility are essential to ensure biocompatibility, but these attributes are not easily incorporated into the scaffolds. Combinatorial coordination chemistry and multicomponent reactions are potential tools that can generate large libraries of diverse compounds(23, 24) for drug screening, but they have seldom been applied to metal-based drug discovery. One report employed a two-step combinatorial synthesis approach coupled with high-throughput screening (HTS) to identify a polypyridyl–Ru complex capable of strongly inducing apoptosis in cancer cells.(25) Another group made use of automated solid-phase combinatorial synthesis to synthesize a library of peptide-tethered Pt(II) complexes for screening and identification of cytotoxic leads.(26, 27) There have also been a number of reports on one-pot multicomponent reactions for the preparation of metal complexes,(28, 29) although, to the best of our knowledge, none have been used for the purpose of drug discovery.
    To that end, we designed a water-promoted multicomponent reaction for the combinatorial assembly of organoruthenium–arene complexes in aqueous solutions. Using a water/DMSO system to carry out the assembly, the reaction directly “selects” for water-stable metallocomplexes of moderate solubility. We report the application of this metal-based three-component assembly (3CA) reaction methodology to prepare a library of water-soluble Ru–arene Schiff-base (RAS) complexes (Figure 2). We perform extensive functional groups variation and screen the compound library for anticancer activity. The identified lead compounds were tested against a variety of cancer cell types and their p53-dependence was also investigated.

    Optimization and Validation of a Novel One-Step Three-Component Assembly Reaction in Aqueous Solvents

    Our approach was to design and optimize a one-pot three-component reaction that can be used to synthesize a large library of Ru–arene complexes with ease, requiring minimal purification prior to characterization and testing. The reaction comprises three components, namely, a Ru–arene complex, a picolinaldehyde, and an aniline. Imine condensation between the picolinaldehyde and aniline forms a Schiff-base ligand that is predisposed for metal chelation. Without metal chelation, such ligands are typically poorly stable in aqueous environment due to hydrolysis of the imine bond. Incorporation of the Ru–arene component yields an organoruthenium complex that is stabilized by the electrodonating Schiff-base chelate. Similar Ru–arene Schiff-base complexes have been reported, but as yet they have not been prepared in a manner suitable for library synthesis.(30) Thus, 3CA comprises Ru–arene percursor A with at least two accessible coordination sites (via labile ligands) that could chelate with Schiff-base ligand formed from condensation between picolinaldehyde B and aniline C, both of which are widely available from commerical sources (Figure 3a). Components A, B, and C with different functional groups could be mixed in a combinatorial fashion in a one-pot reaction to give a library of ruthenium–arene Schiff-base (RAS) complexes (Figure 3). Because the components were initially insoluble in water, complete dissolution provided an indication of a successful assembly. Reactions that yielded insoluble products were assumed to have failed or have led to products that were insufficiently soluble to warrant further studies and were eliminated from the library. An initial library of 48 RAS complexes were synthesized in D2O and directly characterized with 1H NMR and ESI-MS without prior workup or purification. Several complexes were analyzed by RP-HPLC to approximate the levels of purity achieved.
    The 1H NMR spectra typically presented resonances corresponding to the expected products with minor observable byproducts, indicating that RAS complexes were formed almost exclusively (Figure 4a). The RAS complexes exhibited two characteristic features, namely, the disappearance of the singlet peak corresponding to the aldehyde proton at ca. 10 ppm and the appearance of a singlet attributable to the imine proton at 8–9 ppm (a downfield shift from ca. 6 ppm for uncoordinated ligands). In addition, resonances of the η6-arene protons (for A = toluene, cymene, and 1,3,5-triisopropylbenzene) were resolved to distinct peaks due to desymmetrization of the arene ligand upon complexation of the Schiff-base at the Ru center. ESI-MS typically revealed only one m/z peak corresponding to the molecular [M]+ ions with the characteristic Ru isotopic pattern, further confirming the formation of RAS complexes in the 3CA reaction (Figure 4c). RP-HPLC analysis of seven selected RAS complexes from the initial library showed purity levels between 70 and 90% of the crude mixture, which is sufficiently pure for drug screening [see Supporting Information (SI)]. The main cause of the lower purity is the presence of excess reaction components that were added inadvertently and the formation of aquation products.
    Structural information was obtained via single crystal X-ray diffraction studies of the RAS complex synthesized from A3, B1, and C8, with PF6– as the counteranion (Figure 5). The complex adopted an expected pseudo-“piano stool” structure, with the cymene ligand bonded to the Ru atom as the “stool top”. The Schiff-base ligand rotated slightly around N2–C7 to give a “twisted” conformation, presumably to reduce the steric interaction with the cymene ligand. Upon optimization, we noted that a solvent mixture comprising 1:1 v/v DMSO-d6/D2O to be a more suitable solvent system for 3CA for preparation of less water-soluble RAS complexes, which were otherwise insoluble in pure D2O.

    Mechanistic Investigations into Formation of RAS Complexes

    We performed a mechanistic investigation of the 3CA reaction to understand the factors affecting assembly of the target complexes in order to be able to fully exploit its versatility. Several experiments were designed to probe the reaction mechanism and to understand how RAS complexes were formed. These experiments were performed in deuterated solvents to facilitate immediate 1H NMR analysis (see SI). From the experimental results, the factors affecting the formation of RAS complexes were inferred.
    Ru precursor A exists as a dimer in solid state or when dissolved in noncoordinating solvents such as toluene or CHCl3. However, in coordinating solvents such as DMSO, MeOH, or H2O the dimers are cleaved to form reactive [Ru(arene)(solvent)x+1Cl2-x] monomers.(31) We theorized that 3CA occurred via these reactive intermediates and coordinating solvents were necessary to activate A. Thus, 3CA was carried out in the noncoordinating solvent CDCl3, as well as the coordinating solvent systems DMSO-d6, MeOD-d4, D2O, 1:1 v/v DMSO-d6/D2O. Components that were soluble in each of the solvents were chosen so as to eliminate any possible complications arising from a difference in solubility. As expected, RAS complexes were not formed at all in CDCl3. However, the 3CA reaction was also unsuccessful in the coordinating solvents DMSO-d6 and MeOD-d4, producing many unexpected side products. Indeed, assembly was only successful in solvent systems containing D2O.
    The unexpected observation could be explained by the difference in the reactive intermediate formed in DMSO and MeOH compared to that in H2O (Scheme 1).(31) In DMSO and MeOH, monosolvated species [Ru(arene)XCl2] is formed as the only species (X = DMSO, MeOH). In H2O, an additional chloride ligand is displaced from the monosolvated [Ru(arene)(H2O)Cl2] to form the disolvated species [Ru(arene)(H2O)2Cl]+. The presence of two labile solvent ligand in the disolvated species allowed rapid reaction with the ligand chelate, giving rise to fewer side reactions and leading to the formation of RAS complexes exclusively. The presence of water in DMSO/H2O solvent mixture presumably replaced the second chloride to yield the reactive intermediate [Ru(arene)(DMSO)(H2O)Cl]+. This hypothesis was further validated by performing the 3CA reaction at different pH conditions (Scheme 2). We hypothesized that at acidic to neutral pH, the disolvated [Ru(arene)(H2O)2Cl]+ would be formed primarily. At basic pH, the kinetically inert μ-OH bridged dimer [Ru2(arene)2(μ-OH)3]+ would form preferentially, which would hinder 3CA.(32, 33) Thus, 3CA was carried out at pH 4.5 and 10.0 using deuterated reagents in D2O and analyzed by both 1H NMR and ESI-MS. Indeed, the results showed that 3CA was hindered in basic pH medium.
    Next, we attempted to understand why little or no side products were obtained in 3CA by studying the reactivity of the individual components after they had been added sequentially (Scheme 3). We first carried out the reactions in the absence of one of the components, i.e., (i) B and C, (ii) A and B, and (iii) A and C, for 24 h and then supplemented each of the reaction mixtures with the missing component for a further 24 h. Like previous experiments, reactions i, ii, and iii were done in a deuterated solvent, D2O, so that immediate analysis by 1H NMR was possible. For reaction i, the expected imine product was observed after 1 h, and most of the starting material had been converted to the imine product after 24 h. Reaction ii yielded multiple products after 24 h. Even after C was added, there was further reaction. Reaction iii gave a mixture of starting materials and products after 24 h. However, after B was added for 24 h, the reaction mixture resolved to yield a clean 1H NMR spectrum with RAS complex being the only product. This suggested that A reacted reversibly with C and subsequent addition of B would still drive 3CA reaction to completion. Taken together, these experiments explained why the 3CA worked despite the possibility of forming many different side products. First, RAS complexes are thermodynamically the most stable product due to the “chelate effect”. Second, RAS complexes are kinetically favored. At room temperature, the reaction between [Ru(arene)(H2O)2Cl]+ and B or [Ru(arene)(H2O)2Cl]+ and C is slower compared to the reaction between [Ru(arene)(H2O)2Cl]+ and the imine ligand (B + C) or the formation of the imine ligand (Scheme 4).
    To validate this hypothesis, we compared 3CA using the more basic aliphatic amine, 4-aminomethylbenzenesulfonamide, in place of aniline, 4-aminobenzamidine, as component C. Although they are both primary amines, aniline is poorly basic by virtue of the delocalization of the N-atom lone pair into the aromatic ring. We reasoned that aniline would not undergo dative bonding with [Ru(arene)(H2O)2Cl]+ and would not directly compete with 3CA. Indeed, 1H NMR analysis revealed that, unlike 4-aminobenzamidine, 4-aminomethylbenzenesulfonamide reacted irreversibily with A, prematurely arresting 3CA. We further tested the functional group tolerance using analogues of B and C and found that functional groups that competed for direct Ru-binding, such as thiol and imidazoles, also directly interfered with 3CA. Nevertheless, a wide variety of functional groups were tolerated (Figure 3).

    Plate Format Synthesis and Direct Screening of Cytotoxicity

    To demonstrate the versatility of 3CA, we carried out extensive synthesis of RAS complexes using the possible combinations shown in Figure 3 on a plate format. A total of 450 compounds were prepared and characterized. Synthesis were performed on 96-well plates, in a high-throughput manner with 1:1 v/v DMSO/H2O used as the solvent system (see SI) to maximize the solubility of the products while maintaining some degree of aqueous solubility. DMSO stock solutions of all the starting reagents were prepared and mixed in a predetermined order in wells containing an equal volume of H2O. Each well gave a different RAS complex at 5 mM in 1:1 v/v DMSO/H2O by the end of the reaction, and they were analyzed by ESI-MS. Successful assembly was indicated by the molecular [M]+ ion as the predominant m/z peak. Out of the 450 complexes synthesized, only one was insoluble and eliminated as a candidate for screening. All complexes synthesized were used without further purification.
    For the screening of cytotoxicity, A2780 ovarian cancer cells were used because the cells are sensitive to cisplatin treatment and can be transplated to in vivo models as xenografts. Cytotoxicity of the assembled RAS complexes were tested at a single concentration by using the MTT assay.(34) The complexes were first diluted on a separate 96-well plate with RPMI 1640 medium to give a final concentration of 25 μM in medium containing 0.5% (v/v) DMSO. Seven more compounds were insoluble under this condition and were removed from the screen. The remaining 442 RAS complexes were tested against A2780 cells in quintuplicate, and the percentage of cell viability (CV%) after treatment of each drug was then determined. Using this mass screening method, we identified 20 lead compounds with CV% below 10% at 25 μM concentration; out of these 20 lead compounds, 16 had CV% below 5%.
    To determine if there was any correlation between the hydrophobicity and the cytotoxicity of the complexes, CV% could be compared with their clogP. Because there are currently no available software to calculate the clogP of these organometallic complexes, we adopted an alternative standard of measurement based on the sum of clogP values of the arene and the chelate ligand. We assumed that differences in their hydrophobicities were largely due the different R, R′, and R″ groups (Figure 3), since all 442 complexes were based on the same core structure. RAS complex [RAS-A1/B1/C1] (R = R′ = R″ = H) was assigned an arbitrary hydrophobicity index (HI) of 0 and the remaining complexes were assigned HI values relative to this, calculated from the clogP of their ligands. Similar to clogP, the more positive the HI, the greater the hydrophobicity. The CV% value of each compound was then plotted against their HI value (Figure 6a). When HI > 3, CV% decreased significantly. The most cytotoxic RAS complexes were relatively hydrophobic, having HI between 4 and 6, suggesting that cellular uptake might be via passive diffusion.(35)
    We further analyzed the cell viability data against additional measures of druglikeness from “Lipinski’s rule of five,”(36) such as hydrogen bond (H-bond) acceptors, H-bond donors, and molecular weight, to see if there were any additional observable trends (Figure 6b−d). In general, CV% increased with increasing number of H-bond acceptors and donors with a majority of the cytotoxic RAS complexes having less than two H-bond acceptors or donors. In addition, the most cytotoxic of these complexes tends to have a molecular weight between 530 and 660 Da.

    Synthesis and Characterization of Lead Compounds

    In order to validate the cytotoxic mechanisms of the lead compounds, we synthesize them on a preparative scale for detailed in vitro evaluation. This would also help us eliminate false positives, which are relatively common in high-throughput screening. We selected six out of the 20 lead compounds (Figure 7) with low CV% and resynthesized them individually according to Scheme 5, including two outliers (1 and 2) that did not fit into the general cytotoxicity trend. The identity and purity of compound 1–6 were ascertained by 1H NMR, ESI-MS, and RP-HPLC or elemental analysis (EA). The 1H NMR spectra of the 1, 2, 4, 5, and 6 were as expected, with the imine singlet peak appearing around 9.0–9.4 ppm and additional splitting of the arene proton peaks. However, 1H NMR spectrum of 3 showed two sets of compounds instead of one (see SI). This was presumably due to the restriction of free rotation of the C–N bond caused by the steric repulsion between the bulky napthalene group and the triisopropyl groups, which resulted in the formation of pseudo-E and -Z stereoisomers (Figure 8). By increasing the temperature of NMR data acquisition to 343 K, the barrier of rotation could be overcome and the peaks coalesced, giving rise to only one set of resonances. Similar E/Z isomerism was previously reported for another class of Ru–arene Schiff-base complexes.(22) The ESI-MS spectra showed the expected molecular ion peaks for all six compounds. All six compounds were shown to be at least 95% pure, either by EA or RP-HPLC analysis.

    Determination of Aqueous Stability, Solubility and log POW

    The aqueous stability, solubility, and log POW are important factors that affect a potential drug’s bioavailability and cell permeability.(37) The aqueous stability of lead compounds 1–6 were investigated by monitoring their UV profiles over a period of 24 h at every 1 h interval. Each compound was first dissolved in DMSO to make up a 5 mM stock solution which was then diluted with water to 50 μM (1% v/v DMSO). All compounds except 6 demonstrated good stability in water with negligible variation to their UV peak profiles after 24 h. A significant change in the UV peak profile of 6 was observed with two isosbestic points at 290 and 454 nm (see SI). When the experiment was repeated for 6 in the presence of 0.6% w/v NaCl, no significant change in its UV peak profile was observed. This suggests that the chloride ligand of 6 was significantly more labile as compared to those of 1–5 and that aquation could be suppressed with the addition of chloride ions.
    The thermodynamic solubilities of 1–5 were determined at saturation in ddH2O for 24 h. After 24 h, undissolved solid was removed by centrifugation, and Ru concentration of the resulting saturated solutions was determined via ICP-OES. The lead compounds showed moderate solubilities in water, with 3 having solubility of 131.6 mg/mL, on the same order of magnitude as common inorganic salts. The solubility of 6 in 0.6% w/v NaCl was too low to warrant further analyses.
    The log POW of 1–6 was determined using the shake-flask method.(38)1–5 were partitioned between n-octanol and ddH2O, and the amounts of compounds in the aqueous fractions before and after the partitioning were measured using UV–vis spectroscopy at their respective λmax. The same was done for 6 using 0.6% w/v NaCl instead of ddH2O. 1–5 were relatively hydrophilic, as shown by their negative log POW values. The ionic nature of the complexes endowed them with hydrophilic character, despite having hydrophobic surface groups. In general, 1–6 were considered “druglike” based on Lipinski’s rule of five.(36) However, there was no observable correlation between the hydrophilicity (log POW) and the aqueous solubility. In fact, the more hydrophilic 1 and 2 were much less soluble compared to hydrophobic 3 and 4. This could be due to the ability of 1 and 2 to lose a H+ ion from the phenol functional group to form an overall charge-neutral zwitterion, which may have reduced solubility.

    In Vitro Evaluation of Lead Compounds against Cancer Cell Lines

    The efficacy values of RAS complexes 1–6 in the inhibition of cell viability were evaluated against a panel of human cancer cell lines (Table 3). All experiments were carried out in at least triplicates, and IC50 was determined from the average of three separate experiments. Ru concentrations of stock solutions were also determined using ICP-OES and the IC50 values were adjusted to the actual concentration. Contrary to what was expected, 1 and 2 did not show significant cytotoxicity for any of the cell lines. We concluded that 1 and 2 were false positives and removed them from further testing.

    RAS complexes 3–6 showed good killing efficacies against the cancer cell lines tested. Against A2780, the complexes exhibited efficacies comparable to or better than cisplatin, with 4 achieving IC50 values 3 times lower than that of cisplatin. In addition, they did not exhibit any cross-resistance toward cisplatin-resistant A2780cisR, with 6 achieving a resistance factor as low as 0.7 and 4 having IC50 value 10 times lower than that of cisplatin. Furthermore, 3–6 also demonstrated good toxicity toward MCF7, whereas cisplatin showed negligible cytotoxicity. Other classes of Ru–arene complexes have also demonstrated activity against cell lineages with reduced susceptibility to cisplatin or other platinum anticancer drugs.(39, 40) In particular, the class of Ru–arene complexes (RM175) reported by Sadler et al. exhibited resistance factors between 0.7 and 1.0 in A2780cisR, similar to RAS complexes 3–6.(41) This was in contrast to the cross-resistance exhibited by platinum anticancer drugs such as carboplatin or platinum(II)–oxalato complexes,(41, 42) suggesting that the resistance mechanisms induced by platinum anticancer drugs do not impact the biological activity of ruthenium compounds significantly.
    We further evaluated the lead compounds against colorectal carcinoma cell lines known to be resistant to treatment by cisplatin. This intrinsic resistance to cisplatin had previously been linked to overexpression of organic cation transporter (OCT) proteins in colorectal carcinoma cells. On the other hand, oxaliplatin, by virtue of its hydrophobic R,R-diaminocyclohexane motif (DACH), can undergo aquation to yield cationic species that were postulated to be OCT1/2 substrates.(43) As RAS complexes are persistently cationic and contained hydrophobic ligands, they could potentially be substrates of OCT1/2 and effective against colorectal carcinoma cell lines. 1–6 were tested against two such cell lines, HCT116 and SW480, and their activity was compared against that of oxaliplatin (Figure 9).
    With the exception of 6, RAS complexes were more cytotoxic toward HCT116 than SW480. 3 and 5 demonstrated modest toxicity toward HCT116 but displayed poor IC50 values above 20 μM in SW480. 4 displayed good cytotoxicity in both cell lines, achieving IC50 of 1.0 μM in HCT116 and 7.3 μM in SW480, compared to oxaliplatin, which was equally efficacious against both cell lines. Nevertheless, 4 demonstrated greater efficacies toward HCT116 compared to oxaliplatin.

    Cell Cycle Analysis and Apoptosis Assay

    The ability of 4 to cause cell cycle arrest and apoptosis was investigated. HCT116 cells were treated with 4 for 24 h at 2.5 μM and 10 μM. The cell cycle profiles of treated cells stained with propidium iodide (PI) were obtained by flow cytometry analysis and compared with that of an untreated control. Significant cell growth arrest at the G2/M phase was induced by 4 only at the higher concentration (Figure S5, SI). However, at 2.5 μM a modest G0/G1 growth arrest was observed. Flow cytometry analysis using annexin V–EGFP/PI apoptosis detection assay revealed that 4 induced apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 10). The percentage of total apoptotic cells increased from 14% to 50% when concentration increased from 2.5 to 10 μM. The percentage of nercotic cells remains low at both concentrations (<5.0%).

    Mechanistic Considerations: RAS Complexes Exhibit p53-Independent Cytotoxicity

    p53 is a key tumor suppressor gene often mutated in human cancers and is involved in the mediation of cellular DNA repair, apoptosis induction, and cell cycle arrest.(44) p53 activation also mediates the neurotoxicity of anticancer drugs.(45) The chemoresistance of some cancer cell lines toward certain anticancer drugs such as oxaliplatin or doxorubicin (DOX) have been shown to be due to mutated p53-encoding genes or deficient p53 levels, which are less effective in inducing apoptosis even when significant DNA damage has occurred.(46, 47) Anticancer agents that are p53-independent could be promising drug candidates against drug-resistant cancers. However, previous reports indicated that several ruthenium complexes of various structure, such as RAPTA-C, RM175, and RDC11 (Figure 1), induced p53 and p53 target genes at various degrees.(48-50) To investigate if lead RAS complexes inhibit cell viability via a p53-dependent or -independent mechanism, we determined the expression of p53 in HCT116 cells and AGS human adenocarcinoma cells. They were treated with varying concentration of 3–5 and their p53 levels were compared with that of cells treated with either oxaliplatin or cisplatin as controls. In addition, we measured the p53 expression in HCT116 cells after different exposure times (2, 6, 12, and 24 h) to 4 and oxaliplatin. We also tested the p53-dependence of 4 by measuring its cytotoxicity against wild-type human foreskin cells BJwt and the p53 knockdown BJshp53.(51) As a control, DOX was also tested against both cell lines, and the results were compared.
    As expected, time- and concentration-dependent upregulation of p53 was observed in cells treated with oxaliplatin (Figure 11a). In contrast, p53 expression in HCT116 cells remained at basal levels, regardless of the duration or concentration of exposure to 4. HCT116 cells did not show any increase in expression of p53 even at concentrations much higher than their IC50 values after 24 h of treatment (Figure S4, SI). These results were confirmed in the gastric cancer cell line AGS, in which cisplatin treatment induced p53 protein levels while 3–5 had no significant effect. Furthermore, 4 demonstrated similar efficacies in both BJwt and BJshp53, while DOX was 7 times more cytotoxic against BJwt than p53-deficient BJshp53 (Figure 11b–d). Taken together, these results suggest that 3–5 exert their antiproliferation activity via a p53-independent mechanism, unlike RAPTA-C, RM175, or RDC11. Recent studies by Sadler and co-workers on a class of Ru–arene complexes bearing similar structural features also demonstrated a p53-independent mechanism of action.(15) Intriguingly, they reported that the halide ligand affected p53-dependence and that p53-independent activity was only observed for iodido complexes and not for chlorido analogues. Our preliminary results were dissimilar and more extensive studies involving the several target genes of p53 are being performed to validate these findings.
    Previous studies have shown that cytotoxic Ru–arene complexes that target DNA are activated by aquation(52-54) in a similar fashion to cisplatin. Hydrolysis of the chloride ligands yields a reactive aqua species that binds DNA, forming metal–DNA adducts that directly interfere with cell proliferation. An extensive study was carried out on the mechanism of aquation of RM175 and related compounds and showed that it was strongly correlated to their cytotoxicity; highly cytotoxic compounds undergo aquation, while the inactive compounds were resistant to aquation. In contrast, RAS complexes did not exhibit a similar trend, suggesting that DNA may not be the molecular target. RAS compounds 3–5 displayed good cytotoxicity toward the examined panel of cancer cells, even though they are resistant to aquation. On the other hand, the ability to undergo aquation did not confer improved cytotoxic efficacies to 6. To further rule out aquation and subsequent DNA binding as the activation mechanism, we incubated lead complex 4 with DNA nucleoside dGMP and biological nucleophile GSH and monitored its UV–vis profile over a 24 h period. No change in its UV–vis profile was observed in the presence of either dGMP (2 molar equiv) or GSH (10 mM). Furthermore, no new adducts were observed in ESI-MS, indicating that 4 did not interact with the model DNA base and that the chloride ligand on 4 was stable toward nucleophilic substitution (see SI). Coupled with earlier results indicating that 4 operated via a p53-independent pathway, we hypothesized that DNA may not be a target of our RAS complexes, unlike other DNA-binding Ru agents such as RM175.

    Conclusion

    A one-pot combinatorial metal three-component reaction was validated as a methodology to prepare RAS complexes in a large-scale format. The aqueous reaction conditions for compound synthesis directly selects for complexes that exhibit water stability and solubility. Screening of these complexes against A2780 cells led to the identification of several lead compounds, most of which displaying low micromolar IC50 values in the inhibition of cell viability against the tested panel of cancer cell lines. Further investigations revealed RAS complex 4 to be highly efficacious and induced apoptosis via a p53-independent mechanism, suggesting that DNA was not its primary biological target. This results hold promise for treatment of cancers that are drug-resistant due to their dysfunctional p53 status.

    General Procedure for the Synthesis of RAS Complexes

    A 8.16 μmol portion of A (Ru–arene dimer), 16.3 μmol of B (picolinaldehyde analogues), and 16.3 μmol of C (anilines derivatives) were added in one portion and suspended in 1 mL of D2O in a 2 mL graduated microtube with vigorous shaking at rt for 36 h. The sparingly soluble reactants dissolved at the end of the reaction, giving colored solutions. This procedure was repeated with different combinations of the three components. Not all combinations were attempted and some of the attempted combinations gave insoluble suspensions, which were excluded from the library. An initial library of 48 RAS complexes was synthesized, and they were characterized immediately by 1H NMR and ESI-MS without any workup or purification steps.     Inhibition of Cell Viability Assay (Single Concentration Screen)

    The antiproliferation activity of the 442 library of RAS complexes on exponentially growing cancer cells was screened using a MTT assay modified from one described previously.(34) A2780 cells were seeded at 8000 cells/100 μL per well in Cellstar 96-well plates (Greiner Bio-One) and incubated for 24 h. PBS buffer (200 μL) was added to the wells at the perimeter to prevent evaporation of the media from the enclosed wells. The complex solutions used for the exposure were made by serial dilution of the synthesized RAS complexes with RPMI 1640 medium, resulting in a final concentration of 25 μM with 0.5% (v/v) DMSO.
