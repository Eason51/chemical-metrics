    Abstract

    The family of Polo-like kinases is important in the regulation of mitotic progression; this work keys on one member, namely Polo-like kinase 4 (PLK4). PLK4 has been identified as a candidate anticancer target which prompted a search for potent and selective inhibitors of PLK4. The body of the paper describes lead generation and optimization work which yielded nanomolar PLK4 inhibitors. Lead generation began with directed virtual screening, using a ligand-based focused library and a PLK4 homology model. Validated hits were used as starting points for the design and discovery of PLK4 inhibitors of novel structure, namely (E)-3-((1H-indazol-6-yl)methylene)indolin-2-ones. Computational models, based on a published X-ray structure (PLK4 kinase domain), were used to understand and optimize the in vitro activity of the series; potent antiproliferative activity was obtained. The kinase selectivity profile and cell cycle analysis of selected inhibitors are described. The results of a xenograft study with an optimized compound 50 (designated CFI-400437) support the potential of these novel PLK4 inhibitors for cancer therapy.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A number of therapeutic agents used in the treatment of cancer target tubulin assembly during mitosis.(1) These include the tubulin destabilizers, such as vinca alkaloids, and newer agents, such as the taxols, which stabilize microtubules. These successes have encouraged research into nonstructural mechanisms of inducing aberrant mitosis in tumor cells. As our understanding of mitosis has matured and the discrete morphological processes have been linked to biochemical reactions, attention has focused on the mitotic kinesins(2) and kinases.(3) The coordinated action of the mitotic kinases from the cyclin-dependent kinase(4) (CDK), Aurora kinase(5) (AURK), and Polo-like kinase(6) (PLK) families is required for normal mitosis. A priori, any one of the members of the mitotic kinases can be considered as a legitimate target for therapeutic intervention.(7) Much attention has focused on the Aurora kinase family in recent years, in particular Auroras A and B. Pan Aurora inhibitors have been described as well as compounds selective for one or other of this pair; a number of these agents have entered the clinic.(8)
    PLKs are a conserved family of serine/threonine kinases that function at the intersection of mitosis and DNA damage pathways and are often deregulated in cancer.(9, 10) Mammalian cells contain five proteins: PLK1, Snk/PLK2, Prk/Fnk/PLK3, Sak/PLK4, and PLK5, of which PLK1 is the most extensively characterized. A number of PLK1 inhibitors have been reported, and work has progressed to the stage that some have been taken into the clinic.(11) By contrast, PLK4 has not, as yet, received much attention; this enzyme is structurally the most distinct member of the family.(12) Unlike PLK1, 2, and 3, PLK4 has only one polo-box and an active site with high homology to the Aurora kinases. PLK4 localizes to the centriole and is essential for controlling centriole duplication and mitotic progression.(13-17) It is a low abundance protein in normal adult tissues and autoregulates its own stability.(18-22)
    Overexpression of PLK4 causes centriole overduplication,(13-16) which directly results in chromosome segregation errors(23) that may lead to the evolution of more malignant phenotypes. We found that PLK4 depletion caused the death of breast cancer cell lines but not of normal breast cells.(24, 25) Examination of microarray data sets demonstrated that PLK4 is up regulated in breast tumors and specifically the triple-negative/basal-like (TNBC) subtype and that high PLK4 levels in breast tumors are associated with poor survival.(26-28) Together, these observations identified PLK4 as a potential target for the development of therapeutics for cancer, and we validated this hypothesis through a series of in vitro and in vivo experiments. RNAi-mediated depletion of PLK4 in breast cancer cells was found to prevent centriole duplication, causing mitotic defects and cell death and growth suppression of breast cancer xenografts in vivo.(24, 25)
    The potential importance of this enzyme in the development of drugs acting on the human kinome was underscored by Johnsonâs publication,(29) which screened a number of known kinase inhibitors against the PLK family. A striking result was the propensity of this collection to inhibit PLK4, and the rest of the Polo-like kinases were more or less resistant. Three compounds in particular were potent inhibitors of PLK4, namely 1, 2, and 3, which have been developed as an antiangiogenic, an antimitotic agent, and PKC inhibitor, respectively (Chart 1). This result raises the question of the potential mechanistic importance of PLK4 inhibition when interpreting the in vitro and in vivo effects of these molecules. For example, a multikinase inhibitor 4 of very different structure has been shown to be potent against PLK4,(30) and the authors have speculated that the antimitotic effect of this compound may be due, at least in part, to this activity. Isolated reports on PLK4 inhibitors have appeared in the patent literature; compounds 5 and 6 are representative of the macrocyclic inhibitors described by Janssen Pharmaceuticals.(31, 32) These compounds are relatively selective for PLK4 vis a vis other members of the PLK family and inhibit cancer cell growth, but little is known about their general properties or in vivo activity.
    The discovery of potent and selective PLK4 inhibitors would help to further understanding of the role of this enzyme in cancer cell proliferation. Our efforts in the identification of novel PLK4 inhibitors began with a focused screening approach described herein. One series that emerged from our compound screens were the indolinones, a well described class of ATP-competitive kinase inhibitors. These compounds offered several advantages for lead series generation including acceptable ligand efficiencies, a testable binding hypothesis, and ready synthetic accessibility. Given the relatively large number of commercial indolinones available, we had the additional advantage of generating SAR with little to no synthetic effort. The results of our lead generation and subsequent optimization efforts have been preliminarily disclosed(33) and are fully described in the body of this paper. The initial synthetic work was driven by several goals, namely a desire to identify new chemotypes for subsequent elaboration, verifying binding hypotheses, and ultimately the identification of drug like inhibitors.

    Lead Finding

    As a starting point in our lead-finding effort, we built a focused kinase library using two different approaches: (1) a ligand-based library of â¼6000 potential kinase inhibitors using molecular fingerprint consensus scoring(34) and (2) a target-based virtual screening set of â¼1700 molecules identified through an in-house PLK4 homology model. This homology model was built following a well described method(35) using multiple stages of tethered refinement. This process is highly conservative; it ensures that initial clashes are resolved and do not lead to sudden large structural changes during molecular dynamics. The homology model generated in this manner was validated by docking available PLK4 ligands and inspecting the consistency of their binding modes. When the first X-ray structure of PLK4 became available during the course of this work (PDB code: 3COK), an overlay of the PLK4 homology model to this structure was performed. This exercise revealed that the two were in good agreement, with the heavy atom RMS distance of the two active site structures being a mere 0.8 Ã. Another retrospective validation of the homology model was obtained using GOLD(36) and Glide XP(37) docking: the binding modes of the PLK4 ligands, when docked into the homology model and the X-ray structure, were identical. This model also provided an understanding of the interactions between screening hits and the kinase active site and was used in subsequent inhibitor design.
    The focused kinase libraries generated above were augmented with a commercial collection of â¼280 known kinase inhibitors. The merged compound collection was screened against PLK4 using an indirect ELISA assay. Compounds inhibiting >75% at the screening dose of 10 Î¼M were defined as hits and subjected to a validation exercise. Hits were clustered into chemical series using the SAR report within MOE; IC50s were determined for selected inhibitors and their chemical integrity checked by LCMS. Representative compounds from series of further interest were subjected to kinetic analysis in the biochemical assay, and their potential binding modes were determined in a PLK4 homology model.
    Of the several series identified by the focused screening exercise, the 4-hydroxybenzylideneindolin-2-ones held the most promise. The indolinone hits were present in all three sources of molecules, i.e., the ligand and homology model-based libraries as well as the known kinase inhibitors. A kinetic characterization of a representative 4-hydroxybenzylideneindolin-2-one 7 indicated that this compound was an ATP competitive inhibitor of PLK4 (Ki = 0.7 Î¼M). The initial screening hits were augmented by the acquisition of additional commercially available indolinones. SAR relationships (for PLK4 and PLK1) could be discerned; the key points are illustrated in Table 1. It appears that the indolinone NH is important for activity against both PLK4 and PLK1 (compounds 8 vs 9), as is the phenolic hydroxyl (compound 10). A flat SAR was observed for PLK1 and PLK4 with respect to changes in substitution patterns of the phenolic ring (11â13); PLK4 was tolerant of an additional hydroxyl on the ring, whereas PLK1 was not (compound 14).

    Inhibitor Design

    Compound 11 was used as the starting point for new inhibitor design. Indolinones of this sort exist as Z or E isomers,(38) and an equilibrium mixture, generally 1:1, is attained in aqueous solution. It was not possible to unequivocally determine the favored binding configuration of the 4-hydroxybenzylideneindolin-2-ones, however, our working model (Figure 1) was consistent with preferential binding of the Z geometric isomer. The essential features of the SAR were in agreement with our PLK4 binding model (Figure 1, left panel), which ascribed a role for the indolinone amido function (i.e., hydrogen bonding to Glu90 and Cys92 of the hinge region) and the phenolic hydroxyl (weak interaction with His93). Our design idea was to morph the phenol into bioisosteric heterocycles 15â17 (Figure 1, right panel), which could retain the H-bonding capability of the parent. A model of triazole 15 bound into the active site of PLK4 overlays well with the binding mode of compound 11 (Figure 1, left panel). These new compounds embody additional improvements in that the undesirable phenol is replaced and they represent unexplored IP space. The synthesis of these molecules proved to be straightforward (Scheme 1), and it was possible to further derivatize this core in the pursuit of more potent congeners (vida infra).

    Chemical Synthesis

    Triazole 15, (1H-indazol-5-yl-methylene)indolin-2-one 16, and the simple (1H-indazol-6-yl-methylene)indolin-2-ones 17 and 20â27 can be readily obtained by a one-step Knoevenagel condensation between the requisite aldehydes 18 and indolinones 19 (Scheme 1). In all instances, the E geometric isomer predominates; occasionally a significant amount of Z is also present. For compounds 20 and 21, the stereoisomers were separated chromatographically. Stereoisomeric (arylmethylene)indolin-2-ones can be characterized by their 3J(CâO, H) couplings, measured through proton-detected heteronuclear experiments such as HMBC.(39) Excitation-sculptured indirect-detection experiment (EXSIDE) NMR can also be employed to determine long-range CH coupling constant measurements;(40) this technique was used to determine the 3J(CâO, H) couplings for 20 and 21 as 6.4 and 13.2 Hz, respectively. Alternatively, and more generally, the Z-isomer was identified by a diagnostic peak for proton 7 of the indazole ring at 8.9â9.0 ppm. The downfield shift of proton 7 is attributed to the proximity of the carbonyl of the indolinone in the Z-isomer;(41) this deshielding effect is not observed in the E-isomer and hence the chemical shift of indazole proton 7 in this configuration is observed upfield of 8.0 ppm.
    Several pathways (Scheme 2) were employed to synthesize compounds with aryl vinyl substitution on the indazole ring. For example, 3-iodo-1H-indazole-6-carbaldehyde 28 was protected with a SEM group to give a mixture of regioisomers 29 that could be used in subsequent Heck couplings without prior separation. The protected aryl vinyl indazoles 30 were subjected to Knoevenagel condensation with indolinones 31 to yield intermediates 32. Deprotection of the SEM group was effected by various treatments (e.g., Lewis acid; HCl or TBAF/THF) to yield the desired compounds 41â45 and 48â50. In a further variation of this route, deprotection of intermediate 30 to 33 (Ar = pyridin-2-yl, Ph and p-(Me2NCH2)Ph) preceded the final condensation to give inhibitors 37, 46, and 47 respectively.
    An alternate route began with HornerâWadsworthâEmmons olefination of aldehyde 34 to give intermediates 35. In this procedure, subsequent reduction of the pendant nitrile yielded aldehydes 33. The standard Knoevenagel condensation gave final products 38â40. In the case of inhibitor 51 (Ar = 4-carboxyphenyl), the desired carboxylic acid 36 was revealed by base hydrolysis of the precursor ester prior to the Knoevenagel condensation.

    These encouraging results were tempered by the observation that the discernible SAR appeared to be at odds with our working models on several points. First, the predominance of the E isomer negates any possible interaction between His93 and the heterocyclic H-bond donor. Yet, in a head to head comparison, the E isomer, compound 20, was somewhat more potent than the Z isomer, 21. The double bond of (arylmethylene)indolin-2-ones has the potential to isomerize in aqueous and alcoholic media,(38, 42) and we have observed the conversion of homogeneous compound 20 to a 3:2 E/Z mixture within 6 h in DMSO-d6 at rt. Despite the potential for isomeric lability in solution, it appears that the geometric configuration of the starting solid informs in vitro results. This is consistent with the observation that no isomerization occurs upon incubation of compound 20 under the buffer condition mimicking the PLK4 kinase assay.
    H-Bond acceptors/donors at position 5 and 6 of the indolinone resulted in a loss of activity (compounds 25â27), whereas hydrophobes and halogens were favored at the 5 position (compounds 20, 22, and 24). These observations were also inconsistent with the original binding hypothesis. To begin to understand the nature of the binding of the 3-((1H-indazol-6-yl)methylene)indolin-2-ones, a kinetic analysis of compound 17 was performed (Supporting Information). This study showed that compound 17 displays kinetics consistent with direct competition for ATP binding to the active site of PLK4 with an apparent Ki of 22 nM. These results, coupled with an understanding that indazoles are capable of making hinge region interactions in their own right, prompted a reexamination of the binding models. The two possibilities are illustrated in Figure 2: the left panel shows the indolinone making two H-bonds to the hinge region (Glu90 and Cys92); the right panel shows the indazole interacting with the hinge region and also ascribes a role for the indolinone, i.e., H-bond to Asp154. Using the homology model, it was not possible to distinguish between these two modes by computational docking alone.
    The publication of Johnsonâs seminal work on the inhibition of the PLK family by kinase inhibitors(29) suggested a way to test the binding model corresponding to Figure 2, right panel. This work reported that Axitinib, an RTK inhibitor and antiangiogenic agent, was also a potent inhibitor of PLK4 (IC50 = 46 nM in our hands) and it docked and scored highly in our binding model. Overlay of Axitinib (an indazole with putative hinge region interactions) with our own inhibitors generates a hybrid target molecule, namely 37. This pyridin-2-yl vinyl analogue was docked into the 3COK model using GlideXP and was predicted to potently inhibit PLK4 (Figure 3, left panel). The 3COK model, generated from the PLK4 X-ray structure, was optimized to improve quantitative predictivity as measured by the correlation of docking scores and pIC50 values (see Computational Methods in the Experimental Section). Whereas the position of the indazole and indolinone groups are unchanged from the parent 17 (i.e., the H-bonds to the hinge are retained), the vinyl linker places the pyridyl group in a good position for hydrophobic interactions (e.g., with Leu18). In the event, compound 37 inhibited PLK4 with an IC50 of 13 nM (Table 3). This 20-fold improvement in activity is in good agreement with the log unit increase predicted by the model. It should be noted that the PLK4 IC50 of the styryl compound 46 suffers in comparison to 37 by 3-fold but still represents a significant improvement over the parent, 17.

    This result prompted the synthesis of the other pyridine positional isomers. The pyridin-(3 and 4)-yl isomers 38 and 41, respectively, were more active than the pyridin-2-yl isomer (Table 3). Presumably, the polar nitrogen is more readily accommodated in the aqueous milieu at the protein surface. An additional increase in potency was realized by incorporation of the 5-methoxy substitution on the indolinone ring (compounds 40 and 44); alkyl groups (39, 43), chlorine (42) and hydroxy (45) were somewhat less effective. Once again, our model correctly predicted the improvement observed upon substitution of a methoxy group at the 5-position of the indolinone. However, the measured IC50s for inhibitors 40 and 44 must be viewed as apparent values because they approach the nominal concentration of enzyme in the assay, i.e., 0.4 nM. Figure 3, right panel, displays compound 44 docked into the active site of the PLK4 enzyme. Apart from the usual hinge-binding to the backbones of Glu90 and Cys92, there is also a hydrogen bond to the side chain of Asp154. There is extensive hydrophobic contact between the enzyme and the ligand, especially at the aromatic rings and the methoxy group which nestles in a groove bounded by the protein backbone and the side chains of Leu143 and Glu96. In contrast, the Z-isomers of the aryl vinyl indazoles given in Table 3 generally did not dock into the PLK4 active site and those that did had docking scores up to 3 log units lower than the E-isomers.
    Compounds 37â45 represented a significant advance; however, their potent PLK4 inhibition did not result in a uniformly potent antiproliferative effect on breast cancer cell lines. As a group, these compounds had variable effects on cell growth inhibition; GI50s were in the submicromolar range for the MCF-7 and MDA-MB-468 cell lines, only marginally better than Axitinib. A correlation between PLK4 potency and GI50s was discernible in the MCF-7 and MDA-MB-468 cell lines, but the MDA-MB-231, SKBr-3, and HCC-1954 lines appeared to be less responsive. The effects of the potent PLK4 inhibitor 41 on cell cycling of MDA-MB-468 cells are shown in Figure 4. Concentrations of 41 at 0.5 Î¼M and above cause mitotic checkpoint loss, polyploidy and death, underscored by the accumulation of 8N and 16N peaks.
    Another drawback to the pyridinyl inhibitors is captured in Table 4. Compound 41, for example, demonstrated significant inhibitory effects on CYP450s 2C9 and 2C19, which is ascribed to the aromatic ring nitrogen. With a view to attenuating this activity, and understanding that biochemical potency does not always translate into cellular activity, the physicochemical properties of the PLK4 inhibitors were calculated. Data on representative compounds is given in Table 4. The physicochemical properties of the series were modified by incorporation of water-solubilizing substituents on the phenyl ring distal from the indazole core. The beneficial effects of these substitutions on solubility are captured by the ACD calculated values given in Table 4 and that this is achieved without sacrificing lipophilicity is reflected by the calculated log P. Molecular modeling indicated that this region of the inhibitor was projected into solvent and would have no deleterious effect on PLK4 potency (see Figure 3, right panel). For example, the addition of tertiary amines at this position yielded potent PLK4 inhibitors 47â50, moreover, their inhibition of cell growth was significantly better than pyridinyl compounds 37â45 (Table 3). In contrast, appending a carboxylic acid function at the para position of the phenyl ring yielded a subnanomolar inhibitor 51, however, a decrease in cellular activity was observed. Once again, not all breast cancer cell lines were responsive to compounds 47â50 to the same degree. MCF-7, MDA-MB-468, and MDA-MB-231 had GI50s in the double-digit nanomolar range; growth inhibition of SKBR-3 and HCC-1954 was observed only at micromolar concentrations. No relationship was observed between response to inhibitors and PLK4 mRNA levels, which are comparable across the cell lines tested (see Experimental Section). A lack of correlation between target expression and responsiveness of cancer cells to pharmacologic inhibition of the target has been reported for other mitotic kinases.(43) Gratifyingly, compounds 47 and 50 demonstrated not only enhanced growth inhibitory activity but also attenuated CYP450 inhibition (Table 4).

    Across a broad range of chemotypes and biochemical activities, we were able to show a correlation between the measured GI50 in MDA-MB-468 cells and pGI50 calcd (Figure 4). QSAR analysis established that calculated pGI50 was best described by three independent variables, namely PLK4 pIC50, molecular volume, and log P, with an R2 of 0.52 and RMSE of 0.62. The level of variance observed indicates that additional unrecognized factors may be important. According to the predictive equation given in the Computational Methods, PLK4 inhibition was the most important contributor. However, it is expected that the ability of a PLK4 inhibitor to cross cellular membranes (as reflected by its log P) would factor into antiproliferative activity.
    To further illustrate this point, PAMPA values were obtained on representative compounds given in Table 4. All six are potent PLK4 inhibitors; however, the compounds with low permeability (hydroxy substituted indolinone 45 and carboxylate 51) showed correspondingly poor activity in the cell growth assays. Pyridine 41 is 2 orders of magnitude more permeable, but this did not translate into potent cell growth inhibition. Some of the most potent inhibitors of breast cancer cell growth, tertiary amines 47, 49, and 50, also had high PAMPA values; it would appear that permeability and potent PLK4 inhibition are necessary but not sufficient for robust cell activity (Figure 5).

    Kinase Selectivity

    The PLK4 inhibitor, 50 and was profiled against a panel of 274 protein kinases at a screening concentration of 1 Î¼M. This data is presented as a heat map (Figure 6) which indicates that compound 50 displayed better selectivity than Sunitinib and somewhat less than Axitinib, two ATP competitive kinase inhibitors used clinically. Of the 274 kinases tested, 30 were inhibited at greater than 90%, at the screening concentration of 1 Î¼M (Supporting Information). For a subset of the more potently inhibited kinases of interest, IC50s were determined using in-house assays. Compound 50 inhibited Aurora A, Aurora B, KDR, and FLT-3, with IC50s of 0.37, 0.21, 0.48, and 0.18 Î¼M, respectively, i.e., 2 orders of magnitude higher than its IC50 of 0.6 nM against PLK4.

    In Vivo Efficacy

    Given its potent antiproliferative activity against the MDA-MB-468 breast cancer cell line, compound 50 was selected for testing in the corresponding mouse xenograft model. In preparation for this in vivo experiment, we determined the plasma levels of compound 50 in mice upon IP dosing at 50 mg/kg; a Cmax of 92 ng/mL and AUC of 190 ngÂ·h/mL were observed. The mouse plasma protein binding measurement for compound 50 is 99%, i.e., unbound compound in plasma is 1%.     PLK4, identified by an in-house siRNA screen and supporting validation efforts as a tractable, novel target for drug discovery, was subjected to a lead generation effort. A two pronged focused screening campaign identified 3-(hydroxybenzylidene)indolin-2-one hits for subsequent validation work. Bioisosteric replacement of the phenolic function of the validated hits resulted in the identification of (E)-3-((1H-indazol-6-yl)methylene)indolin-2-ones as a novel class of kinase inhibitors. Systematic optimization of this series yielded inhibitors with nanomolar activity against PLK4 and concomitant antiproliferative effects against a panel of breast cancer cell lines. Models of the inhibitor series in a 3COK derived structure help explain and predict the observed PLK4 activity.
    One inhibitor was selected for detailed study; compound 50 (designated CFI-400437) is potent against PLK4 (IC50 = 0.6 nM) and selective against other members of the PLK family (>10 Î¼M). Against a broad panel of kinases, only a small subset is significantly inhibited. However, like other ATP competitive kinase inhibitors, the cell activity and in vivo effects seen with 50 cannot be ascribed solely to the inhibition of PLK4. Compound 50 is a potent inhibitor of MCF-7, MDA-MB-468, and MDA-MB-231 cell growth and very effective in a mouse xenograft model of tumor growth. The data reported herein on this new class of antimitotic agents, culminating in the in vivo efficacy for compound 50, illustrates the potential of these novel PLK4 inhibitors for cancer therapy. Further optimization work toward the identification of a development candidate will be disclosed in due course.

    Computational Methods

    Most of the computational processes, including ligand preparation, ligand-based virtual screening, homology model building, and QSAR-type analyses were performed using the MOE software.(44)
    A focused kinase library was generated using a ligand-based virtual screening approach(34, 45) with the aim of identifying a diverse set of readily available compounds with reasonable physicochemical properties that are âkinase inhibitor-likeâ. This approach began with selecting a training set of 379 diverse compounds that have better than 100 nM activities in different protein kinase assays, drawn from a collection of â¼2700 known kinase inhibitors in the Wombat database.(46, 47) This training set was utilized in molecular fingerprint based consensus scoring using the MACCSF, TGTFO, and MP61 fingerprints.(45) The screened data set comprised of â¼3 million commercially available compounds, put together from 12 leading compound vendors. The initial hit-set of â¼50000 compounds was obtained by selecting the top scoring â¼30000 hits each from the MACCSF and TGTFO hit lists as well as the consensus list obtained using the sum rank of all three. This set further was reduced utilizing property filters (250 < MW < 600, number of H-bond donors <5, number of H-bond acceptors <10, number of chiral centers <5, total number of rotatable bonds <11, and total number of contiguous rotatable bonds <7, as well as a substructure-based filter to remove reactive molecules, based on the MOE descriptor âreactiveâ with some in-house modifications). The remaining compounds were clustered using the MACCS fingerprint into â¼6500 clusters using the top scoring hits from each, and then some compounds were removed after manual selection, yielding â¼6000 compounds, purchased from commercial sources.
    A homology model of the PLK4 kinase domain was generated. The PLK4 homology model was built using the X-ray structure of Aurora-A cocrystallized with PHA-680632(48) (PDB code: 2BMC). This structure has 37% residue identity in the kinase domain with PLK4, using the blosum35 substitution matrix. Primary sequence alignment was followed by threading the PLK4 residues to the Aurora-A structure, fully preserving the main chain secondary structure (i.e., no minimizations were performed on the intermediate and final structures). The solution with the best packing score was selected. After this, a three-stage refinement process was performed using tethered molecular dynamics (MD) and energy minimizations using the MMFFx forcefield in MOE. These tethers were added to all dihedral connections within the protein structure in order to prevent significant perturbations and movements in the positions of heavy atoms. First, strong dihedral tethers were used (for the backbone a force constant of 100 kcal/mol/deg, applied on torsional angle changes over 0.2Â°, whereas for the side chains and ligand they were 10 kcal/mol/deg and 0.5Â°, respectively), then somewhat weaker ones (backbone 10 kcal/mol/deg and 0.5Â°, side chains and ligand 1 kcal/mol/deg and 1Â°, respectively), and finally no tethers at all.
