    Abstract

    Human hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a serious public health threat with high infection rates in children and infants who reside in Asia and the Pacific regions, and no effective drugs are currently available. Enterovirus 71 (EV71) and coxsackievirus A16 are the major etiological pathogens. Based on an essential hydrophobic pocket on the viral capsid protein VP1, we designed and synthesized a series of small molecular weight compounds as inhibitors of EV71. A potential drug candidate named NLD-22 exhibited excellent antiviral activity (with an EC50 of 5.056 nM and a 100% protection rate for mice at a dose of 20 mg/kg) and low toxicity. NLD-22 had a favorable pharmacokinetic profile. High-resolution cryo-electron microscopy structural analysis confirmed NLD-22 bound to the hydrophobic pocket in VP1 to block viral infection. In general, NLD-22 was indicated to be a promising potential drug candidate for the treatment of HFMD.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a highly infectious disease that mainly affects children or immunocompromised adults.(1) Infections usually result in mild symptoms such as fever and vesicular rash on the hands and feet and in the oral cavity.(2) However, in some cases in the AsiaâPacific region, infection can result in neurological and cardiovascular disorders and can be fatal to children.(3) Enterovirus 71 (EV71) and coxsackievirus A16 (CVA16) are the predominant causative agents,(4) with more than 70% of patients exhibiting severe symptoms; additionally, 90% of all fatalities are due to EV71.(5â7) A vaccine to prevent EV71 has been developed, and the incidence of HFMD caused by EV71 has been reduced dramatically.(8) However, the vaccine has no treatment effect on infections. Thus, it is urgent to develop new antiviral drugs to treat EV71 infections.
    EV71 belongs to the Picornaviridae family, and their genes encode 60 copies of the VP1, VP2, VP3, and VP4 proteins to form viral icosahedral capsids.(9) VP1 is a structural protein and an important target for the development of antiviral drugs.(10) It plays a key role in the release of RNA when the virus is attached to a host cell.(11) Based on a detailed study of the structure of VP1, we found a hydrophobic cavity that could be used for drug discovery. Structural analysis and in vitro assays confirmed the predicted binding modes of inhibitors and their ability to block the viral infection. Our approach may be useful in the design of effective drugs for EV71 infections. The hydrophobic âpocket factorâ is a natural lipid sphingosine that is buried in a pocket lying at the base of the gap in the capsid protein VP1.(8,12) Expulsion of this natural molecule after binding of the virus to its receptor triggers the opening of the capsid to facilitate genome release. As previously reported, BPROZ compounds belonging to the pyridyl imidazolidinone group showed good inhibitory activity.(13,14) Therefore, based on previous studies,(15,16) we designed and synthesized a series of small molecular weight hydrophobic compounds that can bind to this cavity to inhibit virus uncoating. Among these compounds, we found that a compound named NLD could effectively protect cells from EV71 infection in vitro (with an EC50 of 25 pM) (Figure 1).(8) The structure of NLD contains an amino group in the pyridine ring, which forms hydrogen bonds with amino acid residues. This interaction makes the molecular combination tighter and strengthens the inhibitory activity. However, in the subsequent in vivo safety evaluation study, NLD showed less than optimal toxic effect and result in a relatively narrow therapeutic index. Therefore, in order to improve the therapeutic index, further investigations were carried out based on this structure, and a new series of NLD derivatives were synthesized and subjected to biological assessment.
    During the preparation of our experiments and this manuscript, a study by Liâs group on EV71 inhibitors was published, which reported the potent EV71 inhibitor compound 14 derived from NLD that also targeted the viral capsid protein VP1 (Figure 2). This compound exhibited excellent antiviral activity in vitro and in vivo. However, the in vivo safety assessment data of compound 14 were quite limited. Here, we report the structureâactivity relationship (SAR) and the pharmacokinetic (PK)/pharmacodynamic (PD) profiles of NLD-22, a compound with favorable antiviral activity. In contrast to compound 14, detailed information of the in vitro and in vivo toxicity studies was also provided, and the results suggested that the NLD series of compounds might be broadly effective against HFMD.(17)

    Design and Synthesis of Imidazolidone Derivatives with Variations at the R1, R2, R3, and R4 Sites. Structural Modifications of NLD

    Pyridyl imidazolidone is the basic pharmacophore of the NLD series of compounds. This molecule consists of three parts: segment A (the pyridyl imidazolidone), segment B (the aliphatic chain), and segment C (the aromatic ring) (Figure 3). Segments A and B have been reported to play important roles in the inhibition of EV71. Based on previous studies, a series of NLD derivatives were synthesized to investigate their SAR and toxicity (Table 1).(14)

The ethoxyhydroxime of NLD was modified by a reduction reaction, and the inhibitory activity increased slightly, but this modification did not reduce the cytotoxicity (Table 1, NLD-1, EC50 = 0.0119 nM, CC50 = 7.372 Î¼M). Further modifications of the ethoxyhydroxime of NLD resulted in low toxicity, but its antiviral activity was dramatically reduced (Table 1, NLD-2 and NLD-3, EC50 > 25 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M). Most of the compounds with heterocyclic groups at R4 showed excellent activity and low cytotoxicity (Table 1, NLD-4â8), especially NLD-5 (EC50 = 0.0084 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M). Extending the alkyl chain on the heterocyclic group reduced the inhibitory activity (Table 1, NLD-6, EC50 = 0.15 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M). This was also the case when the alkyl group was substituted with a trifluoromethyl group (Table 1, NLD-8, EC50 = 0.537 nM, CC50 = 48.06 Î¼M). Therefore, a substituent on the aromatic ring (R4) with either a flexible structure, such as an O-alkyl formamidine, or a rigid cyclic structure, such as an oxadiazole ring, may be favored. Overall, the substituent on the heterocyclic ring was optimized as a methyl or ethyl group.
    We next replaced the hydrogen with a methyl group at the R3 site, which resulted in the complete loss of antiviral activity, although the cytotoxicity remained moderate (Table 1, NLD-9â13, EC50 > 25 nM). When piperazine was introduced into the structure, a similar result was obtained (Table 1, NLD-14, EC50 > 25 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M). Thus, steric hindrance between the inhibitor and VP1 is the likely cause of the loss of activity.
    If the amino group on the pyridine ring of segment A was replaced by a methoxyl or hydroxyl group, as in NLD-16 (EC50 > 25 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M), activity was lost. If the pyridine ring was replaced by an aromatic ring (Table 1, NLD-17, EC50 = 1.12 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M), good antiviral activity and low cytotoxicity could be achieved. However, if an amino substituent was added to the aromatic ring, activity was lost (Table 1, NLD-18, EC50 > 25 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M).
    NLD has an aliphatic chain as the linker. If this linker was replaced with a more rigid alkynyl chain or the number of C-atoms on the aliphatic chain was decreased, the antiviral activity was completely lost (Table 1, NLD-19 and NLD-20, EC50 > 25 nM). However, removal of the chiral center only resulted in a slight decrease in inhibitory activity (Table 1, NLD-21, EC50 = 0.072 nM, CC50 = 58.99 Î¼M). Therefore, the chiral center is not necessary for activity. Based on these results, we synthesized NLD-22 (EC50 = 5.056 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M), which was one of the most active compounds that also had a low level of cytotoxicity. Acetylation of NLD-22 to yield NLD-23 (EC50 > 25 nM, CC50 > 100 Î¼M) failed to improve the activity.

    Acute Toxicity Tests on Juvenile Zebrafish (Danio rerio)

    To further investigate the most potent derivatives, NLD-4, NLD-5, NLD-6, NLD-7, NLD-8, NLD-15, NLD-17, NLD-21, and NLD-22 were evaluated by acute toxicity tests on juvenile zebrafish. As shown in Figure 4a, the acute toxicity increased with concentration, demonstrating a dose dependency. When the juvenile zebrafish was exposed to different concentrations of these inhibitors for 24 or 48 h, NLD-22 did not cause mortality, even up to a concentration of 200 Î¼M. In contrast, NLD at 20 Î¼M caused 100% mortality after 24 h. Furthermore, several derivatives produced pericarditis (Figure 4b).

    Subacute Toxicity Study in Vivo

    Sprague-Dawley (SD) rats were selected and divided into different groups, according to their age [postnatal day (PND) 4, 7, 14 and 21, identified as PND4, PND7, PND14 and PND21, respectively]. Each group was then divided into several groups including the blank control group that was given physiological saline, a solvent control group (20% solutol in physiological saline), and different dosage groups. Animals were observed individually for any clinical signs of toxicity and mortality after oral administration for 28 days, and the body weights were recorded (Figure 5). When PND4 and PND7 rats were treated with 30 or 50 mg/kg/day NLD, all rats survived, but the lenses of the rodents became irreversibly opaque. When PND14 and PND21 rats were treated with 100 mg/kg/day NLD, the rats developed opaque lenses, quadriplegia, and hair loss. A histopathological study also confirmed damage to eye development (Figure 6). When PND4 and PND7 rats were treated with 50 mg/kg/day NLD-22, no significant behavioral changes or toxic reactions were observed in the animals. When PND14 rats were treated with 100 mg/kg/day NLD-22, their morphological characteristics (fur, skin, eyes, and nose) appeared normal. However, with increasing dosage, a few rats showed hair loss. When PND21 rats were treated with NLD-22 up to 200 mg/kg/day, no significant differences were observed in the body weight and behavioral changes between the treatment group and control group. Therefore, the safe dosage for NLD-22 in PND21 rats is greater than 200 mg/kg/day.(19)

    PDs Study in Vivo

    A BALB/c mouse infection model (PND4) was used in our study. Healthy BALB/c mice (PND4) were infected with a lethal dose (10 LD50/0.1 mL) of the EV71 strain genotype C4, which was isolated from Fuyang, Anhui Province, China, via a single intraperitoneal injection. After infection for more than 48 h, these mice showed severe complications of EV71 infection and even death.(11) Then, the infected mice were treated with NLD or NLD-22 once daily starting at 48 Â± 0.5 h after infection for 7 days by intraperitoneal injection to evaluate the efficacy. The results showed that for the mice that were given 10 mg/kg/day NLD, the protection rate was 100% (Figure 7a). After administering 20 mg/kg/day NLD-22, the protection rate of the mice was also 100%, and at a dosage of 10 mg/kg/day, the protection rate of the mice was approximately 80% (Figure 7b). The animals in the virus control group all died on day 5 because of EV71 infection.

    NLD-22 Readily Replaces the Natural Pocket Factor

    To determine whether the derived NLD-22 could replace the natural pocket factor as NLD does,(8) we incubated NLD-22 with purified EV71 for 24 h at 4 Â°C and determined the cryo-electron microscopy (EM) structure at 3.3 Ã. At this resolution, the electron density map clearly showed the binding of NLD-22 to the hydrophobic pocket in VP1. The hydrophobic interactions including an atypical sandwich interaction of three phenoxy rings contributed to the tight association, which is consistent with the NLD-VP1 binding mode. In addition, the two hydrogen bonds that were formed by the amino group on the pyridine moiety and the hydroxyl group on the imidazole ring of NLD-22 with the carboxyl oxygen of Trp203 and the amino group of Ile113 of VP1, respectively, and the ÏâÏ interactions with Phe135 and Phe155 facilitated the binding at the pocket entrance. Due to its tight binding, NLD-22 is capable of abolishing the structural rearrangement, which opens the capsid to facilitate genome release via preventing the dislodging of this molecule after binding of the virus to its receptor (Figure 8).

    Conclusions

    Jump To
    In summary, we designed, synthesized, and evaluated a series of NLD derivatives targeting the VP1 capsid protein based on our previous work. Among them, NLD-22 showed excellent antiviral activity and favorable safety, bioavailability, and PK profile. The EC50 and CC50 values from the cell-based assay revealed that NLD-22 is a potent virus capsid stabilizer and inhibitor. The SD rat liver microsome assay showed a clearance of 15.5 Î¼L/min/mg and an in vivo bioavailability of 80%. Further cryo-EM structural analysis (3.3 Ã) confirmed that NLD-22 binds to Trp203 and Ile113 of VP1 by forming hydrogen bonds to block viral infection. Taken together, NLD-22 shows a broader therapeutic index, a more acceptable PK profile and better drug-like properties than NLD. This work will facilitate the discovery of a promising drug candidate for the treatment of acute HFMD infections.
