    Abstract

    In Alzheimer’s disease, the density and spread of aggregated tau protein track well with neurodegeneration and cognitive decline, making the imaging of aggregated tau a compelling biomarker. A structure–activity relationship exploration around an isoquinoline hit, followed by an exploration of tolerated fluorination positions, allowed us to identify 9 (JNJ-64326067), a potent and selective binder to aggregated tau with a favorable pharmacokinetic profile and no apparent off-target binding. This was confirmed in rat and monkey positron emission tomography studies using [18F]9.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder and the major cause of dementia in the elderly. Postmortem studies of AD brains reveal two pathological hallmarks of the disease: (1) neuritic plaques (SPs), composed primarily of β-amyloid (Aβ) peptide aggregates, and (2) neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs), composed of paired helical filaments (PHFs) of hyperphosphorylated tau protein.(1) In contrast to Aβ-plaques, the density and neocortical spread of NFTs correlate well with progressive neuronal degeneration and cognitive impairment,(2) thus making positron emission tomography (PET) imaging of NFTs a desirable biomarker for AD, to monitor disease progression or success of therapy for tau-centric therapies.
    The development of small-molecule PET imaging radiotracers for the in vivo quantification of NFTs has been pursued by numerous academic and industrial groups,(3) and a few examples are shown in Chart 1 ([18F]1a–c, [18F]2a–b, [18F]3, and [18F]4). From all these radiotracers, [18F]1a (T807, AV-1451), discovered by Siemens and later acquired by Lilly, has been the most extensively studied in AD patients.(4,5) However, although [18F]1a is very selective over aggregated Aβ, recent reports suggest that it shows a suboptimal pharmacokinetic (PK) profile and a certain degree of high affinity off-target binding;(6−9) hence, the signal-to-background ratio in early AD patients may not be optimal. A more favorable PK was achieved with [18F]2a (T808, AV-680), but that tracer unfortunately suffered from defluorination and bone uptake in humans.(10) More recently, Roche ([18F]1b),(11) Genentech ([18F]2b),(12) and UCB ([18F]1c)(13) disclosed structurally similar tau PET tracers to [18F]1a and [18F]2a that attempt to address their limitations. Another set of tracers was disclosed by Tohoku university, with [18F]3 reported to have the most optimal PK profile,(14) but later studies showed that this compound suffered from major off-target binding, in particular to MAO-B enzymes.(15−17) Finally, Merck & Co. have disclosed [18F]4, with a favorable affinity, selectivity, and PK profile which is being evaluated in humans.(18,19)
    Recently, we reported on the identification of nonselective tau binder 5 and a medicinal chemistry optimization program that led to the discovery of 7 (JNJ-64349311),(20) a promising tau PET candidate that later was shown to have a superior preclinical PK profile to 2 (Chart 2, left arrows).(21) During the structure–activity relationship (SAR) exploration of the 2-aminopyridine moiety in 5, we discovered that while selectivity could be achieved by replacement with 4-aminopyridine, as in 6 (Chart 2, left arrows), good potency and selectivity could also be obtained when shifting the quinoline nitrogen of 5 to the quinoline 6-position (Chart 2, right arrows). The resulting isoquinoline 8 was subjected to a similar medicinal chemistry exploration as described for 7, which led to the identification of 9 (JNJ-64326067), a candidate PET ligand that is structurally differentiated from 7, has increased affinity for aggregated tau, and has an improved PK profile. Here, we report the optimization research work that led to the discovery of 9, as well as its radiolabeling with 18F and preclinical evaluation.

    Chemistry

    Jump To
    Compounds 8 and 17–21 were prepared via Buchwald–Hartwig aminations of 6-bromoisoquinoline 10 with aminopyridines 11–16, which were commercially available or obtained by straightforward and/or reported procedures (Scheme 1, route A). Compounds 27–30 were prepared via the reverse Buchwald–Hartwig coupling between 6-aminoisoquinoline 22 and 2-bromo- or 2-chloropyridines 23–26 (Scheme 1, route B).
    Compound 33 was prepared similarly via Buchwald–Hartwig coupling between 1-fluoro-6-bromoisoquinoline 32 and 12. Compound 32 was prepared by CsF-mediated fluorination of commercial 1-chloroisoquinoline 31 (Scheme 2).
    Isoquinoline 9 was prepared on the ∼30 g scale via modified Buchwald–Hartwig coupling of 10 with 2-aminopyridine 35, which in turn was prepared via Suzuki coupling of commercial 34 with methylboronic acid (Scheme 3). The radiofluorination precursor 37 was prepared via the reverse coupling of 22 with nitro-bromopyridine 36. The reactivity of 37 toward fluoride was assessed by heating with KF at 160 °C overnight in dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO), which yielded 9 in 15% yield. An important side product (12%) in this reaction is the nitro substitution product with methylmercaptane, probably resulting from DMSO.
    The radiotracer [18F]9 was subsequently synthesized by replacing the nitro group of 37 with [18F] by heating a DMSO solution of 37 with 18F-fluoride in the presence of K-222/K2CO3 at 160 °C for 10 min (Scheme 4). The product was isolated in an average, decay-corrected, radiochemical yield of 22% (SD of ±20%) (relative to total radioactivity of 18F in the preparative chromatogram), and the radiochemical purity was above 99%. The average molar activity was 75 GBq/μmol at the end of synthesis (EOS, n = 11).
    The tritiated radioligands [3H]9 and [3H]17 were prepared via direct tritiation of 9 and 17, respectively, with tritium gas in the presence of Crabtree’s catalyst (Scheme 5). The obtained molar activity was 2.14 TBq/mmol for [3H]9 and 0.78 TBq/mmol for [3H]17. For [3H]17, NMR analysis confirmed that tritiation occurred only at the 5- and 7-positions, whereas for [3H]9, only MS data were obtained, indicating tritiation at additional positions, presumably the 1- and 3-positions of the isoquinoline. The difference in the degree of labeling of 9 and 17 is likely due to the different amounts of tritium gas used in the labeling experiments.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Binding to aggregated tau extracted from humans, late stage-AD brains (Braak V-VI)(22) were assessed using filter binding assay with [3H]2a as a displacement probe as described previously.(23) Because the isoquinoline moiety was already extensively explored previously,(20) we focused our SAR search around the 2-aminopyridine ring of 8. Shifting the methyl substituent on the pyridine from the 6-position to 5-position boosted tau affinity almost sixfold whilst maintaining good selectivity versus aggregated Aβ (Table 1, compound 17). On the other hand, shifting the nitrogen to the 4-position versus the anilinic nitrogen (Table 1, compound 18) led to a fourfold loss in affinity compared to 8. This affinity was partially regained in 2-methylpyridine derivative 19.
    Table 1. SAR Exploration of Pyridine in 8
    Next, we explored potential sites to introduce a fluorine atom in 8, keeping the position of the nitrogens in both the isoquinoline and pyridine rings fixed (Table 1, 20, 21, 27–30, and 33). Compound 20, which carries fluoromethyl at the 6-position of the pyridine maintained good affinity for tau. Extending the carbon chain in 20 led to a decrease in affinity, as demonstrated in 6-fluoroethyl derivative 27. A 5-fluoromethyl derivative was not prepared, but the corresponding 5-(2-fluoroethyl) derivative 28 showed a significant increase in tau affinity and selectivity versus Aβ, similar as observed between 8 and 17 (Table 1). Finally, further extending the chain as in 2-fluoroethoxy and 3-fluoropropoxy derivatives 29 and 30 reduced selectivity versus Aβ with a modest decrease in affinity for tau compared to 28. Three aromatic fluorinated analogues of 17 were also prepared, as these could be less prone to defluorination in vivo, but also to generate brain-penetrant radiometabolites due to dealkylation compared to aliphatic-fluorinated derivatives (Table 1, 33, 21, and 9). For the introduction of fluorine, we selected positions activated by the pyridine or quinoline nitrogens. Thus, 1-fluoroisoquinoline 33 was prepared, which showed a significant reduction in tau affinity versus 17. In contrast, both 6- and 4-fluoropyridine derivatives 21 and 9 maintained the high tau affinity and selectivity versus Aβ.
    Next to high affinity and selectivity, brain penetration, a short half-life and low nonspecific binding to brain tissue are important features for a brain imaging PET tracer to obtain a good signal-to-noise ratio. In vitro measurement of physicochemical and ADME properties identified 9 as a promising candidate to progress further to in vivo evaluation. As shown in Table 2, 9 improved slightly on affinity versus our earlier lead 7, and significantly in Aβ selectivity compared to 1a, the 19F analogue of the most advanced clinical tau PET tracer in the field. In addition, 9 had physicochemical properties well within the boundaries to be central nervous system (CNS) penetrant. Moreover, like 1a and 7, 9 did not show efflux in an MDCK-MDR1 cell line. Free fraction in the plasma and brain for 9 were lower than 7 but in a comparable range to 1a. Of note is that 21, which also emerged as a promising lead based on its high tau and low Aβ affinity (Table 1), showed a 2.5-fold higher brain tissue binding than 9 (fu,b: 0.52% free), which caused it to be deselected for further progression. Finally, intrinsic clearance of 9 in rat, monkey, and human liver microsomes was very high. Of note is that 9 was found to be an aldehyde oxidase (AO) substrate (see the Supporting Information);(24) hence, the hLM and cynoLM values are underpredicted in these assays. Intrinsic clearance in rat, monkey, and human hepatocytes was confirmed to be high as well. This gave us confidence that the PK profile of 9 would translate from our preclinical rat and monkey models into humans. The higher stability in hLM of 1a versus rLM and cynoLM may be an explanation why this compound shows a suboptimal PK in humans, while it looked more promising in preclinical species.

    The occurrence of brain penetrant radiometabolites and radiodefluorination can limit the use of brain PET tracers. Therefore, we performed a metabolite identification following 120 min incubation of 10 μM 9 with rat, monkey, and human hepatocytes (Scheme 6). As expected, based on LCMS retention times, all metabolites were more polar than parent compound 9. Both oxidation of the quinoline and pyridine moieties were observed, with oxidation of the methyl group on the pyridine (9-1) and additional oxidation of the quinoline being an important metabolite in all three species and even in monkeys (9-2). Displacement of the fluorine by glutathione (GSH) was observed in rat hepatocytes and to a much lesser extent in human hepatocytes (see the Supporting Information for quantitative assessment). Bone uptake of the displaced fluorine was hence expected in rats but judged to be negligible in monkeys and humans.
    Following the promising high in vitro clearance results, a rat microdosing study with 9 [intravenous (i.v.), 0.03 mg/kg] was conducted, in which cortex, cerebellum, and plasma levels were assessed in a 60 min time course. These studies were used to extract relevant PK parameters and compare them to the earlier reported values of 7 (Table 3). First of all, 9 showed a fast clearance from the plasma of 228 mL/min/kg, resulting in an even shorter half-life of 0.08 h compared to 7 (0.31 h). In addition, 9 showed good brain exposure given the low administered dose (AUC cortex = 8.72 ng·h/mL) in line with its Vd,ss of 1.6 L/kg, and a high total cortex to plasma ratio (Kp = 4), giving an estimated Kp,uu of ∼1, as expected from the MDCK-MDR1 data (Table 2). Finally, levels in the cortex and cerebellum correlated well. The comparable distribution of 9 between these regions suggests that there is no preferential retention in either of these regions due to the differential off-target expression, for example, of MAO enzymes.

    For studying the binding characteristics on human AD brain slices, tritiated versions of initially [3H]17, and later [3H]9, were synthesized. For [3H]17, the Kd and Bmax values were determined via saturation binding to isolated tau aggregates extracted from the human AD brain, resulting in the identification of a high affinity binding site with a Kd of 0.92 nM and a Bmax of 7466 fmol/mg. Under the same assay conditions, [3H]2a was found to have a comparable Kd of 4.9 nM and a Bmax of 6346 fmol/mg. Note that for [3H]3, a much lower Kd of 610 nM and a much higher Bmax of 40 791 fmol/mg was measured for tau aggregates (plots see the Supporting Information). This suggests that 3 binds to one or more distinct binding sites to tau, or binds to non-tau components of the human AD brain extract, which we found to contain about 20% aggregated tau by immunoprecipitation. Two distinct binding sites to K18Δ280K-tau fibrils were reported for 18F-THK-5105, a related structure, but not to AD-derived tau (see ref (3)). Interestingly, [3H]3 showed comparable affinity to aggregated Aβ extracted from the human AD brain with a Kd of 443 nM and a Bmax of 5369 fmol/mg, which contradicts the reported selective binding to tau of this ligand versus Aβ.(14) Selectivity of the tracer binding for aggregated tau was further addressed by ARG with initially [3H]17 (see the Supporting Information) and later [3H]9 versus [3H]AV-45 (a reference standard for Aβ binding)(25) on postmortem human AD brain slices containing both tau and Aβ pathology versus slices containing Aβ pathology only based on 4G8 and AT8 immunoreactivity (Figure 1). [3H]AV-45 bound to slices containing both tau and Aβ pathology, as well as slices containing Aβ pathology only; [3H]9 only bound to slices containing tau pathology, confirming the specificity of ligand 9 for aggregated tau versus Aβ.
    Standard binding profiling at CEREP indicated that 9, at a concentration of 1 μM, did not show affinity (<50% inhibition of radioligand probe binding) for the 52 tested human receptors, ion channels, or transporters. Also, in a fluorescent label displacement assay of 400 kinases (KINOMEscan, DiscoverX), only two were inhibited >60% at 1 μM 9 concentration: ADCK4 (84%) and WNK4 (66%), and this inhibitory activity could not be confirmed in a concentration response curve (both IC50 > 3 μM), see the Supporting Information.(26)
    Inhibition of monoamine oxidase-A (MAO-A) and -B (MAO-B) by 9 was assessed fluorometrically using a commercial kit. Up to 10 μM, 9 did not show inhibition of MAO-A or MAO-B enzymatic activity (pIC50 < 5). The maximal percentage inhibition obtained at 10 μM was 1% for MAO-A and 27% for MAO-B (n = 2). In the same assay, 1a had pIC50 values of 5.1 and 5.2 for MAO-A and MAO-B, respectively, while 3 had pIC50 values of <5 and 5.8 (n = 1) for MAO-A and MAO-B, respectively.
    Interaction with MAO enzymes is of particular importance because potent MAO binding has been recently reported for 1a (MAO-A)(19) and 3 (MAO-B).(15,16) Therefore, we further studied binding of [3H]9 to human basal ganglia sections, which are known to be rich in MAO. In-house synthesized [3H]3 was used as the positive control in this experiment. As shown in Figure 2, incubation of 10 nM [3H]9 showed no significant binding to human basal ganglia tissue (Figure 2A) and hence no blocking with 10 μM 9 (B) and 10 μM 3 (C). In a control experiment, 10 nM of [3H]9 showed strong binding to tau aggregates in human AD tissue (frontal cortex), which was tau positive/Aβ positive by immunohistochemistry staining (Figure 2D). Incubation of the non-AD human basal ganglia tissue with [3H]3, however, showed strong labeling of the tissue (Figure 2E). This binding could be blocked with 10 μM 9 (Figure 2F), which is in line with the weak inhibition of MAO-B of 27% that 9 showed at 10 μM in the abovementioned fluorometric assay. The binding of 10 nM [3H]3 could also be blocked with 10 μM 3 (Figure 2G), demonstrating that this binding is specific and displaceable, again likely binding to MAO-B. Indeed, MAO-A and MAO-B expressions in the human striatal tissue was confirmed using immunohistochemistry and Western blotting (see the Supporting Information). Finally, binding of 10 nM [3H]3 to an adjacent human AD section to the one used for [3H]9 (Figure 2D), showed labeling of tau pathology in addition to off-target binding (H), suggesting that even in the human AD frontal cortex, MAO binding probably confounds the labeling of aggregated tau with [3H]3.
    This was further confirmed using [18F]9. First, we identified two different human brain sections, of which one was confirmed rich in both tau and Aβ pathology by immunostaining of Aβ (4G8) and tau (AT8) (Figure 3, respectively, I and J), and one rich in Aβ but not tau pathology by immunostaining of Aβ (4G8) and tau (AT8) (Figure 3, respectively, K and L). Next, in vitro autoradiography of 18F-labeled compound 9 was performed on adjacent sections. [18F]9 bound to sections containing both tau and Aβ pathology but not to the sections containing Aβ pathology only (Figure 3A,E). The presence of tau and/or Aβ pathology in these sections was confirmed with 4G8 and AT8 immunostaining (Figure 3I–L). The autoradiography signal on sections containing both tau and Aβ pathology was blocked by 1 μM 9 (92% blocking) (Figure 3B) and 2a (61% blocking) (Figure 3C) but not by 1 μM AV-45 (only 13% blocking) (Figure 3D). Similar incubations with 9, 2a, and AV-45 obviously had no effect on sections containing only Aβ pathology (Figure 3, respectively, F–H) because no binding of [18F]9 to these sections was observed in the first place. This confirmed [18F]9 binds only to tau pathology, and binding was only blocked by tau ligands, confirming the specificity of ligand 9 for aggregated tau versus Aβ.
    Further in vivo exploration was in first instance carried out by performing PET scans in healthy Wistar rats, which are devoid of tau pathology. Results of the 120 min baseline, pretreatment, and displacement study of [18F]9 are shown in Figure 4 (n = 3 per type of experiment). The time–activity curves (TACs) of the baseline scans of three Wistar rats showed high initial brain uptake [standardized uptake value (SUV) of 2.4 at 1 min p.i.] and rapid brain washout (SUV of 0.2 at 60 min p.i.). A subsequent blocking study with 10 mg/kg of nonradioactive 9 injected s.c. 60 min prior to tracer injection, and a displacement study with 1 mg/kg 9 injected i.v. at 30 min p.i. did not show a reduction in TAC intensity, suggestive of no reversible or irreversible off-target specific binding. No significant differences were recorded for the displacement and pretreatment study, which indicates that there is no non-tau-related binding in rats. Bone uptake was seen at the later timepoints in these rats, suggesting defluorination in vivo, in line with the defluorinated GSH adduct observed in rat hepatocytes.
    PET studies with [18F]9 in a male rhesus monkey revealed a relatively high brain uptake and rapid brain washout (SUV of 5.4 in the whole brain, time to peak uptake: 4.5 min), with a homogeneous distribution in all brain regions, and lower uptake in the corpus callosum and the skull of the monkey (Figure 5). Note that brain uptake and washout was somewhat slower compared to rats but still relatively fast. No bone uptake was observed up to 120 min post injection, in line with the absence of the defluorinated GSH adduct observed in cynomolgus hepatocytes. A blocking study with 1 mg/kg nonradioactive 9 injected i.v. simultaneous with the radiotracer injection was performed to assess possible off-target binding in a rhesus monkey. No pronounced blocking effect was observed in this scan because the SUV signal of the scan was somewhat higher (SUV of 7.5 in the whole brain), with a shorter time to the peak value (3.5 min). Brain washout was also slightly faster compared to the baseline scan. A similar homogenous distribution was also recorded in all brain regions. The carrier effect on tracer delivery is probably due to blocking of low capacity peripheral binding or metabolism sites including plasma protein binding. This increases the tracer availability for brain entry resulting in a higher peak SUV value and relative faster washout to return to similar values as the control condition from 60 min p.i. onward except for the white matter for which tracer dissociation occurs slower.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We describe the optimization of quinoline HTS hit 5, which led to potential tau PET tracer 9, an isoquinoline derivative with high affinity for aggregated tau, low off-target binding (Aβ, MAO enzymes), good brain penetration, and fast clearance with low risk for radiodefluorination or formation of brain penetrant radiometabolites in humans. A small-animal PET study in rats confirmed [18F]9 to be a highly CNS penetrant and fast clearing, and a similarly favorable kinetics profile was observed in the rhesus monkey. These promising results led to the selection of 9 for further testing in humans, the data of which will be reported in due time.

    Analytical Methods
