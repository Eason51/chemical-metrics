    Abstract

    Allosteric kinase inhibitors are thought to have high selectivity and are prime candidates for kinase drug discovery. In addition, the exploration of allosteric mechanisms represents an attractive topic for basic research and drug design. Although the identification and characterization of allosteric kinase inhibitors is still far from being routine, X-ray structures of kinase complexes have been determined for a significant number of such inhibitors. On the basis of structural data, allosteric inhibitors can be confirmed. We report a comprehensive survey of allosteric kinase inhibitors and activators from publicly available X-ray structures, map their binding sites, and determine their distribution over binding pockets in kinases. In addition, we discuss structural features of these compounds and identify active structural analogues and high-confidence target annotations, indicating additional activities for a subset of allosteric inhibitors. This contribution aims to provide a detailed structure-based view of allosteric kinase inhibition.

    This article is part of the New Horizons in Drug Discovery - Understanding and Advancing Kinase Inhibitors special issue.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Since the first approval of a protein kinase inhibitor for therapy (imatinib, 2001), the field of kinase inhibitor drug discovery has excelled.(1−3) In September 2020, 62 kinase inhibitors were approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA). In addition, more than 200 other compounds with Nonproprietary International Names are currently in clinical development. Resulting from the high interest in kinase inhibitors in oncology and other therapeutic areas(3) and a plethora of research efforts, a wealth of kinase and inhibitor data have become available in the public domain (with many more proprietary data remaining in pharmaceutical companies). On the basis of our periodic data surveys, more than 130 000 inhibitors with reliable activity measurements are currently available in public repositories that cover more than 80% of the 518 kinases comprising the human kinome.(4) In addition, more than 5000 X-ray structures of complexes formed by more than 300 kinases and 3000 inhibitors have been deposited in the Protein Data Bank (PDB).(5) Taken together, these data provide a rich source for exploring kinase inhibitor characteristics and a knowledge base for developing new compounds.
    Kinase inhibitors have been classified and assigned to different types depending on their mode of action and location of their binding sites. Type I, I, and II inhibitors are ATP-site-directed, whereas type III and IV inhibitors bind outside the ATP site and act by allosteric mechanisms.(6,7) Type I and II inhibitors bind to an active and inactive kinase conformation, respectively, while type I inhibitors interact with an intermediate conformational state. Type II inhibitors have been further classified. Binding of type IIA inhibitors extends into the so-called back pocket while type IIB inhibitors only bind to the front cleft and gate area of the active site region. The binding site of type III inhibitors is proximal to the ATP site and includes at least two variants, depending on whether the kinase contains a pleckstrin homology domain or not. By contrast, type IV inhibitors target sites that are distant from the ATP site and have different locations. Type V inhibitors are bivalent constructs typically combining an ATP-site-directed inhibitor through a linker fragment with a substrate analogue targeting another site in the catalytic or regulatory domain of kinases.(8,9) As substrates, peptides (or peptidomimetics) are often used such as, for example, phosphorylated peptides targeting SH2 domains. However, a variety of bivalent constructs targeting different binding sites are principally possible. In addition, covalent kinase inhibitors are classified as type VI inhibitors.(7) There has been increasing interest in covalent kinase inhibition in recent years, especially reversible covalent inhibition, because of increased efficacy and kinase (isoform) selectivity, prolonged residence times, or improved pharmacokinetics that are often observed for such compounds compared with competitive inhibitors.(10,11) Type VI inhibitors mostly, but not always, target free cysteine residues in kinases using thiol-reactive groups (warheads)(10) and include both ATP-site-directed compounds that block ATP binding and covalent-allosteric inhibitors.(12) In addition, type VII inhibitors have been proposed that bind to extracellular regions of receptor Tyr kinases and trigger allosteric inhibitory effects.(13) Kinase activators have also been increasingly considered as tools for gaining a deeper understanding of kinase regulation and mechanisms of action. Currently available activators exclusively bind to allosteric sites.
    Much of the high interest in allosteric kinase inhibitors stems from the expectation that these compounds should be more specific than ATP-site-directed inhibitors, taking into consideration that the ATP binding site is largely conserved across the kinome. The vast majority of kinase inhibitors available in the public domain are type I inhibitors.(14) While these inhibitors include highly promiscuous compounds,(14) the expectation that ATP-site-directed compounds should generally be promiscuous cannot be supported on the basis of available activity data.(14,15) If reliable activity measurements in target-based assays are considered, more than 60% of currently available inhibitors are only active against a single kinase, and less than five percent have five or more kinase annotations.(15) Even taking activity data incompleteness into account (i.e., not all inhibitors have been tested against the entire kinome), promiscuity of ATP-site-directed kinase inhibitors cannot be generalized. This is also consistent with results of selectivity-oriented kinase profiling(16−18) and increasing insights into the varying influence of kinase inhibition kinetics(19,20) and the dynamics of active versus inactive conformational states adapted by kinases.(21)
    While ATP-site-directed kinase inhibitors may also be highly selective, allosteric inhibitors display a greater variety of binding modes and mechanisms than other kinase inhibitors and have a principally higher potential for selective inhibition. However, the discovery of allosteric kinase inhibitors is far from being routine and has often been serendipitous. Moreover, confirming allosteric mechanisms of action is prone to complications.(22)
    Although it has thus far not been possible to develop experimental approaches for a more routine discovery of allosteric kinase inhibitors, a significant number of such inhibitors have become available that were confirmed on the basis of X-ray structures. Binding modes of these compounds have typically been explored on a case-by-case basis.
    We have been interested in comprehensively analyzing and comparing allosteric ligands for which X-ray structures of complexes with kinases were determined. Herein, we report a survey of these ligands by studying their distribution over different binding pockets and kinases and analyzing characteristic structural features. In addition, a search was carried out for reported nonkinase targets of these ligands, which is a topic of high interest in polypharmacology-oriented drug discovery.(23) Furthermore, structural analogues of allosteric ligands were systematically identified, and available activity annotations were collected to derive additional target hypotheses.

    2. Allosteric Kinase Ligands from X-ray Structures

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    X-ray structures of complexes between allosteric ligands and human kinases (excluding atypical kinases) and associated data were extracted from the PDB,(5) KLIFS,(24) Profkin,(25) and ASD(26) databases. For X-ray structures, PDB codes were collected and UniProt IDs(27) of kinases identified. Only X-ray structures with a resolution higher than 3.5 Å were considered. The resolution of selected complex X-ray structures ranged from 1.06–3.49 Å, with a median of 2.00 Å. Each structure was inspected and analyzed using the Molecular Operating Environment,(28) and the binding sites of any crystallographic ligands were determined. Structures were compared with the aid of the structural alignment web tool from PDBe-KB.(29) Interactive data curation yielded a total of 232 allosteric ligands that were classified according to inhibitor types and additional compound categories, as summarized in Table 1.

    We found 81 type III inhibitors binding to the well-characterized allosteric pocket adjacent to the ATP site and 15 bivalent inhibitors occupying the ATP site and the adjacent allosteric pocket. In addition, 81 type IV inhibitors were identified and 10 (covalent) type VI inhibitors that bound to various pockets distant from the ATP site. Furthermore, 19 designated activators of kinase activity were found to be accommodated in different allosteric pockets.
    In addition to the unique allosteric ligands reported in Table 1, we also identified 26 multisite ligands that occupied more than one pocket in the same or different kinase structures. These multisite ligands bound to the ATP site plus an allosteric pocket or different allosteric pockets, as further discussed below.

    3. Mapping of Allosteric Binding Sites

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We systematically mapped the binding pockets of the 232 allosteric compounds we identified. Analysis of the corresponding X-ray structures revealed a total of 12 binding sites (designated A-L) including the ATP site (A), depicted in Figure 1. In this figure, the locations of all pockets identified in different kinases are mapped on the structure of the catalytic domain of an exemplary kinase to facilitate a comparison of these sites (no individual kinase contains all of these pockets). Eleven allosteric binding pockets (B-L) were identified. For each mapped pocket, we determined the number of kinases from X-ray structures in which it was present, reported in Figure 1 as the number of UniProt IDs (UPIDs), and the number of unique crystallographic ligands. For example, pocket B located adjacent to the ATP site was found in 19 kinases and accommodated a total of 90 different type III (or VI) inhibitors. By contrast, pocket L was only detected for a single inhibitor in a single kinase.
    While some of these binding sites were already well-characterized in the literature, for others, only little, if any information was available. For each pocket, we identified inhibitor types on the basis of structural data and literature information, if available, and also determined binding sites of activators.     Seven of the 11 allosteric pockets mapped to the front side of the kinase where the catalytic site is located while four pockets mapped to the back side (Figure 1). They were widely distributed across the surface of the catalytic domain. While pocket B represented the known binding site of type III (plus covalent) allosteric inhibitors, Nine of the 10 remaining allosteric pockets were classified as type IV inhibitor binding sites, with the exception of pocket I, for which only type VI inhibitors were available. Structurally characterized bivalent inhibitors bound exclusively to the combination of the A (ATP) site and the adjacent allosteric H pocket. Bivalent inhibitors targeting other pocket combinations were not found. However, Eight of 23 identified bivalent compounds were also classified as multisite ligands because they bound to the H+A pocket combination as well as the F or D pocket, a surprising finding. Four of the 11 allosteric pockets also contained known kinase activators (C, D, E, and F). Both noncovalent and covalent inhibitors (type IV and VI) were accommodated in pockets C, D, and K. Hence, allosteric pockets C on the back and D on the front side (Figure 1) accommodated noncovalent and covalent kinase inhibitors as well as activators and were thus most versatile from a mechanistic point of view. All covalent inhibitors found in allosteric sites exclusively targeted cysteine residues. Seven of the 10 covalent compounds were acrylamides, one was an enone, and the remaining two inhibitors formed disulfide bonds. In the following, characteristics of individual pockets are discussed. For kinases and kinase groups, standard abbreviations are used.(4,27)

    B Pocket

    The binding site of type III inhibitors was confirmed by X-ray crystallography in 2004 (PDB IDs: 1S9I and 1S9J)(30) and is most extensively characterized, with structures containing 90 unique type III or VI inhibitors (Figure 1, Table 2). The pocket participates in the formation of the catalytic hinge region. Trametinib, a MEK inhibitor, was approved by the FDA in 2013 as the first kinase inhibitor acting by a type III allosteric mechanism.(31)

    C Pocket

    This binding pocket is located near the αC-helix proximal to the B pocket and has thus far been identified in PDK1, AURKA, and CDK2. It has also been termed the PDK1-interacting fragment (PIF) pocket in PDK1(32) or Y-pocket in AURKA.(33) The first X-ray structure of a complex with an inhibitor bound to the C pocket was reported in 2009 (PDB ID: 3HRF).(34) For this binding site, a total of 41 unique ligands were identified including type IV and VI inhibitors as well as activators.

    D Pocket

    The first X-ray structure with a ligand binding to the D pocket, also termed lipid pocket, was reported in 2008 (PDB: 3C5U).(35) So far, 24 unique compounds have been identified binding to this pocket including type IV and VI inhibitors as well as activators. Several compounds have no detectable effect on the catalytic activity, while kinase activation by lipids binding to pocket D has also been reported.(36) This binding site has been found in four kinases including three MAP kinase isoforms (MAPK8, MAPK10, MAPK14, CSNK2A1).

    E Pocket

    The E pocket accommodates type IV inhibitors and activators.(37,38) It represents one of the first allosteric sites in kinases that were identified. In 2003, the structure of a complex formed by c-ABL kinase and an endogenous myristoyl inhibitor was determined revealing the E pocket (PDB: 1OPL),(37) which has since then been termed the myristoyl pocket. While myristoyl acts as an inhibitor, activators can also bind to this site.(38) The inhibitory mechanism of action at this site is well understood. Bound ligands closely interact with the αI′-helix. As a consequence, helix bending may occur, which then leads to autoinhibition by the SH2 domain of the kinase. However, not all bound ligands induce helix bending. In the absence of induced helix bending, the kinase remains in an active conformation, and bound activators can further promote catalytic activity. A total of three inhibitors and four activators were identified to bind to this pocket. In addition to c-ABL, this pocket was also detected in CDK2. Asciminib was the first allosteric BCR-ABL1 inhibitor targeting the myristoyl pocket, which currently is in a phase 3 clinical trial for the treatment of chronic myelogenous leukemia.(39)

    F Pocket

    This pocket has been structurally confirmed and first described in 2013.(40) It was then identified in four AMP-dependent kinases at the interface between the kinase domain of the α-subunit and the carbohydrate binding region of the β-subunit. Inhibitors binding at this site trigger conformational changes that prohibit α-/β-subunit interactions.(41) Structures with 25 type IV inhibitors or activators have been determined so far. Activators binding to this pocket are thought to stabilize the association of regulatory subunits.

    G Pocket

    This pocket was first described in 2009 for CHK1.(42) It was reported that ligands binding at this site had inhibitory effects but did not induce detectable conformational changes.(42,43) In our structural survey, we also identified this pocket in kinase PDK1. Six different compounds were found to bind to this site.

    H Pocket

    The H pocket proximal to the ATP site is the primary target of bivalent (type V) inhibitors, 23 of which have so far been characterized. In addition, structures with 25 primarily fragment-like type IV inhibitors bound to this pocket are available. This site was first described in 2017 and termed αD-pocket(44) because its presence depends on the position of the αD-helix.

    J Pocket

    This pocket was first described in 2017 for AURKA,(45) and structures with eight type IV inhibitors targeting this site have been determined. Similar to the F pocket, the J pocket maps to the top of the catalytic domain, albeit to the back side, rather than the front (Figure 1). Inhibitors binding to both of these pockets are implicated in similar allosteric mechanisms preventing dimerization with regulatory subunits.(45) The J pocket was also detected in another kinase (BTK).

    I, K, and L Pockets

    Different from some allosteric binding sites that have been intensely investigated, as discussed above, only little information is available for pockets I, K, and L, which have been first described two to three years ago. So far, each pocket has only been observed in a single kinase including CDK2 (I), AURKA (K), and NTRK1 (L) and structures with only one or two inhibitors are available. For the I pocket, both of these inhibitors were covalently bound.

    4. Induced Allosteric Sites

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    An important question concerning allosteric kinase inhibition with immediate implications for drug design is whether allosteric binding sites are preformed in kinases or whether they are induced upon ligand binding. To address this question, we have compared structures of apo and holo forms of kinases with type IV (or VI) inhibitors. A summary of the results is reported in Table 3. We generally observed that allosteric pockets were often rather shallow, which complicated the assessment of conformational changes in some instances. For example, pocket C (or PIF pocket(46)) is a shallow pocket that was suggested to be conserved across AGC kinases, a family with ∼60 members, on the basis of sequence comparisons.(32,47) Structural comparison of complexes (5DN3, 5DOS, 5ORR) and corresponding apo forms of kinases (5DNR, 1MQ4, 6HJJ) revealed the presence of this pocket in the absence of bound compounds and confirmed additional conformational changes upon binding. These changes included a rotation of the αC-helix and reorientation of side chains in the binding site. By contrast, pocket D, which is located in the C-terminal lobe between the MAP kinase insert (MKI) loop and the αEF-/αF-helices, is deep. In this case, structural comparisons (complexes: 2YIS, 4E8A, 5N67; apo forms: 2YIX, 5MZ3, 5TBE) also revealed the presence of a preformed pocket in this region whose entrance was obstructed by the side chain of residue Trp197. Furthermore, large conformational changes occurred upon ligand binding in the MKI loop and the αEF-/αF- helices. Induced conformational changes were also reported for backbone segments of the MKI loop and αEF-/αF-helices as well as a side chain reorientation of the catalytically relevant Trp197 residue.(36) Similar observations were made for the deep E pocket in the C-terminal lobe (complexes: 3PYY, 2FO0, 5MO4, 6NPE; apo: 3QRJ, 4TWP, 2HZI) that interfaces with the αI′-helix. In this case, the presence of a pocket was also evident in the uncomplexed structures. Some ligands including the endogenous myristoyl as well as synthetic compounds induce a large conformational change in the αI′-helix causing a bend beginning at residue Phe516 that triggers autoinhibition.(38) Other ligands stabilize the native conformation of the helix providing a basis for activator effects.(48)

    Different from D and E, pocket F in the N-terminal lobe of the catalytic kinase domain is relatively shallow, but also visible in apo form structures. Structural comparison (complexes: 5CSH, 6FVF, 5MOE; apo: 5CVH, 5MO8, 4GUB) revealed large conformational changes in the β4-β5 loop (Asp103-Thr108) of CK2α kinase as well as a side chain rotamer change of residue Gln36. It was reported previously that the interaction between the CK2α and CK2β subunits was essential for substrate selectivity and that ligands bound at this allosteric site prevented this subunit interaction.(41) In addition to the changes described above, inhibitor binding at this site also induced a conformational change in the distal αD-helix adjacent to the ATP site.(41)
    Pocket H is another deep pocket in the C-terminal lobe proximal to the ATP pocket. In this case, no evidence for this pocket was observed in the absence of bound ligands, whereas structural comparison (complexes: 5CS6, 5CU3, 5CT0; apo: 5CSV, 5CVH, 6L22) confirmed a large conformational change in the αD-helix (Asn117-Leu128) as a consequence of compound binding, inducing the formation of the H pocket. Previously, ligand binding was found to force the displacement of residue Tyr125 from this site, releasing the αD-helix from the C-lobe and effectively opening this pocket.(49)
    The G, I, J, K, and L pockets were very shallow and only limited structural information was available, as discussed above. In these cases, structural comparisons did not yield evidence for preformation of these pockets or for significant conformational changes occurring upon ligand binding. Such effects were not reported in the literature.
    Only three of the binding sites of type IV inhibitors represented deep pockets (D, E, and H). From structural comparisons, the picture emerged that the deep pockets D and E as well as the comparably shallow pockets C and F were partly preformed in kinase structures. However, they were substantially altered or extended through conformational changes induced by ligand binding. The deep H pocket was the only one that we classified as fully induced by compound binding because there was no evidence for its formation in apo structures. The remaining pockets had only little depth, and in these instances, no firm conclusions concerning preformation or ligand-based induction could be drawn on the basis of our analysis. It is conceivable that these very shallow allosteric pockets only form upon ligand binding, but associated conformational effects were hardly detectable.

    5. Distribution of Allosteric Pockets, Inhibitors, and Multisite Ligands

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The distribution of allosteric pockets across the human kinome was determined, as shown in Figure 2. Allosteric binding sites were detected in all major kinase groups with the exception of the CK1 group, indicating that allosteric compounds could be identified for many different kinases. Kinases from the TKL and STE groups were found to exclusively contain the B pocket adjacent to the ATP site hosting type III and VI inhibitors. Two Tyr kinases and five Ser/Thr kinases contained multiple allosteric pockets including binding sites for type III, IV, V, or VI compounds. Furthermore, a total of four kinases from different groups were found to contain at least three different allosteric sites in different combinations including TRKA (B, F, and L pockets), AURKA (C, J, K), CDK2 (B, E, I), and CK2a1 (B, D, F, H), which had four different allosteric pockets, the largest number we detected.
    We also determined the distribution of allosteric compounds and different properties across the binding pockets. Figure 3A reports the release dates for individual X-ray structures containing allosteric inhibitors, and Figure 3B shows the distribution of their crystallographic resolution. There were no significant differences in resolution between these structures. In Figure 3C, the size (molecular weight) distribution of the allosteric inhibitors is reported. The largest compounds were type III/VI inhibitors occupying the B pocket with a median molecular weight >450 Da. Type IV/VI inhibitors distributed across the C–G pockets were generally small, with a median weight <400 Da. However, for all of these pockets, the size of bound compounds significantly varied. Largest variations were observed for compounds occupying the F pocket, followed by the G pocket, with median values close to 400 Da. Interestingly, bivalent inhibitors binding to the H+A sites had molecular weights comparable to many type III/VI inhibitors of the B pocket. By contrast, compounds binding to the H, I, J, and K pockets were much smaller, with molecular weights between 200 and 300 Da, and often fragment-like. As discussed above, these compounds occupied the shallowest allosteric pockets. Figure 3D reports the corresponding distribution of binding efficiency index values for inhibitors for which potency values were available, revealing that allosteric inhibitors of pocket D had particularly high ligand efficiency. Despite size variations, the ligand efficiency of inhibitors targeting the other pockets was comparable.
    Rather unexpectedly, we also identified 26 multisite ligands that bound to distinct allosteric pockets or the ATP site in the same or different X-ray structures. Nine of these compounds bound to the ATP site and one or two of the allosteric pockets, eight others bound to the H+A sites (bivalent ligands) and one of or both of the allosteric F or D pockets, and nine others to diverse allosteric pockets. Seven of the latter compounds bound to the F and H pockets, indicating that these pockets were capable of accommodating the same (or similar) ligands.
    Figure 4 shows two examples of multisite ligands. On the left, an inhibitor is shown that bound to the ATP site and the B (type III) and C (type IV) pocket in different structures and kinases (DAPK1, CDK2), thus representing an exemplary “promiscuous allosteric” kinase inhibitor. This compound was found in six different X-ray structures. In these structures, it occupied the ATP site twice and the B and C pockets five times. On the right in Figure 4, an inhibitor is shown that simultaneously bound to the ATP site and the D pocket in a single complex structure, hence providing an example for a multisite ligand displaying a mixed competitive/allosteric inhibitory phenotype.

    6. Allosteric Inhibitors with Privileged Substructures

    9. Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Compounds acting by allosteric mechanisms are of high interest in the quest for selective kinase inhibition and often considered prime candidates for drug development. The discovery of such inhibitors is still serendipitous in many instances. Nonetheless, in recent years, increasing numbers of allosteric kinase inhibitors have been reported. The assignment and elucidation of allosteric mechanisms is far from trivial and prone to errors. Experimentally determined structures of kinase-inhibitor complexes provide a sound basis for confirming allosteric inhibitors and their binding sites. Complex structures have preferentially been studied on a case-by-case basis, yielding incremental insights into allosteric sites and mechanisms.
    Our systematic survey of publicly available X-ray structures of complexes of kinases and designated allosteric ligands identified 232 unique compounds including allosteric inhibitors and 19 kinase activators. Mapping of the binding sites of these compounds revealed 11 allosteric sites. These sites were found in varying numbers of kinases and hosted different numbers of ligands. Some allosteric pockets and associated mechanisms of action were well characterized in the literature such as the B pocket (type III/VI inhibitors) or E pocket (type IV inhibitors and activators). By contrast, for other binding sites we mapped, only little information was available. Structurally analogous allosteric inhibitors mostly bound to the same pockets, indicating the presence of specific mechanisms. At the same time, we also identified a number of multisite ligands that were found in different pockets or combinations of pockets. Furthermore, analysis of high-confidence activity data and structural analogues identified a number of confirmed or likely additional targets for a confined subset of allosteric kinase inhibitors. Hence, selectivity of allosteric ligands could not be generalized and there was considerable heterogeneity in their binding characteristics. Since many allosteric kinase inhibitors contained privileged substructures that also occurred in compounds with other activities, such as ligands of G-protein coupled receptors, additional activities of such allosteric compounds might be anticipated.
    For drug design, an important question is whether or not allosteric binding sites are induced upon compound binding or whether they can be targeted in structures of uncomplexed kinases. On the basis of structural comparisons, we found that several allosteric pockets were preformed in kinases but significantly altered and extended upon ligand binding. This provides a difficult scenario for “static” structure-based drug design approaches and indicates the need to complement drug design with an extensive assessment of protein dynamics in such regions. On the other hand, allosteric pockets were often rather shallow, making it difficult to obtain evidence for defined conformational changes upon ligand binding.
    Taken together, the findings presented herein provide a refined structure-based view of allosteric kinase inhibition and should encourage further structural and computational investigations of allosteric mechanisms as well as a comparative assessment of structure–activity relationships among allosteric kinase inhibitors.
