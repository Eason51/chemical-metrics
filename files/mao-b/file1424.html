    Abstract

    Angiogenesis is important in tumorigenesis and tumor progression. Human vascular endothelial growth factor (hVEGF) is an angiogenic growth factor that plays a crucial role in tumor progression. The G-rich region within the 5′-untranslated regions (5′-UTR) of hVEGF-A mRNA can form a “switchable” RNA G-quadruplex structure that is essential for a cap-independent translation initiation. We screened our small-molecule library for binders of this G-tract. One novel quinazoline derivative, compound 1, showed a significant specific interaction with the G-tract and destabilized the G-quadruplex structure. The results of cellular experiments revealed that compound 1 down-regulated hVEGF-A translation and significantly impeded tumor cells migration. We also found that compound 1 exhibited tumor-inhibiting activity in MCF-7 xenograft tumors, which might be related to its ability to reduce hVEGF expression. These findings present a new strategy of hVEGF-A translational control in which small molecules interact with G-quadruplex structure in the 5′UTR.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Angiogenesis is essential for effective wound healing and blood flow restoration in the impaired tissues of embryos and healthy adults. Angiogenic factors, such as vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), act as mediators of tumor angiogenesis by stimulating the growth of new blood vessels from nearby capillaries and providing tumor cells with oxygen and nutrients. The expression of the human VEGF-A (hVEGF-A) is induced by hypoxia and hypoglycaemia,(1) and VEGF-A promotes a wide range of functions, including adhesion, survival, migration, and invasion.(2) Moreover, the role of VEGF-A is now well established and forms the basis for using anti-VEGF-A drugs to treat neovascular disorders.(3)
    G-Quadruplexes are DNA or RNA secondary structures composed of stacked guanine (G) through Hoogsteen hydrogen bonds,(4) and they have been found existing widely in whole genome by genomics and bioinformatics studies.(5-7) These structures may play important roles in several biological processes, such as DNA replication, gene transcription, and the maintenance of genomic stability.(8) Using the G-quadruplex antibody BG4, quadruplexes were shown to be more prevalent in cancer cells than non-neoplastic tissues,(9) which implies that cancer cells are more sensitive than non-neoplastic cells to small G-quadruplex ligands.
    Several G-quadruplexes form in the 5′-untranslated regions (5′-UTR) of mRNAs and act as translational repressors, such as NRAS,(10)Zic-1,(11)MT3-MMP,(12)TRF2,(13) and cyclin D3(14) mRNAs. However, a G-quadruplex in the 5′-UTR of hVEGF-A mRNA may promote the translation via a cap-independent approach.(15, 16) Specifically, an 18-nucleotide (nt) sequence within the internal ribosome entry site A (IRES-A) of the hVEGF-A’s 5′-UTR can independently fold into a RNA G-quadruplex and directly recruits a 40S ribosomal subunit via cap-independent translation initiation.(17) Nevertheless, a recent study showed that this G-quadruplex is optional for cap-independent function, which made the function of the G-quadruplex in IRES more complicated.(18) Although some G-quadruplex stabilizers were shown to inhibit the cap-independent translation in the previous study,(18) no specific ligands have been found to date. In addition, a few molecules, such as cationic porphyrins (TMPyP4)(19) and TAPs,(20) showed activity for disrupting G-quadruplexes, but these molecules do not selectively interact with the hVEGF-A’s IRES. To clarify the function of the G-quadruplex structure in the IRES, in this study, we aimed to find a specific IRES G-rich sequence binder and determine whether it exhibits antitumor activity. In addition, a specific IRES G-rich sequence binder can help for clarifying the role of G-quadruplex in cap-independent translation of hVEGF-A.
    We first obtained additional evidence for the existence of the RNA G-quadruplex in the IRES using the G-quadruplex antibody BG4 and in vitro transcribed long-strand mRNA (Supporting Information, Figure S1). Then, we screened our compound library (built by the School of Pharmaceutical Sciences, Sun Yat-sen University) containing 144 natural products and related derivatives with diverse structures using a surface plasmon resonance (SPR) biosensor. Among these compounds, we found a novel quinazoline derivative, N-((2-(4-(N-(4-(2-diethylaminoethoxy))phenyl)amino)-quinazolin-2-yl)phenyl)-3-(4-methylpiperazin-1-yl)propanamide (compound 1), could specifically bind to the G-rich sequence in the IRES-A in vitro. We further explored the possible mechanism of this compound on the G-rich sequence in the IRES-A through in vitro and cellular experiments and suggested that this compound could destabilize the G-quadruplex structure. Additional in vitro and in vivo experiments suggested that the compound could hinder cell migration and repress tumor growth by decreasing VEGF-A protein expression. Taken together, these findings illustrated that the translational regulatory activity of the G-rich sequence in the IRES repressed hVEGF-A’s translation and that this regulation could be chemically repressed by a small molecule.

    Results

    Survey of Small Molecules that Selectively Binding to the IRES-A RNA

    We used SPR experiments to screen our compound library containing 144 natural products and related derivatives with diverse structures (Supporting Information, Figure S2), including quinolone derivatives, quinazoline derivatives, berberine derivatives, and curcumin derivatives. The oligomers immobilized on a GLH chip included the biotin-labeled Biotin_IRES-A (WT), Biotin_IRES-A (MU1), and Biotin_hairpin (Supporting Information, Table S1).
    The SPR data were shown in Figure 1 and Supporting Information, Table S2; the y-axis shows the dissociation constants (KD), indicating the binding affinity between the compounds and the immobilized oligomers. We divided the compounds into three groups according to the results: group 1 (Figure 1a) included the compounds with certain selectivity for IRES-WT vs hairpin DNA; group 2 (Figure 1b) included the compounds exhibiting similar binding to both RNA oligomers and hairpin DNA; group 3 included the compounds whose binding abilities could not be detected (Supporting Information, Table S2) such as all of the curcumin derivatives.
    In group 2, we found some compounds that had been previously identified as strong DNA G-quadruplex stabilizers, such as the N1-(11H-indolo[3,2-b]quinolin-11-yl)-N3,N3-dimethylpropane-1,3-diamine (Q73 in the screen data and SYUIQ-05 in the reference, numbered as 2 in this study),(21) and the 11-((3-(dimethylamino)propyl)amino)-7-fluoro-5-methyl-11H-indolo[3,2-b]quinolin-5-ium (Q71 in the screen data and 2r in the reference),(22) which lent support to our SPR screening data. Among all the screened compounds, compounds in series a (quinazoline derivatives) exhibited high affinity and selectivity for the IRES-A (WT) sequence; compounds of berberine derivatives and quinolone derivatives showed polarized effects, making the specialized SAR analysis difficult (additional work is ongoing, but the data are not shown in this manuscript).
    Series a appeared to be the best group, although it included only three compounds. This series was derived from a bisubstituted quinazoline derivative that possesses an interaction with the telomeric G-quadruplex DNA, thereby inhibiting tumor cell proliferation and inducing cell senescence.(23) Among them, compound 1 (Scheme 1) bound to the IRES-A (WT) with a KD of 0.928 μM and bound to the hairpin DNA with a KD of 21.2 μM. Because we aimed to select a compound with specific binding to the IRES-A sequence, we chose compound 1 for further studies because it exhibited the highest binding affinity and highest IRES/hairpin selectivity (23-fold) for the 18-nt biotin-labeled IRES-A(WT) oligomer (Figure 1 and Supporting Information, Table S2).
    It is noted that the selectivity of compound 1 binding to the IRES-A (WT) vs the IRES-A (MU1) was not significant (4-fold). This might due to the mutant IRES-A oligomer we used in the SPR assay still has the ability to form partial G-quadruplex structure (Supporting Information, Figures S1 and S3a). To exclude the complexity from the mutant IRES-A, we designed the other mutant sequence, the IRES-A (MU2) sequence, which has no ability to form a stable G-quadruplex (Supporting Information, Figure S3a) for the following experiments.

    Compound 1 Binds to the IRES-A RNA G-Quadruplex with High Affinity and Selectivity

    To verify the binding abilities of compound 1 to RNA in the free state, microscale thermophoresis (MST) measurements were performed using an 18-nt FAM-labeled IRES-A (WT) with compound 1 at increasing concentrations (Figure 2a). After a 5 min incubation at 25 °C, MST analysis was performed on the Monolith NT.115 instrument (NanoTemper Technologies, Germany). The KD value of compound 1 for IRES-A (WT) was 1.29 μM, which was similar to the SPR result. In addition, the binding of compound 1 to the IRES-A mutant RNA oligomer (IRES-MU1) was weaker than that to the IRES-A WT (KD = 13.4 μM, Figure 2c), which was consistent with the data in SPR screening. We further used the IRES-A (MU2) RNA that could not form G-quadruplex structure as a reference, and compound 1 could not reach the binding platform at the highest concentration of 100 μM (Figure 2c). In addition, compound 1 bound to DNA G-rich sequence with much weaker affinity such as the IRES-A counterpart DNA oligomer and the c-kit1 G-quadruplex DNA (Supporting Information, Figure S4). These findings suggested that compound 1 could selectively bind to the G-quadruplex-forming sequence in the IRES-A region.
    Competition dialysis measurements were performed to further verify the selectivity of the compound. The IRES-A (WT) oligomer together with several other oligomers (Supporting Information, Table S1), including a RNA hairpin, random RNA, CT DNA, IRES-MU2, flanking sequences of IRES (flank 1 and flank 2, Supporting Information, Figure S3b), DNA G-quadruplexes (TBA, c-kit 1, and c-myc Pu27), and other RNA G-quadruplexes (ADAM10, TERRA, BCL-2, and NRAS), were placed in independent dialysis cassettes after the general annealing process. All the dialysis cassettes were submerged in a solution containing compound 1 and allowed to dialyze for 24 h. After spectrophotometric quantitation, compound 1 was found to exhibit the highest binding with the IRES-WT and weaker binding with other DNA and RNA structures (Figure 2b).

    Compound 1 Reduces the Stability of the IRES-A G-Quadruplex

    On the basis of the evidence that compound 1 could specifically bind to the G-quadruplex-forming sequence in the IRES-A in vitro, we further investigated whether compound 1 affected G-quadruplex’s stability. CD spectra were collected to determine the secondary structure of the IRES-A (WT) oligomer. The oligomer formed a typical parallel G-quadruplex in a 10 mM Tris-HCl buffer (pH 7.4, 100 mM KCl), and the signals representing G-quadruplexes at approximately 260 nm decreased in a dose-dependent manner after the addition of compound 1 (Figure 3a). In contrast, the G-quadruplex signals of the IRES-MU2, IRES-A counterpart DNA, and the G-rich sequence in hVEGF-A’s promoter (VEGF promoter) changed very little in the presence of compound 1 (Figure 3a and Supporting Information, Figure S5a).
    Next, we performed CD melting experiments to evaluate the thermodynamic stabilization effect of compound 1 on the IRES-A G-quadruplex (Figure 3b, Supporting Information, Figure S5b,c). We used the CD signals at 262 nm to calculate the melting temperature (Tm). The Tm values of the IRES-A G-quadruplex in the absence and presence of 5 μM compound 1 were 80.9 ± 0.8 and 62.2 ± 1.2 °C, respectively (Figure 3b), indicating that the thermostability of the G-quadruplex in this sequence decreased in the presence of compound 1.
    To further validate the destabilization effect of compound 1 on the IRES-A G-quadruplex structure, we performed an electrophoresis mobility shift assay (EMSA). As shown in Figure 3c, as the concentrations of compound 1 increased, the lower bands representing the IRES-A (WT) G-quadruplex weakened and the upper bands representing ssRNA increased. In contrast, compound 1 could not induce significant changes to the reference sequence IRES-A (MU2).
    To obtain additional evidence relating to compound 1’s destabilizing effect, experiments using the G-quadruplex antibody BG4 were applied. A recent study demonstrated the competitive binding of BG4 and a G-quadruplex stabilizer, pyridostatin, to the TERRA G-quadruplex and subsequent rearrangement into a more stable BG4-PDS-RNA complex.(24) However, in the presence of compound 1, the situation was different. A filter binding assay (Figure 4a), a pull-down assay (Figure 4b), and an EMSA (Supporting Information, Figure S6) experiment with the BG4 were performed to detect the influence of compound 1 on BG4’s binding to the oligomer. Both of them showed that the RNA-BG4 complex reduced. We also performed MST measurements in a ternary system containing BG4, in vitro transcribed IRES-A mRNAs, and the compound 1. When the in vitro transcribed mRNA annealed with compound 1, the binding ability of BG4 antibody to IRES-A G-rich mRNA dramatically decreased and the KD value changed from 10.8 nM to 4.64 μM, corresponding to a nearly 430-fold reduction (Figure 4c,d). In contrast, the BG4 antibody could not specifically bind to the IRES-A (MU2) sequence in the absence or presence of compound 1 (Figure 4e,f). These findings further implied that, unlike the stabilizer pyridostatin,(24) compound 1 destabilized the RNA G-quadruplex in the hVEGF-A mRNA, and BG4 could not recognize the single-stranded RNA anymore.
    In addition, we used a RNA immunoprecipitation (RIP) assay to detect the binding of BG4 to the IRES-A region after compound 1 treatment in MCF-7 human breast cancer cells. After transfecting the pcDNA3.1-BG4 plasmid into the MCF-7 cells and incubating them with compound 1 (2.5 or 5 μM), complexes of the BG4 antibody with its RNA targets were obtained using protein A/G plus agarose beads. The complexes were detected via dot blotting, reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR), and quantitative real-time RT-PCR (qRT-PCR). The amount of BG4-bound IRES-A sequences reduced by compound 1 in a dose-dependent manner according to the dot blotting assay (Figure 5a). Furthermore, RT-PCR (Figure 5b) and qRT-PCR (Figure 5c) were employed to quantitatively detect the amount of BG4-bound IRES-A using the IRES-A sequence-specific primers RT_IRES-A and RT_IRES-S (Supporting Information, Table S1). The results were in good agreement with those of dot blotting and showed that the bound IRES-A RNA immunoprecipitated by BG4 significantly decreased after the addition of compound 1. In addition, compound 1 could not influence on the expression of BG4 protein (Supporting Information, Figure S7).
    Taken together, these results demonstrated that compound 1 might destabilize the G-quadruplex structure of IRES-A (WT) RNA, thereby decrease the recognition and binding of the BG4 antibody to the IRES-A (WT) G-quadruplex in a dose-dependent manner.

    Compound 1 Represses Cap-Independent Translation Initiation Mediated via G-Quadruplex within the IRES-A

    The G-rich sequence G774-G790 within the IRES-A of hVEGF-A’s 5′-UTR has been shown to be critical for the translation initiation activity of IRES-A.(17) To explore whether compound 1 affected IRES-A’s function, we constructed a dual-luciferase reporter assay for detecting the translation level of hVEGF-A. For constructing the recombinant plasmids, the 293-nt IRES-A (WT), IRES-A (MU2), or IRES-A (MU3) cDNA was inserted upstream of the Met-luciferase gene (MetLuc) (Figure 6a and Supporting Information, Figure S1a). To be noted, sequence MU2 contains an alternative start codon AUG that might influence on the transcription, and we designed the other mutant sequence MU3 (Supporting Information, Table S1) as another negative reference in the luciferase reporter experiment. By measuring the expression level of luciferase and using the Renilla-luciferase gene (RLuc) as an internal control, this dual-luciferase reporter system allowed for the quantification of the effect of compound 1 on the translation-initiation activity of IRES-A.
    In the dual-luciferase experiments, 1.5 μM was the highest concentration of compound 1 used because the cytotoxicity IC50 of compound 1 in MCF-7 cells was 12.5 μM, and the compound that could be taken up ranged from 65.5% (24 h) to 82.7% (48 h). As shown in Figure 6b, the MCF-7 cells treated with 1.5 μM compound 1 exhibited an 80% decrease in secreted MetLuc activity for the WT plasmid, whereas no significant change was observed for all the mutant plasmids.
    Subsequently, we performed an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) of secreted VEGF-A protein and a Western blot of cellular VEGF-A protein in order to test the influence of compound 1 on IRES-A downstream protein VEGF-A expression in MCF-7 cells. The ELISA results showed a decrease of approximately 75% in VEGF-A protein expression when cells were treated with 1.5 μM compound 1 (Figure 6c). In addition, the Western blot results revealed that VEGF-A protein also decreased after treatment with increasing concentrations of compound 1 (Figure 6d). In contrast, no effect was observed on the transcription process, as shown by RT-PCR and qRT-PCR assays of the IRES-A cDNA in the luciferase plasmids (Figure 6e) and the hVEGF-A gene in the MCF-7 cells genome (Figure 6f,g). Therefore, compound 1 could down-regulate hVEGF-A expression at the translational level but not at the transcriptional level, indicating that this down-regulation likely occurred via interactions with the RNA G-quadruplex structure within the IRES-A region of the hVEGF-A’s 5′-UTR. To further support this finding, a PCR stop assay was performed to confirm that compound 1 did not affect the transcription process, even at a high concentration (10 μM) (Supporting Information, Figure S8).
    To strengthen the data on compound 1’s destabilizing activity and because most G-quadruplex ligands are known to stabilize G-quadruplex structures, it was important to compare the effect of compound 1 on IRES-A G-quadruplex function with that of the G-quadruplex ligands that were previously reported to stabilize this structure. We used compound 2 and the 5-(2-aminoethoxy)-N1,N3-bis(4-(2-aminoethoxy)quinolin-2-yl)isophthalamide (PDS) as reference compounds.(25) As shown in Supporting Information, Figure S9a, compound 2 could stabilize the quadruplex in IRES-A; the melting temperature was over 81 °C because the curve could not reach a platform at the highest detected temperatures. In the dual-luciferase assay (Supporting Information, Figure S9b), compound 2 showed an opposite effect on IRES-A-mediated luciferase expression: increasing the expression of luciferase instead of decreasing the expression by compound 1. However, compound 2 also increased the expression of luciferase inserted by the IRES-MU3 sequence, which might come from its poor selectivity between G-quadruplex and duplex DNA. On the other hand, compound 3 could also stabilize the quadruplex in IRES-A with a melting temperature of over 76 °C (Supporting Information, Figure S9c). Luciferase results turned out that this compound could increase the expression in the WT plasmid but showed no significant effect in the MU3 plasmid (Supporting Information, Figure S9d). This was well supported our hypothesis that destabilizing the quadruplex in the IRES-A region of hVEGF-A can promote the expression of this gene.

    Compound 1 Impedes the Biological Function of the VEGF-A Protein

    On the basis of our results, we attempted to detect the impact of compound 1 on cell migration, which is the main biological function of VEGF-A protein, by performing a cell scratch test(26) and a trans-well test.(27) First, all the MCF-7 cells were treated with 100 μM CoCl2 to create a chemically hypoxic environment for VEGF-A cap-independent expression; the blank control was not treated.(28, 29) The cells were treated with increasing concentrations of compound 1 or 0.03% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) at the same time. On the other hand, small interfering RNA (siRNA) targeting VEGF-A was used as a negative control, and VEGF-A protein was used as another control. Some of the cells treated with compound 1 and incubated for 48 h were further treated with VEGF-A protein and incubated for another 12 h (Figure 7a) to retrigger the migration. Then we quantified the results with ImageJ software to analyze the wound closure of each well. The results showed that the relative wound closure (compared to the DMSO control group) of migrated MCF-7 cells significantly decreased to ∼35% in the presence of 3 μM compound 1; this value was similar to the ∼30% decrease observed for cells transfected with hVEGF-A siRNA group (Figure 7b). Meanwhile, Western blot experiments were conducted to detect the VEGF-A protein expression of corresponding cell samples. This analysis also revealed a drastic reduction when cells were treated with 3 μM compound 1, similar to the result obtained for cells in which the VEGF-A protein was knocked down by siRNA (Figure 7c). These findings indicated that MCF-7 cells migration depended on the amount of VEGF-A protein, and this function of VEGF-A protein could be hindered by compound 1 in a dose-dependent manner. The cells were able to migrate again after treatment with exogenous VEGF-A protein during a 12 h incubation at 37 °C, which further supported that the effect of compound 1 come from its interaction with VEGF-A.
    The trans-well tests were conducted in another human breast tumor cell line, MDA-MB-231, which expresses more VEGF-A protein and has higher invasive and migration capacities than MCF-7 cells.(2) The cells were treated with compound 1 at concentrations ranging from 0.375 to 3 μM. The results were in good agreement with those of the cell scratch test. MDA-MB-231 cell migration was reduced to approximately 25% when cells were treated with 3 μM compound 1 (Figure 7d). To continuously monitor migration, we employed a real-time cell analyzer (RTCA) system to monitor and record the MDA-MB-231 cell migration rate over 24 h after cells were seeded into a 16-well CIM plate in the presence of increasing concentrations of compound 1. Each well consisted of a top chamber, a bottom chamber, a microporous membrane between the two compartments, and microelectrodes as cell-migration sensors. The cell index indicated the amount of cells migrating from the upper compartment to the lower compartment in each well. The results showed that compound 1 suppressed cell migration after 4 h and that this effect plateaued after 10 h (Figure 7e).
    Therefore, in tumor cells, we observed that compound 1 not only down-regulated hVEGF-A expression but also significantly impeded cell migration in a dose-dependent manner.

    Compound 1 Inhibits Tumor Growth in a Human Breast Tumor Xenograft

    Considering the importance of VEGF-A for tumor growth, we evaluated compound 1’s antitumor activity in vivo against a human breast tumor xenograft (MCF-7) in athymic nude mice. The results were presented in Figure 8. An experimental group of five tumor-bearing mice was treated with compound 1 once a day at a dose of 7.5 mg/kg for 20 days. We measured the body weight (Figure 8a) and the tumor diameter (Figure 8b) every day. After 20 days, all the mice were executed and the tumors were dissected and weighed (Figure 8c). In this experiment, doxurubicin, a first-line antineoplastic agent for treating breast cancer, was used as a positive control with a dose of 1 mg/kg once a day.
    First, no significant change in body weight was observed during the treatment (Figure 8a), indicating fewer side effects than the positive control doxurubicin. The negative control group, which was treated with saline only, had an average tumor volume >700 mm3 after 20 days. In contrast, the tumor-bearing mice treated with compound 1 had an average tumor volume of <300 mm3 (Figure 8b). The tumor weight in the presence of compound 1 reduced around 60.1% to a final weight of 0.18 g (Figure 8c). On the other hand, the positive control group showed an effect similar to that of compound 1 with an average tumor volume of <300 mm3 and the tumor weight reduced to 0.14 g (Figure 8c). These data suggested that compound 1 at a dose of 7.5 mg/kg showed antitumor activity on the MCF-7 xenografted mice.
    To determine whether the expression of hVEGF-A was consistently affected within tumors by treatment with compound 1, we assessed the VEGF-A level (brown area in Figure 8d) in tumor tissue using immunohistochemistry (IHC) evaluation. The experimental group exhibited significantly decreased expression relative to the negative control group. The results indicated hVEGF-A down-regulation by compound 1 might contribute to tumor inhibition in vivo.

    Discussion and Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    G-quadruplexes readily formed a stable structure in mRNAs despite the routine role of the putative quadruplex sequence (PQS) in DNA.(30) Applying luciferase assays and nonspecific G-quadruplex binders to reveal the function of the G-quadruplex in cap-independent translation initiation, as in previous studies,(18) was not effective enough for obtaining convincing conclusion. Finding a real specific ligand for this structure would help to reliably illustrate the role of translational regulation in the quadruplex.
    Although several DNA G-quadruplex small ligands, such as pyridostatins,(31) cationic porphyrins,(19) and bisquinoliniums(13, 32) can modulate translation both in vitro or cellular assays and are presumed to target RNA G-quadruplexes, little evidence indicated a compound that could selectively decrease the stability of a RNA G-quadruplex structure. Therefore, we performed a small-scale screening via SPR and identified a new quinazoline, compound 1, with the ability to selectively interact with the RNA-quadruplex-forming mRNA sequence.
    One of the major goals of this study was to determine whether this compound could destabilize the G-quadruplex structure. In addition to CD and EMSA experiments, we also tried to use BG4 to verify this hypothesis. It turned out that the recognition and binding of BG4 to this G-quadruplex structure in the IRES region weakened in the presence of compound 1. It is noteworthy that, considering the complexity of G-quadruplexes’ conformation, there exists another possibility that compound 1 induces a conformational transition or influences on the recognition of transcription repressor, which is hard to distinguish from the destabilizing effect.
    Our present results supported the hypothesis that a small molecule can recognize the G-rich sequence in the IRES region of hVEGF-A mRNA and destabilize the secondary structure and subsequently impede the biological functions of VEGF-A protein to inhibit tumor growth. Determining why this compound has selectivity for the RNA G-quadruplex is very important for the further development of this molecule. However, due to the limitation of cellular detection, it is hard to confirm that compound 1 directly targets the hVEGF-A mRNA within the whole transcriptome.
    Taken together, the ability of compound 1 down-regulating the translation of the hVEGF-A and inhibiting tumor activity opens up new experimental strategies to screen for novel compounds from natural compounds and derivatives that interfere with tumor cells and have therapeutic applications.

    2 Chemistry

    The synthetic pathway for compound 1 was shown in Scheme 1. Intermediates 4, 5, and 6 were prepared mainly according to the previously reported procedure.(32) Compound 7 was prepared by mixing 6 and p-methoxyaniline in EtOH under reflux conditions, and compound 7 reacted with BBr3 in a dichloromethane solution at −78 °C. The resulting solution was stirred overnight at ambient temperature and obtained compound 8. Compound 9 was prepared by mixing 8 and 1, 2-dibromoethane in an acetone solution under a basic condition and refluxed for 24 h, then coupling with diethylamine in tetrahydrofuran with refluxing to obtain compound 10. To obtain the amine functionality, the conventional procedure for the reduction of the nitro group was used, with 80% hydrazine hydrate in the presence of 10% Pd/C and 2-propanol as a solvent. Reaction of amine 11 with chloropropionyl chloride and potassium carbonate in CHCl3 resulted in compound 12. The product was directly used in the next reaction without purification. Finally, the target compound 1 was obtained through aminolysis of compound 12 using 4-methyl piperazine with refluxing.

    3 Microscale Thermophoresis (MST) Measurements

    The BG4 antibody was labeled with commercially available NT-647 dye using a Protein Labeling Kit RED (NanoTemper Technologies, Germany). The concentration of the labeled protein was kept at 100 nM, and the concentration of the in vitro transcribed mRNA was diluted 1:2 from 8 μM 11 times in 10 mM Tris-HCl buffer (100 mM KCl and 0.1 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid [EDTA]). The samples were loaded into standard treated MST-grade glass capillaries. The thermophoresis movements of the fluorescently labeled protein in complex with IRES-A mRNA were measured by monitoring the fluorescence distribution inside the capillary using a MST system (Nano Temper Technologies, Germany). The light intensities of the light-emitting diode (LED) and laser were 20% and 40%, respectively. For the experiments evaluating the binding between RNAs and compound 1, all the procedures and parameters were the same, except the RNA oligomers were labeled with FAM, and the concentration was maintained at 0.5 μM. Additionally, compound 1 was diluted 1:2 from 20 μM 14 times in 10 mM Tris-HCl buffer (100 mM KCl and 0.1 mM EDTA). The experimental thermograms were analyzed by Monolith NT.115 (NanoTemper Technologies, Germany).

    4 Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) Measurements

    SPR experiments were performed on a ProteOn XPR36 Protein Interaction Array system (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA) with a GLH chip. In a typical experiment, biotin-labeled IRES-A (WT), IRES-A (MU1), and hairpin DNA were immobilized in flow cells 1, 2, and 4, leaving flow cell 3 as a blank. Compounds were prepared by 1:2 dilutions in running buffer (50 mM Tris, 150 mM KCl, 0.005% Tween-20, pH 7.4) and injected at a flow rate of 50 μL/min for a 300 s association phase, followed by a 200 s disassociation phase at 25 °C. Data were analyzed with ProteOn manager software using the Langmuir model for fitting kinetic data.

    5 Pull-Down Assay

    First, 5 μM of in vitro transcribed IRES-A mRNAs or synthesized oligomers in 10 mM Tris-HCl buffer (pH 7.4) containing 100 mM KCl were first heated at 95 °C for 5 min and slowly cooled to 25 °C. Then 20 μL of magnetic beads labeled with anti-FLAG label (SIGMA, USA) were incubated for 1.5 h with 200 nM Flag-labeled BG4 antibody or 200 nM BSA protein (as negative control) in 50 μL of TBS buffer (containing 50 mM Tris-HCl and 150 mM NaCl, pH 7.4) at 37 °C. Then 10 μL of 5 μM annealed RNAs or RNA-compound mixture were added to the bead-protein mix, and the beads were captured with a magnet followed by removal of unbound oligomers. Then the bound RNAs were eluted by the elution buffer containing 1% SDS and 0.1 M NaHCO3. The eluted RNAs were then separated on a 1.5% agarose gel or a 16% native polyacrylamide gel, stained with the GelRed (Biotium, China), and visualized on a Tanon 4500sf Imager System (Tanon, China).

    6 RNA Immunoprecipitation Assay (RIP)

    The MCF-7 cells were treated with 2.5 or 5 μM compound 1 or normal Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) for 24 h at 37 °C with CO2. At the end of the incubation, 18.5% formaldehyde was added to each dish to obtain a final concentration of 1% formaldehyde, and the treated cells were incubated at 25 °C for 10 min, followed by the addition of glycine solution. The cells were detached by scraping after adding 1 mL of ice-cold phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) containing 10 μL of the halt cocktail (Thermo Scientific, USA). Then, 100 μL of RIP Lysis buffer (25 mM Tris-HCl, 150 mM KCl, 2 mM EDTA, 1 mM NaF, 1 mM dithiothreitol [DTT], 0.5% NP-40, pH 7.5) containing the halt cocktail and 100 U/mL RNasin ribonuclease inhibitor (Takara, China) were added to the cell pellet and incubated on ice for 10 min. Next, 100 μL of the supernatant was transferred to 400 μL of immunoprecipitation (IP) dilution buffer (Thermo Scientific, USA) containing protease inhibitors and the RNasin ribonuclease inhibitor. For each IP, 500 μL of diluted lysate was added to protein A/G plus agarose beads with 5 μg of BG4 antibody and incubated at 4 °C for 12 h. After centrifugation at 2000g for 30 s, the beads were washed with low-salt washing buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM MgCl2, 2 mM EDTA, 1 mM DTT, 100 U/mL RNase inhibitor, pH 7.4) twice and high-salt washing buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, 300 mM NaCl, 1 mM MgCl2, 2 mM EDTA, 1 mM DTT, 100 U/mL RNase inhibitor, pH 7.4) twice. Then 150 μL of IP elution buffer (Thermo, USA) with 6 μL of 5 M NaCl and 2 μL of 20 mg/mL proteinase K were added and incubated at 65 °C for 30 min. The obtained total RNA samples were used for the dot blotting assay, RT-PCR, and qRT-PCR.

    7 Dot Blotting Assay

    Dot blotting assays were performed as follows: The nylon membrane was washed with 2× saline sodium citrate (SSC) buffer for 10 min at 4 °C prior to use. RNA samples obtained from the RIP assay were diluted to 100 pmol in 100 μL using 2× SSC buffer, and then, the samples were applied to the membrane under vacuum. The cross-linking was conducted using 265 nm ultraviolet (UV) irradiation (1000 μJ/cm2) for 5 min. Then the membrane was placed into a denaturing buffer (1.5 M NaCl and 0.5 M NaOH) for 20 min, followed by a neutralizing buffer (1 M NaCl and 0.5 M Tris-HCl, pH 7.0) for 20 min. A prehybridization step was subsequently performed to prevent unspecific binding, in which the membrane was submerged in a buffer containing 10× Denhardt’s solution, 5× SSC buffer, and 0.5% sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) at 42 °C for 2 h. Hybridization was conducted with 100 pmol biotin-labeled reverse-complement DNA (Biotin-rc DNA) to the IRES-A oligomer at 37 °C for 12 h. Biotin-labeled DNA was detected using the Chemiluminescent Nucleic Acid Detection Module Kit (Thermo Scientific, USA) according to the manufacturer’s protocol.

    8 Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay

    For this assay, 200 ng of constructed pMetLuciferase plasmid (Clontech, USA) containing WT IRES-A or mutant IRES-A and 200 ng of pRL-TK (Promega, USA) was cotransfected into MCF-7 cells using Lipofectamine 2000 (Invitrogen, USA). After 4 h, compounds 1, 2, and 3 were added to the cells at concentrations ranging from 0.1875 to 1.5 μM. The cells were incubated at 37 °C with CO2 for another 24 h, and the transfected cells were first washed with ice-cold PBS to reduce the background signals from the medium. Luciferase assays were subsequently performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions using the Ready-To-Glow secreted luciferase reporter system (Clontech, USA) and the Renilla luciferase assay system (Promega, USA). After a 3 s delay, secreted luciferase signals were collected for 10 s using a microplate reader (Molecular Devices, Flex Station 3, USA), and Renilla signals were collected for 10 s as an internal control. The quantification was performed using a multimode reader (Molecular Devices). The secreted Metluciferase activity was normalized to the Renilla luciferase activity.

    9 Cell Scrape Test

    Approximately 2 × 105 MCF-7 cells were seeded and grown to 80% confluence in 6-well plates, and a scrape was made through the confluent monolayers with a plastic pipet tip. Then, all the cells were treated with 100 μM hypoxia mimetic cobalt chloride (CoCl2) (Invitrogen, USA) to create a chemically hypoxic environment for VEGF-A protein cap-independent expression; the blank control cells were not treated. The cells were divided into control, compound-treated, and compound-treated followed by VEGF-A protein-treated groups. In the control group, the cells were all treated with 0.03% DMSO and transfected with VEGF-A-targeted siRNAs in Opti-MEM reduced serum medium at 37 °C for 48 h or treated with 20 ng/mL VEGF-A protein (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) in Opti-MEM reduced serum medium for 12 h. In the compound-treated group, the cells were treated with increasing concentrations of compound 1 from 0.375 to 3 μM in medium for 48 h. In the compound-treated followed by the VEGF-A protein-treated group, the cells were treated with compound 1 as mentioned above and then with 20 ng/mL VEGF-A protein for another 12 h in Opti-MEM reduced serum medium. All the cells were photographed via phase-contrast microscopy (QIMAGING, Canada) and quantified with ImageJ software.

    10 Transwell Assay

    MDA-MB-231 cells (5 × 104 cells, 100 μL) were plated in the top chambers of 0.8 μm pore trans-wells (Millipore, USA) in Opti-MEM reduced serum medium in the presence or absence of compound 1. Meanwhile, 600 μL of DMEM containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 100 μM CoCl2 were added to the lower chambers. The cells were allowed to migrate for 24 h. At the end of the assay, the cells in the top chamber were removed, and the cells at the bottom of the filter were treated by adding 500 μL of DMEM containing 2.5 mg/mL 3-(4,5)-dimethylthiazol(-z-y1)-3,5-diphenyl-tetrazolium bromide (MTT) to each well. After incubating at 37 °C with 5% CO2 for 4 h, 500 μL of DMSO was added to each well and the plate was gently rotated for 10 min. Absorbance (570 nm) was measured using a microplate reader (BioTek, USA).

    11 xCELLigence Real-Time Cell Analysis (RTCA)

    Cell migration experiments were performed using CIM-16 16-well plates (Roche Diagnostics GmbH, Mannheim, Germany), in which each well consisted of a top chamber and a bottom chamber separated by a microporous membrane containing randomly distributed 8 μm pores. Prior to each experiment, cells were deprived of FBS and incubated for 24 h. Initially, 165 μL (various concentrations of compound 1 in medium with 100 μM CoCl2) and 30 μL of medium were added to both the lower and upper chambers, and the CIM-16 plate was locked in the RTCA DP device (Roche Diagnostics GmbH, Mannheim, Germany) at 37 °C with 5% CO2 for 60 min to achieve equilibrium according to the manufacturer’s guidelines. The lower chambers contained 1× DMEM (Gibco, USA) with or without FBS and were used to assess cell migration when cells were exposed to FBS. Background migration to serum-free (OptiMEM medium) medium was used as a negative control. After the incubation period, a measurement step was performed to determine the background signal generated by cell-free media. To initiate an experiment, cells were detached using 0.25% trypsin and 0.02% EDTA and resuspended in OptiMEM medium. The cells were then counted and seeded in the upper chamber at a concentration of 5 × 104 cells in 100 μL without or with various concentrations of compound 1 that matched the corresponding lower chambers. After adding the cells, the CIM-16 plates were incubated for 30 min at 25 °C in a laminar flow hood to allow the cells to settle onto the membrane according to the manufacturer’s guidelines. Each condition was repeated four times with a programmed signal detection schedule with 15 min intervals for 24 h.

    12 Xenograft Animal Model and Drug Treatments

    BALB/c female nude mice were obtained from the laboratory animal center of Sun Yat-sen University. MCF-7 cells were harvested, pelleted by centrifugation at 800g for 5 min, and resuspended in sterile serum-free medium without EDTA. The cells (1 × 107 in 100 μL) were then subcutaneously implanted into the underarm regions of two mice. After the tumors grew to almost 1000 mm3, the tumor tissues were removed and divided. Then the divided tissues were implanted into the underarm regions of 15 mice. These mice were separated into three groups: negative control, compound 1-treated, and positive control (doxorubicin-treated). Compound 1, doxorubicin, and saline were administered by ip injection to athymic nude mice with human tumor xenografts established using MCF-7 breast cancer cells. Mice were injected ip once a day for 20 days. Negative controls were injected with 150 μL of saline. The positive control group received doxorubicin by ip injection at a dose of 1 mg/kg. Compound 1 was similarly administered to mice at a dose of 7.5 mg/kg. After treating the animals for 20 days, the tumor tissues were collected and IHC assays were conducted using an anti-VEGF-A antibody according to a general procedure.
