    Abstract

    Berberine (BBR), a traditional Chinese medicine, has therapeutic effects on a variety of inflammation-related diseases, but its direct proteomic targets remain unknown. Using activity-based protein profiling, we first demonstrated that BBR directly targets the NEK7 protein via the hydrogen bond between the 2,3-methylenedioxy and 121-arginine (R121) residues. The fact that R121 is located precisely within the key domain involved in the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction allows BBR to specifically block the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction and successively inhibit IL-1β release, independent of the NF-κB and TLR4 signaling pathways. Moreover, BBR displays in vivo anti-inflammatory efficacy in a NEK7-dependent manner. Therefore, we consider NEK7 to be a key target of BBR in the treatment of NLRP3-related inflammatory diseases, and the development of novel NEK7–NLRP3 interaction inhibitors might be easily achieved using NEK7 as a target.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Evolution through natural selection over long periods of time has extensive pharmacological effects on natural products and a precise and complex biological mechanism network, which is worthy of in-depth exploration. Berberine (BBR, Figure 1) has been used in China for half a century as a natural over-the-counter antidiarrheal drug, and its application in various inflammatory disorders, such as type 2 diabetes, hyperlipidemia, atherosclerosis, and Alzheimer’s disease, has been investigated.(1−8) However, its underlying mode of action and direct targets are still unknown, which has greatly limited its further clinical applications. This is a common scientific issue for many natural medicines, such as artemisinin and metformin,(9−12) for which the underlying mechanisms of their multiple pharmacological effects must be determined.
    Three main hypotheses to explain the multiple biological activities of BBR have been proposed as follows: mitochondria,(13−15) intestinal flora,(16−19) and inflammation.(20−22) Canakinumab, known as the interleukin-1β (IL-1β) antibody, was proven to be beneficial for the improvement of several inflammation-related cardiovascular end-points, which suggested that the inhibition of IL-1β might be an effective strategy for the treatment of inflammation-related diseases.(23,24) The production of IL-1β is closely related to the regulation of the nucleotide-binding domain (NOD)-like receptor protein 3 (NLRP3) inflammasome, which consists of the sensor protein NLRP3, NIMA-related kinase 7 (NEK7), and the adaptor protein apoptosis-associated speck-like protein (ASC), as well as procaspase-1.(25,26) The inhibitory effect of BBR on different inflammatory factors, such as IL-1β, nuclear factor-κB (NF-κB), and IL-6, plays a vital role in preventing or alleviating inflammation-related diseases.(14,20−22,27−29) Therefore, taking IL-1β as a biomarker, the direct proteomic targets of BBR against inflammation could be explored and identified to understand and elucidate the mechanism of BBR’s various biological activities from the perspective of inflammation and then discover new targets associated with inflammatory diseases for the development of innovative drugs.
    In this study, using the activity-based protein profiling (ABPP) technology, we demonstrated that BBR directly targets the NEK7 protein by binding to the 121-arginine (R121) residue via hydrogen bonds and specifically blocks the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction to exert anti-inflammatory activity. The in vivo NEK7-dependent anti-inflammatory effect of BBR is also described.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS

    Inhibitory Activity of BBR on IL-1β Secretion

    To evaluate the anti-inflammatory activity of BBR, we initially constructed a screening model for IL-1β secretion in phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate-differentiated human monocytic cell line-1 (PMA-THP-1) cells triggered by lipopolysaccharide (LPS) plus nigericin.(30) As shown in Supporting Information Figure 1a–d, the time- and dose-dependent inhibitory effects of BBR on caspase-1 cleavage and IL-1β secretion were analyzed by both Western blot and enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA), and BBR displayed moderate activity against IL-1β release with an IC50 value of 5.1 μM, which was confirmed in mouse bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) (Supporting Information Figure 1e). In addition, no cell death or apoptosis was observed up to a concentration of 40 μM (Supporting Information Figure 1f–h), indicating that the inhibitory effect of BBR was independent of its cytotoxicity.

    Chemistry

    Construction and Synthesis of BBR Probes

    As displayed in Figure 1, BBR was used as a pharmacophore according to our previous structure–activity relationship (SAR) results,(3,31) and target BBR probes were constructed by connecting BBR with a biotin tag through alkane or polyethylene glycol linkers. Sixteen biotinylated affinity-based BBR probes with different linkers at positions 3, 9, or 10 were designed and prepared, taking 1,(3)5,(3)12,(31) or commercially available jatrorrhizine (JTH) as the starting material. As shown in Scheme 1, all probes were semisynthesized through a three-step procedure, including nucleophilic substitution, deprotection, and condensation reactions with overall yields of 25–51%. For the synthesis of 4a–d, a propane or diethylene glycol linker with a commercially available tert-butoxycarbonyl (BOC)-protected N-terminus was added to the 9-OH of compound 1 in the presence of K2CO3 to produce the key intermediates 2a–d. After the removal of BOC protection, the exposed primary amine group was condensed with the carboxylic acid of biotin to obtain the desired products 4a–d. Following a similar procedure, target probes 8a–d, 11a–d, and 15a–d were also obtained from starting materials 5, JTH, and 12, respectively.
    For the synthesis of the photoaffinity-based BBR probe 16 bearing both diazirine and alkyne functional groups (Figure 1), 7d was condensed with the photoaffinity-labeling (PAL) group(32) with a 32% yield, as shown in Scheme 2. All probes were purified by flash chromatography using CH2Cl2/CH3OH as the gradient eluent.

    Acquisition of Active Probe

    All newly synthesized biotinylated probes were screened for their inhibitory effects on IL-1β-release activities in PMA-THP-1 cells at a concentration of 20 μM by the immunosorbent assay. First, compounds 4a–d with 9-biotinylated substituents were tested, and only 4c and 4d showed weak activity on the downregulation of IL-1β (Table 1 and Supporting Information Figure 2), inferior to that of BBR. When the biotinylated moiety was switched to position 10, compounds 8a–d were formed. Among them, compound 8d with a 9-polyethylene glycol moiety exhibited the highest potency with an inhibitory rate of 83%, which is higher than that of BBR. Then, modification of the 3-position was carried out, and the methylenedioxy ring was selectively opened to yield compounds 11a–d. The results showed that only compounds 11b and 11c displayed moderate inhibitory potency. Compound 11d sharing the same linker with 8d displayed a complete loss in activity, hinting that an appropriate substituent at position 10 might be beneficial for IL-1β inhibitory potency. Meanwhile, compounds 15a–d without methylenedioxy fragments were constructed as negative probes based on our previous SAR showing that the methylenedioxy moiety in BBR was essential for activity.(3,31) As expected, compound 15d, with the same 9-polyethylene glycol linker as compound 8d, completely lost its ability to downregulate IL-1β expression. Therefore, 8d was selected as the positive probe, while inactive 15d was chosen as the negative probe for the next investigation.

    Fishing and Identification of Direct Target Proteins

    Taking 8d and 15d as chemical tools (Figure 2a), we carried out proteomic target fishing and verified the effect of BBR against IL-1β. The overall process of target identification is briefly described in Figure 2b. The conjugates of 8d or 15d and streptavidin magnetic beads were prepared via biotin–avidin interaction and then treated with PMA-THP-1 cell lysates to allow direct binding between the conjugate and target proteins.(33) The proteomes labeled by the BBR probes were separated after streptavidin enrichment, released from the beads, and run on sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE), followed by silver staining, using DMSO treatment as the blank control. Compared with the 15d treatment, one distinct band between 25 and 37 kDa was detected in the 8d treatment (Figure 2c), from which 75 proteins were further identified by liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry/mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS) analysis. Among the 75 proteins, 10 were enzymes, while the rest were ribosomal proteins, spliceosomes, tubulins, and others. Considering that enzymes are the most likely target,(34,35) six enzymes with available crystal structures,(36−39) named PGAM5 (PDB code: 3MXO), NMNAT1 (PDB code: 1KQN), UCK2 (PDB code: 1UFQ), NSUN5 (PDB code: 2B9E), NEK7 (PDB code: 2WQN), and DIM1 (PDB code: 1ZQ9), were first selected as candidate target proteins for further investigation (Supporting Information Figure 3 and Table 1). Based on the molecular docking results with Discovery Studio 4.5 software, BBR fitted well in the binding pockets of three enzymes, NEK7, UCK2, and NMNAT1, which were therefore selected as key candidate targets for further verification by the immunoblot assay. As displayed in Figure 2d, only the NEK7 protein was successfully identified with its specific antibody, implying a direct interaction between BBR and the NEK7 protein.
    To further validate the specific binding between BBR and NEK7 in situ, an active photoaffinity BBR probe 16 with an alkyne group was incubated with PMA-THP-1 cells to directly label the target proteins in situ. Then, under 365 nm light exposure, the diazirine fragment in 16 was readily switched to the highly active carbene, which promptly formed a covalent bond with the target protein in the active pocket. The alkyne group in the formed 16–protein complex was appended with a Cy3-azide or biotin-azide through a click reaction to generate new fluorescent or biotin-labeled complexes(40) for further target identification. As shown in Figure 2f, the differentiated band between 25 and 37 kDa was detected by fluorescence imaging assay, and fluorescence weakening occurred after the addition of BBR (100 μM) competitive inhibition. In addition, the differentiated band was further confirmed to be NEK7 protein by streptavidin-bead enrichment and immunoblot assay, and differentiation could be distinctly weakened by the addition of BBR at the same concentration (Figure 2g). The following pull-down results indicated that 8d could specifically bind to the recombinant protein NEK7, which could also be competitively inhibited by BBR (Figure 2h), again indicating a direct interaction between BBR and NEK7. Next, the dose-dependent interaction between 8d and recombinant NEK7 is demonstrated in Figure 2i. As shown in Figure 2j, BBR displayed a moderate inhibitory effect on NEK7 kinase with an IC50 value of 4.2 μM, consistent with its previous IC50 result against IL-1β release. Furthermore, BBR also showed dose-dependent binding with immobilized NEK-7 in SPR analysis, with a Kd value of 15.6 μM (Figure 2k). Taken together, we demonstrate the specific interaction between BBR and NEK7 and identify NEK7 as a direct proteomic target of BBR at both the biochemical and cellular levels using the ABPP technology.

    Interaction of BBR and NEK7 Via Hydrogen Bonding

    To further understand the interaction pattern of BBR and NEK7, we conducted molecular docking between BBR and NEK7 (Supporting Information Figure 4), mainly focusing on the NEK7 ATP active cavity.(41) Based on the theoretical hydrogen bond and the ionic bond between BBR and binding sites, seven key amino acid residues, including D115, D118, R121, K163, M203, W220, and F236, were predicted to be responsible for the interactions. Then, single-point mutations for all these residues were performed in turns, changing them into alanine (A), which is incapable of forming hydrogen bonds or ionic bonds. As depicted in Figure 3a,b, only the R121A mutation abolished BBR’s binding affinity to NEK7, consistent with the deletion mutation result, indicating that BBR targeted the 34–212 peptide segment in the NEK7 catalytic domain.(25) On the other hand, compound O-BBR lacking a methylenedioxy group (Figure 3c) lost its activity against IL-1β activation completely (Figure 3d), again highlighting the importance of the hydrogen bond between methylenedioxy and R121 (Figure 3a). According to the docking results in Figure 3e, BBR fit well in the ATP-binding pocket of the NEK7 protein by binding to the R121 residue, consistent with the results of the point-mutation experiment.

    Mechanism of BBR in the NEK7–NLRP3 Interaction

    Interestingly, the R121 residue of NEK7 is located exactly in one of the key interaction domains between NEK7 and NLRP3, and a single-point mutation in R121A could greatly abolish NEK7–NLRP3 interactions.(42) Thus, we measured the impact of BBR on the interactions between NEK7 and NLRP3 via a coimmunoprecipitation (Co-IP) assay. As displayed in Figure 4a, BBR dose-dependent inhibition of the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction in 293 cells overexpressing exogenous NEK7/NLRP3 proteins was observed, which was further shown in PMA-THP-1 cells by endogenous Co-IP assay (Figure 4b). In contrast, O-BBR without the methylenedioxy moiety was incapable of blocking the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction (Figure 4c), and BBR failed to suppress their correlation, while 121-arginine was mutated to alanine in 293 cells (Figure 4d). These results further demonstrated that BBR could directly prevent the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction via methylenedioxy binding to the R121 residue of NEK7, thereby suppressing the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome and the subsequent release of a series of inflammatory factors, including IL-1β.

    BBR Specifically Blocks the NEK7–NLRP3 Interaction

    To further identify whether BBR exerts the anti-inflammatory activity by specifically blocking the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction, we evaluated the IL-1β inhibitory potency in the early stage to exclude the impact of the NF-κB and TLR4 signaling pathways, which are also responsible for BBR’s anti-IL-1β activity by downregulating NLRP3 and pro-IL-1β expression after treatment for 12 h or longer.(43,44) We found that BBR dose-dependently inhibited caspase-1 activation and IL-1β production (Figure 5a) but had no effect on NLRP3 and pro-IL-1β expression in either human THP-1 cells (Figure 5a) or BMDM cells (data not shown), suggesting that BBR acts solely by targeting the NEK7–NLRP3 mechanism rather than suppressing the NF-κB and TLR4 pathways against inflammation. Although BBR could regulate NLRP3 expression through other pathways,(45−48) our findings, for the first time, elucidate that BBR could directly bind the NEK7–NLRP3 complex in the early anti-inflammatory stage, independent of the NLRP3 pathways, by BBR, which is an effective method for profiling the complex anti-inflammatory mechanism network of BBR. Then, the above mechanism was further confirmed by the fact that BBR abolished the formation of LPS/nigericin-triggered ASC pyroptosomes (Figure 5b,c). In addition, BBR did not inhibit the activation of IL-1β induced by AIM2 (Figure 5d) or the NLRC4 inflammasome (Figure 5e), which reconfirmed that BBR specifically inhibited the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction mediating IL-1β activation.

    BBR Suppresses NEK7-dependent Inflammation In Vivo

    To verify NEK7 as the target of BBR against inflammation in vivo, a NEK7 knockdown mouse model was successfully established to evaluate the effect of BBR on IL-1β production (Figure 6a). After the mice were infected with adenovirus (Adv) shNEK7 by intravenous injection for 10 days, the expression of NEK7 obviously decreased compared with that in the control group (Figure 6b). Then, WT mice and NEK7 knockdown mice were administered with BBR three times every 12 h, followed by LPS. The mice received an intraperitoneal injection (i.p.) at a dose of 5 mg/kg; therefore, the theoretical exposure concentrations of unbound BBR in the plasma and liver could reach over 1 and 10 μM, respectively.(49,50) BBR obviously alleviated IL-1β production in the control group (Figure 6c), while no significant change was observed in the NEK7 gene knockdown group with BBR treatment, indicating a NEK7-dependent effect of BBR against IL-1β. These results indicated that BBR exerted in vivo effects due to NEK7 inhibition in synergy with other mechanisms.
    We also found that BBR could reduce inflammatory infiltration in the liver compared with that in the LPS-induced group, as expected (Figure 6d), while no significant lesions were found in other tissues (Supporting Information Figure 5). These results provide convincing results that BBR inhibits the secretion of IL-1β by targeting NEK7, thereby exerting its anti-inflammatory effect. Meanwhile, no obvious pathological changes were observed in the NEK7 knockdown group (Figure 6d and Supporting Information Figure 5), which indicated that NEK7 might serve as a druggable target against inflammation with favorable safety characteristics.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    With an ABPP-based chemical proteomic strategy, we first identified NEK7 as an important direct proteomic target of BBR against inflammation and elucidated that BBR exerts its anti-inflammatory efficacy by specifically blocking the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction through hydrogen bonding with the key R121 residue in NEK7, as shown in Figure 7. It is the noncovalent bond that makes BBR display only moderate potency against IL-1β activation and thereby moderate the anti-inflammatory activity. As R121 is located in the key interaction domain of NEK7–NLRP3, BBR specifically blocks the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction via R121, thereby suppressing ASC oligomerization and the activation of NLPR3 inflammasomes as well as of IL-1β release, independent of the NF-κB and TLR4-signaling pathways. Moreover, BBR displays efficacy against IL-1β release in vivo in a NEK7-dependent manner.
    Our data demonstrate for the first time that NEK7 is one of the key direct targets of BBR in the treatment of inflammation-related diseases, such as type 2 diabetes, atherosclerosis, nonalcoholic fatty liver, and neurodegenerative diseases,(1−8,14,51) which might partly explain the mechanism of BBR’s multiple pharmacological activities related to inflammation through the NEK7–NLRP3 interaction. Taking NEK7 kinase as a target for screening, novel NEK7–NLRP3 interaction inhibitors might be easily achieved to combat various inflammation-related diseases. As a multitarget natural product, several other direct proteomic targets of BBR, such as UHRF1, RXRα, actin, and ephrin-B2, have been identified to explain its multiple pharmacological activities, including the anticancer activity.(52−55) Therefore, these findings provide an important explanation to achieve a comprehensive understanding and interpretation of BBR’s polypharmacological activities and its natural evolution. Our results fully support NEK7 as one of the key direct targets of BBR with multipharmacological efficacy, and verification of other direct target proteins of BBR with effects against inflammation is actively underway.

    Chemical Materials and Methods

    All reagents and solvents used are commercially available and used without further purification. Melting points (mp) were obtained with an MPA 100 OptiMelt Automated Melting Point System (Stanford Research Systems, California, USA). 1H NMR spectra were obtained on a Varian INOVA spectrometer (Varian, San Francisco, CA, USA), and 13C NMR spectra were obtained on a Bruker Avance III 400 spectrometer in DMSO-d6 or CD3OD, with Me4Si as the internal standard. ESI high-resolution mass spectra (HRMS) were recorded on an AutoSpec Ultima-TOF mass spectrometer (Micromass UK Ltd., Manchester, UK). Flash chromatography was performed on a CombiFlash Rf 200 (Teledyne, Nebraska, USA). The purities of all the target probes were greater than 95%, as determined by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). The HPLC traces and other analytical data for key target compounds 8d, 15d, and 16 are provided in the Supporting Information (Figures 6–9).

    General Procedure for the Preparation of 4a–d, 8a–d, 11a–d, and 15a–d

    Compound 1 was obtained as a yellow solid according to the reported procedure.(3) To a stirred solution of 1 (1.0 g, 2.8 mmol) in anhydrous CH3CN, K2CO3 (3 eq) was added and heated to 70 °C. Then, the corresponding Br-R-NHBoc or TsO-R-NHBoc (1.5–2.0 eq) was added, then the mixture was stirred for 24 h, cooled to precipitate completely, and filtered. The residue was purified by flash chromatography over silica gel using CH2Cl2/CH3OH as the gradient eluent to afford 2a–d. Compounds 2a–d were dissolved in CH3OH/HCl, and the mixture was stirred for 5–6 h, filtered, and washed with ethanol to acquire 3a–d. D-Biotin (1.1 eq), 1-ethyl-3-(3-dimethylaminopropyl)carbodiimide (EDCI) (1.2 eq), hydroxybenzotriazole (HOBT) (1.2 eq), and triethylamine (2.8 eq) were mixed in DMF (5 mL), to which 3a–d were added, and the mixture was stirred overnight. After flash purification, 4a–d were obtained as yellow solids.

    Biological Evaluation

    Mice

    C57BL/6J mice free of specific pathogens were purchased from SPF (Beijing) Biotechnology Co., Ltd. and maintained under a 12 h light cycle (from 07:00 a.m. to 07:00 p.m.). All animal experiment protocols were approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee at NIFDC, Beijing, China. Animal experiments were approved by the Ethics Committee of the Institute of Medicinal Biotechnology, Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences and Peking Union Medical College, Beijing China, with the approval number IBM20190510D301.

    Reagents

    Anti-IL-1β and anticaspase1 were purchased from R&D Systems; anticleaved caspase1, anti-NLRP3, anti-Flag, anti-HA, anti-EGFP, anti-GAPDH, and antitubulin were purchased from Cell Signaling Technology; and anti-ASC, anti-NEK7, anti-ATPG, anti-UCK2, and recombinant NEK7 were purchased from Abcam. Protein G agarose, ultrapure LPS, nigericin, and CuSO4 were obtained from Sigma-Aldrich. The human IL-1β ELISA kit and the mouse IL-1β ELISA kit were purchased from Boster Biological Technology. AntiFlag magnetic beads and anti-HA magnetic beads were purchased from Bimake. Pierce Streptavidin Magnetic Beads were purchased from Thermo Fisher Scientific. Cy3-azide and biotin-azide were purchased from Lumiprobe Corporation, and tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine (TCEP) and tris[(1-benzyl-1H-1,2,3-triazol-4-yl)methyl]amine (TBTA) were purchased from J&K Scientific.

    Cell Preparation and Stimulation

    BMDMs were isolated from 6–8-week-old mouse bone marrow and cultured for 6 to 7 days in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% FBS and 30% supernatant from L929 cells. HEK-293T and L929 cells were cultured in DMEM with 10% FBS. THP-1 cells were cultured in Roswell Park Memorial Institute medium (RPMI) with 10% FBS and differentiated for 24–48 h with 50 nM PMA before stimulation.
    For the induction of inflammasome activation, PMA-THP-1 cells were plated in 6-well plates and primed for 3 h with ultrapure LPS (1 μg/mL) with or without BBR pretreatment. Then, the cells were stimulated for 30 min with nigericin (5 μM). Cells were transfected with poly(dA:dT) (1 μg) for 8 h using Lipofectamine 2000 with or without BBR pretreatment. The cells were stimulated with Salmonella typhimurium (multiplicity of infection, MOI 100) for 12 h with or without BBR pretreatment. BMDMs were plated in 6-well plates and primed for 1 h with ultrapure LPS (1 μg/mL). After that, BBR was added to the culture for another 2 h and then the cells were stimulated for 30 min with nigericin (5 μM). Cell extracts and precipitated supernatants were analyzed by immunoblot and ELISA.

    Plasmid Construction

    The EGFP-NLRP3 plasmid was purchased from Addgene, and the Flag-NEK7 plasmid was purchased from Sino Biological. Restriction enzymes were purchased from TaKaRa. PCRs were performed using PrimeSTAR Max DNA Polymerase (TaKaRa), and gene mutation was performed using the QuikChange Lightning site-directed mutagenesis kit (Agilent). The deletion mutation of NEK7 was amplified by PCR using Flag-NEK7 as a template. HA-NLRP3 was amplified by PCR using EGFP-NLRP3 as a template. All primer sequences used are listed in Supporting Information Table 2.

    Cell Impedance Assay

    PMA-THP-1 cells were freshly plated at 1 × 104/well in E16 plates (Roche Diagnostics, Basel, Switzerland). The plate was connected to a xCELLigence RTCA SP instrument (Roche Diagnostics) within a humidified cell culture incubator. After 24 h of incubation, cells were treated with BBR. Data were analyzed using RTCA software 1.2 program (Roche Diagnostics). All data are presented as the mean normalized cellular index ± standard error of the mean (SEM) over time.

    Protein Labeling and Pull-Down

    The probe or DMSO was incubated with Pierce Streptavidin Magnetic Beads for 2 h at room temperature. The immobilized beads were washed three times with TBS-T buffer. PMA-THP-1 cells were lysed using HEPES buffer (25 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 2 mM MgCl2) containing 1% NP-40. The protein concentration was determined by the Bradford protein assay (Bio-Rad USA). Then, the lysates were incubated with immobilized beads for 2 h at room temperature. After washing five times with TBS-T, the beads were mixed with 1 × SDS loading buffer, followed by boiling at 95 °C for 10 min. Samples were resolved on a 10% SDS-PAGE gel and silver-stained using the Pierce Silver Stain for Mass Spectrometry kit (Thermo Fisher).

    LC–MS/MS Analysis

    The target bands were identified by a gel-based LC–MS/MS approach (Beijing Qinglian Biotech Co., Ltd., China). MS data were analyzed using MaxQuant software (version 1.5.3.30) with the UniProtKB/Swiss-Prot human database.

    In Situ Labeling of PMA-THP-1 Cells

    For in situ labeling, cells were grown to 80–90% confluence in 6-well plates under the conditions described above. The medium was removed, the cells were washed twice with PBS, and then treated with 2 mL of growth medium containing the probe in the presence or absence of excess BBR (100 μM). After 8 h of incubation at 37 °C/5% CO2, the medium was aspirated, and the cells were gently washed twice with PBS to remove the excessive probe followed by UV irradiation (∼365 nm) for 30 min. The cells were then lysed and pelleted by centrifugation, and the protein concentrations were determined. Equal amounts of samples with different treatments were used for flagging or biotin tagging. For each click reaction, SDS (0.1%), TCEP (1 mM), CuSO4 (1 mM), TBTA (100 μM), and Cy3-azide or biotin-azide (100 μM) were sequentially added to the lysates. Samples were incubated at room temperature for 3 h. Next, the labeled proteins were precipitated with acetone. For fluorescence detection, the samples were dissolved in 1 × SDS loading buffer, followed by boiling at 95 °C for 10 min. For immunoblot detection, the samples were dissolved in HEPES buffer with 1% SDS and incubated with washed beads for 2 h at room temperature. After washing five times with TBS-T, the beads were mixed with 1 × SDS loading buffer, followed by boiling at 95 °C for 10 min. Samples were detected by gel fluorescence or immunoblot blot assay (Tanon 5200 system, Tanon, Shanghai, China).

    NEK7 ATPase Activity

    Purified recombinant human NEK7 (Abcam) was incubated at 37 °C with different concentrations of BBR for 15 min in a reaction buffer. ATP and substrate were then added, and the mixture was further incubated at 37 °C for another 40 min. The amount of ATP converted into adenosine diphosphate (ADP) was determined by luminescent ADP detection with an ADP-Glo Kinase Assay kit (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s protocol.

    Surface-Plasmon Resonance Assay

    The SPR assay was performed on a BIAcore T200 biosensor system (GE Healthcare Life Sciences, Piscataway, NJ, USA) at 25 °C using a CM5 chip. The binding of NEK7 at different concentrations of BBR was performed in 1 × PBS-P+ (GE Healthcare Life Sciences) at a flow rate of 20 μL/min for 120 s. After each binding reaction, a further dissociation time of 60 s was applied after each injection to allow the signal to return to the baseline.

    Co-IP Assay

    For the endogenous interaction assay, PMA-THP-1 cells were stimulated and lysed with HEPES lysis buffer with protease inhibitors. The cell lysates were incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary antibodies and Protein G agarose (Sigma-Aldrich). The proteins bound by the antibody were pulled down and subjected to immunoblotting analysis. For the exogenous interaction assay, HEK-293T cells were transfected with plasmids in 6-well plates. After 6 h, the cells were treated with or without BBR for another 24 h, and then the cells were collected and lysed with HEPES lysis buffer with protease inhibitors. Extracts were immunoprecipitated with anti-Flag or anti-HA beads (Bimake) and then assessed by immunoblot analysis.

    Molecular Modeling Analysis

    An automated docking study was carried out with the 3D structure of NEK7 (PDB code: 2 WQN) using Discovery Studio 4.5 software. The regularized protein was used to determine the important amino acids in the predicted binding pocket. Interactive docking using the LibDock protocol was carried out for all conformers of the ligand BBR to the selected active site after energy minimization using the prepared ligand protocol. The docked compound was assigned a score according to its binding mode onto the binding site.

    ASC Oligomerization Assay

    Briefly, the treated cells were lysed in ice-cold buffer A (20 mM HEPES-KOH, pH 7.5, 10 mM KCl, 1.5 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, and 320 mM sucrose) by shearing through a 21-gauge needle. The supernatants were diluted in a 1:1 ratio with buffer A, mixed with 1 volume of CHAPS buffer (20 mM HEPES–KOH, pH 7.5, 5 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM EGTA, 0.1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, 0.1% CHAPS) and centrifuged to pellet ASC oligomers. The pellets were resuspended in CHAPS buffer containing 4 mM disuccinimidyl suberate to cross-link proteins. The supernatant was discarded and pellets were assessed by immunoblot analysis.

    Immunofluorescence

    PMA-THP-1 cells were plated in 24-well plates and stimulated as described previously. Following treatment, cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde and permeabilized with 0.3% Triton X-100. After blocking with 1% BSA for 1 h, cells were stained with ASC antibodies overnight at 4 °C. After three washes with PBS, cells were stained with Alexa Fluor 484 secondary antibodies (Abcam) for another hour. Hoechst was used to stain nuclei. Images were recorded on a fluorescence microscope (AxioVert.A1; Carl Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany).

    Adv Knockdown

    The mice were transfected with adenovirus (Adv-shNEK7-GFP) to knock down NEK7 (targeting sequence: TGGAGTGCCGGTAGCGTTAAA(26)) and adenovirus-expressing GFP (Adv-GFP) as a mock control. Adv-shNEK7-GFP and Adv-GFP were purchased from GenePharma (Shanghai, China). Briefly, 50 μL of Adv (1011 pfu/mL) was injected into mice via the tail vein.

    LPS-Induced Acute Inflammation in Mice

    Ten-week-old male C57BL/6 mice were injected i.p. with BBR (5 mg/kg) or saline three times every 12 h before the i.p. injection of LPS (30 mg/kg). After 3 h, mice were sacrificed, and blood and tissues were obtained and detected by ELISA or HE staining.

    Statistical Analyses

    All values are expressed as mean and SEM. Statistical analysis was performed by one-way ANOVA using SPSS 16.0 with all data points showing a normal distribution, *P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, and ***P < 0.001. No exclusion of data points was performed. The researchers were not blinded to the distribution of treatment groups when performing experiments and data assessment. Sample sizes were selected on the basis of preliminary results to ensure adequate power.

The mechanism of berberine was investigated using western blot.Berberine was isolated from C. chinensis exts. and showed activity on the HEK293/ephrin-B2 cell membrane chromatog. column. Berberine showed a greater inhibitory effect in high-expressing ephrin-B2 cells (HEK293/ephrin-B2 cells) than in normal HEK293 cells, and decreased the expression of ephrin-B2 and its PDZ binding proteins, which indicates that ephrin-B2 is a target of berberine. Furthermore, berberine downregulates the phosphorylation of VEGFR2 and downstream signaling members (AKT and Erk1/2), which in turn downregulates the expression of MMP2 and MMP9. The above data confirm the inhibitory effects of berberine on ZR-75-30 cell proliferation and cell migration. Overall, our studies demonstrate that berberine inhibits cell growth and migration by targeting ephrin-B2.

        Berberine binds RXRα to suppress β-catenin signaling in colon cancer cells
        Ruan, H.; Zhan, Y. Y.; Hou, J.; Xu, B.; Chen, B.; Tian, Y.; Wu, D.; Zhao, Y.; Zhang, Y.; Chen, X.; Mi, P.; Zhang, L.; Zhang, S.; Wang, X.; Cao, H.; Zhang, W.; Wang, H.; Li, H.; Su, Y.; Zhang, X. K.; Hu, T.
        Oncogene (2017), 36 (50), 6906-6918CODEN: ONCNES; ISSN:0950-9232. (Nature Research)
        Berberine, an isoquinoline alkaloid, is a traditional oriental medicine used to treat diarrhea and gastroenteritis. Recently, we reported that it could inhibit the growth of intestinal polyp in animals and in patients with the familial adenomatous polyposis by downregulating β-catenin signaling. However, the intracellular target mediating the effects of berberine remains elusive. Here, we provide evidence that berberine inhibits β-catenin function via directly binding to a unique region comprising residues Gln275, Arg316 and Arg371 in nuclear receptor retinoid X receptor alpha (RXRα), where berberine concomitantly binding to and synergistically activating RXRα with 9-cis-retinoic acid (9-cis-RA), a natural ligand binding to the classical ligand-binding pocket of RXRα. Berberine binding promotes RXRα interaction with nuclear β-catenin, leading to c-Cbl mediated degrdn. of β-catenin, and consequently inhibits the proliferation of colon cancer cells. Furthermore, berberine suppresses the growth of human colon carcinoma xenograft in nude mice in an RXRα-dependent manner. Together, our study not only identifies RXRα as a direct protein target for berberine but also dissects their binding mode and validates that berberine indeed suppresses β-catenin signaling and cell growth in colon cancer via binding RXRα, which provide new strategies for the design of new RXRα-based antitumor agents and drug combinations.
