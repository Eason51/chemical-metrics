    Abstract

    Effective delivery of anticancer agents across the blood–brain barrier (BBB) required innovative strategies to achieve glioma regression. To resolve this problem, we proposed to develop a metal agent that target and treat glioma based on the unique property of apoferritin (AFt) nanoparticles (NPs). Thus, we synthesized a series of Au(III) 3-(4-metyl piperidine)thiosemicarbazides compounds and analyzed their structure–activity relationships, obtaining a Au agent (C6) with remarkable cytotoxicity in glioma. Moreover, we confirmed that C6 kills glioma cells by inducing lethal autophagy and apoptosis. Importantly, our results revealed that the successfully constructed apoferritin-C6 NPs (AFt-C6 NPs) can effectively cross the BBB, inhibit glioma growth, and selectively accumulate in tumors.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Glioma is a type of malignant tumor that easily spreads into the normal tissue space around the brain.(1) Unfortunately, the few drugs currently available for the treatment of brain tumors are not sufficiently effective, and they are limited by drug resistance and serious side effects.(2) Moreover, the blood–brain barrier (BBB) prevents the accumulation of approximately 98% of the administered hydrophilic and lipophilic drugs in the brain parenchyma, which significantly inhibits their therapeutic efficacy.(3,4) Thus, it is imperative to develop effective, novel drugs and treatment strategies that can circumvent these limitations.
    Autophagy in tumor cells is an adaptive process, and considering the rapid metabolism of tumor cells compared to normal cells, the autophagy levels in the former are higher.(5−10) Currently, the diverse roles of autophagy in cancer treatment have attracted considerable interest in clinical settings.(11−16) However, it should be noted that depending on the interactions between the apoptotic pathway and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress as well as on the duration and strength of autophagy, the mechanism of autophagy may promote the survival of cells instead of destroying them.(13,17) Therefore, to effectively treat glioma, it is necessary to modulate autophagy to promote its lethal activity.(10,18) This subject has been extensively researched, and the studies published thus far indicate that lethal autophagy may be induced by increasing the reactive oxygen species (ROS) content and stimulating apoptosis via mitochondrial dysfunction in response to stress signals.(19,20) Excitingly, on the one hand, recent work has revealed that Au agents can not only generate excessive ROS but also induce cancer cell apoptosis via mitochondrial dysfunction.(21−26) On the other hand, Abram et al. reported that the anticancer activities of Au(III) thiosemicarbazone compounds are remarkably higher than that of thiosemicarbazone alone.(27,28) Considering that metal thiosemicarbazone compounds have shown great promise as next-generation metal anticancer drugs,(29−33) the development of a novel Au thiosemicarbazone compound is expected to significantly improve therapeutic efficiency due to the dual mode of action (dual-MOA) of glioma cells, inducing lethal autophagy as well as apoptosis.
    The design of metal-based drugs for glioma treatment is normally limited by three major obstacles. First, the metal agent is susceptible to destruction by endogenous ions/compounds and it may bind to other macromolecules, resulting in reduced efficiency and unexpected side effects in vivo. Second, the metal agent on its own cannot effectively cross the BBB. Finally, the bioavailability and targeting ability of metal agents in vivo are generally low. Previously, it was shown that nanoparticles (NPs) targeting endogenous receptor-mediated transport (RMT) systems can efficiently deliver drugs across the BBB.(34−36) For example, overexpressed transferrin receptor 1 (TfR1) in BBB endothelial cells (ECs) and glioma cells can deliver ferritin (frt) into the brain.(37,38) Frt is a natural spherical iron storage protein composed of a self-assembled 24 subunit protein shell. Interestingly, when iron is removed from frt, hollowed apoferritin (AFt) may be loaded with different kinds of drugs. Once the drug-loaded AFt binds to TfR1, the drug can penetrate the BBB to enter the brain.(39−41) In acidic environments (pH = 2), the AFt shell disassembles into subunits. However, when the pH increases to neutral, the subunits reassemble, thereby restoring the nanocage structure of AFt. Owing to its unique properties, AFt has been extensively studied as a nanocarrier that can deliver drugs across the BBB.(42−45)
    Taking the above factors into account, we innovatively employed a combinatorial approach encompassing inorganic medicinal chemistry, biomaterials, and pharmaceutics to develop a Au agent to induce lethal autophagy and apoptosis in glioma cells and then constructed AFt-Au agent NPs to cross the BBB and target glioma (Figure 1). Herein, we designed and synthesized a series of Au(III) 3-(4-methylpiperidine)thiosemicarbazide agents (C1–C6) (Figure 2), screened them for their cytotoxic activity, and investigated their structure–activity relationships against glioma cells in vitro (Table 1). C6, showing the highest cytotoxicity, was then used to prepare AFt-Au agent NPs. The ability of these NPs to deliver the Au agent across the BBB was assessed in vitro and in vivo. Finally, the in vivo therapeutic efficiencies of the AFt-C6 NPs and C6 were compared, and their dual-MOA mechanism was confirmed.

    Design and Structure of Au(III) 3-(4-Methylpiperidine)thiosemicarbazide Compounds

    Knowing that Au agents and thiosemicarbazones have separately demonstrated great potential as anticancer drugs, we designed novel Au thiosemicarbazone compounds by combining the advantages of both.(27,46−49) Overall, six Au(III) 3-(4-methylpiperidine)thiosemicarbazide derivatives bearing different salicylaldehyde moieties (C1–C6) (Figure 2) were synthesized. The structures of C1–C6 were verified by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis (Tables S1 and S2). Based on the obtained results, the synthesized Au compounds are isomorphs, and they all have the same skeleton (one tridentate Schiff base ligand, one Au center, and one coordinated Cl atom). Moreover, all prepared compounds contain Au1, O1, N1, S1, and Cl1 in the same plane. This tridentate ligand structure is favorable for forming five- and six-membered closed loop rings, which enhances the stability of Au compounds.

    Structure–Activity Relationships of Au(III) 3-(4-Methylpiperidine)thiosemicarbazide Compounds against Glioma Cells

    In vitro cytotoxicity analyses of the ligands (L1–L6) and Au compounds (C1–C6) were conducted in glioma cells (U87MG) as well as in normal cells (HL-7702). As shown in Table 1, the half maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) values of the Au compounds (IC50 = 0.66–9.31 μM) were significantly lower than those of the corresponding ligands (IC50 ≥ 40 μM). These results suggest that the cytotoxicities of the Au thiosemicarbazide compounds are greater than those of the ligands used to prepare them. Among the investigated Au compounds, C1 with benzene rings at positions C-5 and C-6 showed the lowest cytotoxic activity (IC50 = 9.31 μM ± 0.51 μM) and the Au compounds with methoxy, ethoxy, allyl, or chlorine groups at the C-3 or C-5 positions exhibited greater cytotoxicity (IC50 = 3.51–4.88 μM). However, the most cytotoxic agent was C6 (without substitution at C-3, C-5, or C-6 of salicylaldehyde), which had an IC50 value of 0.66 ± 0.02 μM. Importantly, all of the Au compounds presented greater cytotoxicity against glioma cells than against normal cells (IC50 = 14.07–18.49 μM in human liver HL-7702 cells), which indicated that the Au compounds synthesized herein can selectively destroy cancer cells to some extent. As such, C6 was selected for further study by using AFt NPs.
    To further determine whether the ability of the Au compound uptake by cancer cells was related to their cytotoxic activity against glioma cells, we measured the amount of Au compound uptake by glioma cells. The results showed that the uptake of C6 by glioma cells was greater than that of the other Au compounds (Figure S1A). Indeed, high hydrophilicity and lipophilicity can interfere with drug uptake by cancer cells. Thus, to confirm whether the lipophilicity (log Po/w) of Au compounds plays an important role in the uptake of Au compounds by glioma cells, we measured hydrophilicity and lipophilicity of the Au compounds using the shake-flask technique. Our results demonstrated that the log Po/w values of C1–C6 were 5.12, 4.38, 4.76, 4.54, 4.96, and 2.88, respectively (Figure S1B). Based on Lipinski’s rule, C6 possesses rational lipophilicity and hydrophilicity values to facilitate its uptake by glioma cells because a log Po/w value >3 does not improve the ability of an agent to be taken up by cancer cells, and there will be serious uptake problems when log Po/w > 5 or < 0.

    Feasibility of Developing a Au Agent Based on the Unique Properties of AFt NPs

    Preparation and Characterization of AFt-C6 NPs

    The C6-loaded AFt NPs were prepared according to a previously published method (Figure 3A).(50) The integrity and size of these NPs were analyzed by dynamic light scattering (DLS), and their average diameter was found to be 13.17 nm (Figure S2A,B). Their zeta potential was also determined, and it was used to assess the interactions between the NPs.(51) The obtained results showed that a solution of 0.1 mg/mL AFt-C6 NPs and 0.1 M NaCl had a potential of −18.1 mV, which indicates that the electrostatic properties of the AFt surface before and after C6 loading are similar. The overlay of the AFt-C6 NPs and AFt circular dichroism (CD) spectra in the range of 190–250 nm suggested that the AFt conformation remains unchanged after C6 encapsulation (Figure S2C). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) analysis showed that the AFt-C6 NPs are monodispersed in solution and that they have a well-defined spherical morphology (Figure 3B,C).

    Release Behavior of C6 from AFt-C6 NPs

    Under acidic conditions, the AFt shell breaks down into subunits, thereby releasing the encapsulated C6. To verify the pH-dependent kinetics of C6 release from AFt-C6 NPs, the NPs were incubated for 48 h at pH 7.4, pH 4.7, and pH 2.0 at 37 °C. The release of C6 from these NPs was monitored using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS). As shown in Figure 3D, the AFt-C6 NPs were stable at pH 7.4, and only a minimal amount of free C6 was detected after 48 h of incubation. In contrast, at pH 4.7, the C6-loaded NPs had a half-life of 10 h, and C6 was released up to its maximum of 83% beyond 36 h. At pH = 2.0, C6 was released rapidly from AFt-C6 NPs, reaching a maximum of 95% beyond 5 h. These results suggested that AFt NP-based Au agent delivery systems may be controlled by an endo/lysosome-based drug release mechanism.

    Targeting of the AFt-C6 NPs to Glioma Cells

    Based on the IC50 values listed in Table 1, the AFt-C6 NPs (IC50 ≤ 0.31 ± 0.02 μM) were approximately 2 times more cytotoxic than C6 alone (IC50 ≤ 0.66 ± 0.02 μM) against U87MG cells. However, in human liver HL-7702 cells, the latter exhibited much higher cytotoxicity in vitro (IC50 ≥ 14.07 ± 0.53 μM for C6 and IC50 ≥ 36.77 ± 1.38 μM for AFt-C6 NPs). This indicates that the U87MG tumor-targeting ability of AFt-C6 NPs is greater than that of free C6.
    To confirm the ability of AFt-C6 NPs to selectively target tumor cells, the NPs were incubated with HL-7702 (normal) and U87MG (tumor) cells. Free C6 was also incubated with these same cells for comparison purposes. After 48 h of incubation, the Au and AFt contents in the cells were determined by ICP-MS and western blotting, respectively. The results illustrated in Figure 3E show that HL-7702 and U87MG cells treated with C6 display similar Au contents. However, U87MG cells incubated with AFt-C6 NPs showed significantly higher Au and AFt contents than their HL-7702 counterparts (Figure 3E and Figure S3). The cells were also incubated with cyanine 5.5 (Cy5.5)-labeled AFt-C6 NPs and observed by confocal microscopy. Based on the images presented in Figure 3F,G, the fluorescence intensity of the U87MG cells was much stronger than that in the HL-7702 cells.

    Mechanism of AFt-C6 NP Uptake by Glioma Cells

    According to previous studies, TfR1 mediates the uptake of AFt in U87MG cells via a mechanism driven by endosomes and lysosomes.(34) To confirm this mechanism, the expression levels of TfR1 in U87MG and HL-7702 cells were assessed by western blotting. As shown in Figure S3, TfR1 is more highly expressed on the surface of U87MG cells than on the HL-7702 cell surface. Moreover, confocal microscopy analysis of U87MG cells incubated with Cy5.5-labeled AFt-C6 NPs and stained with the lysosomal marker Lamp1 showed that the NPs were located in the lysosomes (Figure 3F). This suggests that U87MG cells may take up AFt-C6 NPs via lysosome-mediated endocytosis.
    To further confirm the endocytosis mechanism of AFt-C6 NP uptake by glioma cells, we incubated U87MG cells with AFt-C6 NPs after the U87MG cells were pretreated with the endocytosis inhibitor chloroquine (CQ) or with carbonyl cyanide 3-chlorophenylhydrazone (CCCP, a potent mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation uncoupler). U87MG cells pretreated with CQ (20 μM) exhibited cellular uptake blockage of AFt NPs, whereas U87MG cells pretreated with CCCP did not show a significant effect (Figure S4). These results collectively indicated that AFt-C6 NPs are taken up by glioma cells via endocytic transport.

    Models of the AFt-C6 NPs Crossing the BBB In Vitro and In Vivo

    To determine whether AFt-C6 NPs can cross the BBB, an in vitro BBB transcytosis model was established by culturing monolayer mouse brain ECs (bEnd.3) in Transwell plates. Based on ICP-MS analysis, the contents of AFt-C6 NPs in the lower compartment with U87MG cells was five to six times greater than the contents of C6 after 48 h of incubation (Figure 4A,B). This indicates that unlike C6, AFt-C6 NPs can effectively penetrate the BBB of mice in vitro.
    The ability of AFt-C6 NPs to cross the BBB and accumulate in glioma tumors was also investigated in vivo. Healthy mice and orthotopic mice bearing the luciferase-expressing U87MG human glioma tumors were administered Cy5.5-labeled AFt-C6 NPs. The microscopy images presented in Figure 4C show no significant fluorescence in the brain areas of the healthy mice; however, the brains of tumor-bearing mice exhibited rapid accumulation of the fluorescence signal (Figure 4D). This result clearly indicates that AFt-C6 NPs specifically target glioma tumor cells and are capable of traversing the BBB in vivo.
    Furthermore, quantitative analysis of the fluorescence signals showed that the amount of accumulated Cy5.5-labeled AFt-C6 NPs in the brains of orthotropic U87MG tumor-bearing mice was five times greater than that of the accumulation of Cy5.5 (p < 0.001 at 6–12 h post-injection) and that maximum accumulation occurred 6 to 12 h after the injection (Figure 4F). Similarly, AFt enrichment in the brain areas of healthy mice peaked at 6–12 h post-injection (Figure 4E). Therefore, the ability of Cy5.5-labeled AFt-C6 NPs to cross the BBB was solely attributed to AFt.

    Antitumor Behavior of C6 and AFt-C6 NPs in vivo

    Biosafety of C6 and the AFt-C6 NPs in vivo

    To investigate the biosafety of C6 and the AFt-C6 NPs in vivo, Kunming (KM) mice were intravenously injected with NaCl and C6/AFt-C6 NPs. Three days after injection, blood samples were collected from the mice, and the serum levels of blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine (CRE), alkaline phosphatase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), and creatine kinase (CK) were analyzed. The obtained results show that the C6 and AFt-C6 NP treatments do not alter the blood levels of BUN, CK, and CRE in KM mice compared to the NaCl group. Therefore, C6 and AFt-C6 NPs have low nephrotoxicity and cardiotoxicity. Moreover, considering the similarity in the ALT and AST levels of C6-, AFt-C6 NPs-, and NaCl-treated mice (Table S3), the C6 and AFt-C6 NPs did not damage the liver.

    Therapeutic Efficacy of C6 and AFt-C6 NPs In Vivo

    The effects of C6 and the AFt-C6 NPs on the growth of glioma tumors were examined using an orthotropic luciferase-expressing human glioma tumor-bearing (Luc-U87MG) mouse model. The mice were repeatedly injected with C6, AFt-C6 NPs, temozolomide (the first-line chemotherapeutic agent for glioma treatment), or 0.9% normal saline (control group), and the fluorescence signals in their tumors were recorded before treatment, after seven injections, and after the final injection. The in vivo images presented in Figure 5A show that at the end of treatment, the fluorescence intensity in the AFt-C6 NP-treated mice was lower than that detected in the C6-treated mice, and both signals were weaker than the control group signal. In addition, the tumor volumes in the AFt-C6 NP mice were much smaller than those observed in the temozolomide, control, and C6 groups (Figure 5B). After the last injection, the inhibition rates (IRTs) in the temozolomide, C6, and AFt-C6 NP groups were 45.6 ± 2, 22.8 ± 1, and 69.2 ± 3%, respectively (Figure 5C–E). Overall, these results indicate that AFt-C6 NPs can efficiently suppress the growth of orthotopic glioma tumors in mice and that the therapeutic effects of these NPs are much greater than that of free C6 and temozolomide.

    Side Effects of C6 and AFt-C6 NPs In Vivo

    To assess the potential side effects of C6 and AFt-C6 NPs in vivo, the weights of the mice and their organs were recorded at the end of the treatment program, and tissue damage was evaluated via histological examination by hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining. The results demonstrated that the weights of mice treated with AFt-C6 NPs and C6 was higher than those of the mice administered normal saline (Figure 6A). We speculated that the normal control group of mice lost their appetite as the tumors grew, and the weights of the tumors might have caused the normal control mice to become anorexic; however, the groups of mice injected with C6 and the AFt-C6 NPs did not lose their appetite. Furthermore, the heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney tissues isolated from the mice in different groups showed no obvious histopathological abnormalities, lesions, or degenerations (Figure 6B). Similarly, there were no significant changes in the organ weights in the treated group compared to the untreated group (Figure 6C). This confirms that both C6 and AFt-C6 NPs are biocompatible in mice.

    Bioavailability of C6 and AFt-C6 NPs In Vivo

    The Au content in the tumors and major organs of C6- and AFt-C6 NP-treated mice was determined using ICP-MS, which was performed 2 days after the last treatment. As shown in Figure 6D, the tumors in the AFt-C6 NP-treated mice contained significantly more Au (p < 0.05) than the C6-treated mouse tumors. Moreover, reduced Au accumulation was detected in the major organs of the former group. This indicates that the AFt-C6 NPs selectively target and accumulate in tumors. To confirm this conclusion, in vivo tracking experiments of Cy5.5-labeled AFt-C6 NPs were conducted. The images presented in Figure 6E show high fluorescence intensity in the brain tumor area, with minor fluorescence in the major organs.

    Confirming the Dual-MOA Anticancer Mechanism of C6

    In general, cell death occurs through three pathways: apoptosis (type I), autophagy (type II), and necrosis (type III).(52) The former two pathways (apoptosis and autophagy) are triggered by stress signals, such as increased levels of ROS and mitochondrial dysfunction.

    Induction of U87MG Cell Apoptosis In Vitro and In Vivo

    Analysis of the TdT-mediated dUTP nick-end labeling (TUNEL)-stained brain tumor slices collected from different groups showed obvious variations in tumor tissue morphology between the treated groups and the untreated group. The apoptosis rates in the tumor tissues of U87MG-tumor-bearing mice treated with AFt-C6 NPs and C6 were 77.6 and 12.2%, respectively (Figure 7A,B). In addition to their in vivo effects, C6 and AFt-C6 NPs induce U87MG cell apoptosis in vitro, as determined by the Annexin V-FITC/propidium iodide (PI) staining analysis illustrated in Figure 7C.
    According to previous studies, apoptosis may be induced by mitochondrial dysfunction.(53) To determine whether the apoptosis of C6 and AFt-C6 NP-treated U87MG cells is caused by mitochondrial dysfunction, accumulation of the administered Au in the mitochondria was monitored, and the mitochondrial membrane potential and adenosine triphosphate (ATP) levels were assessed. The images presented in Figure S5 show an obvious accumulation of Au in the mitochondria of U87MG cells treated with AFt-C6 NPs. Moreover, the mitochondrial membrane potential and ATP levels clearly decreased after treatment with C6 and AFt-C6 NPs, indicating that mitochondrial dysfunction was underway (Figure 7D,E).(54−56)
    High levels of ROS were also detected in C6- and AFt-C6 NP-treated tumor cells (Figure 7F,G). To confirm whether the mechanism of C6 production of ROS in glioma cells occurred by a valence change in the Au agent, we used cyclic voltammetry to measure the AuI/III redox potentials of C6. The results showed that C6 lies within the range accessible to both cellular oxidants and reductants, and both the Au(I) and Au(III) forms are chemically stable (Figure S6). Thus, C6 could redox cycle to generate intracellular ROS.
    The expression levels of C/EBP homologous protein (CHOP), phosphorylated RNA-dependent protein kinase-like endoplasmic reticulum kinase (PERK), and phosphorylated eukaryotic initiation factor 2a (eIF2a) in U87MG cells were determined by western blotting. U87MG cells were incubated with C6 and AFt-C6 NPs, and 48 h later, the protein levels were analyzed. Based on the results illustrated in Figure 7H,I, both treatments increased the expression levels of CHOP, PERK, and eIF2a, resulting in ER stress, which leads to apoptosis.
    The mitochondrial-mediated apoptosis proteins were also evaluated, and the western blots presented in Figure S7 indicate that C6 and AFt-C6 NPs suppressed the expression of the prosurvival protein Bcl-2 while enhancing the levels of proapoptotic proteins caspase-3, caspase-9, Bak, p53, and Cyt-c. Overall, the results suggest that C6 and AFt-C6 NPs can induce the death of U87MG cells via the mitochondrial-mediated apoptosis pathway.

    Inducing Lethal Autophagy in U87MG Cells In Vitro and In Vivo

    The increased ROS, ER stress, and mitochondrial dysfunction levels in C6- and AFt-C6 NP-treated tumor cells suggest that the cytotoxic effects of the Au agent may include autophagy. To confirm this hypothesis, autophagy markers were evaluated by monitoring the regulation of p62 and the transformation from LC3-I into LC3-II.(57) The western blots and immunohistological staining images shown in Figure 8A–C demonstrate that the C6 and AFt-C6 NP treatments upregulated the expression of LC3-II relative to the untreated group while downregulating the expression of p62. We also investigated autophagy using red-green fluorescent protein (RFP-GFP)-fused LC3 (a specific label for autophagosome formation). As shown in Figure 8D, C6 and AFt-C6 NPs enhanced the accumulation of autophagosomes in U87MG tumor cells after 24 h of incubation. Moreover, transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis of autophagy in U87MG tumor xenograft mice treated with C6 and AFt-C6 NPs generated more autophagosomes than their untreated counterparts (assessment conducted 2 days after the last injection) (Figure 8E).
    To confirm that autophagy is implicated in the death of U87MG cells treated with AFt-C6 NPs, the cells were incubated with 3-methyladenine (3-MA), a molecule that inhibits the initiation of autophagy and blocks autophagosome formation, for 1 h before treatment with the Aft-C6 NPs. The obtained results showed that in the AFt-C6 NP group, preincubation with 3-MA decreased the death rate of U87MG cells by ∼40%, with stronger death inhibition detected at higher concentrations of 3-MA (Figure S8). In addition, the preincubated cells (2 mM 3-MA) presented reduced levels of autophagosome accumulation (Figure 8F), upregulated expression of p62, and downregulated expression of LC3-II (Figure 8G,H). This confirms that AFt-C6 NPs induce the death of U87MG cells by promoting lethal autophagy.

    Relationship between Apoptosis and Autophagy in Glioma Cells

    Apoptotic and autophagy pathways may interact, or they may proceed independently.(19) To investigate the relationship between these two signaling pathways in AFt-C6 NP-induced U87MG cells, the expression level of the apoptotic executioner caspase-3 was analyzed in the presence and absence of 3-MA. The results illustrated in Figure S9 show that after 48 h of treatment, similar levels of caspase-3 were detected in both groups of U87MG cells (with and without 3-MA preincubation). This indicates that the apoptotic and autophagy pathways induced by AFt-C6 NPs are independent of each other (Figure 9).

    Discussion

    Jump To
    The design of effective drugs for the treatment of brain tumors is difficult. Normally, drugs are limited by low tumor selectivity, low therapeutic efficiency, and/or in vivo side effects. To overcome these shortcomings, we developed novel dual-MOA agents based on metal/ligand complexation. In addition to their inhibitory effects on brain tumor growth (Figure 5), the designed Au(III) salicylaldehyde-3-(4-methylpiperidine)thiosemicarbazide complexes are characterized by a few side effects and a dual-MOA mechanism (inducing lethal autophagy and apoptosis) (Figures 7 and 8). Therefore, it is worthwhile to develop C6 as a dual-MOA lead metal drug.
    However, similar to most commercially available brain tumor drugs, Au(III) salicylaldehyde-3-(4-methylpiperidine)thiosemicarbazides cannot efficiently penetrate the BBB on their own. To resolve this issue, we proposed to take advantage of the properties of AFt to construct nanocarriers to overcome this problem. Indeed, our results confirmed our hypothesis because AFt NPs facilitated more Au agents to cross the BBB (Figures 3 and 4). Interestingly, release of the drug may be controlled simply by manipulating the pH conditions (Figure 3). At pH 7.4, only 5% of the Au agents are released from the AFt NPs, whereas at pH 4.7, 83% of these agents may be released. This shows that the AFt-Au agent NPs proposed herein are stable in circulating blood and that they disassemble only when they reach cancer cells, where the environment is acidic.
    In vivo experiments conducted in Luc-U87MG model mice showed that AFt-C6 NPs can inhibit the rate of tumor growth by 69.2%, which is 3.0 times greater than the tumor inhibitory rate of free C6 (IRT = 22.8%) (Figure 5). Furthermore, the AFt NPs significantly enhanced the bioavailability of C6 in vivo, promoting the selective accumulation of this agent in tumor cells and decreasing C6 accumulation in the main organs (Figure 6). Notably, the use of AFt NPs to deliver Au therapeutic agents across the BBB and into glioma cells is an effective strategy for the development of brain cancer treatments.

    Conclusions

    Jump To
    To effectively cause glioma regression, a Au agent was loaded into AFt NPs and delivered to tumor cells. The proposed AFt-Au agent NPs combined the advantages of the dual-MOA Au agent that kills glioma cells by inducing lethal autophagy and apoptosis with the ability of AFt to penetrate the BBB. Moreover, AFt NPs improved the bioavailability and targeting ability of the Au agent in vivo. Collectively, our results allow for the development of a novel dual-MOA metal agent that can effectively target glioma.

    Synthesis and Characterization of AFt-C6 NPs

    To synthesize the AFt–Au agent NPs, AFt was added to 1 mg/mL Au agent solutions, and the final protein concentration was adjusted to 1 mg/mL. Subsequently, 1 M HCl was added to decrease the pH and induce the dissociation of AFt. The acidic pH value of 2.0 was maintained for approximately 15 min, and then 1 M NaOH was added to restore the pH neutrality of the solution (pH of 7.5). The mixture stirred at 100 rpm and room temperature for 2 h and then dialyzed against saline water (NaCl, 0.15 M, renewed six times) for 48 h to eliminate excess Au agents. Thereafter, the solution was centrifuged three times at 10,000 rpm for 15 min each time. The precipitates were subsequently removed, and the NP solutions were concentrated using a Millipore spin filter (10,000 Da cutoff). A BCA protein assay kit (Beyotime Institute of Biotechnology, China) was used to determine the AFt concentration in each solution. The analyses were performed in triplicate, and bovine serum albumin was used as a standard. The Au content was determined using ICP-MS.
    The AFt-C6 NPs were characterized by CD and field SEM (FEI Quanta 200). The CD spectra of samples loaded into a 0.1 cm quartz cell were recorded on a Jasco J-810 spectropolarimeter operating in the far-ultraviolet wavelength range of 200–250 nm. The analyzed samples had a protein concentration of 0.5 mg/mL (0.1 M NaCl), and the spectral data were averaged over three scans. The hydrodynamic diameters of the NPs were determined using a BI-200SM DLS system (Brookhaven Instruments Co., Holtsville, NY). NP solutions with a protein concentration of 0.1 mg/mL (0.1 M NaCl) were filtered through a 0.2 μm filter membrane before analysis, and the particle size measurements were performed in triplicate. Finally, the zeta potential of the AFt-C6 NPs was measured in NaCl solution (0.1 M) at a protein concentration of 0.1 mg/mL.

    In Vivo Animal Studies

    Animal Subjects and Tumor Models

    All animal experiments performed in this study were approved by the Chinese Academy of Sciences Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. The Institutional Animal Ethical Committee (IAEC) project number is SL-007-0. The animals included 15 6 week-old female BALB/c nude mice (Animal Center of the Chinese Academy of Medical Science) that were divided into three groups of five.

    Acute Toxicity Assay

    The acute toxicity of C6 and the AFt-C6 NPs was assessed in eighteen healthy Kunming mice (an equal number of male and female subjects, aged 3–4 weeks, weighing 18–22 g) divided into three groups of six. The mice were intravenously injected with C6 or AFt-C6 NPs at a dose of 2.5 mg/kg body weight, and NaCl was used as a control. Fourteen days later, serum samples were collected from each group, and the levels of BUN, CRE (renal function), CK, ALT, and AST were analyzed.

    In Vivo Antitumor Activity

    The orthotropic luciferase-expressing human glioma tumor model was prepared by introducing 5 × 105 Luc-U87MG cells into the left cerebral hemisphere of mice (15 mice in total) using stereotaxic apparatus. The growth of an orthotropic glioma was monitored by bioluminescence imaging (IVIS imaging system; Lumina LT Series III, PerkinElmer) of an intraperitoneally injected luciferin substrate (150 mg/kg). After approximately 10 days of preliminary testing, a fluorescence signal was observed, indicating that a brain tumor had been generated. The mice were then randomly divided into four groups and treated with intravenous injections of normal saline (vehicle), temozolomide, free C6, or AFt-C6 NPs at a dose of 2.5 mg/kg body weight. One injection was administered every 2 days. The body weights of the mice were measured before every injection. The growth of the orthotropic glioma (Luc-U87MG) was monitored by the in vivo imaging of luciferin. After the last injection, the mice were sacrificed, and their major organs and tumor tissues were collected for immunohistochemical analysis.

    TUNEL Assay

    Paraffin-embedded glioma cancer tissue sections (5 μm) were warmed for 30 min (64 °C), deparaffinized, and then rehydrated. The apoptotic cells in these tissues were assayed using a TUNEL assay kit (A-23210; Molecular Probes, Eugene, OR) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Images of the analyzed tissues were recorded using an Olympus BX43 fluorescence microscope (Japan).

    Selectivity of C6 and AFt-C6 NPs In Vivo

    After the last injection of C6/AFt-C6 NPs, the tumor tissues and major organs of the mice were collected and homogenized. The samples were placed in a freeze-drying machine, and then 0.2 g of each sample was soaked in a solution of 3 mL of concentrated nitric acid and 1 mL of 30% H2O2. The Au contents in the tissue samples (tumors and major organs) were then determined by ICP-MS.

    Release of the Au Agent from AFt NPs

    The release of C6 from AFt-C6 NPs was analyzed in vitro. First, the cell matrix and interstitial space environment were stimulated using a buffer solution of pH 7.4, pH 4.7, or pH 2.0. Then, the NPs were dialyzed in a dialysis pocket, and 2 mL of the dialyzed solution was dispersed in a beaker containing 100 mL of buffer (pH 4.7 or pH 7.4). The amount of released Au agent was monitored as a function of time (seven time points at each pH condition) using ICP-MS.

    Statistical Analysis

    All of the experiments were repeated three to five times, and statistical analysis of the repeated measured values was performed using Student’s t test. Values were considered to be statistically significant at p values ≤0.05, and the results are expressed as the mean ± SD using SPSS 13.0.
