    Abstract

    A facile procedure for in situ peptide cyclization and phthalocyanine conjugation was developed by utilizing a bifunctional linker incorporated with a bis(bromomethyl)benzene unit and a cyclopentadiene moiety. These functional groups facilitated the nucleophilic substitution with the two cysteine residues of the linear peptides followed by the Diels–Alder reaction with the maleimide moiety attached to a zinc(II) phthalocyanine. With this approach, three cyclic peptide–phthalocyanine conjugates were prepared in 20–26% isolated yield via a one-pot procedure. One of the conjugates containing a cyclic form of the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR)-binding peptide sequence CMYIEALDKYAC displayed superior features as an advanced photosensitizer. It showed preferential uptake by two EGFR-positive cancer cell lines (HT29 and HCT116) compared with two EGFR-negative counterparts (HeLa and HEK293), resulting in significantly higher photocytotoxicity. Intravenous administration of this conjugate into HT29 tumor-bearing nude mice resulted in selective localization in tumor and effective inhibition of tumor growth upon photodynamic treatment.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Photodynamic therapy (PDT) has emerged as a promising anticancer modality.(1−3) It utilizes light with an appropriate wavelength to excite a light-sensitive drug localized in tumor to generate cytotoxic reactive oxygen species (ROS) through the interactions with molecular oxygen. Owing to the presence of various potential advantages, such as a convenient and noninvasive treatment procedure, without the problem of drug resistance, and few side effects, PDT has received growing attention in the medical community, and there has been considerable interest in the development of advanced photosensitizing systems with a view to improving the therapeutic outcome.(4−7) To date, a handful of photosensitizers have been clinically used or under clinical trials for oncological indications, which are mainly based on porphyrins, chlorins, and phthalocyanines.(2)
    One of the major challenges in PDT is the low tumor specificity of the photosensitizers, which inevitably would cause undesired photodamage on normal cells and tissues. In addition, the limited water solubility and strong aggregation tendency of common tetrapyrrole-based photosensitizers would also reduce their ROS generation efficiency. To address these issues, photosensitizers can be conjugated with biomolecules, such as antibodies, proteins, peptides, and monosaccharides that can bind specifically with the corresponding cancer-associated biomarkers.(8,9) Among these tumor-targeting ligands, peptides are particularly attractive owing to their ease of chemical modification, high biocompatibility, low immunogenicity, and high tissue permeability.(10−13) Using the phage display library, a number of peptide sequences which can bind specifically with various tumor-associated receptors have been identified.(14,15) Being an important target of cancer therapy, epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) is a transmembrane receptor overexpressed in a range of cancer cells.(16,17) A number of EGFR-targeting peptides, including LARLLT,(18) QRH*,(19) and GE11,(20) have been conjugated with phthalocyanine-based photosensitizers with a view to enhancing their tumor-localization property. However, the low stability of the linear peptides used toward enzymatic proteolysis in serum may affect the pharmacokinetics and bioavailability and eventually the therapeutic efficacy.(21)
    To circumvent this problem, cyclization of these peptide sequences could enhance the metabolic stability. In general, cyclic peptides have higher proteolytic stability than the linear counterparts.(22) Their macrocyclic and rigid architecture could also enhance the bioactivity and binding specificity because of the smaller number of possible conformations to dock onto the corresponding protein targets.(23,24) As a result, cyclic peptides have been widely used as potent and specific inhibitors against proteins and ligands toward tumor-associated receptors.(25−27)
    A conventional peptide cyclization method involves the activation of the C-terminus to condense with the N-terminus of a fully protected peptide via direct amide coupling, followed by the global deprotection.(28) However, this method needs to overcome the disfavored entropy during the cyclization process and the reaction conditions need to be optimized to avoid epimerization and polymerization. Macrocyclization of long peptides involving more than 15 amino acids cannot proceed readily because of their preferred extended conformation arising from the more stable all-trans configuration of amide bonds.(29) In addition to the direct coupling, other methods such as lactamization, azide–alkyne cycloaddition, and ring-closing metathesis have also been used in peptide cyclization.(30−32) These methods, however, have inherent limitations, such as the use of unnatural amino acids or metal catalysts and the need of stringent reaction conditions. Recently, Heinis et al. reported an efficient peptide cyclization methodology which involves dibenzylation of the sulfhydryl groups of two cysteine residues in a linear peptide with a bis(bromomethyl)benzene moiety.(33) This methodology does not require prior protection of the amino acids and can proceed smoothly under a mild basic condition (pH 7–9). Using this synthetic strategy, we have recently synthesized an antimicrobial cyclic peptide–phthalocyanine conjugate for antimicrobial PDT.(34) We report herein an extension of this methodology to prepare a series of phthalocyanine-based photosensitizers conjugated with a cyclic peptide that can potentially bind to human colorectal carcinoma cells with a view to developing selective theranostic agents for bioimaging and photodynamic treatment of colon cancer, which is one of the most common malignancies worldwide.(35) One of these conjugates was found to exhibit strong binding affinity toward EGFR overexpressed in colorectal carcinoma cells, resulting in high and selective in vitro and in vivo theranostic efficacy.

    Results and Discussion

    Molecular Design and Synthesis

    This one-pot synthetic strategy involves the use of a linear peptide with two cysteine residues, which undergoes site-selective nucleophilic substitution with the bis(bromomethyl)benzene unit of a linker appended with a cyclopentadiene moiety at the other terminus that can further conjugate with a maleimide-functionalized zinc(II) phthalocyanine via the Diels–Alder reaction (Figure 1). Compared with the conventional cyclization and conjugation methods, this one-pot strategy could prepare cyclic peptide–phthalocyanine conjugates readily using fully unprotected peptide precursors under mild reaction conditions. For the peptide component, three peptide sequences that are potential ligands for human colorectal carcinoma cells were selected, including H2N-CPIEDRPMC-CONH2 (labeled RPM), which was reported to show high affinity toward poorly differentiated colon carcinoma cells,(36) AcNH-CTPSPFSHC-CONH2 (labeled TCP-1), which was found to localize in the metabolically active part of colon tumor and the neighboring blood vessels,(37) and H2N-CMYIEALDKYAC-CONH2 (labeled EBP), which could target EGFR-overexpressed colon carcinoma cells.(38) They were prepared manually according to our previously described procedure.(19) The bifunctional linker 1 was prepared by treating 1,3,5-tris(bromomethyl)benzene with one equiv of sodium cyclopentadienylide. For the photosensitizer part, a zinc(II) phthalocyanine core was used due to its superior photophysical properties and high photostability.(7) To facilitate the coupling through the cyclopentadiene moiety of the linker, a maleimide unit was introduced to the phthalocyanine. With an electron-deficient and sterically unhindered π system, maleimide serves as an excellent dienophile.(39) Compared to furan and 2,4-hexadiene derivatives, maleimide has a much higher reactivity toward cyclopentadiene and the resulting endo cycloadduct also has a higher thermal stability which does not undergo the retro-Diels–Alder reaction below 275 °C.(40) Moreover, the Diels–Alder cycloadducts are generally more stable in serum than the maleimide–thiol conjugates formed via Michael addition.(41) The target maleimide-substituted phthalocyanine 2 was synthesized according to our previously described procedure.(34)
    The conditions of peptide cyclization were first optimized for the reaction of EBP and 1,3-bis(bromomethyl)benzene. Various peptide concentrations and solvent systems were used, and the yield of the product was determined by reversed-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). As shown in Figure S1 in the Supporting Information and Table 1, the use of a mixture of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) and NH4HCO3 buffer (pH 8.0) (1:1 v/v) as a solvent could lead to the formation of cyclic peptide 3 in 74 and 55% HPLC yield after 2 h when a peptide concentration of 0.5 and 1 mM was used, respectively. Some side products were also formed in this reaction. It seems that a lower concentration of peptide could favor the cyclization process. By changing the solvent to DMF/borate buffer (pH 10.0) (1:1 v/v), the reaction proceeded smoothly, and the yield of the product was essentially quantitative regardless of the peptide concentration, showing that the buffer could largely affect the cyclization process. It is likely that as the pKa of cysteine is 8.3, the use of a buffer at higher pH (10.0 vs 8.0) can promote the deprotonation process, favoring the formation of more nucleophilic thiolate anions for the dialkylation. The cyclic peptide 3 was purified by HPLC and characterized with matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight (MALDI-TOF) mass spectrometry (Figure S2 in the Supporting Information).

    The optimized conditions were then used to perform the one-pot cyclization of all the three linear peptides mentioned above with the bifunctional linker 1, followed by conjugation with the maleimide-functionalized phthalocyanine 2. The course of the formation of the resulting conjugates was monitored by HPLC. Figure 2 shows the results for the reaction of EBP given for illustration. First, the linear peptide EBP (1 mM) was treated with 1 (1 mM) in a mixture of DMF and borate buffer (pH 10.0) (1:1 v/v). After stirring at room temperature for 2 h, the reaction was essentially completed as shown by the disappearance of the signals due to the two starting materials and the occurrence of a strong signal assignable to the cyclopentadiene-functionalized cyclic peptide 4 (Figure 2c), which was characterized with MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry (Figure S3 in the Supporting Information). Without isolating the intermediate product, 2 (2 mM) was added and the resulting mixture was stirred continuously overnight. The target conjugate 5 formed via the Diels–Alder reaction was obtained in 95% HPLC yield (Figure 2e). The conjugate could be purified readily by HPLC and was characterized with MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry (Figure S4 in the Supporting Information). Through this one-pot procedure, the conjugate was obtained in 26% isolated yield. The other two conjugates 6 and 7 were also prepared by the same protocol using the linear peptides RPM and TCP-1, respectively, as the starting materials (Figure 3). The results were similar to those for the preparation of 5 (Figures S5 and S6 in the Supporting Information and Table 2). All the intermediate and final products were also characterized using MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry (Figures S7–S10 in the Supporting Information). It is worth noting that these in situ cyclization and conjugation processes do not require prior protection of the amino acids and the use of any metal catalysts and coupling reagents. This synthetic strategy also has the advantages of high efficiency, clean transformation, and ease of operation. It is envisaged that this methodology can be extended further for the preparation of other cyclic peptide–theranostic agent conjugates for various biomedical applications.

    It is worth mentioning that the peptide cyclization process was susceptible to the solvent and should better be carried out in a mixture of DMF and borate buffer (pH 10.0) (1:1 v/v). If the bifunctional linker 1 is coupled with phthalocyanine 2 first, the resulting conjugate would not be able to dissolve in this solvent system, and solvents with a higher organic solvent content would be required that would affect the reaction yield. Hence, this methodology was designed as a one-pot stepwise process with peptide cyclization occurring prior to coupling with photosensitizers. This stepwise strategy using the bifunctional linker 1 also provides a high flexibility for the preparation of other cyclic peptide-conjugated theranostic agents.

    Electronic Absorption and Photophysical Properties

    Electronic absorption spectra of 5 and 2 were first measured in DMF. The latter was used as a reference compound for comparison. Both compounds showed an intense and sharp Q band at 688 nm, which strictly followed the Lambert–Beer’s law (Figures 4a and S11 in the Supporting Information), reflecting their nonaggregated nature. Upon excitation at 610 nm, both compounds exhibited a fluorescence band at 700 nm with a fluorescence quantum yield (ΦF) of 0.19 (for 5) or 0.21 (for 2) relative to the unsubstituted zinc(II) phthalocyanine (ZnPc) (ΦF = 0.28 in DMF) (Figure 4b).(19) The singlet oxygen quantum yields (ΦΔ) of these compounds were also determined in DMF using 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran (DPBF) as a singlet oxygen probe. The absorbance of DPBF at 415 nm was monitored spectroscopically along with the irradiation time. As shown in Figure S12a in the Supporting Information, both compounds could consume DPBF via the singlet oxygen generated, forming 1,2-dibenzoylbenzene via an unstable peroxide intermediate.(42) The values of ΦΔ were found to be 0.59 and 0.60 for 5 and 2, respectively, relative to ZnPc (ΦΔ = 0.56 in DMF).(19) The results showed that the cyclic peptide moiety did not significantly affect these properties of the phthalocyanine core. The relatively red-shifted Q band, lower fluorescence quantum yield, and higher singlet oxygen generation efficiency are the general features of 1,4-disubstituted zinc(II) phthalocyanines compared with the other substituted analogues.(43) These data of 5 and 2 are summarized in Table 3.

    The same properties of these two compounds were also measured in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) at pH 7.4 with Tween 80 (0.1% v/v) used to enhance the water solubility of the compounds and reduce their aggregation in aqueous media. As shown in Figure 4a,b, the Q band was slightly broadened and the fluorescence emission was also significantly weaker than those recorded in DMF for both compounds, which could be attributed to their higher aggregation tendency in this aqueous medium. Nevertheless, both compounds could sensitize the formation of singlet oxygen upon light irradiation with comparable efficiency as that of ZnPc (Figure S12b in the Supporting Information). In the presence of bovine serum albumin (BSA) (2 μM), both 5 and 2 became highly aggregated as shown by the very broad Q band, vanished fluorescence emission, and negligible singlet oxygen formation (Figure S13 in the Supporting Information). Even in the presence of the cyclic EBP peptide in 5, the aggregation characteristic was not significantly changed.

    Stability in Serum

    The stability of 5 in serum was examined by treating it with fetal bovine serum (FBS) (20% v/v) in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle medium (DMEM) and compared with that of the linear peptide EBP. Figure S14a in the Supporting Information shows the HPLC chromatograms of the mixtures at different time points over 2 h. It was found that the cyclic peptide conjugate 5 remained intact, while the linear counterpart EBP was degraded significantly during this period of time. Attempts were made to isolate and characterize the two major degradation products of linear EBP by electrospray ionization (ESI) mass spectrometry. Although intense signals due to the diprotonated molecular ions were observed (Figure S14b in the Supporting Information), we were not able to identify the species. As shown in Figure 5, which summarizes the results for both peptides, the half-life was less than 20 min for EBP. This study clearly demonstrated that the cyclization of the peptide could enhance its proteolytic stability.

    Cellular Uptake

    To examine the tumor-targeting effect of the cyclic EBP peptide moiety, the cellular uptake of 5 was studied using confocal microscopy and flow cytometry against a range of cancer cell lines with different expression levels of EGFR, including the EGFR-overexpressed HT29 human colorectal adenocarcinoma cells(18,19,44) and HCT116 human colorectal carcinoma cells,(44,45) as well as the low EGFR-expressed HeLa human cervical carcinoma cells(45) and HEK293 human embryonic kidney normal cells.(19) Again, the peptide-free phthalocyanine 2 was used as the negative control. As shown in Figure 6a, after incubation with 5 for 2 h, the two EGFR-positive HT29 and HCT116 cell lines showed significantly stronger intracellular fluorescence compared with the EGFR-negative HeLa and HEK293 cells. In contrast, the intracellular fluorescence intensities remained moderate for all the four cell lines with no significant difference after incubation with 2. Figure 6b shows the quantified fluorescence intensities determined by flow cytometry under these conditions. It can be seen that the fluorescence intensities of 5 in the two colorectal cell lines were substantially higher than those in the other two cell lines. The intensity in HT29 cells was about 25-fold and 8-fold higher than that in HeLa and HEK293 cells, respectively. No significant difference was observed for 2. The results clearly demonstrated the EGFR-targeting effect of the cyclic EBP peptide. It is worth noting that the EGFR-binding affinity of 5 was higher than that of the previously reported linear EBP–doxorubicin conjugate for which an approximately 5-fold difference in cellular uptake between the EGFR-positive and EGFR-negative cells was observed.(38) The stronger EGFR-binding affinity arising from the cyclic EBP peptide compared to the linear counterpart is in accordance with the relative specificity of other tumor-targeting peptides, such as the RGD sequence.(46)
    To further reveal the role of the cyclic EBP peptide, a competition assay using the nonphthalocyanine-conjugated 3 as a competitor was performed. HT29 cells were incubated with different concentrations of this cyclic peptide (100 and 400 μM) for 30 min at 37 °C, and then, 5 (2 μM) was added for coincubation for further 2 h. The intracellular fluorescence intensity was then determined by flow cytometry. As shown in Figure 6c, the intensity decreased significantly when the concentration of 3 increased, suggesting that the internalization of the conjugate was mediated by the cyclic peptide moiety.
    The cellular uptake of the other two cyclic peptide–phthalocyanine conjugates was also examined using flow cytometry. For 6, as it was reported that the disulfide-constrained RPM sequence can distinguish HT29 cells from HCT116 cells by immunohistochemical assays,(36) these two cell lines were used for this study. However, it was found that after incubation with 6 for 2 h, the intracellular fluorescence intensity for HT29 cells was only slightly higher than that for HCT116 cells (Figure S15 in the Supporting Information). The cell selectivity for this conjugate seems to be lower than that for the disulfide-constrained counterpart. The uptake behavior of 7 was similar to that of 5. As shown in Figure S16 in the Supporting Information, the intracellular fluorescence intensity was also higher for HT29 and HCT116 cells compared with HeLa and HEK293 cells, but the difference (ca. 2–6-fold) was smaller than that observed for 5 (Figure 6b). All these results suggested that 5 exhibited the highest selectivity toward the two colorectal cell lines through binding with the overexpressed EGFR. Therefore, this conjugate was selected for further in vitro and in vivo studies.

    Subcellular Localization

    The subcellular localization of 5 was further examined with confocal microscopy. After incubation with 5 (2 μM) for 2 h, the HT29 cells were stained with LysoTracker Green DND-26 (2 μM), MitoTracker Green FM (0.2 μM), or ER-Tracker Green (1 μM) for 30, 15, or 15 min, respectively. As shown in Figure 7, the fluorescence image and intensity profile for 5 could overlap well with those for LysoTracker but not for the other two trackers. These results suggested that 5 tended to reside in the lysosomes, which are the last compartments of the endocytic pathway of receptor-mediated endocytosis.

    In Vitro Photodynamic Activities

    The dark and phototoxicities of 5 against all the four cell lines were also investigated using an MTT assay [MTT = 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide]. As shown in Figure 8, 5 did not show significant dark cytotoxicity toward all these cell lines up to 60 nM. However, upon irradiation (λ > 610 nm, 23 mW cm–2, 28 J cm–2), a substantial cytotoxicity was observed for the EGFR-positive HT29 and HCT116 cells with an IC50 value of 6 and 8 nM, respectively. The photocytotoxicity was much higher than that of the analogues with a linear EGFR-targeting QRH* or GE11 sequence of which the IC50 values for EGFR-positive cell lines were found to be 0.23 μM (for HT29 cells) and 14 μM (for A431 cells), respectively.(19,20) It is believed that the cyclic form of EBP plays an important role in selective binding with the EGFR, and the high potency of 5 can be attributed to its high cellular uptake toward the HT29 and HCT116 cells. In contrast, for the EGFR-negative HeLa and HEK293 cells, the cell viability remained nearly unchanged even upon light irradiation, showing that 5 was essentially nontoxic to these cells within this concentration range. The remarkable difference in photocytotoxicity against the EGFR-positive and EGFR-negative cells clearly demonstrated the targeting role of the cyclic EBP moiety in the conjugate, making it a very potent and cell-selective photosensitizer.

    In Vivo Biodistribution

    To further explore the in vivo targeting property of 5, the biodistribution of this conjugate in HT29 tumor-bearing nude mice was monitored over a period of 24 h. The nonpeptide-conjugated 2 was used as the negative control. As shown in Figure 9a, shortly after the intravenous injection, both compounds were distributed throughout the whole body. After the metabolic clearance, the fluorescence of 5 was still observed at the tumor region at 24 h postinjection, but the fluorescence intensity of 2 at the tumor remained very weak over this period of time. The mice were then euthanized after 24 h, and the tumor and major organs, including the heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney, were harvested. The fluorescence intensities in the tumor and these organs (per unit area) were then measured and compared. Figure 9b,c shows that the accumulation of 5 in tumor, as reflected by the fluorescence intensity therein, was generally higher than that in other organs, except the liver, which is the primary site for drug metabolism, and was about 3-fold higher than that of 2 in the tumor. These results indicated that 5 also exhibited high affinity toward the HT29 tumor in vivo.

    In Vivo Photodynamic Therapy

    Finally, the in vivo PDT efficacy of 5 was also examined. Nude mice bearing an HT29 tumor were intravenously injected with 5 (40 nmol) in 200 μL of distilled water containing 2.5% DMSO and 0.1% Tween 80. The tumor was irradiated with a diode laser at 680 nm (0.3 W cm–2) for 10 min after the injection for 24 h. As a negative control without applying the drug and light irradiation, the mice were simply injected with PBS. After the treatment, the tumor size was monitored continuously for 14 days. As shown in Figure 10a,b, 5 could effectively suppress the tumor growth in mice while the tumor grew continuously for the PBS control group. The body weight of the mice was also monitored during the treatment period (Figure 10c). The negligible change indicated that the PDT treatment did not cause apparent side effect on the mice. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining was also performed on the tumor and the major organs after the treatment with 5 with light irradiation or PBS without light irradiation as the control. As shown in Figure 10d, cellular damage and necrosis were observed in the H&E-stained image of the tumor after the PDT treatment. For the images of the major organs, there was no apparent abnormality or noticeable damage. Even the liver where the photosensitizer was localized (Figure 9b,c), no apparent damage was observed, which is due to the fact that light irradiation was not applied at this site. These results suggested that 5 could also function as an efficient and apparently safe PDT agent in this animal model.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we have developed a facile one-pot methodology for in situ cyclization of peptides and conjugation with photosensitizers through a bifunctional linker. The bis(bromomethyl)benzene and cyclopentadiene moieties of the linker undergo selective nucleophilic substitution with the two cysteine residues of the linear peptides and Diels–Alder reaction with the maleimide unit attached to a zinc(II) phthalocyanine, respectively. Three cyclic peptide-conjugated phthalocyanines have been prepared in 20–26% isolated yield. The conjugate with a cyclic EBP peptide (compound 5) exhibits remarkably high and selective binding affinity to EGFR. The cellular uptake as reflected by the intracellular fluorescence intensity toward the EGFR-positive HT29 and HCT116 cells is up to 25-fold higher than that against the EGFR-negative HeLa and HEK293 cells. This conjugate also shows high photocytotoxicity toward HT29 and HCT116 cells with an IC50 value of 6 and 8 nM, respectively. In vivo studies have demonstrated that the conjugate exhibits high tumor-retention property and is able to retard the tumor growth in HT29 tumor-bearing nude mice upon light irradiation. All the results suggest that 5, which can be prepared readily via a one-pot reaction, is a promising photosensitizer for EGFR-targeted PDT.

    General

    All the reactions were performed under an atmosphere of nitrogen. DMF was purified using an INERT solvent purification system. All other solvents and reagents were of reagent grade and used as received. All the reactions were monitored by thin layer chromatography performed on Merck precoated silica gel 60 F254 plates. Chromatographic purification was performed with column chromatography on silica gel (Macherey-Nagel, 230–400 mesh) and/or size-exclusion chromatography on Bio-Beads S-X1 beads (200–400 mesh). Compounds 1 and 2 were prepared as described.(34)
    MALDI-TOF mass spectra were recorded on a Bruker Autoflex Speed MALDI-TOF mass spectrometer. ESI mass spectra were recorded on a Thermo Finnigan MAT 95 XL mass spectrometer. Electronic absorption and steady-state fluorescence spectra were taken on a Cary 5G UV–vis–NIR spectrophotometer and a HORIBA FluoroMax-4 spectrofluorometer, respectively. The purity of 5, 6, and 7 was examined by HPLC and found to be >95% (Figures S4a, S8a, and S10a, respectively, in the Supporting Information).
    Reversed-phase HPLC separation was performed on an XBridge BEH300 C18 column (5 μm, 4.6 mm × 150 mm) at a flow rate of 1 mL min–1 for analytical purpose or on an XBridge BEH300 Prep C18 column (5 μm, 10 mm × 250 mm) at a flow rate of 3 mL min–1 for preparative purpose using a Waters system equipped with a Waters 1525 binary pump and a Waters 2998 photodiode array detector. The solvents used for HPLC analysis were of HPLC grade. The condition used for the analysis was set as follows: solvent A = 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) in acetonitrile and solvent B = 0.1% TFA in deionized water; gradient: 5% A + 95% B in the first 5 min, then changed to 15% A + 85% B in 10 min, further changed to 100% A + 0% B in 30 min, maintained under this condition for 5 min, changed to 0% A + 100% B in 5 min, and maintained under this condition for a further 5 min.

    Preparation of EBP, RPM, and TCP-1

    These three peptides were synthesized manually using a modified Fmoc solid-phase peptide synthesis protocol with the commercially available N-α-Fmoc-protected amino acids. The rink amide resin was used as the solid support. A solution of 20% piperidine in DMF was used to remove the Fmoc-protecting group, and 1-[bis(dimethylamino)methylene]-1H-1,2,3-triazolo[4,5-b]pyridinium 3-oxide hexafluorophosphate (HATU) was used as the carboxyl group-activating agent.     In Vivo Imaging

    Female Balb/c nude mice (20–25 g) were obtained from the Laboratory Animal Services Centre at The Chinese University of Hong Kong. All animal experiments were approved by the Animal Experimentation Ethics Committee of the University. The mice were kept under pathogen-free conditions with free access to food and water. In vivo fluorescence imaging was performed before and after the injection at different time points up to 24 h with an Odyssey infrared imaging system (excitation wavelength = 680 nm, emission wavelength ≥700 nm). The images were digitized and analyzed using the Odyssey imaging system software (no. 9201-500). After the in vivo imaging studies, the animals were euthanized at 24 h postinjection. The tumors and various organs were then harvested and their fluorescence intensities were measured with the Odyssey infrared imaging system again. Four mice were used for each compound.

    In Vivo Photodynamic Therapy

    HT29 tumor-bearing nude mice were prepared as described above. The length and width of the tumor were measured using a micrometer digital caliper (SCITOP Systems). The tumor volume (mm3) was calculated using the formula: tumor volume = (length × width2)/2. Once the tumors had grown to a size of 80–100 mm3, 5 (40 nmol) dissolved in 200 μL of distilled water containing 2.5% DMSO and 0.1% Tween 80 was injected to the tumor-bearing mice through tail intravenous injection. Five mice were used for this group. At 24 h postinjection, the tumor was illuminated by a diode laser (Biolitec Ceralas) at 680 nm operated at 0.24 W. Illumination on a circular spot with a 1.0 cm diameter for 10 min led to a total fluence of 180 J cm–2. The tumor sizes of the nude mice were monitored periodically for the next 14 days. The tumor volumes were compared with a PBS control group of mice without light treatment. Three mice were used for the control. After 14 days of the PDT treatment, the mice were sacrificed, and the tumor and major organs were harvested. The tissues were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, dehydrated in an alcohol series, mixed with solutions of xylene and paraffin, and embedded in paraffin. The tissue sections were prepared by Leica microtome RM2235. After H&E staining, the tissues were examined with a Carl Zeiss PALM inverted microscope.
