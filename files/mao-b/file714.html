    Abstract

    Polyamine derivatives have a promising prospect in dealing with disseminated tumor cells, a major obstacle in cancer therapy. To develop a bifunctional polyamine derivative that can serve as a fluorescent probe and an antimetastatic agent, three kinds of polyamine conjugates with benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one as a scaffold were designed and synthesized. Compound 5e was selected as a lead by in vitro screening. Two animal models demonstrated that 5e inhibited pulmonary metastasis and tumor growth. As a fluorescent probe, 5e might partially enter cells via a polyamine transporter and subsequently localize in the lysosome. Mechanistic investigations demonstrated the interdependence of 5e-triggered apoptosis and autophagy. Compound 5e modulated the expression of LC3-II, p62, cathepsins, and the expression of capases 3, caspase 8, Bcl-2, and p53. The SSAT-mediated Akt/β-catenin pathways were also inhibited by 5e. The dual features of 5e make it a worthwhile lead compound for further structural optimization.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Lysosomes have long been considered as digestive centers for the degradation and recycling of macromolecules. However, recent developments reveal that lysosomes have biological functions that are more complex. Lysosomes are highly involved in diverse basic cellular processes, such as apoptosis, autophagy, and signal transduction.(1,2) Targeting the lysosome is therefore a promising strategy in cancer therapy.(3−5) The basic small molecules that can accumulate selectively in acidic lysosomes have diverse chemical structures and pharmacological activities. Many lysosomotropic compounds are used clinically as antiasthma (e.g., astemizole), antimyocardial ischemia (e.g., desipramine), or antimalarial (e.g., quinacrine) agents. Lysosome-targeted compounds may also prevent tumor progression and reduce tumor metastasis.(6−8) Indeed, chloroquine (Figure 1) and hydroxychloroquine combined with other antitumor agents have been tested in many clinical trials.(9)
    The role of polyamines in drug discovery is widely recognized.(10,11) Polyamine conjugates composed of polyamine skeletons with various pharmacophores may display intriguing biological properties.(12−15) For example, the epipodophyllotoxin–spermine conjugate (F14512) has been evaluated as an anticancer drug in phase I trials.(16−18) Tumor metastasis is one of the major causes of death in cancer patients. Natural products with a polyamine moiety have been found to prevent tumor cell invasion,(19) with improved anti-invasive activities realized by further structural modifications.(20,21) Our group has provided evidence that some naphthalimide–polyamine conjugates could potently inhibit tumor growth and metastasis in vivo.(22−24) These results demonstrate that the potential of polyamine-based drugs for the treatment of tumor metastasis.
    Moreover, various groups have discovered that polyamine conjugates may have a specific intracellular distribution, including localization in lysosomes,(3) mitochondria,(13) and the nucleus.(14,15) Recently, the development of theranostic agents with bioimaging and therapeutic functions integrated into a single molecule has attracted considerable attention.(25) The “flavone– naphthalimide–polyamine” conjugate as a mitochondrion-targeted agent with dual bioimaging and antimetastatic features(24) was partially designed by following the principle of theranostics.
    Benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-ones, which have structure similar to those of naphthalimides (Figure 1), were originally designed as dyes. Several decades ago, some benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one derivatives were found to inhibit thymidylate synthase.(26,27) Subsequently, several groups used various drug screening techniques to reveal that benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-ones have further remarkable biological properties.(28−33) Recently, 3-O-methylthespesilactam, a natural product containing a benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one scaffold, was found to be a promising pan-JAK inhibitor.(34) To date, there are fewer reports about the antitumor and photophysical properties of benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-ones than those of naphthalimides.
    To gain insight into the influence of the pharmacophoric element on the dual bioimaging and antimetastatic properties, three subsets of polyamine conjugates with benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one as a pharmacophore were designed and synthesized. In vitro and in vivo assays showed that the lysosome-targeted compound 5e potently inhibited pulmonary metastasis and tumor growth by down-regulating the cellular polyamine pool as well as inducing apoptosis and autophagy through lysosome-mediated pathways.

    2.1. Chemistry

    Target compounds (5a–5e) with a polyamine tail on the lactam ring were synthesized according to a route similar to that reported previously.(35,36) As shown in Scheme 1, 3-bromopropyl (or 4-bromobutyl) group was routinely introduced into benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one by using 1,3-dibromopropane (or 1,4-dibromobutane) in the presence of anhydrous potassium carbonate. Generated intermediates 2a and 2b reacted with diverse Boc-protected polyamines (Figure 2, they are either commercial (3a, 3b), or known compounds (3c,(37,38) 3d,(38) 3e,(39,40) 3f(37) and 3g(40)) prepared by a modified procedure(23,37,39)) to give compounds 4a–4e, which were deprotected with 4 M HCl to provide target compounds 5a–5e as hydrochloride salts.
    The synthetic route for target compounds 11a–11e is shown in Scheme 2. Known intermediate 7 was prepared by a conventional method using concentrated nitric acid in glacial acetic acid.(41,42) Compound 7 was hydrogenated in the presence of 10% Pd/C to give compound 8, which was subsequently reacted with chloroacetyl chloride to give compound 9.(43) Then, the nucleophilic substitution reaction of compound 9 with various Boc-protected polyamines (Figure 2) furnished compounds 10a–10e. After purification by flash column chromatography, the Boc-group of these intermediates was removed with 4 M HCl to give target compounds 11a–11e as hydrochloride salts.
    Compound 12 with an introduced formyl group was prepared by a conventional Vilsmeier reaction as shown in Scheme 3.(24) Reductive amination of 12 with Boc-protected polyamines was achieved via a two-step process, resulting in in situ generation of the imines (13a and 13b). Without purification, the imines were reduced with NaBH4 to their respective amines (14a and 14b) in good yield.(44,45) Finally, purified 14a and 14b were deprotected with 4 M HCl to give target compounds 15a and 15b as hydrochloride salts. The structures of all the target compounds were confirmed by 1H NMR, 13C NMR, MS, and elemental analysis.

Amonafide (Figure 1), a naphthalimide agent that reached phase III clinical trials for cancer therapy,(46) was chosen as the reference drug.     Compounds 5c–5e with triamine fragments generally displayed more potent antitumor activities than compounds 5a and 5b with diamine moieties. Further, compound 5e with a 4,4-triamine (homospermidine) motif was active toward all four of the tested cancer cells. This result seems to be in accordance with the behavior of the previously reported N1-(9-anthracenylmethyl)homospermidine conjugate.(45) In the second subset, compounds 11a–11e have an acetylamino group, which is also present in F14512 as a linker for the polyamine vector.(47) These compounds were less potent than compound 5e. However, 11c with a homospermidine moiety and 11e with a 4,4,4-tetraamine (homospermine) moiety performed better than 11b with a 3,3-triamine (norspermidine) and 11d with a 3,3,3-tetraamine (norspermine) moiety, respectively. Third, compounds 15a and 15b, which have a different methylene linker than 11a and 11b, exhibited increased potency in the tested cancer cells, indicating that the linker in these polyamine conjugates plays an important role in their antitumor activities. Because compound 5e was as potent as amonafide toward the tested cancer cells, it was selected for further study.
    Colorectal cancer (CRC) ranks as the third most common cancer worldwide, and metastasis is observed in about 50% of CRC patients.(48,49) Indeed, 5e and amonafide inhibited the growth of both HCT116 and CT-26 colon cancer cells in a dose-dependent manner after treatment for 24 h (Figure 3A). The IC50 values of 5e (amonafide) were 6.90 μM (8.55 μM) for HCT116 cells and 11.58 μM (11.69 μM) for CT-26 cells.
    Tumor metastasis is a major obstacle in present chemotherapy,(50,51) and cell migration is a critical step during tumor diffusion from the primary tumor mass to remote tissues. Considering the potency of polyamine conjugates against tumor metastasis in vivo,(20,23,24) transwell migration assays were conducted to determine whether 5e could inhibit colon cancer migration at the cellular level. As illustrated in Figure 3B, the presence of 5e markedly reduced the migration of HCT116 and CT-26 cells in a dose-dependent manner, and 5e was more potent than amonafide.

    2.3. Tumor Growth and Metastasis Inhibition in Vivo

    To evaluate the antitumor effect of 5ein vivo, we established two CT-26 tumor transplant animal models, namely, a pulmonary metastasis model (for tumor metastasis evaluation) and a primary tumor growth model (for tumor growth inhibition evaluation). CT-26 cells (2 × 105 cells per mouse) were injected into mice via the tail vein. To ensure that all mice bore actively developing pulmonary metastasis tumors before drug treatment, the pulmonary tumors were allowed to grow for 7 days. Then, the mice (8 per group) were injected intravenously with 5e (1.0 mg/kg), amonafide (5.0 mg/kg, positive control), or normal saline (negative control) for 7 consecutive days. On day 15, the mice treated with 5e displayed significantly reduced pulmonary metastasis nodules compared with the control (Figure 4A). Furthermore, the inhibitory effect of 5e (82.5%) was superior to that of amonafide (66.0%) (Figure 4B).
    To assess the impact of 5e on the solid tumor growth, 1 × 106 CT-26 cells were injected underarm of the mice. After 1 week, tumors were palpable and, the mice were randomly divided into three groups (8 per group). The mice were injected via the tail vein once per day with 5e (1.0 mg/kg), amonafide (5.0 mg/kg, positive control), or physiologic saline (negative control) for 14 consecutive days. The tumor growth inhibition rates of each group were calculated relative to the control group. As shown in Figure 5A,B, 5e moderately suppressed the tumor growth (46.9%), compared with the slight inhibition rate (20.0%) of amonafide.
    The body weight of the mice was monitored to evaluate the dose-associated toxicity. The respective weights of the three groups in the two animal models varied in a similar manner during the experiment (Figures 4C and 5C). The slight increase weight indicated that there were no dose-associated toxicities. Meanwhile, we assessed the preliminary systemic toxicities of 5e and amonafide in the two animal models. After the mice were executed by cervical vertebra dislocation, the organs including the heart, liver, lung, kidney, and spleen were inspected. As shown in Figures 4D and 5D, the variations in organ weight indexes implied the effect of 5e on the organ weights in the two models was different from that of amonafide. Compared with the untreated group, 5e treatment increased the weights of the organs such as heart, liver, and spleen in the animal models, suggesting that there were some side-effects of 5e.

    2.4. Cellular Uptake and Localization

    Compound 5e with a benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one fluorophore was expected to have similar optical functions to that of benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one. We examined the absorption and fluorescence emission spectra of compound 5e and benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one (compound 1). The presence of basic side chain in 5e caused the red shift for maximum absorption and emission wavelengths, and decreased the maximum absorption and emission intensities because of PET quenching. The solvent effects on optical properties were observed (Figures S1 and S2). However, compound 5e still displayed bright green fluorescence in water with maximum absorption and emission wavelengths at 340 and 510 nm, respectively, which facilitated further mechanistic investigation of its cellular uptake and distribution. A high content screening (HCS) system and confocal microscopy were used to detect the fluorescence of 5e-treated CRC cells.
    Polyamine conjugates are usually transferred to tumor cells via active PAT systems.(41) To determine whether 5e was taken up by tumor cells via PAT, spermidine (a natural PAT ligand, Spd) competition assays were conducted. As shown in Figure 6A, the presence of Spd (20 μM) slightly reduced the cellular fluorescence intensity of 5e. Meanwhile, Spd also protected the cells from growth inhibition by 5e (Figure 6B). These mutually corroborated results indicated that the increased HCT116 cell viability might result from the attenuated concentration of 5e.
    It is well-known that fast growing tumor cells generally have an elevated polyamine pool. However, it is still unclear how polyamines are involved in regulating cell migration. To determine whether 5e affected the cellular polyamine pool, the expression of one critical enzymes in polyamine metabolism, spermidine/spermine N1-acetyltransferase (SSAT),(10) was assayed by Western blot analysis (Figure 6C). Compound 5e up-regulated the expression of SSAT in HCT116 cells in a dose-dependent manner. Subsequently, the cellular polyamine (spermine, spermidine, and putrescine) levels of HCT116 cells treated with 5e were measured by HPLC.(52) As shown in Figure 6D, 5e moderately down-regulated the concentrations of spermine and spermidine but had little impact on the putrescine content. These results are in accordance with our previous reports in which decreasing polyamine levels by overexpressed SSAT prevented cell migration in hepatocellular carcinoma and CRC cells.(53)
    A polyamine-modified fluorescent probe was reported to stain live tumor cells selectively.(54) The previous finding of a fluorescent polyamine conjugate as a mitochondria-targeted agent(24) inspired us to examine the subcellular location of 5e by confocal microscopy. Compound 5e was quickly taken up by cells in dose- and time-dependent manners, as shown in Figure 7A,B. To visualize the subcellular localization of 5e, various organelle-specific fluorescent dyes, such as Mito-Tracker for mitochondria, Golgi-tracker for the Golgi, ER-Tracker for endoplasmic reticulum (ER), and Lyso-Tracker for lysosomes, were used. Inspection of merged images suggested that 5e might selectively localize within the lysosomes (Figure 7C). To confirm the presence of 5e in lysosomes, the lysosomes of 5e-treated HCT116 cells were isolated.(55) Indeed, the green fluorescence was observed from the isolated lysosomes in the 5e-treated group but not in the control group (Figure 7D). Therefore, 5e might serve as a novel fluorescent probe for lysosomes.

    2.5. Induction of Apoptosis and Autophagy

    Apoptosis, which is controlled by multiple signaling pathways, is one of the major cell death profiles. Many polyamine conjugates, such as F14512, may kill cells via apoptosis.(56) Thus, 5e-induced apoptosis was assessed by two conventional methods. First, HCT116 and CT-26 cells treated with 5e were doubly stained by acridine orange (AO), which emits green fluorescence, and ethidium bromide (EB), which emits red fluorescence. Living cells stained with AO display bright green fluorescence, whereas apoptotic cells stained with EB show bright red fluorescence. The decreased fluorescence intensity of AO and enhanced fluorescence intensity of EB in both CRC cell lines suggested that 5e induced apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner (Figures 8A,B and 9A). Further, flow cytometry experiments using AnnexinV/PI double staining were conducted. The obtained results confirmed the occurrence of apoptosis (Figure 8B,C). For example, treatment with 5e (10 μM, 24 h) resulted in HCT116 cell apoptosis with early apoptotic cells (31.3%) and late apoptotic cells (27.6%).
    Autophagy is a complex catabolic process in which cellular components are transported to lysosomes for degradation.(1) The intracellular localization of 5e in lysosomes suggests that lysosomes should serve critical roles in 5e induced cell death. Therefore, 3-methyladenine (3-MA), an autophagy inhibitor, was used to determine whether 5e induced autophagy.(57) As shown in Figure 8B,D, the addition of 3-MA to CRC cells protected them from growth inhibition by 5e and reduced the proportion of 5e-triggered apoptotic cells (from 58.9% to 36.2% for HCT116 cells and from 65.6% to 33.9% for CT-26 cells). These preliminarily results indicate that the autophagy generated by 5e is favorable for apoptosis.
    Monodansylcadaverine (MDC) is a fluorescent agent that can accumulate in acidic vesicles. Thus, MDC is commonly used for the detection of autophagic vacuoles. LysoTracker Red (LYSO) is a fluorescent probe that stains highly acidic lysosomal vacuoles and determines the activity of vacuolar H+-ATPase (v-ATPase).(58) The status of lysosomes in autophagy can also be detected using LYSO. With the above evidence for autophagy, further assays were conducted by flow cytometry with MDC and LYSO as staining agents. As shown in Figure 9A, the fluorescence of MDC and LYSO in 5e-treated cells showed similar variations, and increased fluorescent intensities were obtained. In addition, the presence of 3-MA reduced the fluorescent intensities of both MDC and LYSO (Figure 9B). These results demonstrate that the changes in lysosome function caused by 5e are beneficial for autophagy.
    To further illustrate the relationship between apoptosis and autophagy, Z-VAD-FMK, a pan-caspase inhibitor, was applied. As shown in Figure 8D, reduced apoptosis was observed in HCT116 cells treated with 5e and Z-VAD-FMK. Interestingly, the presence of Z-VAD-FMK attenuated the fluorescence of MDC and LYSO in 5e-treated cells (Figure 9C), indicating that reduced apoptosis is unfavorable for the occurrence of autophagy. It is thus speculated that 5e induced apoptosis and autophagy depend on each other.

    2.6. Molecular Mechanism

    The in vitro and in vivo experiments described above demonstrated that 5e prevents cell migration and kill cells via apoptosis and autophagy. We thus investigated the molecular mechanism that is responsible for the observed biological results. Because of the intracellular location in lysosomes, the effects of 5e on lysosome-associated proteins were first assayed by Western blot analysis. Lysosomal associated membrane proteins (LAMPs), which serve as lysosomal markers, play important roles in lysosome-mediated physiological processes, such as participating in autophagy.(59) Overexpressed cathepsins exist in many tumor cells. Intracellular and secreted cathepsins promote metastasis by activating associated proteases and degrading extracellular matrix proteins.(60) Treatment with 5e (10 μM) up-regulated the expression of LAMP1 and LAMP2 (Figure 10A).(61) Meanwhile, the expression of cathepsins B, D, and L was down-regulated by 5e in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 10B).
    p62 is a multifunctional protease that acts as a molecular regulator in autophagy. The overexpression of p62 may result in tumor cell invasion.(62) Microtubule-associated protein light chain 3 (LC3-II) is also a key protease in autophagy. As both p62 and LC3-II are known biomarkers of autophagy, the expression of these proteins was measured by Western blot analysis (Figure 10C). Treatment with 5e down-regulated the expression of p62 in a dose-dependent manner. Concentration-dependent accumulation of LC3-II was observed after treatment with 5e, indicating the occurrence of autophagy.
    mTOR is an important cell growth regulator that regulates autophagy and macropinocytosis. The lysosome serves as the hub of mTOR-mediated signaling pathway,(3) and mTOR inhibitors have been tested extensively as antitumor agents. Thus, the impact of 5e on mTOR was determined. As shown in Figure 10D, 5e dose-dependently inhibited the expression of both phophorylated mTOR and mTOR.
    We previously found that overexpressed SSAT can prevent cell migration and invasion in CRC cells via Akt/β-catenin signaling pathways.(53) Interestingly, lysosome-targeted 5e also up-regulated the expression of SSAT and inhibited CRC cell migration in vitro and in vivo. Therefore, β-catenin and MMP2 were determined to establish the signaling pathway for 5e-induced metastasis inhibition. As shown in Figure 10E, 5e attenuated the expression levels of β-catenin and MMP2 in a dose-dependent manner, which is beneficial for inhibiting metastasis. Meanwhile, at the same concentration, the inhibitive effects of 5e (10 μM) on β-catenin and MMP2 were much more potent than that of amonafide (10 μM), providing some evidence for the better antimetastatic properties of 5e than amonafide in vitro and in vivo.
    As mentioned above, the pan-caspase inhibitor Z-VAD-FMK reduced the proportion of apoptosis induced by 5e, implying that compound 5e induced apoptosis partially depending on caspases. Caspase 3 and caspase 8 have been implicated as key mediators in apoptosis.(63,64) Therefore, the expressions of caspase 3, cleaved caspase 3, caspase 8, and cleaved caspase 8 were assayed by Western blot analysis (Figure 10E). As expected, 5e treatment up-regulated the protein expression levels of cleaved caspase 3 and cleaved caspase 8, whereas respective down-regulation of caspase-3 and caspase 8 was observed. These preliminary results from Western blot analysis showed that caspases played important role in 5e-induced apoptosis.
    Moreover, protein expression of Akt and Bcl2 was down-regulated, but enhanced expression of p53 was obtained in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 10D,E). Finally, the autophagy inhibitor 3-MA was also applied to illustrate the relationship between apoptosis and autophagy. Because 3-MA decreased the proportion of apoptosis in HCT116 cells, the expression of apoptosis-associated proteins should be reversed in the presence of 3-MA. Indeed, the expected results were observed as shown in Figure 10F.

    3. Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In an attempt to develop novel agents with theranostic properties, three subsets of polyamine derivatives were designed and synthesized. The preliminary SAR studies not only confirmed the role of both the linker and the homospermidine moiety in cytotoxicity in vitro but also led to the discovery of compound 5e, which was as potent as amonafide toward the tested cancer cells. Further in vivo assays on two CRC models demonstrated that 5e potently inhibited pulmonary metastasis, but only had a moderate effect on primary tumor growth. However, investigations of the toxicological profile revealed that additional structural modifications, especially to the benzo[cd]indol-2(1H)-one scaffold are necessary.
    Fluorescent assays suggested that 5e might partially enter cells via PAT and serve as a fluorescent probe for lysosomes. Investigations of cell death profiles provided clear evidence for the interdependence of 5e-initiated apoptosis and autophagy. The mechanistic study revealed that lysosome-targeted 5e affected the lysosome function by modulating a series of lysosome-associated proteins associated with the onset of autophagy. Apoptosis induced by 5e was related to the regulation of apoptotic proteins including the degradation of caspase 3, caspase 8, and Bcl-2 and the accumulation of p53. Importantly, 5e reduced the cellular polyamine pool by increasing the expression of SSAT, and the subsequent Akt/β-catenin signaling pathways were involved in metastasis inhibition.
    Taken together, these results indicate that 5e is a lysosome-targeted agent with dual bioimaging and antimetastatic features. The present work also highlights the value of 5e as a small molecular probe for investigating crosstalk between apoptosis and autophagy. With potential for CRC metastasis treatment, further efforts to optimize this lead compound and minimize associated side effects are underway in our laboratory.

    4.1. Chemistry

    4.2. Biological Materials and Methods

    4.2.1. Cell Lines and Cell Culture

    The cancer cell lines were cultured in RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS, streptomycin (100 units/mL), and penicillin (100 units/mL) at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2. These cell lines were purchased from American Type Culture Collection (MD, USA). RPMI-1640 medium was obtained from Solarbio (Beijing Solarbio Science, China), and FBS was bought from PAN (PAN SERATECH, Germany).

    4.2.2. Cell Viability Assay

    MTT assay was used to detect the proliferation rate of cancer cells. Briefly, cells were plated in 96-well plates. After 24 h, various concentrations (1, 5, 10, 20, 30, or 50 μM) of compounds were subsequently added and incubated for 24 or 48 h. Then, 50 μL of MTT reagent (1 mg/mL) was added and incubated for 4 h at 37 °C in a humidified incubator containing 5% CO2. After supernatants were removed, 100 μL of dimethyl sulfoxide was added to solubilize the crystal products at room temperature for 10 min. The absorbance was measured by a microtiter plate reader at wavelength of 570 nm. Each experiment was repeated at least three times.

    4.2.3. Lung Metastasis and Tumor Growth Assay

    All animal experiments performed in this article were conducted in compliance with the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals published by Henan University. Healthy male Balb/c mice (Laboratory Animal Center of Henan, Zhengzhou) aged 5 weeks (weighing 18–22 g) were chosen. CT-26 cells (2 × 105 cells per mouse) were injected via the tail vein for lung metastasis. To ensure the growth of pulmonary metastasis before the drug treatment, the tumor was inoculated for 7 days. On day 8, the randomly grouped mice (n = 8 mice per group) were injected via tail vein with 5e (1.0 mg/kg), amonafide (5 mg/kg, positive control), or normal saline (negative control) for 14 days. On day 15, mice were anesthetized and euthanized, and lungs were removed. After fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 1 day, the lung metastasis nodules of each mouse were counted. The inhibition rate was calculated. For solid tumor study, male Balb/c mice were subcutaneously injected of mice’s Alar with 1 × 106 CT-26 cells. After 1 week, tumors were palpable, and the mice were randomly divided into three groups (negative control, 5e, and amonafide group). Then, mice were injected via tail vein with 5e (1.0 mg/kg), amonafide (5 mg/kg, as positive control), or physiologic saline (negative control) for 14 consecutive days. Mice were weighed every day. Mice were finally killed by anesthesia, and tumor tissues were removed and weighed. The method of calculating the inhibition rate is as follows: inhibitory rate (%) = [(A – B)/A] × 100, where A represents the average tumor weight of control group and B represents the average tumor weight of the drug treated or positive group. Meanwhile, the organs (heart, liver, kidney, lung, and spleen) of the mice were removed and weighed at the last day. The method of calculating the organ weight index is as follows: organ index (%) = (organ weight/body weight) × 100.

    4.2.4. Cell Migration Assay

    Migration assays were performed using Boyden chamber with 8 μm pore size polycarbonate membrane (Corning, NY, USA). Chambers were washed with PBS for three times. Then taken into a Transwell chamber in serum-free medium in the 37 °C incubator for activation. The upper compartment of the chamber was added with 200 μL of serum-free medium (containing 1 × 105 cells), while 600 μL of RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10% FBS was filled to the lower compartment. Cells were treated with 5e at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2. After incubation for 24 h, the cancer cells remaining on the top surface of the membrane were gently removed away with cotton swabs. The migrated cells on the lower surface of the membrane was fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 20 min and stained with 0.2% crystal violet. All photographs were taken by using a Leika inverted microscope.

    4.2.5. Cellular Uptake of 5e Assay

    The intracellular fluorescence intensity of 5e was detected by using high content screening (HCS) analysis (Thermo Scientific Cellomics ArrayScan VTI, Cellomics, Pittsburgh, PA, USA). Cells were seeded into 96-well plates at 5000 cells/well for 24 h, and 5e (2.5, and 7.5 μM) and Spd (20 μM) were added into 96-well plates. After incubation for 24 h, the fluorescence intensity was detected by HCS.

    4.2.6. Western Blot Assay

    In order to detect the expression of related proteins, Western blot analysis was performed. Briefly, cells were treated with 5e at the concentration of 2.5, 5, 7.5, and 10 μM for 24 h and were harvested and centrifuged. Cell pellets were washed three times with ice-cold phosphate buffered saline (PBS) and were lysed with RIPA buffer (Beyotime, China). The total concentration of protein was determined using a BCA assay kit (Beyotime, China). The total lysates were denatured in 5 × SDS-loading buffer at 100 °C for 10 min. Equal amounts of total proteins were separated using 12% SDS-PAGE for 2 h and then transferred onto PVDF membranes. Membranes were blocked by 5% dried skimmed milk in TBST at room temperature for 1 h. After blocking, membranes were incubated with corresponding primary antibodies overnight at 4 °C. After being washed three times with TBST, the membranes were incubated with appropriate HRP-conjugated secondary antibody and then washed three times with TBST. The protein expression was detected by using the ECL plus reagents (Beyotime, Jiangsu, China).

    4.2.7. Polyamines Contents Assay

    The polyamine (putrescine, spermidine, and spermine) content of HCT116 cells treated with or without 5e was quantified by using HPLC (Agilent 1260, Agilent Technologies, USA) coupled with a G1321A fluorescence detector. With the dansyl chloride as the derivation reagent and 1,6-diaminohexane as the internal standard substance, the polyamines were separated on the Thermo HYPERSIL BDS C18 chromatographic column (25 × 4.6 mm, 5 μm) using methanol–water (65:35–100:0, 30 min gradient elution) as the mobile phase, and the determined wavelengths wer Ex = 340 nm and Em = 515 nm. Polyamine levels are expressed as ng/mg protein.

    4.2.8. Cellular Localization

    The cellular localization of 5e was detected by Confocal microscopy (Leica, Wetzlar, Germany). In brief, after cells were seeded into laser confocal cell culture dishes at 5000 cells/dishes for 24 h, 5e (5 μM) was added into dishes. After incubation for 2 h, cells were washed three times with PBS, the cells were stained with Lyso-tracker for lysosome (Beyotime, Jiangsu, China), ER-tracker for endoplasmic reticulum (Beyotime, Jiangsu, China), Golgi-tracker for Golgi (Beyotime, Jiangsu, China), or Mito-tracker for mitochondria (Yeasen, Shanghai, China) for 30 min, Then, cells were washed three times with PBS, and the images were captured using a Leika Laser Confocal microscopy.

    4.2.9. Isolation of Lysosome

    Isolation of lysosome was performed by using Lysosomal extraction kit (Beibokit, Shanghai, China). Approximately 2 × 107 HCT116 cells exposed to 5e (10 μM) for 2 h were harvested by trypsinization and washed twice with cold PBS. The lysosomes can be obtained according to the manufacturer’s protocol of Lysosomal extraction kit. An appropriate amount of lysosomes were taken and placed on Laser Confocal cell culture dishes. The images were captured using a Leika Laser Confocal microscopy.

    4.2.10. MDC and LYSO Staining

    Cell autophagy was evaluated using respective MDC and LYSO staining detected by flow cytometry. Colon cancer cells (HCT116 and CT-26) were seeded into six-well plates at 1 × 105 cells/well. After cultured for 24 h, cells were treated with 5e, 3-MA (2 mM), or Z-VAD-FMK (10 μM) for 24 h. Then cells were harvested, washed three times with PBS to remove unbound dyes, and stained according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Analysis was performed by flow cytometry (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA).

    4.2.11. AO/EB Staining

    Colon cancer cells (HCT116 and CT-26) were seeded in 96-well plates (5000 cells/well), cultured for 24 h to acquire a confluent monolayer, and then treated with 5e for 24 h. Cells were seeded with AO (1 mg/mL) and EB (1 mg/mL) in the dark for 30 min, and then washed three times with PBS to remove unbound dyes. Fluorescence intensity of AO and EB was detected with HCS. The images of each concentration were captured.

    4.2.12. Annexin V-FITC/Propidium Iodide Staining

    Apoptosis of colon cancer cells (HCT116 and CT-26) was evaluated using Annexin V-FITC/PI staining detected by flow cytometry. Cells were plated into six-well plates at 1 × 105 cells/well. After cultured for 24 h, cells were hatched with 5e, 3-MA (2 mM), or Z-VAD-FMK (10 μM) for 24 h. Then cells were harvested, washed three times with PBS to remove unbound dyes, and stained according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Analysis was performed by flow cytometry (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA).

    4.2.13. Data Analysis

    All the data are presented as the mean ± SD and analyzed using Student’s t test or analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by q-test. *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, and ***p < 0.001 were considered to be statistically significant.
