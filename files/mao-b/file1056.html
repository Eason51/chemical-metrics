    Abstract

    Ligand- (GRIND) and structure-based (GLUE/GRIND) 3D-QSAR approaches were compared for 55 (aryl-)bridged 2-aminobenzonitriles inhibiting HIV-1 reverse transcriptase (HIV-1 RT). The ligand-based model was built from conformers selected by in vacuo minimization. The available X-ray structure of 3v in complex with HIV-1 RT allowed comparative structure-based calculations using the new docking software GLUE for conformer selection. Both models were validated via statistics and via virtual receptor sites (VRS) considering pharmacophoric regions and mutual distances, which were also compared with experimental evidence. The statistics show slight superiority of the structure-based approach in terms of fitting and prediction. By encoding relevant molecular interaction fields (MIF) into pharmacophoric regions, 10 such regions were derived from both models; they all fit the real receptor except HBD2. Also mutual distances highly agree between the real site and both VRS. Although distances from the structure-based approach are closer to the real receptor, present data prove the validity of the ligand-based GRIND approach.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Recent data from UNAIDS indicate that over 42 million people suffer from HIV/AIDS at the end of 2003,1 documenting the prevalence of this epidemic in the world. In the treatment of HIV infection, four classes of compounds2 are used at present:  protease inhibitors (PIs), nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs), nonnucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NNRTIs), and one fusion/entry inhibitor.3 NNRTIs consist of various classes of compounds;4 they are less toxic and chemically more stable and have slower metabolism and elimination rates than NRTIs.
    Whereas NRTIs act at the catalytic site of HIV reverse transcriptase (HIV-1 RT) by terminating DNA synthesis,5 NNRTIs bind noncompetitively to a hydrophobic pocket located about 10 Å away from the catalytic site6 and force the RT subunit into an inactive conformation.7 This specificity renders NNRTIs vulnerable to mutations among amino acids around the binding site,8 which leads to drug resistance.9 Accordingly, NNRTIs were initially considered to be of little therapeutic value, when used in monotherapy.10
    Screening in-house databases, Chan et al.11 identified 6-arylthio-2-aminobenzonitriles with micromolar antiviral activity against HIV-1. Modification to 6-arylsulfinyl- and 6-arylsulfonyl-2-aminobenzonitriles yielded classes of NNRTIs with nanomolar IC50 values in the latter. Furthermore, these authors resolved the X-ray crystal structure of their database compound 3v (739W94) in complex with HIV-1 RT (Brookhaven Protein Data Bank entry 1JLQ).
    In the present study, we performed comparative QSAR studies on the dataset of Chan et al.;11 a training and test set were built as detailed in the Experimental Section. We first applied a ligand-based strategy using the GRIND approach.12 Second, the availability of the crystal structure 1JLQ allowed us to apply a structure-based technique by combining the GRIND approach with the new docking method GLUE.13 Additional docking experiments were performed to assess the goodness of the method on this type of target:  several HIV1-RT targets were downloaded14 and each ligand was docked by means of GLUE into the corresponding binding site, starting from its SMILES notation.
    This way we derived two comparative PLS models, either neglecting or including X-ray information. GRIND descriptors are commonly used to build a virtual receptor site (VRS) in the absence of real binding site knowledge. In the present case study we used low-flexible compounds to compare the VRS obtained from both the ligand-based (GRIND) and the structure-based approach (GLUE/GRIND) and verify the validity of the VRS versus the real 3D structure of the binding site known from literature.11

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Design of the Study. Chan et al.11 measured the antiviral activity against HIV-1 for a large series of (aryl-)bridged 2-aminobenzonitriles and reported qualitative SAR data on this activity, which are summarized in Figure 1. Additionally, all compounds were evaluated for inhibition of HIV-1 RT; for 55 compounds out of this series, half-maximal inhibitory potencies were observed. SAR data closely resemble those for both activities.
    For our QSAR studies we selected the 55 compounds exhibiting half-maximal HIV-1 RT inhibition. The corresponding pIC50 values are listed in Table 1; they range from 8.54 for the most potent compound (3w) to a value of 4.72 for the weakly active compound 2b and cover a spectrum of approximately 4 log units. To allow external validation of the QSAR models, the entire database was split into a training set of 37 and a test set of 18 compounds. To guarantee a balanced composition of training and test set in terms of chemical diversity, their selection was carried out via principal component analysis (PCA) as described in the Experimental Section. Conformations of the compounds were selected either according to the Powell method (ligand-based approach) or via the new docking software GLUE13 (structure-based approach) as described below. In both cases, the 3D coordinates were obtained from the SMILES by using CONCORD.15 Afterward, the two different conformer datasets were subjected to multivariate analysis.

    Compounds were described by means of the alignment-independent GRIND12 descriptors, which are derived from molecular interaction fields (MIF). MIF are obtained with the GRID16 probes DRY (hydrophobic), N1 (hydrogen-bond (HB) donor interactions), O (HB acceptor interactions), and the novel TIP (shape description).17 MIF describe regions where the probes produce either energetically favorable or unfavorable interactions with the ligands. A filtering procedure, based on the energy of the nodes and the distance between them, extracts a set of grid nodes (filtered fields) characterized by relevant favorable interaction; these are encoded (via MACC2 transform) into a small number of GRIND variables describing the existence at a certain distance of grid node-pairs representing favorable interactions.
    The X-space of GRIND descriptors, which comprises 10 blocks (four auto- and six cross-correlograms) when applying four GRID probes, was used for multivariate statistics via partial least squares (PLS) analysis. PLS analysis served as a regression tool to correlate GRIND descriptors obtained from the training set conformers with the inhibitory activity (Y-space) against HIV-1 RT.
    For the two PLS models based either on the standard GRIND or on the GLUE/GRIND approach, the PLS coefficients were used to evaluate the variables with the highest positive or negative impact on activity. In both cases, the positively most relevant variables were combined into a virtual receptor site (VRS), which in turn allowed mutual comparison as well as a validity check versus the real receptor site.
    QSAR Study Using the GRIND Method. In an initial 3D-QSAR study, the ligand-based GRIND approach was applied, which by definition neglects any X-ray crystal structure information. This corresponds to the great majority of published 3D-QSAR studies. First of all, dataset compounds were converted from SMILES to 3D coordinates with CONCORD15 and then minimized in vacuo with the program Sybyl,18 by applying the Powell method using 1000 iterations. Then, GRIND descriptors were derived for all training set compounds; in turn, fractional factorial design (FFD) was used to select the variables for the X-space, which finally amounted to 245 GRIND descriptors, as well as to define the dimensionality of the PLS model, being optimal with two latent variables. These explained 81% of the variance for HIV-1 RT inhibition with a standard deviation of the error of calculation (SDEC) = 0.45. For internal validation five random groups were used and a q2 = 0.52 was obtained. For external validation, the 18 test set compounds were projected and a standard deviation of the error of prediction (SDEP) = 0.84 was obtained. The statistical details are reported in Table 2, whereas the prediction of the test set is graphically represented in Figure 2. All the compounds were satisfactorily predicted; however, three of them (1x, 3d, 3s) were slightly overestimated, and further, three (1f, 1u, 3t) were slightly underestimated.

    QSAR Study Using the GLUE/GRIND Method. With the aim to test the validity of GRIND models derived in the absence of X-ray information, a structure-based 3D-QSAR study was carried out by selecting conformers via the new docking software GLUE.12 The crystal structure 1JLQ of 3v in complex with HIV-1 RT, refined to an overall R-factor of 0.211, was published by Chan et al.11 An extract of this structure, focusing on the fit of 3v into the binding pocket and highlighting the amino acids involved in binding, is displayed in Figure 5. The crystal structure gave access to hypothesize the bioactive conformation of all dataset compounds.
    The following assumption underlies this GLUE/GRIND approach:  binding to the receptor site should be similar for all dataset compounds due to their chemical homogeneity. Thus, the binding site presumably does not change its conformation upon binding the different compounds. Accordingly, their bioactive conformations are assumed to closely resemble that of compound 3v. To establish the goodness of the GLUE method on this type of target, docking experiments were carried out on eight different HIV1 reverse transcriptases, downloaded from the Protein Data Bank archive.14 Excellent results were provided, as seven out of eight best docked solutions were first-ranked by GLUE and only one was second-ranked (see Table 3). Indeed, the three solutions proposed by GLUE as energetically favored were first selected for each of the 55 compounds. In the case of training set compounds, the conformer used in GLUE/GRIND modeling was the solution, among the three selected, with the lowest rmsd value to 3v, calculated only over their corresponding hydrophobic moieties (the two rings A and B). In 70% of the cases the first GLUE solution was used. Conversely, for the 18 test set compounds all the first three best ranked solutions of GLUE were used for projection into the model, to obtain estimations slightly dependent on the conformations. Hence, a combination of docking energy and X-ray information guided the selection of the dataset conformers:  details are reported in the Experimental Section.
    The docking program GLUE assesses the binding modes of a ligand with respect to the active site of its protein target on the basis of the GRID force field.16 The flexibility of the ligand is treated by considering several conformations and evaluating each one directly within the binding site, which is mapped by running GRID with diverse probes. The obtained MIF represent how the binding site “feels” the probes. Then, ligand atoms are paired in an iterative procedure to the corresponding MIF, to identify all the ways in which atoms of the ligand, considered in all its conformers generated, could bind to the protein target. Several ligand orientations (sometime millions) are temporally stored, and only the most reliable ones are minimized within the cavity driven by the ligand−target interaction energy assessed by the GRID force field. Finally, GLUE calculates the interaction energy between the entire ligand and the protein binding site:  several solutions are proposed, ranked by the values of the energy scoring function. Some more detailed information on the methodology of GLUE is given in the Experimental Section.
    The GRIND procedure was identically applied as in the initial model, using the same probes (DRY, O, N1, and TIP) and the same settings (see Table 2). After selection via FFD, 275 variables remained active, and the dimensionality of the resulting PLS model was again optimal with two latent variables. These explained 82% of the variance for HIV-1 RT inhibition with a SDEC value = 0.43, a q2 value = 0.66, and an external SDEP value = 0.50 calculated on 18 compounds from the test set, each one included three times. The statistics are reported in Table 2 together with the corresponding data from the initial GRIND model, whereas the prediction of the test set is graphically represented in Figure 6. All test set compounds were predicted within confidence limits of the model, and no outliers were observed. For each compound of the test set the three predicted values are reported in the following way:  the middle values are marked as gray circled-points, whereas the more extreme values are marked with horizontal dashes.

Comparison of the plots in Figure 3 clearly shows that variables with negative coefficients are quite rare, whereas the hydrophobic probe DRY is often involved in relevant peaks including its autocorrelogram and all its cross-correlograms. The interpretation of the relevant peaks is summarized in Table 4.
    Among the autocorrelograms, the variables with the largest impact on HIV-1 RT inhibition are closely located to those already discussed for the initial GRIND model; they comprise variable 26 of the block N1−N1 as well as variables 11 (with negative impact) and 36 (with positive impact) of the block TIP−TIP. Although they slightly differ from their counterparts in terms of node-pair distance (between 0.4 and 1.2 Å) and magnitude, their meaning is identical; i.e., they represent the same interaction fields and, therefore, the same groups of the ligand. The block DRY−DRY represents a certain discrepancy; variable 28 (node-pair distance 12.2 Å) exhibits a higher impact on the GLUE/GRIND model, since its PLS coefficient value is twice as high as the value found in the initial GRIND model (0.10 versus 0.05). Among the cross-correlograms, some differences are observed when comparing the structure-based GLUE/GRIND versus the ligand-based GRIND model. Within the blocks DRY−O and DRY−N1, variables 33 and 27 exhibit a significantly higher importance, which is strictly related to the higher relevance attributed to the block DRY in the GLUE/GRIND model.

    Derivation of Virtual Receptor Sites and Comparison with Experimental Evidence. RT is a heterodimer containing a 560-residue subunit (p66) and a 440-residue subunit (p51). Each subunit contains a polymerase domain composed of four subdomains called fingers, palm, thumb, and connection. RT is a major target for drug design. Two classes of RT inhibitors are in clinical use:  nucleoside analogues and nonnucleoside RT inhibitors (NNRTIs).
    The NNRTI binding site is highly flexible. In the absence of a ligand, the site forms a compact hydrophobic structure, which opens to accommodate suitable ligands. This opening involves a displacement of strands β12-β13-β14 causing repositioning of tryptophan 229 (W229), an essential binding site residue, and the reorientation of the side chains of tyrosines 181 and 188 (Y181, Y188). This distortion of RT blocks the polymerase activity. Important binding determinants for NNRTIs include the formation of hydrogen bonds with the peptide backbone atoms of lysine 101 (K101) and hydrophobic interactions with the aromatic side chains of Y181, Y188, and W229. A bound NNRTI creates a steric barrier that prevents movement of the indole ring of W229 back to its location in active HIV-1 RT.
    Several crystal structures of HIV-1 RT complexed with NNRTIs have been determined and have provided important structural information about the NNRTI binding site.9,19-22 These crystal structures have shown distinct properties of the binding site that can be used for structure-based drug design. However, each reported structure revealed a unique binding pattern, indicating that rational drug design should not rely on one particular crystal structure. Therefore, Sudbeck et al.21 used the binding site coordinates of nine NNRTIs to generate a composite binding pocket revealing a molecular surface that defines a larger-than-presumed NNRTI binding pocket and serves to summarize critical binding features.
    In this context of multiple binding modes of NNRTIs, the interesting findings of Lewis et al.22 deserve mention. In their design studies on new anti-HIV compounds, these authors observed that the more active compounds that are also resilient against mutation of the NNRTI binding pocket make use of more binding modes than the less active and resilient compounds.
    The above summarized knowledge from literature underlines that pharmacophoric considerations regarding the NNRTI binding site should be limited to chemically homogeneous subclasses of NNRTIs. Corresponding X-ray information (1JLQ) of one of the database compounds included in this study (3v in complex with HIV-1 RT) was published by Chan et al.11 An extract of this structure, focusing on the fit of 3v into the binding pocket, is shown in Figure 5. Our below-summarized comparative validity check of a ligand-based and a structure-based 3D-QSAR approach refers to this X-ray information. The validity check focuses on statistical aspects (fitting, prediction, PLS coefficients) as well as the construction of virtual receptor sites (VRS) and their comparison with experimental evidence.
    Comparing the statistical quality of the models either derived with the ligand-based or with the structure-based approach clearly shows that the latter yielded slightly better results in terms of fitting (r2 and SDEC values) and prediction (q2 and external SDEP values).
    In more detail, coincidences and discrepancies between the two models can be observed from an analysis of the PLS coefficients. The majority of the variables exhibits the same or a very similar impact on both models, as evidenced in Table 4. The high similarity between the two models is mostly due to the low flexibility of compounds. Discrepancies can be summarized as follows:  (i) the relevance of hydrophobic variables is higher when the structure-based approach is used; (ii) a few variables lose or invert their impact, when passing from the ligand-based to the structure-based procedure, and vice versa; and (iii) a few other variables only quantitatively vary in their impact depending on the procedure.
    For a deeper insight into some model differences, the impact of a couple of variables will be comparatively discussed in more detail. Such comparison refers to variables 26 and 27 of the block N1−N1, and variable 30 of the block O−N1. They all represent a MIF region that is due to the interaction of the probe N1 with one of the linker oxygens. Chan and co-workers11 described the clear correspondence between the chemical nature of the linker moiety (S < SO < SO2) and increasing RT inhibition; the 3D-QSAR model obtained with the ligand-based approach well reflects this experimental evidence. In fact, the corresponding peak is one of the highest within the PLS coefficients plot (upper part of Figure 3). This peak from the block N1−N1 could indicate the presence of a donor region in front of the linker, particularly in front of one or two oxygens from the SO2 group. However, experimental evidence from the X-ray structure of the binding site contradicts the presence of such a region. In fact, it seems to us that the linker, which is located in a hydrophobic region, probably affects the conformational freedom of the database compounds rather than binds to acceptor sites from the binding pocket. In line with this evidence, the model obtained with the structure-based approach is significantly less influenced by these two variables. This could be explained by the fact that compounds with the SO2 linker, conformations of which were derived from docking studies, do not adopt identical orientations within the binding site. Consequently, the distances between the linker oxygens and other groups of the molecules vary more strongly, and no fixed distance is detected as highly related to the variation of inhibitor activity. This interpretation is underlined by comparing the average distances and their standard deviation for both models. Here we report the exemplary values for the distance between the nitrogen of cyano group in ring A and the more distant oxygen from the SO2 linker, which corresponds to variables 26 and 27 of the block N1−N1. For the ligand-based model, the distance range is narrow (5.03−5.10 Å), with an average value of 5.05 Å and the standard deviation lower than 0.01 Å. For the structure-based model, the distance range is broader (4.46−5.04 Å), the average value is 4.79 Å, and the standard deviation is approximately 0.15 Å.
    A further way to compare the two models is the construction of VRS and their comparison with the real receptor site both in terms of pharmacophoric regions as well as their mutual distances. For this purpose, MIF involved in the relevant peaks (Table 4) were first encoded into pharmacophoric regions around the ligand; for example, the HB donor region in front of the cyano group of ring A was referred to as HBD1. Ten pharmacophoric regions were derived this way:  two HB acceptor (HBA1, HBA2), two HB donor (HBD1, HBD2), three hydrophobic regions (DRY1, DRY2, DRY3) and three regions reflecting cavity edges (TIP1, TIP2, TIP3). Nine out of these 10 pharmacophoric regions were found in agreement with the binding site; only region HBD2 does not fit the real receptor, as already discussed above.
    To detect which atoms of the cavity are mainly involved in binding, we used the directive POSI and the LONT files of the program GRID in addition to the analysis of MIF filtered by ALMOND.23 The directive POSI allows us to calculate the GRID energy at a given grid node; a LONT file allows us to check which atoms of the protein target mainly contribute to the calculated GRID energy and to attribute the contributions of hydrogen bonding, charge−charge interaction, or Lennard-Jones contribution to the overall energy.     Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A dataset of (aryl-)bridged 2-aminobenzonitriles inhibiting HIV-1 RT was subjected to comparative 3D-QSAR studies using GRIND variables, obtained from the probes DRY, O, N1, and TIP, as molecular descriptors and PLS analysis as regression tool. First, a ligand-based model was built starting from conformers obtained by in vacuo minimization. Then, the availability of the X-ray structure of database compound 3v in complex with HIV-1 RT allowed comparative structure-based calculations using the new docking software GLUE for conformer selection. The validity check of the ligand-based versus the structure-based model comprised statistical aspects (fitting, prediction, PLS coefficients) and the construction of virtual receptor sites (VRS) considering both qualitative (pharmacophoric regions) and quantitative aspects (mutual distances); last but not least, results were compared with experimental evidence. Comparing the statistical quality of the models shows that the structure-based approach yields slightly better results in terms of fitting (r2 and SDEC values) and prediction (q2 and external SDEP values). However, higher differences might be expected in case of more flexible compounds. By encoding MIF involved in relevant peaks into pharmacophoric regions, 10 such regions were derived from both models. Nine out of these 10 regions were found to agree with the binding site; only region HBD2 does not fit the real receptor. Also regarding mutual distances, high agreement between the real site and both VRS is observed; differences are never greater than 1.0 Å. Nevertheless, distances from the structure-based approach are closer to the real receptor, but also the VRS obtained by GRIND is not too far from the real site. Taken together, our study proves that the GRIND approach allows the derivation of valid 3D-QSAR models in the absence of X-ray information.

    A. Dataset. The dataset comprises 53 (aryl-)bridged 2-aminobenzonitriles with HIV-1 reverse transcriptase inhibitory potency. Bridging occurs via S, SO, or SO2 moieties; the data set contains 19 sulfides, 9 sulfoxides, and 25 sulfones, respectively. For comparison purposes, reference compounds 4a and 4b were included. A listing of the structures is given in Table 1. For QSAR studies a training (n = 37) and a test set (n = 18) were built. The “Subset selection” option available in the software ALMOND23 was used to automatically select a balanced, chemically diverse test set; the two methods “Largest Minimum Distance” and “Most Descriptive Compound” were combined with a weight of 50% each; they run over PCA scores extracted from the GRIND descriptors described below (point F).
    B. Biological Data. Biological data exclusively stem from Chan et al.11 The IC50 values for inhibitory activity against HIV-1 reverse transcriptase, listed in Table 1, range from 8.54 for the most potent compound (3w) to a value of 4.72 for the weakly active compound (2b) and cover a spectrum of approximately 4 logarithmic units.
    C. Geometry Optimization Using the Powell method. Data set compounds were minimized in vacuo with the program Sybyl18 by applying the Powell method on the 3D coordinates obtained by converting the SMILES codes by means of CONCORD.15 The Simplex procedure was used as the initial optimization system, with 0.05 kcal/(mol·Å) as termination gradient and up to 1000 iterations with the Tripos force field.
    D. Docking of Other HIV-1 RT Ligands. From a search on the Brookhaven Protein Data Bank14 eight different structures of HIV1 reverse transcriptase were downloaded. For each ligand, docking on its corresponding target was carried out following the default procedure of program GLUE, described in detail below.
    E. Docking Studies using GLUE. GLUE is a new docking program aimed at detecting energetically favorable binding modes of a ligand with respect to the protein active site using all the options and capabilities of the GRID force field.16 The protein cavity is mapped using several GRID runs:  a set of different probes is used to mimic all chemical groups present in the ligand and the resulting maps are encoded into compact files, which store the interaction energies. Afterward, an iterative procedure identifies all the ways in which four atoms of the ligand could bind to the target, by pairing every atom to the “nearest” MIF used. Hydrophobic and polar atoms of the ligand for which several conformers are quickly produced are fitted over their corresponding energy maps, giving rise to sometimes millions of ligand orientations, which are temporally stored.
    Then, many orientations are quickly eliminated due to redundancy and steric hindrance constraints. Redundancy occurs whenever two or more orientations are close enough to each other, i.e., the rmsd calculated over their 3D structures is lower than 2.0 Å; therefore, they are grouped and one orientation represents the entire group. Conversely, steric hindrance occurs whenever part of the ligand clashes into the binding site:  in this case, the clashing part is accommodated along the site if possible; otherwise, the orientation is excluded.
    Indeed, this refinement allows only reliable orientations to be processed in the next step:  each orientation is optimized within the cavity by means of successive torsions and translations. These are driven by the ligand−target interaction energy computed by the GRID force field:  each little movement is followed by an energy reassessment according to the GRID standard equation applied over the whole ligand and the active site:
    The optimized orientations represent possible binding modes of the ligand within the site. The interaction energy between the entire ligand and the protein binding site is calculated by using the GLUE equation, which provides an energy scoring function (EGLUE) composed by four contributions:  ESR = steric repulsion energy, EES = electrostatic energy, ERHB = hydrogen-bonding charge reinforcement, and EDRY = hydrophobic energy.
    The final output of the docking procedure is a set of solutions ranked according to the corresponding scoring function values, each defined by the 3D coordinates of its atoms and expressed as a PDB file. The three best ranked solutions were selected for modeling:  for compounds of the training set they were once again ranked, whereas for compounds of the test set all three solutions were used for projection into the model. In the former case, for each of the first three solutions the rmsd value was calculated with respect to the X-ray coordinates of compound 3v, by comparing only their hydrophobic moieties. Then, the solution with the lowest rmsd value to 3v was selected.
    F. GRIND.Grid-independent descriptors (GRIND)13 were generated, analyzed, and interpreted using the software ALMOND, version 3.2.23 GRIND have been designed mainly to represent pharmacodynamic properties; they start from molecular interaction fields computed with the program GRID.16 When MIF are computed for the database molecules, the regions showing favorable energies of interaction represent positions where groups of a receptor would interact favorably with a database molecule. Using different probes, one can obtain a set of such positions that defines a virtual receptor site. GRIND are based on the concept of VRS. Basically, GRIND are a small set of variables representing the geometrical relationships between relevant regions of the VRS. The procedure for obtaining GRIND involves three steps:  (a) computing a set of MIF, (b) filtering the MIF to extract the most relevant regions, and (c) encoding the filtered MIF into the GRIND variables. These variables represent the product of the field energy of node-pairs that are separated by certain distances in the 3D space of each compound. For an estimate of distance in angstroms, the variable number has to be multiplied by 0.4, which is the product of the grid spacing (0.5 Å) and the smoothing window value (0.8). GRIND variables are organized in correlograms either representing node-pairs of the same field (autocorrelograms) or node-pairs of different fields (cross-correlograms). The alignment-independent GRIND, obtained this way, can be used directly for the chemometric analysis and can be interpreted with the appropriate software, using graphical representations of the pharmacophoric regions and their interactions, together with the molecular structures, in interactive 3D plots.
    G. Statistical Analysis. Principal component analysis (PCA) and partial least squares (PLS) analysis were performed within the software ALMOND. No scaling was applied. The optimal dimensionality of the PLS model was chosen according to the results of cross-validation. All computations were run on Silicon Graphics SGI O2 R10000 and R12000 workstations.
