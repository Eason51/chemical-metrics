    Abstract

    Reprogramming somatic cells into induced-pluripotent cells (iPSCs) provides new access to all somatic cell types for clinical application without any ethical controversy arising from the use of embryonic stem cells (ESCs). Established protocols for iPSCs generation based on viral transduction with defined factors are limited by low efficiency and the risk of genetic abnormality. Several small molecules have been reported as replacements for defined transcriptional factors, but a chemical able to replace Oct3/4 allowing the generation of human iPSCs is still unavailable. Using a cell-based High Throughput Screening (HTS) campaign, we identified that 2-[4-[(4-methoxyphenyl)methoxy]phenyl]acetonitrile (1), termed O4I1, enhanced Oct3/4 expression. Structural verification and modification by chemical synthesis showed that O4I1 and its derivatives not only promoted expression and stabilization of Oct3/4 but also enhanced its transcriptional activity in diverse human somatic cells, implying the possible benefit from using this class of compounds in regenerative medicine.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Embryonic stem cells (ESCs) emerged as a donor source of cells for regenerative medicine. However, immune rejection following transplantation and ethical concerns of using ESCs have hampered their clinical application. The reprogramming from mature somatic cells to ES-like cells, a pluripotent state of cells termed iPSCs (induced pluripotent stem cells), provides a possible means to circumvent these problems. One well-established and widely spread method is transduction with viral vectors carrying defined factors, such as Oct3/4, Sox2, Lin28, Nanog, c-Myc, and Klf4.(1-3) However, random integration in the genome in this case leads to genetic changes and adverse effects on later differentiation into desired cell types.(4) Therefore, a number of methods including transient transfection, using nonintegrating vectors, protein overexpression, or mRNA- and microRNA based transfection have been developed as alternative approaches to minimize or avoid this integral risk.(5, 6) Nevertheless, most of these protocols have limitations, such as very low efficiency, time-consuming protocols, and/or poor reproducibility. Thus, it is still necessary to look for an efficient and reliable method in reprogramming somatic cells into iPSCs in order to meet clinical requirements.
    Mounting evidence proved that small molecules targeting specific protein(s) can rapidly, reversibly, and precisely regulate cell signaling and clearly overcome the limitation of genetic modulation in reprogramming.(6, 7) Recently, Ding and his group successfully achieved the generation of iPSC by a cocktail of small molecules in the combination with viral transduction of a single factor (Oct3/4) from mouse and human fibroblasts.(8) In another recent report, Li et al. used an azaindole derivative able to activate Oct3/4 and Nanog,(9) and a combination of this small molecule with four defined factors resulted in enhanced reprogramming efficiency in mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs).(9) More recently, Hou et al. demonstrated the generation of mCiPSc (mouse chemically iPS) with just a cocktail of 7 small molecules.(10) However, a small molecule that can facilitate Oct3/4 activation in human cells is still unavailable.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Inspired by this issue, we searched for small molecules able to increase the activation of Oct3/4 from a collection of approximatly 250,000 compounds through a HTS (high throughput screening) campaign in an Oct3/4 promoter based assay driving the luciferase gene. For confirmation, we measured the luminous activity in additional stable Oct3/4 promoter-driven luciferase reporter HEK293 cells (HEK-Oct3/4), incubated with each hit compound at a uniform concentration (20 μM) for 24 h. Analysis of the HTS data using Instant JChem (http://www.chemaxon.com, ChemAxon, Hungary) revealed approximately 300 compounds that yielded at least 3-fold higher activity in the luciferase assay than cells treated with DMSO alone. Among them, 2-[4-[(4-methoxyphenyl)methoxy]phenyl]-acetonitrile, abbreviated as Oct4 inducing compound 1 (O4I1, Figure 1A),(11) was one of the top 5 most active compounds, displaying more than 5-fold induction of luciferase signal relative to the DMSO control (Figure 1B).
    We subsequently embarked on an exercise to independently confirm the results obtained from the HTS campaign for O4I1. Therefore, we resynthesized O4I1 by a nucleophilic substitution of 1-(chloromethyl)-4-methoxybenzene with 2-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-acetonitrile in the presence of sodium hydride as base in dried DMF (Supporting Information, SI. 1) as previously reported.(12) The product was purified by recrystallization from ethyl acetate. The structure and purity of O4I1 were spectrometrically ascertained (Experimental section). We exposed HEK-Oct3/4 reporter cells to our newly synthesized compound and quantitatively analyzed the luminescent activity, which disclosed a concentration-dependent increase and indicated that a 5 μM concentration of O4I1 was sufficient to enhance Oct3/4 overexpression 2-fold and 3-fold after 48 and 72 h of treatment, respectively (Supporting Information, SI. 2). According to the published protocol that a successful reprogramming required at least 2–5 days of exogenous overexpression of defined factors,(1, 13) we additionally examined the duration of compound mediated induction and detected that the effect was initiated after 24 h of incubation and was sustained and increased over the test period (Figure 1C). We next analyzed gene expression with qRT-PCR using a random primer synthesized cDNA from RNA, isolated from HEK293 cells treated with O4I1 for 72 h, and observed increased Oct3/4 mRNA levels by approximately 2.5- and 4-fold with 10 μM and 20 μM compound, respectively (Figure 2A), showing that activation also occurred in the endogenous gene in HEK293 cells.
    As mentioned above, Oct3/4 being one of the most important pluripotent master genes not only initiates cell dedifferentiation(14) but also directly regulates the expression of other pluripotent genes including Nanog and Sox2.(15) Hence, the ability of a small molecule to accumulate functional Oct3/4 protein is an essential step in the evaluation of an Oct3/4 inducing compound. Therefore, we directly assayed the protein level of Oct3/4 using whole cell lysates collected from HEK293 incubated with increasing concentrations of O4I1 for 48 h. A special Oct3/4 antibody, detecting only the Oct3/4 isoform with a molecular weight of 44 kDa, was used in our experiments to avoid any confusion, which might be caused by other Oct3/4 isoforms.(16) The results from this analysis clearly demonstrated a concentration-dependent increase of Oct3/4 starting from 6 μM (Supporting Information, SI. 3), while endogenous Oct3/4 in DMSO treatment was undetectable under this short exposure time. Expectedly, we observed an increase in the levels of Sox2 and Nanog (Supporting Information, SI. 3). We next looked for the duration of the increase in protein levels and examined Oct3/4 and Sox2 expression at different time points. Oct3/4 and Sox2 showed accumulation after 24 and 48 h, respectively (Figure 2B), and the increase lasted at least up to 96 h. Taken together, using O4I1 synthesized in our laboratory, we could confirm our screening result and show that O4I1 affects the expression of Oct3/4 in HEK cells in a concentration dependent manner.
    As shown above, the expression of several pluripotency associated genes was detected in embryonic HEK293 cells. In an effort to test the ability of O4I1 to induce Oct3/4 expression in terminally differentiated cells, we next used a fibroblast, a common source for generating iPSCs due to its accessible availability and relatively high efficiency. We tested the effect of O4I1 in two fibroblast cell lines, namely, human adult primary fibroblast (HF) and commercial human neonatal foreskin fibroblast (HFF). The immunoblotting results clearly showed the induction of Oct3/4 after 72 h of incubation in HF (Figure 2C) as well as after 7 days in HFF in response to O4I1 at 10 μM (Figure 2D).
    Our results clearly demonstrated that the lead structure, O4I1, is able to activate Oct3/4 in various biological assays. To further gain insight into the influence of chemical structure on the activity of Oct3/4 expression, we attempted to alter substituents on the halogen-benzene moiety (Table 1) and examined their biological effects in the HEK-Oct3/4 reporter assay after 72 h of treatment. We found that replacement of −OMe with −F at the R2 position (compound 2) gave the most potent compound, yielding 2-fold higher induction than O4I1, while replacement with −OCF3 (3) or −NO2 (4) resulted in a slight enhancement. Conversely, replacement with −Me (5) or −CN (7) led to a marked reduction on activation (2-fold less than O4I1). Interestingly, compound 6 carrying −Ph instead of −Cl failed in promoting Oct3/4 expression suggesting that bulky, rigid groups as substituents might not be favorable in this position. Moreover, we replaced the acetonitrile-group with a nitrile-group to investigate the effect of depleting C-space upon its activity and revealed that this modification (8, 9, 10, and 11) generally resulted in an approximately 30% reduction in activity relative to the respective corresponding molecule (1, 2, 3, and 4). Within the actonitrile and nitrile series, a similar decrease trend (−F > −OCF3> −OMe ≈ −NO2) was observed (Table 1).

    To monitor the transcriptional activity of drug-induced Oct3/4, we transduced NCCIT cells (embryonal carcinoma cells originated from teratocarcinoma) with a Cignal Lenti Oct3/4 reporter vector (SB Bioscience, Germany). These cells termed NCCIT-Oct3/4 detect the activity of an Oct3/4 transcriptional response element. With this assay, we measured luminescence activity after 72 h of treatment (Table 1) and could confirm the induction with most O4I1 derivates, although the level of induction was considerably lower in most cases in comparison to the results obtained with HEK-Oct3/4 cells. These results might be explained by the pluripotent character of NCCIT cells, in which the stemness-associated networks of proteins are already highly activated. Therefore, these cells are refractory to any additional activation enforced by a small molecule. However, as in the case of HEK-Oct3/4 cells compound 2 appeared to be the most active yielding a 4-fold Oct3/4 driven expression relative to the DMSO control after 72 h of treatment, while 6 reduced Oct3/4 activity. Additionally, we performed an online luciferase assay in NCCIT-OCT3/4 and NCCIT-Nanog cells as previously described,(17) in which the induction of Oct3/4 and Nanog was continually measured upon O4I1 and 2. The results sustained the ability of O4I1 to activate Oct3/4 as well as Nanog, which was improved after chemical modification (Supporting Information, SI. 4 and 5).
    Next, we evaluated the activity of compound 2 in NCCIT cells and Oct3/4-deficient HeLa cells and observed a clear accumulation of Oct3/4 and Nanog in both cell lines analyzed by immunoblotting (Figure 3A and Supporting Information, SI. 6). Similarly, we also found abundances of Oct3/4, Lin28, Sox2, and Nanog analyzed by immunoblotting in 2-treated human primary fibroblasts (HF) (Figure 3B and C), displaying a time-dependent activation of Oct3/4. We carried out a qRT-PCR experiment to evaluate the influence of 2 on pluripotency-associated gene expression in HF cells. The results clearly showed the overexpression of pluripotent genes, which are involved in cellular reprogramming, including Oct3/4 (7-fold), Nanog (12-fold), and Klf4 (6-fold), in response to 2 (Figure 4A), as well as mild enrichment in Rex1 (1.3-fold) and c-Myc (2-fold). Remarkably, we observed a significant reduction in the level of N-Cadherin as well as Zeb1 levels and an increase in the E-Cadherin level, which was further confirmed by FACS analysis using the E-Cadherin antibody (Figure 4B), suggesting the occurrence of mesenchymal-to-epithelial transition (MET) during the treatment, a hallmark of reprogramming initiation.(18, 19) In good agreement, the coexpression of Nanog (red, Figure 4C upper panels) and Klf4 (green, Figure 4C upper), as well as Oct3/4 (red, Figure 4C lower) and Sox2 (green, Figure 4C lower), was readily detected after 5 days of incubation with 2 visualized by immunostaining. Interestingly, a part of cells was only Klf4 positive or Oct3/4 positive. To make this more clear, we counted single and costaining of cells (Supporting Information, SI. 7) and found that only 1/3 of Klf4 positive cells coexpressed Nanog, while Sox2 is only detected in Oct3/4 positive cells, with Sox2 and Oct3/4 expressed in 6% and 9%, respectively. In general, ESCs or iPSCs behave with unique cell cycle character with a short G1 and cumulative S phase.(20) We measured the DNA content with propidium iodide (PI) and found that the treatment led to a slight reduction (from 74% in mock to 66%) in the number of cells in G1 phase and a more than 3-fold enrichment (from 2% to 7%) in S phase (Supporting Information, SI. 8). In a recent publication, a small molecule cocktail, including TGFβ inhibitor, GSK3β inhibitor, MAPK inhibitor, HDAC inhibitor, and PDK1 activator, could convert fibroblasts to iPSCs in combination with viral overexpression of Oct3/4.(8) We used this protocol, but replaced the TGFβ inhibitor and GSK3β inhibitor with our recently identified dual kinase inhibitor (E738)(21) and examined alkaline phosphatase (ALP) activity in living cells, with PI counterstaining to visualize dead cells. Strikingly, few ALP positive/PI negative cells appeared after 5 days of incubation with 2, while the number of ALP positive cells was higher with the small molecule cocktail (Supporting Information, SI. 9). However, this transient activation disappeared 2–3 days later, suggesting that additional factors and adjusted culture conditions will be required.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, we report the discovery of 2-[4-[(4-methoxy phenyl)methoxy]phenyl]-acetonitrile (O4I1) as a potent Oct3/4 inducer. We note that the related compound 4-(benzyloxy)phenyl)acetonitrile can serve as an intermediate for the synthesis of monoamine oxidase type B inhibitor tetrazoles.(12) Surprisingly, its biological activity has not been studied in depth. Here, using newly synthesized O4I1, we showed that it enhances Oct3/4 gene expression, increases Oct3/4 protein levels, and promotes Oct3/4 mediated transcriptional activation. We further chemically modified O4I1 and found compound 2, which markedly elevated the efficiency of Oct3/4 induction. In comparison to the level of Oct3/4 driven by viral transduction (100–1000-fold) in reprogramming, our small molecules only exhibited a mild increase, which could probably be improved in further structure–activity relationship studies. However, experiments from other groups suggest that already moderately higher Oct3/4 levels may contribute to cell differentiation.(22, 23) Thus, the precise level of Oct3/4 expression required for successful reprogramming has still to be elucidated. In a very recent paper, mCiPSCs were achieved by using small molecules only in MEF, though unfortunately the level of Oct3/4 induced by the small molecule cocktail was not shown.(10) Nevertheless, that result still indicated, that the levels of defined factors sufficient for cell differentiation can be achieved by small molecules.(10) Indeed, our data also showed the occurrence of MET in compound-treated fibroblasts. With our results presented here, we demonstrate that O4I1 and its derivatives enhance the activation of Oct3/4 in different cell lines including human fibroblast, implicating the potential use of these small molecules in iPSC generation and regenerative medicine.

    Experimental Section

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    Construction of Cell Lines

    A 2.6 kb promoter of the human Oct3/4 gene (Entrez identifier 5460) was cloned into a plasmid, driving the firefly luciferase gene from Photinus pyralis. Monoclonal cell lines used for screening were generated from the human embryonic kidney cell line (HEK293, ATCC CRL-1573) that contained the DNA of the luciferase reporter plasmid in a stable manner. For the identification of Oct3/4 promoter activation in individual cells and cell clusters with selected compounds, a plasmid containing luciferase as reporter was used. A Cignal Lenti Oct3/4 reporter was purchased from SB Bioscience (Germany) and transfected into NCCIT cells according to the manufactor’s instructions.

    Isolation and Culture of Primary Human Fibroblasts from Skin Samples

    Freshly retained human skin samples from redundant operation material (approved by the Medical Ethics Committee of the Medical Faculty Mannheim, Heidelberg University (2009–-350N-MA)) was transferred into a fibroblast cell culture medium (DMEM containing 10% FBS, 1× nonessential amino acids, 1% penicillin/streptomycin, and 50 μM β-mercaptoethanol). The skin samples containing the dermis and adipose tissue were cut into small pieces. In the presence of 0.25% trypsin in PBS, the tissue was then cut with scalpels in order to dissociate the tissue until it reached a pasty consistency. These tissue fragments were then resuspended in fibroblast cell culture medium, plated on tissue culture dishes, and kept at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere at 5% CO2 until fibroblasts grew out. These fibroblasts were further expanded.

    Cell Culture

    HEK293, HF, and HFF cells were cultured in DMEM (Gibco, Germany) containing 10%, 20%, and 15% FBS (PAA, Germany), respectively, and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Pen/Strep, Gibco, Germany). The cells were maintained under 5% CO2 at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere. Oct3/4 stable reporter HEK293 cells were cultured in DMEM medium containing high glucose, 10% FCS, 1% 1 M Hepes–buffer, 0.5% Pen/Strep, and 0.5% 200 mM glutamine with fresh addition of 100 μg/mL hygromycin before use. Cignal Lenti Oct3/4 infected NCCIT cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 (Gibco) containing 10% FBS and 1% Pen/Strep with fresh addition of 625 ng/mL puromycin (Santa Cruz, Germany)

    Luciferase Reporter Assay

    The luciferase reporter assay was performed using a Beatle juice kit (PJK, Germany) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, HEK293-Oct3/4 or NCCIT-Oct3/4 reporter cells were plated into 24-well tissue culture plates at a density of 100,000 per well for 24 h. The cells were treated with compounds as indicated in the text and harvested with 90 μL of Beatle lysis-juice at 37 °C for 15 min. The protein concentration was determined by the Bradford assay (Sigma, Germany). One hundred microliters of reaction mixture containing luciferin and ATP was added to 20 μL of cell lysis substance in a white plate (Gibco, Germany), incubated for 5 min, and measured by a luminometer plate reader. The activity was determined as percent luminous intensity of treated cells over control cells from at least five-independent experiments. The online luciferase reporter assay in living cells was performed by directly adding luciferin to the culture medium as reported.(17)

    qRT-PCR

    Quantitative reverse-transcription real-time-PCR was performed using a Light Cycler 480 (Roche, Germany) following the manufacturer’s protocol. Briefly, total RNA was isolated from cells using TRIzol (Qiagen, Germany). The same amount of RNA was used to reverse-transcriptionally synthesize cDNA by using random primers. qPCR was performed using the SYBR Green PCR master mix (Roche, Germany) and the following primer pairs (MWG, Eurofins, Germany): (Oct3/4) 5s, GAAGTTGGAGAAGGTGGAAC; 3as, GGTGATCCTCTTCTGCTTCAG; (Nanog) 5s, GAACTGTGTTCTCTTCCACC; 3as, CACCTGTTTGTAGCTGAGGTTC; (Sox2) 5s, CAAGACGCTCATGAAGAAGG; 3as, CATGTGCGCGTAACTGTCCATG; (Lin28A) 5s, GTGGATGTCTTTGTGCACCAG; and 3as, GACACGGATGGATTCCAGAC. β-Actin 5s, CTGACTACCTCATGAAGATCCTC; 3as, CATTGCCAATGGTGATGACCTG was used as an endogenous control. Data were normalized to the value of DMSO treated cells showing the mean ± SD of quadruplicates and are representative of two independent experiments

    Immunoblot

    As described before,(24) the cells were incubated with the compounds as indicated in the text and lysed using urea-lysis buffer (1 mM EDTA, 0.5% Triton X-100, 5 mM NaF, 6 M urea, 1 mM Na3VO4, 10 μg/mL pepstatin, 100 μM PMSF, and 3 μg/mL aprotinin in PBS). Enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) immunoblot analysis was performed. Forty micrograms of total protein was resolved on 10% SDS–PAGE gels and immunoblotted with Oct3/4 (Cell Signaling, NEB, Germany), Nanog (Abcam, USA and Cell Signlaing, Germany), Sox2 (Invitrogen, Germany), Vinculin (Cell Signaling, NEB, Germany) or β-Actin antibodies (Sigma-Aldrich, Germany). Primary antibodies were incubated at a 1:1000 dilution in TBS (pH 7.5) with 0.1% Tween-20 and 5% BSA/milk with gentle agitation overnight at 4 °C. Secondary antibodies (Cell signaling) were incubated in TBS (pH 7.5) with 5% BSA/milk and 0.1% Tween-20 at a 1:10,000 dilution for 1 h at room temperature.

    Immunofluorescence

    Human fibroblasts were seeded in a 96-well plate coated with Geltrex (Life technologies, Germany) at a density of 10,000 cells/well. After 24 h, the cells were incubated with compound 2 in human ESC medium (DMEM/F12 glutamax, 20% knockout serum replacement, 10 ng/mL bFGF, and 1 mM β-mercaptoethanol) for 5 days, fixed with 4% PFA at RT for 15 min, and blocked with blocking buffer (5% goat serum and 0.3% Triton X-100 in PBS) for 1 h. Blocking solution was aspirated and incubated with Nanog (1:1000; Cell Signaling), KLF4 (1:100; Abgent, Biomol), and Sox2 (1:100; Cell Signaling, NEB) Oct3/4 antibody (Cell Signaling, NEB) in antibody dilution buffer (1% BSA and 0.2% Triton X-100 in PBS) at 4 °C overnight. The secondary antibody (Goat antirabbit Alexa Fluor 488, and Goat antimouse Alexa Fluor 594; Dianova) was added and incubated for 1 h. Hoechst 33342 (1 μg/mL in PBS) was used to visualize nuclei. To calculate the percentage of staining positive cells, 10 × 20 visual fields were randomly selected, and the number of positive cells were counted and divided by the number of Hoechst 33342 positive cells.

    FACS Analysis of E-Cadherin Expression and Cell Cycle Analysis

    Human fibroblasts were seeded in a 24-well plate coated with Geltrex and incubated with compound 2 (20 μM) for 3 days in hESC medium. The cells were harvested, fixed, and permeabillized as described before.(24) The E-Cadherin primary antibody (Cell Signaling, NEB) and PerCP-Cy5.5-conjugated secondary antibody (Santa Cruz) were used. Normal unconjugated rabbit IgG was used as the negative control. Cell cycle anylysis was performed as reported.(24)

    Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) Live Stain

    The fibroblasts were treated with 2 (20 μM) in combination with a cocktail for 5 days as previously described with a slight modification,(8) in which E738 was used to replace the TGFβ and GSK3β inhibitors.(21) Alkaline phosphatase (ALP) live stain kit (Life technologies, Germany) was used to determine the activity of ALP, according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

    Statistics

    Statistical and graphical data analyses were performed using Microsoft Excel 2007 software. The statistical significance of compared measurements was performed by using the Student’s one-tailed or two-tailed t test, considering P = 0.05 as statistically significant.

    Chemistry

    Reagents

    Solvents and reagents obtained from commercial suppliers were at least of reagent grade and were distilled or dried according to prevailing methods prior to use, if necessary. The syntheses were done under nitrogen atmosphere. For monitoring the reactions, Alugram SIL G/UV254 sheets for TLC (Macherey & Nagel) were used. Flash column chromatography was accomplished using silica gel 60 (Macherey & Nagel, 0.040–0.063 mm). The purity of compounds was determined to be at least more than 95% by HPLC analysis.

    General Procedure for the Syntheses

    Method a: under nitrogen atmosphere, 2-(4-hydroxyphenyl)acetonitrile or 4-hydroxybenzonitrile (12.5 mmol) in 10 mL of dried DMF was slowly added to a suspension of sodium hydride (15 mmol) in dried DMF at 0 °C. The generation of hydrogen was observed. Afterward, various amount of 1-(chloromethyl)-4-substituted-benzene (10.0 mmol) in dried DMF were dropped into this suspension. The temperature was allowed to warm to room temperature, and the mixture was stirred for additional 4 h. TLC was employed to control the reaction. The mixture was diluted with water (100 mL) and extracted with ethyl acetate. The organic phase was dried over sodium sulfate. After the removal of the solvent, the residue was purified either by recrystallization or by flash chromatography.
    Method b: under nitrogen atmosphere, various amount of 1-(bromomethyl)-4-substituted-benzene (10.0 mmol) in 10 mL of dried DMF were added to a suspension of 2-(4-hydroxyphenyl)acetonitrile or 4-hydroxybenzonitrile (12.5 mmol) and 50 mmol water-free potassium carbonate in 10 mL of dried DMF. The mixture was stirred overnight at room temperature. TLC was employed to control the completeness of the reaction. Afterward, the mixture was diluted with water (100 mL) and extracted with ethyl acetate. The organic phase was dried over sodium sulfate and concentrated by evaporation under reduced pressure. The residue was purified either by recrystallization or by flash chromatography.

Chemical shifts are reported in ppm from tetramethylsilane with solvent as the internal standard (1H DMSO-d6, δ 2.50; 13C DMSO-d6, δ 39.5). The following abbreviations were used to explain the multiplicities in NMR spectra: s =singlet, d = doublet, t = triplet, q = quartet, and m = multiplet. High-resolution mass spectra (HRMS) were recorded on a Bruker ApexQe hybrid 9.4 T FT-ICR (ESI). Elemental analyses were performed on an Element Analyzer PerkinElmer EA 240 or 2400 CHN.
