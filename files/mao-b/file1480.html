    Abstract

    The reprogramming of energy metabolism is considered to be one of the main characteristics of cancer. The development of therapeutic agents targeting glycolysis to alter aberrant glucose metabolism and restore oxidative phosphorylation has emerged as an effective approach for cancer therapy. In this way, we have developed a conjugate AlbA-DCA, which can induce a marked increase in intracellular ROS and alleviate the accumulation of lactic acid in TME. Meanwhile, AlbA-DCA selectively kills cancer cells and exhibits an excellent synergistic effect. Mechanism studies confirm that AlbA-DCA can induce apoptosis and ferroptosis. We also confirm that AlbA-DCA can remold the tumor immunosuppression microenvironment via eliminating M2-TAMs to inhibit both primary and distal tumor progression in a dual-4T1 tumor model in female BALB/c mice. As a result, rational design of natural saponin and PDK inhibitor to induce apoptosis-ferroptosis-M2-TAMs polarization for enhanced cancer therapy is a promising strategy, thus providing a new idea for cancer therapy.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The reprogramming of energy metabolism is considered to be one of the main characteristics of cancer.(1) Glucose is transported into the cytoplasm via glucose transporters and metabolized to pyruvate. Normal cells typically convert pyruvate to acetyl-CoA catalyzed by pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) during mitochondria oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) as the main source of energy. However, due to the up-regulation of the glucose transporter and glycolytic enzymes, such as lactate dehydrogenase A (LDHA) and pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase (PDK), cancer cells avidly consume glucose and convert pyruvate to lactate for energy to survive, resulting in a phenomenon known as the “Warburg effect” or aerobic glycolysis.(2,3) The enhanced aerobic glycolysis continuously transports lactate out of cells, leading to the accumulation of lactic acid in the tumor microenvironment (TME) and causing local acidification.(4) Increasing evidence demonstrates that high levels of aerobic glycolysis-derived lactic acid promote tumor progression through reduced apoptosis, enhanced cell mobility, augmented metastatic potential, and limited immune response.(5−8) Given these correlations, the development of therapeutic agents targeting glycolysis to alter aberrant glucose metabolism and restore OXPHOS has emerged as an effective approach for cancer therapy.
    Dichloroacetate acid (DCA) is used for the treatment of lactic acidosis in humans as an inhibitor of PDK, which is a kinase that regulates the flux of pyruvate into mitochondria by inhibiting the mitochondrial enzyme PDH.(9) By inhibiting PDK and thereby stimulating PDH, DCA can shift cellular metabolism away from aerobic glycolysis to OXPHOS and eventually alleviate the accumulation of lactic acid in TME.(10,11) This metabolic switch facilitates the up-regulation of pro-apoptotic mediators such as cytochrome c (cyt c) and the Bcl-2 family of proteins, both of which can induce apoptosis.(12) Moreover, DCA can also improve the immunosuppressive status modulated by glycolysis-derived lactic acid and promote tumor immunotherapy.(13) Unlike many anticancer drugs, DCA reverses only the mitochondrial status of tumor cells, resulting in a higher sensitivity to malignant cells more vulnerable than normal cells.(12) However, therapeutically high doses of DCA (∼100 mM) are required for anticancer proliferation due to the poor mitochondrial targeting of DCA.(14)
    Construction of mitochondria-targeted molecular scaffold triphenylphosphonium (TPP) cation and DCA load via an esterase-triggered ester bond has been proven to be an effective method for enhancing mitochondrial targeting and antitumor potency.(15,16) Although this design would suggest the effective delivery of DCA to mitochondria, the biodegradable linker may break before reaching the target site, resulting in the actual DCA concentration in mitochondria to be significantly reduced. The fusion of metallodrugs with DCA has also been developed as a DCA delivery strategy for synergistic therapy, including platinum(IV)(17−19) and rhenium(I).(20) However, the toxicity of metallodrugs is also a problem that cannot be ignored. Therefore, the construction of an efficient and safe multifunctional DCA delivery conjugate still needs unremitting effort and development.
    Recent studies have revealed that the antitumor activity of the natural product triterpenoid is associated with mitochondria by ΔΨm depolarization and the release of cytochrome c. This results in activation of the caspase protein family, ultimately leading to apoptosis.(21) In our previous study, we focused on the synthesis and structural modification of a natural oleanane triterpenoid saponin Albiziabioside A (AlbA), originally isolated from the aerial parts of Albizia inundata.(22) For example, we found that AlbA could induce A375 cell apoptosis by regulating mitochondrial function involving a caspase cascade with an IC50 value of 5.47 μM.(23) The AlbA coupled cinnamoyl substituent derivative could also affect mitochondrial pathways and then reverse the apoptotic silence of HCT116 cells with IC50 value 7.6 μM.(24) Furthermore, AlbA and its derivative showed excellent selective killing of cancer cells without affecting normal human cell growth.(23−25) However, like other oleanane triterpenoid saponins, AlbA derivatives reached their limit in regulating the normalization of mitochondrial respiration and inhibiting tumor proliferation.
    In this study, we designed a conjugate AlbA-DCA, AlbA connected to a DCA subunit, using the regulatory capacity of AlbA-disrupting mitochondria and the inhibitory effect of DCA on PDK. We hypothesized that, through the intramolecular synergistic effects of AlbA and DCA, conjugate AlbA-DCA can affect energy metabolism reprogramming of tumor cells, shift cellular metabolism away from aerobic glycolysis to OXPHOS, and thereby alleviate the accumulation of lactic acid in TME, ultimately to induce mitochondrial-dependent apoptosis and improve the tumor immunosuppressive environment to combined cancer therapy. Here, we described the synthesis, characterization, and investigation of antitumor modes of conjugateAlbA-DCA (Scheme 1).

    Design and Synthesis of AlbA-DCA

    Major challenges in designing multifunctional therapeutic agents that couple two different functional drug molecules include the compatibility, stability, and activity of drugs, and the number of suitable attachment sites. With those issues in mind, we designed conjugate AlbA-DCA and control compounds AlbA-CA. We designed this agent to test whether metabolic reprogramming, followed by drug activation, could be achieved in the context of a single multifunctional chemical entity. AlbA-DCA contains a mitochondria-disrupting AlbA moiety reversibly connected to a DCA subunit via a comparatively stable amide linkage, which was expected to be susceptible to carboxylesterase-induced release. Conjugate AlbA-DCA was obtained by coupling DCA-anhydride with intermediate 1, which was synthesized according to our previously reported method,(24) followed by debenzoylation and deacetylation in a catalytic amount of sodium methoxide (Scheme 2). Conjugate AlbA-DCA was purified by silica gel column chromatography and characterized by 1H NMR, 13C NMR, and HRMS analyses. All of the characteristic signals can correspond to the structure one by one in the NMR, such as in the 13C NMR δ 180.0 for -CO- of -COOH, 164.7 for -CO in -Cl2Ac,, 144.5 and 122.4 for -C═C in oleanolic acid, 105.2 and 104.0 for two terminal carbons in Glu and Ara, and 88.8 for -3′C of oleanolic acid. In addition, the molecular formula obtained by high-resolution mass spectrometry test was consistent with the theoretical calculation value, which is C44H69Cl2NO12. Next, we investigated the stability of conjugates. Upon incubation of Alb-DCA in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS [pH 7.4]) and plasma at 37 °C for 2 h, no decomposition was observed. The results provided a certain stability basis for tail vein injection in the follow-up in vivo experiments. On the other hand, exposure of conjugate Alb-DCA to carboxylesterase (1 U/mL) resulted in the scission of the aniline–amide bond (Supporting Information Figure S1).

    Inhibition of PDK Activity

    First, we measured the expression of PDK on several cancer cell lines by Western blot assay, including human melanoma A375 cells, human breast cancer MCF-7 cells, human colon cancer HCT116 cells, and mouse breast cancer 4T1 cells. The results showed that the PDK protein is highly expressed in breast cancer cells, especially MCF-7 (Figure S2). The inhibition effect of conjugate AlbA-DCA, AlbA, Na-DCA, and AlbA-CA on the PDK activity was investigated in vitro using ADP-Glo Luminescent Kinase Assay. Encouragingly, covalently coupled DCA had little effect on its PDK inhibition activity in the MCF-7 cell culture fluid. As shown in Figure 1A, both AlbA-DCA and Na-DCA displayed the same level of inhibition activity. AlbA and AlbA-CA had no effect on its PDK inhibition activity. However, AlbA-DCA displayed no PDK inhibition activity in the PBS and plasma (Figure S3). This finding leads us to suggest that AlbA-DCA is an effective substrate for carboxylesterase and that it may be activated in the tumor microenvironment (compared with plasma, a high concentration of carboxyesterase was found in the tumor microenvironment.(16)). Furthermore, the inhibition effect of conjugate AlbA-DCA, AlbA, and Na-DCA on the PDK activity was investigated in MCF-7 cells using Western blot assay. However, Na-DCA could not effectively down-regulate the expression of PDK, which may be caused by low permeability and the mitochondria-targeted capacity of DCA in the cell (Figure 1B,C). Additionally, AlbA-DCA could effectively down-regulate the PDK expression in a concentration-dependent manner, which was consistent with the kinase experiments.

    Effects of AlbA-DCA on Cancer Cell Metabolic Reversal

    According to the consensus that DCA can alter the energy metabolism reprogramming of tumor cells from aerobic glycolysis back to OXPHOS, a mitochondrial bioenergetic curve of AlbA-DCA in MCF-7 cells metabolic profiling was obtained using Seahorse XF24 extracellular flux analyzer. MCF-7 cells were pretreated with AlbA-DCA (2 μM), AlbA (10 μM), and Na-DCA (30 μM) for 4 h. Then the extracellular acidification rate (ECAR) was measured in response to the sequential injection of glucose (10 mM), oligomycin A (1 μM), and 2-deoxy-d-glucose (2-DG, 100 mM) which are indirect measures of glycolysis, maximal glycolysis capacity, and glycolysis reserve, respectively (Figure 1D1). In a glucose-free medium, injecting glucose into MCF-7 cells caused increases in the ECAR of the control, AlbA and Na-DCA (Figure 1D1,D2). Then, an inhibitor of mitochondrial ATP synthase, oligomycin, was added, which triggered further change in the control, AlbA, and Na-DCA (Figure 1D1,D3). Finally, the hexokinase inhibitor 2-DG was injected and induced a rapid decline in the ECAR (Figure 1D1,D4). However, AlbA-DCA-treated cells did not show any significant changes in the ECAR (Figure 1D). These data confirmed that AlbA-DCA significantly inhibited glycolysis in MCF-7 cells.
    We next investigated the changes in the oxygen consumption rate (OCR) as a method of assessing mitochondrial OXPHOS. Oligomycin, FCCP, and a mix of antimycin/rotenone were serially injected to measure ATP production, maximal respiration, and spare respiration capacity, respectively. The control MCF-7 cells exhibited an OCR curve change as follows (Figure 1E1): an initial basal respiration level that indicated that the cells were using glucose to oxidize to produce oxygen; an attenuated, ATP-linked respiration with the subsequent addition of oligomycin (an ATP synthase inhibitor, 1 μM); a rebound of the OCR peak that represented maximum respiratory capacity after the injection of FCCP (a mitochondrial uncoupling agent, 2 μM); and a substantial decrease after the addition of antimycin/rotenone (1 μM) that completely inhibited mitochondrial respiration. As shown in Figure 1E, the OCR level of AlbA-DCA was less than those of cells treated with AlbA and Na-DCA (Figure 1E1), indicating that AlbA-DCA (6 h) could effectively inhibit mitochondrial respiration and non-mitochondrial respiration. In the shortened treatment time of AlbA-DCA (3 h), the treated MCF-7 cells showed a higher OCR curve with increased basal respiration (Figure 1E2), enhanced ATP production (Figure 1E3), and elevated spare respiration capacity (Figure 1E4) compared to the control cells. These data documented that AlbA-DCA altered the energy metabolism from aerobic glycolysis back to OXPHOS in MCF-7 cells in the early stage.

    Effects of AlbA-DCA on Mitochondria Dysfunction

    On the basis of the targeting of mitochondrial capacity and remodeling of mitochondrial respiration, the effect of AlbA-DCA on mitochondrial membrane potential (MMP, ΔΨm) was examined, and AlbA and Na-DCA were compared using JC-1 staining by flow cytometry. As shown in Figure 2A, after treatment with AlbA (10 μM), a moderate loss of ΔΨm (25.86%) was observed, which also proved that AlbA could target the mitochondria and further disrupt the function. In addition, the AlbA-DCA (2 μM) treatment of MCF-7 cells resulted in a significant decrease in ΔΨm(46.39%), which was consistent with its altered energy metabolism capacity.
    It is well-known that mitochondria are the major source of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which are important mediators and players in cell death. Therefore, we examined the intracellular ROS-inducing capability of AlbA-DCA by flow cytometry using 2′,7′-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate fluorescence (DCF-DA). The results showed that AlbA-DCA induced a marked increase in intracellular ROS, compared to the control, AlbA, and Na-DCA; the rates of increase were 6.7-fold, 2.9-fold, and 1.7-fold, respectively (Figure 2B).

    Effects of AlbA-DCA on the Tumor Acidic Microenvironment

    According to the general knowledge that cancer cells avidly convert pyruvate to lactate, which is then transported out of cells and results in the accumulation of lactic acid and local acidification of TME,(4) and combined with the above experimental results that AlbA-DCA could alter energy metabolism by effectively inhibiting PDK activity, we tested the ability of AlbA-DCA to reduce lactate production. Intracellular lactate levels and the media lactate levels in MCF-7 cells were measured and compared to PDK-medium-expressed 4T1 cells. As shown in Figure 2C,D, treatment with AlbA-DCA at 2 μM for 6 h markedly decreased intracellular lactate levels and the media lactate levels of 4T1 cells. Upon treating the 4T1 cells with AlbA-DCA, both intracellular lactate levels and the media lactate levels were reduced, but the magnitude of decrease was less than that in the MCF-7 cells (Figure 2E,F), which further proved that AlbA-DCA can effectively reverse the glycolysis by inhibiting PDK. However, AlbA and DCA did not reduce both intra- and extracellular lactate in the MCF-7 and 4T1 cells. These observations suggested that AlbA-DCA was efficient in alleviating the accumulation of lactic acid in TME by inhibiting glycolysis.

    AlbA-DCA-Induced Apoptosis via a Caspase-Dependent Pathway

    Reprogramming of energy metabolism in tumor cells results in altered mitochondrial respiration and reduced lactic acid generation, both of which induce cancer cells resistance to mitochondrial apoptosis and ensure cancer progression.(26,27) As such, the ability of AlbA-DCA to induce apoptosis was analyzed by DAPI staining and annexin V-FITC/PI double staining (Figure 3A,B). Na-DCA and AlbA induced increases in the mean percentage of apoptotic cells, mainly in the early apoptotic cells (12.5 and 18.50). In addition, the ability of AlbA-DCA to induce apoptosis was the strongest, with the percentages of early apoptotic cells increasing from 3.53 to 25.16 and late apoptotic cells increasing from 2.97 to 22.92. Taken together, these results suggested that AlbA-DCA induces apoptotic cell death in MCF-7 cells.
    After obtaining the above results, to investigate of the mechanism of AlbA-DCA-induced MCF-7 cell apoptosis, we examined the change in expression of cytochrome c, Bcl-2, and caspase 9 protein. As shown in Figure 3C,D, AlbA-DCA could significantly up-regulate the expression of cytochrome c (4.4-fold) and down-regulate the expression of antiapoptotic protein Bcl-2 (0.5-fold). Furthermore, the expression of caspase-9, the essential initiator caspase via the mitochondrial pathway, was significantly enhanced in AlbA-DCA-treated groups (6.0-fold). Moreover, we also found that Z-VAD-fmk (a wide-spectrum inhibitor of caspases) partially reversed the death of MCF-7 cells (Figure 3E). These results indicated that AlbA-DCA- nduced apoptosis in MCF-7 cells via a caspase-dependent pathway.

    AlbA-DCA-Induced Ferroptosis

    On the basis of the effective AlbA-DCA mediation of energy metabolism reprogramming and ROS accumulation, combined with the result that ROS accumulation induced by mitochondrial dysfunction is involved in the induction of ferroptosis,(25,28,29) we hypothesized that AlbA-DCA can cause the inactivation of GPX4 for ferroptosis-mediated cancer cell death. To verify the occurrence of ferroptosis of AlbA-DCA-induced MCF-7 cells death, various well-known ferroptosis inhibitors and promoters were used. The cytotoxicity induced by AlbA-DCA was significantly alleviated by ferrostatin-1 (Fer-1, an inhibitor of ferroptosis, Figure 4A). Deferoxamine (DFO), an iron chelating agent, also significantly reduced the toxicity induced by AlbA-DCA (Figure 4B). In contrast, the addition of exogenous iron, Fe(II), slightly exacerbated AlbA-DCA-induced mortality (Figure 4C), whereas vitamin E (VE, an antioxidant) obviously relieved the toxicity of AlbA-DCA (Figure 4D). These data revealed that iron-dependent ferroptosis was induced by AlbA-DCA.
    Glutathione peroxidase IV (GPX4) plays a key role in preventing lipid peroxidation, inactivated by glutathione (GSH) depletion, and therefore induces the ferroptosis.(30−32) Accordingly, we further measured GSH depletion in MCF-7 cells. As shown in Figure 4E, the GSH level in MCF-7 cells treated with AlbA-DCA was significantly alleviated, possibly due to the oxidation of GSH into glutathione disulfide (GSSG). We also examined the effect of AlbA-DCA on the expression of GPX4. As shown in Figure 4F,G, the level of GPX4 expression was decreased substantially by AlbA-DCA. In addition, the inhibition of GPX4 activity led to the accumulation of lipid peroxidation in cells, which is considered to be a significant hallmark and indicator of ferroptosis.(33,34) Thus, the effect of AlbA-DCA on lipid peroxidation was evaluated by measuring MDA levels. A more than 2.5-fold increase in the MDA level was induced by AlbA-DCA (Figure 4H). Furthermore, the lipid peroxidation probe BODIPY-C11 was applied to monitor the lipid peroxide level. As shown in Figure 5I, MCF-7 cells in AlbA-DCA groups showed the strongest red fluorescence in confocal images compared to those of cells in the other group. Above all, these results provided evidence that AlbA-DCA suppressed the GPX4 pathway and induced the accumulation of lipid peroxidation, thereby leading to ferroptosis.

    In Vitro Cytotoxicy on Cancer Cells and Normal Human Cells

    The cytotoxicity of AlbA, Na-DCA, and AlbA-DCA against cancer and normal cell lines was tested by MTT assay, and IC50 values were summarized in Table 1. It was found that Na-DCA was nontoxic to all tested cells, whereas AlbA and AlbA-DCA displayed stronger cytotoxicity compared with positive control drug 5-Fu against all cancer cell lines. More importantly, AlbA-DCA possessed significantly improved cytotoxicity against PDK-medium-expressed cancer cells HCT116 (3.87 μM, 5.6-fold), A375 (3.78 μM, 1.5-fold), and 4T1 (1.31 μM, 12.5-fold) compared to AlbA. Moreover, the conjugate AlbA-DCA exhibited the best cytotoxicity against the PDK-high-expressed cancer MCF-7 cells with IC50 value 0.43 μM, which is enhanced 47.5-fold, and can be attributed to the more-efficient PDK inhibition capacity of AlbA-DCA. Interestingly, the IC50 values of AlbA and AlbA-DCA against two normal human cells were much higher than those against tumor cells. These results indicated that AlbA-DCA could selectively kill cancer cells, especially PDK-high-expressing cancer cells.

    Next, to investigate the synergistic effect between AlbA and Na-DCA, the combination index (CI) of AlbA-DCA was calculated. The CI index shows antagonism (CI > 1), additivity (CI = 1), or synergism (CI < 1) between the drugs.(35) AlbA and Na-DCA conjugated in AlbA-DCA were synergistic against all cancer cell lines, with CI values of 0.03, 0.22, 0.70, and 0.09 in the MCF-7, HCT116, A375, and 4T1 cell lines, respectively. Especially in MCF-7 cells, the CI value was much lower than that in other cancer cells, indicating the excellent synergistic effect of AlbA-DCA.

    In Vivo Antitumor Effects and Safety

    To test the antitumor efficacy of AlbA-DCA in vivo, nude mice bearing MCF-7 tumors were randomly divided into five groups and injected with negative control saline, positive control 5-Fu, AlbA-DCA, AlbA, and Na-DCA at the indicated dosages. As shown in Figure 5A,D, moderate therapeutic efficacy of Na-DCA was observed in the mice administrated. This result was consistent with the conclusion that DCA is less active in vivo and requires a large dose to effectively inhibit tumor growth. By comparison, after 14 days of treatment, AlbA could inhibit tumor growth with an observed efficacy that was similar to that of the positive control 5-Fu group. However, AlbA-DCA displayed the best antitumor efficacy and almost completely suppressed tumor progression. After the treatment, tumors were excised for photographing (Figure 5D) and weighing (Figure 5E). In addition, no mouse deaths and no significant changes in body weight were observed (Figure 5B).
    To evaluate the safety of AlbA-DCA in vivo application, we determined blood biochemical indexes and histopathology of the major organs. ALT, AST, and ALP evaluated the liver function, while BUN estimated renal function. As shown in Figure 6A, compared to the saline group, the four above blood biochemical indexes displayed insignificant changes, and this result suggested that AlbA-DCA had no obvious toxicity of liver and kidney. Meanwhile, we observed the histopathology of the heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney of mice treated with AlbA-DCA after being stained with H&E. As shown in Figure 6B, no major abnormality was observed in the stained organ slices compared to the saline ones, indicating the good security of AlbA-DCA.

    In Vivo Immune Response

    The breast tumor microenvironment comprises immune cells, blood vessel cells, fibrocytes, mesenchymal stem cells, adipocytes, and extracellular matrix, among which tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) are the prominent components.(36−38) Moreover, recent studies have shown that lactic acid, secreted by enhanced glycolysis as an immunosuppressive mediator, promotes tumor progression by the polarization of functional phenotype of TAMs into more tolerogenic phenotypes (from M1-like to M2-like).(6,39−42) Therefore, after obtaining the remarkable lactate reduction by AlbA-DCA, we speculated that AlbA-DCA can remold the tumor immunosuppression microenvironment via eliminating M2-TAMs, ultimately inhibiting tumor progression.
    To confirm this hypothesis, a dual-4T1 tumor model in female BALB/c mice was established. According to Figure 7A, only primary tumors were treated with intratumor-injected AlbA-DCA or saline, while distal primary tumors were allowed to grow naturally without any treatment. Both primary and distal tumors showed obvious rapid proliferation and growth after treatment with saline (Figure 7C). The primary tumors in the AlbA-DCA groups presented significant growth inhibition, with tumor volumes of <100 mm3 at the end of the experiment, which was consistent with the results of nude mice in the MCF-7 tumor experiment, indicating the excellent antitumor effect of AlbA-DCA. Interestingly, AlbA-DCA also revealed effective growth inhibition of distal 4T1 tumors. At the end of the experiment, the tumors in both groups were collected and weighed. The average weight of both primary and distal primary tumors significantly decreased, and the average weight in the AlbA-DCA treatment group was lower than that of the saline group (Figure 7B,D). The above findings may be explained by the enhanced immune response generated by the tumor microenvironment after AlbA-DCA treatment of primary tumors, which could then reverse the immunosuppressive environment of both primary and distal tumors and eventually inhibit tumor growth.
    We then examined the expression of Arg-1 and MRC-1, which are high-expression mannose receptors in M2-TAMs, using Western blot and QPCR. As shown in Figure 8A,B, the expression of Arg-1 and MRC-1 mRNA was inhibited 2.1-fold and 3.4-fold, respectively. Similarly, the expression of Arg-1 and MRC-1 protein was also inhibited 2.1-fold and 3.4-fold, respectively (Figure 8C). The depletion of prominent M2-TAMs would down-regulate the secretion of immunosuppressive cytokines (IL-10), which could be beneficial for the polarization of TAMs to form an activated M1 phenotype, and then secrete immunostimulatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-12, and IFN-γ).(43,44) To test this, we evaluated the level of cytokine in the serum and the distant tumor using an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). As shown in Figure 8D, AlbA-DCA exhibited extraordinarily low levels of IL-10 expression in tumors. On the contrary, the ELISA results showed that AlbA-DCA showed much higher levels of TNF-α, IL-12, and IFN-γ in serum and tumors compared to the saline group (Figure 8E–G). The results clarified that AlbA-DCA could remold the tumor immunosuppressive microenvironment via the polarization of TAMs from M2 to M1.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we developed a conjugate AlbA-DCA, a natural oleanane triterpenoid saponin AlbA connected to a PDK inhibitor, via a comparatively stable amide linkage. AlbA-DCA could affect the energy metabolism reprogramming of tumor cells and shift cellular metabolism away from aerobic glycolysis to OXPHOS by inhibiting PDK activity. In this way, AlbA-DCA induced a marked increase in intracellular ROS and alleviated the accumulation of lactic acid in TME. Meanwhile, AlbA-DCA selectively killed cancer cells and had an excellent synergistic effect, especially in PDK-high-expressing cancer cells. Importantly, AlbA-DCA-induced apoptosis via a caspase-dependent pathway. We also demonstrated that AlbA-DCA suppressed the GPX4 pathway and induced the accumulation of lipid peroxidation, thereby leading to ferroptosis. Furthermore, the antitumor experiment on nude mice bearing MCF-7 tumors demonstrated that AlbA-DCA would be an effective and safe agent. We also confirmed that AlbA-DCA could remold the tumor immunosuppression microenvironment via eliminating M2-TAMs to inhibit both primary and distal tumor progression in a dual-4T1 tumor model in female BALB/c mice. As a result, such an AlbA-DCA exhibited promising application as a synergetic natural saponin and PDK inhibitor to induce apoptosis-ferroptosis-M2-TAMs polarization for enhanced cancer therapy.
