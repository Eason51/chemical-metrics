    Abstract

    Contilisant, a permeable, antioxidant, and neuroprotectant agent, showing high nM affinity at H3R and excellent inhibition of the monoamine oxidases and cholinesterases, is an affine and selective S1R agonist in the nanomolar range, based on the binding affinity and functional experiment, a result confirmed by molecular modeling. In addition, contilisant significantly restores the cognitive deficit induced by Aβ1–42 in the radial maze assay in an in vivo Alzheimer’s disease test, comparing very favorably with donepezil.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Among diseases of aging, Alzheimer’s disease (AD) remains the most common cause of memory impairment and dementia in elderly people.(1) AD is characterized by highly interconnected pathological processes leading to the accumulation of abnormal extracellular deposits of β-amyloid (Aβ) peptide, intracellular neurofibrillary tangles, and neuronal death.(2) In the search for the mechanisms leading to neurodegeneration and to find new and more efficient therapies, factors such as mitochondrial dysfunction, neuroinflammation, and oxidative stress(3) have been identified as major determinants in the progress and development of the disease. Indeed, it has been shown that the accumulation of senile plaques increases reactive oxygen species (ROS) that result in damage to the nucleus, mitochondrial membranes, and cytoplasmic proteins of neurons. Consequently, the antioxidant strategy for the development of drugs for diseases of aging has been of paramount importance in recent years.(4) Moreover, the implication of ROS on chronic pain, including neuropathic an inflammatory pain, is widely documented.(5)
    Our multitarget small molecule (MTSM) ASS234(6) (Figure 1) is able to inhibit irreversibly the monoamine oxidases A and B (MAO A/B), preventing the catalytic oxidation of the biogenic amines, such as serotonin (5-HT), norepinephrine, and dopamine, implicated in the cognitive process, and whose deficit has been observed in the brain of AD patients,(7) as well as the subsequent formation of hydrogen peroxide, the starting point for the formation of toxic ROS via Fenton reaction in the presence of biometals.(8) ASS234(5,6) is endowed with reversible, mixed type, potent, and selective capacity for acetylcholinesterase (AChE) inhibition and is able to increase the acetylcholine (ACh) levels in brain to improve memory and cognition impairment, comparable to other typical marketed AChE inhibitors (AChEI) like donepezil, rivastigmine, and galantamine.(9)
    Since the histamine receptor subtype 3 (H3R) is involved in the central regulation of histamine levels by feedback inhibition of histamine release(10) and also modulates release of other neurotransmitters, such as ACh, serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine,(11) it is commonly recognized as a useful pharmacological tool in the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases. The blockade of H3R by inverse agonists/antagonists elevates neurotransmitters in the central nervous system (CNS). The first H3R inverse agonist/antagonist, pitolisant, is under investigation for diverse cognition impairments.(12) Thus, the usefulness of improved precognitive utilities of H3R antagonists/inverse agonists for AD symptoms is widely recognized, but clinical AD trials with H3R as a monotarget have so far failed.(13) Although compounds showing several dual H3R antagonistic/inverse agonistic and AChE inhibitory activity,(14−19) H3R antagonism with Aβ-aggregation inhibition, metal ion chelation, and radical scavenging,(20) or H3R antagonism with MAO inhibition capacity(21) have been recently reported, MTSM(22) is able to modulate H3R and showing MAO and ChE inhibition has never been described but would possibly constitute an original and appropriate therapeutic approach for new molecules targeted to AD. Therefore, hit-compound ASS234(5) was simply modified to fit a generally accepted pharmacophore of H3R antagonists (Figure 1), composed of a tertiary basic amine in the western part, an alkyl spacer, and an eastern arbitrary region. In multiple publications on non-imidazole H3R antagonists, cycloalkylamines like piperidine as basic center connected via a (propyloxy)phenyl chain to the western arbitrary region are suitable pharmacophores, e.g., in irdabisant or JNJ-5207852 (for review see ref (23)).(13) From this analysis we were able to identify contilisant(24) (Figure 1) as a neuroprotective, nontoxic, antioxidant, permeable ligand, showing satisfactory in vitro pharmacological properties on the selected biological targets (hAChE, IC50 = 0.53 μM; hBuChE, IC50 = 1.69 μM; hMAO A, IC50 = 0.145 μM; hMAO B, IC50 = 0.078 μM; hH3R, Ki = 10.8 nM), and able to restore the cognitive impairment induced by LPS in an in vivo NRO test, as an appropriate AD animal model.
    Next and based on these results, we decided to explore new pharmacological properties of contilisant to potentiate its therapeutic power for AD therapy. A simple search confirmed that the (piperidinopropyloxy)phenyl motif, present in agents such as I (Figure 1), confers antagonist properties on the Sigma 1 receptor (S1R).(25) On the other hand, N-benzylpiperidines (NBnPip) show agonist or antagonism S1R profile depending on the scaffold where the motif has been incorporated. This is the case of NBnPip II (Figure 1), showing S1R antagonism with low nanomolar S1R affinity and high S1R/S2R selectivity,(26) or NBnPip III (Figure 1), which shows excellent S1R selectivity and agonism activity.(27) Our hypothesis is that the (piperidinopropyloxy)phenyl motif present in contilisant could behave also as a very flexible pharmacophore able to modulate the affinity by S1/2R. In addition, we were curious about the binding of a NBnPip, such as ASS234 (Figure 1), in these biological targets.
    The S1R appears to be a unique class of protein influencing and participating in a wide range of biological events, including Ca2+ signaling at the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), controlling K+, Ca2+, Cl–, and Na+ families of ion channels at plasma membrane and membranes, thus maintaining ER-mitochondria exchanges and modulating transcription factors.(28) The impact of S1R in learning and memory processes is well-known, highlighting the power of S1R agonists as antiamnesic drugs in a variety of pharmacological or pathological models, possibly due to the fact that S1R activity increases glutamatergic synapses, cholinergic synapses, and the effects of trophic factors, which play a key role in memory.(29) A number of ligands have been described in the past decade showing diverse S1/2R affinity and antagonism/agonism profile,(30−32) among them, not surprisingly, donepezil,(33) but without clear and definitive structure–activity relationship trends. Also, the S1R is present in structures of CNS involved in the process of central sensitization implicated in the neuropathic pain.(34) Therefore, research aimed at studying the effects of S1R in vivo models of pain that involve central sensitization seems likely to yield findings of relevance for treatment of this type of pain. Capsaicin evoked an increase in the sensitivity of pain resulting from central sensitization.(35) Interestingly, S1R antagonists decrease the capsaicin-induced mechanical allodynia while S1R agonists have facilitatory effects on this type of pain. Therefore, the changes in capsaicin-induced mechanical allodynia produced by S1R ligands can be used to discriminate their agonist or antagonist activity.(36) Of note is the discovery of S1RA, an affine and selective S1R antagonist, in advanced clinical studies for the neuropathic pain.(37)
    In this article we report the confirmation that hit-compound contilisant is a permeable antioxidant and highly neuroprotective against a number of relevant toxic insults for AD and is a new tetratarget indole that, in addition to inhibitory properties for neurotransmitter-catabolizing enzymes (ChE, MAO) alongside with H3R, is a new, affine, and selective S1R agonist that significantly restores the cognitive deficit induced by Aβ1–42 in the radial arm-maze assay in an in vivo AD test, comparing very favorably with donepezil.

    Binding Affinities of Contilisant and ASS234

    First, we have addressed the binding affinity of contilisant,(24) and ASS234 for the S1/2R. The affinities of the test compounds toward human S1R (hS1R), rat S2R (rS2R), and rat vesicular acetylcholine transporter (rVAChT) were determined by radioligand displacement experiments performed under equilibrium conditions according to previously published protocols (see Supporting Information).(38) As shown in Table 1, we determined for contilisant and ASS234 nanomolar affinity values toward hS1R with ASS234 possessing a 23-fold higher affinity compared to contilisant. In addition, contilisant and ASS234 showed a clear hSR1/rS2R selectivity (5- and 18-fold, respectively). Although membrane preparations of hS1R- and rS2R-containing material were used, we assess this selectivity as species-independent. On the one hand, we evaluated comparable affinity values for haloperidol toward hS1R and rS2R in our laboratory (hS1R-HEK cells, Ki = 5.90 nM; r cortex, Ki = 5.39 nM). In addition, the high affinity of both test compounds toward rVAChT with Ki values almost identical to the values obtained for hS1R corresponds to frequently observed selectivity issues of ligands designed to specifically bind toward VAChT or S1R.(39) VAChT is a transmembrane protein expressed in cholinergic neurons in CNS and located on presynaptic vesicles of cholinergic neurons. VAChT transports the neurotransmitter ACh into the vesicles for storage and release, thus being is a key feature in the cholinergic neurotransmission(40) and an important target for in vivo imaging of neurodegenerative diseaes, such AD,(41) using positron emission tomography (PET). On the other hand, vesamicol, a well-known VAChT PET radioligand, produces a noncompetitive and reversible block of VAChT responsible for carrying newly synthesized ACh into secretory vesicles, decreasing ACh release.(39)

    Functional Profile of ASS234 and Contilisant on S1R

    Next, we evaluated the effects of subcutaneous (sc) administration of contilisant on mechanical sensitivity in animals treated with a low dose of capsaicin (0.125 μg), which does not induce sensitization to the mechanical stimulus (Figure 2A, dose 0) (see Supporting Information). The sc administration of the ligand S1R agonist contilisant (3–30 mg/kg) induced a decrease in a dose-dependent manner in the paw withdrawal latency in animals subjected to an innocuous (0.5 g force) punctate mechanical stimulus on the capsaicin-injected paw (Figure 2A). On the other hand, the mechanical allodynia induced by the intraplantar (i.pl.) injection of 1 μg of capsaicin (see Figure 2B, dose 0) was dose-dependently reverted when ASS234 (3–30 mg/kg) was sc administered, showing an increasing in the paw withdrawal latency time (Figure 2B). We found that the sc administration of ASS234 produced an inhibition of the capsaicin-induced mechanical allodynia in a dose-dependent manner. It is known that the i.pl. injection of capsaicin induces a sensitization to an innocuous mechanical punctate stimulus (mechanical allodynia), which is expressed as a decrease in the latency time for paw withdrawal.(42) The effects of ASS234 are consistent with previous findings where both S1R antagonists drugs, as mice lacking S1R, decreased the capsaicin-induced mechanical allodynia under the same experimental conditions.(36) On the other hand, the S1R agonists are unable to induce mechanical allodynia by themselves. However, in mice systemic treated with S1R agonists increased mechanical sensitivity after the nociceptive system is primed with a low dose of capsaicin, showing a significant decreasing of the withdrawal latency time on injected hindpaw.(43) Interestingly, we observed that subcutaneously administration of contilisant potentiated the mechanical allodynia induced by a low dose of capsaicin of 0.125 μg.

    Molecular Modeling

    Docking analysis studies clearly support the in vitro data findings. A docking study was performed to identify the molecular interactions in the S1R-ligand recognition and to propose a molecular rationale for the experimental affinity results of ligands ASS234 and contilisant. We have used a validated three-dimensional model of the S1R based on the crystal structure of the hS1R model bound to the antagonist PD144418. In our model, the receptor flexibility was introduced allowing each ligand to adapt to the catalytic site by the rearrangement of the side chains of residues Tyr103, Glu172, Phe107, Asp126, Val152, Phe146, Gln135, His154, Glu158, Ser117, Tyr120, and Tyr206. The binding pocket consists primarily of hydrophobic residues Val84, Trp89, Met93, Leu95, Leu105, Leu182, Phe107, Ile124, Trp164, and Tyr103 with the exception of two acidic residues, Gu172 and Asp126.
    ASS234 fully occupies the active site and establishes a number of interactions with S1TR. As shown in Figure S1A (Supporting Information), the protonated piperidine group engages a salt bridge interaction with the Glu172 and a π–cation interaction with Tyr103. The piperidine ring and the N-benzyl ring are perfectly encased in the hydrophobic cavity lined by residues Leu95, Ala98, Leu182, Ile178, while the phenyl ring shows a π–π interaction with Tyr206. On the other hand, the indole moiety establishes π–-anion interactions with Asp126 and Glu172, as well as a π–π and π–alkyl interactions with Phe107 and Ile124, respectively. Additionally, the ammonium group at the indole C2 side chain and the receptor established two key interactions, such as the attractive charge interactions with His154 and Tyr120 and a hydrogen bond interaction with Ser117, whereas the N-propargyl moiety interacts with Ala122, Gly 155, Gly118, and Tyr120 Figure S1A (Supporting Information). Contilisant has the same orientation at the active site as ASS234 but showing significant differences in the binding mode Figure S1C (Supporting Information). Now, the protonated piperidine ring does not establish any interaction with the receptor, being that the indole ring establishes a π–anion interaction with Glu172 and π–π interactions with Phe107 and Trp89. In addition, the ammonium group at the indole C2 side chain is engaged in prototypical salt bridge interactions with the carboxylic side chain of Asp126 and Glu172, as well as π–cation interaction with His154 Figure S1C (Supporting Information). Very interestingly, there is no case, for both ligands, that the oxygen at C5 is not involved in any hydrogen bond acceptor interaction. The diverse differences in type and strength in the binding at S1R shown for ASS234 and contilisant clearly justify that ASS234 is 23-fold more affine than contilisant.
    As shown in Figure S1B (Supporting Information), the observed binding of ASS234 at the active site of S1R can be accommodated in Glennon’s pharmacophore model,(44) as the basic piperidine nitrogen atom matches the positive ionizable group, located between two hydrophobic sites, the N-benzyl and the alkyl substituents of the indole ring at distances of 3.77 and 9.20 Å, respectively. Conversely, for contilisant (Figure S1D (Supporting Information)), the ammonium group of the propargylamine moiety would be the positive ionizable group, located between two hydrophobic sites, the propargyl and the piperidine functional groups, at distances of 3.58 and 9.56 Å, respectively.

    In Vivo Analysis of Contilisant, ASS234, and Donepezil in the Spatial Memory of Mice Subjected to Animal Model of Dementia Induced by Aβ1–42 Oligomers

    We evaluated the effect of contilisant (1 and 10 mg/kg), ASS234 (1 and 10 mg/kg), and donepezil (1 mg/kg) on the Y-maze task. Contilisant and ASS234 like donepezil protected against memory damage induced by Aβ1–42 oligomers (Figure 3). Moreover, contilisant (1 and 10 mg/kg), per se, did not induce memory damage when we compared with control group (ACSF + water). In addition, we evaluated the effect of contilisant (1 and 10 mg/kg), ASS234 (1 and 10 mg/kg), and donepezil (1 mg/kg) on the radial arm-maze task. The animals treated with Aβ1–42 oligomer presented spatial memory impairment when compared to the control group (ACSF + water), when observed latency to find food in the second, third, and fourth days of the test (Figure 4A). Contilisant (10 mg/kg) can reverse this effect in the second (p < 0.01), third (p < 0.01), and fourth (p < 0.01) day (Figure 4A). On the other hand, ASS234 (1 and 10 mg/kg) can reverse this effect just in the fourth (p < 0.01) day (Figure 4C), whereas donepezil was able to reverse this effect in the third (p < 0.05) and fourth (p < 0.01) day (Figure 4A and Figure 4C). Figure 4B and Figure 4C show the total errors to find food. We observed that the administrations of Aβ1–42 oligomer induced more errors than the control group during all days of the test. Contilisant (10 mg/kg) can reverse this effect in the second (p < 0.01), third (p < 0.01), and fourth (p < 0.01) day (Figure 4B). ASS234 (1 and 10 mg/kg) was able to reverse this effect in the third (p < 0.01) and fourth (p < 0.01) day (Figure 4D), but donepezil, the positive control, can reverse this effect in the third (p < 0.05) and fourth (p < 0.01) day (Figure 4B and Figure 4D). Consequently, contilisant (10 mg/kg) showed major protective effect compared with ASS234 and donepezil because contilisant (10 mg/kg) induced protective effect in all days on the radial arm-maze task.
    AD is characterized by an impairment of memory and cognitive functions.(45) In the early stages of AD, patients present visuospatial disorientation and visuospatial memory impairment.(46) In the same way, animal models of dementia, like AD, induce spatial memory impairment. Therefore, these animal models of dementia are widely used to screen for potential drugs for AD.(47) In this case, we observed that contilisant and ASS234 reversed the cognitive impairment induced by Aβ1–42 oligomers in mice. Contilisant (10 mg/kg) protective effect was better than ASS234 (1 and 10 mg/kg) and donepezil. These results indicate that contilisant maybe a potential drug against dementia like AD.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In the search for new agents for AD therapy, we and others have strongly bet on multipotent molecules, as “dirty” as possible, looking for nonselectivity but able to bind or inhibit selected biochemical targets involved in the progress and development of the disease.(22) Thus, based on small, permeable, neuroprotective, and antioxidant molecules, the key point is which and how many biochemical targets are necessary to identify hit-efficient compounds for AD therapy. Thus, going a step forward to what we have previously described,(24) contilisant is permeable, antioxidant, and highly neuroprotective against a number of relevant toxic insults for AD and is a new tetratarget small molecule that, in addition to inhibitory properties for neurotransmitter-catabolizing enzymes (ChE, MAO), alongside with H3R, is a new and affine S1R agonist, in the nM range, and selective regarding rS2R (5.0), although 3.8-fold less affine than some of the reference compounds used for comparative purposes (Table 1), such as S1RA(32,27) (3.8-fold). Very interestingly, ligand ASS234 is 23- and 6-fold more affine than contilisant and S1RA, respectively (Table 1), showing antagonist profile. Thus, it is clear that on going from ASS234 to contilisant, the relocation of the nitrogen at the piperidine ring and the elimination of the N-Bn functionalization result in a remarkable effect from the point of view of their power and selectivity for the modulation of S1/2Rs and their functional profile. These results have been validated by the molecular modeling analysis. Finally, the in vivo studies showed that contilisant had the better protective effect than ASS234 and donepezil on the Y-maze and radial arm-maze task against cognitive impairment induced by Aβ1–42 oligomers, confirming previous results(24) and suggesting that contilisant is a new MTSM(22) of interest for the potential treatment of AD.
