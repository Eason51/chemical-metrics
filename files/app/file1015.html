    Abstract

    A novel diazaspiro[3.4]octane series was identified from a Plasmodium falciparum whole-cell high-throughput screening campaign. Hits displayed activity against multiple stages of the parasite lifecycle, which together with a novel sp3-rich scaffold provided an attractive starting point for a hit-to-lead medicinal chemistry optimization and biological profiling program. Structure–activity-relationship studies led to the identification of compounds that showed low nanomolar asexual blood-stage activity (<50 nM) together with strong gametocyte sterilizing properties that translated to transmission-blocking activity in the standard membrane feeding assay. Mechanistic studies through resistance selection with one of the analogues followed by whole-genome sequencing implicated the P. falciparum cyclic amine resistance locus in the mode of resistance.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In 2015, when the World Health Organization (WHO) defined the malaria control and elimination goals for 2030 based on trends from the previous decade, a significant decrease in malaria incidence and mortality rates was anticipated along with the elimination of the disease in several countries.(1) However, the previously observed decreases in malaria incidence have worryingly started to stall since 2016, partly due to the emergence of resistance mechanisms at both the parasite and mosquito levels.(2−4) In this context, it is critical to rapidly identify and develop new molecules that are structurally and mechanistically distinct from the current frontline therapies to treat malaria and avoid the spread of resistant parasites. To that end, these new-generation antimalarials ideally need to meet more than one target compound profiles (TCPs) as defined by Medicines for Malaria Venture (MMV)(5) to kill the malaria parasite at different stages of its lifecycle and thus potentially relieve symptoms, protect, avoid relapse, and/or block transmission. To date, pan-activity, whereby the same compound targets all development stages of the malaria parasite, has been difficult to achieve as different biochemical processes are involved during the parasite lifecycle, resulting in significant differences in drug susceptibility between the major parasite stages.
    To identify new chemical entities with potential for further hit-to-lead and lead optimization programs, a Charles River (formerly BioFocus) proprietary library of about 12 700 compounds was screened in parallel against different parasite lifecycle stages (e.g., asexual blood stage (ABS), liver stage, and sexual stages). Hit compounds were further evaluated in a range of assays to determine their physicochemical properties and amenability to successful development.
    Herein, we report the identification and profiling of promising hits from a novel diazaspiro[3.4]octane chemical series, PPI28 (originally designed to target protein–protein interactions (PPI)) (Figure 1), along with the hit-to-lead efforts and mechanistic studies that followed.

    Methods

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    High-Throughput Screening (HTS)

    The 12 700 compounds of the Charles River BioFocus proprietary library were screened in duplicate against ABS PfDd2 parasites, exoerythrocytic liver-stage parasites, and in the dual gamete formation assay (DGFA). IC50 values were determined when compounds showed more than 70% inhibition in both assay duplicates. All assays were performed with Plasmodium falciparum parasites, except for the liver-stage assay, for which the rodent species Plasmodium berghei was used, based on the experimental evidence that activity against the liver stage of the murine species of the parasite generally correlates with that against the human species.(6)
    Out of the original library, 991 preliminary hit compounds were identified, showing more than 70% inhibition of parasite growth in both duplicates of the ABS assay. These hits were further evaluated for off-target activity (e.g., cytotoxicity in HepG2 cells and cardiotoxicity in the human ether-a-go-go related gene (hERG) fluorescence polarization assay). Compounds were also tested in several absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) assays to measure physicochemical properties (e.g., lipophilicity and solubility at physiologically relevant pH 7.4) and to assess metabolic liabilities (phase I and phase II metabolism using metabolic turnover assays in human liver microsomes and rat hepatocytes, and potential for CYP450 inhibition).
    All HTS assay conditions are detailed in Supporting Information File 1, Section A.

    HTS Data Analysis

    The HTS-associated data set was analyzed using StarDrop v.6.4. to v.6.6. (https://www.optibrium.com/stardrop/). Filtering, clustering, and scoring methods were applied to the data set to classify and rank compounds based on their biological, ADME, and structural properties. Subsequent in-depth analysis taking into account the desired profile for a hit compound as defined by MMV (https://www.mmv.org/research-development/information-scientists) as well as other criteria including freedom to operate and synthetic feasibility led to the selection of chemical series for further investigation.

    Cross-Resistance Assays on Hit Compounds

    Cross-resistance assays on hit compounds were carried out on several resistant field isolates and P. falciparum Dd2 lab-generated mutants using the modified [3H]-hypoxanthine incorporation assay(7) (Supporting Information File 1, Section E).

    Resistance Selection Experiments on 1-A

    Resistance selection experiments were performed with compound 1-A, the active enantiomer of 1, in which ABS parasites at 2.5% parasitemia were pressured with five times the ABS Dd2-B2(8) IC50 value. The cultures were monitored daily to confirm clearance of sensitive parasites. After 3 weeks of culture under drug pressure, resistant parasites were cloned, expanded, and profiled for resistance phenotypes via dose-response assays. Three clones showing varying 1-A IC50 values (17–146× Dd2-B2 IC50) were selected for whole-genome sequencing (WGS) (Supporting Information File 1, Section F).

    Chemistry

    Compounds were prepared according to three different synthetic routes A, B, and C (Schemes 1, 2, and 3, respectively), emphasizing variations of R1, R2, and R3 substituents (Figure 1), respectively. From the common intermediate 4, the synthetic paths diverged depending on which side of the molecule was to be modified. All of the building blocks that were not commercially available, mainly for R1 substituents, were prepared in-house, and their synthesis is detailed in Supporting Information File 2.
    The tert-butyl 3-(2-ethoxy-2-oxoethylidene) azetidine-1-carboxylate intermediate 3 was prepared from a Wittig reaction with commercially available tert-butyl 3-oxoazetidine-1-carboxylate and ethyl 2-(triphenylphosphanylidene)acetate as reported in the literature (Scheme 1).(9) A [3 + 2] cycloaddition with 3 and N-(methoxymethyl)-N-(trimethylsilylmethyl) benzylamine in the presence of lithium fluoride afforded intermediate 4.(10)
    Compounds 11–27, 84–106, and S1–S19 were prepared according to route A (Scheme 1), whereby intermediate 4 was subjected to an N-Boc-deprotection followed by a Buchwald–Hartwig coupling to give intermediates 5a–c. Reduction of the ethyl ester functionality with lithium aluminum hydride led to the corresponding alcohol intermediates 6a–c. Subsequent mesylation and nucleophilic substitution with various N-Boc-anilines afforded the corresponding aniline intermediates 8a–c. After debenzylation followed by HOPO/EDCI-mediated amide coupling with the desired carboxylic acid, intermediates 10a–c were obtained, which were subjected to acidic N-Boc-deprotection to yield the final compounds 11–27, 78–100, and S1–S19.
    The phenoxy derivative 29 was obtained by reacting the mesylate intermediate 7a with phenol and K2CO3 as a base to give intermediate 28, followed by debenzylation and amide coupling as described above.
    Compounds 35–60 were prepared according to route B (Scheme 2). Intermediate 5a obtained from a Buchwald–Hartwig coupling as described in route A was debenzylated in the presence of palladium hydroxide to afford the free amine 30, which was then coupled to tert-butyldimethylsilyl (TBDMS)-protected hydroxyphenylacetic acid using EDCI and HOPO to give the amide intermediate 31. Subsequent reduction with sodium borohydride gave the alcohol intermediate 32, which was oxidized to the key aldehyde intermediate 33 using Dess–Martin periodinane, allowing access to compounds with a variety of R2 substituents. All following intermediates were deprotected in an ultimate step to yield the final compounds. The aldoxime derivative 35 was obtained by reacting the aldehyde 33 with hydroxylamine followed by removal of the silyl protecting group. The carboxylic acid 36 was obtained from basic hydrolysis of intermediate 31. From the aldehyde intermediate 33, Grignard additions followed by deprotections afforded hydroxylated analogues 38 and 39, while reductive aminations led to the formation of additional amine derivatives 40–53, in particular aliphatic targets that could not be accessed through the mesylate intermediate. The nitrile 55 was obtained from dehydration of the aldoxime intermediate 34 using phosphonyltrichloride and DABCO.(11) The Mitsunobu reaction was performed to convert alcohol 32 to the azide intermediate 54, from which the triazole 56 and the amine 58 were prepared via copper-catalyzed azide–alkyne cycloaddition and Staudinger reduction, respectively. Amine 58 was further subjected to amide couplings with a couple of carboxylic acids to give analogues 59 and 60.
    Finally compounds 68–81 were prepared following route C (Scheme 3), in which intermediate 4 was debenzylated in the presence of palladium hydroxide. The resulting intermediate 61 was coupled to TBDMS-protected hydroxyphenylacetic acid using the same EDCI/HOPO coupling conditions as described in routes A and B. N-Boc-deprotection, followed by Cbz protection of the azetidine, gave intermediate 63. Subsequent reduction of the R2 ethyl ester followed by Dess–Martin oxidation resulted in intermediate 65. The latter was subjected to a reductive amination followed by Boc protection to give the N-Boc-aniline intermediate 66, which was, in turn, hydrogenated to yield the key azetidine intermediate 67. Various coupling methods were used to N-arylate or N-alkylate the azetidine, which, after simultaneous deprotection of the TBDMS and N-Boc protecting groups under acidic conditions, led to the final compounds 68–83.
    The synthesis and analytical data of all compounds are detailed in Supporting Information File 2.

    Asexual Blood-Stage Activity

    Selected hits and all compounds synthesized during the hit-to-lead campaign were evaluated for in vitro ABS activity against the PfNF54 drug-sensitive strain of P. falciparum. Selected compounds with PfNF54 IC50 < 500 nM were tested against the K1 multidrug-resistant strain of P. falciparum to assess the potential for cross-resistance. A compound was considered to have potential for cross-resistance when (PfK1 IC50)/(PfNF54 IC50) ≥ 4. No cross-resistance was observed with PPI28 compounds. Chloroquine and artesunate were used as reference drugs in all experiments.

    Assessment of Transmission-Blocking Potential

    Compounds with ABS PfNF54 IC50 < 500 nM were assessed for transmission-blocking potential using a panel of assays, including stage IV/V gametocyte assays, a male gamete exflagellation assay, and the standard membrane feeding assay (SMFA).

    Gametocytocidal Activity(12)

    Compounds were assessed for gametocytocidal activity against P. falciparumPfNF54 stage IV/V gametocytes on three different assay platforms, namely, PrestoBlue fluorescence assay, ATP bioluminescence assay, and a luciferase reporter assay. Compounds are considered active against gametocytes if they show an IC50 < 1 μM on at least two assay platforms.

    Exflagellation Inhibition Assay (EIA)

    Compounds with ABS PfNF54 IC50 < 500 nM were also tested in the EIA on >95% stage V gametocytes as described in Supporting Information File 1, Section H.(13)

    SMFA

    Compounds with IC50 < 500 nM in the EIA were assessed in the SMFA to confirm transmission-blocking potential, using the two different species Anopheles gambiae s.s. and Anopheles arabiensis. Transmission-blocking potential was quantified by two different parameters: transmission-blocking activity ( , where p is prevalence of infected mosquitoes, C is control, and T is treated) and transmission-reducing activity (%TRA: , where i is oocyst intensity in the mosquito midgut, C is control, and T is treated).(14) Data were managed in Microsoft Excel and analyzed using GraphPad Prism version 9.0.0. All data were evaluated for normality using the Shapiro–Wilk test. Data were not normally distributed, and the Mann–Whitney U test was performed to determine statistical significance between treatment and control cohorts. Unpaired t-tests were used to compare the TBA and TRA between the two species. A P-value <0.05 was considered statistically significant.

    ADME Profiling

    To assess metabolic stability, selected compounds were incubated with human (h), rat (r), and mouse (m) liver microsomal preparations. The intrinsic clearance CLint and subsequently the hepatic extraction ratio EH were derived from the percentage of compound remaining after 30 min.
    Selected compounds were also assessed for hepatocyte stability. The apparent intrinsic clearance CLint,app and subsequently EH were calculated from the hepatocyte CLint derived from the t1/2.
    Aqueous solubility was measured at pH 6.5 for compounds displaying PfNF54 IC50 < 500 nM.

    hERG Inhibition and Cytotoxicity

    Selected compounds were assessed for potential cardiotoxicity risks by measuring their activity against the hERG potassium channel using in vitro IonWorks patch-clamp electrophysiology.(15)
    Compounds with PfNF54 IC50 < 500 nM were also tested for cytotoxicity against Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells.

    Mutant Pfcarl Profiling and Drug Combination Studies

    Selected compounds were evaluated in a matrix study to better understand possible mechanisms of action (MoA) and/or resistance (MoR) of the diazaspiro[3.4]octane chemical series and potential interactions with other antimalarial compounds. The matrix assay considers compound–compound interactions against a panel of sensitive and drug-resistant parasite strains. In this case, a set of parasite lines harboring various cyclic amine resistance locus (Pfcarl) mutations was included to observe any potential cross-resistance with other known drugs that select for Pfcarl-associated drug-resistance mutations. The methodology of the assay is described in Supporting Information File 1, Section N.
    All studies were performed on racemic mixtures unless mentioned otherwise.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS

    High-Throughput Screening Data Analysis and Chemical Series Prioritization

    The HTS data set was analyzed using filtering and structure-similarity-based clustering methods to identify chemical series of interest for drug discovery programs (Figure 2). Among the series that were prioritized, the diazaspiro[3.4]octane, PPI28, was particularly attractive due to the biological profile of its two most promising hits 1 and 2 and an average lipophilic ligand efficiency (LLE), defined as pIC50 – log P,(16) around 3.5. Hit compounds displayed good activity against ABS parasites (PfDd2 IC50 = 143 and 164 nM for 1 and 2, respectively) along with activity against liver stages and male gamete formation (96 and 99% inhibition at 2 μM, respectively). Evaluation of killing kinetics of 2 in a speed assay(17) indicated a fast-killing profile for the series, which is a desirable feature for molecules that clear ABS parasitemia (TCP-1).(5) Moreover, the diazaspiro[3.4]octane scaffold (Figure 1) contained a high number of sp3 carbons and, as such, notably differed from most antimalarials in clinical trials or use.(18,19) Structures with higher sp3 character are usually considered a beneficial attribute in drug discovery as they often have physicochemical properties (e.g., solubility, lipophilicity) associated with improved drug metabolism and pharmacokinetics (DMPK) and/or toxicological profiles, thus allowing medicinal chemists to navigate a more desirable chemical space.(20) PPI28 hits certainly belonged to a favorable space in terms of physicochemical properties as shown by the log D values in the [1–4] range and the topological polar surface area (TPSA) <90 Å2, permitting modulation of lipophilicity and polarity during the optimization process while remaining in a druggable space. In addition, within the malaria research context, three-dimensional structures offer new opportunities for identifying distinct mechanisms of action from the current antimalarials, many of which possess rather flat core structures.(18) Finally, in terms of the potential for analog optimization, the three attachment points of the PPI28 structure allowed us to envisage a wide range of modifications for structure–activity relationship (SAR) exploration and optimization of properties identified as weaknesses within the series (i.e., metabolic stability and solubility).

    More in-depth parasite lifecycle activity characterization highlighted the transmission-blocking potential (TCP-5)(5) of PPI28 compounds. While potency against late gametocytes (stage IV/V) was weak, their ability to inhibit exflagellation was significant (98–100% inhibition at 2 μM, IC50 ≤ 500 nM) (Supporting Information File 1, Table S11).     The potential for cross-resistance with other drug-resistant parasite lines was also investigated. The active enantiomers were shown to be equipotent against all tested field strains; however, cross-resistance was observed against the laboratory-selected KAF156 Dd2 mutant bearing a single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) in Pfcarl and giving rise to the I1139K mutation (Supporting Information File 1, Tables S6 and S7). Although the imidazolopiperazine KAF156 was in clinical trials at the time of our work,(21) the PPI28 series remained of interest for further exploration due to its attractive biological profile and the opportunities to be developed either as a potential back-up to KAF156 if the MoA appeared to be the same or as a new asset if the MoA was determined to be different. The fact that the structures of PPI28 compounds and KAF156 differed significantly also weighed in favor of investigating the PPI28 series further.
    Resistance selection experiments with 1-A confirmed the MoR to be associated with P. falciparum cyclic amine resistance locus (PfCARL protein; Pf3D7_0321900). Parasites cultured under drug pressure at 5× the IC50 recrudesced within 2 weeks, with resistant clones showing IC50 shifts of 17× or 120–150× the IC50 of the parental strain. These clones were subjected to whole-genome sequencing, revealing either the mutation L1073Q (120–150-fold shift for two clones) or A1122E (17-fold shift) in PfCARL. No additional SNPs were observed. However, a number of distinct copy number variations (CNVs) were observed between the clones (Supporting Information File 1, Section F). Mutations in PfCARL typically represent an MoR rather than the MoA or drug target.(22,23) Transposon mutagenesis studies recently provided strong evidence that PfCARL is essential.(24) This protein resides in the parasite endoplasmic reticulum where it is suspected to be involved in protein folding, processing, and sorting.(25)
    The amide derivative 1-A displayed superior antiplasmodial activity along with superior margins over cytotoxicity and hERG inhibition relative to the other hit 2-A. As a result, SAR studies that followed were based on 1-A, and the carbonyl group at R1 was maintained in all of the compounds that were subsequently prepared to mitigate the cardiotoxicity risk.

    Initial Structure–Activity-Relationship (SAR) Studies

    SAR studies primarily focused on investigating diverse changes at each of the three attachment points R1, R2, and R3 to determine the minimal pharmacophore and explore the scope of tolerated functionalities to maintain or improve potency. The results of the initial SAR exploration are summarized in Figure 3.
    A variety of functional groups to replace the phenol hydroxy group (e.g., amine, or polar groups such as nitrile, sulfone, carboxylic acid, acetamide groups) or the whole phenol moiety (e.g., pyrazole, pyridine, hydroxypiperidine, indazole moieties) were investigated, along with modification of the position of hydroxy substitution (from para to meta) or modulations of the methylene linker (e.g., length, position of the carbonyl, urea). All changes led to a significant loss of potency (PfNF54 IC50 > 6 μM), highlighting the importance of the para-hydroxy phenol moiety for the interaction with the target (Supporting Information File 1, Table S1).
    In a similar approach, a range of functionalities was probed to explore the R2 position and evaluate the impact on potency and other properties such as solubility and metabolic stability. Alkylation of the aniline or replacing the aniline with a phenol was not tolerated for activity, showing the importance of the free NH for target binding. Polar substituents (e.g., oxime 35, carboxylic acid 36, primary and secondary alcohols 37–39, nitrile 55, triazole 56, amides 59–60) resulted in a significant loss in activity, as did the replacement of the benzene ring with pyridine heterocycles (52–53). Likewise, substituting the aniline by a linear or cyclic aliphatic group (e.g., methylcyclopropyl amine or cyclopropyl amide, difluorocyclohexyl amine, tetrahydropyranyl amine, piperazine) led to less potent compounds. Since these results suggested that the phenyl moiety was necessary to retain potency, three-dimensional phenyl replacements such as adamantane and bicyclo[1,1,1]-pentane, known for mimicking phenyl hydrophobic interactions and improving physicochemical properties,(26−29) were investigated as in 47 and 48 but afforded less potent compounds. Only minimal modifications where small electron-withdrawing groups (EWGs) such as fluorine were added on the aniline were tolerated, as exemplified by 49 (Supporting Information File 1, Table S2).
    The SAR scope at the R3 position also appeared to be very limited, with only minor changes being allowed (Supporting Information File 1, Table S3). Substitution of the phenyl by heterocycles (e.g., pyridine and pyrimidine analogues 74–77) or saturated rings (i.e., difluorocyclohexane 78), as well as introduction of a linker [e.g., methylene (79) or amide (81)] led to inactive compounds at the highest concentration tested (6 μM). Again, fluorinating any position of the aryl ring was tolerated, with the 3,5-difluorophenyl substituent giving the best activity (71: PfNF54 IC50 = 30 vs 140 nM for the racemic hit 1). This encouraged us to explore the 5- and 6-positions of the 3-fluorophenyl group to introduce water-solubilizing groups such as morpholine to enhance aqueous solubility. However, these changes were not tolerated, as shown by analogues 82 and 83.
    Several analogues combining different R1, R2, and R3 phenyl and/or phenol fluoro substitutions were subsequently prepared to assess if multiple fluorinations could impact positively both antiplasmodial activity and metabolic stability.
    Numerous biotransformations were likely to affect the compounds of this series; notably, phase I oxidations at R1, R2, and R3 and/or phase II glucuronidation of the phenol at R1. Initial (and higher throughput) metabolic stability studies in liver microsomes focused on identifying phase I liabilities. The poor microsomal stability exhibited by compound 84, bearing a fluoro-substituted phenyl at R1, R2, and R3, compared to the piperidinol analog 85 (EH (h,r,m) = 0.86/0.75/0.85 vs <0.42/0.3/0.33) suggested that the phenol at the R1 position was likely a major site of metabolism and that fluorine groups at the R2 and R3 positions were able to block phase I metabolism on the two phenyl rings. This hypothesis was supported by metabolite identification (Met ID) experiments in microsomes with 1-A, 49, and 68, wherein oxidative metabolism was not seen at R2 and R3 when fluoro-substituted (Figure 4, and Supporting Information File 1, Section L).
    Based on these initial results, we fixed R2 and R3 as 4-fluorophenyl and 4-fluoro phenyl or 3,5-difluorophenyl groups, respectively, to maintain potency while optimizing metabolic stability in the following studies. As the phenol at R1 was determined to be essential for activity, we focused on exploring additional options to replace or stabilize the phenol moiety and on determining if a compound meeting the early lead criteria could be achieved for this series (https://www.mmv.org/research-development/information-scientists).

    Investigation of Phenol Surrogates to Address Metabolism

    Prior to establishing our strategy to mitigate metabolism at the R1 position, a mouse pharmacokinetics (PK) study was performed on the active enantiomer of the R3-fluorinated analogue 68, 68-A, to understand the major routes of phase I and phase II metabolism and address metabolic liabilities in future targets. The PK study was also a key experiment to determine if liver microsomes (phase I metabolism) were a predictive system for in vivo clearance or if another in vitro system such as hepatocytes (phase I and phase II) was preferable. The PK studies indicated that clearance was high, and subsequent Met ID studies on mouse blood and excreta determined that phenol glucuronide was the main metabolite. Products from oxidative metabolism were also identified, albeit in a lower proportion than what was foreseen from in vitro microsomal stability experiments (Supporting Information File 1, Section M). This highlighted that, in this case, phase II metabolism occurred faster than phase I oxidations and that hepatocytes would likely be more predictive than microsomes.
    In light of the Met ID results and the indication from the initial SAR studies that typical phenol bioisosteres did not maintain potency, we focused on several R1 alterations aimed at decreasing electron density and/or hindering the sites of phase I and phase II metabolism (Table 2). Active compounds (PfNF54 IC50 < 500 nM) were assessed for metabolic stability in liver microsomes first, and for those showing improved microsomal stability (EH < 0.5), hepatocyte stability was subsequently evaluated.

Although the N-oxide and pyrimidine analogues showed improved microsomal stability, they did not retain potency (PfNF54 IC50 = 5 μM, EH (h,r,m) = 0.69/0.5/0.75; PfNF54 IC50 = 0.86 μM, EH (h,r,m) = 0.61/0.35/0.53). Conversely, the hydroxy-pyridine 94 exhibited high potency but low microsomal metabolic stability (PfNF54 IC50 = 0.036 μM, EH (h,r,m) = 0.89/0.75/0.87). The remaining hydroxy-pyridines 92 and 93 and hydroxy-pyrimidine 96 did not lead to any notable improvement.
    To lower log D, substituents on the methylene linker such as a pendant amine were added, resulting in the amino acid 97, which displayed an attractive biological profile [PfNF54 IC50 = 16 nM; CHO CC50 = 18 μM (1125× selectivity); hERG IC50 = 1.6 μM (100× selectivity)] and improved ADME properties, i.e., aqueous solubility at pH 6.5 (40 μM) and metabolic stability, although the latter remained high (EH (h,r,m) = 0.81/0.45/0.61). Interestingly, when the amino acid stereocenter was inverted (compared to 97) as in 98, a significant improvement in microsomal stability (EH (h,r,m) < 0.42/0.3/0.33) was observed, albeit at the cost of a 4–5-fold loss in potency. Following up on methylene linker substitutions, several analogues were prepared that bore a phenol or CF3-substituted phenol and various substitution patterns on the CH2, including hydroxy, methyl, dimethyl, and difluoro groups, all of which were well-tolerated for activity (PfNF54 IC50100, 102, 103 <50 nM) except for the electron-withdrawing difluoro substitution as in 99. The latter confirmed previous observations that the phenol pKa must be above 8.5 to maintain activity. Remarkably, in all cases where a stereocenter was involved at R1, the (R) enantiomer was consistently more active than the (S) enantiomer (100/101: 16/105 nM; 102/103: 15/45 nM). In terms of metabolism however, the hydroxy, methyl, and dimethyl substitutions on the methylene bridge did not show any benefits (EH (h,r,m) > 0.8 in all species).
    When we combined the CF3-phenol with a disubstituted methylene linker (e.g., difluoro, dimethyl, gem-substitution with a methyl and a hydroxy group), activity dropped dramatically, as exemplified by the difluorinated analogue 104 (PfNF54 IC50 = 4.2 μM), and compounds remained highly susceptible to metabolism [compounds 104, S12, and S13 (Supporting Information File 1, Table S4), EH (h,r,m) > 0.8 in all species].
    Encouraged by the results with the amino derivatives 97 and 98, it was considered worthwhile to investigate other amino acids at R1 (e.g., tyrosine and phenylalanine). Remarkably, the tyrosine analogue 105 displayed a similar profile to that of 97, which opened new opportunities to explore other interactions with the presumed target, where an additional carbon in the linker was tolerated.
    Further analogues of 105 were prepared where the phenol hydroxy group was replaced by an amine, a methanol, a nitrile, or a trifluoromethyl group to probe the new space and assess whether it allowed for phenol modifications. The aniline derivative 106 stood out with an attractive in vitro profile (PfNF54 IC50 = 33 nM; EH (h,r,m) < 0.42/0.3/0.33; sol. pH 6.5 = 55 μM), while the other analogues were found to be considerably less active (compounds S17–S19, Supporting Information File 1, Table S4).

    Typically, PPI28 compounds did not exhibit gametocytocidal activity (<30% inhibition at 1 μM). However, several compounds showed strong inhibition in the EIA (80–100% inhibition at 2 μM). IC50 values were determined on a selection of compounds and confirmed the series potential for gametocyte sterilization and thereby male gamete targeting with activities ranging from 27 to 470 nM. The targeting of male gametes translated into transmission-reduction as evident in a decrease in oocyst formation in the mosquito gut (TRA) at >70% in A. gambiae s.s. and >58% in A. arabiensis for all compounds at 2 μM. Furthermore, >90% TRA was reported for the phenylalanine analogue S15 in A. gambiae s.s. There was no significant statistical difference observed in the TRA between the two species evaluated (Supporting Information File 3). The reduction in the number of mosquitoes that were infected (TBA) (regardless of how many oocysts were found in the gut) was generally lower, albeit still significant. Essentially, for compounds 87, 98, S3, and S15, 42–72% of the A. gambiae s.s. that fed on a 2 μM drug-treated gametocyte-infected blood meal did not become infected and, as a result, reduced the transmission chain. There was no significant statistical difference between the two species for TBA recorded for six of the compounds; however, this was not true for compounds 68 and S15. The differences observed for the latter might be due to the higher control prevalence/intensity, lower sample size in the A. gambiae s.s. experiments, or other unknown variables. It is worth noting that these results were obtained from female mosquitoes that had only one blood meal, whereas in a mass drug administration (MDA) program, the female mosquitoes would feed every 2–3 days, potentially increasing the blocking activity.

    Mutant Pfcarl Profiling and Drug Combination Studies

    As discussed earlier, drug selection experiments demonstrated that resistance to 1-A was associated with mutations in Pfcarl. To better understand the implications that this drug locus plays in modulating cross-resistance and drug–drug interactions,(22,23) we profiled a set of PPI28 analogues against these selected mutant parasites along with a panel of existing Pfcarl mutant parasites. As shown in Table 6, all diazaspiro[3.4]octane chemical series analogues tested, along with the antimalarial clinical candidate KAF156 and related imidazolopiperazine GNF179, demonstrated altered activities against tested Pfcarl mutant parasites (complete data set shown in Supporting Information File 1, Table S21), which suggests a specific chemical–protein interaction. For example, compound 1-A exhibited reduced activity against all mutant lines, ranging from a 3.5-fold to 95.5-fold shift, except against the E1104K mutant parasite (MMV692834R clone Fl2-A7) that showed increased susceptibility to this compound (15.5 ± 1.7 nM mean IC50 ± standard deviation (SD), compared to the Dd2 value of 78.7 ± 48.0 nM). Interestingly, the E1104K clone was also more susceptible to S3 and S14 relative to Dd2 wild-type parasites, whereas all other compounds were significantly less active against the Pfcarl mutant parasites. It is worth noting that imidazolopiperazine compounds KAF156 and GNF179 only showed significant shifts in IC50 relative to the parental Dd2 line when tested against the E1104K and I1139K clones (>10-fold shift in IC50 compared to <2.2-fold shift against all other Pfcarl mutants). These differences between mutant parasites’ drug susceptibility to the imidazolpiperazine and the diazaspiro[3.4]octane chemical series could potentially be exploited for further drug optimization that minimizes the effects of PfCARL-mediated drug resistance.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Following screening efforts of a Charles River proprietary library of 12 700 compounds against different lifecycle stages of the malaria parasite, a novel diazaspiro[3.4]octane chemical series, PPI28, was selected for a hit-to-lead program. In addition to the attractive biological profile of the hit compounds that showed high potency against ABS, liver stage, and male gametes, the sp3-rich scaffold opened up a wide range of possibilities for optimization at three positions. However, although potency was improved, metabolic liabilities could not be solved.
    Nevertheless, from a biological and mechanistic perspective, the PPI28 series remains very attractive. In addition to very potent activity against ABS parasites, the series shows potential for transmission blocking in the SMFA, by sterilizing male gametes. Mutant Pfcarl profiling experiments also highlighted differentiated responses between the imidazolopiperazine and the diazaspiro[3.4]octane chemical series that can be leveraged for further drug optimization and insight into the mechanism by which PfCARL mediates drug resistance.
    These PPI28 analogues, though not themselves amenable to further studies due to several liabilities, could serve as reference compounds in ligand-based virtual screening efforts to identify pan-active hits with a novel mechanism of action and initiate new malaria drug discovery programs.
