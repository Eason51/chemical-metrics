    Abstract

    Shiga toxin (Stx), a category B biothreat agent, is a ribosome inactivating protein and toxic to human and animals. Here, we designed and synthesized small molecules that block the active site of the Stx A subunit. On the basis of binding energy, 20 molecules were selected for synthesis and evaluation. These molecules were primarily screened using fluorescence-based thermal shift assay and in vitro in Vero cells. Among 32 molecules (including 12 reported), six molecules offered protection with IC50 of 2.60–23.90 μM. 4-Nitro-N-[2-(2-phenylsulfanylethylamino)ethyl]benzamide hydrochloride is the most potent inhibitor with IC50 at 7.96 μM and selectivity index of 22.23 and is better than any known small molecule inhibitor of Stx. Preincubation with Stx offered full protection against Shiga toxin in mice. Surface plasmon resonance assay further confirmed that these molecules bind specifically to Stx A subunit. Further optimization is continued to identify a potential candidate which will be in vivo effective.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Shiga toxin (Stx) and Shiga-like toxins (Stx-1 and Stx-2) are structurally and functionally related toxins that are produced by Shigella dysenteriae type I and few strains of Escherichia coli, respectively.(1, 2) These toxins are listed as category B biological warfare agents by Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and are responsible for life threatening bloody diarrhea, hemorrhagic colitis, and fatal hemorrhagic uremic syndrome (HUS). So far, no effective therapy is available for Stx intoxication. Antimicrobial treatment is associated with increased risk of toxicity due to release of additional toxins after bacterial cell lysis.(3) Hence, treatment mainly relies on supportive therapy.
    Stx belongs to the large family of ribosome inactivating proteins (RIPs) that irreversibly inactivate ribosome by removing specific adenine from the 28S rRNA resulting in inhibition of protein biosynthesis. Stx is constituted of noncovalent association of single A subunit with pentameric B subunit. A subunit is responsible for its N-glycosidase activity that blocks protein synthesis, while B subunit mediates retrograde trafficking of Stx which leads to productive infection.(4, 5)
    A number of approaches are being used to develop treatment against Stx toxicity. Small molecules are of particular interest because of ease of access of a variety of desired compounds and convenient handling and because they are reasonably stable and are suited for oral administration. Since retrograde transport is absolutely necessary for Stx toxicity, blocking of the toxin transport appears to be a promising therapeutic. Recently small molecule inhibitors Retro 1 and 2 are reported that block retrograde trafficking of Stx from early endosome to trans-Golgi network, thus protecting cells from the Stx cytotoxicity.(6) These compounds are also shown to provide protection in mice against lethal dose of Stx and ricin which is a phytotoxin, another member of RIP family.(6-8) Noel and co-workers reported further modifications of retro 2 compound by cyclization with improved protection efficacy against Stx toxicity.(9) Developing small molecules that selectively target enzymatic activity of toxin can be safe and alternative option for the treatment.(8)N-Glycosidase activity of A subunit cleaves a highly conserved adenine nucleotide within the sarcin–ricin loop of eukaryotic 28S rRNA and leads to ribosome arrest and inhibits protein synthesis.(10) Since N-glycosidase activity removes adenines from 28S rRNA, addition of adenine protects ribosomes from inactivation by RIPs. Several adenine derivatives and analogues have been shown to inhibit toxicity by in vitro translation assay.(11)
    In the present study we have identified and synthesized small molecules targeting N-glycosidase activity of Stx A subunit. In addition to these molecules we have also studied 12 previously reported purine derivatives and their analogues as an inhibitor of Stx activity of RNA and DNA depurination.(11) These small molecules were initially screened using fluorescent based thermal shift assay (FTS) and in in vitro assay using Vero cells. Compounds that were found to be promising in initial screening were further evaluated for their efficacy against Stx in Balb/c mice. Interaction of small molecule inhibitor with Stx is further validated by surface plasmon resonance (SPR). Interestingly, molecules that offered protection were all amide derivatives. Further optimization of these compounds may uncover drug lead against Stx toxin.

    In Silico Screening of Small Molecule Inhibitors

    In an effort to identify potential inhibitors against Stx, 300 molecules were mined from NCBI, ChemBridge, and ZINC databases. These molecules were subjected to in silico studies and docked on the active site of A chain of Stx using AutoDock 4.2. Scoring was done on the basis of minimum binding energy (kcal/mol) of protein–ligand complex formation. Of them, the most potent 20 molecules were prioritized for in vitro screening. The binding energy varied from −10.06 to −4.59 kcal/mol and Ki values from 3.21 to 439.63 μM (Table 1). Among these, 1, 2, 3, 12, 16, and 18 are novel molecules and the rest of the molecules are reported; however to the best of our knowledge, they have never been studied against Shiga toxin. These molecules were found to interact with the active site residues of the Stx A subunit which indicated their probability of efficient binding and thus inhibition of the toxin. The docked models of top scoring compounds were visualized using UCSF Chimera(12) (http://www.cgl.ucsf.edu/chimera) to observe the interactions between the lead compounds and target protein (Figure S1 in Supporting Information). In order to perform the comparative analysis, previously reported 12 small molecule Stx inhibitors (R-1 to R-12) were also included in the study and the docking was done for these compounds using Autodock 4.2 (Table S1).

    Synthesis of Small Molecules

    Compound 1 was prepared by treating phenyl-S-ethylamine hydrochloride with 1-(2-chloroethyl)pyrrolidine in the presence of triethylamine as a base. Compound 2 was prepared by selective thiophosphorylation of N1-(2-butylsulfanylethyl)ethane-1,2-diamine with diethyl chlorothiophosphate. Selective amidation of N1-(2-phenylsulfanylethyl)ethan-1,2-diamine with corresponding acyl halide resulted in formation of compounds 3 and 18. Amides 4, 15, 19, and 20 were prepared by direct acylation of appropriate amine with acyl halide. Similarly, selective amidation of N1-(2-propylsulfanylethyl)ethan-1,2-diamine with 4-chlorobenzoyl chloride and cinnamoyl chloride yielded 12 and 16, respectively. Acylation of 2-aminophenol and 2-aminoethanol with cinnamoyl chloride in dichloromethane utilizing triethylamine as base resulted in the formation of compounds 5 and 6, respectively. Compound 7 was prepared by esterifying 3-hydroxyflavone with cinnamoyl chloride. Quinolones compounds 13, 14, and 17 were prepared by cyclization of the amides obtained from 2-aminoacetophenone and corresponding acyl halides. Chalcones, i.e., compounds 8, 9, 10, and 11, were prepared by the method reported in the literature.(13, 14)

    Primary Screening Using Fluorescence-Based Thermal Shift Assay (FTS)

    In the present study, FTS assay was used for initial screening of synthesized molecules for their interaction with recombinant Stx A subunit (rStx A). Thermal stability shift analysis is a fast and powerful platform for screening of molecules that affect thermal stability of proteins after binding interactions.(15) This assay operates on the principle that each protein unfolds at specific temperature and binding of ligands alters the thermal stability of the protein resulting in shift in unfolding temperature. A probe (SYPRO orange) provides fluorescence due to binding to hydrophobic surface resulting from unfolding of the protein. All 32 compounds were evaluated for their interaction with rStx A subunit. Seven molecules are found to bind with greater affinity and altered the stability of protein, resulting in >2 °C change in transition temperature (ΔTm). Among these seven compounds, maximum shift in ΔTm was recorded with compound 15 (7.25 °C) followed by 4 (4.61 °C), R-7 (4.45 °C), 3 (3.87 °C), R-8 (3.75 °C), 16 (2.65 °C), and R-2 (2.32 °C) (Figure 1 and Table S1).

    Stx Induced Cytotoxicity in a Dose and Time Dependent Manner in Vero Cells

    To examine the cytotoxic effect and 50% cytotoxic concentration (CC50) of Stx in cells, the Vero cells were treated with different concentrations of Stx ranging from 0 to 120 ng/mL for 24 h, and the viability was determined by 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) and neutral red (NR) dye uptake assay. From 20 ng/mL, Stx caused a significant dose dependent decrease in viability with increase in concentration at 24 h. Concentration that is 50% cytotoxic (CC50) was found to be 100 ng/mL at 24 h (Figure 2A). This CC50 value of 100 ng/mL was used in further experiments.

    Effect of Compound on Cells and Stx Induced Toxicity

    All 32 small molecules were evaluated for maximum tolerated dose (MTD) in Vero cells. For determination of MTD, cells were treated with varying concentration of compounds for 24 h and then cell viability was measured by NR assay. MTD values of synthesized compounds varied from 5.00 ± 1.23 μM to 123.00 ± 2.45 μM and for reported compounds 9.30 ± 1.53 μM to 250.00 ± 1.75 μM. (Table 2). Further, to test the efficacy of the small molecules as an inhibitor of Stx by NR assay, cells were pretreated with compounds for 1 h followed by Stx exposure. Among the 32 compounds evaluated, four synthesized compounds (3, 4, 15, and 16) and two reported compounds (R-7 and R-8) showed 75–95% inhibition of Stx induced toxicity (Figure 2B and Figure 2C). Efficacy of these six compounds was further validated by MTT and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) release assay. As shown in Figure 2D, these small molecules were found to protect cells from Stx toxicity by MTT assay. Similarly, these compounds also showed significant inhibition of Stx induced LDH release and protected cell membrane integrity (Figure 2E). Other compounds showed no significant reversal of Stx induced toxicity in Vero cells. Those compounds that offered protection were also tested for their dose dependent potential to inhibit Stx toxicity. In Figure S2 (Supporting Information) compound 3 has been shown to inhibit Stx toxicity in a dose dependent manner with the highest inhibition at approximately 12 μM. The doses of compounds (3, 4, 15, 16, R-7, and R-8) inhibiting 50% toxicity of Stx (IC50) were determined in cells treated with different concentrations of compounds by NR assay (Table 2). These six compounds were also tested for their 50% cytotoxic concentration (CC50) using NR assay, and results are mentioned in Table 2. On the basis of the CC50 and IC50 values, the selectivity index (SI = CC50/IC50) of each compound was determined which reflects the quantity of compound that is active against the toxin but is not toxic toward the host cell. SI values of compounds that offered protection were 22.23, 11.29, 15.50, and 15.00 for compounds 3, 4, 15, and 6, respectively. Although compounds 3, 4, 15, and 16 significantly inhibited the Stx toxin induced cell death, compound 3 was found to be most potent with an SI value of 22.23 (Table 2A). Among the purine derivatives included in the study, guanine (R-7) showed maximum 88% inhibition and IC50 value of 11.50 μM and 76% inhibition was observed with allopurinol (R-8) with an IC50 value of 23.90 μM. The SI values were found to be 11.80 and 17.90 for R-7 and R-8, respectively (Table 2B).

    Binding Studies of Small Molecule Inhibitors with Stx A

    To quantitatively assess the binding affinity of compounds 3, 4, 15, and 16 with Stx, we performed SPR experiments using immobilized rStx A on GLH sensor chips at ∼6000 RU (resonance units) as described in Experimental Section. As shown in Figure 3, compound 3 binds to rStx A in a concentration dependent manner with high affinity and yielding an equilibrium dissociation constant (KD) of 7.16 × 10–7 M with a fast on rate (ka = 9.46 × 103 1/(M s)) and fast off rate (kd = 6.77 × 10–2 1/s). Other compounds 4, 15, and 16 also interacted with Stx A but comparatively with lower affinities yielding KD values of 3.09 × 10–6, 9.68 × 10–2, and 8.67 × 10–2 M, respectively (Figure S3).

    In Vivo Evaluation of Compounds 3, 4, 15, 16, R-7, and R-8 in Mouse Model

    To assess whether our finding is restricted to cultured cell lines, we analyzed the efficacy of compounds in mice against supralethal dose of Stx (5 × LD50). In Stx treated group, all mice died between 12 and 24 h with symptoms of shigellosis. The groups in which mice were exposed to Stx with pre- or post-treatment of compounds resulted in the extension of survival time where mice death was recorded in 24–48 h. Interestingly, in the group where compounds were preincubated with 5 × LD50 of Stx and then administered, all the animals survived and no animal showed any symptomatic effects of toxin (Figure 4). This finding suggests the neutralizing potential of these compounds against Shiga toxin toxicity. Surprisingly, R-7 and R-8 did not offer any protection even when they were administered after preincubating with Stx. In vehicle control and treatment with compound alone, groups did not show any sign of toxicity (data not shown).

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Stx is a category B biothreat agent; therapeutic agents that inhibit Stx toxicity are urgently required to prevent the harmful consequences of Stx that are not addressed with current antibiotic-based treatments. Various novel approaches have been attempted to develop inhibitors of various stages of Stx intoxication including cell uptake, trafficking, and release to the cytoplasm.(6, 7, 9, 16-20) The small molecules identified using high throughput screening that inhibit endogenous trafficking of toxin may affect other endogenous cargo proteins and can lead to other undesirable effects. Hence, only those molecules that directly interfere with active site and inhibit theN-glycosidase activity may be helpful.
    Structure based small molecule inhibitors are an attractive approach for the treatment of Stx intoxication. In this study, we utilized in silico screening to identify Stx inhibitors selectively targeting N-glycosidase activity of A subunit of Stx. Initial molecular screening of several small molecules, i.e., amides, chalcones, esters, and quinolones, led us to identify the best predicted inhibitors against shiga toxin. Finally, based on binding energy and Ki, we identified 20 molecules for synthesis and screening. Twelve reported purine derivatives were also included in the study.(11) These molecules act as an uncompetitive inhibitor of the RNA-N-glycosidase reaction and protect inactivation of ribosome by Stx.
    Initial screening of these compounds was done using FTS assay and in vitro assay. FTS assay is an inexpensive method for screening of small molecule inhibitors that rely on change in thermal stability of the proteins after binding of the ligands.(15) Among 32 compounds evaluated, seven compounds were found to alter the stability of rStx A, where shift in transition temperature (ΔTm) was recorded as >2 °C. These 32 compounds were also evaluated in cell based assay against Shiga toxin. Out of those, six compounds offered protection, four of them were new compounds (3, 4, 15, and 16), and two were reported inhibitors (R-7 and R-8). Interestingly, all the newly identified compounds were amides, suggesting amide derivatives may have potential to inhibit catalytic activity of Stx. We observed a good agreement between our initial screening methods, FTS and cell based assay. The compounds that were showing maximum thermal shift were consistent with the result that these small molecules owe the most potent in vitro inhibition activity. The only exception was observed with R-2 that was not able to protect cells after Stx challenge (Table 2).
    Selectivity index (SI) value defines the compound’s ability to preferentially produce a particular effect and is an important parameter for analyzing protective effect of compounds. Among all the compounds studied, compound 3 was found to be most promising in terms of protection against Stx with the highest SI value of 22.23. The magnitude of inhibition was also higher than those of earlier reported small molecule inhibitors including retro 1 and 2 where the IC50 was reported to be at 25.00 μM in cell culture,(19) whereas in the present study, IC50 of lead compounds was varied from 2.63 ± 0.53 to 8.20 ± 0.50 μM. Lower IC50 value in cell based protection assay reflects the higher efficacy of inhibitors, suggesting the greater therapeutic potential of present leads.
    Evaluation of molecules in Vero cells produced interesting results. Compounds having amide functionality were found very effective, while chalcones, quinolones, and ester of 3-hydroxyflavone did not provide any protection. Compounds 1, 3, and 18 were relatively similar except the terminal amide moiety. The modification of terminal amine functionality to pyrrolidine (compound 1) was not found effective. In 3 and 18 the terminal amine was acylated with 4-nitrobenzoyl chloride (aromatic moiety) and cyclohexanecarbonyl chloride (alicyclic moiety), respectively, to generate the amide group. Amidation with aromatic moiety (compound 3) was found to be much more effective than alicyclic moiety (compound 18). Compound 3 contained two aromatic rings with one amine and one amide functionality and was the most effective inhibitor in cell culture studies. Among the cinnamides, 4, 5, 6 and 16, the most effective compound was 16 followed by 4. Cyclization of amides derived from 2-aminoacetophenonequinolone, 14 and 17, was ineffective for cytoprotection, and at the same time compound toxicity was also increased. Placing fluoro group at ortho position (compound 15) compared to para position in aromatic ring (compound 20) proved to be more useful, as compound 15 was impressively effective while the other did not provide any protection.
    Evaluation of reported compound in Vero cells also revealed interesting results. Guanine (R-7) and allopurinol (R-8) are reported to be the least efficient inhibitors of RNA-N glycosidase activity by in vitro protein synthesis assay, but the same showed highest efficacy in Vero cells against Stx in our study.(11) Probably cell based assay used in present study is closer to biological system than in vitro protein synthesis inhibition assay. It could also be possible that both compounds may exhibit some additional effect like interference of toxin binding to its receptor, trafficking, or processing in the ER. 4-Aminopyrazolo[3,4-d]pyrimidine (4-APP), which was reported to be the most efficient small molecule inhibitor, could not be evaluated due to the nonavailability of the compound to us.(11) Guanine was also showing good inhibition against enzymatic activity of ricin, substantiating the hope for finding common inhibitor working against all RIPs.(17)
    To investigate the possibility of nonspecific or multitargets binding of these leads, pan assay interference compounds (PAINS) filter was applied. None of the lead molecules (compounds 3, 4, 15, and 16) were recognized as potential PAINS. Moreover, screening system used in in vitro evaluation is based on Vero cell viability for the estimation of potential inhibitor unlike other HTS methods where above-mentioned interference is a major hurdle.(21) Compound 5 having the substructure included in PAINS was also evaluated in cell based assay and showed no inhibitory activity, further strengthening that compounds 3, 4, 15, and 16 are true positive hits.
    SPR is a highly sensitive label free technique to measure binding and the affinities of potential inhibitors to ligand. It provides information about kinetics of interaction and the half-life of the complex. These biosensors can be used for secondary screening to confirm whether the hit is real and can also provide information about their dynamic interaction in real time. SPR experimentation showed that the amide based small molecules were interacting with rStx A in concentration dependent manner with high affinities, and equilibrium dissociation constants (KD) varied from 7.16 × 10–7 to 8.67 × 10–2 M. There was considerable correlation in SI values obtained from cytoprotection assay and the KD values for interactions between rStx A subunit and amide based compounds. On the basis of both values, compound 3 was found to be the most effective. The only exception was compound 4 which provided lowest SI value but interacted with higher KD than compounds 15 and 16. As shown in Figure 5, when compound 3 is docked in the active site pocket of Stx, the amide group of compound forms a hydrogen bond with Ser112 and is interacting with active site of toxin, i.e., Tyr77, Val78, Tyr114, Glu167, Ala168, Trp203, and Arg170, thus contributing to the specificity and potency of this inhibitor. The amide of compound 4 formed a hydrogen bond with Arg170 that is reported to involve in substrate binding.(17) In docked conformations compound 15 was found to interact with Arg170 and Tyr114, whereas compound 16 was seen interacting with Ser112 and Val78, which are reported as active site residues of Stx.
    Our findings from in vivo assay revealed that all four amide compounds are highly effective in neutralizing toxin by preincubating and protecting mice against Stx toxicity. There were no symptoms observed of Stx toxicity in animals. Protection obtained by preincubating indicates the efficacy of lead compound as Stx inhibitor and their potential to neutralize extracellular toxin. Significant extension in survival time was observed when the mice were challenged with Stx followed by pre- and post-treatment of inhibitors. However, no protection suggests the possibility of modification of compound or pharmacokinetics inside biological system. This may also be due to distribution, absorption, or/and stability of the compound in body system. Both the earlier reported compounds (guanine and allopurinol) were not found effective in all three modes of treatment, though the efficacy of leads identified in the present study needs further optimization with respect to neutralization of toxin in pre- and postchallenge. All these leads are amide derivatives, but compound 3 was found to be most promising and efficacious and requires further study. Further these compounds are nonoptimized hits and modification in the chemistry of compounds may further improve in vivo efficacy.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Our findings indicate that targeting the catalytic site with small molecules represents a feasible therapeutic approach that may allow successful reversal of Shiga toxicity. Since our molecules are specific inhibitors of enzymatic activity of Shiga toxin, they do not cause any undesirable effects unlike inhibitors of retrograde pathway which may affect trafficking of other vital endogenous cargo proteins. Among all the compounds studied, 4-nitro-N-[2-(2-phenylsulfanylethylaminoethyl]benzamide hydrochloride (compound 3) is found to be most promising in terms of protection against Stx with the highest SI value of 22.23 and the magnitude of inhibition is higher than those of earlier reported small molecules with an IC50 of 2.63 ± 0.53 μM. Preincubation of Shiga toxin with these amides based small molecules also protected animals. Lower IC50 values in cell based assay reflects the higher efficacy of inhibitors, suggesting the greater therapeutic potential, and their activities can be enhanced by further lead optimization efforts which are under progress.

    1 Chemistry Methods

    Reagents of analytical grade (>98%) were obtained from commercial suppliers and used without further purification. Column chromatography was performed on silica gel (60–120 mesh). 1H, 31P, and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on Bruker AVANCE II 400 MHz and Bruker AVANCE III 600 MHz. Chemical shifts were expressed in parts per millions (δ) downfield from the internal standard tetramethylsilane and were reported as s (singlet), d (doublet), t (triplet), q (quartet), and m (multiplet). Melting points were measured by scientific-MP-DS apparatus. Mass spectra were obtained from Agilent-5975C GC–MS instrument, and elemental analysis was performed on Elementar vario MICRO-cube CHNS analyzer. Purity was checked by elemental analysis and was >98% for all the compounds synthesized. The reported compounds (R-compounds) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO) and were >99% pure except R-3, R-5, and R-9 where the purity was ∼98%.

    2 Synthesis of Compounds. N-(2-(Phenylthio)ethyl)-2-(pyrrolidin-1-yl)ethanamine Hydrochloride (1)

    Phenyl-S-ethylamine hydrochloride 10 mmol (1.89 g) and 1-(2-chloroethyl)pyrrolidine 2.0 mmol (2.60 g) was dissolved in 10 mL of ethanol. The contents were heated to reflux. To this, triethylamine 5 mmol (7.50 mL) in 30 mL of ethanol was added dropwise over a period of 3–4 h. After the addition is over reaction mixture was heated until the reaction was complete, which was monitored by GC–MS and TLC (mobile phase CH3OH/CHCl3/NH3OH, 3:2:1). On evaporation of ethanol, solid residue was obtained, which was dissolved in chloroform and washed with sodium carbonate. Evaporation of chloroform resulted in an oily mass. The oil was dissolved in acetone and converted into its hydrochloride salt with dry HCl. The hydrochloride salt of N-alkyl derivative was obtained as solid precipitate which was purified by recrystallization from acetone–methanol mixture.     3 Shiga Toxin

    3.1 Prepararion of Shiga Toxin

    Standard culture of S. dysenteriae type 1 was obtained from National Institute of Cholera and Enteric Diseases (NICED), Kolkata. Pure colony of S. dysenteriae type 1 was inoculated in 5 mL of brain–heart infusion (BHI) broth and incubated overnight at 37 °C with shaking at 200 rpm. BHI broth (200 mL) was inoculated with 1% of the mother culture and incubated at 37 °C, with shaking at 200 rpm for 24 h. The overnight grown culture was centrifuged at 10 000g for 10 min at 4 °C. The pellet was resuspended in 50 mM Tris-Cl buffer (pH 8.0), lysozyme (20 μg/mL) and incubated at 4 °C for 1 h. The resuspended solution was subjected to sonication (5 cycles of 9 s on and 9 s off), followed by centrifugation. The supernatant obtained was filtered with 0.22 μm syringe filter (Millipore), and after protein estimation (micro-BCA, Pierce) aliquots were stored in −80 °C. These aliquot were further used for cytotoxicity assay and other experiments. This lysate was referred as Stx.

    3.2 Preparation of Recombinant Shiga Toxin A Subunit (rStx A)

    Expression, purification, and immunological characterization of recombinant Shiga toxin A subunit was done in laboratory as described earlier.(24) Purified recombinant StxA subunit was used for the thermal shift assay and SPR studies.

    4 Screening of Small Molecule Inhibitors against Stx

    4.1 In Silico Screening Using AutoDock 4.2

    Small molecule structures in ligand.pdb format were prepared using ArgusLab 4.0.1 (http://www.arguslab.com). The 1DM0.pdb file of Stx A subunit was obtained from RCSB (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7656009). AutoDock 4.2 is used for docking studies.(22) The Grid box was centered on the active site amino acid residues Ser112, Tyr77, Val78, Tyr114, Glu167, Ala168, Trp203, and Arg170. AutoDock was launched and resulting docking conformations compared to determine similarities, and then they are clustered accordingly. The root-mean-square deviation (rmsd) is used to determine whether two docked conformations are similar enough to be in the same cluster. Then these clusters are ranked from the lowest energies to highest.

    4.2 Screening of Small Molecule Using Fluorescence-Based Thermal Shift Assay (FTS)

    Thermal shift assay is the primary method of identification of interaction between protein and small molecule inhibitors. SYPRO orange is a fluorescent dye used to monitor protein unfolding. Melting of the globular proteins exposes the hydrophobic region of proteins that results in large increase in fluorescence.(15) The shift in temperatures obtained in the presence of ligands as compared to absence of ligands of unfolded protein is used for estimation of change in transition temperature (ΔTm). Fluorescence based thermal shift assay (FTS) was conducted on Step One real time PCR detection system (Applied Biosystems, Life Technologies, USA). The device is made of heating/cooling system for temperature control and a detector based charge coupled device to detect fluorescence change in the wells in real time. The 48-well microtiter plate was used for FTS assay. A total of 20 μL/well solution was prepared containing 1.76 μM rStx A, 5× SYPRO orange and small molecules at 10 μM concentration in PBS. The plate was heated at 1% of ramp rate (∼5 °C/min) from 25 to 90 °C, and Rox is selected as reporter for detection of fluorescence intensity.

    4.3 Surface Plasmon Resonance Analysis

    The interaction between small molecules inhibitors and rStx A was further explored using a ProteOn XPR36 instrument (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA). This instrument provides real-time data of 36 simultaneous interactions in one experiment. The instrument features six injection needles with the ability to use the horizontal for six ligand immobilization conditions and vertical for six concentrations/analytes orientations of the fluidics of the sensor chip.     5 Cell Culture and Evaluation of Small Molecule Inhibitors

    On the basis of molecular docking studies 20 molecules were selected for in vitro screening. The short listed molecules consisted of amides, chalcones, esters, and quinolones. We have also evaluated 12 compounds that were previously reported against Stx for its efficacy.(11)

    5.1 Cell Maintenance

    Vero cells were obtained from National Centre for Cell Science, Pune, and maintained in Dulbeco’s modified Eagle medium (DMEM) containing 10% FBS in an incubator at 37 °C humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2 and 95% air.

    5.2 CC50 Determination of Toxin

    The concentration of Stx toxic to 50% of the cells (CC50) was determined from the plots of cell viability by the neutral red (NR) uptake method and 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay (Sigma Chemical, St. Louis, MO, USA). Vero cells were dispensed in flat-bottomed 96-well microtiter plates (Nunc, Denmark) at a density 104 cells/well and maintained overnight at 37 °C. Then the cells were incubated with various concentration of Stx (20–120 ng/mL) for further 24 h. For NR assay after 24 h of treatment, 0.33% neutral red solution (10 μL/well) was added to cells and incubated for 4 h. Incorporated dye was solubilized in 50% methanol containing 1% acetic acid, and absorbance was read at 540 nm (Biotek Instruments, USA). For MTT assay, MTT (5 mg/mL, 20 μL/well) solution was added to each well and incubated for 4 h. Formazan crystals were solubilized with DMSO, and absorbance was measured at 570 nm using a multiscanner. Data were normalized to the measurement from control cultures which were considered 100% survival.

    5.3 Toxicity Measurements of the Compounds

    Similar culture conditions as described above were used for the toxicity measurements of the small molecules. Small molecules used for this study listed in Table 1 were dissolved in sterile filtered absolute DMSO, and 20 mM stock solution was prepared. Compounds were 10-fold serially diluted (1000–0.0001 μM) in media for the CC50 determination. We have also determined highest concentration of compounds that is nontoxic to cells known as maximum tolerable dose (MTD).

    5.4 Determination of Efficacy of Compounds against Stx

    For screening of efficacy and determination of IC50 (concentration of compounds reversing 50% toxicity of Stx, assuming 100% toxicity of Stx) of compounds against Stx, cells were treated with varying concentrations of compounds for 1 h prior to Stx treatment. Cells were also treated with DMSO as a vehicle control, and no toxicity was observed when administered alone (data not shown). The cell viability assay was performed at 24 h post-treatment employing neutral red dye uptake assay. Efficacies of promising compounds were also determined by MTT assay. We have observed the dose dependent efficacy of compounds that offered protection against Stx. Selectivity index (SI) as an estimate of therapeutic window of compounds was calculated from CC50 and IC50 of compounds (SI = CC50/IC50).

    5.5 Lactate Dehydrogenase Release Assay

    Efficacy of compounds that offered protection against Stx by MTT and NR assay was further validated by LDH release assay. LDH activity in the culture media was measured spectrophotometrically at 490 nm according to the manufacturer’s protocol (Sigma Chemical, St. Louis, MO, USA) as an index of plasma membrane damage and membrane integrity loss. Enzyme activity was expressed as the percentage of extracellular LDH activity of the total LDH activity of the cells.

    6 Animal Treatment Strategy and Husbandry

    Balb/c male mice were randomly bred in institute’s animal facility, weighing between 22 and 24 g, and were used in this study. The animals were housed in standard conditions of temperature and humidity. The animals were fed standard pellet diet (Ashirwad Brand, Chandigarh, India). Food and water were provided ad libitum. The animals were used according to the guidelines of Committee for the Purpose of Control and Supervision on Experiments on Animals, and Institutional Animal Ethics Committee approved the experiment (BT/01//DRDE/2012).
    Compounds that offered protection in in vitro study were further evaluated in mice for their efficacy against Stx toxicity. Animals were divided into five groups of eight animals each. For treatment, median lethal dose of Stx required to kill half the members of a tested population (LD50) was determined for 7 days using a reported method.(25) For all treatment intraperitoneal route (ip) of administration was used. Toxin alone treated group was administered a single dose of 5 × LD50 of Stx by ip route. In the second group mice were treated with the toxin that was preincubated with compounds for 1 h. In the third group animals were treated with compounds 1 h prior to toxin injection. In the fourth group toxin was injected 1 h prior to the treatment by compound. Control animals received the same volume of PBS as the experimental group. All the mice were treated with 20 mM compounds at fixed dose of 100 μL.

    7 Statistical Analysis

    The complete data were expressed as the mean ± SEM of three or more independent experiments with triplicate samples. Statistical comparisons between groups were performed using one-way ANOVA followed by Dunnett’s test or Student’s t-test or Student–Newman–Keuls multiple comparison test. Differences with p less than 0.05 were taken as significant.
