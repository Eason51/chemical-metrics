    Abstract

    An oligoethyleneoxy linker was introduced for conjugation between 99mTc/Re-bis(aminoethanethiol) (BAT) and β-amyloid (Aβ) binding scaffolds. Rhenium complexes exhibited high to moderate binding affinity to Aβ1–42 aggregates and efficient fluorescent staining to Aβ plaques in brain tissue. After radiolabeling, the 99mTc-labeled probes revealed improved brain pharmacokinetics in normal ICR mice. Probe [99mTc]15 with potent binding affinity (Ki = 13.4 nM) and the highest initial brain uptake (2.10% ID/g at 2 min) in normal ICR mice was evaluated further. In vitro autoradiography showed specific labeling of Aβ plaques by [99mTc]15 in transgenic (Tg) mouse brain tissue. Ex vivo autoradiography further demonstrated its efficient labeling of Aβ plaques in a living Tg mouse. In vivo single photon emission computed tomography (SPECT)/CT imaging in six rhesus monkeys revealed remarkably improved brain uptakes (1.94–2.63% ID within 20 min) of [99mTc]15, making it highly potential to be used in humans for Aβ plaques imaging in the brain.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a major concern of public health, featuring memory loss and gradual executive dysfunction. Consisting of abnormally folded β-amyloid (Aβ) protein, Aβ plaques are extensively found in AD brains, which form due to an imbalance between production and clearance of β-amyloid species.(1-3) Developed in the early stage of AD, Aβ plaques are afflicted with many factors related to AD and considered to be pivotal in the pathological process.(4) Although recent research shows that the deposit of Aβ plaques does not necessarily lead to the onset of AD, and many anti-amyloid drug candidates failed in phase 3 clinical trial,(5) Aβ plaques remain a valuable biomarker in AD pathology.(6) The absence of Aβ plaques in the brain may help doctors rule out AD in dementia diagnosis; therefore, imaging of Aβ plaques is of great significance for a better detailed understanding of AD.(7)
    Many probes targeting Aβ plaques have been developed for noninvasive imaging, including magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), near-infrared (NIR) fluorescence imaging, positron emission tomography (PET), and single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT). MRI provides high spatial and temporal resolution without irradiation, gadolinium complexes and iron oxide nanoparticles, have been developed as contrast agents targeted toward Aβ plaques.(8) NIR fluorescence is feasible for real-time imaging on small animals; many probes have been investigated including AOI-987,(9) CRANAD-2,(10) CRANAD-58,(11) BAP-1,(12) and DANIR-3b.(13) For clinical use, PET and SPECT are more extensively utilized in hospitals. The extensive clinical use of 11C-2-[4′-(methylamino)phenyl]-6-hydrobenzothiazole [11C]1 ([11C]PIB) and FDA approval of three 18F-labeled probes have marked the success of Aβ probes for PET imaging.(14) In contrast, no SPECT probes for Aβ plaques imaging is available for clinical use. Only a few radio-iodinated probes including 123I-6-iodo-2-(4′-dimethylamino)phenyl-imidazo[1,2-a]-pyridine [123I]2 ([123I]IMPY)(15, 16) and 123I-2-(6-(1H-1,2,3-triazol-1-yl)pyridin-3-yl)-6-iodoimidazo[1,2-a]pyridine ([123I]ABC577)(17) have been studied in humans. As for 99mTc-labeled Aβ probes, the balance between binding affinity and favorable brain pharmacokinetics is difficult to achieve.(18) Nevertheless, the idea of 99mTc-labeled probes is still attractive due to the ideal isotope properties, potential for kit-labeling, and ready availability of SPECT imaging equipment.(19)
    Previously, we reported a 2-phenylbenzothiazole derivative (Figure 1, [99mTc]3) possessing both potent binding affinity toward Aβ1–42 aggregates (Ki = 8.8 nM) and favorable brain pharmacokinetics (2.11% ID/g at 2 min p.i. and 0.62% ID/g at 60 min p.i.) in normal mice.(20) Moreover, SPECT imaging revealed radioactivity accumulation and washout in the brain of rhesus monkey, suggesting it successfully crossed the blood–brain barrier (BBB) of rhesus monkeys, and this was the first report of a 99mTc-labeled Aβ probe tested in nonhuman primates. Although the brain uptake in rhesus monkeys (0.78–1.23% ID at 0–10 min p.i.) is still not sufficient for brain imaging, it demonstrated the potential use of 99mTc-labeled probes for Aβ imaging with SPECT.
    Pegylation is a common approach to improve in vivo pharmacokinetics of a pharmaceutical molecule, especially for increasing circulation times.(21-23) As for probes targeting into the brain, an oligoethyleneoxy linker has been applied to many 18F-labeled probes including 18F-(E)-4-(2-(6-(2-(2-(2-fluoroethoxy)ethoxy)ethoxy)pyridin-3-yl)vinyl)-N-methyl benzenamine [18F]4 ([18F]AV-45)(24) and 18F-(E)-4-(N-methylamino)-4′-{2-[2-(2-fluoro-ethoxy)-ethoxy]-ethoxy}-stilbene ([18F]BAY94-9172).(25) The oligoethyleneoxy linker may reduce the molecular lipophilicity and improve the brain pharmacokinetics, i.e. initial brain uptake and washout rate from normal brain, finally resulting in less nonspecific binding to white matter in the brain.(26, 27) In this work, an oligoethyleneoxy linker was employed to conjugate the Aβ binding scaffold to bis(aminoethanethiol) (BAT) chelating group for 99mTc-labeling (Figure 1). The BAT chelating group is easily labeled with 99mTc and considered to be more suitable for crossing the BBB. Based on our previous study, we first applied the oligoethyleneoxy linker to the 2-arylbenzothiazole scaffold from [11C]1, resulting in considerably improved brain pharmacokinetics. Taking the process a step further, 2-arylbenzothiazole was replaced by 3-styrylpyridine, another Aβ binding scaffold from [18F]4 with high and specific binding to Aβ plaques. Two steps of rational structure modification were designed to challenge the limit of 99mTc-labeled Aβ probes, resulting in generally improved brain pharmacokinetics and a promising candidate for practical use with sufficient brain uptake in rhesus monkeys.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    Chemistry

    The synthesis of 3-styrylpyridine derivatives was started by the modification of 6-chloronicotinaldehyde with an oligoethyleneoxy chain through nucleophilic substitution (Scheme 1). The formation of the (E)-3-styrylpyridine scaffold was accomplished by Horner–Wadsworth–Emmons reaction between the oligoethyleneoxy nicotinaldehyde (5, 6) and diethyl 4-nitrobenzylphosphonate, which preferentially afforded (E)-isomers in high yield (>75%).(28) The nitryl group was readily reduced by N2H4·H2O–Pd/C and subsequently converted to the dimethylamino group with paraformaldehyde and NaBH3(CN) in glacial acetic acid. The terminal hydroxyl group of the oligoethyleneoxy chain was brominated by N-bromosuccinimide (NBS) and then conjugated to the protected BAT chelating group, yielding the precursors (13, 14) for 99mTc labeling. As nonradioactive surrogates for 99mTc-labeled products, their corresponding rhenium complexes (15, 16) were synthesized with (PPh3)2ReOCl3 and deprotected precursors in the presence of sodium acetate.
    For the synthesis of 2-arylbenzothiazole derivatives, the scaffolds of 2-arylbenzothiazole (17, 18) were readily prepared as reported previously,(23, 27) followed by Williamson reaction with 2-(2-chloroethoxy)ethanol (Scheme 2). The bromination of the terminal hydroxyl group, conjugation to the BAT chelating group, and synthesis of corresponding rhenium complexes (25, 26) were accomplished by the same methods as 3-styrylpyridine derivatives.

    In Vitro Fluorescent Staining of Aβ Plaques in Tg Mice and AD Brains

    Based on the fluorescent properties of the 3-styrylpyridine and 2-arylbenzothiazole scaffold, the binding efficiency of the rhenium complexes to Aβ plaques in brain tissues was monitored by a fluorescence microscope. As expected, bright fluorescent spots were observed in the Tg (C57BL6, APP/PS1, 12 months old) mouse brain tissue after incubation with each rhenium complex, which were confirmed to be Aβ plaques by thioflavin-S staining in adjacent brain tissue (Figure S1). Moreover, Aβ plaques were also visualized with low background in brain tissue from an AD patient (Figure 2). The results indicated the efficient and specific binding capability of the rhenium complexes toward Aβ plaques in brain tissues.

    Binding Affinity to Aβ1–42 Aggregates

    An in vitro competitive binding assay was conducted to quantify the binding affinity of the rhenium complexes to Aβ1–42 aggregates, which is the predominant Aβ species deposited in the brain of AD. Competing with [125I]2, rhenium complexes 15 and 25 exhibited high affinity (Ki value, 13.4 and 30.9 nM), while 16 and 26 showed medium binding affinity toward Aβ1–42 aggregates (Table 1). 2 and 4 were also tested under the same experimental conditions to validate the binding assays for testing the new rhenium complexes. For the 3-styrylpyridine derivatives, compound 15 with a shorter oligoethyleneoxy linker displayed higher affinity. For the 2-arylbenzothiazole derivatives, more potent binding was observed for compound 25 bearing a phenyl substituent compared to a pyridyl substituent. Overall, all of the four synthesized rhenium complexes possessed efficient binding affinity, consisting with the results of fluorescent staining of Aβ plaques in brain tissues.

    Radiochemistry

    The desired 99mTc-labeled complexes were prepared via ligand exchange reaction (Scheme 3) and purified through radio-HPLC. Reaction between freshly labeled 99mTc-glucoheptonate (99mTc-GH) and deprotected precursors at 90 °C for 10 min offered 99mTc-labeled products with radiochemical yields over 60%. Radiochemical purity was greater than 95% after purification by radio-HPLC. Identification of the 99mTc-labeled products was accomplished by comparing the retention time from HPLC with the corresponding rhenium complex as reference (Figure S2). Additionally, [99mTc]15 was stable in water for 6 h (purity: 95.0%, rt), and also stable in mice serum for at least 1 h (purity: 95.9%, 37 °C) (Figure S4).
    Lipophilicity of the 99mTc-labeled tracers was estimated by measuring the distribution coefficient between 1-octanol and water (log D). The log D values of the tracers were between 2.48 and 3.15, which were considered to be suitable for BBB penetration. Additionally, the HPLC retention time of 99mTc-labeled tracers were positively related to their log D values. The tracer with a shorter HPLC retention time tended to possess a lower log D value (Table 2).

    Biodistribution in Normal Mice

    The ability to penetrate BBB is crucial for the imaging probes designed to target biomarkers in the brain. A biodistribution study in normal ICR mice revealed generally improved brain pharmacokinetics. Specifically, the 2-arylbenzothiazole derivatives [99mTc]25 and [99mTc]26 showed much higher initial brain uptake and also faster clearance than their analogues bearing an alkyl linker of the same atom numbers as the oligoethyleneoxy linker. This proved the oligoethyleneoxy linker to be beneficial for the brain pharmacokinetics of the 99mTc-labeled probes compared with alkyl linkers. However, the initial brain uptake was not as high as [99mTc]3. Replacement of the 2-arylbenzothiazole scaffold with 3-styrylpyridine led to more favorable initial brain uptake ([99mTc]15, 2.10% ID/g), which was comparable to [99mTc]3 (2.11% ID/g). A longer oligoethyleneoxy linker resulted in a higher clearance ratio (brain2 min/brain60 min, [99mTc]16, 7.33) but lower initial brain uptake ([99mTc]16, 1.10% ID/g). Overall, all the four probes penetrated the BBB with initial brain uptakes at 2 min p.i. above 0.9% ID/g (Table 2 and S1), while the previously reported 99mTc-labeled probes with alkyl linkers mostly exhibited poor brain uptakes (<1.0% ID/g). As for the clearance from normal mice brain, the probes revealed a higher clearance ratio (2.91–7.33) than most of the previously reported 99mTc-labeled probes with alkyl linkers (<2.5), suggesting a low level of nonspecific binding in the brain. The 3-styrylpyridine probe with a longer oligoethyleneoxy linker ([99mTc]16) and 2-pyridylbenzothiazole probe ([99mTc]26) showed faster clearance (ratio 7.33 and 6.13) than [18F]4 (3.90). [99mTc]15 with the highest initial brain uptake and binding affinity was evaluated further as a potential SPECT imaging probe for Aβ plaques in the brain.

    In Vitro Autoradiography

    An in vitro autoradiography study was conducted by treating Tg mouse brain tissue with [99mTc]15. After 1 h of incubation, washing procedures, and phosphorus plate imaging, clear spots were observed in the cortex and cerebellum regions (Figure 3). The brain tissue was subsequently fluorescent stained with DANIR-3b, a highly specific near-infrared probe for Aβ plaques.(13) The consistence between the autoradiography image and the fluorescent staining confirmed the radioactive spots as Aβ plaques. In contrast, no radioactive spots or Aβ plaques were found on the age-matched wild-type mouse brain tissue. The results suggested high binding affinity and specificity of the radiolabeled probe [99mTc]15 toward Aβ plaques.

    Ex Vivo Autoradiography

    The binding ability of [99mTc]15 was further evaluated in living mice with or without Aβ plaques in the brain. The autoradiography images of the brains from Tg mouse and age-matched wild-type mouse significantly differentiated from each other (Figure 4). The radio ligand almost distributed homogeneously in the normal brain; no distinguishable labeled spots were found for the wild-type mouse. In contrast, distinct radioactive spots were observed on brain sections of Tg mouse, which were well-matched with the thioflavin-S stained Aβ plaques. This demonstrated [99mTc]15 penetrated the BBB and specifically labeled Aβ plaques in the living Tg mouse brain. Efficient in vivo labeling of Aβ plaques qualified [99mTc]15 as a useful imaging agent.

    In Vivo SPECT/CT Imaging in Rhesus Monkeys

    Brain scans (6 × 10 min) and whole body scan (80–100 min) were successively obtained on a total number of six rhesus monkeys (three female and three male, aging from 4 to 30 year) after intravenous injection of [99mTc]15. Although [99mTc]15 exhibited similar brain pharmacokinetics as [99mTc]3 in normal mice, differences were found in SPECT/CT imaging in rhesus monkeys. SPECT images showed significant and fast brain uptake and quick washout from the brain on both male and female rhesus monkeys of different ages (Figures 5 and S3.1–3.6). Semiquantitative data revealed brain uptake peaking (1.94–2.63% ID) within 20 min postinjection with a moderate clearance ratio (1.34–1.47) (Table 3). The initial brain uptakes were remarkably higher than [99mTc]3 (0.78–1.23% ID). It should be noted that the same rhesus monkeys tested in a previous study were also included in this study (F27 and M04), making the results more persuasive. The notably improved brain uptake in rhesus monkeys made [99mTc]15 highly potential to be used in humans, which is hardly reached by previously developed 99mTc-labeled Aβ probes. The average initial brain uptake for the old female monkeys (2.27 ± 0.31% ID) was a little higher than the young male monkeys (2.05 ± 0.09% ID), and the average clearance ratio for the old female monkeys (1.42 ± 0.05) was quite close to that of young male monkeys (1.39 ± 0.06). Thus, no significant difference was observed between old and young monkeys, or male and female, in this study. Additionally, [99mTc]15 displayed much less radioactivity accumulation in the glands of the head than [99mTc]3, which reduced the possible interference to the brain imaging. As for the excretion of [99mTc]15, hepatobiliary and urinary elimination were indicated by the whole body scan (Figure S3.1–3.6), which revealed extensive accumulation of radioactivity in the liver and kidneys. Since there were probably no Aβ plaques in these monkeys’ brains, this study only demonstrated the BBB penetrability of [99mTc]15, but was not able to evaluate the in vivo specific binding of [99mTc]15 in rhesus monkeys, and this will be investigated in further human studies.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, we designed and synthesized four oligoethyleneoxy-modified 99mTc-labeled probes for Aβ plaques imaging. In vitro fluorescent staining indicated efficient binding of the rhenium complexes toward Aβ plaques in both Tg mice and AD patient brain tissues. An in vitro competitive binding assay resulted in high to medium binding affinity for the rhenium complexes to Aβ1–42 aggregates. After radiolabeling, the 99mTc-labeled probes revealed significantly improved brain pharmacokinetics in normal ICR mice, probably benefited from the oligoethyleneoxy linker. [99mTc]15 was studied further with autoradiography and in vivo SPECT/CT imaging. [99mTc]15 showed potent labeling of Aβ plaques in Tg mouse brain both in vitro and ex vivo. Moreover, [99mTc]15 showed remarkably improved brain uptakes in rhesus monkeys. These results demonstrated [99mTc]15 to be highly potential for Aβ plaques imaging in human brain.

    General

    All reagents and solvents used were purchased from commercial sources without further purification. Na99mTcO4 was eluted from a 99 Mo-99mTc generator (Beijing Atomic High-Tech Co. Ltd.). GH kits were purchased from Beijing ShiHong Pharmaceutical Center. NMR spectra were acquired with a Bruker Avance III spectrometer (1H NMR at 400 MHz and 13C NMR at 101 MHz) under room temperature. Chemical shifts are reported as δ values referring to the internal standard (TMS). Mass spectra were acquired on a Surveyor MSQ Plus instrument (Waltham, MA, USA). Fluorescent staining was conducted on an EVOS FL imaging system (Life Technologies, USA). Radioactivity was determined by an automatic γ-counter (WALLAC/Wizard 1470, USA). HPLC were conducted on a Shimadzu LC-20AD system equipped with a γ-radiation scintillation detector (Bioscan Flow Count 3200 NaI/PMT) and a UV detector (SPD-20AV, λ = 254 nm). All the key compounds were confirmed to be more than 95% pure analyzed by HPLC or radio-HPLC. Monkey SPECT/CT scans were conducted at Xuanwu Hospital on a Discovery NM/CT 670 system (GE Healthcare). Aβ1–42 peptides (trifluoroacetate form) were purchased from Peptide Institute, Inc. (Japan). Normal ICR mice (male, 20–22 g) were purchased from Vital River Co. Ltd. Transgenic mice (C57BL6, APPswe/PSEN1, male), and age-matched wild-type mice (C57BL6, male) were obtained from the Institute of Laboratory Animal Sciences, Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences. Human brain tissue from an autopsy-confirmed AD patient (91 years old, male) was obtained from the Chinese Brain Bank Center. Rhesus monkeys (25 years old, female; 27 years old, female; 30 years old, female; 4 years old, male; 8 years old, male; 12 years old, male) were provided by the Department of Laboratory Animal Sciences, School of Basic Medical Sciences, Capital Medical University. All protocols involving use of animals were approved by the Animal Care Committee of Beijing Normal University and Capital Medical University and also in accordance with ACS Ethical Guidelines.

    Chemistry

    Compounds 6, 8, 10, 17, and 18 were prepared as reported previously.(23, 27, 29, 30)

    6-(2-(2-Hydroxyethoxy)ethoxy)nicotinaldehyde (5)

    Diethylene glycol (4.00 g, 37.7 mmol) was dissolved in DMF (20 mL), and potassium tert-butoxide (t-BuOK, 3.37g, 30.0 mmol) was added carefully and stirred for 30 min. Then 2-chloropyridine-5-carbaldehyde (6-chloronicotinaldehyde) was added slowly. After stirring for 24 h at room temperature, water (150 mL) was added, followed by extraction with CH2Cl2 (3 × 50 mL). Drying over Na2SO4 and removal of the solvent offered the crude product, which was purified through column chromatography (petroleum ether–ethyl acetate, 1:2) to give a colorless oil. Yield, 32%. 1H NMR (400 MHz, CDCl3) δ 9.95 (s, 1H), 8.61 (d, J = 2.2 Hz, 1H), 8.07 (dd, J = 8.7, 2.3 Hz, 1H), 6.88 (d, J = 8.6 Hz, 1H), 4.60 (t, J = 4.7 Hz, 2H), 3.88 (t, J = 4.8 Hz, 2H), 3.76 (t, J = 4.9 Hz, 2H), 3.66 (t, J = 4.9 Hz, 2H).

    6-(2-(2-(2-Hydroxyethoxy)ethoxy)ethoxy)nicotinaldehyde (6)

    The compound was prepared by the same procedure as 5 starting from 2-chloropyridine-5-carbaldehyde and triethylene glycol, yielding a colorless oil. Yield, 33%.

    Radiochemistry and biological assays were briefly described here, which were conducted mainly according to previously reported procedures.(20, 27)

    In Vitro Fluorescent Staining

    Paraffin-embedded brain slices were deparaffinized in xylene and washed by ethanol and water and then incubated in the solution of Re complexes (1.0 μM, in 10% ethanol/water) for 10 min. After washing, the slices were observed by an EVOS FL imaging system (Life Technologies, USA) using a DAPI filter set. To confirm the Aβ plaques, adjacent brain slices from Tg mice were stained by thioflavin-S.

    In Vitro Binding Assay

    To borosilicate glass tubes (12 mm × 75 mm, Fisher), [125I]2 (100 μL, approximately 100 000 cpm) Re complex (100 μL, 10–4 M to 10–9.5 M in ethanol), bovine serum albumin (100 μL, 0.1%), and Aβ1–42 aggregates (100 μL, 0.76 μM) were successively added and well mixed. After incubation at 37 °C for 2 h, filtration was performed on a cell harvester (Mp-48T, Brandel) through Whatman GF/B filters. The radioactivity on the filter was measured by a γ-counter (WALLAC/Wizard 1470, USA). IC50 values were calculated by GraphPad Prism 4.0, and Ki values were determined based on the Cheng–Prusoff equation: Ki = IC50/(1 + [L]/Kd).(31)

    Radiochemistry

    As an intermediate for radiolabeling, [99mTc]GH was prepared by injecting freshly eluted Na99mTcO4 (5–100 mCi, 1 mL) to a GH kit (containing 8.0 mg glucoheptonate) and keeping it at room temperature for 10 min. To a vial containing precursor (0.1 mg), trifluoroacetic acid (100 μL) was added, after 10 min at rt, triethylsilane (2 μL). The deprotected precursor was dried by nitrogen gas, dissolved by ethanol (100 μL), and then transferred into [99mTc]GH solution. The ligand exchange reaction was conducted at 90 °C for 10 min. Purification and analysis for product identification and purity confirmation were performed via radio-HPLC on Venusil MP C18 columns (Bonna-Agela Technologies, 5 μm, 4.6 mm × 250 mm) with acetonitrile/water (80%/20%) at a flow rate of 1 mL/min. The HPLC analysis data also demonstrated all the Re complexes and 99mTc-labeled products to be more than 95% pure (Figure S2). An in vitro stability assay was conducted according to procedures described previously.(32)

    Distribution Coefficient Measurement

    Purified 99mTc-labeled ligand (50 μCi) was added into 3.6 mL of n-octanol and 3.0 mL of PBS (0.05 M, pH 7.4). The mixture was vortexed for 3 min and then centrifuged for 5 min at 3000 rpm. Samples from n-octanol (50 μL) and PBS (500 μL) were carefully taken in triplicate and measured by a γ-counter (WALLAC/Wizard 1470, USA). Samples from the n-octanol layer (2.0 mL) were repartitioned until consistent distribution coefficient values were obtained.

    Biodistribution in Normal Mice

    Purified 99mTc-labeled ligand (5 μCi, 10% EtOH in saline, 100 μL) was administered intravenously to ICR mice (male, 18–22 g, four groups, n = 5), which were sacrificed at 2, 10, 30, or 60 min postinjection. Organs of interest were collected, weighed, and detected by a γ-counter (WALLAC/Wizard 1470, USA) for radioactivity measurement. The results were expressed as %ID/g or %ID.

    In Vitro Autoradiography

    A brain slice was incubated in [99mTc]15 (100 μL, 20 μCi) for 1 h, washed with 40% ethanol, and then exposed to a phosphorus plate (medium size) for 1 h. The image was captured by a storage phosphor system (PerkinElmer, USA) at 600 dpi resolution. To confirm specific binding for Aβ plaques, the same brain slice was subsequently fluorescent stained by DANIR-3b.

    Ex Vivo Autoradiography

    After injection of [99mTc]15 (0.6 mCi, 10% EtOH in saline, 300 μL), a Tg mouse and an age-matched wild-type mouse were sacrificed at 30 min p.i. The brains were immediately removed, frozen in an optimal cutting temperature compound, and then cut into 15-μm-thick sections. The brain sections were exposed to phosphorus plates for 12 h and scanned by a storage phosphor system. Thioflavin-S staining of the same brain sections were conducted to verify the labeling of Aβ plaques by [99mTc]15.

    In Vivo SPECT/CT Imaging in Rhesus Monkeys

    A rhesus monkey was anesthetized and positioned on the bed of SPECT/CT equipment (Discovery NM/CT 670 system, GE Healthcare). A sealed plastic tube containing [99mTc]15 (20 μCi, 0.2% ID) as a radioactivity reference was attached near the head. After intravenous administration of [99mTc]15 (10 mCi, 10% ethanol, 2 mL), the scan protocol started immediately. Head scanning (6 × 10 min) and a whole-body scan (approximately 20 min) were successively performed. Regions of the brain were manually drawn on each CT image on the sagittal view and transferred to the corresponding SPECT images. The brain uptake value was obtained by comparing the total counts of all the brain regions to the counts of the radioactivity reference, expressed as %ID.
