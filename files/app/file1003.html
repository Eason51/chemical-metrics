    Abstract

    Notch is a membrane inserted protein activated by the membrane-inserted γ-secretase proteolytic complex. The Notch pathway is a potential therapeutic target for the treatment of renal diseases but also controls the function of other cells, requiring cell-targeting of Notch antagonists. Toward selective targeting, we have developed the γ-secretase inhibitor-based prodrugs 13a and 15a as substrates for γ-glutamyltranspeptidase (γ-GT) and/or γ-glutamylcyclotransferase (γ-GCT) as well as aminopeptidase A (APA), which are overexpressed in renal diseases, and have evaluated them in experimental in vitro and in vivo models. In nondiseased mice, the cleavage product from Ac-γ-Glu-γ-secretase inhibitor prodrug 13a (γ-GT-targeting and γ-GCT-targeting) but not from Ac-α-Glu-γ-secretase inhibitor prodrug 15a (APA-targeting) accumulated in kidneys when compared to blood and liver. Potential nephroprotective effects of the γ-secretase inhibitor targeted prodrugs were investigated in vivo in a mouse model of acute kidney injury, demonstrating that the expression of Notch1 and cleaved Notch1 could be selectively down-regulated upon treatment with the Ac-γ-Glu-γ-secretase-inhibitor 13a.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Recent advanced knowledge on renal diseases has yielded several candidate pathways for designing cell-targeted therapeutics, which include the Notch pathway.(1-8) Within the kidney, injury to glomerular or tubular cells is the initiating cause of many acute and chronic diseases, leading to progressive dysfunction and end-stage renal disease. The glomerulus is the main filtration barrier that determines global kidney function. Inflammatory and noninflammatory stresses affect the glomerulus and lead to alterations in its structure, thus in its permeability and function, leading to chronic kidney disease (CKD). Injury to the tubulointerstitial tissue is a major cause of acute kidney injury (AKI) in particular in weakened hospitalized patients.
    Notch is a membrane-inserted protein. Activation of Notch signaling occurs following Notch receptor–ligand interaction and subsequent enzymatic proteolysis by the γ-secretase complex, resulting in the cytoplasmic release of Notch intracellular domain (NICD) which translocates into the nucleus to initiate the downstream transcriptional machinery. The enzyme γ-secretase is a large protease complex composed of two aspartyl protease catalytic subunits (presenilin-1 and -2) and three support subunits (Pen-2, Aph-1, and nicastrin), all being membrane proteins. Substrates of γ-secretase first bind to nicastrin, then are transferred between the two presenilin subunits to the γ-secretase which performs an intramembrane hydrolysis.(9) The γ-secretase complex is able to activate Notch by hydrolyzing a peptide bond of the Notch protein at an intramembrane site, allowing the cleaved Notch intracellular domain to migrate to the nucleus where it activates responsive genes.(10) The intramembrane activity of the γ-secretase has also been involved in the release from the membrane of other biologically relevant membrane proteins involved in normal and pathological processes, including insulin-like growth factor or sorting receptors.(11, 12) Therefore, for selective therapy, it is mandatory to achieve only localized inhibition of this activity, thus protecting the other functions of this enzyme and of the Notch pathway in particular. An important limitation for the clinical use of γ-secretase inhibitors is the nonspecific inhibition of the pleiotropic Notch pathway in multiple cells.(13) Indeed, active Notch signaling plays a critical role in many fundamental processes in the homeostasis of normal cells, including cell proliferation, apoptosis, and activation of differentiation programs. This is particularly important for high turnover cells such as in the gut mucosa and hematopoietic cells,(14) as illustrated by diarrhea being a frequent side effect of γ-secretase inhibitors. Moreover, recent data have highlighted that Notch signaling could confer tumor suppressive functions in specific organs such as the liver, hematopoietic stem cells, and the skin; therefore, nontargeted inhibition could result in cancers in these tissues.(15, 16)
    The Notch pathway has been reported to participate in renal diseases and tissue damage in the kidney,(1-8) thus being a potential target to treat renal diseases. However, as the γ-secretase and the Notch pathway are also important in controlling the function of other cells, including normal cells, it is necessary to develop tools for specifically targeting Notch antagonists to diseased kidney cells.(17-20) We and others(21-29) have previously shown that the activities of the peptidases aminopeptidase A (APA) and γ-glutamyltranspeptidase (γ-GT) are highly increased in tissues from patients and rodent experimental models of human diseases, including kidney diseases (Figure 1), suggesting that these enzymes may be used for drug targeting purposes. Thus, in the present project, γ-secretase-based prodrugs were developed as potential substrates for γ-GT and APA and were evaluated first in in vitro cellular assays and then in mice. While the in vitro results showed limited predictability, the data obtained in mice demonstrated that it was possible to identify a γ-Glu prodrug but not an α-Glu prodrug that was selectively activated to form the active γ-secretase inhibitor in the kidney but not in liver and plasma. To investigate potential beneficial nephroprotective effects of targeting Notch with this γ-secretase-inhibiting γ-GT-targeted prodrug, we used an in vivo mouse model of aristolochic acid (AA)-induced acute kidney damage. Immunohistochemistry performed on kidneys from these experimental mice demonstrated an up-regulation of the expression of Notch1 and its cleaved form, which could be selectively down-regulated following administration of the Ac-γ-Glu-γ-secretase-inhibitor prodrug.

    Chemistry

    Our previously unpublished observations suggested that the enzymatic activities of APA and γ-GT were increased in human diseased kidneys (Figure 1). We therefore designed prodrugs of a γ-secretase inhibitor as substrates for these enzymes. From a number of potential γ-secretase inhibitors with functional groups amenable to attaching prodrug targeting groups, we have selected SecrI (8)(30) as a promising candidate. 8 is offering high potency, metabolic robustness, and several potential positions for derivatization as well as an accompanying SAR suggesting that the prodrugs would have strongly decreased Notch activity. The synthesis of the SecrI (8) has been previously described;(30) however, a chiral separation was needed and the absolute configuration of the malonamide center was not reported. We have now resynthesized 8 starting from the known chiral malonic half-ester 1(31) (Scheme 1) and thereby clarified the absolute configuration. Standard peptide coupling followed by catalytic hydrogenation and saponification with lithium hydroxide afforded 4. The oxazepine 7 has been prepared from 5(30) via alkylation with benzyl 2-bromoethyl ether followed by Boc-removal with TFA. Peptide coupling of 4 with 7 and subsequent catalytic hydrogenation afforded 8 as a single isomer with (S)-configuration at the malonamide center.
    The most obvious way for attaching prodrug groups was the two alcohols in 8. Chemically most accessible was the primary alcohol. Because esters are not stable in plasma, we decided to build a carbamate linked amine 11 (amine-SecrI). The synthesis was straightforward (Scheme 2). Reaction with bis(4-nitrophenyl)carbonate gave carbonate 9 in 90% yield, then the mono BOC protected ethylenediamine could be attached in 93% yield to give 10. Deprotection with HCl in dioxane gave the amine-SecrI (11), allowing attachment of the N-protected and free α- and γ-glutamyl substrates. Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) was synthesized in two steps with Ac-Glu(OSu)-OBzl followed by hydrogenation of the benzyl ester (Scheme 2). The nonprotected γ-glutamyl prodrug, H-γ-Glu-SecrI (13b) was synthesized in two steps with BOC-Glu-OtBu and EDCI coupling followed by HCl deprotection in total of 82% yield (Scheme 2). The same amine building block 11 was used for the synthesis of α-Glu-prodrugs 15a and 15b. EDCI coupling of amine 11 with Ac-Glu(OtBu)-OH in the presence of 1-hydroxy-7-azabenzotriazole gave amide 14a in quantitative yield. Ester deprotection with HCl in dioxane gave Ac-α-Glu-SecrI (15a) in 81% yield. H-α-Glu-SecrI (15b) was synthesized the same way with BOC-Glu(OtBu)-OH coupling in quantitative yield followed by double deprotection to give H-α-Glu-SecrI (15b) in 80% yield (Scheme 3). The chemical structures and the enzymatic activities required for releasing active drugs are summarized in Scheme 4.

    Biology

    The γ-secretase inhibitor approach of this project is based on families of selective molecules previously developed at F. Hoffmann-LaRoche for targeting the Notch signaling pathway.(30, 32) On the basis of these previous synthetic compounds, free amino acid prodrugs and N-acetylamino acid prodrugs of the γ-secretase inhibitor separated by a linker were prepared as selective prodrug substrates for APA and γ-GT, since these enzymatic activities are highly expressed in diseased human kidneys (Figure 1). The N-acetyl group approach was chosen for fine-tuning the stability and prolonging the biological half-lives of our compounds. Several previous publications have demonstrated that the kidney is highly active in the accumulation and metabolism of N-acetyl-γ-glutamyl derivatives of drugs(33-38) showing a significantly increased kidney/liver distribution. This property was attributed to the high concentration of γ-GT, a specific and not completely identified transporter in the kidney,(34) and the intracellular N-acylamine acid deacylase (ACY1)(33) and γ-glutamylcyclotransferase,(34, 39) altogether able to hydrolyze N-acetyl-γ-glutamyl derivatives of prodrugs, thus releasing the free drugs. The N-acetyl strategy had never been attempted for prodrug substrates for APA. However, from the known selectivity of this extracellular peptidase,(21, 22, 40-42) it can be expected that the N-acetyl derivatives are not substrates for this enzyme, again requiring an intracellular transporter, a deacetylation step, and a re-exporter, while a free N-terminal α-Glu-SecrI would be a substrate for this peptidase.

    In Vitro Results

    A first screen of each compound was performed in vitro to determine cleavage rates, and selective inhibition was verified by using fresh frozen mouse kidney sections ex vivo for enzymatic assays. Extracellular release of the γ-secretase inhibitor from prodrugs by human kidney cells (HEK293, HEK293-derived Notch-reporter 215/F2 cells(32, 43) and HK2 cells) as well as the rat brain-derived EC219 endothelial cells which express very high levels of APA and γ-GT activities(44) were evaluated for liberating active inhibitors SecrI (8) or amine-SecrI (11) (Figure 2A). The EC219 and HK2 cells, but not the HEK293 cells, were able to release the amine-SecrI (11) from the H-α-Glu-SecrI (15b) into the extracellular space, but not the free inhibitor 8, in a pattern involving APA activity. As expected, the Ac-α-Glu (15a) derivative was not a substrate for this enzyme and the Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) derivative was not hydrolyzed by γ-GT, since we postulated the need for an intracellular acylase to prepare the substrate for the extracellular APA and γ-GT cell surface activities. But more surprisingly, the free H-γ-Glu-SecrI (15a) was also not hydrolyzed by γ-GT, supporting the hypothesis of Drieman et al.(34) of the involvement of a specific Ac-γ-Glu transmembrane transporter to the intracellular γ-Glu-cyclotranferase (γ-GCT), widely expressed, including in the kidney.(39) To confirm that the Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) may be a substrate for relevant cellular enzymatic activities, we determined if the Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) prodrug may inhibit the hydrolysis of α-Glu-naphthylamide and γ-Glu-naphthylamide derivatives by enzymohistochemistry, APA activity as a negative enzymatic control, and amine-SecrI (11) as a drug for negative control in ex vivo kidneys of nondiseased mice (Figure 2B). Only the Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a), but not the Ac-α-Glu-SecrI (15a) or the amine-SecrI (11), was able to inhibit γ-GT-like activity, while APA activity was not inhibited by any compound. Thus, these results clearly showed that the Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) prodrug inhibited the extracelluar activity of γ-GT and/or the intracellular γ-GCT activity, suggesting that the release of the active inhibitor is performed inside cells. Finally, the requirement of proteolytic activation of the prodrugs to inhibit Notch cleavage by the γ-secretase was investigated using a reporter assay (Table 1). The DR215/F2 cell line expresses a reporter construct designed to monitor the activity of the Notch signal transduction pathway. The DR215/F2 cell line(43) carries two transgenes: the firefly luciferase reporter gene expressed under the control of the Gal4 promoter and the human Notch1 activator gene fused with the yeast Gal4 transcription factor. This assay confirmed that the prodrugs were able to inhibit γ-secretase and the activation of the Notch pathway. The DR215/F2 cell reporter assay was also used to determine the IC50 for blockade of the Notch pathway by all the compounds designed and prepared. The free γ-secretase inhibitor (8) and the amine-γ-SecrI (11) demonstrated inhibition in the low nanomolar range, whereas the nonhydrolyzed prodrugs were much less active, displaying IC50 values in the high nM range for the Ac-α-Glu-SecrI (15a) and the H-α-Glu-SecrI (15b) and even in the μM range for the H-γ-Glu-SecrI (13b) and Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) (Table 1).

    In Vivo Results

    Pharmacokinetic Assessment in C57BL/6 Healthy Wild-Type Mice

    We next determined the stability, tissue distribution, and cleavage rate in plasma, liver, and kidney of the designed prodrugs in nondiseased mice. In the in vivo screen, the kinetics and distribution of γ-secretase inhibitor prodrugs Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a), Ac-α-Glu-SecrI (15a), and H-α-Glu-SecrI (15b) were compared with the active γ-secretase inhibitor amine-SecrI (11). After ip administration (10 mg/kg) in C57BL/6 mice, the disappearance of the prodrugs and the formation of the active amine-SecrI (11) were compared in plasma, liver, and kidney (Table 2). First, we determined the pharmacokinetics (PK) behavior of amine-SecrI (11) following ip administration (Table 2A). As expected, amine-SecrI (11) had very low plasma exposure, indicating rapid plasma clearance. Liver and kidney exposure was much higher, with approximately 4.5-fold higher exposure in liver compared to kidney. As expected for the prodrug Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a), we saw at 0.5 h a high conversion to amine-SecrI (11) which was essentially fully kidney-selective with no detectable plasma concentrations (Table 2B). The selectivity toward kidney appeared to decrease over time, probably due to fast clearance and re-equilibration of the amine-SecrI (11). However, the high selectivity at earlier time-points ensured that the total exposure over time was much higher in the kidney compared to the liver. The Ac-α-Glu-SecrI (15a) prodrug was converted to a much lower extent than the Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) to amine-SecrI (11) and more in the liver (400%) than in the kidney (Table 2C). The H-α-Glu-SecrI (15b) showed the overall highest conversion to amine-SecrI (11) but again more efficiently in the liver (700% at 0.5 h) than in the kidney (Table 2D). In summary, these results clearly demonstrated that the low plasma exposure of Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) was due to a rapid uptake into liver and kidney followed by kidney-selective hydrolysis to form amine-SecrI (11) rather than direct hydrolysis in plasma, since amine-SecrI (11) stayed at all time-points below the limit of quantitation in plasma. We never observed significant amounts of hydrolysis of the linker in 11 to the free antagonist 8. On the basis of these results, the Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) was selected as the best candidate for a proof of concept study.

    Effects of Amine-SecrI (11) and Its Prodrug Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) in a Mouse Model of Acute Kidney Injury

    To investigate potential nephroprotective effects of Notch blockade using γ-secretase inhibition and to evaluate the effects of our enzyme-targeted prodrug strategy, we used the in vivo AA mouse model of acute renal tubular damage.(45, 46) AA is a natural herbal component which is toxic to renal tubular epithelial cells, inducing apoptosis with impaired regeneration and autophagy of proximal tubular epithelial cells leading to progressive tubular atrophy and irreversible renal failure. The amine-SecrI (11) which showed high kidney exposure in the single dose administration (Table 2A) was administered at 10 mg/kg b.i.d., and its prodrug Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) was administered at 30 mg/kg b.i.d., with treatment starting 1 day prior to the injection of a single dose of AA ip. We chose to administer the prodrug 13a at a higher dose compared to amine-SecrI (11) to account for the relative differences in kidney exposures achieved for active amine-SecrI (11) during single dose PK (Table 2 and description above). At day 7, 13 h after last prodrug administration, the animals were sacrificed and plasma, liver, and kidney samples collected for exposure determination (Table 3) as well as immunohistochemistry (Figure 3) for the evaluation of Notch1 and cleaved Notch1. We detected high exposure of kidney and liver with amine-SecrI (11) even 13 h after administration (Table 3A) and a comparatively lower exposure of amine-SecrI (11) in the Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) group (Table 3B and Table 3C), which confirmed our dose selections as predicted from the PK experiments (also compare with 7 h time-points in Table 2). There was a potential selectivity for kidney exposure in Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a)-treated animals only (Table 3B), however difficult to ascertain due to the late time-point. We also observed a small selectivity in the AA- and Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a)-treated animals (Table 3C), suggesting that the effect of prodrug cleavage was still working in diseased kidneys. In the immunohistochemistry evaluation (Figure 3), we observed up-regulation of Notch1 and cleaved Notch1 in diseased kidneys of AA-treated mice in the absence of the prodrugs, which were reduced in AA-exposed and 11-treated or 13a-treated mice (Figure 3), suggesting a beneficial effect of the N-acylase-γ-GT-targeted γ-secretase prodrug in controlling the activation of the Notch pathway in acute kidney injury. Neither 11 nor 13a injected alone induced any modification of Notch or cleaved Notch levels in the kidneys of healthy wild-type mice (results not shown). Overall, these experiments demonstrated that the N-acetyl-γ-glutamyl prodrug 13a was cleaved to the amine-SecrI (11) but not to the free γ-secretase inhibitor 8 in vivo, was kidney-selective, and was active after cleavage in vivo by enzyme(s) expressed in acutely injured renal tubular cells to control the activation of the Notch pathway. Importantly, no overt side effects such as diarrhea or macroscopic changes in vital organs (heart, lung, liver, spleen, gut) were observed in the treated groups. Standard liver toxicity assays (liver enzymes and histology) have shown that neither compound 11 nor compound 13a displayed toxicity (data not shown).

    Discussion and Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    AKI is a risk factor for progressive CKD, a serious clinical condition with no effective treatment, associated with patient’s high morbidity including cardiovascular and metabolic complications. Kidney diseases can be primary or occur as part of multisystemic genetic, inflammatory, autoimmune, toxic, or metabolic disorders. Three main structures can be affected in the kidney, the glomerulus, the tubulointerstitial tissue, or the vessels, leading to distinct clinical syndromes in the early stages of the disease. However, irrespective to the initial insult, disease progression will lead to irreversible glomerulosclerosis and tubulointerstitial fibrosis, thus eventual end-stage renal disease requiring dialysis or kidney transplantation. Current therapies mainly aim at treating the primary disease to limit CKD progression. Previous studies have found that genes belonging to the Notch pathway were up-regulated in kidney samples from patients and in animal models of renal disease.(1, 3, 4, 6-8) Genetic studies performed in mice with conditional expression of the active Notch1 protein showed massive glomerulosclerosis, leading ultimately to renal failure and death of the animals. Genetic deletion of Notch transcriptional binding partner as well as treatment with γ-secretase inhibitors, thus inhibiting the γ-secretase-mediated Notch activation and translocation to the nucleus, protected the animals from nephrotic syndrome. Thus, targeted pharmacologic inhibition of the Notch pathway signaling may prevent kidney damage in a variety of diseases. Regulation of the Notch pathway signaling mainly occurs at the levels of ligand binding and γ-secretase complex-mediated cleavage. Then, cleaved Notch (NICD) migrates to the nucleus where it activates responsive genes, which include Notch1 itself. The strategy proposed here aimed to locally increase the concentration of γ-secretase inhibitors as Notch antagonists in the kidney by preparing prodrugs of γ-secretase inhibitor(s) coupled to substrates for selective hydrolytic enzyme activities expressed at high levels by specific cells in injured kidneys. Liberation of SecrI (8) was not achieved, as the intended cyclization of the 2-aminoethylcarbamate (11) did not take place. We could however demonstrate in vivo that the potent γ-secretase inhibitor amine-SecrI (11) was selectively liberated in the kidney. Furthermore our results in a mouse model of acute kidney tubulointerstitial disease demonstrated that the amine-SecrI (11) selectively liberated from prodrug N-Ac-γ-Glu-SecrI (13a) in the kidney exerted a regulatory effect on the Notch pathway.
    Prodrug strategies are complex, while potentially selective and efficient systems. Small peptides are rapidly trimmed from their N-terminus in the plasma; therefore, in order to protect N-terminal glutamyl, we used a strategy blocking the N-terminal with an acetyl group. For the family of N-protected compounds targeting the γ-secretase-Notch pathway, the strategy proposed in the project confirmed the interest of using Ac-γ-Glu prodrugs of a γ-secretase inhibitor targeting the γ-GT and/or γ-GCT, but not Ac-α-Glu prodrugs targeting APA and the angiotensin system, to control the activation of the Notch pathway in the context of kidney disease. We could show that such an approach was able to improve the stability of the therapeutics and the selectivity for the diseased kidney of the designed prodrugs potentially opening the way for improving treatment and decreasing side effects of therapies aimed at controlling the Notch pathway for patients with acute and chronic kidney disorders.

    Preparation of the γ-Secretase Inhibitor Analogues for in Vitro and in Vivo Experiments

    The compounds were dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) at 10 mM stock solution, aliquoted, and stored at −4 °C until use, then diluted in PBS or cell culture medium. In the experimental settings, controls containing increasing concentrations of DMSO corresponding to the amount of DMSO of the diluted compounds were always included. For in vivo experiments, the compounds were dissolved in 0.9% NaCl, incubated 30 min at 40 °C, and sonicated for 5 min.

    Animal Model of Induced Acute Kidney Disease

    All experiments with animals were conducted in accordance with federal and local regulation at the respective study location. Kidney injury was induced by intraperitoneal (ip) injection of aristolochic acid (AA, Sigma-Aldrich, 1 × 5 mg/kg) in 10 week old BALB/c male mice. The γ-secretase inhibitors prodrugs were diluted in NaCl 0.9% and administered ip, starting 1 day before the injection of AA (day – 1) and then twice daily until day 6 evening (n = 5 mice/experimental group). At day 7 morning (13 h after the last prodrug injection), blood was collected and the mice were sacrificed to remove the liver and both kidneys. The kidneys were cut in four fragments. One fragment was immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, one fragment was included in OCT (Tissue-Tek, VWR International, Switzerland) and frozen, one fragment was frozen at −80 °C, and one fragment was fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde and included in paraffin.

    Histoenzymography

    Evaluation by histoenzymography of APA and γ-GT enzymatic activities on frozen tissue was performed as previously described.(29) Briefly, OCT-embedded frozen kidneys were cut in 7 μm sections in a cryostat, fixed for 5 min in cold (−20 °C) acetone, air-dried at rt, and rehydrated for 5 min in 0.9% NaCl. Naphthylamide substrates were obtained from commercial sources (α-Glu-4-methoxy-β-naphtylamide (APA) from Bachem; and γ-Glu-4-methoxy-β-naphtylamide (γ-GT) from Serva). Substrates (1 mg) were dissolved in 1 mL of DMF, and 2 mg of Fast Blue B (Sigma-Aldrich) was dissolved in 2 mL of 0.2 M cacodylate buffer, pH 7.4, containing 700 mg/L CaCl2; then both solutions were slowly mixed immediately before use and the pH was adjusted to 6.5–7.0. The solution was filtered if some precipitation occurred, added to the slides, and incubated in a humidified atmosphere at 37 °C for 15–60 min until a red coloration was visible. The slides were washed in distilled water, counterstained with hematoxylin (Mayer) for 1 min, rinsed under tap water, then with Scott solution for 1 min, rinsed under tap water, mounted in Aquamount (Immu-mount, Thermo Shandon Pittsburgh, PA, USA), and analyzed with a Nikon Eclipse E800 microscope and digital DXM1200 camera using ACT-1 software.

    Cells and in Vitro Assays

    The preparation and characterization of rat brain-derived EC219 endothelial cells have been previously described.(44, 46) These cells express high levels of APA and γ-GT activities and can be used for determining kinetics in living cells(44, 46) and were chosen as APA- and γ-GT-positive representative cell models. EC219 cells were grown in DMEM medium containing 1 g/L glucose, 10% heat-inactivated fetal calf serum (FCS, EuroClone, U.K.) supplemented with 100 U/mL penicillin and 100 μg/mL streptomycin (P/S) on uncoated cell culture plastic ware. The HEK293 and HK2 human kidney cells are available from the ATCC (American Tissue Culture Collection, Manassas, VA, USA). HEK293 cells were grown on uncoated cell culture plastic ware in Iscove modified Dulbecco medium (IMDM, Life Technologies) containing 10% FCS and P/S. HK2 cells were grown on uncoated cell culture plastic ware in DMEM/F-12 GlutaMAX medium (Gibco) containing 5% FCS, P/S, 1% ITS (1.0 mg/mL bovine insulin, 0.55 mg/mL human transferrin, and 0.5 μg/mL sodium selenite, Sigma-Aldrich), and 5 ng/mL hydrocortisone. The human HEK293-derived DR215/F2 embryonic kidney cell line, stably transfected with pFR Luc and APP-Notch Gal (plasmid DR215),(30, 43) was maintained using IMDM with 10% FCS supplemented with P/S and kept under double-selection with 150 μg/mLl G-418 and 50 μg/mL hygromycin B on coated (1:100 poly-l-lysine) cell culture plastic ware. The use of the DR215/F2 cell line for reporting Notch activation has been described.(32, 43) In this assay, the Notch-Gal4 protein is bound to the cell membrane via its transmembrane domain. The Notch intracellular domain is released by proteolytic cleavage mediated by the γ-secretase and moves to the cell nucleus where it activates the reporter luciferase gene transcript. Inhibition of γ-secretase thus decreases luciferase activity. Briefly, cultures were grown in 96-well microplates to 80% confluence; the cells were then washed in PBS and exposed to decreasing concentrations of the γ-secretase-inhibitor analogues in complete IMDM culture medium without red phenol at 37 °C for 19 h. Then 75 μL of supernatant from each well was removed and the luciferase activity was determined by adding 25 μL of Steady-Glo luciferase assay reagent (Promega). After incubation at rt (180 min for the IC50 determination, 20 min for the evaluation of Notch activation), the luminescence of individual microwells was measured in a microplate luminometer (SpectraMax Paradigm, Molecular Devices) or Synergy Mx (Monochromator, Biotek), for IC50 and Notch activation, respectively, and the values were analyzed using the Graphpad software.
