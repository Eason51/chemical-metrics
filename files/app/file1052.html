    Abstract

    Anthracyclines are a class of antitumor compounds that are successful and widely used but suffer from cardiotoxicity and acquired tumor resistance. Formaldehyde interacts with anthracyclines to enhance antitumor efficacy, bypass resistance mechanisms, improve the therapeutic profile, and change the mechanism of action from a topoisomerase II poison to a DNA cross-linker. Contrary to current dogma, we show that both efficient DNA cross-linking and potent synergy in combination with formaldehyde correlate with the anthracycline’s ability to form cyclic formaldehyde conjugates as oxazolidine moieties and that the cyclic conjugates are better cross-linking agents and cytotoxins than acyclic conjugates. We also provide evidence that suggests that the oxazolidine forms in situ, since cotreatment with doxorubicin and formaldehyde is highly cytotoxic to dox-resistant tumor cell lines, and that this benefit is absent in combinations of formaldehyde and epirubicin, which cannot form stable oxazolidines. These results have potential clinical implications in the active field of anthracycline prodrug design and development.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The anthracycline class of antineoplastic agents is represented in current chemotherapy protocols by four compounds: doxorubicin (dox, 1), daunorubicin (dau, 2), idarubicin (ida, 3), and epirubicin (epi, 4) (Chart 1). The class shares the structural features of a tetracyclic anthraquinone ring system coupled to an amino sugar (daunosamine) via a glycosidic linkage. Structural differences between the agents are minimal; ida differs from dau by modification at the 4-position, dox is equivalent to dau in all respects except for a hydroxyl moiety at the 14-position, and epi and dox differ only in the stereochemistry of the hydroxyl group at the 4′-location in the daunosamine.
    Clinically, the anthracyclines are some of the most successful anticancer drugs ever developed, with dox being the most commonly used. Applications are wide-ranging and the compounds are used across a broad spectrum of both solid (carcinomas and sarcomas) and nonsolid tumors.(1) The accepted mechanism of action derives from interaction with topoisomerase II (topo II) through formation of a stable ternary complex of cut DNA–drug–topo II. The religation of the DNA is inhibited by the presence of the drug, resulting in double-strand DNA breaks.(2-4) Other modes of action have been demonstrated, though their contributions to the clinical effectiveness of the drugs are unclear.(5-8) Successful treatment with anthracyclines is hindered most significantly by both acute and chronic cardiotoxicity, leading to congestive heart failure in the long term.(9-11) Furthermore, anthracyclines are susceptible to the development of resistance by a variety of mechanisms, the most relevant of which results from P170 glycoprotein (P170gp)-mediated export of the drug from the cell.(12-14)
    A growing body of evidence points toward the conclusion that anthracyclines may be activated to greater potencies either by coadministration with formaldehyde-releasing compounds or by inclusion of formaldehyde in the structure. In recent years, we have synthesized and characterized several compounds falling into the latter category, the most relevant of which is doxazolidine (doxaz, 5).(15) The oxazolidine moiety results from reaction of formaldehyde with the cis configuration of the vicinyl aminol on daunosamine to form a geminyl aminol (6a), which then ring-closes either directly or via the Schiff base (6b, Scheme 1, top). Doxaz is 1–4 orders of magnitude more potent than dox against a broad spectrum of human cancer cell lines, including those that are resistant to dox.(15, 16) This gain in potency is achieved without a corresponding increase in toxicity toward cardiomyocytes, resulting in greatly increased therapeutic potential. Reaction with an additional equivalent of formaldehyde results in pseudodimerization of doxaz units to form doxoform (doxF, 7),(17) which is also highly toxic, likely through rapid hydrolysis to free the two units of doxaz. Corresponding structures have been hypothesized or synthesized with dau.(17) In contrast, consistent with literature precedent,(18, 19) the trans-amino alcohol found in epi does not result in stable oxazolidines upon reaction with formaldehyde but instead favors the formation of a bicyclic structure consisting of two epi units and three units of formaldehyde known as epiform (epiF, 10; Scheme 1, bottom). While epiF does display increased cytotoxicity over epi and overcomes resistance, it is far less potent than doxaz or doxF. Each of these anthracycline–formaldehyde conjugates is hydrolytically unstable to varying degrees. Doxaz and doxF are the most unstable, exhibiting half-lives of approximately 3 min.(15, 17) EpiF is more stable with respect to hydrolysis to aminols 8 and 9 (Scheme 1). The half-life under physiological conditions is approximately 2 h, and loss of the formaldehyde from 8 and 9 to form epi was not observed at pH 7.4.(20)
    The presence of formaldehyde in anthracycline treatments results in a significant modification of the mechanism of action. As stated before, the primary mechanism of action for anthracyclines is inhibition of the religation of DNA via topo II. Deficiency in this enzyme is sufficient to reduce sensitivity to dox by approximately 10-fold but results in very little change to doxaz sensitivity.(16) Mass spectrometry and other experiments from our lab and others indicate that the presence of formaldehyde enables direct DNA alkylation at 5′-GC-3′ sequences by dox,(15, 17, 21, 22) dau,(21, 23) and epi(20, 24, 25) in sensitive and resistant cell lines, thus allowing function independent of topo II. Coldwell et al.(26) have demonstrated that dox–formaldehyde conjugates can form with clinically relevant concentrations and endogenous formaldehyde, although the contribution of these cross-links to the clinical effect of dox is currently unclear. In the current model for cross-link formation with anthracycline–formaldehyde conjugates, adducts are formed by attack on the formaldehyde carbon by the 2-amino group of a tautomer of a guanine base in the minor groove of DNA (Scheme 2).(15) The “virtual cross-link” is completed by hydrogen bonding between the drug’s 9-OH and the guanine on the opposite strand.(27)
    Here, we present evidence that the oxazolidine moiety is the critical structural feature that allows highly efficient DNA cross-linking and that combinations of anthracyclines and formaldehyde are highly synergistic only when stable oxazolidine formation is possible. Our data suggest, in opposition to what has been dogmatic in anthracycline–formaldehyde interactions, that the oxazolidine is capable of forming in situ and that the significant potentiation of anthracycline activity by formaldehyde is primarily a result of that formation. Our work suggests that significant clinical benefit may be gained by combining oxazolidine-capable anthracyclines with treatments that elevate tumor formaldehyde levels, particularly with drug-resistant tumors.

    Doxazolidine Induces Sequence-Dependent, Functionally Relevant DNA Cross-Links in Living, Cellular Systems

    Previous work from our lab has demonstrated that incubation of doxaz, via the dimer doxoform, with short sequences of GC-rich DNA results in alkylation of the DNA by the drug.(17) Furthermore, results from our lab and others have shown that, under conditions in which oxidation–reduction processes allow for the production of formaldehyde, DNA-drug adducts form in a sequence-specific manner.(27-29) Much of this work, along with the crystal structure of daunorubicin covalently bound to DNA,(23) was produced in cell-free systems using isolated DNA and tightly controlled conditions. We now demonstrate widespread cross-linking activity from doxaz in a complex and dynamic cellular system utilizing the alkaline single-cell gel electrophoresis (SCGE) assay, commonly referred to as the comet assay (see Supporting Information for a cartoon). In this assay, cells are treated with the prospective genotoxic agent and then embedded in agarose, lysed, and electrophoresed under alkaline conditions. The free ends and small fragments of DNA that result from strand breaks will migrate through the gel, while large, intact DNA does not. Thus, after staining, the structures resemble comets in which the density of the stain in the tail is indicative of the extent of DNA strand breaks.
    Initially, we treated cells as adherent cultures, followed by washing, trypsinizing, counting, and diluting prior to lysis in the agarose gels. However, in this form, we found that the assay was poorly reproducible; untreated cells varied significantly in the extent of DNA damage (data not shown). Subsequent tests indicated that handling of the cells during trypsinization was of critical importance. Since we planned to extensively utilize the comet assay to test many different conditions of drug combinations, the protocol was modified to reduce experimental variability resulting from trypsinization of individual treatment groups. Instead, a large population of cells was trypsinized as a whole and then divided into treatment groups, which were treated in suspension. This had the added benefit of allowing the cells to be embedded into the gel and lysed more quickly after removal of the drugs, thereby preventing cellular repair machinery from masking any genotoxic effects.
    Using our modified comet assay protocol, we tested the genotoxic effects of dox, epi, and doxaz over 90 min of treatment in both dox-sensitive MIA PaCa-2 human pancreatic carcinoma cells and dox-resistant NCI/ADR-RES (ADR) human ovarian carcinoma cells (Figure 1 and Table 1). As expected, 100 μM dox and 100 μM epi both induced significant increases in DNA damage content (p < 0.001) for both cell lines versus untreated cells. The dox-sensitive MIA PaCa-2 cells showed a greater change upon dox treatment than the dox-resistant ADR cells (p < 0.001). In MIA PaCa-2 cells, 100 μM epi and 100 μM dox resulted in equivalent DNA damage and equal responses in the IC50 assay: 620 and 630 nM for dox and epi, respectively (Table 2). However, for ADR cells, the comet results did not directly compare to the relative toxicities of the drugs. Interestingly, epi had a greater DNA-damaging effect than dox in a 90 min treatment, but only dox displayed measurable toxicity in an IC50 assay (66 μM); the IC50 for epi was beyond the range of the experiment. When the cells were treated with 250 nM doxaz (IC50 22 nM for ADR and 48 nM for MIA PaCa-2), there was no increase in comet tail density. Even with treatment at 10 μM, a dose that corresponds to 200–500 times the IC50 value in these cell lines, there was no indication of DNA strand breaks. These results demonstrate that the equal growth-inhibitory effect of doxaz in both dox-sensitive and dox-resistant cells is accompanied by the same effect on DNA and that doxaz is mechanistically different in cells than dox. Given this equivalence and the observation that untreated ADR cells are a more uniform population, subsequent comet assays were performed primarily in this cell system.

    To further test the ability of doxaz to cross-link DNA, ADR cells were first treated with 15 mM hydrogen peroxide for 10 min to induce nonspecific DNA strand breaks. The peroxide-treated cells were then treated with dox, epi, or doxaz for 90 min. We hypothesized that cross-linking ability would be indicated by a reduction in tail staining density, as the free ends that result from peroxide treatment could potentially be bridged by the cross-linking agent and prevented from migrating through the gel.(30) Treatment of ADR cells with either 100 μM dox or epi increased comet tail density beyond that of peroxide alone (Figures 2 and 3A), further confirming their ability to induce strand breaks in chromosomes. However, upon treatment with 10 μM doxaz, the resulting comets displayed a dramatic decrease in the density of the tails relative to peroxide treatment alone (p < 0.001, Figure 3B). Taken together these data provide support for the model that doxaz does not function via induction of strand-break lesions in the cellular DNA, as do the other anthracyclines tested, but instead functions as a DNA cross-linking agent. These data are in agreement with previous results for isolated DNA(17) and show that doxaz treatment results in widespread cross-linking of DNA, which is detectable by a comet assay.
    Having established that doxaz treatment results in cross-linking of cellular DNA, we wanted to show that the lesion is sequence-specific against the background of a complex DNA sequence as well as show that the lesions have functional consequences that are distinct from dox treatment. To address this, we assessed the ability of the drugs to inhibit the activity of two restriction endonucleases against the full-length plasmid pcDNA3.1. In our previous experiments, doxF showed cross-linking ability when exposed to the short sequence (GC)4.(17) On the basis of this result, we hypothesized that functionally relevant doxaz cross-links would display greater inhibitory activity against NotI, which cuts at the GC-heavy sequence 5′-GC/GGCCGC-3′, than would dox. We further hypothesized that dox and doxaz would show similar inhibitory activity against NdeI, which cuts at the AT-rich sequence 5′-CA/TATG-3′. The experimental design is described in detail in Supporting Information and the results are shown in Figures S1–S4. We observed that dox and doxaz behaved similarly for NdeI (Figures S1 and S2) but doxaz showed far greater inhibitory activity against NotI (Figures S3 and S4) and resulted in a relatively long-term total blockage of endonuclease activity. Therefore, we conclude that, against both simple and complex DNA sequences, doxaz–DNA cross-links are formed quickly and preferentially in GC-heavy sequences and that these lesions are functionally distinct from lesions that result from exposure to dox.

    Cytotoxic Activity of Dox, but Not Epi, Is Synergistic with Formaldehyde

    As observed, treatments with either dox or epi result in increased comet tail density, which is consistent with their activities as topo II inhibitors, resulting in double-strand DNA breaks. Previously reported combinations of dox and, to a lesser extent, epi with formaldehyde-releasing prodrugs result in DNA cross-linking and synergy in cell killing assays.(25, 31, 32) Since these reports followed the discovery of doxaz, these effects were hypothesized to be due to released formaldehyde reacting with dox; however, the prodrugs also release inhibitors of chromatin remodeling enzymes, which complicates analysis of the combination. We were interested in testing the hypothesis that formaldehyde is indeed responsible for these effects but also in observing whether a significant difference existed between dox and epi, since the former can cyclize to an oxazolidine with formaldehyde while the latter cannot. To address these questions, we treated both MIA PaCa-2 and ADR cells with dox or epi in combination with various concentrations of formaldehyde (derived from formalin). Given the previous results, which indicate that doxaz very quickly cross-links DNA, we also hoped to demonstrate that synergistic effects of anthracycine–formaldehyde conjugates could be seen after treatments as short as 30 min. When treated with dox alone for 30 min (Figure 4A, black bars) or 90 min (Figure 4A, gray bars), MIA PaCa-2 cells responded with IC50 values of approximately 4 and 0.6 μM, respectively. Formaldehyde toxicity was detected only at the 90-min time point (530 μM, Table 2), while doxaz was similarly toxic at both treatment times (51 and 48 nM). When dox was combined with low doses of formaldehyde (150, 100, and 67 μM, which allow 75–100% cell survival over 90 min), the resulting IC50 values were 3.5–9.5-fold lower than for dox alone; moreover, the extent of IC50 depression was dose-dependent with respect to formaldehyde concentration. When the data were plotted as an isobologram (Figure 4B), the combinations clearly fell under the additive line, suggesting a strong synergistic interaction. This is further demonstrated by the combination index calculated for the points: CI = 0.4, where CI < 1 is synergistic, 1 is additive, and >1 is antagonistic. With 30-min treatments, increased sensitivity to the combination was observed to a lesser extent; 150 μM formaldehyde resulted in a 4-fold IC50 drop (from 3.8 to 0.95 μM) as opposed to 9.5-fold with 90-min treatments. Since formaldehyde treatment alone failed to produce a usable IC50 value after 30-min treatments, isobolograms could not be constructed; however, since there was no observed toxicity from 30 min of formaldehyde treatment, a measurable response over dox alone is likely due to synergistic effects of the combination.
    Like MIA PaCa-2 cells, ADR cells responded synergistically to combinations of dox and formaldehyde. Compared to MIA PaCa-2, however, the effects were far more pronounced, owing to the dox-resistant phenotype of ADR cells. Dox IC50 values were 220 and 66 μM for treatments of 30 and 90 min, respectively, while for formaldehyde, the values were around 600 μM for both times (Figure 4C and Table 2). When dox was combined with formaldehyde, the dox IC50 dropped 55-, 85-, and 173-fold for 67, 100, and 150 μM formaldehyde, respectively, for a 90-min treatment. The range of formaldehyde concentrations tested was extended lower (10, 25, and 50 μM), which resulted in 2.5–46-fold IC50 reduction. For 30 min, the IC50 reductions were 9–300-fold over the standard range (67–150 μM) of formaldehyde. Similar to MIA PaCa-2 cells, the 90-min combinations were profoundly synergistic at all combinations when plotted as an isobologram (Figure 4D), and the CI values ranged from 0.02 to 0.2.
    While dox–formaldehyde combinations were significantly synergistic, the same was not true for epi–formaldehyde combinations. When epi was combined with formaldehyde for 90 min in MIA PaCa-2 cells, there was no observed reduction in the IC50 over epi alone (Figure 5A); the IC50 varied between 0.5 and 1.1 μM. In an isobologram, the combinations were generally additive to antagonistic (Figure 5B). Since no effect was observed at 90 min, the shorter treatment time was not tested. In ADR cells, where a 90-min IC50 for epi was not measurable, the combinations with formaldehyde also failed to yield an IC50 (data not shown), suggesting that any interaction between epi and formaldehyde was not synergistic enough to bring toxicity into the range of the assay.
    These results demonstrate that formaldehyde can greatly increase the toxicity of anthracycline treatments over the short term but only when the anthracycline is capable of forming an oxazolidine ring. This leads to the surprising conclusion that coadministration of dox and formaldehyde is yielding an oxazolidine moiety in situ. Additionally, these experiments are potentially informative as to the location of oxazolidine formation. High levels of P170gp are expressed by ADR cells, resulting in active removal of dox from the cellular interior. As we have shown, doxaz is not sensitive to the presence of P170gp and retains its cytotoxic activity in the ADR cells. Likewise, the ability of dox–formaldehyde cotreatments to elicit a response in the ADR cells indicates that the dox–formaldehyde conjugate is insensitive to P170gp and suggests that conjugation occurs prior to entry into the cytoplasm. A possibility is that the reaction between formaldehyde and dox is an in situ reproduction of our previous bench methods for synthesis of doxaz and the dimer doxF, which utilized a biphasic system of aqueous buffer and chloroform. As doxaz and doxF were formed, which are both uncharged at physiological to slightly acidic pH, they concentrated in the chloroform, where they were protected from hydrolysis. A similar system could be in use here, where doxaz and/or doxF concentrate in the hydrophobic interior of the plasma membrane, where it would be similarly protected from hydrolysis. From the plasma membrane reservoir, the drug–formaldehyde conjugates can then diffuse out to interact with DNA.

    Dox–Formaldehyde Combinations Cross-Link DNA More Effectively Than Epi–Formaldehyde Combinations

    If the oxazolidine moiety is indeed being synthesized in situ, a comet assay should show doxaz-like cross-linking activity from the dox–formaldehyde combination. Therefore, ADR cells were treated with 15 mM hydrogen peroxide to induce DNA strand breaks, followed by single-agent treatments or various combinations of formaldehyde (100 μM), dox (5 and 100 μM), and epi (5 and 100 μM) for 90 min (Figures 6 and 7). As before, treatment with peroxide resulted in widespread damage, indicated by migration of DNA from the head to the tail (mean population of 6% DNA in the tail for control and 47% in the tail for peroxide). Formaldehyde (100 μM), owing to its own cross-linking activity, caused a small DNA shift back toward the comet head: 47% in the tail for peroxide alone versus 36% in the tail for formaldehyde and peroxide. As single agents, both 100 μM dox and 100 μM epi resulted in increased tail density. Surprisingly, both drugs (at those doses) also combined with 100 μM formaldehyde to show cross-linking activity, though the effect is significantly stronger with dox (10% in the tail) than with epi (18% in the tail). When the concentration of anthracycline was reduced to 5 μM, both drugs still showed cross-linking activity, but the differences between them were much larger. While the combination of epi and formaldehyde was just barely better than formaldehyde alone (32% vs 36% in the tail, p = 0.0258), the combination of dox and formaldehyde still exhibited profound cross-linking activity; it was significantly better than both formaldehyde alone (22% vs 36% in the tail, p < 0.001) and epi–formaldehyde (22% vs 32%, p < 0.001).
    These results further support the conclusion that the oxazolidine moiety is capable of forming in situ from combinations of dox and formaldehyde. It also suggests that the oxazolidine is far more efficient at cross-linking DNA than acyclic forms. Interestingly, when doses of the drugs are very high, cross-links can be observed with both dox and epi. We believe this indicates that both cyclic and acyclic species have cross-linking activity and that, at high concentrations, enough genomic DNA is cross-linked to make cyclic and acyclic forms appear similar. However, when the drug concentration is reduced 20-fold, the difference between the two forms is much more obvious. When challenged with lower doses of anthracyclines, only dox, with its vicinal cis-aminol, can form the oxazolidine structure and cause high levels of genomic DNA cross-linking. Previous work indicated activation of cell-death pathways, increased cytotoxicity, and cross-linking activity from cell treatments with epi and formaldehyde-releasing prodrugs.(25) Given the data here, we conclude that, with longer reaction times, we would observe such synergy and cross-linking from cotreatments of epi and formalin-derived formaldehyde. It is curious that although the IC50 experiments in ADR cells presented here had epi concentrations as high as 100 μM, the experiments failed to demonstrate measurable IC50 values despite signs of cross-linking activity. This leads to the supposition that epi–DNA adducts are less stable than dox–DNA adducts, though earlier reports also demonstrated that DNA adducts from both drugs have approximately the same stability.(25) Those experiments were performed in cell-free systems; possibly, in the complex environment of the cell, the adduct stabilities differ, by either passive mechanisms or active cellular repair. The resulting differences could be responsible for the lack of epi–DNA adducts causing cell death when short-term treatments are employed.

    Structural Analysis of Doxoform Synthesis and Hydrolysis

    The experiments presented so far provide strong evidence that oxazolidine is a more effective DNA cross-linking agent and a far more toxic species than linear formaldehyde conjugates. However, the oxazolidine and aminol moieties were not being compared in the same structural background; a better comparison would be between oxazolidine and linear conjugates of dox. Therefore, we were curious about the possibility of synthesizing, characterizing, and potentially isolating some of the intermediates of the reaction between dox and paraformaldehyde. Indeed, with epi, some years ago we partially characterized moderately stable aminol structures resulting from epiF hydrolysis.(20) If the dox aminol intermediates are stable and isolable under conditions compatible with cell culture, we could then directly address the question of toxicity and/or DNA cross-linking activity of the acyclic aminol versus the cyclic oxazolidine.
    To observe and characterize formaldehyde adducts intermediate between doxorubicin and doxazolidine, DMSO-d6 solutions of doxazolidine and/or doxoform were hydrolyzed with D2O and monitored by 500-MHz 1H NMR. In previous experiments, epiF was hydrolyzed with 95% phosphate-buffered D2O/5% DMSO-d6. However, under these conditions, samples of doxF were insoluble and precipitated. Therefore, pilot experiments were performed in which mixtures of doxaz/doxF were hydrolyzed with stepwise addition of D2O up to 20% (at which point the samples began to precipitate) and followed over time. Measurements taken soon after the addition of D2O were hard to shim and of poor quality, perhaps owing to both difficulties in mixing the sample in the NMR tube and the exothermicity during mixing of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) and D2O. After several minutes of vortexing at room temperature, the spectral quality improved greatly and indicated that the system reached an equilibrium prior to the first interpretable NMR spectrum. Subsequent spectra showed little to no change, even after multiple days, suggesting that doxF and epiF exhibit similar behavior upon hydrolysis. The equilibrium was then perturbed by the addition of more D2O. Observations in the 5′-CH3 region of the spectra (δ 1.0–1.3 ppm) demonstrated that the doublet corresponding to these protons in doxF decreased with increasing concentrations of D2O, while additional doublet signals, likely corresponding to a combination of dox, doxaz, and/or potential intermediates, increased. We also observed both pattern migration and peak broadening, which we attributed to the changing solvent properties, and we hypothesized that characterization of intermediates may be best addressed with the more stable solvent conditions of a closely monitored synthesis of doxF.
    A controlled synthesis of doxaz and doxF in the NMR tube was performed by reacting approximately 1.5 equiv of washed paraformaldehyde with dox free base in DMSO-d6. The reaction was followed by NMR, both one-dimensional (1D) 1H NMR and homonuclear two-dimensional correlation spectroscopy (COSY). After 1 h, three intermediates (ia, ib, and ic, with doublets at δ 1.16, 1.10, and 1.05 ppm, respectively) were visible in the 5′-CH3 region (Figure 8A). None of these intermediates contained the AX pattern indicative of an oxazolidine moiety in the region δ 4–5 ppm (Figure 8B) and are likely linear formaldehyde adducts similar to those in Scheme 3. The first appearance of an oxazolidine (δ 4.24 and 4.41 ppm) occurred by 3.5 h, coincident with the appearance of a fourth doublet at δ 1.19 ppm, and was assigned to doxaz. By 20 h of reaction, the composition was quite complicated and the relative intensities of the 5′-CH3 doublets had changed significantly; doxaz increased, as did a downfield doublet at δ 1.22 ppm, which corresponds to doxF. With approximately 3 days of reaction time, the complicated mixture had simplified considerably and consisted almost entirely of three species: doxF, doxaz, and an additional species with a 5′-CH3 doublet at δ 1.16 ppm that was later identified as unreacted doxorubicin. We also observed a small amount of unreacted paraformaldehyde. With additional time (7 and 14 days), the fractions of both doxaz and dox continued to slowly decline while that of doxF increased.
    Following 16 days of reaction, at which point the majority of the mixture was doxF, the reaction was reversed by addition of D2O to the NMR sample. Owing to the quickly established equilibrium we have described, spectra were taken approximately 30 min after D2O addition to allow for chemical and thermal equilibration. As before, the fraction of doxF decreased with increasing D2O (Figure 9). However, the peak patterns resulting from the hydrolysis reaction were not the same as those observed during the synthesis phase. While the 5′-CH3 doublet for doxaz increased slowly with higher D2O, surprisingly, the peaks for dox changed very little (Figure 9A). This shows that doxaz is more stable to hydrolysis than doxF and that, under these conditions, little to no hydrolysis of the oxazolidine ring was occurring. This was confirmed by observations in the δ 4–5 ppm region, which showed that while the oxazolidine signals of doxF were decreasing, the same was not true for the oxazolidine signals of doxaz (Figure 9B). Of particular interest was the very early appearance of a new 5′-CH3 doublet, which was accompanied by a new pair of coupled doublets at δ 4.51 and 4.41 ppm (the latter under the downfield doublet for the doxaz AX pattern). The combination of chemical shift and small coupling constant (4 Hz) is indicative of geminal coupling of protons on a methylene carbon between two heteroatoms in a five-membered ring(33) and therefore highly suggestive of an oxazolidine moiety. We assigned this species as the doxaz–aminol intermediate (11) between doxaz and doxF. Doxaz–aminol was not observed during the synthetic phase, and its change was greater with additional D2O than the change in doxaz. These data suggest that this structure is relatively well stabilized by the presence of D2O and perhaps that the reaction of doxaz with residual paraformaldehyde was facilitated by concentrations of D2O up to 10–12%. At the highest D2O concentration (16%), there was nearly equal (∼40%) representation of doxF and the combination of doxaz and doxaz–aminol, as indicated by the doublets in the 5′-CH3 region of the spectrum, while dox remained unchanged at 18% of total integration.
    Under these conditions, the stability of doxaz was serving as the functional end point; with each 2% increase in D2O, the magnitude of the changes was minimized and we were unable to observe any hydrolysis products downstream of doxaz. Moreover, the addition of much more D2O would have resulted in precipitation of most of the species present. Therefore, we concluded that complete characterization and isolation of a relatively pure sample of a strictly acyclic formaldehyde adduct of dox is unlikely. However, to show that additional hydrolysis of the system was still possible, trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) was added to a final concentration to 0.1%. Under these conditions, doxaz, doxaz–aminol, and doxF immediately disappeared and dox was the only detectable species remaining.
    Initially, the goal of these experiments was to characterize and, if possible, isolate an acyclic formaldehyde adduct of dox. The synthetic phase provided useful information and showed that the synthesis of doxaz and doxF from dox free base and low concentrations of paraformaldehyde proceeded in a manner that was more complicated than we have previously appreciated. Even with very low paraformaldehyde concentrations in DMSO, doxaz and doxF appear early in the reaction when the sample is still a complicated mixture also containing dox and multiple intermediates. Therefore, the acyclic aminol is not a synthetically isolable end point when DMSO is used as a solvent. In a similar manner, attempting to produce a relatively pure sample of an acyclic intermediate by hydrolyzing doxF was hampered by the combination of poor water solubility of the drugs and relative stability of doxaz and doxaz–aminol. It is interesting that the synthesis produces at least three acyclic intermediates, while the hydrolysis phase failed to show evidence of any, instead producing doxaz–aminol, which was not detectable in the synthetic phase. While it was possible that the broad peaks in the hydrolysis experiment obscured additional intermediate peaks, there was no evidence of this in the COSY spectra. From these data, we conclude that while the acyclic aminol species is a required intermediate between dox and doxaz, it is too transient to lend itself to characterization or isolation.

    Slow Release of Doxaz Is More Potent Than Slow Release of Acyclic Schiff Base Conjugates of Dox and Formaldehyde

    We have shown that there are marked differences in biological activity between dox and epi when they are combined with formaldehyde. These data are informative, but a better comparison would be between the different types of adducts within a setting of equivalent structure. The NMR experiments already presented show that any comparison of the cytotoxicity of cyclic versus acyclic formaldehyde adducts of dox must be done through indirect methods, as the acyclic adducts of dox are not isolable in a purity that allows for good biological characterization.
    Previously, our lab produced and characterized doxsaliform (doxSF, Scheme 4), a prodrug that releases the dox–formaldehyde Schiff base according to simple first-order kinetics.(34) The release is governed by a time-release mechanism deriving from elimination of a salicylamide trigger with an aqueous half-life of 57 min. A comparison of doxSF with doxaz released enzymatically at approximately the same rate is a better test of the efficacy of acyclic versus cyclic formaldehyde conjugates of anthracyclines.
    Owing to the need to synthesize and characterize otherwise unneeded doxSF specifically for this experiment, direct head-to-head comparisons were not made. Instead, the literature IC50 value for a 3-h treatment with doxSF was used.(15) To limit the effects only to those deriving from formaldehyde conjugates, the tests were conducted in ADR cells. In these cells, doxSF has an IC50 slightly more than 10-fold lower than dox (0.8 μM versus 10 μM), showing that acyclic dox–formaldehyde conjugates are more efficacious than dox alone. To release doxaz slowly, cells were treated with pig liver esterase (PLE) and the doxaz prodrug PPD (Scheme 4). Enzyme concentrations varied between 0.05 and 10 units/mL, where the definition of a unit is the amount of enzyme necessary to catalyze the conversion of 1 μM PPD per hour in a 50 μM reaction. The ADR cells express below-average levels of the primary human enzyme responsible for activating PPD (CES2) and responded to a 3-h treatment with PPD alone with an IC50 of approximately 2000 nM (Figure 10A).(35) When PPD was co-added with PLE, all combinations were 30–300-fold more potent than doxSF; the IC50 value varied between 30 and 3 nM and was dependent upon the amount of enzyme present in the treatment. Above 2.5 units/mL, the IC50 failed to decrease with increasing enzyme, most likely indicating 100% conversion of PPD to doxaz. As an additional comparison, the cell density of each treatment group was compared at 800 nM, the IC50 value for doxSF (Figure 10B). In this analysis, the combinations of PPD and PLE were 25–500-fold more potent than doxSF.
    Even at very low enzyme levels, slow release of doxaz is much more toxic than slow release of the dox Schiff base, providing strong evidence that the oxazolidine moiety confers much greater cytotoxic activity. To properly compare PPD and doxSF, it was important to assess the approximate rate of doxaz release from PPD under these enzymatic conditions. Assessing enzymatic rates with low levels of PPD is difficult due to the limit of detection in our HPLC system, which is approximately 100–200 nM, while the cells respond to much lower concentrations. We determined in pilot experiments that reproducible kinetic measurements could be performed at PPD concentrations as low as 1 μM, which was the highest concentration used in the cell experiments. Therefore, the activity of three different concentrations of PLE on 1 μM PPD was followed by observing the appearance of dox as a function of time (Figure 10C). Each data set is well fit by a first-order kinetics model (R2 values all greater than 0.993), and the half-lives are inversely proportional to the amount of enzyme added. These data indicate that PLE concentrations between 0.5 and 0.25 unit/mL will produce doxaz at a rate approximately equivalent to the release of the dox Schiff base from doxSF. Importantly, since catalytic systems increasingly exhibit first-order kinetics as substrate concentrations decrease to values much lower than KM,(36) it is likely that 0.25–0.5 unit/mL will produce half-lives in the range of 1 h for the full concentration range used in the cell experiment. These enzyme values produce IC50 values of 4.7 nM for 0.5 unit/mL and interpolate to 8–12 nM for 0.25 unit/mL, compared to 800 nM for doxSF releasing the Schiff base at the same rate.

    Conclusions

    Jump To
    There is ample evidence that the cytotoxic and DNA cross-linking activities of anthracyclines can be potentiated by formaldehyde, either by prior reaction or by combination with formaldehyde prodrugs. Of particular importance is our observation that the dox–formaldehyde conjugate doxaz is extremely potent, even in cell lines with P170gp-mediated resistance to anthracyclines. This potency is accompanied by very efficient DNA alkylation activity that results in adducts which are functionally inhibitory in a sequence-specific manner. We also provide evidence that the oxazolidine resulting from formaldehyde reacting with the vicinal cis-aminol in dox confers far greater DNA alkylation activity and cytotoxicity than formaldehyde conjugates in which the formaldehyde is carried in an acyclic form, such as the aminol or Schiff base. We were surprised, however, to observe that doxaz is produced in situ; cotreatments with dox and formaldehyde show considerably synergistic cytotoxicity and are far better at cross-linking cellular DNA than formaldehyde alone, even in P170gp-positive cells. From these data, we conclude that dox and formaldehyde are rapidly reacting in a water-excluded location, such as the hydrophobic interior of the membrane. When epi was combined with formaldehyde, we failed to observe any synergistic cell death and a dampened DNA cross-linking effect. We attribute this to the vicinal trans-aminol in the daunosamine of epi, which permits only linear formaldehyde conjugates as opposed to the oxazolidine moiety in the case of dox. In attempting to address the possibility of direct cytotoxic comparisons of cyclic versus acyclic formaldehyde adducts of dox, we demonstrated that the acyclic intermediates between dox and doxF are not viable end points in either the synthetic or hydrolytic directions, making isolation and subsequent direct treatment of cells impossible. However, we did show that when they are released from prodrugs at equal rates, the oxazolidine is far more cytotoxic than the Schiff base of dox, which is required in approximately 100-fold greater concentration to achieve an equivalent effect.
    These data make clear that the oxazolidine moiety is of critical importance for both the efficient cross-linking activity and high cytotoxicity observed in anthracycline-formaldehyde conjugates, though the exact molecular nature of that importance is still unclear. We found that the oxazolidine moiety was more stable than expected, even in the presence of modest concentrations of water. This stability and the natural affinity of the anthraquinone moiety for DNA could lead to a substantial nuclear buildup of doxaz, which then either directly cross-links DNA or hydrolyzes to the aminol or Schiff base prior to alkylation. Evidence in favor of the oxazolidine moiety as the primary structure responsible for efficient cross-linking is provided by the low potency of doxSF, which we expect has a similar nuclear buildup prior to activation due to its intact anthraquinone system.
    In moving forward with development of potential clinical applications for formaldehyde-activated anthracyclines, the questions of which species does the cross-linking and the exact kinetics of formaldehyde elimination are probably only academic in nature. It is relevant, however, that the ability of an anthracycline aminol to close to an oxazolidine is an important part of activation by formaldehyde to a highly potent drug. We have shown that another anthracycline with a vicinal cis-aminol, daunorubicin, can also be reacted with formaldehyde to form the potent conjugates daunoform and daunozolidine.(17) We predict that other anthracyclines with similar stereochemistry to daunosamine, such as idarubicin, will also exhibit great increases in potency when activated with formaldehyde or formaldehyde-releasing prodrugs.
    Interestingly, it may also be possible to sidestep the stereochemistry limitation on oxazolidine formation. Work by Crabb et al. in 1986(37) confirmed that model cis- and trans-aminols react with formaldehyde to produce oxazolidines in the case of the former but products analogous to epidoxoform in the latter. However, when the amino group of the trans isomer was alkylated to form a monomethyl group, the trans isomer was then capable of oxazolidine formation. We therefore propose that the anthracycline equivalent, N-methylepidoxorubicin, may react with formaldehyde to form N-methylepidoxazolidine. Given the results presented here, we expect this hitherto-unsynthesized compound to exhibit a selective increase in anticancer activity over cardiotoxicity and profound DNA cross-linking ability that we observe with doxazolidine. Such a result would release the stereochemical limitation on potently cytotoxic anthracycline–formaldehyde interactions.

    Single-Cell Gel Electrophoresis (Comet) Assays

    Initially, cells in log-growth phase were trypsinized and seeded into 6- or 12-well plates at a density of approximately 200 000–400 000 cells/well and allowed to adhere overnight (at least 16–18 h). Treatment then commenced the following morning, followed by trypsinization and embedding of 8000–10 000 cells/gel. However, the DNA integrity of untreated cells was highly variable and subsequent tests indicated that trypsinization time and handing of the cells in trypsin had significant effects on results, with short trypsinization and rough handling generally producing the highest levels of detectable DNA damage. Therefore, to reduce experimental variability, the following optimized protocol was adopted.
    A single population of NCI/ADR-RES or MIA PaCa-2 cells in log-growth phase was trypsinized until they detached from the plate without resorting to physical agitation. A suspension of single cells was created by gently pipetting the trypsin/cell mixture with a 1000 μL pipet. The trypsin reaction was then stopped by adding it to at least 4 volumes of complete medium. The quality of the suspension and density of the cells was checked with a hemocytometer. For analysis of drug treatments alone, the cells were diluted in medium to a concentration of 50 000 cells/mL and treated with either 100 μM dox, 100 μM epi, or doxaz at high (10 μM) and low (0.250 μM) doses for 90 min at 37 °C with periodic mixing. Control cells were treated with DMSO, which was standardized to 1% in all treatments. Following the treatment period, the cells were centrifuged in a tabletop centrifuge at 1200g for 5 min. The cell pellets were resuspended in 50 μL of PBS, from which 10 μL (8000–10 000 cells) was mixed with 90 μL of melted 0.5% agarose held at 40 °C. This mixture was quickly applied to agarose-coated glass microscope slides (coated in 1.5% agarose and dried for 1–2 h at 58 °C), coverslipped, and allowed to gel for 10–15 min at 4 °C. The coverslips were removed and the slides were placed into cold alkaline lysis buffer (2.5 M NaCl, 10 mM Tris, pH 10.5, and 100 mM Na2EDTA, with 5% DMSO and 1% Triton X-100 added immediately before use) and lysed for 2 h at 4 °C. The gels were then equilibrated in cold alkaline electrophoresis buffer (300 mM NaOH and 1 mM Na2EDTA) for 20 min at 4 °C before electrophoresis in an ice bath for 20 min at 25 V (300 mA limit). The gels and slides were carefully transferred to cold neutralization buffer (500 mM Tris, pH 7.5) for 10 min and then rinsed with three changes of cold deionized water (3–5 min each). The slides were prepared for silver staining consistent with previously described protocol.(40) The slides were fixed with 5 mM ZnSO4, 5% glycerol, and 15% acetic acid for 10 min at room temperature, then rinsed three times with deionized water and allowed to dry completely overnight. The slides were then rehydrated for 60 s and stained (5% sodium carbonate, 0.068% ammonium acetate, 0.068% silver nitrate, 0.17% silicotungstic acid, and 0.051% formaldehyde), which was typically complete in 6–10 min. The slides were dipped once in deionized water and then placed in 10% acetic acid for 10 min at room temperature. Finally, the slides were rinsed, dried, and photographed with a bright-field microscope [VistaVision (VWR Inc., Radnor, PA) equipped with a Samsung TL350 digital camera]. Scoring of grayscale images was performed with CometScore Freeware, version 1.6.1.13 (TriTek Corp, Sumerduck, VA), and typically 100–200 comets were scored per gel.
    For treatment with hydrogen peroxide to induce DNA damage, cells were suspended to a density of 325 000 cells/mL and treated with 15 μM hydrogen peroxide for 10 min at 37 °C, followed by centrifugation (1200g, 5 min). The pellet was resuspended in medium, and aliquots of 50 000 cells were removed and diluted to 1 mL with medium containing the drugs of interest for treatment. The assays continued as described.

    NMR Analysis of Synthesis and Hydrolysis

    In pilot experiments, either pure doxF or mixtures of doxF and doxaz were synthesized in chloroform as described. The chloroform was evaporated and the material was dissolved in DMSO-d6. When diluted to 5% DMSO-d6 with D2O, the drugs were insoluble. Therefore, stepwise addition of D2O to solutions of doxF and/or doxaz was utilized.
    To initially observe the effects of hydrolysis, 500 μL of 2–5 mM DMSO-d6 solutions of pure doxF or doxF/doxaz mixtures were placed into an oven-dried NMR tube. A high-quality baseline spectrum was taken at 500 MHz, after which D2O was added to a final concentration of 1% (v/v) and mixed by vortexing. Room-temperature (20 °C) spectra were taken starting immediately but were of poor quality, as indicated by broad peaks and poor shimming, until approximately 10–15 min, at which point resolution improved. Spectra continued to improve slightly until 30–45 min, after which no further gains in quality were observed. The composition of the sample, monitoring in particular the informative regions of δ 0.8–1.5 ppm (5′-CH3) and δ 4–5 ppm (oxazolidine), remained constant at constant concentrations of D2O in all interpretable spectra, including those taken after several days of room-temperature incubation. Stepwise addition of D2O up to 20% final concentration (v/v) resulted in the establishment of a new equilibrium with each increment. It was observed that, beyond 20% D2O in DMSO-d6, spectral quality decreased significantly and drug precipitation began to occur, which limited our ability to use signal integration as a means of following sample composition. At 35% D2O, nearly all the drug precipitated.

Therefore, paraformaldehyde for further experiments was rinsed three times in deuteriochloroform and filtered through a glass frit.

To a clean and oven-dried NMR tube, 3.8 μmol was added and diluted with 420 μL of DMSO-d6, for a final concentration of 9 mM. A baseline 1D 1H spectrum was acquired, along with homonuclear COSY.     Enzymatic Release of Doxaz from Pentyl PABC-Doxaz

    Pig liver esterase (PLE) as a lyophilized powder was purchased from Sigma (Milwaukee, WI) and dissolved in phosphate-buffered saline containing 25% glycerol (final concentration 9.38 mg of solid/mL). The solution was clarified by centrifugation at 10000g for 5 min at room temperature, and the supernatant was distributed into small aliquots and stored at −20 °C. The enzymatic activity of the enzyme solutions was quantified by reacting 5 volumes of enzyme solution (1, 2.5, 5, 10, and 20 μL) with 50 μM PPD(35, 41, 42) in 95% PBS/5% DMSO (final volume 200 μL) at 37 °C. Samples of 50 μL were removed at various time points and the production of dox was assessed by HPLC. All reactions eventually proceeded to 100% conversion. Rates of the reaction were taken from the initial rates (<25% conversion), and 1 unit was defined as the amount of enzyme required to convert 1 μM PPD to dox per hour under these conditions. From this assay, the enzyme stock was calculated to contain 2100 units/μL, and this stock was used for subsequent experiments.
    To determine the cytotoxicity of PPD/PLE cotreatments, the cells were subjected to IC50 experiments generally as described. Here, pig liver esterase was added to the medium in concentrations of 0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 2.5, 5, and 10 units/mL, followed by PPD in various concentrations. All treatments were performed in complete RPMI medium and a final DMSO concentration of 1%. The cells were treated for 3 h, after which the IC50 plates were treated identically to the growth inhibition experiments.
    Experiments to determine the rates of enzymatic cleavage of PPD under cell culture conditions were conducted by HPLC. Given the limit of detection in the HPLC system, initial experiments were done to determine the lowest concentration of PPD that would give quality and reproducible kinetic measurements. In duplicate, PPD (10.0, 3.2, 1.0, and 0.5 μM) was reacted with 1 unit/mL PLE in complete RPMI medium at 37 °C. Aliquots were removed at 1 and 3 h, quenched by adding an equal volume of isopropyl alcohol, and analyzed by HPLC for formation of dox. The integration parameters were set to a height cutoff of 0.085 milliabsorbance units, which represents approximately 4 times the average baseline noise variation (±0.02 milliabsorbance units). Under these conditions, 1 μM PPD was determined to be the minimum reliable concentration. Therefore, duplicate experiments were performed in which 1 μM PPD was reacted with 1.0, 0.5, and 0.25 unit/mL PLE under the same conditions to determine whether reaction rates at this concentration fit well to a first-order kinetic model and, if so, which enzyme concentration corresponded to approximately 1 h half-life. Integrated peak areas for dox were converted to fractional conversion and plotted in GraphPad Prism 5.0, and the data were fit with a single-exponential model, which resulted in good fits (R2 ≥ 0.993) in all cases.
