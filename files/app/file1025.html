    Abstract

    The metalloproteinase meprin β emerged as a current drug target for the treatment of a number of disorders, among those fibrosis, inflammatory bowel disease and Morbus Alzheimer. A major obstacle in the development of metalloprotease inhibitors is target selectivity to avoid side effects by blocking related enzymes with physiological functions. Here, we describe the structure-guided design of a novel series of compounds, based on previously reported highly active meprin β inhibitors. The bioisosteric replacement of the sulfonamide scaffold gave rise to a next generation of meprin inhibitors. Selected compounds based on this novel amine scaffold exhibit high activity against meprin β and also remarkable selectivity over related metalloproteases, i.e., matrix metalloproteases and A disintegrin and metalloproteinases.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Proteases represent the largest family of enzymes in vertebrates. In human, they are involved in the regulation of manifold physiological as well as pathophysiological processes. Thus, their inhibition has always been a valuable source for the development of novel therapeutic concepts.(1) A subfamily of proteases that draw notable attention in the past are metalloproteases. In particular, the family of metzincins containing, among others, matrix metalloproteases (MMPs) and A disintegrin and metalloproteinases (ADAMs) served as targets for drug development. However, most drug discovery programs failed caused by low selectivity of the inhibitors, leading to severe side effects. Nevertheless, a better understanding of protease biology and new opportunities in inhibitor design, e.g., nonhydroxamates or nonzinc-binding inhibitors, offer novel perspectives for the discovery of drugs in this field.(2,3)
    A family of zinc-dependent proteases closely related to the MMPs and ADAMs are the astacins. In mammals, this family includes meprin α and β, ovastacin, and BMP-1 (together with alternative splice variants and tolloid-like proteases).(4,5) The latter has already been in focus for the development of novel treatments for fibrotic disorders in the past.(6,7)
    In particular, meprin β emerged as a potential drug target in recent years. Several substrates have been identified that link meprin β activity to pathophysiological conditions and to the onset of different diseases.(8,9) Both meprins act as procollagenases and are the only known proteases that cleave the protein at the N- as well as the C-terminus.(10) This is a crucial requirement for the initiation of fibrillation and collagen formation. Hence, they are involved in the formation of connective tissue but also in the development of fibrotic disorders, i.e., lung fibrosis, scarring, and keloids, and are valuable drug targets for treating these diseases.(9,11) Furthermore, meprin β is potentially involved in the pathogenesis of inflammatory bowel disease via the processing of proinflammatory cytokines, while meprin α exerts a rather protective role.(12) Membrane bound meprin β, but not meprin α, has also been shown to cleave the amyloid-precursor protein (APP) independently from BACE1.(13−15) Thus, the release of neurotoxic Aβ-peptides renders meprin β as a new player in the amyloid cascade, potentially involved in the onset of Alzheimer’s disease.
    Nevertheless, little is known about inhibitors of meprin β. The naturally occurring actinonin and the sulfonamide N-isobutyl-N-(4-methoxyphenylsulfonyl)-glycyl hydroxamic acid (NNGH) are the first reported inhibitors with activities in the micromolar range (Figure 1).(16)
    More recently, a high-throughput screen revealed NF449 and PPNDS as very potent meprin β inhibitors.(17) Albeit these compounds are the most potent meprin inhibitors reported to date, they are rather unselective and are also inhibitors of other metalloproteases.
    Meprin β exhibits a unique substrate specificity, i.e., a high preference for acidic amino acids adjacent to the scissile bond.(18) The structural basis for this specificity is the occurrence of three arginine residues (Arg146, Arg184, Arg238) within the active site cleft, shaping the S1, S1′, and S2′ subpockets(19) (Figure 2). This creates a basic environment, suitable for the interaction with acidic amino acids of the respective substrates and inhibitors.
    On the basis of this unique architecture, we reported the first systematic study of the structure–activity relationship of meprin β-inhibitors recently.(20) Starting from NNGH, compounds 1 and 2 were identified as potent inhibitors (Figure 1). The SAR of this compound series clearly revealed that acidic moieties are crucial for inhibiting meprin β, most likely by forming ionic interactions with the above-mentioned arginines in the active site. Although compound 1 was also identified as a potent inhibitor of MMPs and ADAMs, compound 2 already exhibited a favorable selectivity profile. This could be attributed to the polar carboxylic acids that are unable to address the lipophilic S1′-pocket of MMPs. In contrast, the methoxyaryl moiety of 1 would fit within the S1′ pockets of MMPs and ADAMs. Thus, it is also found in other MMP inhibitors, e.g., NNGH. On the basis of these initial observations, we here describe the further evolutionary optimization of these sulfonamide based inhibitors with regard to potency and selectivity. A new series of compounds was thereby designed and evaluated, giving rise to a next generation of selective meprin β inhibitors.

    Compound Design

    The majority of inhibitors targeting metzincin-metalloproteases are derived from either succinyl hydroxamic acids, e.g., actinonin, Batimastat, and TAPI, or sulfonamide hydroxamic acids, e.g., NNGH.(21,22) Important for a high binding affinity of the respective compounds is the coordination of the catalytic metal ion via the hydroxamic acid moiety. Additionally, inhibitors of both scaffolds also form important hydrogen bonds to the protein backbone.(23) In case of the sulfonamide type inhibitors, this is achieved via the sulfonamide-oxygens.(24) These residues form hydrogen bonds to a loop adjacent to the active site, the so-called S-shaped loop, which connects two beta-sheets of the respective protease. The connection of the strands β3 and β4 forming the northern wall of the active site cleft creates a bulge that is involved in inhibitor and substrate binding.(25) Two exemplary structures are shown in Figure 3. The inhibitors of ADAM17 (A and D; pdb code: 2DDF) and MMP13 (B and E; pdb code: 3ZXH) are bound to the active site, forming a hydrogen bond between a carbonyl- (ADAM17) or sulfonamide-oxygen (MMP13) and the loop connecting β3 and β4, colored in yellow.
    The ability of compound 1 to address these interactions, as well, provides a rationale for the high activity of this inhibitor toward several metalloproteases(20) (Figure 4A). However, in proteases of the astacin family, this S-loop is missing or only built of very few amino acids.(25) As depicted in Figure 3C,F, the crystal structure of meprin β (pdb code: 4GWN) shows a sharp turn of this small loop. This creates an opened active site cleft in this area compared to other related enzymes of the metzincin family. However, this affects predominantly the S1′ and S2′ subsites that are essentially contributing to the substrate specificity and selectivity of the proteases. Hence, it rules out the formation of a hydrogen bond between the loop-backbone and the respective inhibitor.
    It was already stated earlier that, due to the lacking S-shaped loop, the binding of inhibitors to meprin β is restricted to side chain interactions, rather than the formation of hydrogen bonds involving the actual inhibitor scaffold.(16) Due to this missing interaction motif on the protein side, we assumed that inhibitors of meprin β might not necessarily feature the respective hydrogen bond acceptor on the inhibitor side. Thus, for meprin β inhibitors of the sulfonamide scaffold like 1 and 2, the SO2 moiety would just serve as a linker, directing the aryl-substituent into the S1′ pocket (Figure 4B). In order to remove this substructure but maintain the tetrahedral geometry of the sulfonyl function, it was replaced by a methylene group. The resulting tertiary amines are likely to resemble the bioactive conformation of the inhibitors, but rule out the formation of any hydrogen bonds involving the scaffold and the β3−β4 connecting loop (Figure 4C). Hence, one important interaction between the inhibitors and other metzincin proteases, i.e., MMPs and ADAMs, would be prohibited, leading to an improved selectivity of the compounds. Although, the remaining interactions of the inhibitors with the S1′ pocket and adjacent binding sites should enable binding to meprin β with sufficient affinity (Figure 4D).

    Chemistry

    The synthesis of the compounds was accomplished starting from Fmoc-protected amino acids 3a–o (Scheme 1). In the first step, the protected hydroxamic acid was introduced by coupling the amino acid derivatives to tritylhydroxylamine. Fmoc deprotection by means of DBU in THF yielded the respective primary amines 4a–o. For the synthesis of the asymmetrically substituted tertiary amines (6a–o), the first benzyl residue was introduced via reductive amination using either tert-butyl 3-formylbenzoate or another suitable aldehyde and sodium borohydride, yielding intermediates 5a–i. The second benzyl residue was introduced via N-alkylation using benzyl halides in the presence of triethylamine under microwave irradiation or at room temperature. Final concurrent removal of all protecting groups using trifluoroacetic acid in dichloromethane yielded the respective tertiary amines 6a–o. However, the introduction of the second benzyl residue was not possible in case of any compound derived from Cα-branched amino acids. Thus, the respective secondary amines obtained by the reaction of 4d–o with tert-butyl 3-formylbenzoate were directly subjected to the removal of protecting groups, yielding the secondary amines 7b–m. For the synthesis of the symmetric substituted tertiary amines 8a–o, 4a was alkylated with excess benzyl halide in the presence of triethylamine in DMF, either at room temperature or under microwave irradiation, followed by subsequent cleavage of the protecting groups. For compounds 8g–l, the final deprotection was carried out using boron tribromide. This enabled the simultaneous cleavage of the methoxy-protecting groups of the respective phenols and the trityl-protecting group of the hydroxamate.

    Structure–Activity Relationships

    To probe the hypothesis whether amines could serve as a bioisosteric replacement of the sulfonamide moiety and exhibit activity toward meprin β rather than binding to other metalloproteases, the activity and selectivity of the respective analogue of compound 1 was assessed in a preliminary experiment. Compound 6a proved to be a potent inhibitor of meprin β with similar activity compared to compound 1, unaffected by the SO2 → CH2 switch (Table 1). Hence, binding to meprin β was possible without the formation of a hydrogen bond involving the sulfonamide moiety, underpinning the assumed binding mode of the inhibitors within the active site of meprin β, ruling out an interaction with the S-shaped loop as found for MMP inhibitors to their targets. Furthermore, no inhibition of MMP2, 9, 13 and ADAM10 and 17 by 6a could be observed (vide infra). Albeit compound 2 was a selective inhibitor of meprin β per se,(20) the respective amine analogue 8a also exhibited similar inhibition of meprin β without affecting the other investigated proteases (Table 2). Thus, the replacement of the sulfonamide by a simple tertiary amine proved to be a successful modification, giving rise to a novel class of selective meprin inhibitors by taking advantage of the special structural features of the astacin proteases.

    To deepen the understanding of the structure–activity relationships of this inhibitor class, further amine derivatives were evaluated regarding their meprin β inhibition. First, the influence of the spacer length between the hydroxamic acid and the amine was investigated. The introduction of an ethylene or propylene spacer, creating a β-alanine- (6b) or GABA-derivative (6c), respectively, led to a decreased activity by a factor ∼10, compared to 6a (Table 1). Thus, all subsequent modifications were just investigated on the basis of the glycyl hydroxamic acid scaffold, represented by 6a. As reported earlier, the SAR of the sulfonamides revealed the necessity of acidic substituents and a preference of a meta-substituted carboxybenzyl side chain. Thus, this moiety was kept constant again, and different additional benzyl residues were introduced. The fact that the amine successfully could replace the sulfonamide without losing activity against meprin β was further corroborated by 6e, g, n, and o. These compounds exhibit virtually the same activity like their sulfonamide analogues included in the previous study.(20) However, the para-methoxy moiety of 6a could only be replaced by chlorine (6k) without losing the activity. All other modifications led to a decreased inhibition of meprin β, either by creating potential steric hindrance (6d, f, and n) or by altered, obviously unfavorable, electrostatic properties of the arylmoiety (6g, h, j, l, m).
    As already mentioned above, 8a exhibited similar potency, compared to 2. The SAR results derived from this symmetric substituted amine are listed in Table 2. Again, as already found for the sulfonamide series, a clear preference of the meta-substitution of both benzyl residues was found. The para-carboxyl substituted compound (8b) exhibited a decreased potency by a factor ∼10. The activity of the combined meta- and para-carboxyl substituted asymmetric amine 6o was decreased by a factor ∼5 compared to 8a, respectively. However, this contradicts the recent proposals that predicted a favorable activity for para-carboxyl substituted compounds as well.(26) The importance of an acidic substitution is corroborated by compounds 8c, d, e, and f. The esterification and amidation of the carboxylic acids or the introduction of a cyano-group led to a significant drop or virtually complete loss of activity. Just the introduction of a dioxolane (8f) maintained some activity in the single digit micromolar range. However, the activity of 8e and f was slightly shifted to an increased inhibition of meprin α (8e, IC50 = 1.47 ± 0.12 μM; 8f, IC50 = 0.83 ± 0.05 μM(27)).
    Because carboxylic acid derivatives often exhibit poor pharmacokinetic properties, this moiety was replaced by bioisosteric halophenols.(28,29) The introduction of a difluorophenol (8h) and a chlorofluorophenol (8i) mimicking the meta-carboxylic acid resulted in inhibitors with similar potency compared to 8a. The same was observed by replacing the para-carboxylic acid (8b) by the respective difluorophenol (8g), even though exhibiting the same decreased activity compared to 8a. Albeit the halophenols were able to replace the carboxylic acid, their reduced acidity also led to a decreased selectivity for meprin β, by significantly increasing the activity against meprin α compared to 8a (vide infra). The S1 and S2′ subpockets within the active site of meprin β are formed by arginines Arg184 and Arg146, respectively. In meprin α, both residues are replaced by tyrosines.(30) For 8a, this may lead to repulsive interactions, reflected by poor meprin α inhibition and high selectivity. These repulsive interactions might be diminished for the halophenols 8h and i, leading to increased potency against meprin α and less selectivity. Nevertheless, sufficient acidity of the phenols is important for inhibition of meprin β. Further decreasing the pKa of the phenol by deleting one fluorine leads to a decreased activity by a factor ∼50 (8k) or ∼100 (8l), respectively. The potency is also strongly decreased by moving one fluorine to the ortho position (8j), most likely caused by introducing some steric hindrance. As expected, the methylation of the phenols (8m and n) led to a drop in potency by ruling out an interaction of the acidic group with the active site arginines. More recently, −OCHF2 was reported as a potential bioisosteric replacement of a hydroxyl group, acting as a “lipophilic H-bond donor”.(31) However, the introduction of a −OCHF2 moiety in meta position of the benzyl residues (8o) resulted in a complete loss of inhibitory activity. This corroborates the importance of “true“ acidic functional groups, which cannot be replaced just by a hydrogen bond donor.
    As already mentioned in the chemistry section, we were only successful in the synthesis of tertiary amines derived from glycine, not from any Cα-branched amino acid. Hence, it was only possible to elaborate the influence of Cα-substitution on the activity of the compounds on basis of the secondary amines 7a–m (Table 3). However, the introduction of any substituent, in both R and S configurations, resulted in a significant drop of potency compared to the glycine derivative 7a, most likely caused by steric hindrance within the narrow active site cleft of meprin β, created by the bulky side chain of Arg184 forming the S1 pocket. This effect was less pronounced for the aspartate or glutamate derivatives 7k, l, and m. These acidic side chains match the substrate specificity of meprin β, and stronger ionic interactions, most likely with Arg184, might partially override the steric hindrance of the side chains.

    Docking and Putative Binding Mode

    To gain an insight into a putative binding mode of the inhibitors, compounds 6a, 8a, and 8i were docked into the active site of meprin β using GOLD.(32) The docking solutions were visually inspected with regard to a reasonable complexation of the zinc atom. Two plausible docking poses of 6a and the top ranked poses of 8a and i are shown in Figure 5. In general, the found docking poses suggest a binding of all three compounds to the S1 and S1′ pockets, formed by Arg184 and Arg238, respectively. However, a binding mode involving an interaction with the S2′ pocket was not predicted. Due to the high flexibility of the dibenzylamine moiety, which allows a flipping of the two benzyl residues within the active site, two different binding modes are assumed for 6a. The first involves an interaction of the meta-carboxylic acid with Arg238, i.e., the S1′ subsite, and a potential interaction of the para-methoxyaryl moiety with Arg184 (S1 subsite; Figure 5A). The second, flipped binding mode suggests an interaction of the carboxylate with Arg184, while the para-methoxyphenyl is directed toward the S1′ pocket (Figure 5B). In both cases, ionic interactions or charged hydrogen bonds of the carboxylic acid and the respective arginine could be assumed.
    The para-methoxyphenyl residue and the arginine on the other side could form a cation−π interaction or hydrogen bond, as well. However, reliable information based on the docking results could not be gained. Due to the flexibility of the compounds, the orientation of the asymmetric substituted derivatives within the active site remains elusive. For compound 8a, the predicted binding mode involves charged interactions targeting both arginines, the S1 (Arg184) and S1′ (Arg238) subsite (Figure 5C). The charged interaction with Arg184 could explain the high selectivity of 8a for meprin β (Supplementary Figure 1A). The respective residue in meprin α is a tyrosine (Tyr187), while the arginine forming the S1′ pocket is conserved.(30) Thus, direction of one carboxylic acid to the S1 pocket would cause repulsive forces (Supplementary Figure 1B). However, for 6a, a flipping of the benzyl moieties would enable a binding mode within the active site of meprin α with the carboxylic acid targeting the S1′ and the para-methoxyaryl moiety addressing the S1 pocket via hydrogen bonding (Supplementary Figure 1C). This may be reflected by the increased activity toward meprin α of compound 6a compared to 8a. The same ionic interactions like those predicted for 8a are also addressed by 8i (Figure 5D), although by weaker acidic phenols that do not completely rule out an interaction with the tyrosine in meprin α. In addition to the predicted charged interactions of the carboxylates and phenolates with Arg184 and Arg238, the aryl moieties of 8a and i addressing the S1′ pocket could be fixed via a hydrogen bond or OH–Cl interaction within this subsite involving Ser212.

    Selectivity and Preliminary ADME Properties

    The aim of the present study was to optimize the recently reported sulfonamide derived meprin β inhibitors regarding their selectivity toward other related metalloproteases. Compound 1 was found to be not only a potent meprin β inhibitor(20) but also exhibited high activity on selected MMPs and ADAMs. However, the structure-guided design of the novel tertiary amine scaffold revealed a remarkable improvement of compound selectivity. Compound 6a, the direct analogue of 1, does not exhibit significant inhibition of the studied proteases. Even at a compound concentration of 200 μM, the inhibition of MMP2, 9, 13 and ADAM10 and 17 is only modest, and no reduction to half-maximal enzyme activity was observed, respectively (Table 4). Thus, compared to 1, compound 6a was more selective. As mentioned above, the inhibition of meprin β is not affected by the replacement of the sulfonamide by the methylene group. Furthermore, the selectivity over meprin α is similar to 1 with a factor ∼40. The same selectivity profile within the tested protease panel was observed for the remaining evaluated compounds 6k, 8a, 8h, and 8i. None of the other investigated proteases were significantly inhibited (for comparison: the meprin α IC50 reported for 8a corresponds to a residual enzyme activity of <10% @ 200 μM inhibitor). Furthermore, only moderate inhibition of cytochromes 3A4 or 2C9 was observed (Table 4). Hence, the tertiary amine hydroxamic acid scaffold reported here seems to be a valuable basis for the discovery of novel inhibitors of meprin or astacin metalloproteases in general.

    However, as already observed for the sulfonamides, the highest selectivity for meprin β over meprin α is achieved by the introduction of two meta-carboxylic acids (8a), leading to a selectivity factor of ∼300 between both isoforms. The deletion of one acid (6a and 6k) or the introduction of the less acidic halophenols (8h and 8i) increases the potency toward meprin α. Due to the acceptable potency of 8h and 8i for inhibition of both, meprin α and β, they may serve as pan-meprin inhibitors and thus enable the investigation of the inhibition of both proteases in diverse disease models, without affecting other metalloproteases.
    Most interesting is the lack of activity of 8a, h, and i against bone morphogenetic protein 1 (BMP-1), another astacin protease present in humans. Although the structure of BMP-1 has the same overall fold as meprin β, subtle differences in the active site allow a discrimination of both proteases by inhibitors.(33) The arginine residue Arg184 shaping the S1-pocket of meprin β is replaced by glutamine in BMP-1. Hence, the loss of favorable interactions between the inhibitor’s acidic groups and the protein’s basic residues could explain the observed selectivity.
    Since no cytotoxicity was observed (Table 4), the three most active compounds (8a, h, and i) were selected for oral and intraperitoneal rat screening experiments to assess their bioavailability and first insights into their pharmacokinetic properties (Table 5). However, the plasma concentrations after oral administration are rather low. This might be due to the polar properties of the compounds, combining a hydroxamic acid together with acidic functional groups hampering the absorption within the gastrointestinal tract or degradation during their passage. However, the bioavailability and the plasma concentrations of 8a and 8i after intraperitoneal application are in an acceptable range. Albeit a general metabolic liability of hydroxamic acids has been reported,(34,35) the compounds exhibit a reasonable stability and plasma clearance, reflected by their acceptable half-life (t1/2) after intravenous application and their good bioavailability after i.p. application, which also includes a first-pass metabolism of the compounds.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Compelling data renders the metalloproteinase meprin β as a promising drug target. To study the role of the enzyme in various diseases, potent and selective probes are necessary. However, the majority of known meprin β inhibitors are lacking a sufficient selectivity. Thus, it is hard to discriminate and link the effects caused by inhibitors, e.g., actinonin, to meprin β activity rather than to other metalloproteases, e.g., MMPs. Recently, we already reported the first selective inhibitors of meprin β, based on an established sulfonamide scaffold. The aim of the present study was to further optimize the selectivity of the inhibitors against other members of the metzincin family. Using a structure-based approach, the successful replacement of the sulfonamide by a tertiary amine led to inhibitors with high inhibitory potency against meprin β and a remarkable selectivity against other metalloproteinases. Hence, the inhibitors reported herein could be used as potent probes to investigate meprin β or the concomitant inhibition of both meprins in complex systems. Although their oral bioavailability is comparatively low, they exhibit reasonable absorption after intraperitoneal application. This makes them suitable as tool compounds for the utilization in animal models to further study the role of meprin β or meprin β/α in various disease models. Furthermore, the compounds could also serve as leads for further optimizations. Even their low bioavailability may be beneficial for particular indications, i.e., the local application for their utilization in inflammatory bowel disease or fibrosis.

    General Method for the Synthesis of the Secondary Amines 5a–i

The mixture was quenched with water and extracted with EtOAc (3 × 25 mL). The combined organic layers were dried over Na2SO4.     Docking

    For the molecular docking of compounds 6a, 8a, and 8i, the pdb file 4GWN (www.rcsb.org) was used as receptor, with a manual replacement of Cd2+ by Zn2+. The docking procedure was set up in GOLD (v5.5). The active site was defined by the Zn2+ within a radius of 10 Å. The ligands were utilized in their deprotonated forms. For each compound, 20 docking runs were performed, with a set search efficacy of 100%, and ChemScore was used as the scoring function. The resulting solutions for each ligand were imported and visualized in MOE (ChemicalComputingGroup, version 2016.08). For 6a, two solutions with a plausible Zn-binding were manually selected, which represented the two reasonable possible binding modes of the compound. For 8a and 8i, the top ranked solutions were selected, showing a reasonable binding mode, respectively.

    Enzymatic Assays

    Recombinant human meprin β was expressed in yeast and characterized as previously described.(36) Recombinant human meprin α was expressed and purified from insect cells (S2) and characterized analogously. MMP2, 9, 13, and ADAM10 and 17 were purchased from a commercial vendor (R&D systems). MMPs were activated prior to measurement by APMA (p-aminophenylmercuric acetate) treatment according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The protease domain of BMP1 was expressed in E. coli as inclusion bodies and refolded according to Mac Sweeney et al.(37)
    The determination of enzymatic activity was based on the cleavage of internally quenched peptide substrates (see Supporting Information). A typical assay of 250 μL total volume measured in black 96-well plates consisted of 100 μL of buffer, 50 μL of enzyme at a final concentration of 5 × 10–9 to 5 × 10–10 M (one concentration in the mentioned range, depending on the activity of the respective enzyme batch; in case of BMP-1, high enzyme concentrations had to be applied, 7 × 10–7 M), 50 μLof substrate (in buffer, 0.5% DMSO), and 50 μL of inhibitor solution (in buffer, 1% DMSO). In the case of 125 μL assay volume (black 96 half area well plates), all volumes were cut in half. Enzymatic activity of ADAMs was measured in 384-well plates with 60 μL total assay volume consisting of 20 μL of inhibitor, 20 μL of buffer, 10 μL of enzyme, and 10 μL of substrate. To ensure reproducibility, the parameters were determined at least in duplicates independently on separate days. For IC50 values, the influence of 12 inhibitor concentrations ranging from 0 to 5 × 10–5 M on the enzymatic activity was investigated in the presence of one standard substrate concentration (10 μM). Initial velocities were determined and converted into concentration units applying a standard curve obtained after complete conversion of different substrate concentrations under assay conditions. All measurements were performed using a fluorescence plate reader (FLUOstar OPTIMA, BMG Labtech) at 30 °C. The excitation/emission wavelength was 340/410 nm. The kinetic data was evaluated using GraphPad Prism (version 5.04, GraphPad Software). The inhibition of Cyctochromes 3A4 and 2A9 was analyzed using commercial assays (Cyp3A4 Blue and Cyp2C9 Blue, Life Technologies) according to the manufacturer’s protocols and guidelines.

    Cell Viability Assay

    Cell viability was assessed in human hepatocellular carcinoma cell line Hep-G2 and in human neuroblastoma cell line SH-SY5Y. Hep-G2 cells were cultivated in RPMI1640 (ThermoFisher) supplemented with 10% FBS, and SH-SY5Y cells were cultivated in DMEM (high-glucose, pyruvate) (ThermoFisher) also supplemented with 10% FBS in a humidified atmosphere of 37 °C and 5% CO2 according to standard cell culture procedures. For the assay, cells were plated in 96-well microtiter plates (Greiner bio-one) at densities of 50,000 cells/well (Hep-G2) and 60,000 cells/well (SH-SY5Y), respectively. After 24 h, compounds dissolved in DMSO are added to fresh medium at concentrations of 30 and 100 μM (final concentration of DMSO: 1% (v/v)) and applied to the cells for another 24 h. On the next day, cellular viability is determined using the CytoTox-ONE kit (Promega) based on the viability in control wells incubated with culture medium and 1% DMSO. The CytoTox-ONE assay kit was used according to the manufacturer’s recommendations.

    Determination of Pharmacokinetic Parameters

    Pharmacokinetic parameters were determined in catheterized Sprague–Dawley rats after approval by the animal welfare committee of the local state of Saxony-Anhalt (approval number: 203.m-42502-2-1361). Briefly, male RjHan:SD rats were purchased from Janvier Laboratories and housed in pairs for a minimum of 2 weeks prior to surgery. For the catheter placement, rats were anesthetized using Isofluran, and a 2 cm incision was made at the ventral part of the neck. Catheters were placed in the right V. jugularis and left A. carotis communis and tunneled to the dorsal neck. Surgical incisions were closed by sutures. Analgesia was done by subcutaneously injecting Meloxicam 1 mg/kg during and for 2 days after surgery. After a recovery period of several days, test compounds were applied orally (p.o.; 8a, 5 mg/kg; 8h,i, 10 mg/kg), intravenously (i.v., 6 mg/kg), or intraperitoneally (i.p., 10 mg/kg). In general, the catheter placed in the V. jugularis was used for i.v. applications. Blood (50–70 μL per time point) was collected from the catheter placed in the A. carotis communis. AUCs were calculated using the AUC function in GraphPad Prism 5 (GraphPad Software Inc.). Total body clearance (CL) was calculated according to the following formula: CL = D/AUC0-infinity.
