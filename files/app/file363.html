    Therapeutic Potential of the Peptide Leucine Arginine As a New Nonplant Bowman–Birk-Like Serine Protease Inhibitor

    Abstract

    The peptide leucine arginine (pLR) belongs to a new class of cyclic peptides isolated from frog skin. Its primary sequence is similar to the reactive loop of plant Bowman–Birk inhibitors (BBI), and the recently discovered circular sunflower trypsin inhibitor-1 (SFTI-1). The conformational properties of pLR in solution were determined by NMR spectroscopy and revealed excellent structural similarity to BBI and SFTI-1. Moreover, pLR is a highly potent trypsin inhibitor, with Ki values in the nanomolar range, and, due to its small size, a potential inhibitor of the serine protease tryptase. Since tryptase plays a crucial role in the development of allergic airway inflammation, the therapeutic potential of pLR in a murine asthma model was investigated. Treatment of ovalbumin-sensitized mice with pLR during allergen challenge reduced the acute asthma phenotype. Most importantly, application even at the end of a long-lasting chronic asthma model decreased the development of chronic airway inflammation and tissue remodeling.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The skin of frogs is rich in peptides that are part of their innate defense system featuring a variety of activities. Many of these peptides have been shown to kill microorganisms, including bacteria and fungi as well as cancer cells.(1-3) In recent years, a group of analogous peptides, named ranacyclins, was isolated from several species of European ranids mainly for their antimicrobial activity.(4) The peptide leucine–arginine (pLR, LVRCWTKSYPPKPCFVR) was isolated from skin secretion of the North American leopard frog, Lithobates (formerly Rana) pipiens.(5) PLR exhibits a high sequence identity to the reactive loop of plant Bowman–Birk inhibitors (BBI); to the recently discovered circular sunflower trypsin inhibitor-1 (SFTI-1);(6) and to HV-BBI, an isolated peptide from amphibian skin with trypsin-inhibitory activity.(7) In addition, pLR was found to stimulate histamine release from mast cells and to inhibit the early development of granulocyte macrophage colonies from bone marrow stem cells.(5) Because of the presence of the Bowman–Birk-like protease inhibitor motif within pLR,(8) it appeared likely that pLR also has serine protease-inhibitory activity. This assumption is supported by the fact that pLR was found originally in a skin preparation consisting partly of mast cells, which contain the serine protease tryptase as the main component,.(9) Up to that point, the only known natural inhibitor of tryptase had been leech-derived tryptase inhibitor (LDTI).(10)
    Tryptases have been found to be involved in several diseases, such as mastocytosis,(11) acute myeloid leukemia,(12) and atherosclerosis.(13) Mast cell tryptase also has been implicated in the development of allergic asthma and inflammatory disorders.(14) Current therapies for asthma, such as inhaled corticosteroids and β2-agonists, relieve symptoms but do not cure or reverse the progression of this disease. Recently, it has been shown that mast cell tryptase could be a promising target in allergic inflammation.(14, 15) Mast cells express α, β, and γ-tryptases, but predominantly, β-tryptases accumulate in secretory granules and are therefore the major isoenzymes released during mast cell degranulation.(16) β-Tryptase promotes human mast cell degranulation,(17) potentiates the histamine-induced bronchoconstriction,(18) induces eosinophil and neutrophil migration,(19) and stimulates proliferation of lung fibroblasts and airway smooth muscle cells.(20)
    An important step in understanding the inherent function of these peptides is to recognize homologies and similarities both at primary sequence and at the structural level. Here, we have identified a strong structural similarity of pLR with the three-dimensional structure of the active site of BBI as well as with the recently discovered SFTI-1. Moreover, we substantiated our hypothesis that pLR based on its small size is (in contrast to BBI) an inhibitor of the serine protease tryptase. Furthermore, using a murine asthma model, we demonstrate that pLR reduced the acute asthma-like phenotype and decreased the signs of airway remodeling and chronic airway inflammation when applied, even as late as seven weeks after first immunization. Our findings indicate the high potential of pLR to ameliorate inflammatory disorders such as allergic asthma.

    Peptide Sequences

    Peptides used in the study are listed in Table 1. In pLR and its analogs, two cysteines are linked via a disulfide bridge, forming a ring with 11 conserved residues (Table 1). SFTI-1 from sunflower exhibits a special structural feature of an additional head-to-tail ring to the disulfide-bridged cycle. BBI belongs to a class of protease inhibitors with up to 120 amino acids comprising a disulfide-linked nine-residue active loop.(21) The active loop of classical BBI, following Schechter and Berger’s nomenclature,(22) spans the subsites P3 to P6′ from one cysteine to the other. This loop is present in BBI and in the amphibian peptides (Figure 1). The only difference in this characteristic pattern is an insertion of tryptophan between positions P2 and P3 and a proline between P5′ and P6′ in pLR and the new amphibian peptides, resulting in a 11-residue rather than a 9-residue ring. Functionally, the P1 site is important because all naturally occurring inhibitors of trypsin have a basic amino acid, such as lysine or arginine, in that position. In pLR and analogues, this P1 site is occupied by lysine, indicating a possible binding affinity for trypsin or trypsin-like enzymes. The strictly conserved proline at the P3′ site and a proline frequently at the P4′-site are also found in pLR and the amphibian peptides. Similarly, for the inhibition of trypsin-like enzymes, the P1′ residue is a conserved serine.

    Structure Function Analysis

    Trypsin inhibition was investigated to compare the inhibitory potency of pLR and ranacyclins with the effectiveness of the classical BBI from soybean (Table 1). In comparison with the peptides (pLR, 110 nM; ranacyclin T, 116 nM; ranacyclin E, 129 nM), the activity of BBI (8 nM) is more than one magnitude higher. In addition, the influence of amino acid replacements and deletions at selected positions of pLR on the trypsin inhibition was investigated. It was found that the replacement of lysine-8 by alanine at the P1 site and the replacement of proline-11 by alanine at the P3′ site result in a total loss of inhibitory activity, respectively (Figure 2). In comparison with the native sequence of pLR, a distinct effect was obtained for the exchange of threonine-7 by alanine at P2, decreasing the trypsin inhibition (1.1 μM). In contrast, only small effects on Ki in the range of 142–400 nM were obtained for alanine replacements at positions P1′, P2′, P4′, and P5′. Similarly, only marginal effects were observed for truncations at the N- and C-termini, respectively. The inhibition potential toward trypsin decreased from Ki = 110 for pLR to 322 nM for pLR1-15. Even the terminally double-truncated peptide pLR5-15 showed residual activity, with a Ki of 335 nM. The switch from an 11-mer to a 9-mer ring by deletion of tryptophan-6 and proline-14 (pLRdesW,P) resulted in a 2-fold decrease in inhibitory potential. The sunflower trypsin inhibitor SFTI-1 as a member of a 9-ring inhibitor and as a naturally occurring bicyclic (disulfide bridge and head-to-tail cycle) peptide and its corresponding monocyclic one (disulfide bridge) were tested in this trypsin assay. The result confirmed a high inhibitory potential of these peptides with a Ki of 8 nM for the native peptide SFTI-1 and 20 nM for the modified analogue SFTI-1-cyc missing the head-to-tail ring. Similarly, a positive effect was observed after head-to-tail cyclization of pLR with a change from 110 to 70 nM (pLR-cyc).

    Conformational Studies

    Because of its good solubility, pLR was dissolved in water at a concentration of 1 mM. The solution remained stable for several weeks without showing any precipitation. Complete 1H NMR assignments were made by using the standard sequential assignment procedure(23) and the main-chain-directed approach(24) on the basis of double quantum filtered COSY (DQF-COSY), TOCSY, and NOESY spectra. Any overlap of sequential NN(i,i+1) cross-peaks as well as of cross peaks in the fingerprint (NH–CαH) region of the NOESY spectrum was resolved by varying the temperature in the range from 25 to 45 °C, facilitating a complete sequential assignment. The continuous line of sequential NH–CαH connectivity is broken only at the proline residues. There, sequential Ci-Cδ/δ′H(i+1) NOE signals were used for the sequential assignment of linkage between proline-11 and proline-12, and lysine-13 and proline-14 residues, respectively.
    The spin systems for all prolines were identified and used to assign the X-proline bond conformation. Proline-11 is in the cis conformation, as evidenced by a strong CαHi-CαH(i+1) NOE signal for tyrosine-10 and proline-11, whereas two strong CαHi-Cδ/δ′H(i+1) NOE signals(25) between proline-11 and proline-12, and between lysine-13 and proline-14 are diagnostic for those peptide bonds to be in the trans conformation. Secondary chemical shifts for the backbone Cα protons were calculated using the measured shifts and the random coil values given by Wishart et al.(26) For pLR, most Cα proton chemical shifts and α-13C chemical shifts differ from the random coil values by more than 0.1 ppm, suggesting a structured peptide in water. In particular, negative calculated shift deviations for α-carbon resonances and positive deviations for Cα proton resonances are indicative of an extended conformation.
    The solution conformation of pLR was calculated using a total of 197 distance constraints (Table 2). Figure 3 shows the ensemble of 20 structures superimposed over the backbone heavy atoms of residues 5–15. The mean rmsd from the average structure of the solution structures is 1.10 Å for all heavy atoms and 0.32 Å for backbone heavy atoms in the loop region between cysteine-5 and cysteine-15. The rmsd values of the complete structure, including both termini, are much larger, reflecting the complete flexibility of the N- and C-terminus in pLR, that is, all residues outside the ring.

    The cis conformation at tyrosine-10, proline-11, and the backbone conformation of all other residues in the loop is well-defined throughout all ensemble structures. The structure is best described as a three-residue turn (at position 9–11), preceded and followed by four residue-long extended conformations. Notably, regular cross-strand hydrogen bonds are not observed in these structures: only a single H-bond is observed between the carbonyl of threonine-7 and the amide group of lysine-13. This limited regularity is largely a consequence of the conformational preference of the three proline residues. Although residues 6–7 and 13–14 were nevertheless classified as β-strands in all ensemble structures, the number of Pro residues coupled with the small loop size and the rare cis-conformation expectedly resulted in statistically unusual backbone properties; for example, a relatively low proportion of 67.1% of residues in most favored regions of the Ramachandran plot (see Table 2).
    Most side chains exhibit partial restrictions in their side chain mobility. Although lysine-8 appears to be very flexible, several NOEs are observed for lysine-13 and result in a well-defined side chain conformation for this residue. The side chain of tryptophan-6 is well-defined. Its location beneath the β-sheet is characterized by several cross-strand NOE observations. The disulfide bridge is not well-defined, likely because of the nearly unrestricted flexibility of the adjacent terminal regions.

    Functional Studies

    Treatment of OVA-immunized BALB/c mice with pLR during antigen challenge reduces the acute asthma phenotype. To investigate the effect of the new nonplant Bowman–Birk-like trypsin inhibitor from the frog skin on the allergic immune response, we tested pLR as well as SFTI-1 in our well-established murine asthma model.(27-29) An asthma-like phenotype with AHR, eosinophilic airway inflammation, mucus hypersecretion, and high IgE levels is induced by sensitization of Balb/c mice to OVA by systemic and intrapulmonary applications of this model allergen. Pulmonary treatment with pLR or SFTI-1 during OVA challenge inhibited the development of airway inflammation in OVA-immunized BALB/c mice. This was demonstrated by a decreased number of eosinophils in the BAL fluid (Figure 4A) and a reduced infiltration of inflammatory cells in H&E-stained lung sections, verified by an objective, investigator-independent computer analysis (Figure 4B). In addition, pLR or SFTI-1 treatment also led to a diminished mucus production in PAS-stained lung sections (Figure 4C). These findings are associated with decreased serum levels of OVA-specific IgE (Figure 4D) and IgG1 (OVA [titer]: 479154 ± 148373; OVA + SFTI-1 [titer]: 176312 ± 56893, p < 0.05; OVA + pLR [titer]: 128075 + 7900, p < 0.05) in serum of sensitized mice treated with pLR or SFTI-1. An effect on IgG2a levels was not observed (data not shown).
    In parallel, both peptides significantly reduced the methacholine-induced AHR in OVA-immunized mice as measured in a whole body plethysmograph (Figure 4E). This was confirmed by assessing AHR using direct invasive assays for lung resistance in intubated mice (Figure 4F). To investigate the effect of pLR and SFTI-1 on cytokine production in OVA-immunized BALB/c mice, splenocytes and cells from the mediastinal lymph nodes were isolated on day 25 and restimulated in vitro with OVA for 3 days. Analysis of cytokine levels in cell culture supernatants showed that both peptides significantly decreased the production of the Th2-cytokines IL-13 and IL-5 in spleen cells as well as in the lymphnode cells. The levels of the Th1 cytokine IFN-γ was significantly diminished in the spleen by pLR, whereas no effects were observable in the lymph nodes (Figure 4G, H). In summary, treatment of OVA-sensitized mice during the antigen challenge with pLR or SFTI-1 clearly decreased the acute asthma-like phenotype.

    Treatment with pLR Reduces Chronic Airway Inflammation and Airway Remodeling

    To test the effect of pLR and SFTI-1 on the development of chronic inflammation and airway remodeling, we treated mice intranasally (i.n.) with the peptides in an established chronic asthma model.(27, 30) At the time we had applied the peptides (during the last 3 weeks of the chronic asthma model), an acute asthma-like phenotype had been established, as demonstrated in Figure 4. However, treatment with pLR or SFTI-1 significantly reduced the number of eosinophils in the BAL fluid and the chronic airway inflammation as well as the OVA-specific IgE serum levels and AHR of OVA-sensitized mice compared with the control mice (Figure 5A-D). Furthermore, treatment of mice with either peptide decreased several features of airway remodeling. The amount of total lung collagen was significantly reduced.
    Similarly, the subepithelial deposition of extracellular matrix proteins, examined by Martius Scarlet Blue-stained lung sections, a technique demonstrated to be specific for collagen and old fibrin,(31, 32) was diminished in pLR- or SFTI-1-treated mice compared with the control mice (Figure 5E, F). To show the effect of long-term allergen challenge, the influence of the peptides on smooth muscle cells and their proliferation in OVA-sensitized mice, we stained lung sections for α-smooth muscle actin (α-SMA) and proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA). There was a significant decrease in the smooth muscle area (α-SMA-stained cells) and in the area of proliferating smooth muscle cells (PCNA+-stained cells) in mice treated with the peptides (Figure 5G, H). Thus, pLR or SFTI-1 are able to inhibit the development of chronic airway inflammation and airway remodeling, even after treatment, for cases in which an acute asthma phenotype is present, demonstrating the therapeutic potential of the peptides.

    pLR Decreases Tryptase Activity in the BAL Fluid

    To investigate whether pLR or SFTI-1 influences the tryptase-like protease activity in vivo, we examined the proteolytic activity in the BAL fluid from pLR/SFTI-1-treated animals. Treatment with pLR or SFTI-1 decreased dose-dependently the tryptase-like protease activity in OVA-immunized mice up to 60% at a concentration of 100 nM compared with untreated animals (Figure 6A).

    pLR Decreases Mast Cell Activation in Vivo

    OVA immunization induced an 8-fold increase in serum mMCP-1 compared with control mice measured 30 min after the last antigen challenge. Treatment with pLR or SFTI-1 significantly reduced the levels of mMCP-1 in OVA-immunized mice, implicating decreased mast cell activation by the peptides (Figure 6B). At a concentration of 0.1 μM pLR or SFTI-1, there was no detectable effect on the plasma histamine release; however, a higher concentration of 10 μM pLR led to a significant release of plasma histamine (Figure 6C).

    pLR Decreases Isoprostane Levels in the Lung

    In addition to its inhibitory action on tryptase, we probed for other effects of pLR, which could possibly play a role in influencing the developing of airway inflammation. To do this, we measured 8-isoprostane levels as a marker of lipid peroxidation and oxidative stress in the lung.(33, 34) OVA immunization led to a 1.8-fold increase of 8-isoprostane in lung homogenates. Treatment of mice with pLR significantly reduced isoprostane levels, whereas SFTI-1 showed no significant effects (Figure 7).

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In the present study, we have investigated the three-dimensional structure of pLR, a member of a new class of small cyclic peptides, which are isolated from the skin of the frogs Rana pipiens and Rana esculenta. The primary sequence of pLR is highly conserved to the reactive loop of Bowman–Birk protease inhibitors found in the seeds of legumes and in many other plants. BBIs are typically double-headed proteins and are classified by their unique structural features and inhibitory characteristics.(35) Characteristically, the reactive loop of BBI forms a 9-residue ring stabilized by a disulfide bond that fits into the active site of the enzyme.(36) A further prominent structural feature of this loop is a sharp turn in the backbone at a proline residue, adopting a cis instead of trans conformation between the P2′ and P3′ sites.(37)

    Solution Conformation of pLR

    The conformation of pLR determined by 1H NMR spectroscopy in water shows a well-defined backbone containing three proline residues with a disulfide bridge building an 11-residue ring. Despite the enlarged ring architecture from 9 residues in classical BBI to 11 residues in pLR, the structure of pLR (Figure 8B) is almost identical to the crystal structures of the reactive loop of BBI (Figure 8C) and to the solution structure of SFTI-1 (Figure 8A), the small peptidic Bowman–Birk trypsin inhibitor from sunflower seeds.(38) All backbone dihedral angles for equivalent residues in these structures are within 30° (data not shown). The three structures comprise two antiparallel extended regions with β-strand character connected by a type VIb β-turn forming a hairpin. All three structures show the typical cis conformation of the peptide amide bond between positions P2′ and P3′ at the center of this turn, one characteristic feature of the canonical trypsin inhibitor conformation.(21)
    The structural similarity is also apparent from a comparison of the lowest energy structures of the NMR ensembles of SFTI-1 and pLR, and the inhibitory loop of the BBI crystal structure (Figure 8A-C). The best fit coordinate superposition yields a rmsd of <0.5 for all pairwise comparisons. Side chains occupy similar positions. Notably, a tyrosine in pLR replaces the hydrophobic isoleucine in SFTI-1 and sBBI at the tip of the loop. The lengthening of extended regions in pLR does not affect the solution conformation significantly. The disulfide bridge simply locates to the opposite side of the structure compared with SFTI-1 and sBBI without affecting the backbone conformation. The tryptophan side chain, unique to pLR, orients beneath the extended backbone region, occupying the space allocated by the disulfide bridge in the 9-residue loop inhibitors. The increase in the loop length in pLR has also only a small effect on the flexibility of the solution conformation. When compared with the cyclic SFTI-1, an SFTI-1 analogue without head-to-tail cyclization(38) and peptides comprising the sBBI loop sequence,(39) all peptides are highly structured and show remarkable definition in their ensembles of solution conformations. The observation that these sequences are independent structural motifs(39) is confirmed. The defined fold depends only on the presence of the disulfide loop, not its exact position. Although the definition in the solution ensemble of pLR is somewhat less compared with the NMR studies of the nine-residue inhibitors when judged by the loop rmsd values and the absence of a cross strand hydrogen bond network, other NMR observations, such as elevated backbone coupling constants and the presence of only one cis/trans/trans Pro peptide bond isomer confirms the presence of a major solution conformer and the absence of extensive conformational averaging within the 11-residue loop of pLR.
    Further evidence for the similarity of pLR and the BBI family was obtained by functional analysis of pLR and its replacement analogues in comparison to a classical member of BBI, the soybean trypsin inhibitor, and to SFTI-1. In a trypsin assay, the inhibition constants were determined to be 110 nM for pLR and 8 nM for the BBI and SFTI-1, respectively. It should be noted that the two latter inhibitors are the most active natural serine protease inhibitors found so far. The structure of SFTI-1 is very rigid because of double tethering by one disulfide bridge and an additional head-to-tail connection. The rigidity of a perfect fit probably causes this high activity of SFTI-1.(38) Indeed, and by analogy to SFTI-1, the introduction of a head-to-tail cycle in pLR resulted in an increased inhibitory potential. Moreover, the influence of the cis conformation at the P3′ position on the biological activity of pLR was investigated by replacement of proline by alanine. In comparison with proline, alanine has a significant lower preference to adopt a cis conformation. The observed loss of inhibitory activity of pLR confirms the suggested importance of a cis configuration at P3′ for the biological activity in this class of inhibitors. Furthermore, we have also shown that the replacement of lysine at the P1 site, which is responsible for inhibition of serine proteases from type trypsin, by alanine results in a total loss of inhibitory activity of pLR.
    Taken together, our results from conformational and structure–function studies suggest the inhibition of serine proteases is a general function of pLR and analogues.

    pLR and Tryptase

    One interesting target of pLR is the serine protease tryptase. Tryptase is an atypical serine protease because its active form is a tetramer formed of four identical monomers with a central pore as the active center.(40) Consequently, the space for accessible substrates as well as inhibitors is determined by conformational restrictions due to the size of the cavity. Therefore, macromolecular inhibitors, such as BBI, are largely inaccessible to tryptase. Importantly, no endogenous inhibitor for human tryptase has been identified in biological fluids so far.(14) Moreover, taking into account that pLR was originally extracted from the skin of the Leopard frog (Rana pipiens) and its small size, the role of pLR as a potential endogenous inhibitor for tryptase in mast cells should be characterized in more detail. Following this hypothesis, we investigated the therapeutic potential of pLR in a murine model of antigen-induced acute allergic asthma and chronic airway inflammation and tissue remodeling.(28, 30, 41)

    pLR in the Mouse Model

    Our data demonstrate that pLR significantly reduced the acute asthma-like phenotype in OVA-immunized Balb/c after local treatment during antigen challenge. The protection was associated with markedly reduced Th2 cytokine production in OVA restimulated splenocytes or mediastinal lymph nodes and, to a less extent, in lung cells (data not shown). The secretion of the Th1 cytokine IFN-γ was either also reduced or not affected, indicating no involvement of IFN-γ cross-regulation and down-modulation of the Th2-driven allergic immune response, as shown in some systems before.(27, 42) The results are comparable with studies using different tryptase inhibitors, such as nafamostat mesilate, gabexate mesilate(43) or MOL 6131.(44) We administered the peptides using a technique similar to that of Oh et al.(44) and, in contrast to other studies,(43, 45) intranasally. Oh et al. demonstrated that delivery of the tryptase inhibitor MOL 6131 via the respiratory route was superior to oral dosing or intraperitoneal injection. Notably, current therapies, such as corticosteroids and β2-agonist, were mainly applied via inhalation to produce optimal action and to reduce side effects.
    It has been shown that tryptase initiates and drives a variety of processes relevant to chronic airway inflammation and remodeling. Tryptase stimulates fibroblast proliferation(46) and increases the synthesis and secretion of type I collagen,(47) suggesting a role in chronic fibrosis. Tryptase may further contribute to airway remodeling by stimulating proliferation of airway epithelial cells,(48) bronchial smooth muscle cells,(49) and endothelial cells.(50) In our study, the application of pLR in a long-lasting chronic asthma model clearly reduced increased total lung collagen and the subepithelial deposition of extracellular matrix proteins (collagen and fibrin). Moreover, the proliferation of smooth muscle cells in mice treated with the peptides was significantly decreased. Most importantly, these inhibitory effects were observed when pLR was given to mice 7 weeks after the first OVA immunization, when the asthma phenotype with increased AHR, airway inflammation, mucus production, and IgE levels had already been established. These results demonstrate not only the inhibition of the development of chronic inflammation and airway remodeling after pLR treatment, but also show the high therapeutic potential of peptide pLR.
    Another remarkable observation was a similar effect of SFTI-1 on asthma development as well as its therapeutic potential. This result was absolutely predictable once the similarity in size, sequence, and solution conformations between pLR and SFTI-1 was recognized.
    To attribute the asthma-reducing effects of the peptides to their interaction with the mast cell tryptase, we determined the tryptase activity in vivo. Sensitization of mice with the antigen OVA increased the tryptase activity measured in the BAL fluid. Both peptides were able to reduce the tryptase activity in a dose-dependent manner with a significant inhibition at 100 nM. In addition, SFTI-1 and pLR decreased mast cell activation in OVA-sensitized mice, when administered during allergen challenge, indicated by mMCP 1 serum levels. These results are comparable with studies using the serine protease inhibitors nafamostat mesilate or gabexate mesilate in a murine asthma model.(43)
    The first study describing immunomodulatory properties of pLR showed that the peptide is a potent activator of histamine release.(5) Histamine plays a central role during the allergic immune response.(51) Therefore, a more proallergic than antiallergic effect of pLR was expected. However, pLR indeed led to an increase in plasma histamine levels in vivo, but only at comparatively high concentrations, starting at 10 μM. The concentration of 100 nM used in the asthma model did not induce histamine release, but clearly inhibited the tryptase activity. Therefore, pLR possibly appears to exhibit both proallergic and antiallergic properties by stimulation of histamine release at higher concentrations and inhibition of mast cell tryptase at low concentrations. On the other hand, the antiinflammatory effects of pLR were restricted to the inhibition not only of tryptase activity in the early phase of mast cell activation but also of the immunoregulatory function in the late phase of attenuating Th2 cytokine production, chronic inflammation, and airway remodeling. Studies using different tryptase inhibitors have demonstrated antiinflammatory effects beyond their inhibitory action, too.(43) We also showed that pLR was able to reduce oxidative stress in OVA-sensitized mice, as indicated by decreased isoprostane levels in lung homogenates. This is in line with previous findings that peptides from frog skin have antioxidant capacity as a secondary role in the defense system.(52, 53)
    In summary, our data clearly demonstrate that pLR is a potent tryptase inhibitor and reduces the acute as well as the chronic asthma phenotype in sensitized mice. Furthermore, our results indicate that pLR not only may act as a tryptase inhibitor but also has antioxidant properties. Therefore, pLR and other peptides from the amphibian skin are interesting candidates for exploratory investigations of further activities and of their application in therapeutic strategies.

    Chemicals and Reagents

    Fmoc-protected amino acids, benzotriazole-1-yl-oxy-tris-pyrrolidinophosphonium hexafluorophosphate (PyBOP), dimethylformamide (DMF), and HPLC-grade acetonitrile were purchased from Biosolve (Valkenswaard, the Netherland); diisopropylethylamine (DIEA), N-methyl-morpholine (NMM), piperidine, phenol, and trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) were from Fischer Chemicals AG (Zürich, Switzerland); and the resins (TentaGel S Ram 0.21 mmol/g and 2-Cl-trityl resin 0.3 mmol/g) were from Rapp Polymere (Tübingen, Germany). Water was deionized and purified in a Milli-Q system (Millipore, Eschborn, Germany).

    Peptide Synthesis

    Solid phase peptide synthesis of the peptides was performed on an automated peptide sysnthesizer, MultiPep from Intavis AG (Köln, Germany), using standard Fmoc chemistry. The linear disulfide-bridge-linked peptides with a C-terminal amid were synthesized on TentaGel S-RAM resin. Double couplings were made with a 4-fold excess of PyBOP and amino acids in the presence of NMM for 1 h. The final side chain deprotection and cleavage from the solid support employed a mixture of TFA, water, and thioanisole (95:2.5:2.5, vol %) for the peptides. The peptides were purified to >95% purity using preparative RP-HPLC (Shimadzu LC-8, Duisburg, Germany) equipped with a PLRP-S column (300 × 25 mm, Agilent, Waldbronn, Germany). For both analytical and preparative use, the mobile phases were water (A) and acetonitrile (B), each containing 0.1% TFA. Samples were eluted with a linear gradient from 5% B to 90% B in 30 min for analytical runs and in 90 min for preparative runs. The purified and lyophilized peptides were then oxidized in 0.1 M NH4HCO3 at a peptide concentration of 0.1 mg/mL for duration of 24 h. The final intramolecular disulfide-linked peptides were obtained after a second purification step by HPLC.
    The head-to-tail cyclic peptides pLR-cyc, SFTI-1, and sunflower were synthesized on 2-Cl-trityl resin as described above. pLR-cyc and SFTI-1 were cleaved form the solid support by acetic acid/trifluoroethanol/dichlormethane as fully protected peptides. Head-to-tail couplings were performed without prior purification in DMF and in the presence of 2 equiv of PyBOP and 4 equiv of N,N-diisopropylethylethylamine (DIEA) for 2 h. When the head-to-tail coupling was completed, peptides were side-chain-deprotected with the mixture of TFA/water/thioanisole (95:2.5:2.5, vol %) for 2 h. The formation of the second cycle between two cysteines was done by oxidation as described for pLR. The peptides were purified to >95% purity using preparative RP-HPLC. Finally, all peptides were characterized by analytical HPLC (Agilent 1100) and MALDI-MS (Bruker Microflex LT, Bremen, Germany), which gave the expected [M + H]+ mass peaks.

    Trypsin Assay

    Biological assays were performed at pH 8.5 using a 50 mM Tris–HCL. Peptides were prepared as 1 mM stock solutions. The inhibition assay was carried out in a total volume of 360 μL wherein trypsin (120 μL, final concentration 70 nM) was added to various concentrations of peptides (final concentration 5 μM), immediately followed by addition of the chromogenic substrate benzoyl-l-arginine p-nitroanilide (l-BApNA) (40 μL, final concentration 822 μM). The hydrolysis of the substrate was followed on a microplate reader (FluoStar) at 408 nm at room temperature for 5, 10, 30, and 60 min. Apparent inhibitor constants Ki(app) values were obtained by fitting velocity of substrate hydrolysis to the appropriate equation by nonlinear regression using the TabelCurve software version 1.1 from Systat Software Inc. (Chicago, IL). For the potent inhibitors it was necessary to fit data to a tight binding equation:(54)(1)where vi and v0 are the rates with and without inhibitor and [E]0 and [I] are total enzyme and inhibitor concentrations, respectively.(2)Ki values were determined from Ki(app) by correction for substrate competition using eq 2 with the total substrate concentration [S] of 822 μM and the Michaelis constant KM of 1.5 mmol/L.

    NMR Spectroscopy

    The peptides were dissolved in 90% H2O and 10% D2O or 100% D2O at a final peptide concentration of 1 mM. The pH was adjusted with 0.1 M DCl to 3.5 to minimize amide proton exchange. The NMR spectra were acquired at different temperatures from 295 to 310 K on a Bruker Avance 600 spectrometer equipped with a cryogenically cooled, z-gradient, triple-resonance probe head. All spectra were referenced to the methyl resonance of TSP at 0.0 ppm, which was added as internal standard. The observed proton resonance line widths under these conditions were as expected for structured peptides of this molecular weight, which indicated the absence of aggregation effects.
    Typical acquisition parameters at 600 MHz were spectral width of 6000 Hz and a pulse width (90°) of 8.7 μs. The suppression of the H2O resonance was usually accomplished by excitation sculpting.(55) One-dimensional 1H NMR spectra were acquired using 256 transients and 20 032 data points, and zero-filled to 65 536 data points. For 2-dimensional spectra, 2048 complex data points, 32 transients and 256 complex increments were acquired. Double-quantum-filtered phase-sensitive 2D correlated spectroscopy (DQF-COSY)(56, 57) and 2D total correlation spectroscopy (TOCSY)(58, 59) were used for spin type assignment. Sequential assignments were made using phase-sensitive 2D nuclear Overhauser effect spectroscopy (NOESY)(60) and standard procedures.(25) Several mixing times between 100 and 200 ms for NOESY and 65–80 ms for TOCSY spectra were acquired. A 13C-HSQC experiment acquired at natural abundance served to obtain CαH chemical shifts. The 2D data were processed in Topspin, and analyzed in NMRview.(61) NOEs were manually assigned and converted into distance restraints using procedures in NMRview. Dihedral restraints were obtained via classification of HNHA-coupling constants measured for well-separated resonances in 1D-NMR experiments and using the program Talos+.(62) One hundred structures were calculated using Cyana 2.1.(63) These were subsequently refined with RECOORD scripts in CNS for water refinement.(64) The 20 energetically best structures were selected as the final ensemble, which was deposited in the RCSB data bank under the accession code RCSB103167, PDB ID 2m3n. Chemical shifts were deposited in the BMRB database, accession code 18970.

    Mice

    Female BALB/cByJ mice (6–8 weeks of age) were obtained from the Elevage Janvier Laboratory (Le Genest St Isle, France). Mice were bred and maintained in an animal facility at the University of Leipzig (Germany) and housed under conventional conditions with 23 °C room temperature, 60% humidity, and 12 h day/night rhythm. All animal experiments involved groups of 4–6 mice/cage and were performed at least two times. All animal experiments were performed according to institutional and state guidelines. The Committee on Animal Welfare of Saxony approved animal protocols used in this study.

    Immunization and Treatment with the Peptides

    To induce acute airway inflammation Balb/c mice were immunized intraperitoneally (i.p.) with OVA (20 μg, Sigma-Aldrich, Steinheim, Germany) adsorbed to 2 mg of an aqueous solution of aluminum hydroxide and magnesium hydroxide (Alum, Perbio Science, Bonn, Germany) on days 1 and 14, followed by 20 μg OVA in 40 μL normal saline given i.n. on days 14–16 and 22–23. Control mice received alum i.p. and normal saline i.n. AHR was measured on day 24, and mice were sacrificed on day 25. Both peptides (100 nM) were applied i.n. on days 15, 16, 22, and 23 at 30 min before OVA challenge. Control mice received normal saline i.n. To induce a chronic asthma-like phenotype, Balb/c mice were immunized i.p. with OVA on days 1 and 14 and challenged with OVA i.n. twice per week for 8 weeks. Peptides were given 30 min before each OVA challenge starting in week 7. AHR was measured on day 71, and mice were sacrificed the following day.

    Measurement of Airway Responsiveness

    Airway responsiveness was assessed by methacholine-induced airflow obstruction from conscious mice placed in a whole body plethysmograph (enhanced Pause, Penh; EMKA Technologies, Paris, France) and lung resistance (RL) of anesthetized mice was measured by invasive plethysmography (EMKA Technologies) in response to inhaled methacholine (Sigma-Aldrich) as described previously.(27, 28, 41)

    Collection of Bronchoalveolar Lavage (BAL) Fluid

    Cells in the lavage fluid were counted using a hemocytometer and BAL cell differentials were determined as described previously.(27, 41)

    Lung Histology and Computer-Based Quantification of Inflammation, Mucus Production, Extracellular Matrix Deposition, and Airway Smooth Muscle Proliferation

    Left lung was fixed in 10% formalin and stained with haematoxylin and eosin (H&E, Merck, Darmstadt, Germany) or with Alcian blue-PAS (Sigma-Aldrich). Total matrix deposition was assessed on Martius Scarlet Blue (MSB)-stained sections.(32) Lung sections were also immunostained with anti-α-smooth muscle actin (SMA) primary Ab to distinguish smooth muscle cells and with proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA) to stain proliferating smooth muscle cells as described previously.(30) For quantification and objective evaluation, lung sections were analyzed with HistoClick Software as described previously.(27, 30)

    Collagen Analysis

    Collagen content was measured in lung tissue homogenates by a biochemical assay according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Sircol collagen assay, Biocolor, Ireland), as described previously.(27, 28, 30)

    OVA-Specific IgE and IgG1 Assay

    OVA-specific IgE and IgG1 serum levels were measured by sandwich ELISA according to a standard protocol as described previously.(27)

    Cytokine Production

    Splenocytes or mediastinal lymph node cells (5 × 106 cells/mL per well) were isolated and restimulated in vitro with 200 μg/mL OVA in culture medium (RPMI medium supplemented with 10% FCS, 100 U/mL penicilin, 100 μg/mL streptomycin) 1 day after airway function test. Lung tissues were homogenized in PBS, and protein was measured at 700 nm with 5 μL of homogenate and 200 μL of 1:5 diluted Biorad solution (Bio-Rad, München, Germany) with bovine serum albumin as standard. Cytokines were measured in supernatants from restimulated spleen cells after 3 days or from lung tissues using DuoSet ELISA kits (R&D Systems, Minneapolis, MN) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

    Tryptase Activity in BAL Fluid

    Tryptase activity in BAL fluid is measured by the detection of the chromophore p-nitroaniline (pNA) after cleavage from the labeled substrate tosyl-gly pros-lys-pNA. The free pNA is quantified at 405 nm. The tryptase activity was represented as the difference from optical density at 1 h.

    Measurement of Mouse Mast Cell Protease 1

    Balb/c mice were immunized with OVA on day 0 and challenged with OVA i.n. on days 8 and 9. Peptides were administered i.n. 30 min before OVA. Thirty minutes after the last challenge, mice were sacrificed, and serum was collected. Mouse mast cell protease 1 (mMCP-1) was measured using an ELISA assay (Moredun Scientific, Midlothian, United Kingdom) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

    Measurement of 8-Isoprostane

    Eighteen hours after the last mycotoxin exposure, lung tissues were homogenized in buffer (0.1 M phosphate, 1 mM EDTA, 10 μM indomethacin and 0.05% butylated hydroxytoluene, BHT at pH 7.4) using lysing matrix A (FastPrep24 homogenizer, MP Biomedicals, LLC, Eschwege, Germany). Aliquots of the obtained supernatants were hydrolyzed (15% KOH) and deproteinized (ethanol containing 0.01% BHT). 8-Isoprostane concentration was determined by specific immunoassay according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Cayman Chemical Company, Ann Arbor, MI). Protein concentration of 5 μL homogenates were measured at 700 nm using DC Protein Assay (Bio-Rad, München, Germany), comparing it with a diluted series of bovine serum albumin as standard.

    Plasma Histamine Assay

    Blood samples were obtained from mice 5 min after treatment with the peptides (0.1 to 10 μM). Blood was collected into BD Microtainer PST lithium-heparin tubes (BD Diagnostics, Heidelberg, Germany). After centrifugation, plasma aliquots were collected and frozen at −80 °C until use. Plasma histamine levels were determined using an immunoassay kit (IBL, Immuno-Biological Laboratories, Hamburg, Germany) following the manufacturer’s instructions.

    Data Analysis

    Data were expressed as means ± SEM. Data were analyzed for significance using the Student’s unpaired t test. Differences were considered significant at a P value of <0.05.
