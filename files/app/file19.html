    Abstract

    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is associated with impaired Aβ degradation in the brain. Enhancing the process of Aβ clearance is an attractive potential AD therapy. Treatment with LXR agonists may reduce Aβ levels in vivo. However, the clinical potential of many LXR agonists is limited because of their nonselective actions on LXRα/β, which lead to undesired hepatic lipogenesis via LXRα-dependent pathways. In this study, ABCA1 up-regulators were identified from a series of flavonoids and were found to preferentially activate LXRβ and up-regulate expression of ABCA1 and apoE in different cell lines. Further investigations confirmed that these compounds facilitate intracellular Aβ clearance in Aβ-loaded BV2 cells. Administration of compound 19 reduced total brain Aβ and plaque burden in APP/PS1 double transgenic mice, associated with elevated ABCA1 and apoE expression. Compared with the nonselective LXR agonists, the active compounds reported here induced less accumulation of undesired lipids and triglycerides in HepG2 cells.

    1 Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is an age-dependent neurodegenerative disease characterized by the formation of neurofibrillary tangles and deposition of amyloid plaques within the brain.(1) The major component of amyloid plaques is the peptide β-amyloid (Aβ),(2) which is generated proteolytically from β-amyloid precursor protein (APP).(3) Accumulation of Aβ in the brain is proposed to drive AD pathogenesis and is associated with an imbalance between Aβ production and clearance.(4) Over the past few years, evidence has accumulated that facilitating the Aβ clearance process is a promising therapeutic approach for AD.(5)
    Under normal conditions in the brain, Aβ peptides are taken up by the brain’s resident macrophages, microglia cells, which degrade the peptides (mainly via neprilysin (NEP) and insulin-degrading enzyme (IDE)) to maintain Aβ homeostasis.(6) Recent reports suggest that ATP-binding cassette transporter A1 (ABCA1) and apolipoprotein E (apoE) are essential to these processes.(5, 7) ABCA1 is a prominent regulator of whole body cholesterol metabolism, removing excess cholesterol from peripheral tissues to the liver and increasing high-density lipoprotein (HDL) levels in plasma.(8) By promoting cholesterol efflux, ABCA1 also mediates the formation of lipidated apoE and ApoE–HDL particles. ApoE is the predominant apolipoprotein in the central nervous system (CNS) and is secreted by astrocytes and microglia.(9) Although apoE4, one of the isoforms of apoE, is believed to increase the risk of late-onset AD,(10) apoE promotes Aβ trafficking(11) and clearance from the brain with the assistance of ABCA1.(12) Lack of ABCA1 may reduce lipidated apoE(13) and increase both soluble and insoluble Aβ levels, exacerbating the deposition of Aβ in mouse models of AD.(14) Elevating the levels of ABCA1 and apoE is regarded as an alternative strategy for AD therapy.(7, 11c, 15)
    The expression of both ABCA1 and apoE can be transcriptionally regulated by liver X receptors (LXRs: LXRα and LXRβ), which generally work in coordination with retinoid X receptors (RXRs).(16) It has been reported that LXR agonists such as GW3965(5b, 5c) and T0901317(17) (Figure 1A) reduce both Aβ level and amyloid plaques and improve cognition in mouse models of AD. Unfortunately, most of these agonists target both LXRα and LXRβ and may therefore cause hypertriglyceridemia and hepatic steatosis by up-regulating sterol regulatory element binding protein 1c (SREBP-1c) and fatty acid synthase (FAS) in the liver.(18) This limits their clinical potential. Studies from LXR knockout animals indicate that LXRα is the predominant subtype controlling SREBP-1c transcription and is responsible for the observed hepatic lipogenesis.(19) Therefore, LXRβ subtype-selective agonists may exert beneficial effects while minimizing the undesired effects,(20) improving the therapeutic potential of LXR agonists for AD therapy.
    Herein we describe the synthesis and biological evaluation of a series of synthetic flavonoids based on naturally occurring flavone and chalcone scaffolds. Selected active compounds from the ABCA1 promoter activation assay were found to facilitate intracellular Aβ degradation in Aβ-loaded microglia and to decrease brain Aβ levels and amyloid plaques in APP/PS1 double transgenic mice in an LXRβ-mediated manner. The undesirable accumulation of lipids and triglyceride accumulation in the human liver carcinoma HepG2 cells was reduced compared with the well-known nonselective LXR agonists GW3965 and T0901317.

    2.1 Chemistry

    Flavonoids constitute a large group of natural products occurring in plants and foods, such as fruits, vegetables, and soybeans.(21) Previous research showed that some naturally occurring flavonoids such as daidzein,(22) pratensein,(22) hesperetin,(23) and quercetin(24) (Figure 1B) are able to up-regulate cellular expression of ABCA1. Although their potential for anti-AD therapy was not evaluated, these work inspired us to search for novel flavonoids and derivatives promoting Aβ clearance. In the present work, we chose the typical flavone and chalcone skeletons as the templates for structure diversity expanding.
    7-Hydroxyl flavone derivatives (3–11) were designed by introduction of either fused rings, heterocycles (3–6, 11), or varied substitutions (7–10). Compound 3–11 were synthesized following the reported procedure(25) as illustrated in Scheme 1. Briefly, 2-chloro-1-(2, 4-dihydroxyphenyl)-ethanone (2) was prepared first by Houben–Hoesch reaction of resorcinol (1) with 2-chloroacetonitrile, then 3–11 were successfully obtained by aldol condensation–cyclization of 2 with the appropriate aromatic aldehydes.
    7-Amino substituted flavone derivatives (15–17) were synthesized according to the reported procedure(26) with slight modifications. Briefly, 3-acetamidophenyl acetate (13) was prepared by acetylation of 3-aminophenol (12). The key intermediate 4′-acetamido-2′-hydroxyl-acetophenone (14) was achieved by Friedel–Crafts arrangement (Scheme 2). Microwave-assisted Claisen–Schmidt condensation of the resulted 14 with the appropriate aromatic aldehydes gave 4′-acetamido substituted chalcones. The chalcones were converted to the flavone derivatives in DMSO with catalytic iodine. Then the protective acetyl group was removed under microwave irradiation to yield the corresponding amino derivatives (15–17).
    For the synthesis of the 4′-amino-2′-hydroxyl chalcone derivatives (19–34 and 36–37), 4′-amino-2′-hydroxyacetophenone 18 was first prepared from 14 in 6N HCl at 50 °C, then final chalcone analogues were obtained by Claisen–Schmidt condensation of 18 with different aromatic aldehydes under microwave irradiation (Scheme 3). For the synthesis of compound 35, condensation of 18 with indole-3-carboxaldehyde in a refluxing solution of ethanol and catalytic piperidine gave the desired product (Scheme 3).
    Compounds 38–39 and 44–51 were designed and synthesized as reference compounds to probe the contribution of α,β-unsaturated ketone and 2-hydroxyl or 4-amino groups on their bioactivities. 38 and 39 were obtained by reduction of the α,β-unsaturated ketone of 19 and 36, respectively (Scheme 3). The synthesis of 2′-hydroxyl and/or 4′-amino methylated products 44–50, as well as the 4′-amino acetylated product 51, were achieved from corresponding modified intermediates (43, 42, 40, and 14) and different aromatic aldehydes respectively (Scheme 3–4).
    In total, 41 compounds were obtained successfully, 37 of which (3, 6, 8–9, 11, 15–17, 19–39, and 44–51) were identified as new compounds.

    2.2 Biological Evaluation

    2.2.1 ABCA1 Activator Screening of Synthetic Flavonoids

    We first used the ABCA1 promoter activation assay for screening. The assay was performed using mouse macrophage RAW264.7 cells transfected with the promoter reporter gene pGL3-ABCA1-Luc, a firefly luciferase gene construct fused to the promoter region of the mouse ABCA1 gene. The screening library and commercially available compounds (GW3965, RXR agonist 9-cis-retinoic acid, and PPAR agonists fenofibrate and rosiglitazone) were tested at a concentration of 1 μM, using 0.1% DMSO as the vehicle control. Activity is expressed as relative luciferase activity, normalized to the Renilla luciferase signal. ABCA1 promoter activation for the vehicle control was defined as 1-fold activation. The results are summarized in Table 1, where tested compounds are classified as high, moderate, and low efficacy activators according to their response ranges (greater than 1.7-, 1.5–1.7-, and 1.3–1.5-fold, respectively). Compounds with lower than 1.3-fold response were defined as inactive.

    As shown in Table 1, the LXR agonist GW3965 induced significant promoter activation, with a response of 1.82-fold over the vehicle control. RXR agonist 9-cis-retinoic acid showed 2.18-fold activation, while PPAR agonists fenofibrate and rosiglitazone caused no apparent activation (1.10- and 1.19-fold, respectively) at the concentration tested. These results are consistent with previous reports.(22, 27) Among the flavone series, one high efficacy (5) along with two moderate (4 and 7) and five low efficacy (3, 6, and 15–17) compounds were identified. The 7-hydroxyl flavone derivative with a 4-carboxyl group on the phenyl ring (7) showed moderate activity (1.66-fold activation). Meanwhile, a large substitution on the phenyl (8–10) or thiophen-2-yl (11) rings with isopropyl (8, 1.22-fold), phenoxyl (9, 1.17-fold), phenyl (10, 1.15-fold), or 4-methoxyphenyl (11, 1.03-fold) groups reduced the efficacy dramatically. Compounds bearing fused-ring moieties 3–6 (R = quinolonyl, naphthyl, indolyl, and thionaphthenyl, respectively) showed low to high efficacy (1.36–1.90-fold). The indolyl substituted compound 5 showed the highest efficacy (1.9-fold activation) in the flavone series. These results suggest that a conjugated aromatic structure with an aromatic ring (R) of proper volume would be favorable for increasing the activity.
    The investigation of chalcone analogues was focused on 4′-amino-2′-hydroxyl substituted derivatives (19–37). We chose 4′-amino instead of the 4′- hydroxyl substitution because we expected an improved molecular polarity to potentially increase the solubility under physiological conditions. As summarized in Table 2, most of the chalcones showed good ability to activate the ABCA1 promoter, with activity closer (21, 28, 30, 32, 35, and 37) or greater than (19, 23, 29, 34, and 36) the positive control GW3965. In total, 14 of the 19 chalcones (73.7%) were hit, 12 high (19–21, 23, 28–30, 32, and 34–37), one moderate (31), and one-low efficacy compound (24) were found. Compound 36, bearing a naphthalenyl moiety, caused the strongest activation of the ABCA1 promoter.

    Notably, the activities of 4′-amino-2′-hydroxyl chalcones 36, 34, 29, 32, 31, and 19–21 were 34.8–68.2% greater than those of the corresponding flavones 4, 6, 8–10, and 15–17 (except 35 and 33 vs 5 and 11). Structurally, differences between the tested flavone and corresponding chalcone analogues are that chalcones have more flexible conformation, stronger Michael receptor properties of α,β-unsaturated ketone, and 4′-amino, 2′-hydroxyl substitutions. We found that methylation (36, 19–21 vs 46–49 and 19 vs 50, respectively) or acetylation (20 vs 51) of either the 2′-hydroxyl or 4′-amino reduced the activity sharply (from 1.92–2.64- to 0.88–1.50-fold). These results indicate that the 2′-hydroxyl and 4′-amino groups play important roles in interacting with the target, most likely by acting as hydrogen bond donors. Interestingly, methylation of both the 2′-hydroxy and 4′-amino groups increased activity slightly (44 vs 47 and 50; 45 vs 46). We suspect that both methylated compounds 44 and 45 may have a different binding model when acting with their potential target. On the other hand, although reduction of the double bond in the α,β-unsaturated ketone moiety of 36 produced a much less active compound 39, the activity of compound 38 was only slightly reduced compared to its unsaturated counterpart 19. Considering that highly active flavone derivatives were found, we doubt that the Michael receptor properties of α,β-unsaturated ketone moiety contribute significantly to activity.
    Introduction of an electron donor in the phenyl group (21, 23, 28–30, 32, and 37) generally increased activity (1.73–2.09-fold) compared to electron-withdrawing groups (24 and 26, 1.36 and 1.12-fold activation, respectively). The weaker activity of compounds 22 and 27 (1.26- and 0.98-fold, respectively) were exceptions, which may be due to their increased steric bulk.
    To further evaluate the effects of the hits on ABCA1 promoter activation, various concentrations of representative compounds 19 and 36 were applied to the RAW264.7 cells. The results indicate that 19 and 36 could activate the ABCA1 promoter in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 2).

    2.2.2 Flavonoid Analogues 5, 19, and 36 Are LXR Agonists Preferentially Targeting LXRβ

    It is well-known that transcription of ABCA1 can be regulated by LXR. To determine whether the ABCA1 promoter activators were LXR agonists, a cell-based luciferase transactivation assay was used. HEK293 is an excellent cell line for transfection experiments and has been a popular research tool in the study of LXR activation.(18c, 28) Because of poor transfection efficiency in macrophages or microglia, we chose HEK293 cells to cotransfect the receptor plasmids hLXRα or hLXRβ with a firefly luciferase reporter gene containing LXR response elements. Compounds 5, 19, and 36, which represented the active flavones, chalcones bearing a single aromatic ring and fused aromatic ring, respectively, were chosen for the evaluation. After transfection, the cells were treated with 5, 19, and 36 (0.1 or 1 μM). GW3965 and 0.1% DMSO were used as the positive control and the vehicle control, respectively. As expected for a nonselective LXRα/β agonist, 0.1 and 1 μM GW3965 induced clear increases in LXRα (1.53- and 2.38-fold) and LXRβ (1.42- and 1.86-fold) activation (Figure 3).(29) Interestingly, unlike GW3965, our compounds 5, 19, and 36 preferentially stimulated LXRβ activation. LXRβ was strongly activated (1.76–2.22-fold) upon treatment with 1 μM 5, 19, and 36, while LXRα was only weakly activated (1.11–1.32-fold) .

    2.2.3 Compounds 5, 19, and 36 Induce Expression of ABCA1 Protein in RAW 264.7 Cells and BV2 Microglia

    We next examined ABCA1 expression in RAW264.7 cells and BV2 microglia upon treatment with 5, 19, and 36. Time-dependent expression assays were performed first to determine the time of maximal expression for each compound. The results showed that ABCA1 expression in RAW264.7 cells could be increased by 1 μM 5, 19, or 36 with maximum response at 18, 36, and 36 h, respectively (Figure 4A). RAW264.7 cells were therefore treated for 18, 36, and 36 h with various concentrations of 5, 19, and 36, respectively. ABCA1 expression was up-regulated, as expected, in a dose-dependent manner upon treatment with 5, 19, or 36 (Figure 4B,C). Similar results were observed in BV2 cells: ABCA1 expression reached a maximum at 12, 24, and 48 h by 1 μM 5, 19, and 36, respectively (Figure 5A). ABCA1 expression increased dose-dependently (Figure 5B,C). These results confirm that the ABCA1 promoter activators 5, 19, and 36 increase ABCA1 expression in RAW264.7 cells and BV2 microglia.

    2.2.4 5, 19 and 36 Induce apoE Protein Expression in BV2 Microglia

    The data above show that the active flavonoids activate LXRβ and up-regulate ABCA1 expression. As another gene target of LXR, apoE plays a critical role in various steps of Aβ clearance. To evaluate the effects of 5, 19, and 36 on apoE expression in BV2 microglia, apoE protein levels were examined by Western blot (Figure 6). The influence of these compounds on apoE expression differed. Compound 5 weakly modulated apoE expression (1.1- and 1.4-fold at 0.1 and 1.0 μM, respectively). In contrast, 19 significantly increased apoE expression: 1.6-, 1.9-, and 2.7-fold increases were observed at 0.1, 1.0, and 10.0 μM, respectively. Compound 36, the strongest LXRβ agonist and ABCA1 promoter activator identified in the current work, exhibited lower activity (1.4-, 1.4-, and 1.7-fold increases at 0.1, 1.0, and 10.0 μM, respectively) than 19. Clearly, the LXR downstream protein ABCA1 and apoE responded differently to these compounds.

    2.2.5 Intracellular Aβ Degradation Is Enhanced by the Active Flavonoids in Aβ-Loaded BV2 Microglia

    To evaluate the abilities of these flavonoids to promote Aβ degradation, 12 active ABCA1 promoter activators (4–5, 7, 19–21, 23, 28, and 34–37) were examined in Aβ-loaded BV2 microglia, following a literature procedure.(5b, 12b) Briefly, BV2 microglia were pretreated with 1 μM test compound for 18 h and then incubated with soluble Aβ42 for an additional 24 h. Aβ42 levels in BV2 microglia were measured via ELISA, using 0.1% DMSO and GW3965 as the vehicle and positive controls, respectively. The results are summarized in Figure 7A.
    The results indicate that 1.0 μM GW3965 substantially facilitated clearance of Aβ in BV2 microglia and a reduction in Aβ of approximately 55% was observed relative to the vehicle control, consistent with the findings of Jiang et al.(5b) Most of these compounds (5, 19–20, and 34–37) enhanced Aβ degradation by approximately 37–59% in BV2 microglia. Generally, there was a correlation between activation of the ABCA1 promoter and the residual intracellular Aβ42 levels after treatment with the test compounds (Figure 7B). For example, the weaker activators 4 and 7 promoted less Aβ clearance. Although there was inconsistence in compound potency between activation of the ABCA1 promoter and Aβ clearance, it is understandable that the discrepancy might be due to a number of factors. For example, (i) the status of compounds in different cell models (macrophage RAW264.7 and BV2 microglia cell) used for different purposes may be different; (ii) the efficacy of the test compounds in promoting ABCA1 activation and Aβ clearance is also influenced by the cell’s genetic background, which would may also result in different sensitivities to test compounds; (iii) although activation of the ABCA1 promoter may be important in enhancement of Aβ clearance, our results suggest that the active compounds induce up-regulation of apoE to different degrees, which may also influence Aβ clearance. As a result, compound 19 demonstrated greater efficacy in enhancing intracellular Aβ42 clearance (59%) despite having a lower potencies than 36 for the activation of LXRβ and the up-regulation of ABCA1.
    Further investigation revealed that intracellular Aβ degradation was enhanced by 19 and 36 in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 8A). Treatment with 0.01, 0.1, or 1 μM 19 reduced the intracellular Aβ levels by 29, 52, and 59%, respectively, which was comparable to that of GW3965. Treatment with 36 (0.01, 0.1, and 1 μM) also enhanced Aβ clearance, a 44% reduction in intracellular Aβ was obtained at 0.1 μM treatment. However, lower Aβ clearance was observed at 1 μM in several tests, which may be due to complex factors such as those outlined above. Nevertheless, our data suggest that most of these ABCA1 promoter activators enhance intracellular Aβ degradation in BV2 microglia.
    As mentioned above, Aβ peptides are degraded in vivo mainly by NEP and IDE, and the expression level of both proteases may affect degradation. It is well-known that flavonoids are pleiotropic molecules that have the possibility to act on multiple targets. To determine whether the active flavonoids up-regulate expressions of IDE and NEP and therefore facilitate Aβ degradation, the effect of the most potent compound (19) on expressions of these proteases was examined. As shown in Figure 8B, mRNA levels of IDE and NEP showed no clear change upon treatment with various concentrations of 19. These results indicate that the active compounds reduce intracellular Aβ levels in BV2 microglia mainly by up-regulating ABCA1 and apoE protein levels.

    2.2.6 Administration of 19 Reduces Brain Aβ Level and Plaque Burden in APP/PS1 Transgenic Mice, Accompanied by Up-regulation of ABCA1 and apoE

    The APP/PS1 double transgenic mouse is one of the most extensively used transgenic mouse models of AD. The APP/PS1 mice overexpress the Swedish mutation of APP and PS1 lacking exon 9, leading to increase of Aβ production.(30) By 6–7 months of age, these mice show high levels of Aβ and increased Aβ plaques in the brain,(31) as also seen in the brains of AD patients. To evaluate the effects of 19 in vivo, six-month-old APP/PS1 mice were treated intraperitoneally with 19 for 6 weeks (10 mg/kg three times weekly). Brain tissues were then removed and subjected to protein extraction and immunohistochemical analysis. We found that administration of 19 increased brain expression of ABCA1 and apoE by 2.3- and 1.6-fold, respectively (Figure 9). Quantitative analysis showed that 19-treated mice exhibited a lower total brain Aβ42 level, a reduction of 31% and 26% in soluble and insoluble Aβ42 were observed, respectively, compared to the vehicle (Figure 10A). Thioflavin S staining and immunofluorescence results (Figure 10B and Supporting Information Figure S1) indicated that Aβ plaque number and load (brain area occupied by Aβ plaques) were also reduced compared with vehicle. Antiamyloid fibril antibody OC staining showed that average Aβ plaque number and load were reduced by approximately 30% in the cortex and approximately 20 and 28%, respectively, in the hippocampus (Figure 10C). Overall, these results suggest that treatment of 19 reduces total Aβ level and plaque deposition in APP/PS1 mice, accompanied by elevated expressions of brain ABCA1 and apoE.

    2.2.7 Compounds 5, 19, and 36 Induce Limited Lipid and Triglyceride Accumulation in HepG2 Cells

    Our results suggest that 5, 19, and 36 are selective LXRβ agonists that up-regulate ABCA1. To determine the effects of compounds 5, 19, and 36 on triglyceride synthesis and lipogenesis in hepatic cells, HepG2 cells were incubated with various concentrations of tested compounds for 4 days. Intracellular triglyceride levels were measured using a triglyceride assay kit; lipid aggregates were stained by oil red O then analyzed by light microscopy. LXRα/β dual agonists GW3965 and T0901317 (1 μM) increased triglyceride levels by 43 and 175% over the vehicle control, respectively (Figure 11), whereas 10 μM GW3965 led to a 242% increase. At the same time, lipid aggregates accumulated abundantly in cell plasma (Figure 12B–C). By contrast, 5, 19, and 36 only slightly raised intracellular triglycerides, even at 10 μM. Only 18% and 19% increases in triglyceride were observed on treatment with 10 μM 5 and 36, respectively, while 10 μM treatment with 19 induced no apparent increase in triglycerides (Figure 11). Few lipid aggregates could be found in cell plasma after treatment with these compounds (Figure 12D–F). These results suggest that the selective LXRβ agonists promote markedly less lipid and triglyceride accumulation in liver cells than GW3965 and T0901317.

    2.2.8 Molecular Docking

    To better understand the interactions of 19 with LXRβ, the compound was docked into the ligand-binding site using Surflex-Dock in Sybyl 7.3.5 (Tripos Inc.). The crystal structure of LXRβ in complex with the ligand T0901317 (PDB code: 1PQC) was adopted.(32) The results are shown in Figure 13. The binding mode of 19 was similar to those of T0901317 and other LXRβ agonists.(18c, 32, 33) Ligand recognition was achieved by hydrogen bond between the hydroxyl and amino groups with His435 and Thr272 residue, respectively, while the 5-methyl furan group protruded into a hydrophobic pocket formed by residues Phe271, Phe329, Phe340, and Phe349 (Figure 13A). A π–π stacking interaction was observed between the furan ring and phenyl ring of Phe271 (Figure 13B). These observations suggest that for these chalcones, 4′-NH2 and 2′-OH are required for ligand recognition and that a plane aromatic motif with appropriate volume would be favorable. The docking results were in accordance with the experimental observation.

    2.2.9 Cell Proliferation Assay

    To ensure that 5, 19, and 36 were nontoxic to RAW264.7, BV2, HEK 293, and HepG2 cells within the concentration range tested, the cytotoxicity profiles of these compounds were investigated at concentrations up to 50 μM by the sulforhodamine B (SRB) method. As illustrated in Figure 14, most of the cell lines retained good viability after treatment with high concentrations of the test compounds. Only 5 inhibited the viability of HepG2 cells by approximately 45% at 50 μM concentration. The results suggest that 5, 19, and 36 exhibit low cytotoxicity toward all the cell lines and that errors caused by cytotoxicity were avoided in current studies.

    3 Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, a series of new flavone and chalcone derivatives were synthesized and screened as potential ABCA1 promoter activators using a reporter gene assay. Molecular mechanism investigations revealed that the hits preferentially activated the LXRβ subtype and up-regulated the intracellular protein levels of ABCA1 and apoE. An intracellular Aβ clearance assay revealed that 19 facilitate Aβ degradation. Treatment with 19 at 10 mg/kg reduced Aβ levels and plaque deposition in APP/PS1 mice, accompanied by elevated expression of ABCA1 and apoE in the brain. On the basis of the reported works and our results, we would conclude that by activating the LXRβ, these compounds mediate the up-regulation of ABCA1 and apoE, therefore enhance the formation of lipidated apoE and ApoE-HDL particles, which promotes Aβ trafficking and clearance. However, it should be pointed out that flavonoids are pleiotropic molecules and have possibility to act on multiple targets. Although we have excluded the influence of the main proteases NEP and IDE based on our assay profiles, other possibilities of the mechanisms remain to be investigated in the future.
    Because of the primarily distribution of LXRα in the liver and its involvement in hepatic lipogenesis, LXRα/β dual agonists may cause serious side effects. Interestingly, although their overall potency in the activation of LXRs and the promotion of Aβ degradation was not significantly higher than the positive control GW3965, we found that these compounds preferentially activate the LXRβ, leading to greatly reduced triglyceride and lipid accumulation in HepG2 cells compared to the LXR dual agonists GW3965 and T0901317. Therefore, despite the unknown mechanism of this selectivity, which will require systematic investigation in future work, our findings offer an opportunity to search for novel LXR agonists based on natural flavones and chalcones as potential treatments for AD with an improved therapeutic index.

    4.1 General

    All commercial reagents and solvents were purchased from vendors and were used without further purification or distillation. Microwave reactions were performed in a Biotage Initiator 2.5 microwave reactor. Reactions were monitored by TLC on a glass plate coated with silica gel with fluorescent indicator (GF254).     4.10 Cell Culture

    Cells (murine macrophage cell line RAW264.7, murine microglia cell line BV2, human embryonic kidney 293 cell line HEK293, and human liver carcinoma cell line HepG2) were grown in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM) containing l-glutamine supplemented with penicillin (100 U/mL), streptomycin (100 μg/mL), and 10% (v/v) heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (FBS) in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2 and 95% O2 at 37 °C.

    4.11 Animals and Treatments

    Six-month-old double transgenic B6C3-Tg(APPswe,PSEN1dE9)85Dbo/Mmjax (APP/PS1) mice and C57BL/6 mice (wild-type, WT) were purchased from Guangdong Medical Laboratory Animal Center (Guangdong, China) for in vivo study. The APP/PS1 transgenic mice express a chimeric mouse/human amyloid precursor protein (Mo/HuAPP695swe) and a mutant human presenilin 1 (PS1-dE9), both directed to CNS neurons. By 6–7 months of age, these mice have high expression level of Aβ and increased Aβ plaques in brain, which is similar to those seen in brains of AD patients. All procedures involving animals were approved by the Animal Experimentation Ethics Committee of Sun Yat-sen University. Transgenic mice were randomly divided into a treated group and an untreated group. The treated group mice were injected intraperitoneally with 19 at a dose of 10 mg/kg three times weekly, and the untreated and WT groups received an equivalent volume of vehicle. The treatment was continued for 6 weeks, and then the mice were sacrificed. Brain tissues was then quickly removed, one hemisphere was fixed and processed for immunohistochemical analysis, and the other was snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen and subjected to protein extraction.

    4.12 Transfection and Luciferase Assay

    For the ABCA1 promoter activation assay, RAW264.7 cells were grown on 96-well plates at 5 × 104 cells per well overnight. Cells were transiently cotransfected with a murine ABCA1 promoter luciferase construct pGL3-ABCA1-Luc and a renilla luciferase vector (pRL-SV40) by using Lipofectamine 2000 transfection reagent in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. Then 6 h after transfection, cells were incubated with the tested compounds for 24 h. Cells were lysed and assayed for firefly or renilla luciferase activities using Dual-Glo Luciferase system. Firefly luciferase activity was normalized to Renilla luciferase for each well. Activation studies on LXR were performed as described previously by Cronican et al.(34) with slight modifications. The HEK 293 cells were plated into 96-well plates at 3 × 104 cells per well and allowed to attach overnight at 37 °C. Expression constructs pCMX-hLXRα and pCMX-hLXRβ, together with the reporter plasmid tk-LXRE×3-Luc and the internal control plasmid pRL-SV40, were cotransfected into cells using Lipofectamine 2000 transfection reagent in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. After 20 h, the cells were treated with the tested compounds, LXRα/β dual agonist GW3965 as the positive control and 0.1%DMSO as the vehicle control. Luminescence measurement was processed 24 h later. The results are expressed as relative firefly luciferase activity normalized to the renilla luciferase signal (fold change compared to vehicle control).

    4.13 Western Blot Analysis

    Western blotting assays were performed as previously described.(35) For the cells, cell lysates were prepared in lysis buffer containing 1% NP-40, 20 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.6), 0.15 M NaCl, 3 mM EDTA, 3 mM EGTA, 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, and protease inhibitor cocktail tablets. For the brain tissues, samples were added to cold lysis buffer and homogenized by using a homogenizer. Centrifuge the lysates or homogenates, then the supernatant was collected. Equal amounts of protein samples were separated on 8–10% sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS)–polyacrylamide gels and electrophoretically transferred to polyvinylidene difluoride membranes. After blocking with 5% nonfat milk at room temperature for 2 h, the membranes were incubated with the primary anti-ABCA1 and anti-apoE antibodies at 4 °C overnight. The membranes were then incubated with a horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibody for 1.5 h at room temperature, and the immunoreactive protein bands were visualized using enhanced chemiluminescence reagents.

    4.14 Aβ ELISA

    Intracellular Aβ clearance assay was performed as previously described with slight modifications.(5b, 12b) BV2 microglia cells were plated overnight in 12-well plates in DMEM containing 2% FBS. Cells were pretreated with 0.1% DMSO or varied concentrations of the tested compounds for 18 h at 37 °C and then were incubated with 2 μg/mL soluble Aβ42 in serum-free medium containing 10 μg/mL BSA for another 24 h. Briefly, cells were washed extensively with PBS to ensure the removal of Aβ, which may be attached to the cell surface and then were lysed in lysis buffer containing protease inhibitors. Remaining intracellular Aβ was quantified by using high sensitivity Aβ42 ELISA kit and normalized to total protein. For the brain Aβ, soluble and insoluble Aβ42 level of the sample homogenates were quantified using human Aβ42 brain ELISA kit in according with the manufacturer’s instructions.

    4.15 Real-Time Quantitative PCR

    The mRNA expressions of Aβ proteases IDE and NEP was analyzed by real-time quantitative PCR. BV2 microglia cells were plated overnight in 6-well plates in DMEM containing 2% FBS. Cells were treated with 19 for 18 h at 37 °C. Then total RNA was isolated using TRIZOL reagent and reverse-transcribed to cDNA using RT PreMix Kit according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Real-time quantitative PCR was performed with the above prepared cDNA and SYBR Green Master Mix. The primer sequences of each gene used for the experiment are shown as follows:

    • IDE forward primer, 5′-TGCACTATTATCCCCTAAATG-3′

    • IDE reverse primer, 5′-TGTCTATCAAGTCGGGTCTAA-3′

    • NEP forward primer, 5′-GTAAGCAGCCTCAGCCGAAAC-3′

    • NEP reverse primer, 5′-CCACATAAAGCCTCCCCACAG-3′

    • GAPDH forward primer, 5′-TCAACAGCAACTCCCACTCTTCCA-3′

    • GAPDH reverse primer, 5′-ACCCTGTTGCTGTAGCCGTATTCA-3′

    Relative changes in gene expression were calculated by using the comparative threshold cycle (ΔΔCt) method, and GAPDH was used as the internal control gene.

    4.16 Thioflavin S Staining

    Thioflavin S is a homogeneous dye mixture and could nonselectively binds β sheet contents of proteins, such as those in amyloid aggregates and therefore provides a quick alternative to detect the amyloid deposits. Postfixed hemispheres were embedded in paraffin and coronally sectioned at 6 μm thickness. Sections (6–8 sections per mouse) were randomly selected starting from the appearance of CA3 and the dentate gyrus, spaced 0.06 mm apart. The sections were deparaffinized in xylene and rehydrated. The thioflavin S staining was performed as described previously.(36) Sections were washed with 70% and 80% ethanol for 1 min and then incubated in filtered thioflavin S solution (1% in 80% ethanol) for 15 min. Then the sections were washed with 80% and 70% ethanol for 1 min and rinsed with distilled water twice at last. Sections were then examined under a fluorescence microscope (Nikon ECLIPSE Ti–S, Japan).

    4.17 Immunofluorescence

    The randomly selected 6 μm sections as described above were deparaffinized in xylene and rehydrated. Then the sections were pretreated with 3% H2O2 for 15 min to block endogenous peroxidase activity, then the sections washed with distilled water and 0.1 M Tris-buffered saline (TBS), PH 7.5. Antigen retrieval was done with 10 mM citrate buffer (pH 6.0) for 10 min. Sections were subsequently washed in TBS with 0.1% Triton X-100 (TBST) and blocked with 3% bovine serum albumin in TBST for 30 min. Then sections were incubated with antibody OC against Aβ fibrils for 2 h at room temperature. Following two washes with TBST, sections were incubated with the secondary Alexa Fluor 594-conjugated antirabbit IgG antibody for 1 h and then examined under the fluorescence microscope.

    4.18 Lipid Staining and Intracellular Triglyceride Level Measurement

    HepG2 cells were grown in a 6-well plate (1 × 105 /well) at 37 °C overnight and then treated with various concentrations of tested compounds for 4 days. The cells were fixed with 4% formaldehyde for 20 min, rinsed with PBS, dipped in 60% 2-propanol, and stained with 0.3% (w/v) oil red O in 60% 2-propanol for 15 min and then washed with PBS for three times. Cell nuclei were then stained with hematoxylin staining solution for 2 min, then washed with PBS for three times. The stained cells were examined under a light microscope (Nikon ECLIPSE Ti–S, Japan). For the intracellular triglyceride measurement, the cells were lysed and the triglyceride levels were analyzed by using a triglyceride assay kit in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions.

    4.19 Cell Proliferation Assay

    Cell proliferation assay procedures were performed as described previously by Zuo et al. with slight modifications.(37) Sulforhodamine B (SRB) assay was performed to evaluate cell viability. Cells (RAW264.7, BV2 microglia, HEK 293 and HepG2) were plated at 5000 cells per well and allowed to grow at 37 °C overnight. Then the cells were treated with various concentrations of the tested compounds for 48 h at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2, 0.5% DMSO acted as the vehicle control. The cell growth was stopped by gently adding 50% trichloroacetic acid to all the wells, and the plates were incubated at 4 °C for 1 h to fix the cells attached to the bottom. After being washed with distilled water for five times, the samples were stained with SRB dye (0.4% in 1% acetic acid) for 30 min and then washed with 1% acetic acid for another five times. The adsorbed dye was dissolved in Tris base solution (10 mM, pH 10.4) and then processed for optical density (OD) recording on a TECAN infiniteM200 pro multimode reader at 515 nm. The results are expressed as % relative cell viability compared to DMSO vehicle control.

    4.20 Molecular Docking

    The 3D structures of 19 were constructed using the docking protocol for ligand preparation in Sybyl 7.3.5 (Tripos Inc.). Molecular docking studies of compounds with the hLXRβ binding pocket were performed with Surflex-Dock in Sybyl 7.3.5. The ligand in the crystal structure of hLXRβ-T0901317 complex (PDB code: 1PQC) was chosen as a reference to construct the protomol. Threshold was 0.5, bloat was 0, additional starting conformation per molecular was set to 5, and other parameters were set as default.
