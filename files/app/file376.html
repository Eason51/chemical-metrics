    Abstract

    A bottleneck in fragment-based lead development is the lack of systematic approaches to elaborate the initial fragment hits, which usually bind with low affinity to their target. Herein, we describe an analysis using X-ray crystallography of a diverse library of compounds prepared using microscale parallel synthesis. This approach yielded an 8-fold increase in affinity and detailed structural information for the resulting complex, providing an efficient and broadly applicable approach to early fragment development.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Over the past 20 years, fragment-based screening has become established as a robust method to find starting points for drug development.(1) Due to their small size, fragment hits typically form a small number of interactions with their target protein, which results in a low binding affinity. As a consequence, significant medicinal chemistry effort is required to produce higher affinity lead compounds.(2) This design–synthesis–evaluation cycle required to elaborate fragment hits can become a bottleneck to development. Therefore, methods that enable a more rapid and systematic approach to fragment elaboration are needed.
    One such method, termed off-rate screening (ORS) by surface plasmon resonance (SPR), was described by Murray et al.(3) In this approach, compounds with higher affinity are detected by virtue of their slower dissociation kinetics observed in SPR sensorgrams. The dissociation rate is independent of free analyte concentration, which means that this method does not require the concentration of a compound to be determined before it is tested. Moreover, since weakly binding compounds dissociate rapidly, the dissociation phase of the sensorgram for a mixture is dominated by the most slowly dissociating component, even when it is a minor component of the mixture.(3) In addition, SPR requires only small quantities of materials, allowing moderately sized libraries of compounds to be synthesized on a small scale for testing by ORS without extensive purification. However, this approach requires that the dissociation rate constant for the test compounds be slow enough to be resolved by SPR. Typically, this requires that the dissociation rate constant koff < 1 s–1. Assuming an association rate constant in the range of kon = 1 × 105–1 × 106 M–1 s–1, this produces a threshold whereby the equilibrium dissociation constant for the products must be KD <1–10 μM before ORS can be employed. As noted above, it is common for the initial fragment hits, particularly for difficult targets, to bind with weak affinity, i.e., often KD > 500 μM. Therefore, in order for ORS to be successful, a significant improvement (>50-fold) would be required to meet the affinity threshold where dissociation kinetics are likely to be observed. In an alternative approach, X-ray crystallography has been used previously in fragment development in combination with dynamic combinatorial chemistry (DCC) to identify more potent compounds in situ.(4) However, DCC is limited in scope by the small number of reactions suitable for in situ chemistry. Therefore, we sought to develop a method applicable to a broader range of chemistries(5) (harsher conditions and organic solvents), as well as be applicable to the development of low-affinity fragments that bind too weakly to be amenable to ORS by SPR.
    Advantages of using X-ray crystallography include that it can be used to characterize binding across a broad range of affinities, including weakly binding compounds, and that it provides important structural information for subsequent chemical elaboration. Furthermore, recent advances in X-ray crystallography data collection and analysis, such as automation of crystallization experiments, development of single-photon-counting detectors, pipeline processing software, and multi-data-set analysis methods for identification of ligand binding, have enabled significantly higher throughput and more routine screening of libraries.(6−8) We sought to exploit these developments and to utilize X-ray crystallography to evaluate the binding of libraries of compounds prepared in a similar manner to that reported for ORS by SPR, thereby taking advantage of the ability to synthesize compounds on a small scale without extensive purification. The drawbacks of X-ray crystallography screening may include false positives/negatives, ambiguity in the binding mode due to crystal contacts, and errors in interpretation of electron density. Despite these challenges, we hypothesized that screening unpurified reaction products by X-ray crystallography could identify compounds of higher affinity and provide key structural information to enable the rapid elaboration and evaluation of fragments that bind to targets for which ORS by SPR and other techniques are not applicable.
    Herein, we report an initial proof-of-principle study that we performed on an Escherichia coli disulfide bond forming protein A (EcDsbA). We have previously undertaken a fragment screen against EcDsbA and demonstrated that fragment binding is amenable to analysis by X-ray crystallography.(9)EcDsbA is a disulfide oxidoreductase enzyme that is responsible for catalyzing the formation of disulfide bonds in a wide range of substrate proteins, including secreted proteins and virulence factors.(10) Therefore, inhibitors of EcDsbA have been suggested as antivirulence agents.(11)EcDsbA can be considered a “difficult” target for drug development since its binding site is a shallow, extended hydrophobic groove that accommodates a diverse array of substrates. In addition, due to its hydrophobic nature, there are relatively few opportunities within this binding groove for specific polar interactions that enable the generation of high-affinity small-molecule binders. The aim of the current study was to improve the affinity of a previously validated hit fragment containing a diaryl ether core,(9) using this method, which we termed rapid elaboration of fragments into leads using X-ray crystallography (REFiLX).

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Diaryl ether 1 was identified from a fragment screen and demonstrated weak binding to EcDsbA (KD > 1000 μM; Figure 1b).(9) Structure-based approaches to elaborate fragment hit 1 by expanding the phenol to an acetic acid to optimize interactions with His32 together with further substitution of the benzonitrile ring to fill the hydrophobic pocket led to the synthesis of phenylacetic acid 2, which bound to EcDsbA with an improved affinity of KD = 490 ± 20 μM (Figure 1d). Analysis of the X-ray crystal structure of 2 bound to EcDsbA suggests that the acetate moiety forms a charge reinforced hydrogen-bonding interaction with the imidazole NH of His32 in the active site of EcDsbA, while the cyano group participates in a hydrogen bond with the side-chain amide NH of Gln35. The diaryl ether core fills a portion of the hydrophobic groove adjacent to the active site of EcDsbA and contributes to π-stacking interactions with the side chains of His32 and Phe174 (Figure 1a).
    To explore the development of analogues expanding from the carboxylic acid of 2, a library of 93 diverse amine reagents was used. The library was designed to maximize the coverage of the three-dimensional (3D) pharmacophore space around the expansion vector while at the same time balancing physicochemical properties (Clog P, total polar surface area (tPSA), flexibility, complexity, etc.) and synthetic tractability. This was achieved using a custom in-house fingerprint and selection algorithm. A full list of the diverse amine library as well as desired products is given in Table 1. The library was constrained to add 5–12 heavy atoms to the parent fragment, which, assuming a group efficiency of 0.3 kcal/(mol HAC), would potentially yield ∼10–100-fold improvement in affinity.(12) Protecting groups were used where the reagents contained functional groups that were likely to interfere with the amide formation chemistry to be employed, which required deprotection prior to screening. Due to the wide range of reactivities expected for the amine reagents in the library, we first optimized the reaction conditions to identify those that gave the best yields across a small test set of representative amines of varying reactivities, comprising one aromatic, one primary, and one secondary amine. A series of 45 reactions were conducted varying the coupling agent, equivalents of amine, temperature, and solvent (Table S2). Initial exploration of solvent and equivalents of amine suggested that acetonitrile (MeCN) solvent with 1.5 equiv of amine was preferred, giving all three products in purity of 14–43%. Further optimization reactions in MeCN then indicated that the 1-[bis(dimethylamino)methylene]-1H-1,2,3-triazolo[4,5-b]pyridinium 3-oxide hexafluorophosphate (HATU) coupling agent at 25 °C with 1.5 equiv of amine was preferred, showing a product for the aromatic, primary, and secondary amines with increasing purity ranging between 20 and 85% and in line with the expected reactivity. Simultaneously, crystals of EcDsbA were evaluated for their sensitivity to possible reaction conditions (reagents, solvents, and byproducts) and soaking times for the different conditions (see the Experimental Section). These sensitivity experiments revealed that crystals soaked with (1-cyano-2-ethoxy-2-oxoethylidenaminooxy)dimethylamino-morpholino-carbenium hexafluorophosphate (COMU) diffracted at significantly lower resolution (2.4 ± 0.2 Å) compared to those soaked with 1-ethyl-3-(3-dimethylaminopropyl)carbodiimide hydrochloride (EDC) and HATU (1.8 ± 0.1 and 1.9 ± 0.1 Å, respectively). The final reaction conditions chosen for the library synthesis were the HATU coupling agent in MeCN with 1.5 equiv of amine at 25 °C. These conditions gave consistent purity and produced high-resolution data by X-ray crystallography (Figure 2).

    Reactions were performed on a microscale (2 μmol) in parallel in 96-well plates where each well contained a single amine reagent. Upon completion of the reactions, deprotections were performed as required (trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) treatment for acid labile and NaOH treatment for base labile protecting groups), solvents were removed by evaporation, and the reactions were dissolved in DMSO to generate a stock. The stocks were prepared without further purification at a notional concentration of 100 mM assuming 100% conversion to product in each reaction. The reactions were analyzed via LCMS to identify the presence of the desired product and to estimate purity. For 65 of the 93 reactions, the product was unambiguously identified by both UV–vis and mass spectrometries (MS). Of the remaining 28 reactions, only 3 identified the desired product mass in MS, while 15 showed only an additional peak in the UV–vis trace, consistent with the expected product. For all reactions where a UV–vis peak was observed for the product, purities were calculated by measuring the area of the new peak divided by the total area of all peaks, excluding the HATU peak. The remaining 10 reactions showed no evidence of product by either MS or UV–vis. The purities ranged from 0 (i.e., no product observed) to 90%, with an average of 25% across all 93 reactions. Significant amounts of phenylacetic acid starting material 2 were observed in the majority of low-purity samples. The importance of purity/conversion in the subsequent X-ray crystallography screening was investigated further, as described below. Each DMSO stock was diluted 10-fold and soaked into EcDsbA crystals using previously described soaking and cryoprotection conditions.(9) Data were collected at MX1 and MX2 beamlines at the Australian Synchrotron and processed with the automated data-processing pipeline implemented at the beamline.(13−15) Briefly, each data set was indexed, integrated, and scaled with xdsme and Aimless.(16−18) The output statistical description of the data was manually inspected, and data sets with poor statistics were rejected at this stage (see the Experimental Section). In total, 90 of the 93 samples had data sets with acceptable collection statistics and gave high-resolution data within the range of 1.8–2.5 Å (Figures S1 and S2). For 9 of these 90 samples, lower sample concentration and shorter soaking times were required. The remaining three samples did not produce data sets of sufficient quality after multiple soaking and collection attempts and were not pursued further. All data sets were phased by molecular replacement (MR) and refined automatically using the Auto-Rickshaw molecular replacement multi-data-set pipeline,(19,20) and the output was inspected manually. In addition, the data sets were analyzed using Pan-Dataset Density Analysis (PanDDA)(6) to automatically identify bound ligands. Eighty-two of the 90 samples that were analyzed showed electron density consistent with the parent phenylacetic acid 2.
    Four samples were identified as hits due to the presence of additional electron density in the EcDsbA maps, which was inconsistent with the parent phenylacetic acid 2 and consistent with the expected positioning of the corresponding amide expansion (Figure 2 and Scheme 1; compounds 6–9). These four hits were resynthesized on a batch scale and purified to obtain full chemical characterization and to enable the measurement of KD. For the chiral morpholine carboxylic acid (compound 7), synthesis of the racemic mixture was conducted. The binding modes of the purified compounds were validated by X-ray crystallography.
    The solubility of resynthesized analogues was determined by recording a quantitative one-dimensional (1D) 1H NMR of each compound in aqueous (aq) buffer at multiple concentrations as previously described (Table 1).(21) Binding to EcDsbA was assessed by recording 1H–15N heteronuclear single quantum coherence (HSQC) spectra of oxidized 15N-labeled EcDsbA with increasing amounts of ligand. Titrations were continued until the ligand concentration reached either 1 mM or, where the ligand was not soluble at 1 mM, the maximum concentration at which they were deemed soluble. Affinities were determined by measuring chemical shift perturbations (CSPs) of backbone amide resonances as a function of ligand concentration and fitting the data to a single-site binding model, which accounts for the ligand depletion expected in the data. All of the hits demonstrated similar or better affinity for EcDsbA compared to the starting phenylacetic acid 2, with the highest affinity analogue pyrazole 9 showing an 8-fold improvement in affinity (KD = 62 ± 9 μM, Figure 3). The binding affinity for this fragment was confirmed by recording additional HSQC data at a protein concentration below the measured KD (Figure S3). Purified compounds were analyzed by cocrystallization, which confirmed the binding mode to EcDsbA and produced more complete electron density maps compared with those obtained from the crude products. This allowed us to model ligands into the electron density maps unambiguously (Table S3). Analysis of the crystal structure of pyrazole 9 bound to EcDsbA identified polar and π-stacking interactions similar to those observed for the parent 2 (Figure 3a) with additional water-mediated interactions of the amide carbonyl oxygen of pyrazole 9 with the backbone NH of His32. The pyrazole side chain of 9 projects out from the shallow groove of EcDsbA and folds back toward the active site, where it forms an additional π-stacking interaction with the imidazole ring of His32. The additional interactions observed in this unexpected binding mode most likely underpin the increase in affinity of pyrazole 9 over the parent 2 in binding to EcDsbA.
    Analysis of the LCMS data for the four hits as described above revealed that they had >50% purity in the crude library synthesis. To assess the effects of reaction conversion and purity on the outcome of the REFiLX screening strategy, several additional compounds were resynthesized and purified (Scheme 1 compounds 10–12). The library analogue oxadiazole 11 is structurally similar to the highest affinity hit pyrazole 9 and gave only low reaction purity in the library (6%) as estimated by LCMS; hence, it was resynthesized to test for false negatives in the screen. Screening by 1H–15N HQSC determined its KD to be 230 ± 20 μM (Figure S4), indicating that oxadiazole 11 did indeed bind with a 2-fold improvement in KD over the parent 2. Additional high conversion products 10 and low conversion product 12 that were not identified as hits were also resynthesized and confirmed as having similar or weaker affinity for EcDsbA compared to parent 2 (Table 1). A structure was subsequently determined for oxadiazole 11 following cocrystallization of the pure compound with EcDsbA (Figure S4). The resulting electron density map shows partial electron density in a region of the structure that is consistent with a potentially plausible binding position for the amide extension of oxadiazole 11. This additional density is only partial, and it does not provide clear evidence of the binding mode for the oxadiazole extension in the complex. However, had this density been observed in the initial screen, it would have led to 11 being identified as a possible hit. Oxadiazole 11 was not identified as a hit by REFiLX despite its improved affinity compared to parent 2, which may have been due to its low purity in the library synthesis. This variability in conversion to product represents one of the potential limitations of screening unpurified reaction products by X-ray crystallography.
    These limitations were further investigated via a series of soaking experiments containing both parent 2 and higher affinity hit pyrazole 9, which were prepared from the purified compounds as mixtures at relative concentrations corresponding to conversions of 6, 13, and 28%. These samples were made in the absence or presence of a faux background amidation reaction mixture (see the Experimental Section). Density of the pyrazole extension was observed at all conversions in the absence of the background faux amidation reaction mixture; however, the electron density was observed to be less clear for the pyrazole expansion in the presence of faux background reactions and this was sensitive to conversion (Figure S5). This suggests that X-ray screening can produce false negatives from crude reactions at low conversions (<28%) and is consistent with the outcome observed with oxadiazole 11. Nonetheless, in the current data set, 39 samples in the library achieved conversion/purity of ≥28% by LCMS.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We have implemented a fragment development approach that uses X-ray crystallography to screen chemical libraries synthesized in parallel on a microscale without extensive purification to identify compounds that bind to a protein target. Using this approach, termed REFiLX, we synthesized a library of 93 amides, which were each soaked into crystals of EcDsbA. We observed additional electron density in the X-ray data for four products, which were deemed to represent “hits” in the screen (Table S3). The amide expansion with the highest affinity hit was pyrazole 9 with an affinity of KD = 62 ± 9 μM. This approach is complementary to the strategy of ORS by SPR,(3) with the distinction that REFiLX uses X-ray crystallography as the readout to identify higher affinity binders. An advantage of REFiLX is that it can operate over a wide affinity range. For example, although pyrazole 9 provided an 8-fold affinity gain over the parent compound, the dissociation kinetics for pyrazole 9 would be expected to be too fast to be measured by SPR. Current limitations of REFiLX include the availability of a suitably robust crystal system that is amenable to soaking and stable in the presence of organic cosolvents and the requirement for reasonable conversion in the parallel synthesis (≥∼28% in the current study). REFiLX can provide structural information on the most promising compounds in a library to guide further design of high-affinity ligands. We therefore expect that REFiLX will provide a useful tool in the armamentarium for early-stage fragment elaboration, particularly against challenging targets such as EcDsbA, where the initial fragment hits bind with very low affinity.

    General Chemistry

    1H and 13C NMR spectra were acquired at 401 and 101 MHz, respectively, on a Bruker Avance III Nanobay spectrometer coupled to a BACS 60 automatic sample changer. Data were processed using MestReNova v6.0.2 software. 1H and 13C spectra were referenced with the residual proteo-solvent peak as follows: 1H NMR shifts: 7.26 ppm CDCl3, 2.50 ppm (CD3)2SO, 3.31 ppm CD3OD, and 0.030 ppm for 4,4-dimethyl-4-silapentane-1-sulfonic acid (DSS) in D2O. 13C NMR shifts: 77.16 ppm CDCl3, 39.52 ppm (CD3)2SO, and 49.00 CD3OD.(22) Overlapped carbon resonance frequencies were confirmed by two-dimensional (2D) 1H–13C HSQC or heteronuclear multiple bond correlation (HMBC) experiments where possible.
    High-resolution mass spectrometry (HRMS) data were acquired on an Agilent 6224 time-of-flight (TOF) LC/MS mass spectrometer coupled to an Agilent 1290 Infinity coupled to a 2795 Alliance separations module. Data were mass-corrected via a dual-spray electrospray ionization (ESI) source with the following parameters: electrospray ionization, drying gas-flow: 11 L/min; nebulizer: 45 psi; drying gas temperature: 325 °C; capillary voltage (Vcap): 4000 V; fragmentor: 160 V; skimmer: 65 V; OCT RFV: 750 V; scan range acquired: 100–1500 m/z. Internal reference ions: positive ion mode = m/z = 121.050873 and 922.009798. Acquisition and analysis were performed using Agilent Mass Hunter Data Acquisition software vB.05.00 build 5.0.5042.2 and Mass Hunter Qualitative Analysis vB.05.00 build 5.0.519.13, respectively.
    Liquid chromatography–mass spectroscopy (LCMS) data were acquired using one of two methods, method 1 or method 2 as specified in individual experiments. Method 1 used a Shimadzu 2020 MS system (equipped with a NexeraX2 SIL-3AC autosampler, CTO-20A oven, NexeraX2 LC-30AD binary pump system, DGU-20A degasser, SPD-M30A photodiode array detector, CBM-20A communication BUS manager), and the data were processed using LabSolutions v.5.80 software. The system was equipped with a reverse-phase Merck C18 Chromolith FastGradient RP-18 endcapped (50 mm × 2.0 mm monolithic, 100 Å, 2 μm) column maintained at 35 °C. Standard gradient: 0–0.25 min isocratic 5% buffer B, 0.25–3.5 min gradient to 100% buffer B, 3.5–4 min isocratic 100% buffer B, 4.0–4.01 min gradient to 5% buffer B, 4.01–5.0 min isocratic 5% buffer B at 35 °C. Buffer A: 99.9% H2O, 0.1% AcOH; buffer B: 99.9% MeCN, 0.1% AcOH. Method 2 used an Agilent 6100 series single Quad LC/MS (equipped with a 1200 series G13111A quaternary pump, a G1329A thermostatted autosampler, and a G1314B variable wavelength detector), and the data were processed using LC/MSD Chemstation rev.B.04.01 SP1 coupled with easy access software. The system was equipped with a reverse-phase luna C8(2) (5 μm, 50 × 4.6 mm2, 100 Å) column maintained at 30 °C. All data were acquired and reference mass-corrected via a dual-spray electrospray ionization (ESI) source. Each scan or data point on the total ion chromatogram (TIC) is the average of 13 700 transients, producing one spectrum per second. Mass spectra were created by averaging the scans across each peak and background-subtracted against the first 10 s of the TIC. Data acquisition was carried out using Agilent Mass Hunter Data Acquisition Software version B.05.00 build 5.0.5042, and analysis was performed using Mass Hunter Qualitative Analysis version B.05.00 build 5.0.519.13. Elution in the LCMS standard method was performed on a gradient of 5% buffer B to 100% buffer B over 6 min at 0.5 mL/min. Buffer A = 99.9% H2O, 0.1% TFA. Buffer B = 99.9% MeCN, 0.1% TFA.
    The purity and retention time of final products were determined on an Agilent 1260 Infinity analytical reverse-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) system fitted with an Agilent Zorbex SB-C18 rapid-resolution high-throughput 2.1 mm × 50 mm, 1.8 μm column using the specified gradient of buffer A: 99.9% H2O, 0.1% TFA; and buffer B: 99.9% MeCN, 0.1% TFA. Standard method 95% A and 5% B over 0.1 min, a gradient of 5–100% buffer B gradient over 9 min, and 100% buffer B over 2 min at a flow rate of 1 mL/min monitored at both 214 and 254 nm using a Waters 996 photodiode array detector. All screening compounds were >95% pure as determined by HPLC analysis.
    Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) was carried out on silica gel 60 F254 precoated plates (0.25 mm, Merck). TLC plates were visualized under UV illumination at 254 nm.     Protein Expression and Purification

    Unlabeled and uniformly 15N-labeled EcDsbA were expressed using autoinduction as described previously.(9,23) All media components were sterilized prior to use by autoclave or 0.22 μm filtration. Pre-expression cultures were prepared by inoculating 10 mL of Luria–Bertani broth supplemented with 50 μg/mL kanamycin from frozen glycerol stocks of E. coli BL21(DE3) carrying the plasmid B0013-(5644bb) coding for EcDsbA, as described previously,(24) and incubating for 16 h at 37 °C with agitation at 220 rpm. ZYM-5052 or N-5052 media supplemented with 50 μg/mL kanamycin in baffled conical flasks were inoculated with 1% v/v preculture and incubated for 24–30 h at 37 °C with agitation at 170 rpm, after which the cells were harvested by centrifugation at 3200g at 4 °C for 20 min. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellets were stored at −20 °C for subsequent protein extraction and purification.
    Each frozen bacterial pellet was thawed, resuspended in an equal volume of osmotic shock buffer (20 mM Tris (pH 9.0), 10 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), 50% w/v sucrose), and maintained at 4 °C with gentle stirring for 60 min, after which the cell suspension was rapidly diluted to 10 times its volume with deionized H2O at 4 °C. The suspension was maintained at 4 °C and stirred for a further 90 min before the cell pellet and periplasmic extract were separated by centrifugation at 50 000g at 4 °C for 30 min. The lysate was carefully decanted from the pellet and stored at −20 °C. The cell pellet was resuspended in 10 times its volume of lysis buffer (20 mM Tris (pH 8.0), 25 mM NaCl, 4 mg/mL colistin sulfate) and maintained at room temperature with gentle stirring for 18–24 h. This suspension was then centrifuged at 50 000g at 4 °C for 30 min, and the lysate was decanted and added to the thawed osmotic shock lysate. The pellet was discarded, and the target protein was purified from the combined lysate. (NH4)2SO4 was added to the combined lysate with gentle stirring to a concentration of 0.8 M, and the solution was syringe-filtered (0.22 μm, Millipore) for loading onto a HiLoad 1610 Phenyl Sepharose HP hydrophobic interaction chromatography column (GE Healthcare) using 20 mM Tris (pH 8.0), 50 mM NaCl, and 1 M (NH4)2SO4. The bound proteins were eluted on a gradient from 1 to 0 M (NH4)2SO4. Fractions were analyzed by sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE), and those containing proteins running at a mass consistent with EcDsbA were pooled and concentrated to a volume of 10 mL using an Amicon centrifugal diafiltration unit (10 000 molecular weight cutoff (MWCO), Millipore). This sample was buffer-exchanged to 50 mM N-(2-hydroxyethyl)piperazine-N′-ethanesulfonic acid (HEPES) (pH 6.8) and 50 mM NaCl using a HiPrep 2610 desalting column (GE Healthcare). Following the buffer exchange, the fractions were analyzed by SDS-PAGE as above, and those containing EcDsbA were pooled and loaded onto a MonoQ HR 10/10 anion-exchange column (GE Healthcare). In these buffer conditions, purified EcDsbA was collected in the flow-through fraction. Protein purity was confirmed by SDS-PAGE.
    Following the anion-exchange chromatography step, the EcDsbA solution was concentrated to a volume of approximately 8–9 mL (Amicon 3000 MWCO, Millipore) and treated with 0.1 volume of freshly prepared 15 mM copper–phenanthroline solution to oxidize the active site disulfide. After 1 h at 4 °C, copper–phenanthroline was removed from the protein solution by buffer exchange (50 mM HEPES pH 6.8, 50 mM NaCl) using a HiPrep 2610 desalting column.
    Protein samples were adjusted to their required final concentrations using Amicon centrifugal diafiltration units (3000 MWCO, Millipore) or dilution using a storage/NMR buffer (50 mM HEPES (pH 6.8), 50 mM NaCl). For NMR experiments, the buffer was exchanged to 50 mM sodium phosphate, 25 mM NaCl, pH 6.8, and D2O was added to a final concentration of 10% (v/v); see below. Final protein concentrations were quantified using a NanoVue UV spectrophotometer (GE Healthcare) based on a calculated extinction coefficient for oxidized EcDsbA of ε = 28545 M–1 cm–1.

    Crystallization of apo EcDsbA

    The previously published crystallization conditions were used as a guide to obtain apo-EcDsbA crystals.(9,25) Protein was crystallized using hanging drop vapor diffusion. Then, 1 μL of 30 mg/mL EcDsbA was mixed with an equal volume of crystallization buffer (11–13% poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) 8000, 5–7.5% glycerol, 1 mM CuCl2, 100 mM sodium cacodylate, pH 6.1) and equilibrated against 0.5 mL of reservoir buffer at 18 °C. Crystals appeared within one to three days and grew to average dimensions of 0.6 mm × 0.4 mm × 0.2 mm.

    Soaking of EcDsbA Crystals with Reagent Mixtures

    Reagent mixtures for crystal soaking trials were prepared for three coupling reagents: EDC (200 mM in Milli-Q H2O), COMU (200 mM in DMF), and HATU (200 mM in DMF). EcDsbA crystals were soaked with each coupling agent at a final concentration of 20 mM for 3 h and then flash-cooled in liquid nitrogen and stored under liquid nitrogen in AS-type pucks in a Taylor Wharton CX100 Dewar, which had been precooled with liquid nitrogen.
    The final soaking protocol for evaluating binding of the crude reaction product library involved transferring EcDsbA crystals into 2 μL drops, which had been dispensed onto coverslips and comprised 24% PEG 8000, 22% glycerol, and 100 mM sodium cacodylate at pH 6.1 plus the crude reaction product in 10% v/v DMSO. The nominal concentration of the crude reaction products in the drop was 10 mM, which was calculated assuming 100% conversion in the reaction. Subsequently, coverslips were placed over the wells of crystallization plates containing 500 μL of the reservoir buffer and sealed. Crystals were left in this solution for 2–3 h and then flash-cooled in liquid nitrogen stored under liquid nitrogen in AS-type pucks in a Taylor Wharton CX100 Dewar precooled with liquid nitrogen prior to measuring diffraction data.

Phenylacetic acid 2 and pyrazole 9 were added to each well as per Table S4, and the solution was mixed. Crystals of EcDsbA were soaked in each solution for 3 h as described above, looped out of the solution, flash-cooled in liquid nitrogen, and stored in loops under liquid nitrogen in AS-type pucks in a precooled Taylor Wharton CX100 Dewar.

    Cocrystallization and Cryoprotection of Purified Compound/EcDsbA Complexes

    Purified resynthesized compounds were cocrystallized with EcDsbA. For cocrystallization experiments, compounds were solubilized in DMSO to a final concentration of 100 mM. Prior to the crystallization experiment, 95 μL of 30 mg/mL EcDsbA was mixed with 5 μL of the compound to give a final compound concentration of 5 mM in 5% v/v DMSO and incubated at 4 °C overnight. The conditions used to obtain the cocrystals (100–300 mM KBr, 25–35% PEG 2000 monomethyl ether (MME)) were based on previous reports.(9) Drops comprising 200/200 nL of protein–ligand solution/crystallization buffer were set up in sitting-drop vapor diffusion plates using an automated robotic facility in the CSIRO Collaborative Crystallization Centre (www.csiro.au/C3), Melbourne, Australia. Plates were incubated at 18 °C, and crystals appeared after 1–5 days. Prior to data collection, crystals were harvested with Hampton Research CryoLoops, briefly swiped through reservoir buffer, and flash-cooled in liquid nitrogen. Vitrified crystals were stored in AS-type pucks and shipped to the Australian synchrotron in a Taylor Wharton CX100 dry shipping dewar, which had been precooled with liquid nitrogen.

    Automated Data Processing, Data Collection Statistics Analysis, and Refinement of Crystals Soaked with Reagent Mixtures

    Library data were processed using the automated data-processing pipeline implemented at the MX1 beamline, where data were indexed, integrated, and scaled with xdsme and AIMLESS.(16−18) The resulting data collection statistics for each data set were reviewed. For all data sets, the resolution cutoff used was based on the following criteria in the highest resolution range: CC1/2 was at least 0.6, ⟨I/σ(I)⟩ was greater than 1.0, and completeness was greater than 90%.(26) In cases where data collection statistics did not fulfill these quality requirements or crystals diffracted to lower than 2.5 Å resolution, the crystallographic experiment was repeated. From the initial data set, nine crude products required repeat soaks. For the repeated soaks, the concentration of the crude reaction component was reduced initially to a nominal 5 mM and further to 2.5 mM if required. For compound 7, crystals were observed to dissolve in the 2.5 mM solution, and in this case, the concentration of the crude component was reduced to a nominal 1 mM and the soaking time was cut down to 20 min. Overall 110 data sets were collected for the crude reaction products from the library. The data sets were phased by MR using chain A of the apo-EcDsbA structure (PDB: 1FVK) as a search model.(27) MR and automated refinement steps were carried out using the automated MR pipeline implemented in Auto-Rickshaw.(19,20) Briefly, MR was performed using the MOLREP(28) automated refinement protocol. This involved initial refinement in CNS(29) followed by refinement in REFMAC5.(30) In the CNS rigid-body refinement, B-factor (BINDIVIDUAL refinement) and positional refinement were undertaken. The resulting model was used in REFMAC5 for maximum likelihood refinement including individual B-factor refinement. The final round of refinement was carried out in PHENIX(31,32) using rigid body, individual sites, individual sites in real space, and individual atomic displacement parameter (ADP) refinement with the addition of water molecules. TLS refinement was not used at this stage. The range of Rwork and Rfree values after automated refinement of the 90 library-soaked diffraction data sets is reported in Figure S2. At this stage, the models were inspected manually using Coot.(33) In parallel, these models were used as input to PanDDA.(6) To enable the PanDDA analysis, apo-EcDsbA crystals were soaked with DMSO to generate 35 ground-state models. For each “event,” the PanDDA maps were inspected.
    The structural models of four data sets that were identified as hits (compounds 6–9) were manually refined as described below. The resulting molecular models were deposited to the protein databank (PDB). Statistics for the structures are presented in Table S5. All automatically refined data sets and structure factors have been deposited to the PDB (accession codes are given in Table 1). X-ray data collection and refinement statistics for the crystals soaked with the 90 unpurified reaction products from the library are reported in Figure S1.

    Manual Data Processing, Phasing, and Refinement

    The data sets collected for cocrystals of EcDsbA in complex with purified compounds were processed with XDS(16) or IMOSFLM(34) and AIMLESS.(17,35) Phasing was performed by MR with Phaser(36) using chain A of EcDsbA (PDB code 1FVK)(27) as a search model. Iterative cycles of manual model building and refinement were performed using Coot(33) and phenix.refine.(31,32) The B-factor model consisted of the isotropic B-factor for each non-hydrogen atom plus TLS refinement.     Assessing Solubility of Synthesized Analogues by qNMR

    Purified compounds were evaluated for their solubility and propensity to aggregate in aqueous solutions by recording a series of 1D 1H qNMR experiments at increasing concentrations. Compounds were accurately weighed and dissolved in DMSO-d6 at a notional concentration of 100 mM. The NMR buffer for these experiments was prepared with 50 mM sodium phosphate and 25 mM NaCl at pH 7.4 in 100% D2O containing 100 μM 4,4-dimethyl-4-silapentane-1-sulfonic acid (DSS) as an internal standard. A serial dilution of the DMSO-d6 stock was performed to prepare a four-point twofold dilution series with final notional concentrations of 125, 250, 500,and 1000 μM containing 2% DMSO-d6 in the NMR buffer in a total volume of 600 μL. NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker 400 MHz spectrometer with TD = 32 K, D1 = 5 s, 64 scans, and water suppression at 4.7 ppm. NMR spectra were processed in MNOVA and referenced to the DSS peak at 0.030 ppm. Compounds were deemed to have failed the solubility measure at a given concentration if they did not show a doubling in intensity concordant with doubling concentration or if any resonances in the spectrum showed chemical shift changes >0.004 ppm (1.6 Hz) from the lowest recorded concentration. Ligand concentrations were calculated relative to the DSS integral in the 1D 1H qNMR spectra and used to calculate the correct stock concentration, and this corrected concentration was used in subsequent experiments for KD determination.
