    Abstract

    Signal peptide peptidase-like 2a (SPPL2a) is an aspartic intramembrane protease which has recently been shown to play an important role in the development and function of antigen presenting cells such as B lymphocytes and dendritic cells. In this paper, we describe the discovery of the first selective and orally active SPPL2a inhibitor (S)-2-cyclopropyl-N1-((S)-5,11-dioxo-10,11-dihydro-1H,3H,5H-spiro[benzo[d]pyrazolo[1,2-a][1,2]diazepine-2,1′-cyclopropan]-10-yl)-N4-(5-fluoro-2-methylpyridin-3-yl)succinamide 40 (SPL-707). This compound shows adequate selectivity against the closely related enzymes γ-secretase and SPP and a good pharmacokinetic profile in mouse and rat. Compound 40 significantly inhibited processing of the SPPL2a substrate CD74/p8 fragment in rodents at doses ≤10 mg/kg b.i.d. po. Oral dosing of 40 for 11 days at ≥10 mg/kg b.i.d. recapitulated the phenotype seen in Sppl2a knockout (ko) and ENU mutant mice (reduced number of specific B cells and myeloid dendritic cells). Thus, we believe that SPPL2a represents an interesting and druggable pharmacological target, potentially providing a novel approach for the treatment of autoimmune diseases by targeting B cells and dendritic cells.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Regulated intramembrane proteolysis (RIP) is one of the processes controlling communication between cells and the extracellular environment.(1) RIP is mediated by a special family of proteases, the intramembrane cleaving proteases (I-CLiPs), which control signaling pathways by cleaving the transmembrane region of their substrates into extracellular and intracellular domains.(2) Depending on their catalytic center, metallo, aspartyl, and serine I-CLiPs have been described.(3) The most prominent and best characterized member of this enzyme class is γ-secretase, an aspartyl I-CLiP involved in the generation of the Aβ peptide from the amyloid precursor protein (APP), which is found in amyloid plaques in the brain of Alzheimer disease patients and thus is involved in the pathology of Alzheimer’s disease.(4) SPPL2a (signal peptide peptidase-like 2a) has recently been described as an enzyme related to presenilins, the catalytic subunit of γ-secretase.(5, 6) SPPL2a is an aspartyl I-CLiP mainly located in the membrane of endosomes,(7) containing nine transmembrane domains (TMD) bearing the two catalytic aspartates on TMD6 and TMD7. It belongs to the signal peptide peptidase/signal peptide peptidase-like (SPP/SPPL) family of aspartic intramembrane proteases which includes SPP, SPPL2a, SPPL2b, SPPL2c, and SPPL3. Whereas the topology of the γ-secretase complex enables cleavage of type I transmembrane proteins, members of the SPP/SPPL family cleave type II transmembrane proteins. In addition, γ-secretase is known as a complex, with presenilin being the catalytically active part and three other proteins (nicastrin, presenilin enhancer 2 (Pen-2) and anteriorpharynx-defective 1 (Aph-1), while SPPL2a does not seem to require any additional cofactors.(8) SPPL2a was initially shown to be engaged in the processing of the TNFα N-terminal fragment (NTF) in vitro, leading to IL-12 production in human dendritic cells.(7) SPPL2a cleavage of type II membrane proteins like TNFα requires a prior shedding of their ectodomain by sheddases (mainly metalloproteases such as, e.g., ADAM 10 or ADAM17),(7) a process which generates the actual SPPL2a substrates, the remaining membrane bound NTF.(8) Several type II-oriented transmembrane proteins including FasL/CD95,(9) Bri2(Itm2b),(10) CD74,(11-13) TMEM106B,(14) and NRG1 type III,(15) and even a type III protein FVenv (viral),(16) were shown to be processed by SPPL2a. To date, however, the NTF of CD74 (invariant chain of the major histocompatibility complex II, MHC-II) is the only substrate shown to be cleaved by SPPL2a in vivo.(11-13) Prior to cleavage by SPPL2a, CD74 is processed sequentially by several proteases, including cathepsin S, which is necessary for the generation of the CLIP peptide, which plays an important role in MHC-II associated antigen presentation. Processing of full-length CD74 finally leads to the generation of the N-terminal 8 kDa CD74, p8 (CD74/p8) transmembrane fragment, which in turn is further processed by SPPL2a generating a short cytoplasmic peptide p4 (4 kDa). Studies in SPPL2a deficient mice showed that accumulation of nonphysiological amounts of CD74/p8 leads to an arrest of splenic B cell maturation, resulting in the depletion of mature B cells. In addition, accumulation of p8 leads to a strong reduction in myeloid dendritic cell (mDC) numbers in SPPL2a-deficient mice.(11-13) However, in Sppl2a/CD74 double knockout (ko) mice, the development of mature B cells and mDCs was restored,(11) indicating that the accumulation of the CD74/p8 fragment has a detrimental effect on the development of antigen presenting cells like B cells and mDCs. The disturbed B cell development observed upon inhibition of SPPL2a correlates with an impaired tonic B cell receptor, and the accumulation of the CD74/p8 negatively impacts the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway, leading to activation of pro-apoptotic genes p21, p27, and Bim in B220+ cells.(17) These results suggest that SPPL2a is strongly involved in the regulation of the adaptive immunity, and thus by inhibiting SPPL2a it might be possible to reduce its antigen presenting capacity.(18) Such a reduction could be therapeutically beneficial in autoimmune diseases, among others rheumatoid arthritis or multiple sclerosis, where presentation of autoantigens is a strong driver of the pathology.(19) To further investigate the effect of SPPL2a inhibition on the immune system, we aimed to develop an orally available, selective, low molecular weight SPPL2a inhibitor, and our results are disclosed in this publication.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    SPPL2a is an aspartyl i-CLiP related to presenilin, the catalytic subunit of the γ-secretase complex. For decades, γ-secretase was an important target explored by many pharmaceutical companies interested in the discovery of a low molecular weight inhibitor for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease and cancer.(20) Thanks to these efforts, several γ-secretase inhibitors were developed and reached the clinic. Besides the amyloid precursor protein (APP) whose proteolysis by γ-secretase leads to β-amyloid (Aβ) formation, the primary component of the amyloid plaques found in the brain of Alzheimer disease patients, Notch is another prominent γ-secretase substrate class.(21) Aberrant Notch1 signaling is implicated in different cancer subtypes and therefore focus of several clinical investigations of γ-secretase inhibitors for the treatment of cancer.(22) However, inhibition of Notch signaling bares the risk of serious side effects including severe intestinal toxicity or development of skin cancer as seen in Alzheimer patients treated with γ-secretase inhibitors.(23, 24) Recently, it was shown that some γ-secretase inhibitors are only partially selective and inhibit also SPPL-family members.(25, 26) Therefore, we have tested several literature described γ-secretase inhibitors for their effect on SPPL2a along with 41 (Z-LL2),(7) a literature known SPPL2a inhibitor. A reporter gene assay (RGA) was used to evaluate the inhibition of the SPPL2a activity. The assay uses HEK293 cells cotransfected with expression vectors for human SPPL2a, TNFα-NTF fused to the VP16 transactivation domain, Gal4 transcription factor, and a Gal4-driven luciferase reporter plasmid. In the absence of an SPPL2a inhibitor, the N-terminal fragment of the cleaved substrate fused to VP16/Gal4 translocates into the nucleus and drives expression of luciferase which is assessed by luminescent measurement. The same assay format was used also for the assessment of the inhibitory effect on γ-secretase mediated cleavage of Notch1. However, in this case, only a Notch1-based substrate fused to VP16/Gal4 and the luciferase reporter plasmid are cotransfected into HEK293 cells, which bear endogenous γ-secretase activity.
    While 41 was highly potent on SPPL2a with a selectivity of >1000 against γ-secretase processing of Notch1 (Table 1), its peptidic nature and the presence of a ketone functionality discouraged us to further optimize this scaffold. On the other hand, the γ-secretase inhibitor 42 (LY-411,575)(25, 26) displays more drug like properties(27) and its high potency on SPPL2a in the low nanomolar range made it an appealing starting point. Even if 42 inhibits γ-secretase mediated Notch1 processing with a significantly lower, subnanomolar IC50 (Table 1), replacement of the dibenzoazepinone in 42 with a benzodiazepinone tricycle(28) and the use of a succinate instead of an α-amino acid backbone provided compound 30, which kept the SPPL2a potency while losing γ-secretase activity. The new scaffold seemed fairly modular for achieving our goal, i.e., finding a selective, orally available SPPL2a inhibitor.

    Chemistry

    The tricycles were accessed by formation of the central diazepinone ring (Scheme 1). Compound 2 was synthesized by a cyclization of pyrazolidine(29) with a chloro-ester 1 being prepared by thionyl chloride mediated ring opening of isochromanone.(30) The primary amine in 4 was introduced by oxime (3) formation followed by its reduction. The required (S)-amine 7 was obtained by chiral separation of rac-Boc-amine 5 using preparative chiral SFC and acidic cleavage of the Boc-group in 6. RuO2-mediated oxidation(31) of the benzylic position in 6 led to compound 8, which provided an enantiomerically pure oxo-analogue 9 after cleavage of the Boc-protecting group.
    Fluoro-tricyclic amine 15 was prepared from fluoroisochromanone 11 (Scheme 2) using the synthesis described for 7. The prerequisite 8-fluoro-isochromane-3-one (11) was synthesized starting from 2-bromo-6-fluorobenzoic acid by a copper mediated introduction of a carboethoxymethyl group,(32) followed by chemoselective NaBH4 reduction of the acid 10 upon its activation to a mixed anhydride and TsOH cyclization. A spiro-cyclopropyl analogue of the oxo-tricycle 9 was prepared according to the same route as described for 7, with the exception that the intermediate 17 was obtained by cyclization(33) of homophthalic anhydride with spiro-cyclopropyl pyrazolidine 16. This intermediate (16) was prepared from cyclopropanedimethanol by its activation into bismesylate, followed by its reaction with N,N′-di-Boc-hydrazine and cleavage of Boc-protecting groups.
    The required chiral succinates were prepared using the Evans auxiliary method(34) developed for preparation of enantiomerically pure α-branched aliphatic carboxylic acids (Scheme 3). Accordingly, the acylated (S)-oxazolidines (19 and 22) were stereoselectively alkylated with t-Bu bromoacetate and the desired monoprotected succinates 21 and 24 were obtained with high diastereoselectivity (as assessed by 1H NMR of 20 and 23) after cleavage of the chiral auxiliary in intermediates 20 and 23. These acids were subsequently attached to the tricyclic amines by amide coupling. After hydrolysis of the t-Bu ester, aromatic amines were introduced by amide coupling employing either EDC in pyridine or by activation of the acid with MsCl. Such conditions were required to complete the coupling with a reasonable yield because most of the used anilines were poor nucleophiles. In the case of o-toluidine, HATU as a coupling reagent also provided the corresponding product 31 in a good yield. The absolute stereochemistry of the SPPL2a active compounds was unambiguously determined by the X-ray crystal structure obtained for 40 (Supporting Information).

    Optimization

    The initial optimization was focused on improving the selectivity against γ-secretase. SAR around the aniline part revealed that this region was very sensitive for both SPPL2a activity and γ-secretase selectivity (Table 2). While the methyl substituent in the ortho-position (31) was tolerated by SPPL2a, the γ-secretase activity was negatively influenced, leading to the first nonpeptidic SPPL2a inhibitor with decent γ-secretase selectivity. In contrast, the methyl substituent in para-position was not well accepted by SPPL2a, but such an analogue (33) was potently inhibiting γ-secretase. Interestingly, the meta-substituent (32) seemed to be still tolerated by SPPL2a while it was less potent on γ-secretase, thus providing some degree of selectivity against γ-secretase also by meta-substitution. Without any structural information, it is difficult to fully understand what drives the SPPL2a selectivity. Still, one may speculate that in SPPL2a there is a pocket fitting the ortho-substituent which seems to be shifted toward the para-position in γ-secretase. In addition, in SPPL2a the pocket must be of a limited size as already trifluoromethyl substituent is detrimental for SPPL2a activity as exemplified by 34 (Table 2).

    Further in vitro profiling of 31, at that stage the most promising compound, showed that it also blocked the SPPL2a dependent processing of the CD74/p8 fragment in the mouse B cell line A20 with an IC50 of 0.37 μM (Table 4). It means that the endogenous SPPL2a and the cleavage of its endogenous, in vivo relevant substrate could be potently inhibited by 31. Moreover, 31 also kept activity in a CD74/p8-based mouse whole blood assay (see methods for detailed assay description) with an IC50 of 0.55 μM. In addition, 31 showed favorable pharmacokinetic parameters in mice (clearance, 46 mL/min/kg; Vss, 1.2 L/kg; t1/2, 0.7 h; F, ∼100%, AUCpo dn, 908 h·nM), which allowed to assess its in vivo effects on SPPL2a mediated processing of the CD74/p8 fragment. Oral dosing of 31 or 42 to mice resulted in a pronounced accumulation of the SPPL2a substrate CD74/p8 in splenocytes of treated mice 4 h after compound administration (Figure 1A). Even a single 10 mg/kg oral dose of 31 strongly inhibited CD74/p8 processing at an average terminal spleen concentration of 127 ± 37 nM at the 4 h time point (Figure 1B), which was already below the determined whole blood activity of 31. This probably led to the observed higher variability within the 10 mg/kg dosing group. However, even in the 10 mg/kg dose group, the exposure of 31 was long enough above the critical concentration necessary to inhibit SPPL2a in vivo to enable visible CD74/p8 accumulation. In addition, the expression of Hes1 mRNA, a direct Notch1 target gene and established biomarker for monitoring Notch1 mediated toxicity,(35, 36) was assessed in jejunum and thymus from the same animals (Figure 1C). The data showed that 31 did not affect Notch1 processing at any dose. On the contrary, a significant reduction of Hes1 mRNA levels were observed in mice treated with 42, indicative of γ-secretase inhibition and as a consequence of perturbed Notch1 signaling (Figure 1C). This indicates that a 50-fold selectivity over γ-secretase, as measured in our cellular assays, was sufficient to avoid the unwanted side effects caused by inhibition of Notch1 processing in vivo even at the 100 mg/kg dose.
    To evaluate the reversibility of SPPL2a inhibition and accumulation of CD74/p8, an in vitro washout experiment was performed (Figure 2). In this experiment, B cells were incubated with 31 for 16 h to allow maximal accumulation of CD74/p8. Then the cells were washed with medium lacking inhibitor and further incubated at 37 °C for up to 2 h and subsequently analyzed for remaining p8 levels by Western blotting. The kinetic analysis showed a fast reversibility of the CD74/p8 accumulation with a 50% reduction within ∼25 min after washout, and a reduction to undetectable levels after 2 h. These data demonstrate that inhibition of SPPL2a with compound 31 is reversible. It indicates that a continuous inhibition of SPPL2a in vivo is needed for ensuring a robust accumulation of CD74/p8, a process required for a functional in vivo effect, e.g., a reduction of B cells and dendritic cells as described for Sppl2a deficient mice.(11-13) On the basis of this hypothesis, the PK parameters of 31 (clearance and t1/2) in mouse and rat appeared suboptimal and, consequently, high doses would be required to reach a prolonged high level of SPPL2a inhibition in vivo, essential for testing in chronic animal models. Therefore, 31 was not considered for the exploration of the pharmacological effects of an SPPL2a inhibitor in a broad range of chronic animal models and focus was shifted toward the further improvement of the PK parameters.
    For further compound optimization toward lower total clearance, the metabolism of unlabeled 31 was examined following incubations with liver microsomes from mouse (MLM), rat (RLM), and human (HLM). The results, summarized in Figure 3, suggested that 31 was metabolized more rapidly in human liver microsomes but leading to the same metabolic profile compared to rodent’s liver microsomes (Table 3). The structure of the identified metabolites were proposed based on elemental composition, fragmentation relative to the parent compound, and number of exchangeable hydrogen atoms. The investigation proposed metabolism of 31 by aromatic and aliphatic C-hydroxlyation which affects mainly the aniline (m3, m4) and the tricycle part (m2, m5–m8) while the linker moiety remained unaffected.

    In addition to the oxidative metabolism, a proteolytic amide cleavage was observed which leads to metabolite m1 (25) at low abundance. By formation of carboxylic acid 25, the release of o-toluidine can be assumed. This metabolite is a known carcinogen suspected of causing bladder cancer.(37) Therefore, the aromatic amine had to be replaced (Table 4). While the exact mechanism leading to the mutagenic effect of anilines is not yet fully understood, it seems that DNA damage mediated by oxidative o-toluidine metabolites (o-nitrosotoluene and 4-amino-3-methylphenol) is responsible for its carcinogenicity.(38) There is a general agreement that the carcinogenicity of aromatic amines is mainly driven by their metabolic N-oxidation into corresponding N-hydroxylamines. Such species can further be O-conjugated by acetylation or sulfonylation which, after cleavage, leads to a nitrenium ion that reacts with and modifies DNA.(37) Mutagenicity of aromatic amines was shown to increase with increasing lipophilicity, a parameter that might indicate the ability of chemicals to undergo metabolism required for their activation.(39) Hence, more polar, heterocyclic amines were explored and combined with the ortho-methyl substituent required for γ-secretase selectivity (Table 4).

    A simple pyridine analogue 35 was not well tolerated by SPPL2a. Because the 3,5-difluorophenyl compound 30 showed a high SPPL2a activity and halogens such as fluorine or chlorine in meta-position to the aromatic amine may reduce the risk of mutagenicity,(40)35 was decorated with a fluorine in the 5-position. The resulting analogue 36 showed indeed a dramatic improvement in potency, while the 3-amino-5-fluoro-2-methylpyridine proved to be negative in the Ames test,(41) a gene mutation test in bacteria which uses back-mutation to histidine proficiency as the end point. Moreover, the compound was more potent in the CD74/p8 processing assay, including the CD74/p8 processing in the presence of mouse whole blood (IC50 of 0.050 μM for 36 vs 0.550 μM for 31), and showed also improved γ-secretase selectivity and solubility (Table 4).
    As the next step, the tricycle was optimized to block the main metabolic weak spot, the benzylic-CH2 position. For this purpose, two hypotheses were tested. The first idea was based on the assumption that a neighboring halogen (e.g., in the ortho-position on the phenyl ring) may decrease the electron density at this position and may therefore protect it from metabolic oxidation. While the fluorine atom in 37 was tolerated for keeping SPPL2a potency, the in vivo clearance in rat was indeed improved (Table 5). However, the oral exposure remained unchanged as a result of lower oral bioavailability (54% for 37 vs ∼100% for 36) most probably caused by decreased solubility of 37 (257 μM at pH 6.8 vs >1000 μM for 36). The second approach aimed for direct blocking of the oxidation by adding an oxo-substituent to the benzylic position (38). Unfortunately, the oxo-substituent in this position was not well tolerated as the SPPL2a potency was significantly decreased (38 vs 36, Table 5). Because 38 became fairly polar with a log P of 0.9 as compared to 1.7 for 36, we set out to increase the overall lipophilicity to compensate the decreased potency, which might have been caused by impaired cell permeability due to the high polarity. Adding a cyclopropyl group into the linker led to analogue 39 with increased SPPL2a potency and a log P of 1.6, being comparable to 36. A second cyclopropyl group on the tricycle like in compound 40 (SPL-707) helped again to increase the lipophilicity (log P of 2.3) and resulted in a further improved SPPL2a potency. While 40 displayed similar potency as 36, a significantly lower clearance and much higher oral exposure was observed for this compound in rats and mice. The oral exposure determined following a 10 mg/kg dose of 40 in rats appeared suitable to achieve sustained exposure after b.i.d. dosing in a chronic in vivo study because the drug concentration might be sufficient to achieve CD74/p8 accumulation even at later time points (Cmax,1h dn, 805 nM; C7h dn, 421 nM). In addition, this compound displayed the best mouse pharmacokinetic parameters measured for any SPPL2a inhibitor in this series (CL, 5 mL/min/kg; F, 94%; AUCpo dn, 6071 h·nM; C7h dn, 264 nM) and thus allowed for testing the compound in both mouse and rat models.

The EGFP-labeled substrates contain a nuclear localization signal (NLS) which drives translocation of the cleaved substrate into the nucleus. The inhibition of SPPL2a prevents substrate cleavage and thus also the translocation of EGFP-NLS to the nucleus. The same assay format was used to assess selectivity against proteases from the same family.     Thereafter, 40 was tested in the wash-out experiment as described for 31 (Figure 2). Complete disappearance of CD74/p8 after removal of 40 occurred after 2 h, while 50% reduction was observed at around 50 min (data not shown). These results are consistent with those observed for 31, reinforcing the need for continuous SPPL2a inhibition to achieve a permanent p8 accumulation. Subsequently, 40 was tested first in the mouse PK/PD model described for 31. However, to predict performance in a chronic study with b.i.d. dosing using 8 and 16 h dosing intervals, readout of the study was set to 16 h post oral dosing of 40 (Figure 4A). A similar inhibition of CD74/p8-fragment processing was seen following a single oral dose of either 30 mg/kg of 40 or 150 mg/kg of 31. A significant p8 accumulation (∼50% of maximal) was observed even at the 10 mg/kg dose of 40, while no p8 accumulation was detected for the lower doses of 40, as could be expected by the low plasma levels observed at 16 h post dose (Figure 4B).
    Compound 40 was also highly efficacious in a 24 h rat PK/PD model, in which two oral doses of 40 were administered to more accurately predict performance in chronic b.i.d. dosing studies with 8 and 16 h dosing intervals (Figure 5). In this experiment, full inhibition of CD74/p8 processing was achieved already with 1 mg/kg 40 b.i.d. in blood and 3 mg/kg 40 b.i.d. dose in spleen, as assessed by measuring relative CD74/p8 accumulation (Figure 5A). Total blood and spleen concentrations of 40 were indicative for a nearly dose proportional increase in trough concentration between 1 and 10 mg/kg (Figure 5B). Based on the results obtained in the PK/PD models, 40 was expected to inhibit SPPL2a activity continuously and therefore be efficacious in multiday in vivo studies at an oral dose of 3 mg/kg b.i.d. in rat and 10–15 mg/kg b.i.d. in mice, respectively.
    Based on the published Sppl2a ko and Sppl2a ENU mutant mouse phenotype, there is a fair amount of evidence that accumulation of CD74/p8 is interfering with the development/maturation and function of specific B cell subsets and mDCs.(11-13) The fact that the SPPL2a knockout phenotype is rescued in the SPPL2a/CD74 double knockout mice strongly argues in favor of a dominant effect of CD74/p8 accumulation.(11, 13) To understand to which extent a pharmacological inhibition of SPPL2a recapitulates the Sppl2a ko phenotype or whether the phenotype rather results from SPPL2a ablation during development, 40 was administered orally at 3, 10, and 30 mg/kg b.i.d. to mice for 11 days (Figure 6).
    At termination of this experiment, B cell and DC numbers and subsets in the spleen were analyzed by flow cytometry. At oral doses of 10 and 30 mg/kg b.i.d. 40, a highly significant, dose-dependent reduction of total B cell numbers was observed (Figure 6A) as a result of specific reduction of mature B cell subsets (Figure 6B). In addition, mDC numbers showed a significant, dose-dependent reduction in all dosing groups, whereas plasmacytoid DC (pDC) numbers were not significantly affected (Figure 6C). In line with the observations in the PK/PD models, the lower through plasma concentration of 40 at termination of the study in the 3 mg/kg b.i.d. dosing group (Figure 6D) resulted only in partial accumulation of CD74/p8 (not shown) and as a consequence partial reduction of B cell and mDC numbers. This strongly argues for the need of continuous, full SPPL2a inhibition during the course of the study to achieve pronounced effects on B cell and mDC numbers. Overall, the extent of changes in B cell subsets and mDCs seen in the highest dose group closely resembled the phenotype observed in the Sppl2a mutant and ko mice, therefore clearly showing that the ko phenotype is mostly independent from the effect of SPPL2a deficiency during development. T cell numbers and CD4 CD8 ratio did not show any significant changes at any of the doses of 40, in line to what was observed in Sppl2a mutant/ko mice (not shown). In addition, also at the highest dose, 40 did not interfere with γ-secretase dependent Notch1 signaling as seen by unchanged Hes1 mRNA levels in the thymus (Figure 6E).

    Conclusions

    Jump To
    In this paper, we describe the discovery of the first selective and orally active SPPL2a inhibitor 40. Compound 40 was developed starting from the γ-secretase inhibitor 42. Because of some severe toxic side effects (e.g., intestinal toxicity or development of skin cancer) seen with γ-secretase inhibitors in the clinic, most probably originating from the inhibition of Notch-processing, we wished to find SPPL2a inhibitors avoiding the cross-reactivity with γ-secretase. SAR driven optimization revealed that an aromatic methyl group in the ortho-position to aniline NH was key for achieving selectivity over γ-secretase for this series. The new analogues showed that a selectivity window of >50 against γ-secretase was sufficient for avoiding the Notch-mediated toxic effects in vivo. The suboptimal pharmacokinetic parameters were optimized based on the results obtained from a metabolic weak spot identification study. In mouse and rat, 40 showed highly significant inhibition of CD74/p8 processing at doses ≤10 mg/kg b.i.d. po. In addition, after oral dosing of 40 for 11 days at 10 mg/kg b.i.d. to mice, the phenotype seen with ko and ENU mutant mice (reduced number of B cells and mDCs) was entirely recapitulated. Therefore, we believe that our findings show that SPPL2a is a novel target with promising therapeutic application and could be explored as an approach for the treatment of autoimmune diseases, where antigen presentation is an important driver of pathology.

    Cellular Assays

    Cell Culture

    The mouse B cell line A20 (TIB208, purchased from ATCC) was maintained in complete RPMI1640 medium containing 10% FBS. U-2 OS cells (HTB96, purchased from ATCC) were maintained in DMEM/GlutaMaxTM-I (Invitrogen) supplemented with 10% FBS (Invitrogen). HEK293 cells (CRL-1573, purchased from ATCC). All cells were negatively tested for mycoplasma contamination either by the vendor or in-house.

    SPPL2a and Notch1/γ-Secretase RGA’s in HEK293 Cells

    The SPPL2a luciferase RGA is based on a TNFα(aa1-76)-NTF substrate and the γ-secretase assay has been developed using Notch1 as substrate. The latter assay has been described previously.(26) In brief, DNA vectors encoding human Notch1-VP16-Gal4 fusion protein and a Gal4-luciferase reporter for the γ-secretase RGA, or human SPPL2a, VP16-TNFa(aa1-76)-NTF substrate, and the Gal4-luciferase reporter plasmid for the SPPL2a RGA, were transiently transfected in HEK293 cells using FuGENE (Promega). After transfection, the cell suspensions were diluted and distributed to white solid 384-well plate at 10000 cells/50 μL/well. After 3 h, 200 nL of compound in DMSO was stamped into the wells in concentration response covering final inhibitor concentrations of 10 μM to 0.3 nM in triplicate. Subsequently, plates were incubated for 24 h at 37 °C, 5% CO2 in a humidified incubator before the addition of 30 μL Bright Glo (Promega). After incubation for 5 min at rt, luminescence was measured and IC50 values were determined by plotting compound concentration vs normalized luminescence values.

    SPPL2a, SPP, SPPL2b, Mouse SPPL2a, and Rat SPPL2a High-Content Imaging Assays (HCA) in U-2 OS Cells

    The high-content imaging assays for human SPPL2a, SPPL2b, and SPP using stable U-2 OS cell lines have been described previously.(26) In brief, stable U-2 OS cell lines expressing either human SPPL2a or SPPL2b constitutively and a EGFP-labeled TNFα(aa1-76) NTF substrate under a doxycycline-regulated promoter or human SPP constitutively and a EGFP-labeled EnvSigSeq-SEAP fusion protein substrate under a doxycycline-regulated promoter were used for the imaging assays. The imaging assays to monitor mouse SPPL2a and rat SPPL2a activity were established analogous to the human SPPL2a assay but using the sequence of mouse and rat TNFα(aa1-77)-NTF fused to EGFP-NLS, respectively. For all stable cell lines, cells were seeded at 3000 cells/30 μL/384-well in DMEM/GlutaMaxTM-I (Invitrogen) supplemented with tetracycline-free 10% FBS (Amimed) and incubated at 37 °C, 5% CO2 for 3–4 h. Subsequently, 3.3 μL of inhibitors, prediluted in doxycycline-containing medium for 11-point concentration–response curves, were added to each well using a CyBi well liquid handling device (Cybio AG, Jena, Germany) to result in final inhibitor concentrations ranging from 100 μM to 1 nM (final DMSO concentration 0.9% (v/v)) and 5 μg/mL doxycycline). The cells were incubated with inhibitor at 37 °C, 5% CO2 for 24 h. Thereafter cell were fixed in 4% PFA/PBS and in parallel, nuclei were stained with Hoechst (Invitrogen) 1:5000 in PBS for 30 min. The plates were imaged using a Cellomics ArrayScan VTI HCS reader with 10×/0.3NA objective (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Six images per well were acquired. Images for the EGFP signal (Ex395, Em509) and the Hoechst nuclear dye (Ex350, Em425) were acquired simultaneously with image analysis using the “Nuclear Translocation” assay algorithm of the Cellomics ArrayScan software. Nuclei were detected based on the Hoechst staining, the nuclear mask was transferred to the EGFP channel, and a 4 pixel wide cytoplasm ring region was defined around the nucleus. The intensity of the EGFP signal was measured both in the nuclear and in the cytoplasm ring region of each individual cell (in general 800–1000 individual cells were analyzed per well), and the difference of the average nuclear versus average cytoplasmic intensity of the EGFP signal was calculated (“CircRingAvgIntenDiffCh2” = “CircAvgIntenCh2” – “RingAvgIntenCh2″). Additionally the number of cells were acquired (feature termed “ValidCellCount”) and used to calculate cell toxicity (CC50). Percent inhibition was calculated relative to the positive (0.5 μM 42 = 100% inhibition) and negative (DMSO = 0% inhibition) controls. The IC50 value was calculated from the plot of percentage of inhibition vs inhibitor concentration using nonlinear regression analysis software, e.g., Origin (OriginLab Corp.).

    CD74/p8 Mouse Whole Blood Assay

    First, 200 μL of mouse whole blood were transferred in a 800 μL V-deep well plate containing 10 μL of 20× conc compound in DMSO/D-PBS (final concentration of DMSO was 0.5%) and incubated for 5.5 h at 37 °C, 5% CO2 under the rotation. The blood was then transferred to 1 mL U-deep well plate containing 600 μL of RBC lysis buffer. After extensive washing with RBC lysis buffer, the cell pellet was resuspended 2–3 times with 2× 300 μL of D-PBS and centrifuged. The cell pellet was lysed with 40 μL of lysis buffer (25 mM Tris/HCl pH 6.8/150 mM NaCl/2 mM EDTA/1% Triton X-100/0.5% deoxycholate + complete protease inhibitors) for 20 min and cleared by centrifugation at 4000 rpm for 30 min at 4 °C. Then 12 μL of the lysate was mixed with 10 μL of SDS denaturing solution. The solution was processed by Western blotting, and the CD74/p8 bands were visualized after incubation with a rat antimouse CD74 Ab (ln–1). Dose response was determined by quantifying the p8 band intensity calibrated with the CD74 full-length/b-actin band.

    Metabolite Identification

    In Vitro Experiment with Liver Microsomes

    Stock solutions of 31 (2 mmol/L) were prepared in DMSO. Alamethicin solution was prepared (0.125 mmol/L) in water. UDPGA solution (24 mmol/L) was prepared in phosphate buffer 100 mM pH 7.4. In a further experiment, 50 mmol/L GSH in phosphate buffer 100 mM, pH 7.4, was added as a second cofactor. In all samples with active enzymes, a 1:1 mixture of GSH and [13C2-15N-glycine]-GSH was used. In vitro metabolites were produced by incubation of 31 at 37 °C for up to 60 min with liver microsomes from mouse, rat, and human. Then 3 μL of liver microsomes, containing 20 mg of protein/mL, were mixed with 417 μL of phosphate buffer, 60 μL of alamethicin solution, and 60 μL of UDPGA. To this reaction mixture, 3 μL of stock solution (2 mM in DMSO) of 31 was added and preincubated for 3 min at 37 °C. After preincubation, the final reaction was started by addition of 60 μL of the NADPH-regenerating system, containing isocitrate-dehydrogenase (1 U/mL), NADP (1 mmol/L), and isocitrate (5 mmol/L). After 1 h, the reaction was stopped with 600 μL of ice-cold acetonitrile. Experiments were conducted according to a generic protocol. The reaction mixture was stored at −80 °C.

    Sample Preparation of In Vitro Samples

    Prior to use, the mixture was centrifuged (10000g, 5 min) and 100 μL of supernatant diluted with 400 μL of water. This final solution was centrifuged again, and aliquots of 5–10 μL were used for HPLC/MS analysis.

    Capillary High-Performance Liquid Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry (HPLC/MS)

    Capillary HPLC was performed on a system consisting of a Chorus-220 HPLC pump (CTC Analytics, Zwingen, Switzerland), a Hot Dog-5090 column oven (Prolab, Reinach, Switzerland), and a HTS-PAL autosampler with cooled sample stacks (CTC Analytics, Zwingen, Switzerland). Separations were performed on a Reprosil-Pure-C18-AQ HPLC column (150 mm × 0.3 mm i.d., 3.0 μm particle size) from Maisch (Ammerbuch-Entringen, Germany). Separations were performed at 45 °C. The flow rate was 4.5 μL/min, and the injection volume was 1 μL per separation. The solvent system used consisted of aqueous ammonium formate (10 mM, with 0.02% TFA, pH 4)/MeCN (95/5, v/v) as solvent A and aqueous ammonium formate (10 mM, with 0.02% TFA, pH 4)/MeCN/MeOH (5/90/5, v/v/v) as solvent B. The metabolites were separated using a linear solvent gradient: 0 min (5% B), 2 min (5% B), 27 min (95% B), and 32 min (95% B, 6.5 μL/min). Re-equilibration of the column was performed at 5% B for 5 min. Prior to analysis, 10 μL of sample was diluted 1/100 (v/v) with water/MeCN (90/10, v/v). Samples were kept in the autosampler at 10 °C. A LTQ XL Orbitrap (Linear Quadrupole 2D Ion Trap/Orbitrap, Thermo Scientific, CA, USA) mass spectrometer was used operating in positive mode electrospray ionization (ESI). Settings for the mass spectrometer were 12 (arbitrary units) for sheath gas flow rate (N2) and a capillary temperature of 275 °C. Auxiliary and sweep gas were not used. The settings for capillary voltage and tube lens voltage were 45 and 95 V, respectively. Auto gain control (AGC) target settings were 5 × 105 and 1 × 104 for full MS and MS/MS, respectively. The resolution was set to 30000 in full scan mode. The omnipresent polysiloxane background ion [C2H6SiO]6+ with m/z 445.12003 was used as external lock mass.

    In Vivo Experiments

    All animals used were 5–12 weeks of age and were maintained in the specific pathogen-free facilities. All animal studies were performed in accordance with the animal experimentation guidelines and laws laid down by the Swiss Federal and Cantonal Authorities. Procedures conducted in the US were approved by the Genomics Institute of the Novartis Research Foundation Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee.

    In Vivo Pharmacokinetic Studies in Rats

    First, 96–120 h before administration of the test substance, adult female wild-type Sprague–Dawley rats (Iffa Credo, France) were anesthetized with isoflurane and catheters were surgically implanted under aseptic precautions (use of sterile instruments and surgical material in combination with local antibiotic prophylaxis) into the femoral artery and vein. Then the catheters were exteriorized in the neck region, connected to a Harvard swivel system (Harvard Instruments), and filled with 0.9% saline containing 100 U·mL–1 heparin. After recovery from anesthesia, the animals were housed individually in special cages with free access to food and tap water until and throughout the experiment. Analgesic treatment with Temgesic (10 μg/kg sc, application volume 1 mL/kg) was performed the evening following surgery and the next morning. Compound administration in cassette format was in the morning (6–8 AM). Blood samples were collected at various time points from the femoral artery catheter into Eppendorf tubes coated with sodium EDTA. Blood samples were immediately frozen at −20 °C until final processing (maximum storage was 8 days). Intravenous and oral dosing was typically performed in the same animals after a 48 h wash-out interval between the single dose applications. The test substances were administered intravenously as a solution in 1-methyl-2-pyrrolidone and polyethylene glycol 200 (30:70, v/v) at a dose of 1 mg/kg per compound and orally as a homogeneous aqueous suspension in Tween 80 and carboxymethyl cellulose sodium 0.5/0.5/99 (w/w) at a dose of 3 mg/kg per compound.

    In Vivo Rapid PK Studies in Rats and Mice

    Total of six animals were used for each compound, three for intravenous and three for oral administration. For mouse rapid PK studies, six blood samples (50 μL each) were taken via tail vein bleed and the blood samples from the three animals were pooled and centrifuged to obtain pooled plasma samples. For rat rapid PK studies, six blood samples (100 μL each) were taken via saphenous vein puncture. After centrifugation, 20 μL of the plasma samples are pooled across the three rats based on time point within a dosing arm. The pooled plasma samples were diluted appropriately using a generic dilution scheme to ensure that concentrations are within the dynamic range of the standard curve (1–5000 ng/mL). Automated sample preparation and protein precipitation were carried out. Liquid chromatography/mass spectrometry (LC/MS/MS) analysis was used with a fast generic gradient elution method together with atmospheric pressure chemical ionization (APCI) or electrospray (ESI) in the positive or negative ion mode on an API-4000 triple quadruple mass spectrometer.

    Mouse and Rat PK Analysis

    The concentrations of compounds in whole blood were quantified using a liquid chromatography/mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) assay. To 10 μL of each blood sample, 2 μL (conc 2.5 μg/mL) of an internal standard was added and the samples were precipitated with 120 μL of acetonitrile. The samples were vortexed thoroughly then centrifuged (10 min, 4 °C). The supernatant (120 μL) was transferred to a clean 96-well plate and mixed with 50 μL of Milli-Q water. The samples were injected (2 μL) onto suitable analytical columns, using gradient methods at various flow rates. Mobile phases typically consisting of 0.1% formic acid in water (solvent A) and 0.1% formic acid in acetonitrile (solvent B) were used. Compounds and the internal standards were eluted at various retention times. The HPLC systems were interfaced to mass spectrometers. MS/MS analyses were carried out using electrospray ionization (ESI), typically in the positive ionization mode. Compounds and the internal standards were monitored using multiple reaction monitoring (MRM). The standard curves employed for sample quantitation ranged over several log units. The lower limit of quantitation (LLOQ) in blood was determined and used as cutoff for the analytical sensitivity. Known amounts of the compounds were spiked into blood to create quality control samples with three to four known concentrations. The accuracy of the in vivo blood sample concentration determination was considered acceptable when the intra-assay accuracies obtained for the quality control samples were within 70% to 130% of the expected concentrations. Subsequently, from the time concentration data, pharmacokinetic parameters were calculated by noncompartmental regression analyses using an in house fitting program.
