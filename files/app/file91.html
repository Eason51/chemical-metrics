    Abstract

    ELAV-like (ELAVL) RNA-binding proteins play a pivotal role in post-transcriptional processes, and their dysregulation is involved in several pathologies. This work was focused on HuD (ELAVL4), which is specifically expressed in nervous tissues, and involved in differentiation and synaptic plasticity mechanisms. HuD represents a new, albeit unexplored, candidate target for the treatment of several relevant neurodegenerative diseases. The aim of this pioneering work was the identification of new molecules able to recognize and bind HuD, thus interfering with its activity. We combined virtual screening, molecular dynamics (MD), and STD-NMR techniques. Starting from around 51 000 compounds, four promising hits eventually provided experimental evidence of their ability to bind HuD. Among the selected best hits, folic acid was found to be the most interesting one, being able to well recognize the HuD binding site. Our results provide a basis for the identification of new HuD interfering compounds which may be useful against neurodegenerative syndromes.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    RNA-binding proteins (RBPs) play a prominent role in the fate of target messenger RNAs (mRNAs) coding for pivotal proteins in key cellular functions. Among RBPs, the family of mammalian ELAV/Hu RBPs is involved in controlling the functional activities of diverse RNA populations. The ubiquitously expressed HuR (ELAVL1) and the neuron-specific members HuB (ELAVL2), HuC (ELAVL3), and HuD (ELAVL4) are the four known members of the ELAV family.(1,2) The ELAV/Hu proteins bind to the (A/U)-rich elements (AREs), which are preferentially localized within the 3′-untranslated regions (UTRs) of selected mRNAs and finely regulate their metabolic pathways. These proteins can affect the post-transcriptional fate of the targeted mRNAs at multiple levels, such as polyadenylation, alternative splicing, and nuclear export to cytoplasmic localization, stability, and translation.(3−5) Among the neuronal RNA-binding proteins, HuD has been intensively studied. It is expressed in nervous tissues where it is mainly involved in neuronal differentiation, in synaptic plasticity mechanisms, both in the central and in the peripheral nervous system, in tissue reparation after nerve injury and in memory processes.(6−8) Moreover, HuD is specifically implicated in the motor neuron function, indeed an intimate relationship between HuD and motor neuron diseases has been demonstrated.(9,10) Altered expression and malfunctioning of the protein have been associated with the pathogenesis of nervous system disorders and childhood brain tumors (Figure 1). For example, overexpression of HuD has been detected in patients suffering from schizophrenia, while polymorphisms of the HuD gene have been related to the pathogenesis of Parkinson’s disease, affecting, in particular, the age at onset.(11−13) Conversely, the role played by HuD in the pathogenesis of Alzheimer’s disease (AD) seems to be ambiguous. On one hand, some studies suggested that HuD expression decreases as a function of clinical dementia progression, an event that favors the accumulation of amyloid-β (Aβ) peptides in AD brains.(14,15) In line with this concept, in human SH-SY5Y neuroblastoma cells, it has been demonstrated that HuD gene silencing decreases the expression of ADAM10 protein, the α-secretase that prevents Aβ formation.(16) On the other hand, other studies associated overexpression of HuD to AD incidence according to at least two different mechanisms: (1) HuD may contribute to the stabilization of amyloid precursor protein (APP) and β-site APP-cleaving enzyme 1, the secretase that favors Aβ formation (BACE1) and (2) HuD is involved in the upregulation of neuroserpin, an inhibitor of tissue plasminogen activator, hence its upregulation reduces the brain levels of plasmin, one of the main enzymes involved in Aβ clearance.(17,18)
    To sum up, targeting HuD could thus turn out to be effective in counteracting neurodegenerative diseases. Nevertheless, this protein still remains poorly investigated from a medicinal chemistry standpoint.
    From a structural perspective, three RNA recognition motifs (RRMs) are present in all ELAVL proteins. Specifically, RRM1 and RRM2 are essential for mRNA binding and constitute a sort of “clamp” that holds the mRNA strand, while the third RRM interacts with the poly(A) tail and is involved in the protein–protein interactions required for multimerization.(20,21) Sequence-alignment studies of different ELAV proteins showed that each of the first two RRM domains contains two highly conserved sequences of eight and six amino acids, named ribonucleoprotein 1 and 2 (RNP1 and RNP2) motifs, respectively. From a three-dimensional point of view, the binding occurs at the interface of the first two RRM domains, where four of the β-sheets (two for each RRM domain) form the upper and lower “surfaces” of the clamp. Specifically, the four β-sheets contain the highly conserved sequences RNP1 and RNP2.(20,22)
    In our previous experiences with ELAV proteins, we had evaluated and confirmed the importance of the ELAVL RNP sequences in binding and stabilizing specific target mRNAs using real-time quantitative PCR, saturation transfer difference (STD) NMR, and diffusion-ordered spectroscopy (DOSY).(23−25) Moreover, we had assessed the potential of computer-aided drug design (CADD) as a useful tool in future drug-discovery programs involving ELAVL proteins.(26)
    The aim of the current work is the identification of new ligands able to bind the HuD binding site and alter the formation of the HuD-mRNA complex, thus taking the first step toward the discovery of novel compounds with potential application in the treatment of neurological disorders.
    Initially, we carried out virtual screening (VS) studies, using a database of natural compounds and Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved drugs. Currently, VS techniques represent a powerful and useful tool for the drug-discovery process and even if the VS approach is not immune to traps and failings, a large number of successful applications have been reported.(27−30) In addition, the drug repurposing of already approved drugs is a good alternative for drug development as it offers various advantages. First of all, repositioned drugs have already obtained approval from the FDA, which means they have successfully passed safety trials and therefore the risk of failure is lower,(31) and they have thoroughly been investigated for absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicity (ADMET) mechanisms. Moreover, established formulations and manufacturing methods and phase IV (post-marketing surveillance) safety data are generally already available for these drugs. In summary, usually less money investments are needed and consequently, this strategy is economically attractive. Accordingly, in this study, we performed a VS of natural compounds and FDA-approved drugs. Starting from about 51 000 compounds, 10 promising hits were selected.
    Afterward, considering the commercial availability and the suitability for NMR analysis, 4 compounds were purchased. To analyze the ligand–HuD interaction and the capability of ligands to bind HuD, saturation transfer difference (STD) NMR investigations were performed.(32−35) For each of the 4 compounds the best docking pose was submitted for molecular dynamics simulations (MDs) to deeply investigate the behavior of the ligands in the HuD binding site. Interestingly, the experimental data showed that the 4 selected hits can bind HuD. Finally, we observed a clear correlation between the molecular modeling outcomes and the observed STD-NMR results.

    Target Optimization and Virtual Screening

    Molecular docking simulations were performed to elucidate the binding mode and the interactions between the compounds and the HuD binding site. Due to the similarity between the 3D structures of HuD and HuR, in agreement with our previous works, we used the HuD “closed” conformation structure for the molecular recognition studies (Figure 2).(36−39)
    The docking results were filtered considering the Lipinski rule of 5(40) and all poses of compounds were ranked and analyzed based on the Glide score (G-score) value. All of the compounds with a G-score value within 3 kcal/mol from the best hit were considered, thus10 hits for the further thermodynamic studies were selected (Figure 3) (Table 1).

    NMR Analysis

    The solubility and stability over time of the compounds reported in Table 1 dissolved in D2O and phosphate buffer were monitored by performing 1H NMR experiments for 24 h. Four molecules folic acid, rosmarinic acid, cefazolin, and enalapril maleate proved to be sufficiently soluble and stable to perform the interaction studies with the protein in phosphate buffer. Mono-dimensional and bi-dimensional spectra of all compounds were analyzed and used for their characterization. COSY and HSQC were used for the chemical shift assignment, while NOESY spectra were useful for the identification of conformational preferences.(41)
    The interaction studies between the ligands and the isolated protein were performed using STD-NMR experiments and it was proved that all of the ligands were able to bind the protein. To describe the ligand–protein interaction, we reported the absolute STD values that are strongly indicative for mapping the ligand epitope. Although in the literature the STD technique is also used for the determination of the dissociation constant, the determination of KD was not possible for these molecules due to their relatively low solubility (<20 mM). We performed a detailed analysis of the binding epitope and the STD absolute percentages obtained for each ligand are reported in Table 2.

    Folic Acid

    The STD spectrum of folic acid highlighted a strong interaction for both the aromatic portions. Interestingly, the absolute STD value observed for the proton of the pteridine ring (9.4%, a surprisingly high value compared with the STD data obtained for the protons of the compounds derived from this screening or from other screened natural molecules(37,42)) suggests that this moiety is strongly involved in the binding event. An additional interaction was observed for the second aromatic moiety, as reported in Table 2, indicating that the whole molecule is well positioned within the binding site.

    Rosmarinic Acid

    Rosmarinic acid showed several interactions, as evidenced by the STD signals, corresponding to all of the protons of the molecule. The most intense interaction (0.5 STD %) is attributed to proton 8′ of the α,β-unsaturated moiety, while all of the other protons produce interaction signals with the range of intensity between 0.26 and 0.19 STD % (Table 2). The STD data suggest the involvement of both aromatic moieties in the binding event.

    Cefazolin

    Regarding cefazolin, three interactions are detected in the STD spectrum and they are related to the methyl group of the thiadiazolic ring, to proton 5 belonging to the β-lactam ring, and to proton 8 of the tetrazole (Table 2). In particular, proton 8 shows the most intense STD signal (9.5%), thus suggesting that the aromatic tetrazole ring is tightly bound to the protein. On the other hand, proton 5 and proton 1 also show relevant STD signals (1.7 and 0.8%, respectively), hinting that the thiadiazolic and the β-lactam rings may also play a key role in the binding event.

    Enalapril Maleate

    From the proton spectrum analysis, carried out in D2O and in buffer, we noticed that enalapril is sensitive to the pH of the solution, due to the presence of an acid and an amino group in the structure. The interaction studies allowed the identification of the epitope that is essentially represented by the aromatic, the ethoxy and pyrrolidine moieties (Table 2).

    Ligand–Protein Interaction Analysis

    By analyzing the binding mode of the 4 best candidates, we observed that all of the compounds are involved in productive interactions within the HuD binding site.
    Folic acid is well accommodated in the HuD binding pocket. The dihydropteridine nucleus forms an H-bond with the side chain of Arg155 and two H-bonds with Asn126. The amino group is engaged in an H-bond interaction with the side chain of Thr153.
    Through its carboxyl groups, folic acid establishes different H-bond and salt-bridge interactions with the guanidine groups of Arg116, Arg123, and Arg172 (Figure 4A).
    Rosmarinic acid interacts through its carboxyl moiety with the guanidine groups of Arg116, Arg123, and Arg172 establishing many H-bonding and different salt-bridge interactions. The 3-(3,4-dihydroxyphenyl) ring forms a π–π stacking interaction with Phe84 and three H-bonds with Arg116. The ester oxygen forms an H-bond with Arg123. The 2-(3,4-dihydroxyphenyl) ring is engaged in an H-bond interaction with Thr153 (Figure 4B).
    Cefazolin interacts with Arg123 and Arg172 forming salt bridges and an H-bond interaction with its carboxyl group. The thiadiazole ring forms two H-bonding interactions with Arg116, while the amide oxygen atom forms an H-bonding and an aromatic H-bonding interaction with Tyr45. Moreover, Arg155 forms a hydrogen bond with one of the tetrazole nitrogen atoms (Figure 4C).
    Enalapril establishes H-bonding interactions with Arg123 and Arg172 through the carboxyl and carbonyl groups. The aromatic ring is involved in a π-cation interaction with Arg155 and in an aromatic hydrogen-bonding interaction with Leu80. Arg116 and Arg172 are both involved in forming salt-bridge interactions with the oxygen atom of the carboxyl group (Figure 4D).
    The virtual screening results showed that all of the compounds are involved in salt-bridge interactions with Arg172 (Figure 4) (for the 3D representation of other compounds see Figure S9).

    Molecular Dynamics

    To further investigate the ligand–protein interaction profile, we performed MDs. Starting from the best pose of folic acid, rosmarinic acid, cefazolin, and enalapril, 100 ns of MDs were carried out. During the whole trajectory the root mean square deviation (RMSD) for each complex was calculated and the analysis revealed a higher stability of the protein in the presence of folic acid (RMSD = 4.13 ± 0.42 Å), cefazolin (RMSD = 6.08 ± 0.77 Å), and enalapril (RMSD = 6.33 ± 1.00 Å) compared to rosmarinic acid (RMSD = 7.98 ± 3.94 Å) as shown in the protein RMSD plot (Figure S10).
    A similar trend was also observed in the behavior of the ligands. As the ligand RMSD plot shows (Figure S11), folic acid (RMSD = 3.75 ± 0.44 Å) and cefazolin (RMSD = 4.99 ± 0.49 Å) maintain a more stable behavior throughout the whole simulation compared to rosmarinc acid (RMSD = 8.88 ± 4.12 Å) and enalapril (RMSD = 9.72 ± 2.14 Å).
    For each ligand, the root mean square fluctuation (RMSF), which gives information about the ligand atom position changes during the whole simulation, was also calculated to characterize the fluctuations in the ligand position within the complex (Figure 5). Clearly, the general pattern of fluctuation is significantly lower for folic acid and cefazolin compared to rosmarinic acid and enalapril.
    In addition, folic acid shows several long-lasting interactions with residues of both the RRM domains (Tyr82, Tyr114, Arg116, Asn126, Thr153, Arg155, Phe170, Arg172), hence keeping the protein in a closed conformation. Although cefazolin and enalapril are involved in a minor number of interactions with the protein, they are still able to stabilize it because these interactions involve residues from both the RRM domains and are persistent throughout the simulation. On the other hand, rosmarinic acid is not able to maintain all of the interactions with the two RRM domains during the whole simulation (Figure S12).
    Furthermore, the presence of folic acid seems to best stabilize the protein as is shown by the RMSF plots of the protein backbone calculated during the simulations in the presence of folic acid and cefazolin (Figure S13).
    Noteworthily, these results are in agreement with the experimental data, as the minimum rate of fluctuations can indeed explain the strong signals observed during the STD experiments.
    Folic acid and cefazolin, which are characterized by strong STD signals, are stable and well accommodated in the HuD binding site and associated with a lower level of RMSF. STD-NMR analysis revealed that all of the selected hits can bind HuD and the epitope mapping evidenced that the selected molecules bind HuD with different features. In particular, folic acid and cefazolin exhibited the highest STD signals, thus suggesting that both molecules are well stabilized inside the HuD binding site, as confirmed by the MDs results. The MDs outcomes highlighted a lower level of fluctuations and more stable behavior of the two compounds compared to enalapril and rosmarinic acid.
    In particular, folic acid is the most attractive compound, in fact, it is well accommodated in the HuD binding site, establishing strong and lasting interactions within the HuD binding pocket.
    The obtained data hence provided a deeper insight into the binding mode of these compounds and highlighted a clear correlation between the molecular modeling outcomes and the observed STD-NMR results.

    Cell Data

    In preliminary experiments, the effect of folic acid on the expression of HuD in SH-SY5Y cells using alpha-tubulin as the loading control was assessed. SH-SY5Y cells were exposed to 100 nM folic acid for 4 h and HuD protein levels were measured in the total homogenates. As shown in Figure S14, an increased expression of the HuD protein was observed in cells treated with folic acid with respect to controls.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this work we applied a structure-based virtual screening (SBVS) approach to a database of both FDA-approved drugs and natural compounds, thus offering the possibility of drug repositioning and shortening the drug development process.(31) The initial in silico studies indicated some promising compounds which were in-depth investigated. STD-NMR analysis revealed that all of the selected hits can bind HuD. The STD-NMR results are in agreement with the MDs data. Among all compounds, the most interesting one is folic acid. In fact, by combining STD-NMR and MDs results we found that folic acid is able to bind HuD and well stabilize the protein, forming different stable interactions and mimicking the mRNA binding mode.
    HuD is implicated in the pathogenesis of AD by contributing to the accumulation of Aβ peptides in AD brains, to the stabilization of APP and BACE1, and to the upregulation of neuroserpin.(17,18)
    Hence, given the role of HuD in the genesis and accumulation of Aβ, our findings support the notion that folic acid supplementation may represent, together with other micronutrients, a valid nutritional intervention in AD.(43−46)
    Folic acid is a key cofactor in the one-carbon metabolism promoting the re-methylation of homocysteine to methionine. Methionine is further converted to S-adenosylmethionine, which is the primary methyl donor in most mammalian reactions. Concerning the underlying biological mechanism discussed here, it is tempting to speculate that folic acid may bind the HuD protein and regulate its activity/function via methylation, modulating, for example, its direct interaction with ARE-containing mRNAs. Accordingly, in PC12 cells, it has been demonstrated that the methylation of HuD by arginine methyltransferase CARM1 accelerates the decay of the target cyclin inhibitor mRNA p21cip1/waf1 (whose protein is involved in cell cycle arrest), thus maintaining the cells in a proliferative state.
    Conversely, the inhibition of HuD methylation increases p21cip1/waf1 mRNA half-life, resulting in a slow growth rate and robust neurogenesis in response to nerve growth factors. These findings thus suggest that unmethylated HuD may lower the decay of this specific target transcript by forming a tighter complex with it.(47,48) With reference to AD, it would be interesting, for instance, to explore whether folic acid administration could be associated with HuD methylation and, in turn, with an accelerated decay of its specific target transcripts APP and BACE1.
    Thus, the ability of folic acid to affect HuD protein expression was evaluated in a preliminary biological setting. Within this context, the human neuroblastoma cells represent a valuable tool for drawing the effect of compounds influencing HuD activity/function.(49) Cell-based assay results support the molecular modeling and STD-NMR data, highlighting the ability of folic acid to recognize and affect HuD expression. In fact, in human SH-SY5Y cells treated with folic acid, an increase in HuD protein levels was observed.
    In this regard, our hypothesis is that folic acid interaction with HuD triggers a series of intracellular signals that finally result in an increase of HuD protein expression.
    These findings could be an important starting point for the future treatment of some neurological diseases and in particular for AD, due to the crucial role of HuD protein in the pathogenesis of AD.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The RNA-binding protein HuD plays a crucial role in the post-transcriptional control of gene expression during neuronal development and its dysfunction/dysregulation may contribute to the pathogenesis of several disorders involving the nervous system, at both the central and peripheral level (Figure 1). In this work, we combined SBVS and STD-NMR studies to identify compounds that can bind HuD. Three of the four identified hits (folic acid, cefazolin, and enalapril) are drugs already on the market for the treatment of other pathological conditions. They could therefore be repurposed as novel candidates for the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases. Their development process would be considerably faster since they have already been tested in terms of their safety in preclinical models and human trials. Of particular interest is the evidence that folic acid can act as a HuD-mRNA interfering compound, since it could clarify a still unknown mechanism of action of this widely used vitamin and could justify its use in the prevention of some neurodegenerative diseases.(48) Additional experiments are ongoing to deeply understand the biological outcomes resulting from HuD and folic acid interaction. In conclusion, our results pave the way toward the validation of HuD as a drug target and may open a novel route for the discovery of innovative agents to counteract neurodegenerative diseases.

    Molecular Modeling

    Starting from the crystal structure of HuD and the (A/U)-rich element of c-fos mRNA, deposited in the Protein Data Bank (PDB) with the PDB code 1FXL,(17) our molecular modeling studies were carried out. The receptor structure was properly processed using the Protein Preparation Wizard.(50) Hydrogens were added, disulfide bonds were created, and seleno-methionines were converted into methionines. The hydrogen-bonding network was optimized and the pKa of the residues along with their protonation states were calculated at pH 7.4. The complex was then submitted to 10000 iterations of energy minimization using the Polak-Ribiere conjugate gradient (PRCG) algorithm in the MacroModel tool and OPLS_2005 as the force field.(51,52) Molecular dynamics (MD) simulation was performed to explore all of the conformational states of HuD and to examine the potential HuD rearrangement.(36) The MD was run using the Desmond package v. 3.8.(53,54) The system was immersed in an orthorhombic box of TIP4P water molecules, extending at least 10 Å from the protein and counter ions were added to neutralize the system charge. The system temperature was set at 300 K and the NPT ensemble was selected. The simulation was carried out for 500 ns and trajectories and energies were recorded at 500 ps intervals. The resulting trajectory was clustered with respect to the root mean square deviation (RMSD) to explore the collection of all of the obtained structures, giving ten representative structures, which were submitted to 10000 iterations of energy minimization using the Polak-Ribiere conjugated gradient (PRCG) algorithm, using the MacroModel tool and OPLS-2005 as the force field.(52,54,55)
    The two conformations with the highest and the lowest potential energy values, corresponding to an “open” HuD conformation and a closed HuD conformation, were selected. According to our previous works,(36−38) we noticed that more interactions were established between the ligands and the receptor in the presence of the closed conformation, thus, for further docking studies, we chose the HuD closed conformation. Molecular docking recognition studies were carried out using Glide v. 6.7 software, using the standard precision (SP) algorithm and the binding pocket was identified by placing a cube centered on the c-fos mRNA. Ten poses per ligand were generated.(56) For the virtual screening studies, we used a library of 50914 compounds obtained from different databases available online: FooDB, the world’s largest and most comprehensive resource on food constituents, chemistry, and biology;(57) DrugBank, a comprehensive, freely accessible, online database containing information on drugs and drug targets;(58) ZINC15 (a free database of commercially available compounds) biogenic subset, which contains derivatives of natural products, endogenous human metabolites, metabolites, and natural products.(59) The library was prepared using the LigPrep tool.(60) Hydrogens were added, salts were removed, and ionization states were calculated using Epik at pH 7.4 ± 0.0. Each structure was submitted to 10 000 iterations of energy minimization using the Polak-Ribiere conjugated gradient (PRCG) algorithm, using the MacroModel tool and OPLS_2005 force field.(52,55) After the virtual screening, the results were filtered considering the Lipinski rule of 5(40) and the compounds obtained were ranked by the G-score value. Considering the theoretical binding affinity score value of the best hit we retained, for further studies, all of the structures with a Glide score value within 3 kcal/mol from the best hit. Eventually, 10 compounds were selected. Each selected hit was searched within several vendors databases and finally, based on their commercial availability, 4 best hits were selected and submitted for STD-NMR experimental evaluation. The best docking pose of the 4 selected compounds was subjected to 100 ns MDs. The MDs were carried out according to the protocol described above. The simulation interaction diagram tool implemented in Desmond v. 3.8(53,54) was used to analyze the behavior of the ligands and the protein. The analysis included the RMSD and RMSF calculations evaluated during the whole simulation.

    Evaluation of Pan Assay Interference Compounds (PAINS)

    The PAINS properties of the chemical structures of the investigated compounds were theoretically investigated using the zinc PAINS Pattern Identifier web server.(61,62) The method highlighted that among the 10 compounds rosmarinic acid and methoxatin contain PAINS: the catechol moiety in rosmarinic acid and the imine and quinone moieties in methoxantine, respectively. The method did not highlight any PAINS related to the other investigated molecules. Interestingly, folic acid, the most promising hit, does not contain any PAINS.

    Stability Evaluation and Characterization

    The stability test and the characterization of all of the compounds were performed at 298 K on a Bruker Avance 400 MHz spectrometer in a 5 mm NMR tube. The experiments were carried out in D2O and aqueous phosphate buffer at pH 7.4 (20 mM). To obtain clear spectra, the water signal suppression was achieved using an excitation sculpting sequence from the Bruker library. All proton and carbon chemical shifts were assigned unambiguously, and the assignment is reported in the SM (Tables S1–S4).

    HuD Expression and Purification

    HuD gene was cloned in a pET-42a vector, provided by Prof A. Quattrone, (University of Trento) and expressed in Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) cultured in Luria Broth (LB) medium. After induction with 1 mM IPTG, cells were grown at 310 K for 6 h and collected. Pellets were dissolved in equilibration buffer (20 mM Tris-Cl pH 8, 100 mM NaCl, 3 mM MgCl2, 0.5% Triton X-100) and disrupted by sonication. After centrifugation, the supernatant was filtered and used for purification on a Ni-NTA column using a 20–500 mM imidazole gradient. The protein was stored in phosphate buffer (20 mM) and imidazole (300 mM). The final protein concentration was 3 μM. For the NMR studies, the protein was transferred in a Sartorius Vivaspin 500 concentrator (molecular weight cut-off of 5 K) and subjected to four cycles with a Hermle Z 233 M-2 laboratory centrifuge, at 10 000 rpm for 10 min, to remove the imidazole that could interfere with the interaction study. The final protein concentration was about 12 μM in 20 mM phosphate buffer.

    STD-NMR Interaction Studies

    The interaction between the ligands and the isolated protein was detected using STD-NMR experiments. The spectra were acquired with a Bruker Avance 600 MHz instrument at 283 K, in a 3 mm NMR tube and in the phosphate buffer previously described (200 μl). All protein–ligand samples were prepared in a 1000:1 ligand/protein ratio. The interaction between ligands and isolated proteins was detected using STD experiments. In the STD experiments, water suppression was achieved using the WATERGATE 3-9-19 pulse sequence. The on-resonance irradiation of the protein was applied at −0.05 ppm. Off-resonance irradiation was applied at 200 ppm, where no protein signals were visible. Selective presaturation of the protein was achieved by a train of Gauss-shaped pulses of 49 ms length each. The total length of the saturation train depends on the L7 parameter (the loop counter). STD experiments were performed with L7 = 60 leading to a total saturation time of 2.94 s. The intensities of all STD effects (absolute STD) were calculated by division through integrals over the respective signals in the STD-NMR reference spectra. The different signal intensities of the individual protons are best analyzed from the integral values in the reference and STD spectra, respectively. (I0 – Isat)/I0 is the fractional STD effect, expressing the signal intensity in the STD spectrum as a fraction of the intensity of an unsaturated reference spectrum. In this equation, I0 is the intensity of one signal in the off-resonance or reference NMR spectrum, Isat is the intensity of a signal in the on-resonance NMR spectrum, and I0– Isat represents the intensity of the STD-NMR spectrum.

    Cell Culture

    Human neuroblastoma SH-SY5Y cells were obtained from ATCC (Manassas, VA) and cultured in a humidified incubator at 37 7̅C with 5% CO2. The SH-SY5Y cells were grown in Eagle’s minimum essential medium (EMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, 1% penicillin–streptomycin, l-glutamine (2 mM), nonessential amino acids (1 mM), and sodium pyruvate (1 mM). The cells were treated with 100 nM folic acid for 4 h and then homogenized in a specific cell lysis buffer (Cell Signaling Technology).

    Western Blotting

    Proteins were measured according to Bradford’s method using bovine albumin as the internal standard. Proteins were diluted in 2xSDS protein gel loading solution, boiled for 5 min, and separated on 12% SDS-PAGE gel. The anti-HuD mouse monoclonal antibody (Sigma-Aldrich) and the rat monoclonal antibody anti-α-tubulin (Thermo Fisher Scientific) were diluted (1:500 and 1:1000, respectively) in TBST buffer [10 mM Tris-HCl, 100 mM NaCl, 0.1% (v/v) Tween 20, pH 7.5] containing 6% milk. The nitrocellulose membrane signals were detected by chemiluminescence. The same membranes were re-probed with the α-tubulin antibody and used to normalize the data. Statistical analysis of Western blot data was performed on the densitometric values obtained using the ImageJ image-processing program (https://imagej.nih.gov/ij).

    Statistical Analysis

    For statistical analysis, the GraphPad Instat statistical package (version 3.05 GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA) was used. The data were analyzed by an unpaired t-test. Differences were considered statistically significant when p values <0.05.

The new version contains over 120 million purchasable "drug-like" compds. - effectively all org. mols. that are for sale - a quarter of which are available for immediate delivery. ZINC connects purchasable compds. to high-value ones such as metabolites, drugs, natural products, and annotated compds. from the literature. Compds. may be accessed by the genes for which they are annotated as well as the major and minor target classes to which those genes belong. It offers new anal. tools that are easy for nonspecialists yet with few limitations for experts. ZINC retains its original 3D roots - all mols. are available in biol. relevant, ready-to-dock formats. ZINC is freely available at http://zinc15.docking.org.
