    Abstract

    We recently isolated an aptamer that binds to the Tat protein of HIV-1 with extremely high affinity and specificity (Yamamoto, R.; et al. Genes Cells 2000, 5, 371.). In the present study, we exploited this strong binding to develop a novel coupling method that links genotype with phenotype. To strengthen the original RNA−protein interaction still further, we connected three units of the aptamer in tandem and three units of a peptide derived from Tat that interacted with the aptamer. The binding of the resultant RNA, which consisted of three units of the aptamer, to the resultant peptide, which consisted of three units of the peptide, was extremely strong. In fact, the RNA−protein interaction was one of the strongest ever reported, with an apparent Kd below 16 pM. This strong interaction was attempted for the selection of functional proteins, namely, dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) or streptavidin, which we chose as an example, and we succeeded in the expected selection, although to a limited extent, of the target protein. The noncovalent but strong interaction described above should be useful as a novel tool for the future selection of functional proteins from pools of random sequences of amino acids.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Methods for the selection and identification of functional proteins from pools of random sequences of amino acids are of great current interest. Many successful screening systems for the selection of specific functional proteins, such as phage-display and two-hybrid systems, are based on living cells.1-6 In such selection systems, the sequence information (genotype) that encodes the selected protein (phenotype) can be determined from the DNA introduced into each cell that displays the appropriate phenotype. The coupling of genotype and phenotype is the single critical determinant in the selection of functional proteins from random pools. However, as long as such methods are dependent on living cells, the diversity of sequence libraries is limited because, for example, of the inefficiency of transfection processes, limits to the size of displayed proteins, and limits to the nature of the protein in question, since any protein that is detrimental to cells cannot be selected.
    To overcome these problems, several systems for selection for proteins in cell-free systems, including a ribosome-display system, mRNA-display (in vitro virus) system, and a micelle system, have recently been developed.7-28 Presently available cell-free systems, however, still include steps that reduce diversity, although some successful examples were recently reported.26 In the ribosome-display system, the ribosome forms a stable complex with the translated protein and the mRNA that encodes the protein because the release of the protein from the complex is slowed by the removal of a termination codon and by the addition of Mg(OAc)2 and anti-ssrA oligonucleotides. Under such conditions, the release factor cannot effectively induce the release of the protein from the ribosome complex. However, the yield of the isolated mRNAs after one round of ribosome display was not very high.10,28 Thus, further stabilization of the mRNA−ribosome−protein complex will be required for wider applications. In mRNA-display (in vitro virus) systems, a translated protein binds covalently to the respective mRNA, which has been labeled with puromycin at the 3‘ end. In this system, chemical synthesis, for attachment of puromycin to mRNA, is always required, and this process might be expected to reduce the size of the available library. In the micelle system, each individual, chemically modified DNA molecule is packaged in one colloidal micelle and, upon transcription and translation, the modified DNA is linked to the encoded protein within the micelle compartment. However, the dilution of the reaction mixture that is designed to ensure that one DNA molecule is packaged per individual micelle might also be expected to reduce the diversity of the sequence library. Therefore, although cell-free systems might, in theory, have the advantage of allowing examination of a larger number of sequences compared to systems that use living cells, a significant portion of a pool's diversity might not be fully exploited in the presently available cell-free systems.
    In this study, we attempted to develop a simpler system for selection of proteins in vitro that would not include any steps that might potentially reduce the diversity of the pool of sequences. Our approach was based on the extremely strong interaction between an RNA and a protein. We recently selected an aptamer (designated Tat aptamer) that binds to the Tat (trans activation) protein of HIV-1 two orders of magnitude more strongly than the RNA that naturally interacts with Tat, which is known as TAR (trans-activating response region).29 The interaction between the Tat aptamer and a peptide derived from Tat (RE peptide, 38 amino acids; or CQ peptide, 36 amino acids) was extremely strong with a dissociation constant (Kd) in the subnanomolar range. We postulated that such a strong RNA−protein interaction might be useful for linking genotype and phenotype, namely, an mRNA and its encoded protein. Because the stabilization and maintenance of the RNA−protein complex are key determinants in the successful selection of a target protein, we tried to reduce the Kd of the complex still further by increasing the number of aptamer and peptide motifs.30,31 In our system, the RNA aptamer, linked to a specific mRNA, should bind automatically to the encoded protein, upon translation, that contains the RE or CQ peptide. Thus, with such a high-affinity aptamer, formation and long-term maintenance of the complex should be straightforward. As a result, mRNAs after one round of the selection will be collected with high yields. And most importantly, upon complete optimization, this kind of system, exploiting the extremely strong RNA−protein interaction, should make the best use of the diversity of a pool in a cell-free system.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Enhanced Interaction between Tandemly Connected Aptamers and Tat-Derived Peptides. We chose, arbitrarily, dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) as the model functional protein, since its properties have been well characterized.32-37 DHFR, which catalyzes reduction of dihydrofolate to tetrahydrofolate, plays an important role in supplying cofactors of one-carbon-transfer reactions catalyzed by thymidylate synthase. To examine the effects on binding affinity of several aptamers and Tat-derived peptides (RE), we prepared three kinds of protein [DHFR−(RE)n] (Figure 1) and three types of tandem RNA aptamer [(Apt)n] (Figure 2A), in which “n” ranged from 1 to 3 and corresponded to the number of copies of each motif. The bands of purified proteins after SDS−PAGE are shown in Figure 1, and we confirmed that each protein had the expected DHFR activity (data not shown), proving that attachment of RE peptide motifs had not interfered with the correct folding of the DHFR domain. To maintain the appropriate secondary structure of each aptamer, we linked the Tat aptamers to one another via six adenosine nucleotides in tandem to yield (Apt)2 and (Apt)3. The purified RNAs after PAGE on a nondenaturing gel are shown in the control lanes (C) in Figure 2A. The gel in Figure 2A also demonstrates that, as we had expected, the Tat aptamers did not interact with DHFR. The gel shows that the mobility of the aptamers did not change after mixing with increasing concentrations of wild-type DHFR (the concentration of DHFR was increased from lane C toward the right in each case, as indicated by the triangles at the top of the gel).
    As we anticipated, the mobility on gels of aptamers (Apt)1-3 changed after they had been mixed with the fusion proteins DHFR−(RE)1-3 (Figure 2B−D). In these experiments, we mixed 32P-labeled aptamers (final concentration, 30 pM) with an excess of each fusion protein, and the amount of complex formed was quantified with an imaging analyzer. Apparent values of Kd were calculated to be 24 nM, 200 pM, and less than 16 pM for n = 1, n = 2, and n = 3, respectively. Clearly, among the combinations that we examined, the interaction between (Apt)3 and DHFR−(RE)3 was the strongest [Kd(app) ≤ 16 pM]. To the best of our knowledge, this is one of the strongest interactions between an RNA and a protein reported to date. Although this complex appeared to adopt multiple conformations, stronger interactions were clearly achieved with an increase in the number of both the aptamer and the RE peptide. We used the (Apt)3 and DHFR−(RE)3 motifs in subsequent experiments because the maintenance of the stability of the RNA−protein complex, in other words, an extremely strong interaction between the RNA and the protein, is the key factor in the coupling of genotype and phenotype.
    Linkage of Genotype and Phenotype by the Interaction between (Apt)3 and (RE)3. To confirm the potential utility of (Apt)3 and DHFR−(RE)3, we prepared two types of plasmid. The plasmids encoded a wild-type and a mutant DHFR−(RE)3 fusion protein, and the encoded mRNAs and translated proteins are shown schematically in Figure 3A [intact, pDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3; mutant, pMuDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3]. Each plasmid included the sequence encoding DHFR with three RE peptides [(RE)3] in tandem, as well as the sequence of three Tat aptamers (Apt)3 in tandem. The mutant protein [MuDHFR−(RE)3] derived from pMuDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 differed from the intact protein [DHFR−(RE)3] derived from pDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 at only two positions, with the amino acids substitutions Asp 27 → Ala 27 and Phe 31 → Ala 31 at the active site of DHFR. These mutations abolished the ability to bind methotrexate (MTX).32-37 As a second control, we also prepared a plasmid that encoded the intact DHFR−(RE)3 fusion protein but no aptamer sequences, namely, pDHFR−(RE)3, so that the translated product, DHFR−(RE)3, would be unable to interact with its mRNA (Figure 3A, right).
    If the (Apt)3−(RE)3 interaction could really be used to couple genotype and phenotype, only the intact system (Figure 3A, left) should yield both the protein (DHFR) and its mRNA that would bind to MTX−agarose beads when the reaction mixture, after translation, is mixed with MTX−agarose beads. By contrast, the mutant system (Figure 3A, center) should yield neither the protein (DHFR) nor its mRNA for binding to MTX−agarose beads, since the mutant DHFR was designed to be unable to bind to MTX. Finally, the control system (Figure 3A, right) should yield only the protein (DHFR) and not the mRNA for binding to MTX−agarose beads, since DHFR−(RE)3 would be unable to interact with its mRNA because the letter lacks the (Apt)3 aptamers.
    To confirm our predictions, we prepared three kinds of 32P-labeled capped-mRNA, using T7 polymerase, from the respective plasmids and used them as templates to translate the respective proteins in a rabbit reticulocyte lysate. During translation, each protein was labeled with 35S-methionine. After translation, the appropriate binding buffer and MTX−agarose beads were added to each lysate, and binding reactions were allowed to proceed for 30 min at 4 °C (this low temperature reduced the rate of degradation of mRNA by RNases in the lysate). Supernatants of the binding solutions containing unbound mRNA and protein were removed, and the MTX−agarose beads were washed twice with the binding buffer. The products eluted from beads in an elution buffer at an elevated temperature (95 °C) were analyzed by SDS−PAGE. We confirmed, by checking each lysate after translation, that amounts of translated proteins and input mRNAs were identical in each case (Figure 3B, lanes 1−3).
    Analysis of the eluted products demonstrated that, as we expected, DHFR was detectable in the intact and control systems (Figure 3B, lanes 4 and 6) while themutant protein was barely detectable (lane 5). The yields of the intact DHFR and the control DHFR, respectively, were 9- and 16-fold higher than that of the mutant DHFR (Figure 3C, left). Moreover, the mRNA was detected only in the intact system (Figure 3B, lane 4), even though nonspecific binding of mRNA to the MTX beads could not be completely avoided (lanes 5 and 6) in this experiment, since the MTX beads were somewhat positively charged after attachment of MTX. The yield of the intact mRNA was only 2.5-fold higher than that of the background, i.e., nonspecifically bound mutant and control mRNAs (Figure 3C, right). We are at present testing other attachment procedures to prevent formation of beads with positive charges. Nevertheless, quantification of bands demonstrated clearly that the amounts of the proteins and mRNA were well above the background level, and these results confirmed the validity of the interactions that are shown schematically in Figure 3A.
    Attempts To Select mRNA That Is Attached to Its Phenotype (Streptavidin) in Vitro. If (Apt)3 and DHFR−(RE)3 are to function properly in the selection of the target protein, the respective protein should end up bound specifically to its mRNA when two distinguishable mRNAs are translated together. Since the presently available MTX−agarose beads were positively charged and nonspecific binding of irrelevant mRNAs could not be avoided completely (parts B and C of Figure 3), we tested alternative biotin−agarose beads. For this purpose, as a positive control, we made a new plasmid, pStreptavidin−(CQ)3−(Apt)3, in that the gene for DHFR within the pDHFR−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 plasmid had been replaced by the gene for streptavidin (recall that both RE and CQ are Tat-derived peptides with identical functions).29 In this system (Figure 4), the translated streptavidin but not the DHFR should end up bound to the biotin−agarose beads when we start from a mixture of mRNAs derived from pStreptavidin−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 and pDHFR−(CQ)3−(Apt)3.38
    We prepared two kinds of 32P-labeled capped-mRNA from pStreptavidin−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 and pDHFR−(CQ)3−(Apt)3, using T7 polymerase. The mRNAs encoding streptavidin−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 and DHFR−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 were translated in lysate as a mixture in the ratio of 1:1 (green, Figure 4B) and also were translated individually (red and blue, Figure 4B) in order to confirm that the mRNAs were bound to the biotin−agarose through a complex between the Tat aptamer and CQ peptide. Translation using 32P-labeled mRNAs was examined to determine the amount of bound mRNA at each step during the selection procedure. After translation for 15 min, the appropriate binding buffer and biotin−agarose were added to lysates and binding reactions were allowed to proceed for 10 min at 4 °C. Biotin−agarose had been blocked, before combination with a translation mixture, by exposure to sterilized milk (Block Ace; Dainippon Pharmaceutical Co., Japan), and each binding and washing step was performed in the presence of the milk, since milk decreases the nonspecific binding of ribosome complexes to agarose.14 After centrifugation, supernatants that contained unbound mRNA and protein were discarded and respective pelleted ligands were washed twice with binding buffer. At each step during the selection procedure, the relative amount of mRNA that remained in the mixture was determined by monitoring radioactivity (Figure 4B).
    Results shown in Figure 4B are the averages from four independent sets of experiments. DHFR−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 mRNA (dark-blue line in Figure 4B) was soon washed away, while streptavidin−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 mRNA (red line in Figure 4B) was firmly retained with more than 1.5% of the input mRNA remaining after a second wash (Figure 4B). And streptavidin−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 mRNA was enriched 17-fold higher than DHFR−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 mRNA (Figure 4B). It is important to note that the amount of recovered mRNA, which remained at 1.5% after repeated washes, was significantly higher than the corresponding values reported previously. In ribosome display, the amount of recovered mRNA was as little as 0.015% (or 0.2% under certain conditions) of the input RNA, although some mRNA was recovered at a higher yield (8%) because the selection did not require complete folding of a short peptide such as His-tag28.
    We then eluted bound products from ligands (biotin−agarose) with elution buffer containing 1 M NaCl. The eluted mRNAs were purified with an RNeasy minikit (QIAGEN, Germany) and amplified by reverse transcriptase polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) in order to confirm the enrichment of the mRNA of interest (Figure 4C). We confirmed previously by PCR that each sample of mRNA did not contain any contaminating DNA (data not shown). Furthermore, we confirmed that the amount of each input mRNA was constant (Figure 4C, lanes 1−3). Our analyses by RT-PCR clearly demonstrated that mRNA-encoding streptavidin was successfully recovered by biotin−agarose (Figure 4C, lane 4), whereas the nonspecific mRNA-encoding DHFR was not detected (Figure 4C, lane 5). Clearly, the nonspecific binding of mRNAs to the biotin−agarose is very low and mRNA-encoding streptavidin binds to biotin−agarose correctly and efficiently.
    When we mixed the mRNAs that encoded streptavidin and DHFR together at a ratio of 1:1 (mole/mole), mRNA-encoding streptavidin was correctly concentrated from the mixture via binding to biotin−agarose (Figure 4C, lane 6), although a small amount of unwanted DHFR mRNA was detected (Figure 4, lane 6). These data demonstrate that the interactions are primarily cis and not trans, although a small amount of RNA−protein networks could not be avoided (see below). In support of this interpretation, when the mixed mRNAs (1:1 mole/mole ratio) were subjected to biotin−agarose, only the expected yield of mRNA was obtained (green line in Figure 4B). In this case, the amounts of the input mRNAs were kept constant, and thus, when the mixture was subjected to biotin−agarose (green line), the absolute amount of streptavidin mRNA or DHFR mRNA was half of that of the individual experiment (red line or blue line). If trans interactions had been the dominant interactions, close to 1.5% recovery (similar to that of the red line) would have been achieved from the 1:1 mixture of mRNAs (specifically 0.7% streptavidin mRNA plus 0.7% in trans-acted nonspecific DHFR mRNA, leading to total ∼1.5% recovered mRNAs) rather than the recovery of half the amount (0.7%, green line). It should be noted that unwanted DHFR mRNAs containing the tandem aptamer sequence can bind only trans to the biotin−agarose bound streptavidin that contains the CQ peptide region.
    Importantly, the present results with a partial success suggest that the strong RNA−protein interaction might be useful for the selection of functional proteins if the mRNA−protein interaction in a trans configuration could be further minimized by the adjustment of the length of linkers and proper combination of the respective aptamers and peptides (see below).
    Combination of the Strong Interaction between (RE)3 and (Apt)3 with the Ribosome-Display System in the Selection of Functional Proteins. Since we could partially select the functional streptavidin by the use of the strong RNA−protein interaction (Figure 4C, lane 6), we wondered whether the situation might be improved by the combination with the ribosome-display system. In the ribosome-display system, the ribosome forms a relatively stable complex with the translated protein and its respective mRNA because the release of the protein from the complex is slowed by the removal of a termination codon and by the addition of Mg(OAc)2 and anti-ssrA oligonucleotides.10,11 To test the effects of removal of the termination codon in our system, we newly prepared a DHFR mRNA that lacked the termination codon (DHFR − Stop mRNA, right panel in Figure 5A). As a control, we used a DHFR mRNA with the termination codon (DHFR + Stop mRNA, central panel).
    We compared the amounts of mRNA bound to MTX beads, upon translation in lysate and after the selection procedure, for three types of mRNA, namely, DHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 + Stop [this is same as the DHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 used above], DHFR + Stop, and DHFR − Stop. During the translation, the DHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 + Stop mRNA with a termination codon binds to the translated DHFR protein by the interaction between (RE)3 and (Apt)3 and the ribosome does not take part in this protein−mRNA complex because the ribosome is released from mRNA by the presence of the termination codon. These three kinds of mRNAs were translated in a rabbit reticulocyte lysate and were subjected to MTX−agarose beads under the same conditions described in Figure 3. The relative amounts of selected mRNAs were detected by the count of 32P-labeled RT-PCR products and the originally bound mRNAs (parts B and C of Figure 5). Results shown in Figure 5C are the averages from four independent sets of experiments (in one case, analysis of the PCR products was carried out and the results are shown in Figure 5B). On average, the selected DHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 + Stop mRNA (lane 1, Figure 5C) was 2.5-fold higher in concentration than the background level of DHFR + Stop mRNA (lane 2, Figure 5C), whereas the corresponding value for the DHFR − Stop mRNA was 1.4-fold in the absence of Mg(OAc)2 and anti-ssrA oligonucleotides (lane 3, Figure 5C). Therefore, in the present system (not optimized for the ribosome display), the (RE)3−(Apt)3 interaction was slightly more effective than the removal of the termination codon in the stabilization of the complex between the respective mRNA and its product.
    The ribosome-display system worked extremely well when functional antibodies were selected against their respective antigens and the ribosome−mRNA−protein ternary complex was stable for 10 days in an appropriate binding buffer.20,24 Our results (Figure 5C) indicate, however, that the ribosome−mRNA−protein complex that had formed by the removal of the termination codon in lysate might not be stable enough, at least under the conditions we used for the general translation purpose (which might not be optimized for the ribosome display).
    Therefore, it should be advantageous to combine the (RE)3−(Apt)3 interaction and the ribosome-display system for the improved stabilization of the complex between the respective mRNA and its product. For this purpose, we prepared another plasmid in that the (Apt)3−(RE)3 region was placed upstream of the gene for DHFR (Figure 6). Furthermore, to examine the effects of releasing factors in the stabilization of the complex by the removal of the termination codon, we prepared polyclonal IgG antibodies against releasing factors (anti-RFs) E. coli RF1, RF2, and RF3.39 Since we placed the (Apt)3−(RE)3 region at the 5‘ end in order to avoid appearance of termination codons within this region at the 3‘ end, we carried out, in the present studies shown in Figure 6, the translation of the respective mRNAs in E. coli S30 extracts by placing the SD sequence between (Apt)3 and (RE)3.
    We compared the amounts of mRNA bound to MTX beads for two types of mRNA, namely, DHFR − Stop mRNA (Figure 6, parts 1 and 2) and (Apt)3−(RE)3−DHFR − Stop mRNA (Figure 6, parts 3 and 4), in the presence (parts 1 and 3) or in the absence (parts 2 and 4) of the polyclonal IgG antibodies against the releasing factors (anti-RFs). Translated mRNAs were subjected to MTX−agarose-bead selection under the same conditions described in Figure 3 (not optimized for the ribosome display). The relative amounts of selected mRNAs were detected by RT-PCR (parts B and C of Figure 6). Results shown in Figure 6C are the averages from two independent sets of experiments. These results clearly demonstrate that the combination of the (RE)3−(Apt)3 interaction and the ribosome-display system, especially (unexpectedly, since the termination codon had been removed from the respective mRNA) in the presence of the polyclonal IgG antibodies against the releasing factors (although the exact reasons need to be elucidated), is advantageous in stabilizing the mRNA−ribosome complex.
    Possible Existence of RNA−Protein Networks. Experimental results described above clearly demonstrated that the strong aptamer−peptide interaction could further stabilize the ribosome-display complex, and thus, this kind of interaction should be useful for the selection of functional proteins. However, the efficiency of selection of correct proteins at this moment is not as high as we anticipated from the expectations of in vitro binding assays (Figure 2). Assuming a diffusion-limited association constant of 107 M-1 s-1, the dissociation rate constant of the best aptamer−peptide complex will be 1.6 × 104 s-1. This corresponds to a half-life of 52 min, which should be sufficient for the selection of the majority of functional proteins. Although we carried out the selection of the functional DHFR or streptavidin within 1 h, we had limited success.
    The discrepancy between the in vitro binding constants and the efficiency of the selection could possibly be reconciled by the formation of RNA−protein networks, as schematically shown in the top diagram of Figure 7A. It is possible that the “conformations” exhibiting slower gel mobility, at least in part (Figure 2), are actually RNA−protein networks. Such networks would complicate the selection of proteins from libraries. Therefore, we are at present trying to prepare other combinations of aptamers and peptides, as schematically shown in the bottom diagram of Figure 7A (in addition to the improvement of the beads). Then, we should be able to optimize the system by the selection of the best linker sequence for the optimal interaction between the aptamers and respective peptides.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In our present system, we arbitrarily chose linkers for the Tat aptamers and Tat derived peptides (RE and CQ) without any attempt at optimization. Nevertheless, the possibility that a strong interaction between RNA and protein can be used to couple genotype and phenotype was clearly demonstrated. Exploiting such unnatural combinations, namely, interactions that do not exist in nature, we should be able to select or engineer even stronger RNA−protein interactions. Then complexes of protein and mRNA with even longer half-lives can be generated and selection should become very easy. Moreover, our approach can be combined with the ribosome-display system by simply eliminating the termination codon from the fusion gene. The result should be a significantly more stable protein−mRNA complex as a consequence of attachment at two sites, as shown in part 3 of Figure 6. Once our approach has been optimized, there should be no steps at which a decrease might occur in the diversity of a sequence library. Most importantly, our proposed selection procedure is very simple and straightforward. Therefore, the present noncovalent but strong interaction should be considered as a novel tool for selection of functional proteins (Figure 7B), in addition to already available systems.1-28,40

    Preparation of Three Kinds of Protein [DHFR−(RE)n]. For preparation of DHFR−(RE)1-3 fusion protein, the gene for DHFR with a Pst I site at its 3‘ end, derived from plasmid pTZDHFR20 (ref 36), was inserted into plasmid pET30a (Novagen, Madison, WI). Sense and antisense oligonucleotides encoding the RE peptide (38 amino acids; RKKRR QRRRP PQGSQ TBQVS LSKQP TSQSR GDPTG PKE) were synthesized, annealed, and polymerized by rTaq polymerase (Takara Shuzo Co., Kyoto, Japan). The resultant dsDNAs were cut at the Pst I and EcoT22I sites and inserted on the 3‘ side of the gene for DHFR at the Pst I site. Because the Pst I and Eco T22I restriction sites can be connected and the resulting site can no longer be cleaved by either Pst I or Eco T22I, we were able to connect the sequence that encoded the RE peptide to the Pst I site on the 3‘ side of the gene for DHFR to obtain continuously pDHFR−(RE)1 through pDHFR−(RE)3. These plasmids were used to transfect E. coli NovaBlue (DE3) (Novagen), and cells were incubated in 500 mL of LB medium at 30 °C. Then 1 mM of IPTG (isopropyl-1-thio-β-d-galactoside) was added at the logarithmic phase of growth, and incubation was continued at 30 °C for 4 h. The proteins were expressed as polyhistidine-tagged polypeptides. Proteins were purified according to the protocol supplied with the HisTrap chelating kit (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech, Uppsala, Sweden), and small molecules were excluded using a HiTrap desalting system (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech). Furthermore, to remove degraded proteins, we performed cation-exchange HPLC using HiTrap SP (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech). Purity of proteins was checked by SDS−PAGE (Figure 1). Concentrations of purified proteins were measured with a BCA assay kit (Pierce, Rockford, IL).
    Preparation of Three Types of Tandem RNA Aptamer [(Apt)n]. For preparation of tandem Tat aptamers [(Apt)1-3], sense and antisense oligonucleotides (5‘-AAA AAA CGA AGC TTG ATC CCG TTT GCC GGT CGA TCG CTT CG-3‘ and 5‘-TTT TTT CGA AGC GAT CGA CCG GCA AAC GGG ATC AAG CTT C-3‘) were synthesized. These oligonucleotides were annealed and ligated to yield tandemly connected Tat aptamers [(Apt)1-3] with a DNA ligation kit version 2 (Takara Shuzo Co.). Ligated DNA sequences coding one to three Tat aptamers were isolated after cloning (TA cloning kit, Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). The DNAs coding from one to three aptamers were transcribed with a T7 Ampliscribe kit (Epicentre Technologies, Madison, WI).
    Measurements of Dissociation Constants (Kd). For gel mobility shift assays, each transcribed aptamer [(Apt)1-3] was isolated by PAGE on a 15% polyacrylamide denaturing gel and dephosphorylated at its 5‘ end by alkaline phosphatase (E. coli A19; Takara Shuzo Co.) at 37 °C for 1 h. Then aptamers were labeled with 32P by T4 polynucleotide kinase at 37 °C for 1 h and purified by PAGE (15% polyacrylamide). A fixed concentration of 5‘ end-labeled aptamer plus 40 nM tRNA was denatured in Tat binding buffer [10 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0), 70 mM NaCl, 2 mM EDTA and 0.1% Nonidet P-40] at 95 °C for 3 min, kept at room temperature for renaturation, and then mixed with a range of excess concentrations of DHFR−(RE)1-3 in Tat binding buffer in a total volume of 10 μL at 30 °C for more than 1 h. Free and protein-bound or peptide-bound RNAs were separated by electrophoresis on a 5−15% nondenaturing polyacrylamide gel in 0.5 × TBE (Tris-borate/EDTA) buffer and were analyzed.
    Construction of pDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 (Intact), pMuDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 (Mutant), and pDHFR−(RE)3 (Control). For site-directed mutagenesis of the core region of DHFR, we prepared four oligonucleotides (5‘-ACA ATT CCC CTC TAG AAA TA-3‘, 5‘-GTG GTG GTG CTC GAG AAT TC-3‘, 5‘-CCT GCC GCC CTC GCC TGG GCC AAA CGC AAC ACC TTA AAT AAA-3‘, and 5‘-GCG TTT GGC CCA GGC GAG GGC GGC AGG CAG GTT CCA CGG-3‘). pMuDHFR−(RE)3 was constructed by the introduction of two mutations into the gene for DHFR using these oligonucleotides and the megaprimer PCR mutagenesis strategy. Next, three aptamer sequences in tandem (Apt)3 were introduced into the Eco RI site downstream of the RE peptides in pDHFR−(RE)3 and pMuDHFR−(RE)3 to yield pDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 (intact) and pMuDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 (mutant).
    Linkage of Genotype and Phenotype by the Interaction between (Apt)3 and (RE)3. Linearized plasmids [pDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 (intact), pMuDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 (mutant), and pDHFR−(RE)3 (control)] were transcribed to yield capped mRNAs that were labeled with 32P (Cap structure analogue; New England Bio Labs, Beverly, MA) in vitro at 37 °C for 2 h using a T7 Ampliscribe kit (Epicenter Technologies). Labeled mRNAs were purified on NICK columns (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech). One microgram of capped-mRNA-encoding DHFR−(RE)3 (intact) or MuDHFR−(RE)3 (mutant) was translated in 20 μL of rabbit reticulocyte lysate for 20 min at 30 °C (rabbit reticulocyte lysate system; Promega, Madison, WI). As a control, mRNA without aptamers was also translated (control). Each protein was labeled with 35S-methionine. After translation, 180 μL of binding buffer [10 mM sodium phosphate buffer (pH 7.5), 70 mM NaCl, 10 μM tRNA] and 20 μL of a suspension of MTX−agarose beads (Sigma, St. Louis, MO), which had been treated with succinic anhydride for neutralization of positive charges on MTX−agarose beads, were added to the lysate and binding was allowed to proceed for 30 min at 4 °C. A total of 200 μL of supernatant containing unbound mRNA and protein was removed from the mixture, and MTX beads were washed twice with 180 μL of binding buffer. Finally, bound mRNA and protein were isolated from the MTX beads by elution at an elevated temperature (95 °C) with SDS-containing solution. Each sample was then subjected to SDS−PAGE on a 15% gel.
    Selection of mRNA Encoding Streptavidin in Vitro from a Pool of Mixtures of Streptavidin and DHFR mRNAs. The construction of pDHFR−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 was nearly identical with that of pDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3. In short, sense and antisense oligonucleotides encoding the CQ peptide (36 amino acids; CFTTK ALGIS YGRKK RRQRR RPPQG SQTBQ VSLSK Q) were synthesized and the resultant fragment containing the CQ peptide was introduced between the sequences of DHFR and aptamer. A template DNA encoding streptavidin was a kind gift from Professor Masahiko Sisido and Mr. Takahiro Hohsaka of Okayama University. The DNA fragment containing the streptavidin gene was inserted upstream of the CQ peptide sequence in place of the DHFR gene. Respective 32P-labeled RNAs [streptavidin−(CQ)3−(Apt)3 and DHFR−(CQ)3−(Apt)3] were prepared as described above. Three sets of mRNA (0.5 μg in each set) (a mixture of mRNAs in the ratio of 1:1 or each mRNA-encoding streptavidin or DHFR as a control) were translated without adding DTT and Mg(OAc)2 using the Flexi rabbit reticulocyte lysate system (Promega) (50 μL) for 15 min at 30 °C. After translation, 1 mL of binding buffer [140 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl, 10.1 mM Na2HPO4, 1.8 mM KH2PO4, 0.04% Tween20, 2% Block Ace (Dainippon Pharmaceutical Co., Japan), 5 mM MgCl2 (pH 7.3)] and 10 μL of a suspension of biotin−agarose beads (Sigma) were added to the lysate and binding was allowed to proceed for 10 min on ice. Supernatant containing unbound mRNAs and protein were removed from the mixture, and MTX beads were washed twice with 200 μL of washing buffer [140 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl, 10.1 mM Na2HPO4, 1.8 mM KH2PO4, 0.04% Tween20, 2% Block Ace (Dainippon Pharmaceutical Co.), 50 mM EDTA (pH 7.3)]. Bound mRNAs were shaken vigorously in elution buffer [1 M NaCl, 50 mM EDTA] at room temperature for 30 min in order to isolate the binding mRNA from biotin beads. Each sample was purified with an RNeasy kit (QIAGEN, Hilden, Germany) and was used for reverse transcription and PCR.
    Reverse Transcription and PCR. Reverse transcription was performed with AMV reverse transcriptase (Promega) according to the supplier's recommendation. PCR was performed using 15 cycles with Ex Taq polymerase (Takara Co.). Products of PCR were analyzed by electrophoresis on agarose gels.
    Combination of the Strong Interaction between (RE)3 and (Apt)3 with the Ribosome-Display System in the Selection of Functional Proteins. The pET30a that contains the DHFR gene (pDHFR) and pDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 plasmid, described above, was used as a template for the preparation of respective 32P-labeled mRNAs [DHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3 + Stop, DHFR + Stop, and DHFR − Stop mRNAs]. 32P-labeled mRNAs were translated and were subjected to selection by MTX beads as described above. Bound mRNAs were isolated from the MTX beads by elution at an elevated temperature. The comparative amounts of selected mRNAs were detected by a count of 32P-labeled mRNA and RT-PCR.
    The p(Apt)3−(RE)3−DHFR was constructed in the same way as pDHFR−(RE)3−(Apt)3. The linear DNAs for in vitro transcription were prepared as PCR products from respective template plasmids [p(Apt)3−(RE)3−DHFR and pDHFR] that possessed T7 promoter sequence. The mRNAs were prepared as described above. Polyclonal IgG antibodies against E. coli RF1 to RF3 were prepared separately for each releasing factor (E. coli RF1 to RF3), which was a kind gift from Professor Takuya Ueda in the University of Tokyo. The total amounts of mRNA in start pool were 2 μg, and in vitro translation was carried out at 37 °C for 10 min using the E. coli extract system for linear templates (Promega). The translated products were put on ice for 1 min in order to stop the translation reaction and performed the in vitro selection using MTX beads, as described above. The recovered mRNA was purified by RNeasy mini kits (QIAGEN), and it was reverse-transcribed by M-MLV reverse transcriptase (Promega). This reverse-transcribed sample, i.e., cDNA, was amplified by PCR (ExTaq; Takara) and analyzed by 2.0% agarose electrophoresis. To perform the semiquantitative analysis, the agarose gel was stained by SYBR Green I (Molecular Probes, Eugene, OR) and analyzed by Fluoro Imager 595 (Molecular Dynamics, Sunnyvale, CA). The strength of the interaction between (RE)3 and (Apt)3 was estimated on the basis of the relative amounts of recovered mRNAs as RT-PCR products.
