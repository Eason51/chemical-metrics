    Abstract

    A series of 2,3,4,4a,10,10a-hexahydropyrano[3,2-b]chromene analogs was developed that demonstrated high selectivity (>2000-fold) for BACE1 vs Cathepsin D (CatD). Three different Asp-binding moieties were examined: spirocyclic acyl guanidines, aminooxazolines, and aminothiazolines in order to modulate potency, selectivity, efflux, and permeability. Guided by structure based design, changes to P2′ and P3 moieties were explored. A conformationally restricted P2′ methyl group provided inhibitors with excellent cell potency (37–137 nM) and selectivity (435 to >2000-fold) for BACE1 vs CatD. These efforts lead to compound 59, which demonstrated a 69% reduction in rat CSF Aβ1–40 at 60 mg/kg (PO).

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    As of 2013, there were an estimated 44 million people suffering from dementia, worldwide.(1) This number is expected to triple by the year 2050. Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the most common form of dementia, and the global economic burden is $604 billion annually.(2) There is no cure for the disease, as current treatments merely address the symptoms.(3) The disease’s origin is still under investigation. One of the more widely accepted causes is the accumulation of the amyloid β (Aβ) protein in the brain, which has been observed in both the early onset(4) and more common late-onset(5) variations of AD.
    The Aβ protein arises from amyloid precursor protein (APP) after sequential cleavage by β- and γ-secretases.(6) Though both secretases have been actively pursued as drug targets by the pharmaceutical industry, the β-secretase (BACE1) has gained traction due in part to recent genetic evidence(7) as well as human clinical trial results showing a correlation of BACE1 inhibition with Aβ reduction.(8) In addition to processing APP, BACE1 is also responsible for cleaving other peptides that may reduce its usefulness as a drug target.(9) However, these potential side effects have not prevented a BACE1 inhibitor (MK-8931) from progressing to Phase 3 clinical trials.(10)
    Developing a BACE1 drug for AD has been highly challenging because the properties for many of the reported inhibitors(11) have not been consistent with those needed(12) to cross the blood–brain barrier (BBB). In addition, very high selectivity versus the aspartic acid protease, Cathepsin D (CatD), which possesses a similar active site to BACE1, may be required to avoid retinal toxicity.(8a, 13) Based on our previous work(14) and others(15) on BACE1 inhibitors containing the Asp-binding moieties depicted in Figure 1, we pursued aminooxazolines (2) and aminothiazolines (3), as they were less effluxed at the BBB and generally more selective against CatD.
    It was observed that compounds containing a β-methyl group (e.g., 4) were highly selective versus CatD, presumably because the methyl group clashed with an immobile tyrosine in the CatD active site.(14a) As part of our SAR exploration around this core, we examined the effect of moving the THP oxygen from the 7- to the 8-position, giving compounds such as 54 (Figure 2). Serendipitously, these 8-tetrahydropyran-2-yl chromans (hereafter referred to as “8-THP chromans”) were even more selective than their 7-THP counterparts. The SAR and PK for these 8-THP chromans, which lead to the advancement of 59 (Table 4) to PD studies, and its effect on lowering CSF Aβ will be the focus of this paper.

    Structure–Activity Relationships (SAR)

    As noted above, we previously(14a) found that 7-THP chromans containing a β-methyl group were highly selective against CatD (4, Figure 2), and this prompted us to explore the SAR for the corresponding 6- and 8-THP chromans. Data for the 6-THP chromans is published elsewhere.(17) 8-THP chroman analogs were evaluated for activity in BACE1 and CatD enzyme assays,(18) a cell assay for production of Aβ1–40 in cells carrying the Swedish double mutation,(19) and for efflux ratio and permeability in an MDR1-transfected LLC-PK1 cell line. Our goal was to identify compounds with <100 nM cell potency, >1000-fold selectivity for CatD (due to its role in multiple physiological functions(13)), an efflux ratio <2 (to minimize P-gp liability) in an MDR1-transfected LLC-PK1 cell line, and apparent cell permeability in the apical to basolateral direction (Papp AB) > 10 × 10–6 cm/s. We identified 8-THP chroman 59, which met all of these criteria, except it demonstrated an efflux ratio of 3.4. Despite this efflux, compound 59 was able to attain a sufficient coverage ratio (Cfree,brain/Cell IC50)(20) in rat to decrease Aβ by 69% in the CSF (Table 4, vide infra).
    In our earlier work,(14a) we found that the 2-fluoropyridin-3-yl group, which binds in the S3 pocket, was optimal for giving the best coverage ratios in mice and marked reductions of CSF Aβ in three different species. Thus, we held this P3 group constant while varying the Asp-binding moiety, testing the effect of the β-methyl group, and determining which of the two trans diastereomers(21) would provide better potency and selectivity (Table 1). As we previously observed, the acyl guanidine was more potent than the corresponding aminooxazoline and aminothiazoline warheads in both enzyme and cell assays.(22) In contrast, the efflux ratios and permeability values (Papp AB) were much better for the less polar warheads (e.g., cLogPs of 3.0 and 1.9 for 53 and 50 vs 1.4 for 47).
    The β-methyl group had a dramatic impact on potency for all three Asp-binding moieties (Table 1). For example, compounds 47, 50, and 53 were 3–30-fold more potent in the enzyme assay than the corresponding des-methyl analogs 45, 49, and 52, respectively. We believe the β-methyl group forms a favorable hydrophobic interaction with Tyr132 in the BACE1 active site, resulting in increased potency (see Figure 3, compound 50). This observation may explain why type A diastereomers of Table 1 were 2–15-fold more potent than type B diastereomers for BACE1. Additionally, type A diastereomers were 3–4-fold less potent for CatD when the β-methyl group was present. This may be rationalized by the methyl group of type A (e.g., 50) clashing more severely with the flexible loop of CatD, which usually sits lower than the corresponding residues of BACE1 (see Figure 4).(23a, 23b) Furthermore, 51 (type B) appears to force Ile179 into a conformation not seen in Apo-BACE1 or many other BACE1 crystal structures (Figure 3), which agrees with its diminished potency for BACE1. Though both 50 and 51 appear to form water-mediated bridges with the carbonyl oxygen of Gly 95, the monohydrate bridge of compound 51 argues for it being more potent against BACE1, which is not the case. However, these waters are in weak density and are sometimes removed during refinement, so little weight should be given to this observation. Taken together, this SAR led us to further explore changes to the P3 moiety for aminoxazolines and aminothiazolines of diastereomer type A containing a β-methyl group (Table 2).

    There was only a 1–3-fold difference between enzyme potencies for the two warheads in Table 2, and an even smaller difference in cell potency, all within the fluctuation of the assay. Likewise, there was no clear trend as to which warhead gave better selectivity. In general, the less polar aminothiazoles resulted in lower efflux ratios (1.2–3.3). P3 moieties of Table 2 were selected for potency based on our previous work,(14) and all compounds of Table 2 were <150 nM in the BACE1 enzyme and cell assay. Excellent selectivity against CatD (>430-fold) was obtained. Consistent with our earlier publications, the most polar P3 group, the pyrimidin-5-yl moiety, provided the greatest selectivity (i.e., >2200- and 1100-fold selective for 61 and 62, respectively).

    Pharmacokinetic Studies in Mice

    From the 8-THP chromans described above, we selected compounds 45, 50, and 58 to evaluate in mouse PK (Table 3).(25) In spite of the efflux liability for the acyl guanidine 45, we wanted to compare its coverage ratio (Cfree,brain/Cell IC50) with 63, which had previously demonstrated excellent reduction of CSF Aβ in three different species.(14a) Aminooxazolines 50 and 58 were chosen to assess whether their lower efflux ratios in LLC-PK1 cells transfected with human MDR1 (6.2 and 3.3, respectively) would provide greater brain levels.
    Compounds were dosed as racemates, iv and po (3, 10 mg/kg, respectively) in male CD-1 mice, with plasma samples withdrawn and brains harvested (oral groups only) at 1 h and 4 h time points (Table 3). With the exception of compound 58, MLMs underpredicted hepatic clearance.(26) Both compounds 45 and 50 were cleared faster than hepatic blood flow, and as a result had significantly lower plasma and brain concentrations than 63. Neither compound was progressed to PD studies. Cyanophenyl chroman 58 demonstrated higher unbound systemic clearance than 63; however, the two compounds had similar BBB penetration. The coverage ratio for 58 was ∼10-fold lower than 63 due to its being 10-fold less potent in the cell assay.

    Pharmacodynamic Studies

    Though racemate 58 had been used in PK experiments (above), compound 59 (eutomer of 58) was selected for PD studies in rat (Table 4). Compound 64(14a) (eutomer of 63) is included in Table 4 for the sake of comparison. Free brain and CSF concentrations were remarkably similar for the two compounds at both time points. The free b/p ratio for 59 was 10-fold greater than for 64, likely due in part to a lower efflux ratio for 59. The coverage ratio for compound 59 was considerably lower due to a 7-fold lower cell IC50; however, the % reduction in CSF Aβ1–40 for 59 was higher at both time points. We do not currently understand this disconnect between coverage ratio and PD effect.

    Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Building on the SAR for 7-THP chromans that we developed previously(14a) and using structure-based design, substitution on a 2,3,4,4a,10,10a-hexahydropyrano[3,2-b]chromene (8-THP chroman) core was performed to improve BACE1 potency, selectivity against CatD, and efflux ratio. Three different Asp-binding moieties (i.e., acyl guanidine, aminooxazoline, aminothiazoline) were examined. Despite being the most potent, the acyl guanidine moiety correlated with high efflux. The cell potency (37–140 nM) and CatD selectivity (430–>2200-fold) of the aminooxazoline and aminothiazoline analogs were dramatically enhanced by methyl substitution at the β-position. CatD selectivity was improved relative to the previously published 7-THP chromans,(14a) by increasing BACE1 enzyme potency while maintaining CatD potency. Optimization of the P3 moiety lead to aminooxazoline 59, which was progressed to rat PD studies. Though 59 had a 7-fold lower coverage ratio in rat than our program’s previous lead molecule 64, it demonstrated a significant improvement in CSF Aβ1–40 reduction (69%). Given the high safety hurdle associated with a chronically dosed Alzheimer’s drug, the discovery that these β-methyl-8-THP chromans were both highly selective and efficacious in vivo opens up a new and potentially advantageous chemical space for future efforts at targeting BACE1.

    X-ray Crystal Structure

    Human BACE1 (residues 57–453) was expressed in E. coli with a noncleavable 6-His tag at the C-terminus. The protein was refolded and purified in a manner similar to that from Tomasselli et al.(27) Briefly, cells were lysed using mechanical disruption and inclusion bodies recovered by differential centrifugation. Inclusion body proteins were solubilized in urea and refolded by dilution into water. Following a 3 week refold period, BACE protein was recovered by Q-Sepharose anion-exchange chromatography. Fractions containing active protein were pooled and further purified over Source-Q anion exchange and Sephadex 200 size-exclusion columns. Purified BACE protein was concentrated to 10 mg/mL and frozen for crystallographic studies. Crystals were grown by hanging drop vapor diffusion with a precipitant of 20% PEG200, 0.1 M sodium acetate, pH 4.5–5.6, and 0–2 mM cobalt chloride. Crystals grew in space group C2221 in approximately 1 week. Crystals were then transferred to a soaking solution (27–30% PEG200, 0.1 M NaOAc, pH 5.0–5.6, 0–2 mM CoCl2, and 10% DMSO) with compound at a final concentration of 1 mM. Crystals were soaked for 18–24 h and either flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen or mounted directly for data collection. The soaking solution also served as a cryprotectant for data collection at 100 K.
    Diffraction data were collected on a Rigaku FR-E generator and Raxis IV++ detector (Rigaku Inc., 9009 New Trails Drive, The Woodlands, TX 77381–5209, U.S.). Data were processed in Moslfm,(28) and the initial structure was solved by molecular replacement from 1FNK.pdb using Molrep. All crystal structures were refined in Refmac,all three of these programs being part of the CCP4 suite (Winn et al.,2011).(29) Model building was performed in Coot.(30)

    BACE1 Enzyme Assay

    The activity of purified recombinant human BACE1, expressed in CHO cells, was determined using a custom synthesized biotinylated peptide based upon sAPP. Following an incubation for 2 h at 22 °C, the samples were excited at 320 nm and the emission ratio of 665 nm/615 nm was determined.

    Cathepsin D Enzyme Assay

    The catalytic activity of Cathepsin D, purified from human spleen (Calbiochem), was assessed in a FRET peptide substrate assay using 50 mM sodium acetate (pH 4.4) and 0.1% CHAPS.     Plasma Protein and Brain Tissue Binding

    Aliquots of undiluted plasma or diluted brain samples (three volumes of 0.1 M sodium phosphate buffer, pH 7.4) from each animal species were spiked with test compound in DMSO to give final concentrations of 5 μM (0.1% final DMSO) test article. Rapid equilibrium dialysis (RED) inserts were prepared in triplicate by adding 500 μL of PBS for plasma protein binding or 0.1 M sodium phosphate for brain tissue binding to one chamber and 300 μL of spiked plasma or brain homogenate to the opposing chamber. The RED units (inserts and bases) were sealed and incubated at 37 °C on a plate shaker at 300 rpm for 6 h to equilibrate the samples. One hundred microliters of each spiked matrix sample and respective buffer were transferred to a 96-deep well plate for sample extraction. The matrix samples were diluted with 100 μL of either PBS or 0.1 M sodium phosphate buffer and the buffer samples were diluted with 100 μL of either brain tissue homogenate or plasma to yield identical matrices between buffer and nonbuffer samples. Plasma or brain proteins were precipitated with 800 μL of CH3CN spiked with 0.2 μM final concentration (0.02% DMSO) of labetalol, the internal standard. The plates were spun in a centrifuge for 10 min at 3000g. One hundred microliters of the supernatant were transferred into a shallow 96-well plate and diluted 1:1 (v/v) with HPLC grade water and sealed for LC-MS/MS analysis (method described below).

    Liver Microsomal Incubations

    A 100 mM potassium phosphate assay buffer solution (KPB) was prepared as follows. Both KH2PO4 and K2HPO4 were dissolved separately in reagent grade water resulting in final concentrations 100 mM. A 75:25 mixture v/v of K2HPO4:KH2PO4 was prepared and the pH of the solution was adjusted to 7.4 using diluted HCl or diluted NaOH solutions. A stock solution of the test article(s) was prepared at 10 mM (active compound) in DMSO. The stock solution was diluted immediately before use to 2.5 μM using the KPB solution to create the working standard. All test compounds were completely soluble by visual inspection at rt. The NADPH-regenerating solution (NRS) was prepared on the day of analysis by diluting one volume of 17 mg/mL NADP+ with one volume of 78 mg/mL glucose-6-phosphate (both prepared in KPB, pH 7.4) and 7.9 volumes of 20 mM MgCl2. The final concentrations of NADP+ and glucose-6-phosphate were 1.7 and 7.8 mg/mL, respectively. Immediately prior to use, the NRS was activated by the addition of 10 μL of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (150 Units/mL in KPB, pH 7.4) per mL of NRS stock solution. Liver microsomes were diluted to 2.5 mg protein/mL using KPB. For each test article or positive control (i.e., dextromethorphan, diazepam, diltiazem, phenacetin, tolbutamide, and verapamil), 20 μL of 2.5 μM working standard solution of test compound and 20 μL of microsomes (2.5 mg protein/mL) were added to each well of a 96-well polypropylene plate (Costar, VWR, West Chester, PA) in duplicate. The plates were placed in an incubator at 37 °C for 5 min before adding the start solution. A 10-μL aliquot of the NRS solution was added to each original well to initiate metabolism. The concentration of the test compound during incubation was 1 μM. One incubation plate was prepared for each time point (i.e., 0 and 20 min). Incubations were conducted at 37 °C and 100% relative humidity. At each time point, the appropriate incubation plate was removed from the incubator and a solution containing internal standard (150 μL, 0.25 μM labetalol in 60% CH3CN) was added to each well. The plate was immediately spun in a centrifuge at 2,095g for 7 min at rt using an Allegra benchtop centrifuge (Beckman Coulter, Fullerton, CA). A 200-μL aliquot of the supernatant was transferred from each well to a 96-well shallow plate (Costar). The plates were sealed using reusable plate mats. Analysis was performed as described below.

    Hepatocyte Incubations

    A stock solution of the test article(s) was prepared at 10 mM (active compound) in DMSO. The in vitro stability of each test article or positive control was assessed in the presence of hepatocytes as follows. Cryopreserved hepatocytes were thawed, isolated from shipping media and diluted to a density of 1 × 106 viable cells/mL, according to the supplier’s guidelines, using Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium, 1X, high glucose (DMEM, Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). One incubation plate was prepared for each time point (i.e., 0, 60, and 120 min) with samples being prepared in duplicate. Incubations were conducted at 37 °C and 100% relative humidity. At each time point, the appropriate incubation plate was removed from the incubator and a solution containing internal standard (200 μL, 0.25 μM labetalol in 60% CH3CN) was added to each well. The plate was mixed at 700 rpm for 1 min on a plate shaker (IKA MTS 2/4 Digital Microtiter Shaker, VWR) and immediately spun in a centrifuge at 2,095g for 10 min at rt using an Allegra benchtop centrifuge (Beckman Coulter, Fullerton, CA).
