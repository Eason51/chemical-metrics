    Abstract

    The training of new medicinal chemists is vital to the future of the field, and as graduate students at this critical stage, we are uniquely positioned to comment on our training. Herein, we discuss the perspectives from graduate researchers before, during, and after graduate school by utilizing survey data obtained from five medicinal chemistry programs in the Midwest and recent alumni of the University of Minnesota. We also reflect on the female perspective within the field of medicinal chemistry. Finally, we offer recommendations to both students and faculty in the hopes of helping future generations succeed in the field.

    SPECIAL ISSUE

    This article is part of the Women in Medicinal Chemistry special issue.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The journey toward becoming a medicinal chemist can be compared to climbing a mountain range. It begins at a trailhead with a map of the route ahead (Figure 1). As you embark on your expedition, the path is clear of obstacles. There may be a few hazards along the way, but the trail can be followed with relative ease. Then the path opens to a craggy expanse. There is a vague hint of a trail in front of you, and it may be difficult to see at times. You trek onward. The path gets steeper; your feet get heavier. Eventually, a fork appears in the road. How do you choose without fully knowing what lies ahead? You make a decision and take a few steps forward. You pause, but with some hesitation you keep going. As your altitude increases, it gets harder to breathe, and you wonder if you have taken the right path. Looking back, you can no longer see the trail where everything started; looking ahead, the peak is obscured by fog. You push yourself forward, but your doubts begin to resurface. Suddenly, the peak is visible, and your hike is almost over. Using the last of your energy, you climb to the top. From the summit, you can finally see the journey for what it truly is, and you know that it was worth the effort.
    As graduate students in the midst of this climb, we are uniquely positioned to comment on the training of medicinal chemists during its most critical stage. Within this Miniperspective, we discuss the views of graduate and postdoctoral researchers prior to enrolling in a medicinal chemistry graduate program and during their time in such a program, all while looking toward their future careers. We also offer insight on the female perspective within the field of medicinal chemistry. In order to express the diverse views of researchers in medicinal chemistry graduate programs (and to avoid author biases), current graduate and postdoctoral researchers in medicinal chemistry programs throughout the Midwest (the University of MinnesotaâTwin Cities (UMN), the University of WisconsinâMadison, the University of MichiganâAnn Arbor, the University of Illinois at Chicago, and the University of Iowa) were surveyed. Graduate and postdoctoral researchers were administered the same set of 31 survey questions (15 multiple-choice and 16 short-answer; see Supporting Information for survey questions), and the results were analyzed in aggregate. The questions asked about the respondentsâ perspectives on their undergraduate and graduate education, career prospects, work-life balance, and their opinions of the field of medicinal chemistry overall. Postdoctoral researchers were included in the survey due to their invaluable opinions regarding career exploration. Demographic information such as age, gender, race, citizenship, and candidacy status was not collected in order to maintain the anonymity of the respondents; however, some of this information is summarized in Table 1 as it is publicly available. We should note that a majority of our survey respondents (64%) are researchers from UMN, which may bias our results toward the perspectives of researchers from UMN as opposed to being reflective of the perspectives of all researchers in these midwestern medicinal chemistry graduate programs. Furthermore, the data are representative of midwestern medicinal chemistry graduate programs and not all U.S. programs. The classifications of these research universities and the diversity of their respective student populations may therefore bias our results. Additionally, we surveyed 21 recent alumni who graduated in the past 10 years from the UMN Medicinal Chemistry Graduate Program to gain insight on their experiences as well as their outlook on the future of the field (see Supporting Information for survey questions). Overall, this Miniperspective is significant because it is written entirely by graduate students and offers a fresh perspective on the field. Additionally, as members of the Women in Medicinal Chemistry Organization at UMN, we consider the intersectional views and experiences of female researchers. We hope that by providing recommendations for how to improve the current research environment, we can help future generations succeed in the field.

    Views before Graduate School

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    How students prepare themselves is critical to their success in graduate school. However, given that students come from diverse backgrounds with differing accessibilities to research and professional development opportunities, not everyone enters graduate school with the same level of preparation. It is important that both undergraduate and graduate faculty recognize this and work on improving undergraduate training programs. It is also important that students be aware of the differences between undergraduate institutions as they prepare themselves for graduate school in order to improve their chances of being successful. Significant areas of preparation include research and coursework as well as mentorship. However, before considering and enrolling in a graduate program, students first need to be aware of existing programs.
    Awareness regarding the existence of medicinal chemistry programs is currently limited. Of survey respondents, 40% were not aware of the field until at least their senior year of their undergraduate studies, which is when many students start applying to graduate school (Figure S1). Part of this lack of awareness may be attributed to the low number of institutions that offer undergraduate courses in medicinal chemistry: our survey indicated that only 40% of respondents were given the opportunity to enroll in such a course (Figure S2). This may also explain why 35% of respondents did not understand the fundamental concepts of medicinal chemistry until graduate school (Figure S3).
    Lack of awareness is a serious issue that should be addressed as the field moves forward so that younger generations can develop interest and experience in medicinal chemistry at an earlier age. Recommendations from existing efforts to bring awareness to women and minorities in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) can be applied to our field. For example, the American Association of University Women (AAUW) has published on the âleaky pipelineâ phenomenon that results in the attrition of women in STEM.(1) AAUW recommends that universities reach out to high school students (we would recommend including undergraduates as well) to inform prospective students about their programs. Offering specialized courses or integrating medicinal chemistry concepts into organic chemistry courses should also be considered. Additionally, strict graduate admission policies, such as specific coursework and standardized test scores, may discourage potentially successful scientists who do not have access to the resources necessary to meet these requirements. For example, studies show that the Graduate Records Examination (GRE), a common admissions requirement, is not a good predictor of graduate student performance on qualifying examinations or their abilities to author scientific papers and receive grants and fellowships.(2) Changing these policies may encourage more prospective scientists to apply, thus broadening the educational diversity within the field. Some programs have already modified their requirements for this very reason, notably molecular biology departments: 44% of the top-ranked programs in the U.S. no longer require the GRE.(3) Other disciplines such as chemistry, however, have yet to follow this trend.(3) Furthermore, AAUW stresses the importance of mentors and recommends that principle investigators (PIs) mentor more undergraduates.(1) We believe that focusing on outreach to both high school and undergraduate students and broadening graduate admission policies are the first steps toward bringing more awareness and educational diversity to the field of medicinal chemistry.
    Regarding preparation for graduate school, undergraduate students come from diverse educational backgrounds. This includes the degrees that they earn as well as the institutions that they attend: primarily undergraduate institutions (PUIs), research universities (RUs), and international institutions. These institutions prepare students for graduate school through research, coursework, and mentorship; however, the degree of preparation in these areas may vary based upon the institution.
    Undergraduate research has the potential to be an enriching experience that will prepare students for the graduate lab environment. In order for these experiences to be beneficial, students need an environment that fosters skills in areas such as self-reliance, time management, professionalism, and teamwork.(4) There are various reasons why some laboratories may not be able to give an undergraduate a well-rounded research experience. Some of these reasons include a PIâs teaching or service commitments, the belief that undergraduates cannot commit enough time to make significant progress on a project, or university resources. The amount of funding can influence a labâs ability to conduct research, instrumentation quality, and publication output. Not only do PUIs typically have fewer resources, but RUs are also tiered based on the amount of research performed and the amount of funding awarded: R1, R2, and R3 as defined by the Carnegie Commission on Higher Education.(5) An R1 university is the top tier with âat least $5 million in total research expendituresâ as well as a âvery high research level.â(5) Upon the basis of this definition, 6 states in the U.S. have no R1 universities, 10 states have only one R1 university, and the rest have two or more R1 universities.(5) This means that location can limit an undergraduate studentâs ability to perform research. Students can be further limited by tuition costs as well as research interests, making it difficult for them to be invested in research.
    To ensure that undergraduate students have more equity in valuable research experiences, we recommend that faculty encourage students to get involved in research early in their undergraduate careers. Additionally, schools should consider adding research-based courses into their mandatory curriculum for STEM majors if they have the resources. If a student does not have access to opportunities that fit their needs at their home institution, we recommend exploring Research Experiences for Undergraduates (REUs) at other universities. Faculty encouragement to seek these out as soon as possible is important because there are limited spaces available. Additionally, universities that have REUs available should consider prioritizing students from PUIs or lower tier RUs over R1 institutions. We believe that these recommendations as well as those provided in the following paragraphs will help students in their preparations for graduate school, especially in medicinal chemistry programs.
    Coursework is another critical area of preparation as it is the foundation of knowledge that undergraduate students build upon when they start graduate school. Medicinal chemistry is at the intersection of chemistry and biology, meaning students come into such programs with a wide array of degrees. UMNâs program currently has students whose backgrounds include chemistry, chemical engineering, biochemistry, microbiology, biology, pharmaceutical sciences, and medicinal chemistry (Figure S4). It is important to note that there can be significant differences in curricula between different institutions even for the same undergraduate major.
    These diverse backgrounds are advantageous and should be encouraged as each student can share their foundational knowledge with others, which ultimately contributes to the interdisciplinarity of the field that many students cite as a reason for pursuing a degree in medicinal chemistry (Figure S5). Students who were successful in their undergraduate studies, regardless of their degree, should be able to fill in any gaps in their graduate education with remedial coursework, individual or group studying, and the understanding and support of graduate faculty and staff. To minimize these gaps before entering graduate school, we encourage undergraduate students to seek out cross-training by earning a minor, attending seminars, or acquiring an internship in a related field.
    Alongside research and coursework, having a mentor during oneâs undergraduate studies can be incredibly impactful. In fact, the Strada-Gallup alumni survey, which analyzes the long-term success of college students across the U.S., found that having a mentor was a critical marker of success in college graduates.(6) Mentors are an important part of an undergraduate studentâs support system: they can help encourage interest in coursework and research, refer students to professional development programs, provide networking opportunities, and give career advice. Mentors and mentees also form relationships that often last: three-quarters of alumni from the previously mentioned Strada-Gallup alumni survey reported that they had been in contact with a mentor within the last year.(7) Typically, a mentor is either a faculty research advisor or a course instructor, but mentors can be graduate or other undergraduate students as well. These student mentors provide a different perspective than a faculty mentor as they are able to draw from their recent experiences with coursework, research, networking, and applying for future education or career prospects. Additionally, both faculty and student mentors can offer resources and guidance toward applying to graduate school. As previously mentioned, awareness of the field of medicinal chemistry is low, but mentorship can change that: 40% of respondents from our survey indicated that they discovered the field through a mentor.
    Establishing a relationship with a mentor is vital to oneâs success, but it can be intimidating for students as they begin their undergraduate studies. We recommend that universities and faculty strive to reach out and meet students halfway in this regard. One way to engage with students is by hosting faculty meet-and-greets where students can acquaint themselves with professors outside the classroom. Similarly, students should be more involved in the process of choosing an academic advisor to make the connection more meaningful as they are often assigned automatically. This is different from research advisors, who are often sought out by the student, but both academic and research advisors can be helpful in guiding a student toward graduate school. Academic advisors can schedule biannual meetings to discuss studentsâ coursework requirements and desired career paths. Research advisors can help create a welcoming environment by implementing an open-door policy where topics such as science, inclusivity, and mental health can be discussed. No matter who the mentor is, it is arguably one of the most important aspects of undergraduate training.
    The preparation of undergraduate students is essential to the future of medicinal chemistry as a field. The results of our survey demonstrate that the field of medicinal chemistry is not well-known among undergraduate students, which directly affects the quantity and educational diversity of students who pursue careers in the field. To better engage and prepare undergraduate students, we emphasize stronger mentoring and more opportunities for undergraduate research. As graduate students who have recently transitioned from an undergraduate institution to a medicinal chemistry graduate program, we can attest to the importance of implementing these changes, and we believe that they will positively contribute to the future of the field of medicinal chemistry.

    Views of Graduate School

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The transition from undergraduate to graduate studies will take some adjustments regardless of a studentâs background. During their journey through graduate school, students will learn essential skills as they become independent scientists and face new pressures. Students entering the field of medicinal chemistry will be exposed to unique experiences and obstacles, due in part to the low number of medicinal chemistry programs compared to other STEM programs. In the U.S., there are 72 official graduate programs that offer a medicinal chemistry or pharmaceutical science degree (M.S./Ph.D.), 9 of which only offer Masterâs degrees, according to the American Association of Colleges of Pharmacy (AACP).(8) In the following section, we will discuss our current views of graduate school including perceptions of research training, mentoring, and career preparation. We also examine the controversial topic of burnout.
    Medicinal chemistry graduate programs are typically formatted similarly in terms of curricula and general program structure based upon our analysis of publicly available student handbooks and graduate program policies. Required coursework in a medicinal chemistry graduate program generally includes courses in medicinal chemistry, organic chemistry, biochemistry, bioassays, and ethics. There are a number of approved courses that can also be taken as electives depending on a studentâs specific interests. Students within the surveyed programs have the opportunity to complete rotations during their first year, which allows students to get to know different PIs and their corresponding laboratory environments before committing several years to a lab. During their second year, students generally begin the transition from the classroom to the lab, applying what they have learned throughout these foundational and specialized courses. Research is where medicinal chemistry graduate programs tend to shine. Upon the basis of the results of our survey, one of the main reasons why students enrolled in a medicinal chemistry graduate program instead of another STEM program is the fieldâs interdisciplinary nature (Figure S5), which is reflected in our laboratory training. Most students gain experience in a wide variety of techniques, ranging from organic synthesis and structural biology to biological assay development and preclinical studies. In fact, approximately 80% of survey respondents believe that their laboratory experiences are preparing them for their ideal careers (Figure S6). Moreover, 76% of the UMN alumni surveyed said that the skills that they learned and the training that they received in graduate school translated to their current position (Figure S7). We can conclude that the interdisciplinary training that graduate students in medicinal chemistry programs receive is highly valued and beneficial to their careers.
    Again, we bring up the topic of mentorship but this time in the context of graduate school. We will be focusing on the relationship between graduate students and their advisors, but it is important to note that other mentors exist in graduate school. Relationships with other graduate students, postdoctoral researchers, and various lab members can be indispensable during oneâs graduate studies. A positive relationship with oneâs advisor, however, is critical for success in graduate school, in regard to both degree progress and mental health. It has been shown that effective mentorship reduces graduate student stress and leads to the retention of students from underrepresented groups.(9) Therefore, building a strong relationship with oneâs advisor is key at early stages in oneâs graduate career.(9) Conversations about expectations and learning styles are important to have early in oneâs career. We emphasize the importance of graduate students playing an active role in their own mentorship. The ultimate goal of graduate school is to become an independent scientist, so allowing students to control the direction of their projects can increase productivity and creativity. We recommend the following: offer more training to both faculty and students on how to be effective mentors, set clear expectations early in the relationship, and continue to improve mentoring during graduate school.
    While in graduate school, students may also experience burnout, a physically, mentally, and emotionally stressed state that can consume oneâs professional and personal lives. The World Health Organization (WHO) recently added burnout to their 11th revision of the International Classification of Diseases, where burnout was defined as âa syndrome conceptualized as resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed and is characterized by feelings of energy depletion or exhaustion, increased mental distance from oneâs job, or feelings of negativism or cynicism related to oneâs job, and reduced professional efficacy.â(10) When surveyed, 88% of respondents reported having experienced burnout at least once during their time in graduate school (Figure S8): 44% responded with an absolute âyesâ, 44% with a âsomewhatâ, and 12% with an absolute ânoâ. These results are similar to the results of other studies regarding burnout, stress, anxiety, and depression in graduate students. In a study of 2279 graduate students, Vanderford et al. found that 43â46% of graduate students studying in the bioscience field were depressed and 41% experienced severe anxiety with education-related stressors being the largest contributor to their anxiety and depression.(11) A different study conducted in 2011 by GradResources, a service organization dedicated to bettering the lives of graduate students, also reported similar findings in a survey of 578 graduate students, where 43% of students said that they were experiencing more stress than they could handle.(12) Medicinal chemistry programs should both initiate preventative measures to decrease burnout in the future as well as actively address the current status of burnout in the graduate student community.
    In recent years, burnout has become more widely recognized and has been discussed in various academic settings and journals.(13â15) Even still, there are not enough resources available or advertised to graduate students: 61% of survey respondents said that they do not feel like there are adequate resources and support for burnout (Figure S9). As a result, students can develop harmful coping mechanisms such as eating disorders, alcoholism, workaholism, or isolationism, all of which were mentioned by survey respondents. It can be particularly unsettling to deal with burnout when one feels like they will not be supported by their community if they come forward. Unfortunately, according to our survey, 75% of respondents felt that burnout was looked down upon in the scientific community (Figure S10), and respondents felt that they would be considered âlazyâ, âreplaceableâ, and âunfit to be in this fieldâ as well as that burnout was a âsign of weaknessâ. This ideology has created an unhealthy environment for graduate students. Recognizing that burnout is a real, preventable condition would benefit students as they are one of the driving forces of scientific advancement.
    It is important to distinguish between a healthy amount of stress and the signs of burnout. Graduate students are always busy, making it difficult to tell if they are experiencing burnout. One common sign of burnout is a lack of control.(16) It is normal for graduate students to have a full calendar; however, those whose schedules are dictated by their experiments, time on instruments, their PIâs schedules, and assistantship obligations are at risk of burning out. Burnout can also manifest as physical signs such as headaches, neck and back pains, weight fluctuations, gastrointestinal distress, and heart palpitations. Additionally, burnout contributes to negative emotional signs like anxiety, depression, irritability, and loss of confidence.(16) These effects can lead to a loss of productivity, which can impede progress through a graduate program.
    Overcoming burnout can be challenging, but regaining control may be the first step.(16) We recommend students refine their organizational skills and break larger projects into smaller, more manageable tasks. Having a more defined work schedule can allow for more time for oneself as well. Personal time can be used for important tasks such as appointments, therapy, or personal activities such as hobbies, socializing, or volunteering. We also recommend students attend to their bodyâs nutritional needs by getting more sleep and eating mindfully. Finally, and most importantly, if you see someone you think might be experiencing burnout, check in with them, validate their feelings, and offer positive solutions such as those listed above.
    As for suggestions for medicinal chemistry graduate programs, we recommend they work to advertise university-wide resources, inform PIs about common signs of burnout, and create a more compassionate environment overall. Currently available resources for burnout likely include university mental health clinics as well as referrals for external practitioners, online self-guided therapy programs such as Learn to Live or meditation apps, and relaxing activities such as events with therapy animals. It is also imperative to provide comprehensive student health insurance that covers mental health costs. Additionally, encourage reasonable allotted time-off policies among laboratories. Overall, when small changes are incorporated into everyday functioning, a graduate studentâs quality of life improves greatly.
    Graduate training is, in many ways, very different from undergraduate training. The most glaring difference is that the focus of graduate school is now on laboratory training and research excellence rather than coursework and exams. Internal and external pressures on research and publications can cause high levels of physical, mental, and emotional stress, possibly leading to burnout and negative views of the scientific and academic communities. Promotion of mental health resources and effective mentorship strategies could help scientists progress from an undergraduate student to an independent graduate researcher and eventually to a professional medicinal chemist.

    Views of the Future

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Once graduate students reach doctoral candidacy, they must start considering their transition from graduate school to the beginning of their independent scientific careers. As with all things, the future is uncertain; therefore, the thoughts expressed within this section can only speak to the current societal and political climates as well as trends in science now. In this section, we will discuss current trends in the job market, different career options available to graduate students, and the perspective of international students.
    Traditionally, graduate students have continued their studies with a postdoctoral position. However, upon the basis of discussions we have had with both academic and industrial professionals through seminars, conferences, networking, and the UMN alumni survey, the views on whether a postdoctoral position is truly necessary are quite varied (Figures S11 and S12). Some graduates view a postdoctoral position as a means to further their training and expand their educational repertoire. Contrarily, some graduates may feel like their only option is finding a postdoctoral position in order to continue earning money, keep their healthcare benefits, and appear more marketable. In fact, the 2017 National Science Foundation (NSF) Survey of Earned Doctorates indicated a steady decline in the number of doctorate recipients obtaining steady employment in the science and engineering sectors since 2001.(17) This raises a larger question: are there too many students graduating from Ph.D. programs, thereby limiting the job market? Results from this NSF survey would suggest yes. It was found that the number of science and engineering doctorates awarded annually jumped from 26â¯896 to 41â¯438 between 2001 and 2017.(17) These statistics are relevant to the field of medicinal chemistry because other graduate programs, namely, chemistry, are producing students that compete directly with medicinal chemistry students for pharmaceutical research and development (R&D) positions.(18,19) The apparent growth in the number of doctorates conferred does not match the steady-state job market for tenure-track academic positions, and the downsizing and outsourcing occurring in industry cannot pick up the slack.(20â22) This indicates that there is a need for more honest conversations about the current job market with students, both graduate and undergraduate, and that relevant information and resources be made available.
    Speaking of the industrial job market, which is evolving rapidly, the training of future medicinal chemists needs to evolve with it. In the past, pharmaceutical drug research was focused on small-molecule and natural products chemistry. This led to the preference for and investment in synthetic chemists as primary R&D hires due to the belief that the foundations of medicinal chemistry could be learned on the job.(18) However, there has been a shift toward biologics, high molecular weight compounds that mimic or are derived from living organisms (i.e., proteins, gene therapy, etc.), in recent years.(18,23) This trend can be seen in the top drugs of 2018 based upon sales in the U.S. as 4 out of the top 5 highest grossing drugs were biologics.(24) This does not mean that chemistry training is less important, but it emphasizes that interdisciplinary coursework and cross-training in research skills are quite valuable in the current drug discovery process. UMN alumni commented on these changes as well when asked about their outlook on the future of the field of medicinal chemistry. They noted this dramatic shift as a âbrightâ and âexcitingâ change. In order to take advantage of this trend, graduate students need to develop connections with those in industry. To facilitate these networks, it would be helpful if medicinal chemistry graduate programs developed relationships with pharmaceutical or biotechnology companies. This could be structured similarly to the connections between well-known synthetic organic chemistry laboratories and pharmaceutical companies from the past(18) and would allow these companies to take advantage of our valuable training as medicinal chemists. Establishing and advertising summer internship programs and postdoctoral positions could also help strengthen this relationship.
    Regarding preparation for careers in academia, our current training is focused more on skills that benefit maintaining a research laboratory like state-of-the-art research techniques and project management but less on skills that benefit teaching like interactive lecturing and lesson planning. Graduate students in medicinal chemistry programs who are interested in careers in academia need more opportunities to develop these skills. Many institutions like UMN have programs that provide graduate students with formal pedagogical training,(25,26) but we further recommend that medicinal chemistry graduate programs incorporate these experiences directly into their programs like the Department of Chemistry at West Virginia University, which introduced an optional elective into their curriculum for graduate students to learn how to teach.(27) We also recommend that medicinal chemistry graduate programs work with colleges within their home institutions or with nearby PUIs to provide graduate students with a variety of undergraduate teaching experiences necessary to succeed in academia like the Mentorship Program for Aspiring Chemistry Teachers, which is organized by the Department of Chemistry at UMN.(28) Our hope is that through these efforts, graduate students and postdoctoral researchers develop their teaching skills and that the education of future undergraduate and graduate students in STEM programs is improved. In order to sustain these improvements, we recommend that current and future faculty continuously work to develop their pedagogies by reading publications in relevant journals, such as the ACS Journal of Chemical Education, as well as attending workshops organized by educational programs at home institutions or professional organizations. Currently, student-centered learning and active learning are popular topics in higher education and have been shown to increase both undergraduate and graduate student learning in STEM.(29â31) Therefore, we recommend that current medicinal chemistry graduate faculty consider employing these strategies in their teaching while working to provide future professors with opportunities to practice their own teaching.
    Often left out of these discussions about career prospects are nontraditional career paths such as those in government, nonprofit organizations, entrepreneurship or even at the interface of any of these career sectors. The skills gained during oneâs graduate career such as project management, problem-solving, and science communication are translatable and can be applied to many nontraditional careers. However, finding career options outside academia and industry is difficult without a supportive network. When current graduate students and postdoctoral fellows were asked if they felt that their program catered to the needs of students interested in nontraditional careers, only 45% responded âyesâ (Figure S13). In contrast, 73% of respondents felt that their programs catered to the needs of those interested in academia (Figure S14), and 74% felt that their programs supported those interested in industrial careers (Figure S15), while only 30% felt that their programs supported those interested in careers in government (Figure S16). We recommend that medicinal chemistry graduate programs conduct exit interviews and collect data about the career progress of their graduates. This information would offer career guidance to current students, strengthen potential career-based courses, and result in an ever-expanding network of graduates from these programs.
    While career prospects for American students are difficult, international students have additional challenges due to immigration policies.(32,33) International students make up almost half of the total number of doctorate recipients from major chemistry programs in the U.S. and 31% of the medicinal chemistry graduate students from the universities surveyed.(34) While the number of graduating international students increases every year, the percentage of international graduates successfully obtaining positions is decreasing due to the fact that the number of granted work visas does not change.(35,36) One obstacle faced by international students attempting to obtain jobs in the U.S. is that small companies, which are usually good starting points for doctorate recipients, may not have the financial ability to sponsor international visas or hire an immigration lawyer. This could lead to more international students pursuing postdoctoral positions in order to strengthen their marketable skills and obtain permanent residency.(36) Acquiring permanent residency can take up to a decade, keeping international researchers in academia for extended periods of time even though postdoctoral positions are often meant to be short-term appointments.(37) These dedicated and well-educated international researchers can greatly contribute to the research efforts of the U.S. as they did during the graduate careers.
    As more young scientists enter the job market, there is a lot to consider. The sociopolitical climate is always changing, which can affect funding and career opportunities. Providing graduate students with more career-oriented workshops, internship experiences, and networking opportunities affords them the ability to obtain a fulfilling and long-lasting career, and exposing graduate students to various career options through seminars, guest speakers, or job fairs could help students consider both traditional and nontraditional career paths.

    A Female Perspective

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    There is an approximately equal proportion of male and female graduate students enrolling in STEM programs.(38) However, there is a disproportionate number of females further up the career ladder according to statistical reports like AAUWâs report âWhy So Few? Women in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematicsâ.(1) Lack of female leadership can lead to a feeling of not belonging or a loss of confidence among women regarding their success in STEM fields. This may be a contributing factor to women leaving STEM careers at a much higher rate than men.(39)
    Given that this âleaky pipelineâ is documented across STEM fields,(39) it is not surprising that it exists in the field of medicinal chemistry. As the article âMedicinal Chemistry: Where are All the Women?â describes, female representation is disproportionate in multiple aspects of scientific involvement including the American Chemical Society (ACS) Division of Medicinal Chemistry membership, authorship in the ACS Journal of Medicinal Chemistry, and leadership positions (i.e., ACS chairs, journal editors, etc.).(40) Furthermore, a recent UMN alumnus reported that all five of the female graduates in their cohort were employed outside the field of medicinal chemistry with only one staying within STEM, thus illustrating the trend of females leaving the field as they progress further in their careers.
    Acknowledging the fact that there is a lack of retention of women in medicinal chemistry has prompted the female graduate students at UMN to form the Women in Medicinal Chemistry Organization, which promotes gender equality and female empowerment. Through this organization, we lead tutoring sessions to prepare all graduate students for preliminary qualifying examinations and plan networking opportunities with invited female seminar speakers; additionally, we have started a journal club to promote critical literature review and organized students to attend the 2019 Empowering Women in Organic Chemistry Conference in Philadelphia. Recognizing the gender disparity within the field of medicinal chemistry and addressing the universal issue of high attrition rates among female scientists could help change the outlook for future generations and the overall atmosphere within the scientific community. We recommend other medicinal chemistry graduate programs implement similar support groups. Furthermore, meetings such as the inaugural Empowering Women in Organic Chemistry Conference are a great way to encourage equity between genders by giving women a platform to speak about their careers and research projects. By attendance of conferences like these, regardless of gender and career status, opportunities will arise for building professional relationships and learning more about gender equity in the workplace.
    AAUW, as well as similar organizations, offers many recommendations in order to empower and retain women in STEM. Reading the reports and publications made by these organizations can spread awareness about the ongoing issues women face and can help instigate reform. One recommendation often made within these reports is that female mentors can play a large role in the success of other female scientists. Mentors provide networking opportunities, guidance, and support for their mentees. However, implicit bias, or unconscious stereotyping, may impact a mentorâs decision to advise a prospective mentee as well as the quality of mentorship given.(41,42) Male mentors may give different levels of support to male and female mentees without realizing it as implicit biases tend to cause people to favor their own âin-groupâ or people who resemble themselves.(43) In particular, women experience difficulties finding suitable mentors because women are still a minority population in STEM fields, creating a disproportionate burden on senior-level female scientists to mentor a higher number of women early in their careers, potentially slowing their own career advancement.(44) Men can alleviate this burden by actively mentoring women and providing support, knowledge, and career expertise. Encouraging peer mentorship would also add to the pool of available mentors.(39)
    Another issue that deserves recognition is the unequal expectations for caretaking that disproportionately affect women as they are typically the primary caretakers in the household. In 1989, Arlie Hochschild coined the term âsecond shiftâ to describe the unpaid labor women do (i.e., running a household, raising children, looking after the elderly, etc.) in addition to their paid labor in the workplace.(45) Traditionally, men have benefited from the second shift, enjoying leisurely activities or spending more time at work while women are often forced to choose between the two, potentially sacrificing the quality of both.(45) Caretaking does not just extend to having children: caretaking can include assisting with the care of elderly family members both physically and financially or any other number of familial pressures that one might experience. While this is a larger societal issue, it has also greatly impacted scientific research, especially in academia. These disproportionate expectations regarding caretaking could be contributing to why women who want have children wait until later in their careers, which could result in higher risk pregnancies and higher chances of infertility.(46) These two factors might also influence a personâs choice to adopt, consider surrogacy, or undergo fertility treatments, all of which come at both a financial and emotional cost. We reviewed the parental leave policies of top-grossing pharmaceutical companies to examine how flexible and transparent industry policies are as well as to compare them with policies enforced by universities. Overall, pharmaceutical companies offer comprehensive family leave packages that address a variety of situations. Universities, however, could offer more consistent and improved familial leave. The maternity/paternity leave policies at UMN, the University of WisconsinâMadison, the University of MichiganâAnn Arbor, the University of Illinois at Chicago, and the University of Iowa varied greatly in how much leave new parents are entitled to with and without pay as well as which situations (giving birth, surrogacy, adoption, fostering, legal guardianship, etc.) constituted the leave. None of these universities had clear policies regarding graduate students, who are both students and often considered employees. Upon reviewing the five policies enforced by these universities, it was clear that not all universities offer the same rights regarding leave and that their policies are not always explicit. To alleviate some of the burdens women typically face by caretaking, universities could be more consistent and explicit about their policies as well as update them to better reflect the needs of their students and employees.
    The female perspective is sometimes overlooked in graduate school and professional careers. However, the inherent differences in our socialization as females is what gives us a unique perspective, which can ultimately benefit scientific discovery. Identities are intersectional. We are not just scientists. We are not just females. We are female scientists, which affords us a distinct perspective that should be taken into account. Gender disparity is a real issue in medicinal chemistry that needs to be acknowledged and, further, needs to be actively addressed.
