    Abstract

    We previously observed a cutaneous type IV immune response in nonhuman primates (NHP) with the mGlu5 negative allosteric modulator (NAM) 7. To determine if this adverse event was chemotype- or mechanism-based, we evaluated a distinct series of mGlu5 NAMs. Increasing the sp3 character of high-throughput screening hit 40 afforded a novel morpholinopyrimidone mGlu5 NAM series. Its prototype, (R)-6-neopentyl-2-(pyridin-2-ylmethoxy)-6,7-dihydropyrimido[2,1-c][1,4]oxazin-4(9H)-one (PF-06462894, 8), possessed favorable properties and a predicted low clinical dose (2 mg twice daily). Compound 8 did not show any evidence of immune activation in a mouse drug allergy model. Additionally, plasma samples from toxicology studies confirmed that 8 did not form any reactive metabolites. However, 8 caused the identical microscopic skin lesions in NHPs found with 7, albeit with lower severity. Holistically, this work supports the hypothesis that this unique toxicity may be mechanism-based although additional work is required to confirm this and determine clinical relevance.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Glutamate is the major excitatory neurotransmitter in the mammalian central nervous system (CNS) that exerts its signaling function through activation of both ionotropic (iGlus) and metabotropic glutamate receptors (mGlus). While iGlus are ligand-gated ion channels responsible for fast excitatory signaling, mGlus belong to class C of the G protein-coupled receptor superfamily that causes slower neurotransmission.(1, 2) The eight identified mGlu subtypes are classified into groups I (mGlu1,5), II (mGlu2,3), and III (mGlu4,6–8) based on sequence homology, second messenger coupling, and pharmacology.(3) The mGlu5 receptor is localized postsynaptically and coupled to Gq/11 proteins that activate phospholipase C, which leads to hydrolysis of phosphoinositide and formation of the two intracellular secondary messengers, inositol triphosphate and diacylglycerol.(4, 5) Neurotherapeutic interest in mGlu5 negative allosteric modulators (NAMs) is based on a plethora of preclinical data linking both mGlu5 antagonism and mGlu5 knockout mice to CNS disorders such as anxiety,(6) pain,(7) depression,(8) epilepsy,(9) neurodegeneration,(10) Parkinson’s disease,(11) cocaine-dependence,(12) and fragile X syndrome (FXS).(13-15) Some of these indications have been tested clinically with the mGlu5 NAMs 1 (AFQ056, mavoglurant; Figure 1), 2 (ADX48621, dipraglurant), and 3 (RG7090, basimglurant),(16) and 3 has been evaluated in a phase II clinical trial as an adjunct therapy for major depression. Although 3 did not meet its primary clinical end point, antidepressant effects across secondary end points based on patient-rated scores were noted.(17) Like the early mGlu5-selective antagonist 4 (MPEP),(18) clinical NAMs 1–3 contain an alkyne moiety, a potentially reactive motif that could lead to adverse safety findings, especially at daily doses >10 mg.(19, 20) To minimize the risk of such toxicities, medicinal chemists have recently identified alternative, alkyne-lacking mGlu5 NAMs that have been reviewed previously(21) and are exemplified by phase I mGlu5 NAM 5 (AZD9272)(22) and the amino-pyrimidine chemotype represented by compound 6 (VU0366058).(23)
    In our quest to identify alkyne-lacking mGlu5 NAMs, we discovered 1-methyl-3-(4-methylpyridin-3-yl)-6-(pyridin-2-ylmethoxy)-1H-pyrazolo[3,4-b]-pyrazine (PF470, 7),(24) a highly potent, selective, and orally bioavailable compound with robust efficacy in a 1-methyl-4-phenyl-1,2,3,6-tetrahydropyridine (MPTP)-rendered Parkinsonian nonhuman primate (NHP) model of PD-LID (levodopa induced dyskinesia).(25) However, clinical progression of 7 was terminated due to findings consistent with an immune-mediated type IV hypersensitivity response in a 90-d NHP regulatory toxicology study.(25, 26) To understand if these adverse findings are most likely chemotype- or mechanism-based, we thoroughly profiled a novel series of mGlu5 NAMs characterized by a central morpholinopyrimidone moiety, whose medicinal chemistry discovery and optimization effort is reported in this publication. The lead compound, (R)-6-neopentyl-2-(pyridin-2-ylmethoxy)-6,7-dihydropyrimido[2,1-c][1,4]oxazin-4(9H)-one (PF-06462894, 8), had favorable biopharmaceutical properties, affording projected low daily oral doses and a long half-life in humans and was therefore profiled in both rat and NHP studies.

    Chemistry

    The synthesis of early leads 9, 10, and 11 employed a malonate condensation with an appropriate 2-aminopyridine to yield the pyrimidone core, which was alkylated to afford the final targets (Scheme 1). Specifically, 2-aminopyridines 12 and 13 (13 was readily synthesized in two steps from 3-hydroxypyridine, see Supporting Information) were condensed with bis(2,4,6-trichlorophenyl)malonate to afford hydroxypyrimidones 14 and 15, respectively, which were etherified with either 3-chlorobenzyl bromide to yield analogues 9 and 10 or with 2-(bromomethyl)-6-methylpyridine to give compound 11. Similarly, tetrahydropyridin-2-amine 16 and dihydro-oxazin-3-amine 17 were condensed with dimethyl malonate to afford the partially saturated cores 18 and 19, which were subsequently alkylated with m-chlorobenzyl tosylate to give analogues 20 and 21.
    Two synthetic strategies were used to access the substituted morpholinopyrimidones shown in Table 3. In the first strategy, amino alcohols 22a/d were acylated with chloroacetyl chloride to give compounds 23a/d, and a subsequent intramolecular alkylation then afforded morpholinones 24a/d (Scheme 2, method A). Their stepwise conversion first to imidates 25a/d using Meerwein’s reagent and displacement with ammonium chloride gave amidines 26a/d, which could be isolated as the hydrochloride salt or used without isolation. Condensation with dimethyl malonate afforded morpholinopyrimidone cores 27a/d. Despite the relatively short length of this synthetic sequence, isolation and purification made high operational throughput challenging, thus we sought an approach that would allow more rapid access to the desired SAR.
    To improve throughput, an alternate means of accessing the requisite amidine was explored using a modification of a known procedure(27) (Scheme 2, method B). Specifically, addition of catalytic amounts of methoxide to chloroacetonitrile afforded the corresponding imidate, which upon addition of amino alcohols 21b/c gave acyclic amidines 28b/c. Further addition of sodium methoxide effected alkylation to cyclic amidines 29b/c, which were not isolated but condensed in the same reaction vessel with dimethyl malonate to provide the desired morpholinopyrimidones 27b/c. The products isolated after only an aqueous workup were sufficiently pure to use directly, and a final etherification of hydroxypyrimidones 27a–d was accomplished by either Mitsunobu conditions or alkylation in the presence of tetrabutylammonium bromide to give the key analogues 8 and 30–39.(28) The yields of this three-step, one-pot approach to cores 27b/c were modest at best (typically <20%). However, the efficient throughput and limited physical manipulations enabled rapid access to a variety of novel hydroxypyrimidone cores despite the introduction of diversity in the first step. As such, this route represented a speed-to-compound approach and was used to quickly assess SAR in the series in both a singleton and parallel format. Preferred compounds were resynthesized through an optimized stepwise route favored for its higher yields and readily isolated intermediates, as illustrated by the large-scale synthesis of 8 and 38 (Scheme 3).
    As shown in Scheme 3, key compounds 8 and 38 were prepared on scale starting from the amino alcohols 22c/d. Acylation with chloroacetyl chloride to give 23c/d was followed by an intramolecular alkylation to yield morpholinones 24c/d. Stepwise conversion first to imidates 25c/d using Meerwein’s reagent, then displacement with ammonium chloride, afforded the amidines 26c/d, which were subsequently condensed with dimethyl malonate to afford the morpholinopyrimidone cores 27c/d. Alkylation as described above yielded the final analogues 8 and 38.

    In Vitro Pharmacology

    Our effort to find alkyne-lacking mGlu5 NAMs distinct from 7 (Figure 1) began with a high-throughput screen (HTS) of our proprietary compound collection. As outlined previously,(25) this effort yielded 15 diverse chemotypes, one of which was pyridopyrimidone 40 (Table 1), a molecule structurally distinct from 7 and other reported mGlu5 NAMs. Molecule 40 demonstrated a favorable, submicromolar mGlu5 binding affinity (mGlu5Ki = 194 nM) and functional inhibitory activity (mGlu5 IC50 = 168 nM). Additionally, 40 had moderate human liver microsome (HLM) stability (CLint,app = 33 mL/min/kg), high passive transcellular permeability (RRCK Papp,A→B = 26 × 10–6 cm/s),(29) and no human P-glycoprotein liability (MDR1/MDCK BA/AB = 1.2).(30) The favorable balance of potency and metabolic stability of 40, relative to lipophilicity (clogP = 1.6), is also reflected by lipophilic efficiency (LipE)(31, 32) and lipophilic metabolism efficiency (LipMetE)(33) values that are in a good range for an HTS starting point (40: LipE = 5.1, LipMetE = 0.06). As described previously,(31-33) these medicinal chemistry parameters can be used evaluate the design progression during lead optimization and are calculated as follows: LipE = −log(mGlu5Ki in Molar) – clogP; LipMetE = clogD7.4 – log(CLint,u); CLint,u is defined as the microsomal unbound clearance.
    Because of its desirable properties and structural novelty, analogue 40 was an ideal lead for further optimization. Initial SAR studies maintained the pyridopyrimidone core and revealed compounds (representative analogues are shown in Table 1) with an improved affinity relative to 40. For example, replacing the thio-ether linker in 40 with an ether led to 9, an analogue with a >20-fold improved human mGlu5 binding affinity (mGlu5Ki = 7 nM; the chloro-phenyl right-hand piece improves affinity by about 5-fold relative to the pyridyl side chain, vide infra). In addition, left-hand core substitution was tolerated with regard to affinity as exemplified by 10, which has LipE and LipMetE values comparable to lead 40, thus indicating efficient utilization of its added lipophilicity for affinity and microsomal stability (10: LipE = 4.6, LipMetE = 2.2, clogP = 3.8). Notably, despite the higher binding affinity of chloro-phenyl-containing 10 (mGlu5Ki = 4.0 nM) relative to its methyl-pyridyl congener 11 (mGlu5Ki = 31 nM), 11 resides in a more favorable CNS property space as exemplified by its lower clogP(34) (10, clogP = 3.8; 11, clogP = 2.1) and higher TPSA (10, TPSA = 66 Å2; 11, TPSA = 79 Å2). This is likely also reflected by the higher HLM stability (10, CLint,app = 26 mL/min/kg; 11, CLint,app < 12 mL/min/kg) and passive permeability (10, RRCK = 7 × 10–6 cm/s; 11, RRCK = 24 × 10–6 cm/s) of 11 relative to 10. However, the in vivo receptor occupancy (IVRO) of 11 in rat [unbound plasma compound concentration (Cp,u)-derived IC50 = 278 nM, vide infra] was significantly right-shifted, which prompted us to further optimize the properties of this series with a focus on increasing its intrinsic sp3 character.

    It is well-documented that achiral compounds with high sp2 character have a lower chance of clinical success than chiral compounds incorporating higher levels of saturation, partly as increased saturation correlates with increased aqueous solubility and a lower probability to interact with off-targets.(35) As shown with 20, we were able to readily increase the sp3 character by replacing the central pyridopyrimidone moiety with its corresponding tetrahydro-version. Relative to 9, compound 20 maintained binding at mGlu5 (9, mGlu5Ki = 7.0 nM; 20, mGlu5Ki = 10 nM), indicating that aromaticity adjacent to the pyrimidone moiety is not required for binding affinity. A total of four additional structures with increased aliphaticity were explored, and the morpholinopyrimidones, exemplified by 21, were identified as the most favorable core with respect to affinity and properties (the SAR of additional cores is shown in the Supporting Information). As seen in Table 2, 21 (mGlu5Ki = 27 nM) largely maintained its affinity relative to 20 (mGlu5Ki = 10 nM), while its higher polarity (21, cLogP = 1.3, TPSA = 53 Å2; 20, cLogP = 3.0, TPSA = 44 Å2) somewhat increased HLM stability and passive permeability (Papp,A→B: 21, CLint,app = 26 mL/min/kg Papp,A→B = 31 × 10–6 cm/s; 20, CLint,app < 40 mL/min/kg Papp,A→B = 26 × 10–6 cm/s). Analogous to 10 and 11, substituents on the left-hand portion of the core were tolerated and led to a significant improvement in affinity, with the (R)-isomers having a higher affinity than the (S)-isomers (see, for example: 31, mGlu5Ki = 1 nM; 30, mGlu5Ki = 3 nM).

    To improve the moderate lability in HLM (CLint,app = 39 mL/min/kg) and passive permeabilty (Papp,A→B = 4 × 10–6 cm/s) of 31, further SAR exploration focused on variations of the ether side chain and the benzyl substituent on the morpholino moiety. As shown in Table 3, a range of 5- and 6-membered heteroaromatic rings yielded compounds with good mGlu5 affinity (e.g., 32–35, mGlu5Ki = 3.8–16 nM), with the 2-pyridyl ether 32 offering the best combination of affinity (mGlu5Ki = 10 nM) and low HLM turnover (CLint,app = 8.7 mL/min/kg). As indicated by analogue 36 (mGlu5Ki = 98 nM), aromaticity was required for affinity in the right-hand portion of the series of molecules. However, the sp3 character of analogues such as 32 could be further increased by replacing the phenyl group in the left-hand portion with aliphatic moieties (8, 37–39), indicating that the activity of the benzyl substituent in 32 was due to lipophilic rather than π-system interactions. As such, 8 and 38 offered the most favorable combination of high affinity (mGlu5Ki ≤ 6.1 nM), HLM stability (CLint,app < 8 mL/min/kg), and high passive permeability (Papp,A→B ≥ 28 × 10–6 cm/s), which is reflected by a favorable balance of LipE and LipMetE values (LipE ≥ 6.7 and LipMetE ≥ 0.5 for both 8 and 38).

    A binding model of 8 to mGlu5 transmembrane domain (mGlu5 TMD) was built by both traditional docking(34) and induced-fit docking(36) based on the X-ray crystal structure of mGlu5 TMD with 1 (PDB 4OO9).(37) The induced-fit docking method produces a reasonable model to explain the observed structure–activity relationship (see Supporting Information). In this model, 8 occupies the binding site in a similar fashion to 1 (Figure 2). The morpholinopyrimidone ring of 8 overlays with the bicycle of 1, and the t-butyl group is positioned toward the extracellular region in the pocket formed by Val-7405.40, Ile-6513.36, and Leu-7445.44, favoring the (R)-isomer (30 vs 31, Table 2). Meanwhile, the pyridine ring aligns with the 3-methylphenyl ring of 1, situated between Pro-6553.40 and Ala-8107.40. The induced-fit docking study demonstrates different interaction motifs of 8 in the binding site that are not observed with 1. The morpholinopyrimidone carbonyl accepts a hydrogen bond from Asn-4745.47. Because of the absence of a hydroxyl group as in 1, Ser-8057.35 does not interact with 8 directly, but with the backbone carbonyl of Thr-8017.31 (Figure 2B). At the same time, the pyridine ring of 8 accepts a hydrogen bond from Tyr-6593.44 hydroxyl. Because this tyrosine hydroxyl could also interact with the buried crystal water in the 4OO9 structure,(37) one could envision that in the absence of a pyridine nitrogen (as in 31, 34, 38, and 39), the tyrosine hydroxyl could switch its interaction to the crystal water, consistent with the observation that the pyridine nitrogen is not critical for activity. A nonaromatic substituent at the R2 position, such as in 36, is less favorable in the flat pocket formed by Pro-6553.40 and Ala-8107.40. Similarly, meta-substitution of the aryl ring, such as in 33 and 39, improves activity because it reaches further into the binding pocket, similar to the methyl group on the phenyl ring in 1 (Figure 2B).

    in Vivo Studies with 8 and 38

    Having discovered 8 and 38 as mGlu5 NAMs in the desired property space based on their in vitro pharmacological and absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) profiles, both compounds underwent pharmacokinetic (PK) studies in rats and NHP (Table 4). From a neuropharmacokinetic perspective in rats, 8 and 38 had net equilibrium at the blood–brain barrier with parallel elimination rates in both central and peripheral compartments. In both rats and NHP, 8 had high total blood clearance (CLb), low steady-state volume of distribution (Vss), short half-life (t1/2), and low oral bioavailability (F). Alternatively in these species, 38 had moderate CLb and Vss, short-to-moderate t1/2, and moderate F. Because of both compounds’ lack of metabolism in interpecies intestinal microsomes (data not shown) and inherent high permeability, the observed low-to-moderate F of 8 and 38 seemed predominately due to hepatic first-pass extraction.

    With favorable pharmacokinetics for in vivo profiling, the exposure-mGlu5 receptor occupancy (RO) relationship for both 8 and 38 were defined in rats by an in vivo binding assay using [3H]3-methoxy-5-pyridin-2-ylethynylpyridine ([3H]MPEPy) and application of a direct-response model.(25) The Cp,u-derived IC50 [95% confidence interval (CI)] for 8 and 38 were 4.21 (3.54, 4.98) nM and 6.67 (5.13, 8.58) nM, respectively (Table 4), thus 40–60-fold left-shifted relative to that of the original lead 11 (Cp,u-derived IC50 = 278 nM, see Supporting Information for IVRO curve, vide supra). On the basis of its favorable PK and rat in vivo RO profile, 38 advanced to a 14-d rat exploratory toxicology study (ETS). Adverse findings (based on degenerative effects in the liver of rats and monkeys, see Supporting Information for details) different from those described in this paper (vide infra) led 38 to have an insufficient therapeutic index (TI). Unlike compound 8 (vide infra), 38 was therefore not further developed. Subsequently, using previously described dosing methodology with positron emission tomography (PET) and [18F]FPEB,(25) the NHP Cp,u-derived IC50 (95% CI) of 8 was determined to be 2.68 (1.34, 4.41) nM. For 8, the mGlu5 RO versus Cp,u in rats and NHP, as well as representative NHP PET displacement images, are shown in Figure 3. The excellent consistency between human in vitro Ki and Cp,u-based IC50 in both rats and NHP provided high confidence in the quantitative clinical translatability of the binding interactions of 8 with mGlu5. Essentially equivalent IC50 values in rats and NHP also suggested similar interneurocompartmental relationships in both species, which is to be expected(32) for a non-P-glycoprotein/non-BCRP substrate, demonstrating equilibrium at the blood–brain barrier in rats. This was ultimately confirmed by empirically determining Cb,u:Cp,u in both species (Table 4). However, instead of next using these exposure-RO data to determine the RO at which it might attenuate the levodopa induced dyskinesia in MPTP-rendered parkinsonian NHP (i.e., “efficacy”),(16) compound 8 was immediately progressed to rat and NHP toxicology studies. This strategic decision was directly based on our prior experience(25) in NHP with the structurally distinct mGlu5 NAM 7 that had significant effectiveness in this model(38) of PD-LID at an RO of 77 < x ≤ 90%. Integration of the collective in vitro and preclinical in vivo properties with expected efficacy at 80% mGlu5 RO (human Cp of 31 ng/mL) projected 8 to display clinically low CLp (1.4 mL/min/kg), moderate Vss (1.0 L/kg) and t1/2 (8 h), and high F (91%) to afford a 2 mg BID (bis in die) dosing regimen to maintain 80% RO as an average central concentration at steady-state.

    in Vitro and in Vivo Safety Profile of 8

    Broader selectivity screening with 8 (10 μM) demonstrated no significant activities (i.e., IC50 or EC50 > 10 μM) in kinase panels, a proprietary phospodiesterase panel and a broad spectrum BioPrint panel, in which the μ opioid receptor had the greatest interaction (EC20 of 10 μM, see Supporting Information for the complete list of off-targets profiled). Compound 8 further differentiated from 7 in the mouse allergy model (MAM),(39) an in vivo model developed internally to predict the allergenic potential of systemically administered low molecular weight drugs.(39) In this model, draining lymph node cellularity is determined by flow cytometry following a once daily subcutaneous injection of test compound for three consecutive days. A stimulation index (SI; mean total lymph node cell number in the drug-treated group/mean total lymph node cell number in the vehicle control group) of >2.5 is considered a positive response in the model. There appears to be a good correlation between drugs associated with hypersensitivity reactions in the clinic and a positive response in the MAM.(39) Because several pieces of data suggested that the skin lesions observed in the NHP regulatory toxicology study with 7 were immune-mediated, the MAM was used as a tool to assess the allergenic potential of 8 compared to 7. Whereas 7 resulted in a positive response (SI > 5.4), compound 8 was negative (SI values ≤ 1.7, n = 3 experiments), indicating low potential for hypersensitivity reactions.
    Because of the favorable in vitro and in vivo profile of 8 and its differentiation from 7 in the MAM, the safety of 8 was subsequently assessed in 14-d exploratory toxicology studies (ETS) in rats and NHPs (i.e., cynomolgus macaques). As reported previously(26) and summarized in Table 5, in rats at 50, 150, or 500 mg/kg/d (PO; N = 3/gender/dose), no adverse clinical or histopathological findings were observed up to a steady-state AUC0–24-based exposure that was 448 times the 80% RO (500 mg/kg/d). In NHPs (50, 150, and 600 mg/kg/d, PO, N = 1/gender/dose), 600 mg/kg/d was poorly tolerated in the female, resulting in early euthanasia on day 1, although this occurred without clinical or microscopic evidence of skin toxicity. In the remaining animals at 50 and 150 mg/kg/d, the compound was clinically tolerated for the duration of the study (14 days) reaching steady-state AUC0–24-based exposures up to 86 times the 80% RO. However, at this exposure (150 mg/kg/d), histopathological analysis detected microscopic lesions in skin (chest, scrotum) and oral mucosa that were identical to those observed in the 90-day toxicology study with 7. Although these skin lesions were of lower severity relative to those observed with 7, the similar histopathological characteristics(26) indicated that both were due to the same underlying pathogenesis. As described by Palanisamy,(26) collectively, the microscopic features of these skin lesions, including the cellular makeup and distribution of the inflammatory infiltrate and the time of onset for lesion development were consistent with a delayed type IV hypersensitivity reaction.

    Interestingly, 8 did not form any detectable glutathione adducts in liver microsomes (±NADPH),(40) suggesting neither itself nor its CYP-generated metabolic intermediates/metabolites form macromolecular adducts in vivo (see Supporting Information). Furthermore, due to the possibility of toxicity being metabolite-dependent, both rat (500 mg/kg/d) and NHP (150 mg/kg/d) plasma samples [day 1 and day 12 (rat) or day 13 (NHP)] from the respective 14-d ETS underwent qualitative metabolite profiling using UV-supplemented LC-MS/MS; a quantitative analysis could not be performed due to the use of nonradiolabeled 8 and the lack of an authentic standard for each detected metabolite. In both species, 8 had complex metabolite profiles but showed structurally common metabolites with no differences in plasma metabolite profiles acutely (day 1) and at steady-state (day 12 or day 13) within or across genders. In NHP, 23 different metabolites were observed with the most abundant (based on both UV and MS signals) metabolites arising from monohydroxylation (6 metabolites), N-oxygenation (1), O-dealkylation (1), morpholine-opening (1), and glucuronidation of primary metabolites (10). The respective interspecies biotransformation data for 8 and 7(41) are highly consistent and show no evidence that is compelling enough to suggest a common metabolite or metabolite substructure is responsible for the NHP-specific toxicity induced by both compounds.
    We have previously proposed that the cutaneous type IV immune response in NHPs observed with mGlu5 NAM 7 may be due to a mechanism-based event. As reported herein, 8 leads to the identical microscopic skin lesions in NHPs found with 7, although the skin lesions with 8 are of reduced severity relative to those with compound 7. In our opinion, this supports the hypothesis that this skin toxicity is mechanism-, not compound-, mediated, because we believe compounds 7 and 8 are from structurally distinct series. This is supported by the finding that compounds 7 and 8 have distinct fingerprints in a phenotypic screen using standardized panels of human primary cell/coculture systems.(42) In addition, both compounds have seemingly innocuous secondary pharmacological profiles without any intrinsic (or metabolism-dependent) chemical reactivity. It is worth noting, however, that additional work is required for the unequivocal confirmation of this hypothesis because the current data cannot fully rule out that an unidentified commonality between molecules 7 and 8 caused the adverse skin reactions. For example, while the ancillary pharmacology of both compounds has been screened extensively, exhaustive screening is not feasible and it is possible that both 7 and 8 have an effect at an unknown off-target that triggered the skin lesions. We have also not resynthesized the metabolites of 7 and 8 to fully characterize their primary and secondary pharmacology, and it cannot completely be ruled out that a metabolite common to compounds 7 and 8 caused the type IV immune response in NHPs. Additional work is also necessary to understand why similar adverse events were not observed preclinically with other mGlu5 NAMs, especially with compounds that advanced into clinical development (i.e., compounds 1, 2, and 3).

    Conclusions

    In summary, we have developed a series of alkyne-lacking mGlu5 NAMs with favorable physicochemical properties and ADME characteristics. Our design strategy was guided by increasing the three-dimensionality of our early leads 9, 10 and 11 by introducing bond saturation within the central core structure. In vitro and in vivo profiling in both rats and NHPs predicted lead 8 to require low clinically therapeutic doses (2 mg BID) to achieve an average of 80% mGlu5 RO at steady state. Despite these positive attributes, further development of 8 was stopped due to microscopic skin lesions in NHP, which were classified as a type IV immune response analogous to the observations with our prior candidate 7.(25) Compound 8 does not have any known significant off-target activities, and the characterization of plasma samples from both rat and NHP toxicology studies suggest it does not form any reactive metabolites. In addition, 8 did not lead to immune activation in a mouse drug allergy model. In totality, the results support the hypothesis that in NHP these adverse findings may have been caused by functionally blocking mGlu5. Additional work is required to rule out any other causes for this common skin reaction with compounds 7 and 8. It is currently also unknown why a similar adverse event has not been reported with any of the clinical mGlu5 NAMs 1, 2, and 3. Finally, it will be important to understand the translatability of this NHP finding to humans in order to adequately assess the clinical safety of the mGlu5 NAM mechanism. However, because of the risk of a similar skin toxicity in humans and its clinically nonmonitorable nature, the described work led to the discontinuation of both 8 and the Pfizer mGlu5 NAM program.
