    Abstract

    Oral PI3Kδ inhibitors such as Idelalisib and Duvelisib have shown efficacy as anticancer agents and Idelalisib has been approved for the treatment of three B-cell cancers. However, Idelalisib has a black box warning on its product label regarding the risks of fatal and serious toxicities including hepatic toxicity, severe diarrhea, colitis, pneumonitis, infections, and intestinal perforation. Some of these side effects are mechanism-related and could hinder the development of Idelalisib for less severe conditions. For respiratory diseases, compounds administered by inhalation are delivered directly to the site of action and may improve the therapeutic index of a drug, minimizing undesired side effects. This work describes the discovery and optimization of inhaled PI3Kδ inhibitors intended for the treatment of severe asthma and COPD. Once the potency was in the desired range, efforts were focused on identifying the particular physicochemical properties that could translate into better lung retention. This medicinal chemistry exercise led to the identification of LAS195319 as a candidate for clinical development.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The PI3K (phosphoinositide 3-kinase) family of lipid kinases comprises three classes, PI3K class I, class II, and class III. PI3K class I is the most characterized, being activated by cell surface receptors to produce phosphatidylinositol-3,4,5-trisphosphate (PIP3). PIP3 is a key cellular mediator that activates downstream signaling pathways involved in relevant cellular processes such as growth, proliferation, chemotaxis, metabolism, and survival. PI3K class I is further subdivided into four different isoforms PI3Kα, PI3Kβ, PI3Kδ, and PI3Kγ, the first two isoforms being ubiquitously expressed and PI3Kδ and PI3Kγ showing a more restricted expression pattern that seems to play a major role in leukocytes.(1)
    PI3Kδ is expressed, although not exclusively, in immune cells such as T-cells, B-cells, neutrophils, mast cells, and basophils modulating relevant functions that include cell migration, cytokine production, antibody production, and even steroid resistance.(2,3) Such effects in immune cells suggest that selective pharmacological inhibition of PI3Kδ could be a promising approach for treating respiratory diseases such as asthma and COPD (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease).
    Considering that oral PI3Kδ inhibitors approved as anticancer agents, such as Idelalisib, have reported serious adverse effects including hepatic toxicity, severe diarrhea, colitis, pneumonitis, infections, and intestinal perforation,(4) our internal approach was focused on reducing systemic side effects by administering the compounds by the inhaled route. Inhaled drug delivery presents a huge opportunity for the treatment of respiratory diseases, delivering drugs directly to the site of action which may result in a rapid onset of desired effects and with a potential reduction or avoidance of systemic side effects. Thus, despite challenges associated with designing drugs for inhaled delivery,(5) this route of administration represents an attractive alternative for the treatment of these diseases compared to conventional routes of administration. In this respect, GlaxoSmithKline and RespiVert have both reported inhaled PI3Kδ and dual PI3Kδ/PI3Kγ inhibitors which have progressed to clinical studies.(6)
    The aim of this work is to provide an overview of physicochemical properties that may lead to improved lung retention times of inhaled compounds with the intention of avoiding compounds with extremely poor solubility. The low solubility approach can initially provide good duration in the lung, but persistence of undissolved material may be irrelevant as these solid particles are unavailable to elicit a pharmacological effect and may provoke an irritant response if such particles remain in the airways.
    Herein is presented the medicinal chemistry program that allowed for the optimization of a series of neutral compounds for inhaled administration and led to the discovery of LAS195319 as an inhaled clinical candidate for the treatment of respiratory diseases such as asthma and COPD.

    Medicinal Chemistry Strategy

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Initially the strategy focused on finding compounds with high potency, to support low inhaled dose and high plasma clearance to ensure low systemic exposure. Recently, we reported the identification of a novel oral PI3Kδ inhibitor 1(7) (Scheme 1). Similar to IC87114,(8) the co-crystal structure of 1 and PI3Kδ shows that the ligand also adopts a propeller-shaped conformation in which the pyrrolotriazinone moiety is sandwiched into an induced hydrophobic specificity pocket between Trp760 and Met752. Increasing potency by interacting with the affinity pocket has already been described by Shokat et al. at University of California.(9) This pocket is flanked on the bottom by the side chains of Asp787 and Asp911 and on the top by Tyr813 as shown in Figure 1. Thus, efforts toward the identification of highly potent PI3Kδ inhibitors were based on introducing functional groups that could interact with the amino acids present in this region and also give rise to additional clearance routes (e.g., glucuronidation of phenols). Before starting the SAR work around the affinity pocket, the methyl group present in the central core of IC87114 was introduced onto the pyrrolotriazinone scaffold of 1 to give compound 2, which led to an increase in both potency and metabolism (Scheme 1).

    It is clear from the data outlined in Table 1 that incorporating a substituted aromatic or heteroaromatic ring in the affinity pocket leads, in general, to high enzymatic potency for PI3Kδ and high selectivity (>100-fold) over the related α and β isoforms. The high enzymatic potency observed could, in part, be due to the presence of heteroatoms in the aromatic ring that may establish new interactions with the amino acids present in this pocket. These amino acids are well conserved between isoforms, so the presence of such interactions alone cannot explain the good selectivity obtained. However, other known propeller-shaped inhibitors which are described to stabilize a conformational change that opens the above-mentioned hydrophobic specificity pocket in the active site have good selectivity, and this process seems to occur more readily in PI3Kδ than in the other isoforms.(8) However, a propeller shape does not always guarantee PI3Kδ specificity (vide infra).
    In order to increase the systemic clearance of compounds, functional groups which could favor additional routes of metabolism were introduced. Phase II metabolism involves reactions that chemically change either the drug or oxidative metabolites into compounds (conjugates) that are soluble enough to be excreted in urine. For this purpose the OH substituent on the aromatic ring of phenols, well-known to form these conjugates, was introduced onto different positions of the aromatic group which resided in the affinity pocket. Compound 14, with the OH in the para position, showed high in vitro glucuronidation but moderate in vitro potency. Docking studies suggested that in this position there was little potential for new interactions, but heteroatoms in the meta position could potentially form hydrogen bonds either with Lys779 at one side of the pocket or with Tyr813 at the opposite side. Indeed, introduction of a hydrogen-bond acceptor atom such as a fluorine (compound 15) in this position increased both the affinity for PI3Kδ and also the extent of glucuronidation of the parent compound. Aminopyrimidine 16 showed potency similar to compound 14 but much lower in vitro metabolism. Then, a series of compounds was prepared in which the phenol was fixed in the meta position and a hydrogen bond acceptor group was introduced in the other meta position with the intention to interact with both Lys779 and Tyr813. Such compounds (17–19) had excellent affinity for PI3Kδ and high in vitro clearance. Compound 20 also showed good PI3Kδ potency, probably due to the capacity of the relatively acidic sulfonamide NH group and/or the oxygens of the sulfone to form hydrogen bonds in this pocket. Removing the OH group of compound 20 gave an equipotent compound 21, indicating that double interaction might not be taking place. Other constrained NH derivatives were also studied (compounds 22 and 23) leading to less potent compounds. Finally, introduction of different linkers between the hinge binding motif and the phenol ring was also studied (compounds 24–26), the carboxamide analogue 26 being the most potent.
    In parallel, another well-known kinase hinge binding motif, the pyrrolopyrimidine (which led to series B), was explored as shown in Table 2. In this case ortho-substituted phenols were tolerated (compounds 32–35) although potency was improved by introducing of additional hydrogen-bond acceptor groups in the meta position (compound 33–35), as observed in the previous SAR. The para-substituted phenol 36 was more potent than the corresponding analogue 14 and this could be explained by docking studies, which showed a potential new interaction with Lys779 not observed with analogue 14. Potency could be slightly improved by adding a hydrogen-bond acceptor atom such as fluorine in the meta position (compound 37). Docking studies suggested that a new interaction with Asp787 could be gained from this meta position. However, poor selectivity versus PI3Kα was observed for this compound, demonstrating that not all propeller-shaped compounds have good PI3Kδ specificity. One could hypothesize that the double interaction with the affinity pocket could compensate for the energy required to open the above-mentioned specificity pocket in other PI3K-isoforms. This lack of selectivity was also found in compounds 38 and 39, although the latter just fulfilled our selectivity criteria (≥100-fold versus PI3Kδ). Removal of the phenolic OH-group of 39 gave rise to the equally potent and more selective compound 40. In this case, the double interaction with Lys779 and Asp787 is not possible and, although this did not have an impact on PI3K delta affinity, it could explain the higher selectivity observed versus the other isoforms (vide supra). Phenol bioisosteres such as indoles were then studied leading to potent compounds 41 and 42. In addition, compounds containing small heterocycles such us pyrazoles were synthesized giving rise to the highly potent compounds 43 and 44. Finally, addition of various linkers between the hinge binding motif and the phenol ring was studied and led to potent compounds 45–47 with high in vitro clearance.

    The X-ray co-crystal structure of 39 (Figure 2) bound to PI3Kδ was resolved at 2.72 Å, revealing a number of polar interactions in the affinity pocket as was predicted. The OH of the phenol group forms hydrogen bonds with the side chain atoms of Asp787 and Tyr813, while one of the oxygens of the sulfone forms a hydrogen bond with the side chain of Lys779. In addition, the pyrrolopyrimidine forms hydrogen bonds to the hinge region with the main chain atoms of Glu826 and Val828. Similar to the co-crystal structure obtained with compound 1, the molecule adopts a propeller-shaped conformation where the pyrrolotriazinone moiety is sandwiched into the induced hydrophobic specificity pocket between Trp760 and Met752.
    Since many compounds with good in vitro profiles were found in these initial studies, before embarking on further SAR work around the affinity pocket, an in vivo pharmacokinetic assay was used to identify molecules with the highest lung retention. Briefly, five compounds were pooled and administered in solution to rats via the intratracheal route using cassette dosing. Each compound was administered at a dose of 0.015 mg/kg, a low enough dose to minimize any potential solubility issue which could have an impact on the lung retention profile of the compounds assayed (the thermodynamic solubility of some compounds tested in water at pH = 7.4 was <0.01 μg/mL) . A total of 64 compounds (20 from series A and 44 from series B) were tested in this assay, and the lung and plasma profiles of the compounds were evaluated. Figure 3 shows the correlation between the amount of compound recovered in the lung (expressed as percent of dose) at the first kinetic time point after administration versus the overall exposure (AUC) in the lung. From this plot we can conclude that good initial lung recovery does not always translate into good lung retention, but what seems clear is that compounds that show poor initial lung recovery never translate into compounds with good lung retention.
    The lung and plasma concentrations at different kinetic time points were studied for each compound, and a ratio between both was then calculated. For compounds intended to be administered via the inhaled route, the plasma concentrations and overall systemic exposure should be minimized as much as possible in order to avoid potential systemic side effects. Figure 4 shows the correlation between the lung/plasma ratio at equilibrium (1 h after administration) versus the dose-normalized lung exposure (AUC/dose) for each compound. In this case a good correlation is observed between both parameters, allowing the selection of compounds with good overall lung exposure and an optimal lung/plasma split. Many compounds showed lung/plasma ratios of over a 100-fold, and a few even showed ratios of over 1000-fold.
    To better understand the physicochemical properties that could influence lung retention, several parameters were plotted against each other in order to study possible correlations.
    First of all, permeability was studied and compared with dose-normalized lung exposure as previously described. As shown in Figure 5, compounds with limited permeability, PAMPA < 1, show, in general, better lung retention than those with higher permeability.
    Other physical properties such as hydrogen bond donor counts and polar surface area have been described to be positively correlated with lung retention.(10) Thus, when tPSA was plotted versus MW (Figure 6), it was observed that compounds with higher lung exposure (represented by the bigger circles) were the ones with MW above 550 and with a tPSA higher than 150. However, as polar surface area tends to increase with molecular weight, it is difficult to find examples in the top left and bottom right quadrants and one cannot discard that only one and not both parameters are required for higher lung retention times. An increase in the number of H-bond donors also positively impacted on lung exposure as shown in Figure 7. However, it should be taken into account that increasing the number of H-bond donors is likely to compromise the cellular permeability of compounds for an intracellular target. To this end, the ratio between cellular and enzymatic potency was calculated for all compounds and a higher proportion of compounds with ratios > 10 was found for those having five H-bond donors (data not shown). Therefore, examples with six H-bond donors were not synthesized.
    The time course graphs of compound recovery from lung tissue after i.t. administration of a set of compounds from both series at 0.015 mg/kg in solution were superimposed and colored by the number of H-bond donors present in each molecule (Figure 8). The visual data suggest that increasing the number of H-bond donors leads to a positive impact on the distribution phase, resulting in a higher lung AUC.
    With this information it can be concluded that, for this series of nonbasic compounds, the physicochemical properties (and the respective optimal values) contributing to higher lung retention are PAMPA < 1, MW > 550, PSA > 150, and H-bond donors >3. For this reason, chemical efforts for subsequent compounds were focused on the synthesis of molecules fulfilling the aforementioned parameters.
    Following this strategy compound 50 was identified and compared with compound 15, an equipotent inhibitor. As shown in Table 3, only compound 50 fulfills the criteria previously described in terms of permeability and physicochemical properties. Such differences should translate into better lung retention for compound 50 compared to compound 15.

    Thus, both compounds were further profiled in rat in vivo PK experiments. As shown in Table 4, both compounds showed high clearance and high volumes of distribution, resulting in short terminal half-lives and poor oral bioavailability. These compounds were then administered via the intratracheal route both in solution and in suspension (Table 5). As predicted, compound 50 showed a much higher lung exposure compared to compound 15 in solution as well as in suspension. In addition, a much higher lung/plasma partition ratio was obtained with compound 50, reinforcing our goal of reducing on-target systemic side effects. Lung half-lives were slightly increased after administration of the compounds in suspension as compared to in solution, probably due to the dissolution rate of these poorly soluble compounds (thermodynamic aqueous solubility of 36 μg/mL for compound 15 and 3 μg/mL for compound 50). Compound 50 also showed an improved lung profile versus other closely related compounds 41 and 42 (data not shown)—neither of these examples fulfilled the above-defined criteria.

    Considering the difference in terms of lung retention, a better in vivo efficacy was expected for compound 50 compared to compound 15. The OVA-induced inflammation model in the brown Norway rat was selected to assess the in vivo efficacy of both compounds. In this model, an OVA challenge in sensitized rats causes an allergic inflammatory response that provokes an influx of inflammatory cells to the animal airways and induces functional lung changes that mimic certain features of human asthma. Intratracheal administration of compound 50 (LAS195319) as a suspension at 0.3 and 0.1 mg/kg i.t. One hour before OVA aerosol challenge caused an inhibition of eosinophil and neutrophil infiltration in BAL fluid of 55% and 77%, respectively, at the higher dose (Figure 9) and 21% and 42% at the lower dose. Fluticasone at 0.3 mg/kg used as positive control, causing inhibition of neutrophil and eosinophil infiltration of 89% and 82%, respectively. Results obtained with compound 15 demonstrated that it was required to increase the dose in order to see efficacy, showing no effect in eosinophil infiltration and a modest inhibition of neutrophil infiltration in BAL fluid of 37% when administered at 1 mg/kg. (data not shown).
    At the efficacious dose of 0.3 mg/kg for LAS195319, the maximum free drug concentration in plasma (free Cmax) after i.t. administration was 0.3 nM, not covering the cellular IC50 determined for this compound and thus, minimizing the potential for side effects associated with systemic drug exposure.
    As a consequence of these encouraging results, LAS195319 was further characterized. Enzymatically, the compound showed higher selectivity versus PI3Kα than for PI3Kβ and PI3Kγ (Table 2). The IC50 for cellular inhibition of PI3Kδ using THP-1 cells stimulated with M-CSF was measured at 2.7 nM for this compound (Table 3). To evaluate the cellular inhibition of PI3Kβ, an assay based on stimulation of HUVEC cells with sphingosine-1-P was employed.(11) The results, 61% inhibition at 10 μM, indicated that the poor enzymatic selectivity between δ and β isoforms (20-fold) was not translated to the cellular level. In addition, LAS195319 was highly selective against an extensive panel of protein and lipid kinases and GPCRs (see the Supporting Information). In terms of cardiac safety, LAS195319 did not show any activity on the hERG channel up to a concentration of 10 μM. Finally, LAS195319 confirmed very low oral bioavailability in a second preclinical species such as dog (F < 1%) as was desired to avoid the systemic side effects that have been described for oral PI3Kδ inhibitors.
    Taken together, these properties made LAS195319 an ideal inhaled candidate for clinical development for the treatment of respiratory diseases. A full biological profile will be published in due course.

    Chemistry

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The synthesis of the key intermediate 9 started from the pyrrole carboxylic acid 3, which was transformed into the amide 4 by formation of the acyl chloride and subsequent reaction with aniline (Scheme 2). N-Amination of the pyrrole 4 was achieved by a biphasic reaction with chloramine generated in situ from the oxidation of ammonia with sodium hypochlorite using N-methyl-N,N-dioctyloctan-1-aminium chloride as phase-transfer catalyst. Under these conditions the amination occurs selectively on the NH of the pyrrole, giving 5 as the sole regioisomer. Condensation of 5 with Boc-protected alanine mediated by propylphosphonic anhydride gave amide 6. The cyclization of 6 to the corresponding pyrrolotriazine 8 involved a two-step process.(12) In the first step, treatment of 6 with the complex formed from triphenylphosphine and bromine in methylene chloride gave the O-cyclized intermediate 7, which, without isolation, was rearranged to the more stable pyrrolotriazine 8 by treatment with a 7 M solution of ammonia in methanol. Finally, deprotection of the Boc group with trifluoroacetic acid gave amine 9.
    Aminopyrimidine-containing compounds 14–26 were prepared according to the synthetic route described in Scheme 3. Nucleophilic attack of amine 9 on chloropyrimidines 10 in the presence of cesium fluoride gave compounds of type 12. Reaction of compounds 12 (either X = Br or the more reactive X = I) with the corresponding boronic acids or boronic esters under Suzuki reaction conditions yielded final compounds 14–24. Alternatively, thioether 25 was obtained by a copper-catalyzed arylation of the corresponding aromatic thiol with intermediate 12.
    The preparation of compound 26 started with the nucleophilic attack of amine 9 on the protected chloropyrimidine ester 11. In this case, milder conditions to those described above were used—refluxing ethanol in the presence of DIEA was sufficient to complete the reaction.     For the preparation of the compounds with the pyrrolopyrimidine hinge binder 32–47, an analogous synthetic route was followed (Scheme 4).
    Nucleophilic attack of amine 9 on chloropyrrolopyrimidine 27 in the presence of cesium fluoride gave intermediate 28. Reaction of bromo derivative 28 with the corresponding boronic acids or boronic esters under Suzuki reaction conditions and followed by SEM deprotection yielded compounds 32–45. The copper-catalyzed reaction of 28 with an aromatic thiol failed but the desired target 46 was finally able to be prepared by nucleophilic substitution of amine 9 with the prefunctionalized chloropyrrolopyrimidine 29, followed by SEM deprotection. This deprotection took place in two steps as mainly the corresponding hemiaminal intermediates were obtained after subjecting the SEM-protected compounds to acidic conditions—subsequent treatment with ammonia was required to yield the final compounds.
    The nucleophilic substitution reaction of amine 9 with the unprotected chloropyrrolopyrimidine ester 30 was conducted in tBuOH. Due to the enhanced reactivity of 30, the addition of cesium fluoride was not needed in this case for the reaction to reach completion. Subsequent removal of the benzyl group by Pd-catalyzed hydrogenation gave carboxylic acid 31, which was reacted with the corresponding aniline after propylphosphonic anhydride activation to yield amide 47.
    Although LAS195319 was first synthesized by the route described in Scheme 4, during the course of the Suzuki reaction partial hydrolysis of the sulfamide was observed. Thus, a multigram synthesis of this compound was achieved following an alternative synthetic route as described in Scheme 5. Bromo derivative 28 was reacted under Suzuki conditions with boronate 48 to give the free indolamine intermediate 49. The sulfamide was then formed by treatment of 49 with sulfamoyl chloride in the presence of pyridine in THF. Finally, deprotection of the SEM group furnished LAS195319 in an overall improved yield.
