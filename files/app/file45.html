    Abstract

    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative disorder that has a higher prevalence and incidence in people older than 60 years. The need for improved AD therapies is unmet as the current therapies are symptomatic with modest efficacy. Partial agonists of the 5-HT4 receptor (5-HT4R) offer both symptomatic and disease-modifying treatments as they shift amyloid-precursor-protein (APP) processing from the amyloidogenic pathway to the nonamyloidogenic pathway by activating the α-secretase enzyme. In addition, they also offer symptomatic treatment by increasing levels of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine in the brain. Because of this fascinating dual mechanism of action, several chemical scaffolds having 5-HT4R pharmacophores were designed and evaluated. Most of the synthesized compounds showed potent in vitro affinities and in vivo efficacies. Upon analysis of focused structure–activity relationships, compound 4o was identified as a potent 5-HT4R partial agonist with favorable ADME properties and good in vivo efficacy. GR-125487, a selective 5-HT4R antagonist, attenuated the activity of compound 4o in the novel-object-recognition-test cognition model.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a progressively debilitating neurodegenerative disorder mostly affecting the aging population. Although the underlying causative factors are yet to be identified, symptoms include confusion, memory loss, and dementia, ultimately leading to death. The current pharmacotherapy provides only symptomatic relief through the augmentation of cholinergic function and has undesired side effects, such as nausea, vomiting, and bradycardia. Moreover, no disease-modifying therapy is available to the patients to date. Thus, there is a need for new therapeutic targets for the treatment of this disorder. The 5-HT4 receptor has attracted a lot of attention. 5-HT4R belongs to a superfamily of seven transmembrane G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) and is coupled to a G protein containing a Gαs subunit.(1) 5-HT4R is reported to have a potential role in many central nervous system (CNS) disorders, such as AD,(2) and peripherally mediated disorders, such as irritable-bowel syndrome(2) and gastroparesis.(4) The 5-HT4 receptors are highly expressed in brain regions like the hippocampus, amygdala, and cerebral cortex, suggesting the involvement of the receptor in cognitive processes.(5) 5-HT4R agonists modulate amyloid-precursor-protein (APP)-derived peptides, amyloid β (Aβ), and soluble amyloid-precursor protein α (sAPPα).(6)
    sAPPα is nonamyloidogenic protein that is reported to have a potent neuroprotective role against the neurotoxic effects of glutamate and β-amyloid.(6) Human 5-HT4R isoforms are positively coupled to adenylyl cyclase production. Thus, the activation of this G-protein-coupled receptor activates adenylyl cyclase, consequently increasing the production of cAMP. The increased cAMP in turn increases sAPPα release. The stimulatory effect of 5-HT4R on sAPPα release is mimicked by forskolin, a direct activator of adenylyl cyclase, and 8-bromo-cAMP, a membrane-permeant cAMP analogue, suggesting the involvement of adenylyl cyclase and cAMP in modulating sAPPα by 5-HT4R.(7) 5-HT4R also increases neuronal acetylcholine (ACh) levels in the brain.(8) Thus, 5-HT4R partial agonists may be of use for both disease-modifying and symptomatic treatments of cognitive disorders. Owing to its dual mechanism of action in treating AD and other cognition-related diseases, 5-HT4R became an attractive target for drug discovery. Several structurally diverse heteroaromatic derivatives,(9−13) as 5-HT4R agonists or partial agonists, have been explored for both CNS and peripheral indications. The 5-HT4R partial agonist PRX-03140 showed significant improvement of ADAS-cog scores in a Phase 2a study for AD.(14,15) Apart from this compound, several other compounds were in development, such as BIMU-1,(16) RS-67333,(17) prucalopride,(3) and PF-04995274,(9) among others (Figure 1). In our continued efforts at discovering novel small molecules with diverse mechanism of action to treat AD,(18−20) we shifted our attention to 5-HT4R agonists partly because of their dual mechanism of action and partly because of the availability of the proof of concept in the form of the PRX-03140 Phase 2 positive results. Our internally discovered clinical candidate SUVN-D4010, a 5-HT4R partial agonist, has completed Phase 1 clinical trials in healthy subjects(21) and also Phase 2 clinical trials, enabling long-term safety studies. The structure of this compound has not been disclosed so far.
    Most of the reported 5-HT4R ligands that have been explored for both CNS and peripheral indications have either benzamide or imidazole carboxamide scaffolds. Previous work(19) from our group disclosed a series of 3-isopropylimidazo[1,5-a]pyridine carboxamide derivatives (5a, series 1) as 5-HT4R partial agonists that displayed cognition-enhancing properties in animal models. However, the brain penetration of these compounds was poor (Cbrain/Cplasma ≤ 0.3). As we are targeting the CNS receptor, low brain penetration in rats makes this scaffold unsuitable for further development. Our goal was to identify a potent 5-HT4R agonist with adequate brain penetration, acceptable ADME, and preclinical efficacy as a possible drug candidate for the treatment of AD. The series 1 compounds(19) were modified by keeping the basic pharmacophore and the isopropyl substituent unchanged (Figure 2) to obtain several diverse chemical scaffolds. The 5-HT4R pharmacophore consists of an aromatic moiety, a coplanar carbonyl group or its bioisostere, and a voluminous substituent in the basic amino framework of the molecule.(22,23)

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Successive modifications in the position of the nitrogen atom, the position of the isopropyl group, and the position of the carboxamide group in the heteroaromatic ring resulted in novel 1-isopropylimidazo[1,5-a]pyridine carboxamide derivatives (series 2), 2-isopropylimidazo[1,2-a]pyridine carboxamide derivatives (series 3), and 2-isopropylpyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine carboxamide derivatives (series 4). The replacement of the carboxamide group in each of the series with an amide bioisostere resulted in 1,3,4-oxadiazole derivatives. The results of these modifications, in terms of their in vitro binding affinity, pharmacokinetic, hERG-affinity, receptor-occupancy, and in vivo efficacy profiles, constitute the subject matter of this manuscript.

    Chemistry

    The synthesis of heteroaromatic carboxylic acid A1 commenced from commercially available 2-bromopyridine, 1, which was treated with n-BuLi followed by isobutyraldehyde to obtain compound 2. The hydroxyl group in compound 2 was converted into a leaving group by mesylation and then was displaced with sodium azide to obtain azido compound 3. The azide group in compound 3 was reduced with a triphenylphosphine/H2O combination (a Staudinger reaction),(24) which afforded amino compound 4. Amino compound 4 was treated with ethyl chlorooxoacetate to obtain intermediate 5, which upon cyclization with POCl3 in 1,2-dichloroethane afforded imidazo[1,5-a]pyridine ethyl ester, 6. Ester 6 was hydrolyzed with NaOH and upon acidification yielded 1-isopropyl-imidazo[1,5-a]pyridine-3-carboxylic acid, A1 (Scheme 1). The 2-isopropylimidazo[1,2-a]pyridine carboxylic acid, A2, was synthesized from commercially available ethyl 2-aminonicotinate, 7, which upon being refluxed with 1-chloro-3-methyl-butan-2-one in ethanol yielded imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine ester derivative 8, which after hydrolysis afforded the corresponding acid, A2 (Scheme 2). Synthesis of 2-isopropylpyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine-7-carboxylic acid, A3, started from a reaction of 2-picoline, 9, with isobutyryl chloride, which afforded intermediate 10. Intermediate 10 upon cyclization with O-mesitylenesulfonylhydroxylamine afforded 2-isopropylpyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine, 11, which after a reaction with n-BuLi followed by carboxylation with carbon dioxide (dry ice) and acidification yielded acid A3 (Scheme 3).
    Ethyl isonipecotate 12 was alkylated, either by reactions with alkyl halides or by reductive amination with aldehydes to obtain compounds 13a–13c. Upon ester hydrolysis with lithium hydroxide, compounds 13a–13c afforded lithium carboxylates 14a–14c, respectively (Scheme 4). Acid hydrazides 15, 16, and 18 were synthesized from the corresponding esters, 6, 8, and 17. Ester 17 was obtained from acid A3 upon treatment with dry HCl in methanol (Scheme 5).
    Acids A1–A3 were reacted with the earlier reported(19) N-Boc-substituted piperidine, B1, or the 3-aza-bicyclo[3.1.0]hexane amine, B2, in the presence of TBTU or HATU to obtain the Boc-protected carboxamide derivatives, 19a, 19b, 21a, 21b, 23a, and 23b, which upon Boc deprotection with dry HCl in isopropanol afforded compounds 20a, 20b, 22a, 22b, 24a, and 24b, respectively. Upon N-alkylation, either by reactions with alkyl halides or reductive amination with aldehydes, these compounds afforded 1-isopropylimidazo[1,5-a]pyridine carboxamides (2a–2g), 2-isopropylimidazo[1,2-a]pyridine carboxamides (3a–3q), and 2-isopropylpyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine-7-carboxamides (4a–4l), respectively (Scheme 6). The 1,3,4-oxadiazole derivatives 2h–2j, 3r, 3s, and 4m–4o were synthesized by reactions between hydrazides 15, 16, and 18 and substituted lithium carboxylates 14a–14c in the presence of POCl3 (Scheme 7).
    Isopropylimidazo[1,5-a]pyridine carboxamide derivatives 2a–2e, with different substitutions on the piperidine (B1) nitrogen, showed in vitro potency (EC50 < 100 nM) toward 5-HT4R with partial-agonist activity (Emax < 100%, Table 1). Compounds with bulkier substitutions, such as methylpiperidine (2c) and methylpyran (2d), on the piperidine nitrogen showed increased potency (EC50 < 10 nM) as compared with simple substitutions bearing compounds such as isopropyl (2a) and methoxypropyl (2b). Similarly, compounds with N-substituted 3-aza-bicyclo[3.1.0]hexane rings (B2) showed increased potency toward 5-HT4R as compared with the simple piperidine (B1) counterparts. Compounds with a methoxypropyl substitution (2f) and a pyran substitution (2g) on the nitrogen of 3-aza-bicyclo[3.1.0]hexane showed increased potency as compared with the piperidine counterparts (2b and 2e, respectively).

    The development and application of bioisosteres have been adopted as a fundamental tactical approach useful for addressing a number of aspects, such as improving potency, enhancing selectivity, altering physical properties, reducing or redirecting metabolism, eliminating or modifying toxicophores, and acquiring novel intellectual property associated with drug candidates.(25) The 1,3,4-oxadiazoles derivatives,(26)2h–2j, as carboxamide bioisosteres, were synthesized and evaluated. However, these compounds showed weak in vitro potency toward 5-HT4R. In brief, the carboxamide derivatives showed moderate in vitro potency, and the 1,3,4-oxadiazole derivatives showed weak potency toward 5-HT4R.(27) Compounds from this series were evaluated for their metabolic stability in rat and human microsomes. These compounds showed moderate to good microsomal metabolic stability. The piperidine analogues with methoxypropyl (2b) and tetrahydropyran (2e) groups and the 3-aza-bicyclo[3.1.0]hexane analogues with methoxypropyl (2f) and pyran (2g) groups showed higher metabolic stability. Compounds 2b and 2g, which showed good metabolic stability, were evaluated in rat pharmacokinetic (PK) studies. Both of the compounds showed poor oral bioavailability, suggesting a lack of correlation between microsomal stability and oral exposure. The brain penetration (Cb/Cp), however, had substantially improved (1.8 to 2) as compared with that of the earlier-reported series 1 compound 5a (0.31).(19) The 1,3,4-oxadiazole derivatives were not evaluated in PK experiments owing to their poor in vitro potency.
    Given the improvement in brain penetration in the series 2 compounds, the scope of the SAR was further expanded by synthesizing and evaluating a series of isopropylimidazo[1,2-a]pyridine derivatives (series 3, Table 2). Most of the piperidine ring (B1) compounds (3a–3h) showed potent in vitro activity toward 5-HT4R (EC50 < 10 nM). Compounds with N-substituted 3-aza-bicyclo[3.1.0]hexane rings (3i–3m) however were 2–10-fold less potent (compounds 3a vs 3i and 3b vs 3j). During the course of this research, a publication(28) from Pfizer reported the formation of a cyclized oxazolidine metabolite of PF-04995274, which was reported as unique to humans and was not observed in animal studies. Given the partial structural similarity to PF-04995274 (i.e., the tert-hydroxyl groups on the piperidine N-substituents of compounds 3f, 3g, 3h), these compounds may theoretically have the potential to form such unique metabolites. Though these compounds showed potent in vitro activity at 5-HT4R, no additional explorations were carried out. The rat pharmacokinetic studies of compounds 3b and 3c showed high clearance, poor exposure, and poor bioavailability, observations akin to those of the series 2 compounds. On the basis of its high in vitro potency and high brain penetration, compound 3c was evaluated in the long-term memory deficit assay the novel-object-recognition test (NORT) to see if the in vitro activity translated into in vivo efficacy. The NORT(29,30) has been regarded as a spontaneous delayed-nonmatching-to-sample (DNMS) test. The test is based on a spontaneous behavior. The main assumption at the base of this test is that access to novelty (object or environment) can elicit approach behaviors in animals. NORT does not involve reference-memory components; thus, it can be considered a “pure” recognition-memory test and a valid task to assess working memory. Also, the test does not involve positive or negative reinforcement (food or electric shocks), and this makes NORT comparable to the memory tests currently used with humans. These advantages make NORT quick and easily implemented, and therefore it has been widely used for assessing mild cognitive impairment in preclinical research. This apparent “unconditioned preference” for novelty has been used in the NORT in order to study memory functions by assessing the ability of an animal to recognize a novel object in a familiar environment. Although this model has face and predictive validity for memory deficits, the percentage of translational success from rats to humans is poor to date. In this experiment, the rats treated with 3c at 0.3 and 1 mg/kg, po, spent significantly more time exploring the novel object than the familiar object in the test phase; that is, the rats remembered the familiar object even after a 24 h time gap. However, the vehicle-treated rats spent almost equal time exploring the novel object as well as the familiar object, forgetting the familiar object after the 24 h time gap (Figure 3). Encouraged by these results, further modification of the chemical scaffold was done with the aim of obtaining compounds with good oral bioavailability. Chloro substitution was brought at the 3-position of imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine to obtain compounds 3n and 3o, and the carbon atom (C-3) was replaced with a nitrogen atom to obtain triazolo compounds 3p and 3q. However, these modifications resulted in very weak potency (3q) or a complete loss of potency (3n–3p). However, the microsomal metabolic stability of these compounds had increased substantially. We hypothesized that this improvement in the microsomal metabolic stability was probably due to blocking of the high-electron-density 3-position of imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine. However, the 1,3,4-oxadiazole derivatives, 3r and 3s, showed weak affinity toward 5-HT4R with favorable metabolic stability.

    As attempts to improve the metabolic stability by structural modifications resulted in less potent or inactive compounds, further SAR studies were carried out through the synthesis of pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine derivatives (series 4, Table 3). As it was evident from the results shown in Table 3, the compounds that had piperidines (B1) with N-substitutions of methoxypropyl (4a) or pyranylmethyl (4b) groups were more potent than the compound with an isopropyl group (4c). The corresponding N-substituted 3-aza-bicyclo[3.1.0]hexane derivatives (4d–4f) were less potent, similar to the series 2 and series 3 compounds. Most of the evaluated compounds were extensively metabolized in both rat and human liver microsomes. On the basis of the availability of resources, compounds 4b and 4c have been explored in pharmacokinetic (PK) studies. Both of the compounds showed poor oral bioavailability, and 4c showed poor brain penetration as well. In an attempt to increase the microsomal metabolic stability, 3-chloro-pyrazolopyridine derivatives (4g–4i) and triazolopyridine derivatives (4j–4l) were synthesized. However, these compounds did not show any activity toward 5-HT4R. In line with earlier observations, the metabolic stability of 3-chloro-pyrazolopyridine derivatives 4g–4i was moderately improved, whereas the metabolic stability of triazolopyridine derivatives 4k–4l was increased substantially. The 1,3,4-oxadiazole derivatives (4m–4o), however, showed potent in vitro activity and also showed improved metabolic stability. Compounds 4m, 4n, and 4o showed good oral bioavailability; compounds 4n and 4o showed improved brain penetration. However, the N-isopropyl-substituted derivative, 4m, showed poor brain penetration (Table 3). Compounds 4n and 4o were evaluated in efficacy models of cognition. In NORT, compound 4o showed robust efficacy at 3 and 10 mg, po, whereas compound 4n showed efficacy at 1 and 10 mg, po (Figure 3). The reference compound, RS-67333, a 5-HT4R partial agonist,(17) showed efficacy at 0.3 and 1.0 mg/kg, ip. In a separate experiment, the observed activity of 4o in NORT was blocked by the administration of the selective 5-HT4R antagonist GR-125487,(31,32) giving ample evidence that the efficacy observed may be arising from its agonistic activity at 5-HT4R (Figure 4).

    In order to further substantiate the finding that the observed efficacy was arising from 5-HT4R agonism, compounds 4n and 4o were evaluated for their receptor occupancy (RO).(33) Both of the compounds showed significant RO, with absolute ED50 values of 8.47 and 6.15 mg/kg, po, respectively (Figure 5), suggesting that the in vivo efficacy observed for these compounds may be mediated through 5-HT4R. The partial agonist from Pfizer, PF-04995274, showed a dose-dependent RO with an ED50 value of 0.017 mg/kg, sc, which is comparable to the reported value (0.008 mg/kg, sc).(34)
    The protein-binding experiment was done in rat, dog, and human liver microsomes to ascertain the free fraction available for 4n and 4o. Compound 4n showed free fractions of 26% in rat plasma and 14% in brain homogenate, whereas compound 4o showed free fractions of 17% in rat plasma and 24% in brain homogenate. The percent free fractions for both compounds in plasma were similar in other species, like dog and human. Both 4n and 4o showed minimal inhibition of cytochrome P450 3A4 and 2D6 enzymes and had absolute IC50 values of >10 μM. On the basis of its favorable in vitro affinity, higher oral exposures, dose-dependent RO, robust efficacy in NORT, and significant free fraction in the brain, compound 4o was selected for further evaluation in microdialysis studies. It is known in the literature that 5-HT4R agonists improve memory formation by enhancing the synaptic release of acetylcholine (the neurotransmitter responsible for cognition processes) in the brain, which in turn enhances the cholinergic transmission.(8) The microdialysis experiments showed that compound 4o increased acetylcholine levels dose-dependently in the frontal cortex upon oral dosing at 3 and 10 mg/kg (Figure 6). The 5-HT4R partial agonist PRX-03140 was used as a positive control. At 1 mg/kg, ip, PRX-03140 produced an about 254% increase from the predose levels. The increase in acetylcholine levels by PRX-03140 was in agreement with the literature reports.(35)
    5-HT4R agonists have the ability to shift the equilibrium of amyloid-precursor-protein (APP) processing from the amyloidogenic form to the nonamyloidogenic form, thereby showing a disease-modifying potential.(36) The APP processing shifts the equilibrium from the amyloidogenic form to the nonamyloidogenic form by generating sAPPα, which is reported to provide neuroprotective effects.(7,37,38) In this experiment, the 5-HT4R agonist prucalopride was used as a positive control. Treatment with prucalopride (10 mg/kg, sc) produced a 65% increase above the control levels. These results are in agreement with the literature.(37) The increase in sAPPα levels produced by compound 4o (1–10 mg/kg, sc) was higher than the effects produced by prucalopride (10 mg/kg, sc). The increase in cortical sAPPα levels in mice demonstrated that compound 4o may have a disease-modifying potential (Figure 7).
    Compounds 4n and 4o showed comparable potencies toward 5-HT4R isoforms (5-HT4D, 5-HT4E, and 5-HT4L) with EC50 values ranging from 6 to 62 nM.     In summary, starting from the poorly brain-penetrant 5a, effecting successive modifications in the position of the nitrogen atom, the position of the isopropyl group, and the position of the carboxamide group in the heteroaromatic ring resulted in the identification of a series of pyrazolo[1,5-a]pyridine derivatives represented by compound 4o. Lead compound 4o is a potent and selective 5-HT4R partial agonist with high oral exposures and good brain penetration. In vivo RO studies demonstrated a clear relationship among the in vitro affinity, free brain exposure, in vivo efficacy, and RO . We have also demonstrated in this report that the compounds 3c, 4n, and 4o improved long-term memory deficits in NORT, a finding that is consistent with previous reports. The attenuation of the efficacy of compound 4o in NORT by the selective 5-HT4R antagonist GR-125487 and the dose-dependent 5-HT4 RO provides ample evidence that the cognitive effects of compound 4o were mediated through 5-HT4R. Compound 4o increased neurotransmitter ACh release in the prefrontal cortex, a finding that is consistent with previous reports. Compound 4o increased neuroprotective sAPPα levels, thus offering a disease-modifying therapy. A safety assessment of this compound is in progress to ascertain the therapeutic margins between efficacious concentrations and safety findings.

    Determination of EC50 Values for 5-HT4R

    The compounds were screened in a cell-based reporter-gene assay and an end-point luminescence assay that measures the levels of cAMP inside cells upon the activation or inhibition of the receptor using the luciferase reporter. Serotonin (5-HT) and luciferin was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. T4 DNA Ligase and high-fidelity Taq polymerase were procured from Roche. Superscript Reverse Transcriptase and mammalian expression vector pcDNA3.1 were purchased from Invitrogen. The CRE-Luc reporter-gene construct was supplied by Stratagene. Cell-culture media and sera were procured from Invitrogen. All other DNA-restriction and -modification enzymes were from New England Biolabs. All other reagents and common chemicals were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. The human 5-HT4R cDNA clone was purchased from Origene. The coding sequence was amplified by PCR using gene-specific primers. The amplified DNA was cloned into the mammalian expression vector pcDNA 3.1. The authenticity of the cloned genes was determined by restriction analysis and nucleotide sequencing. The CHO cell line was purchased from the American Type Culture Collection. The cells were routinely cultured in Hams F12 medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum at 37 °C in 5% CO2. The cells were transfected with the 5-HT4 isoforms along with 3-fold excess of the CRE-Luc reporter gene using Lipofectamine, as recommended by the supplier. The cells were further cultured in the same medium supplemented with 800 μg/mL G418. Individual colonies were picked after 2 weeks of selection and screened for serotonin-induced luciferase activity. Colonies exhibiting maximum serotonin-induced luciferase activity were selected and amplified for further investigation. The recombinant CHO cells were plated in 96 well clear-bottom white plates (Corning) and cultured overnight as described above. Cells were grown overnight in serum-free medium before the incubation with the compounds to prevent receptor desensitization due to the presence of endogenous ligands in the serum.
    The reference endogenous agonist serotonin and test compounds, in Opti-MEM medium at 11 different test concentrations from 10 μM to 0.1 nM in 3-fold serial dilutions, were separately incubated with the cells in individual wells for 4 h at 37 °C in 5% CO2. Compounds whose potencies were less than 1 nM were tested in a dose–response study whose test concentrations were from 1 μM to 0.01 nM. After the incubation, the medium was removed, and the cells were washed with phosphate-buffered saline and lysed in lysis buffer (10 mM Tris, pH 8.0; 50 mM NaCl; 1 mM DTT; 1× protease-inhibitor cocktail; and 0.1% NP40). The luciferase activity was measured in the individual wells using a luciferin substrate in a Victor Light Luminometer (PerkinElmer).
    The maximum response produced by each drug was normalized to the 5-HT-induced maximum response. The data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism software to derive the EC50 values that corresponded to the concentrations of agonists required to obtain half-maximal stimulation of adenylyl cyclase. Each values was expressed as the geometric mean of two independent experiments performed in triplicate. Reference compound PF-04995274, a reported partial agonist,(9) showed partial-agonist activity in our 5-HT4E cell-based reporter-gene assay with an EC50 value of 1.3 nM and an Emax of 18%, which is well in agreement with the published data (EC50 = 0.26 nM, Emax = 7%). The EC50 values of some other reported compounds were also determined using our assay and compared with the published data. The EC50 values obtained versus the reported values are tabulated in the Supporting Information of this manuscript.

    Microsomal Metabolic Stability

    The compounds were incubated with microsomes at 0.5 mg/mL protein in 100 mM phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) at 37 °C. The reactions were initiated by the addition of NADPH. All incubations were carried out for 30 min. At 30 min, an aliquot of the sample was removed and transferred into 300 μL of acetonitrile. The samples were stored at −20 °C until analysis. The samples were analyzed using LC-MS/MS. The results were expressed as the percent metabolized at 30 min postincubation.

    CYP 3A4 and 2D6 Inhibition

    The CYP 3A4 and 2D6 inhibitory potentials of the test compounds were studied using human liver microsomes. Inhibitory activity was evaluated by incubating the test compound (0.1–45 μM) in duplicate for 2 (3A4) and 12 min (2D6) with human liver microsomes in the presence of an isoform-specific substrate (midazolam for 3A4 or dextromethorphan for 2D6) and an NADPH-regeneration system. Incubations were terminated by the addition of ice-cold acetonitrile containing an internal standard, and the metabolites were quantified using LC-MS/MS. Peak-area ratios of the analytes versus the internal standards were used for calculating IC50 values.

    Pharmacokinetic Study in Rats

    Male Wistar rats (225 ± 25 g) were used as experimental animals. Three animals were housed in each cage. Two days prior to the dosing day, the male Wistar rats were anesthetized with isoflurane for the surgical placement of jugular-vein catheters. Animals were fasted overnight before oral dosing (po), and food pellets were allowed 2 h postdosing, whereas during intravenous dosing, food and water were provided ad libitum. Three rats were dosed with the compounds orally (3 mg/kg) and intravenously (1 mg/kg). Dose formulation was prepared by using water as a vehicle (10 mL/kg for oral dosing and 2 mL/kg for intravenous dosing). At each time point, blood was collected through the jugular vein and immediately replenished with an equivalent volume of normal saline from freely moving rats. The collected blood was transferred to a labeled Eppendorf tube containing 10 μL of heparin as an anticoagulant. The time points for the blood-sample collection were predose and 0.08 (iv only), 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 24 h postdose (n = 3). The collected blood was centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 10 min. The plasma was prepared and stored at −70 °C until analysis. The test-compound concentrations were extracted from the plasma samples by protein precipitation using four volumes of acetonitrile (v/v) containing a predetermined internal standard. The precipitates were centrifuged at 11 292g and 10 °C for 10 min, and the supernatants were transferred and injected in an LC-MS/MS. The analytes of interest were quantified using the suitable multiple-reaction-monitoring mode in the range of 2–2000 ng/mL or ng/g against a set of calibration standards and quality-control samples. The study samples were analyzed using the calibration samples within each batch and the quality-control samples across the batches. The pharmacokinetic parameters were determined by standard noncompartmental analysis (Phoenix WinNonLin software, version 6.2 or higher) using a linear trapezoidal with linear interpolation method.

    Rodent-Brain-Penetration Study

    Male Wistar rats (225 ± 25 g) were used as experimental animals. Three animals were housed in each cage. The animals were given water and food ad libitum throughout the experiment and maintained on a 12 h light–dark cycle. Brain penetration was determined in a discrete manner in the rats. The compounds were formulated in water and administered orally at 10 mg/kg (free-base equivalent). Blood samples were removed via cardiac punctures after isoflurane anesthesia at 1 h postdose. The animals were sacrificed to collect the brain tissue. The plasma was separated, and the brain samples were homogenized and stored at −70 °C until analysis. The concentrations of the compounds in the plasma and brain were determined using an LC-MS/MS method. The test-compound concentrations were extracted from the brain homogenates by protein precipitation using four volumes of acetonitrile (v/v) containing a predetermined internal standard. The precipitates were centrifuged at 11 292g and 10 °C for 10 min, and the supernatants were transferred and injected in an LC-MS/MS. The compounds were quantified in the calibration range of 1–2000 ng/mL in the plasma and brain homogenates. The study samples were analyzed using calibration samples within each batch and quality-control samples across the batches. Extent of the brain-to-plasma ratio was calculated (Cb/Cp).

    Novel-Object-Recognition Task

    Male Wistar rats 10–12 weeks of age were used. The arena was an open field made of acrylic (50 × 50 × 50 cm). Twenty-four hours prior to testing, the rats were habituated to individual test arenas for 20 min in the absence of any objects. Twenty-four hours after the habituation, the rats (7–8 animals per group) were administered their respective treatments. After a post-treatment interval of 30 min, the rats were subjected to a familiarization phase (T1). The rats were placed individually in the open field containing two identical objects (a1 and a2) for 3 min. The choice trial (T2) was carried out 24 h after the T1 trial. The rats were administered their respective treatments. After a post-treatment interval of 30 min, the rats were subjected to the choice trial (T2). The rats were allowed to explore the open field for 3 min in the presence of one familiar object (a3) and one novel object (b). Exploration times were noted.

    Novel-Object-Recognition Task with the 5-HT4R Selective Antagonist, GR-125487

    On day 1, the Wistar rats (n = 7–8 per group) were habituated to the arena for 20 min. On day 2 or 3, 30 min before trial 1, the animals were administered the vehicle (water for injection, WFI) or compound 4o (3 mg/kg, po). On day 2 or 3, GR-125487 (1 mg/kg, sc) was administered 40 min before the trial. Trial 1 was a familiarization task in the arena, with 2 yellow bottles of same dimensions for a period of 3 min. Trial 2 was a recognition task in the arena, with one yellow and one black bottle for a period of 3 min. The vehicle-treated rats spent almost equal time exploring the novel object and the familiar object in the test (i.e., they did not remember the familiar object). The rats treated with compound 4o at 3 mg/kg, po, spent significantly more time exploring the novel object than the familiar object in the test phase. The rats treated with GR-125487 at 1 mg/kg, sc, spent almost equal time exploring the novel object and the familiar object in the test phase. Groups treated with the 4o (3 mg/kg, po) and GR 125487 (1 mg/kg, sc) combination spent almost equal time exploring the novel object and the familiar object in the test phase. GR-125487 antagonized or blocked the action of compound 4o.

    Determination of In Vivo Receptor Occupancy (RO)

    Male Wistar rats (n = 4 per group) were administered the vehicle or test compounds (0.3, 1, 3, 10, and 30 mg/kg, po). After 1 h of treatment with the test compounds or the vehicle, the rats were restrained and administered a tracer (SB207145, 1 μg/kg) intravenously. Thirty minutes after the tracer administration, the rats were sacrificed, and each brain was separated for the isolation of the cerebellum (nonspecific region) and striatum (specific region). The isolated brain tissues were transferred to prelabeled vials and stored on dry ice until the quantification of the tracer (SB207145) using LC-MS/MS. Percent receptor occupancy was calculated using the concentration of the tracer in the striatum compared with the concentration in the cerebellum (receptor null region).

    In Vivo Brain Microdialysis

    Animals were placed in a stereotaxic frame with an incisor bar set at 3.2 mm under the horizontal plane, passing through the interaural line. A microdialysis guide cannula (CMA/11, CMA Microdialysis) was implanted into the frontal cortex (AP = +3.2 mm, ML = −3.2 mm, DV = −1.5 mm). The coordinates were taken according to Paxinos and Watson(41) with reference points taken from the bregma and vertical from the skull. On the day of the experiment, a pre-equilibrated microdialysis probe (CMA/11, 2 mm, CMA Microdialysis) was inserted into the guide cannula, directing the dialysis membrane into the frontal cortex. The probe was perfused with aCSF at 1.5 μL/min set by a microinfusion pump (Picoplus). After a stabilization period of 2 h, sample collection was initiated with a sampling interval of 20 min; five basal samples were collected prior to the administration of compound 4o (3 or 10 mg/kg, po) and dialysate samples were collected for an additional period of 4 h. Samples were stored at −70 °C until the quantification of acetylcholine by LC-MS/MS.(42)

    Estimation of Mouse-Brain Cortical sAPPα Levels

    Male C57BL/6J mice (20–30 g) were randomly divided (n = 7 per group) into different treatment groups. In the control group, the mice were subcutaneously (sc) administered sterile water by injection. The mice from treatment groups each received a single sc injection of a test compound (dose volume of 10 mL/kg) or prucalopride (10 mg/kg) dissolved in sterile water. The mice were sacrificed by cervical dislocation at 60 or 90 min after the administration of the test compound or prucalopride, respectively. The brains were quickly isolated, and the cortexes were dissected at −20 °C. Each cortex was immediately kept on a dry ice and weighed before being stored at −80 °C until the quantification of sAPPα using an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) via commercially available kits from IBL (currently known as Tecan).(43−45) Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism (version 4). The data are means ± SD of sAPPα levels expressed as percentages of the control values (from the mice that received water by injection). Values were compared between the different groups by an unpaired test. The significance level was set at *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, and ***p < 0.001.
