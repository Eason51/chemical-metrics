    Abstract

    Alzheimer’s disease is a grave social problem in an aging population. A major problem is the passage of drugs through the blood–brain barrier. This work tests the hypothesis that the conjugation of peptidomimetic β-secretase inhibitors with a fragment of amyloid-β peptide facilitates entrance into the central nervous system. HVR-3 (compound 4), one of the conjugation products, was found to be as potent as OM00-3, a known peptidomimetic inhibitor, 4-fold more selective toward β-secretase 1 in relation to β-secretase 2 and 3-fold more resistant to in vitro metabolization in human serum. Its intravenous administration to mice and Wistar rats generated an active metabolite recovered from the rodent’s brains.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Alzheimer’s disease (AD), a progressive neurodegenerative disease of the central nervous system (CNS), is generally characterized by a slow and inflexible progression of dementia associated with cognitive and memory decline, speech loss, and personality changes. Its hallmark pathological lesions occur in the brain memory and cognition regions, consisting of neurofibrillary tangles and amyloid plaques.(1) These plaques are composed of amyloid-β peptide (Aβ), a highly insoluble peptide with tendency to oligomerize and aggregate, which is produced from the sequential cleavage of the amyloid-β precursor protein (APP) by β-secretase 1 (BACE-1) and γ-secretase.(2) In addition, neurofibrillary tangles, which are formed by the accumulation of abnormal filaments of tau protein, have been reported to be induced by Aβ, through a mechanism of hyperphosphorylation, an initiating step for tangles occurrence.(3) Considering this, agents capable of reducing the production of Aβ, such as BACE-1 inhibitors, may block or reduce the occurrence of amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles and consequently prevent AD progression.(4)
    The first reported BACE-1 inhibitors were peptidomimetics composed of a transition-state analogue at the scissile peptide bond.(5-7) The Tang and Ghosh group developed compound 1 (OM00-3) (Figure 1),(6) one of the yet most potent BACE-1 inhibitors reported and only composed of natural amino acids plus a synthetic hydroxyethylene leucine–alanine dipeptide transition-state isostere. Compound 1 is an optimized compound for the inhibition of BACE-1.(8) Despite the high potency shown in vitro toward BACE-1, this kind of inhibitor has however been shown to be ineffective in the treatment of AD because of the lack of the required pharmacokinetic properties, particularly the ability to cross the blood–brain barrier (BBB). Large size and high hydrophilicity were considered the two main issues preventing passive BBB crossing, and therefore, attention was pointed toward the development of nonpeptidic inhibitors,(9, 10) which being smaller and more hydrophobic have better conditions to permeate the BBB. Unfortunately, their therapeutic potential in the CNS has been limited by P-glycoprotein efflux. Also, smaller size contributes to nonspecificity and therefore to increased toxicity, contrary to peptides that are generally considered to be safe. Currently there is no effective treatment capable of modifying the progression of AD and available drugs only act on symptomatic improvement. The development of drugs capable of blocking or delaying the disease progression remains a challenge due to the inability of BACE-1 inhibitors to cross the BBB.(10)
    In this work we report a new approach to overcoming the low permeability of peptidomimetic BACE-1 inhibitors across the BBB. This approach is based on the conjugation of a carrier peptide with a peptidmometic BACE-1 inhibitor in order to facilitate its transcytosis across the BBB.(11) Compound 1 will be used as a starting compound of the rational design, since it was reported to be a high potent peptidomimetic inhibitor(8) but simultaneously incapable of penetrating cells and crossing the plasma membrane,(12) being widely accepted to be too large to transverse the BBB.(12) The transcytosis route has the advantage of not limiting the compounds size for BBB permeation(12) and has been targeted for example through conjugation of peptidomimetic inhibitors with a fragment of Tat protein from HIV-1,(12) as well as with the Antennapedia peptide, penetratin.(13)
    The innovation reported in the present work is the use of Aβ 18–23′ as a carrier peptide for BACE-1 inhibitors through the BBB. The potential usefulness of this strategy is based on the property of Aβ to recognize the receptor for advanced glycation endproducts (RAGE), enabling entrance into the CNS across the BBB through receptor mediated-transcytosis.(14, 15) Aβ 18–23′ is a hexapeptide sequence corresponding to the binding region of Aβ to RAGE.(16) Factors that may contribute to the success of using this carrier peptide in AD include the following: (a) RAGE is overexpressed in AD in both the BBB and neurons,(17) potentially enabling a higher BBB crossing efficiency and internalization within the endossomes of neurons, where the drug will act through RAGE-mediated endocytosis as reported for Aβ;(18) (b) Aβ 18–23′ acts as a competitive inhibitor of the entry of Aβ peptide into cells expressing RAGE,(16) which potentially helps to avoid re-entry of Aβ into the CNS, as supported by the vascular theory for the development of AD;(19) (c) RAGE has advantages in comparison with more ubiquitous receptors such as transferrin receptor (TfR) or lipoprotein receptor-related protein (LRP) in which competition with natural ligands may contribute to adverse effects.(20) In the present study we specifically tested the hypothesis of conjugating compound 1(6) with Aβ 18–23′ (16) in order to target the CNS, thus leading to a potential drug for AD treatment.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The rational design of peptidomimetic BACE-1 inhibitors was based on the conjugation of 1 with Aβ 18–23′ and on the results obtained from several tests including BACE-1 inhibition, selectivity in relation to BACE-2, metabolization, and toxicological profile and also a pharmacokinetic study in order to evaluate distribution into the brain.

    Influence of Conjugation with Aβ 18–23′ on BACE-1 Inhibition Potency

    The strategy used to design compound 2 (HVR-1) (Table 1) is outlined in Figure 1 and involved truncation of the three residue sequence at the C-terminal of 1 before conjugation with Aβ 18–23′, in order to obtain the shortest sequence possible. This involved minimal disturbance to the original structure, since the three-residue sequence at the N-terminal of Aβ 18–23′ only differs by one amino acid from the truncated sequence. This lead to a peptide of 11 residues, while the mere addition of Aβ 18–23′ to 1 would give a sequence of 14 residues. Therefore, the glutamic acid in position P3′ of 1 becomes a phenylalanine in position 7 of 2. This change should not affect binding to BACE-1 since the preference indexes of both residues in BACE-1 substrates are identical,(8) but in order to evaluate to what extent the conjugation of 1 with Aβ 18–23′ affects the inhibition of BACE-1, compound 2 was tested for BACE-1 inhibition.
    Table 1. Molecular Structure and Inhibition Potency of Peptidomimetic BACE-1 Inhibitors (Mean Value ± Standard Deviation, n = 3)
    As illustrated in Figure 2, conjugation affects the potency of the inhibitor to only a very small extent (Ki(compound 2) = 2.0 ± 0.1 nM vs Ki(compound 1) = 1.2 ± 0.4 nM, mean value ± standard deviation, n = 3, p ≤ 0.05, independent two-tailed Student’s t-test). The value reported here for the Ki of 1 is 4-fold higher than the previously reported value of 0.31 nM.(8) This is due to the fact that a permanently active form of BACE-1 was used before, while in the present study a pro-BACE-1 was employed possessing a prodomain in equilibrium with an opened and closed form, leading to a reduction of the enzyme activity.(21) For a similar inhibitor, OM99-2, a 7-fold potency decrease was observed when pro-BACE-1 (Ki = 9.8 nM) was used instead of active BACE-1 (Ki = 1.4 nM).(21, 22) Moreover, the potency decrease observed for 2 (about 2-fold) in comparison with 1 is less than that reported for the conjugation of OM99-2 with a carrier peptide of the Tat protein of HIV-1 or when 1 was conjugated with a nonapeptide of arginines, where the inhibition potency over the active form of BACE-1 decreased 28-fold (39 nM) and 6-fold (1.7 nM), respectively, when compared to the reported value of 0.31 nM.(8)

    Effect of the Exchange of a Phenylalanine by a Tyrosine at Position 8

    BACE-2, the closest homologous enzyme to BACE-1, has received great attention by researchers because of the need to obtain a BACE-1 selective drug to avoid possible secondary effects such as diabetes.(23) The disease may arise from BACE-2 inhibition during the production of insulin by pancreatic β-cells.(24) In addition, BACE-2 contributes to the degradation of Aβ,(25) meaning that its inhibition may lead to the progression of AD.
    The results reported for the preference of amino acids at the eight subsites of BACE-1 and BACE-2 substrates reveal that the preference index for phenylalanine at position P4′ (Figure 1) in the case of BACE-2 is very high when compared to the null preference index for tyrosine,(23) while it is similar to the one for tyrosine in the case of BACE-1.(8) This means that an exchange of a phenylalanine by a tyrosine in 2 should have little effect on the inhibition of BACE-1 while significantly reducing affinity for BACE-2. Also, this change should have minimal effect on RAGE binding since tyrosine, like phenylalanine, is a hydrophobic aromatic amino acid, which is a requisite for RAGE binding. Gospodarska et al. reported that the Aβ 18–23′ sequence involving a hydrophobic stretch, flanked by two negatively charged residues at the C-terminal, is crucial for RAGE recognition.(16) On the basis of these facts, we hypothesized that the exchange of the phenylalanine for a tyrosine at position P4′, corresponding to position 8 of 2, affording compound 3 (HVR-2) (Table 1), should increase the selectivity for BACE-1 in relation to BACE-2.
    As outlined in Figure 3, compound 3 shows a ratio between KiappBACE-2 and Ki BACE-1 of 7.5, which is higher than that of 2 (3.7) and of 1 (2.5). The design of 3 led therefore to an inhibitor 2-fold more selective than 2, which in its turn was already more selective than 1, indicating that conjugation with the carrier peptide also contributes to an increase of the selectivity for BACE-1 in relation to BACE-2.

    Influence of N-Terminal Acylation on the Metabolization Profile

    N-Acylation and C-amidation are regularly used in nature to obtain end-protected neuropeptides with improved stability and activity.(26) Small l-amino acid peptides with free N- and C-terminals suffer fast metabolization by exopeptidases, sometimes within a few minutes as in the case of somatostatin, but its half-life was improved from 3 to 400 min after N-terminal acylation.(27) Also, N-terminal acylation may improve the cell penetration and passage though barriers such as the BBB because of increased lipophilicity.(26) Considering this, in order to increase the inhibitor resistance against metabolization, 3 was acetylated at the N-terminal, rendering compound 4 (HVR-3) (Table 1).
    A comparative metabolic stability study of 1, 3, and 4 was carried out through in vitro incubation in human serum and mice brain homogenate. Half-lives were determined from the exponential metabolization profiles shown in Figure 4. N-acylation contributed significantly to stability as the half-lives of 4 increased 3-fold in serum and 2-fold in brain homogenate, compared to those of 3. The carrier peptide Aβ 18–23′ caused however some detrimental effect in the stability of 1 in brain homogenate since 1 is 2-fold more stable than 3. The metabolization half-lives of 4 in serum (6.0 h) and brain homogenate (3.4 h) indicate potential for its use as a drug. Some previously reported peptides tested for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease only lasted a few minutes in mice brain homogenate even after N-terminal protection.(28)
    The potency of 4 over BACE-1 is not statistically different from that of 1, having a Ki of 2.0 ± 0.6 (mean value ± standard deviation, n = 3, p > 0.05, independent two-tailed Student’s t-test). Finally, among the studied compounds, 4 was also the most selective for BACE-1 in relation to BACE-2, showing a ratio of 9.2 between KiappBACE-2 and Ki BACE-1 (Figure 3), being 4-fold more selective than 1.

    Evaluation of the Cytotoxic Potential in Caco-2 Cells

    Caco-2 cells were used as a preliminary assay to study compounds toxicity.(29) The cytotoxicity assay evaluated the dependence of Caco-2 cell viability with the inhibitor concentration, of compounds 1, 2, 3, and 4 during incubation periods of 4 and 24 h. Cellular viability was maintained above 80% within a range of 25 nM to 50 μM for all inhibitors, even after 24 h, demonstrating that the compounds are not toxic up to a concentration magnitude that exceeds their inhibition constant of more than 25 000-fold.

    In Vivo Pharmacokinetic Study for Evaluation of Compound 4 Delivery into Brain

    An in vivo pharmacokinetic study was performed in order to evaluate the capacity of the best inhibitor in vitro, compound 4, to reach the brain in mice. Both serum and brain samples were analyzed after intravenous administration of 4. In serum samples, compound 4 was not detected by HPLC-FLU half an hour after intravenous administration. This indicates that 4 is rapidly distributed, metabolized, or eliminated in mice. Accordingly, compound 4 with a peak of 1326.5 amu and a retention time of 23.6 min could not be detected in brain samples; however, a metabolite with a peak of 1285.4 amu (Figure 5) and a very close retention time of 22.9 min was detected in brain samples by HPLC-FLU and LC–MS even 2 h after administration. The fact that this metabolite does not appear in serum samples dismisses any potential contamination of brain samples by the blood.
    In order to identify the metabolite and according to the mass found, it was hypothesized that it could be compound 5 (HVR-4) (Table 1), as the molecular ion found (1285.4 amu) is consistent with the calculated exact mass for 5 [M + H]+ (1285.6 amu). The MS2 fragmentation of the molecular ion, 1285.4 amu, afforded the fragmentation spectrum shown in Figure 5, and the main m/z peaks were assigned based on the expected ions given by mMass software for compound 5:(30) M – H2O (expected = 1267.6, found = 1267.4), M – 2H2O (expected = 1249.6, found = 1249.4), b10 (expected = 1152.5, found = 1152.5), z10 – H2O (expected = 1121.5, found = 1121.7), b9 (expected = 1023.5, found = 1023.3), b8 (expected = 952.4, found = 952.4), a8 (expected = 924.5, found = 924.4), b7 (expected = 789.3, found = 789.4), a7 – H2O (expected = 743.4, found = 743.2), b6 (expected = 642.4, found = 642.4), b6 – H2O (expected = 624.4, found = 624.3), z5 – H2O (expected = 610.2, found = 610.2), and b5 (expected = 543.3, found = 543.2). The correspondence between the observed m/z peaks (Figure 5) with the expected ions given by mMass suggests compound 5 as the metabolite found in the brain samples.
    In order to further confirm the chemical structure of the metabolite as 5, compound 3 was hydrolyzed in order to generate 5 by chemical deamination of the C-terminal. The C-terminal amide of 3 is the only primary amide present making it the most prone to suffer hydrolysis under moderate basic conditions (Figure 6). The hydrolysis product was analyzed by HPLC-FLU and LC–MS, having the same retention time of 22.9 min (Figure 7) as the metabolite (Figure 5), and the major molecular ion found (1285.6 amu) (Figure 8) is consistent with the calculated exact mass for 5 [M + H]+ (1285.6 amu). A final confirmation of the metabolite structure was provided by the correspondence of the MS2 fragmentation spectrum of the metabolite (Figure 5) with that of the product of hydrolysis (Figure 8), where all the main peaks identified in the product of hydrolysis (Figure 8) were also identified in the fragmentation spectrum of the metabolite, even including peak b10 – H20 (1134.6) not highlighted in Figure 5 but still present.
    In a parallel experiment used to detect 4 by MALDI-TOF/TOF in several brain regions of Wistar rats, 4 also suffered biotransformation into 5. In agreement with previous experiments in mice, 4 was not detected in the rat cortex after 2 h, but 5 was. On the other hand, both 4 and 5 were detected in serum samples of Wistar rats after 2 h, raising the hypothesis that disposition of 4 is faster in mice than in Wistar rats.
    During the biotransformation of 4 into 5, the peptide suffered hydrolytic reactions at both the N- and C-terminal amides. Despite being generally considered robust amine derivatives,(31) amides do suffer metabolization. As shown in Figure 4, 4 is in fact 2-fold more stable than 5 in human serum, possibly because the latter is more susceptible to the attack of serum exopeptidases. On the other hand the opposite happened in mice brain homogenate (Figure 4), where 5 is more stable than 4. In the brain the action of amidases acting on terminal amides seems to be more relevant than exopeptidases. Primary amides, such as the C-terminal amide of 4, are easily converted into the corresponding acid by amidases,(32) which are nonspecific hydrolytic enzymes mainly found in the liver.(33) Amidases may also be found in other organs including kidney and brain, where N-deacylation of acetophenetidines has been observed.(34)
    The modifications suffered by 4 when converted to 5 are slight and resulted in an active metabolite as potent as 4 (1.7 ± 0.2 nM, mean value ± standard deviation, n = 3, p > 0.05, independent two-tailed Student’s t-test). The high potency of 5 was expected since it has a very similar structure to 3 (the only difference between 5 and 3 is in the C-terminal, possessing a carboxylic acid instead of an amide) that is as potent as 4. The benefits of having an amide at the C-terminal is dual, namely, the increase in the resistance against metabolization in serum and the absence of a negative charge that could disturb RAGE binding and consequently BBB crossing.(16) Since 5 was found in the brain, this means either that RAGE binding is not affected or that deamidation is slow enough to allow prior distribution to the brain. Moreover the presence of the carboxylic acid at the C-terminal also did not affect BACE-1 inhibition due to the long distance in relation to the binding site (Figure 1).
    Looking from a different perspective, the chemical modifications induced by the biotransformation of 4 can be even more important for therapy than the initial compound itself. The primary amine formed at the N-terminal and the carboxylic acid at the C-terminal increase the molecule hydrophilicity by the generation of a positive and a negative charge, respectively, at physiological pH. These chemical changes will contribute to impede 5 clearance from the CNS into the bloodstream, leading to a higher residence time in the CNS since a more hydrophilic molecule will have more difficulty diffusing across the BBB into the bloodstream.(26) This hypothesis gains even more support by the fact that 5 was found at approximately 20 nM in mice brain up to 1 h after administration, which is within the therapeutic concentration range, while 4 was not detected after half an hour after administration.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The conjugation of 1 with Aβ 18–23′ giving compounds 2, 3, and 4 shows little or no effect on the BACE-1 inhibition potential and at the same time contributes to increased selectivity in relation to BACE-2, with 4 exhibiting 4-fold more selectivity than 1 for BACE-1 in relation to BACE-2. Compound 4 was also demonstrated to be more resistant against in vitro metabolization in Human serum (3-fold).
    Preliminary data in Caco-2 cells indicate that compounds 1, 2, 3, and 4 are potentially nontoxic in concentrations that are therapeutically relevant, which complies with the general idea of peptides being low toxicity drugs.
    Compound 4 was not detected in serum or in mice brain after intravenous administration, but 5, an active metabolite of 4, was found in mice brain and Wistar rat cortex after 2 h after administration. Peptide 4 suffered two biotransformation steps elicited by amidases consisting of an N-deacylation and a C-deamination. This transformation afforded an active metabolite, 5 having increased hydrophilicity that may contribute to a larger residence time in the CNS, thus increasing its therapeutic index.
    We conclude that the design of compound 4, a peptidomimetic BACE-1 inhibitor based on the conjugation of 1 with Aβ 18–23′, was successful in targeting the CNS. Altogether these results corroborate the potential of 4 to reach CNS as a prodrug for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease. Future developments include the use of AD mice models to determine the efficacy of 4 as a prodrug.

    Peptide Synthesis and Purification

    Peptides were synthesized by manual standard solid phase peptide synthesis procedure using standard protocols,(35) starting from Fmoc-protected amino acids (Novabiochem) activated with N,N,N′,N′-tetramethyl-O-(1H-benzotriazol-1-yl)uronium hexafluorophosphate (HBTU) and 1-hydroxybenzotriazole (HOBt) using N,N-diisopropylethylamine (DIEA) in dimethylformamide. A Rink amide resin (MBHA resin [100–200 mesh], 0.59 mmol/g substitution, Novabiochem) was used for peptide synthesis yielding a C-terminal amide upon resin cleavage. Exceptions were 1 and the fluorogenic product (Mca-SEVNL-COOH) of the hydrolysis of Mca-SEVNLDAEFK-DNP by BACE-1 for which two Wang resins were used to generate a C-terminal carboxylic acid instead; these were respectively Fmoc-Phe-Wang resin ([100–200 mesh], 0.61 mmol/g substitution, Novabiochem) and Fmoc-Leu-Wang resin ([100–200 mesh], 0.64 mmol/g substitution, Novabiochem). Capping of the N-terminal was accomplished for 4 (Table 1) with 50 equiv of acetic anhydride and 25 equiv of DIEA in dimethylformamide during 1 h, after Fmoc deprotection of the last added amino acid with 20% piperidine in dimethylformamide. In the synthesis of Mca-SEVNL-COOH, 7-methoxycoumarin (Mca) (Novabiochem) was added to the N-terminal of SEVNL-COOH activated by O-(7-azabenzotriazol-1-yl)-1,1,3,3-tetramethyluronium hexafluorophosphate (HATU) and HOBt in dimethylformamide. The Fmoc-Leu*Ala isostere was synthesized as previously described(36) and was incorporated into peptides 1, 2, 3, and 4 using the same standard protocols as for natural amino acids.
    Peptides were purified by preparative reverse-phase HPLC and lyophylized. Chromatographic conditions were as follows: column C12 (Jupiter Proteo, Phenomenex) 250 mm × 21.2 mm, 4 μm; eluent (A) water/TFA (99.9:0.1, v/v), (B) acetonitrile/water/TFA (90:9.9:0.1, v/v/v). The flow rate was 10 mL/min, and the column was kept at room temperature. Reanalysis by analytical reverse-phase HPLC and electrospray mass spectometry (ESI-MS analysis) is in accordance with a homogeneous product of 98% purity or higher. Molecular structures and molecular masses of the synthetic peptides inhibitors of BACE-1 are outlined in Table 1.

    Assay for BACE-1 and BACE-2 Activities

    The kinetic experiments were preformed in 20 mM sodium acetate buffer, pH 4.5 at 37 °C in 15% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), 1% Triton X-100 (Sigma-Aldrich), 250 nM of Mca-SEVNLDAEFK-DNP (Bachem) as substrate and 2 U/mL of BACE-1 (human, recombinant, 21 267 U/mg, purity >90%, Sigma-Aldrich) or 10 U/mL of BACE-2 (human, recombinant, 40 000 U/mg, purity >80%, Enzo). The initial rates of substrate hydrolysis were confirmed to be directly proportional to the enzyme concentrations in a range of 1–5 U/mL for BACE-1 and 5–20 U/mL for BACE-2. Initial rates were determined below conversion of 5% of substrate, after stopping the reaction with 25% of a 2.5 M sodium acetate solution after 2 and 1 h for BACE-1 and BACE-2, respectively. The hydrolysis of the fluorogenic substrate was quantified by reverse phase HPLC with fluorescence detection (HPLC-FLU) using an excitation wavelength of 323 nm and an emission wavelength of 382 nm (Merck Hitachi, Elite LaChrom). A C18 column (Gemini, Phenomenex) 150 mm, 4.60 mm, 5 μm was used at 30 °C with a linear gradient from (A) water/TFA (99.9:0.1, v/v), to (B) acetonitrile/water/TFA (90:9.9:0.1, v/v/v) at 1 mL/min. The program started at 90% A for two min, graded to 70% A during 10 min and finally to 0% A during 2 min. The retention time of the reaction product (Mca-SEVNL-COOH) was 11 min, and quantification was performed by external calibration, using a peptide synthesized and purified as in the section Peptide Synthesis and Purification.

    Determination of Apparent Tight-Binding Inhibition Constants

    Apparent tight-binding inhibition constants, Kiapp constants, were determined from the fitting of experimental dose–response data to the Morrison equation (eq 1),(1)where v is the initial rate observed at each inhibitor concentration [I], v0 is the initial rate observed without inhibitor, and [E] is the enzyme concentration.(37, 38) Enzyme as well as substrate concentrations were kept constant with increasing inhibitor concentration, ranging from 0.04 to 283 nM, according to the potency of the inhibitor. The Morrison equation fit to experimental data was carried out through nonlinear regression by minimizing the residual sum of squares between the experimental data points of the relative activity vs the logarithm of the inhibitor concentration and those estimated by the model, using Solver add-in from Microsoft Excel 2003, considering the following options: Newton method, 100 iterations, precision of 10–6, 5% of tolerance, and 1 × 10–4 convergence.

    Determination of the Inhibition Constants for BACE-1

    Inhibition constants, Ki, were determined for BACE-1 from the direct application of the Cheng–Prusoff equation (eq 2)(39) and using the KM value reported of 4.5 μM for the Mca-SEVNLDAEFK-DNP substrate.(21) For BACE-2, the KM has not yet been reported, and therefore, Ki was not determined.(2)

    In Vitro Metabolic Stability Assay

    Compounds were prepared at 50 μM in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) with 2% DMSO. An amount of 10 μL of the solution was added to human serum (40 μL) or to 10% of mice brain homogenate in PBS with 0.5% Triton X-100 (40 μL). All compounds were tested using the same biological samples of human serum and mice brain homogenate to ensure identical deactivation conditions. The solutions were incubated during different time periods at 37 °C, after which metabolization was stopped by adding one volume of acetonitrile. Precipitated serum and brain proteins were removed by centrifugation at 14 000g for 10 min. Tests were made confirming that acetonitrile does not precipitate the synthetic peptides. The remaining peptide in the supernatant was quantified by HPLC-FLU (Merck Hitachi, Elite LaChrom), (exλ = 255 nm and emλ = 285 nm, to detect compounds containing phenylalanine, and exλ = 275 nm and emλ = 307 nm whenever tyrosine is present). Chromatographic conditions were as described for determination of enzyme activities. A calibration curve was built for each compound, and the decay in the concentration of the peptide in the incubated samples was followed until approximately 50% decay.
    The metabolization kinetics of the synthetic peptides followed a monophasic behavior in vitro. The fit of a monophasic deactivation model to experimental data was carried out through nonlinear regression by minimizing the residual sum of squares between the experimental data points of the relative quantity of intact product vs time and those estimated by the model, using Solver add-in from Microsoft Excel 2003, considering the following options: Newton method, 100 iterations, precision of 10–6, 5% of tolerance, and 1 × 10–4 convergence. The cytotoxicity assay was performed using the Promega CellTiter 96 cellular viability assay, MTS, to determine the number of viable cells in culture. Confluent Caco-2 cells were incubated with increasing concentrations of testing compounds from 2.5 nM to 50 μM in 0.5% FBS supplemented DMEM for 4 and 24 h exposure periods. After the incubation period the medium was removed and 100 μL of MTS mixture was added to each well. Cells were incubated with MTS for 4 h at 37 °C with 5% CO2 at fully humidified atmosphere.
