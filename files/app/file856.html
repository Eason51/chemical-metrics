    Abstract

    Utrophin modulation is a promising therapeutic strategy for Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD), which should be applicable to all patient populations. Following on from ezutromid, the first-generation utrophin modulator, we describe the development of a second generation of utrophin modulators, based on the bioisosteric replacement of the sulfone group with a phosphinate ester and substitution of the metabolically labile naphthalene with a haloaryl substituent. The improved physicochemical and absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) properties, further reflected in the enhanced pharmacokinetic profile of the most advanced compounds, 30 and 27, led to significantly better in vivo exposure compared to ezutromid and alleviation of the dystrophic phenotype in mdx mice. While 30 was found to have dose-limiting hepatotoxicity, 27 and its enantiomers exhibited limited off-target effects, resulting in a safe profile and highlighting their potential utility as next-generation utrophin modulators suitable for progression toward a future DMD therapy.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) is an X-linked recessive progressive muscle-wasting disorder that affects 1 in 5000 male births.(1) DMD is caused by mutations in the dystrophin gene, leading to the functional loss of the cytoplasmic protein, dystrophin. Dystrophin (427 kDa) is a vital component of the dystrophin associated protein complex (DAPC), providing structural stability, strength, and flexibility to the skeletal muscle by connecting the internal cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix. The absence of dystrophin results in muscle necrosis and regeneration, leading to replacement of muscle fibers with adipose and fibrotic tissues. Symptoms include progressive muscle weakness, motor development delay, and abnormal gait from early childhood, and patients usually lose ambulation by 8–12 years of age. Premature death typically results at age 20–30 because of respiratory and cardiac failure.(2,3)
    For the majority of the patient population, current treatments involve alleviating downstream effects (inflammation and fibrosis) with the use of clinical management and corticosteroids.(2,3) Although symptomatic and supportive treatments have increased the life expectancy of DMD patients raising median survival from 26 to 41 years,(4) no effective disease-modifying treatment currently exists. Therapies addressing patient subpopulations through mutation-specific approaches such as stop-codon read through (ataluren) and exon skipping (eteplirsen and golodirsen, for exons 51 and 53, respectively) have received conditional approval, and gene therapy treatments are being developed.(2,5) Consequently, there is an urgent need for a therapy that is applicable to the whole patient population.
    For many years, we and others have been investigating an alternative strategy to treat DMD: to upregulate the expression of utrophin, a structural and functional paralog of dystrophin, to compensate for the lack of dystrophin in patients.(6) The partial alleviation of DMD pathology has been achieved through the expression of a truncated utrophin minigene in transgenic mdx mice, providing evidence for utrophin modulation as a viable treatment strategy.(7) The expression of a full-length utrophin gene in the mdx model also prevented functional signs of DMD pathology in a dose-dependent manner.(8)
    Ezutromid 1 (formerly SMT C1100) was a first-in-class small molecule utrophin modulator developed by Summit Therapeutics which was well tolerated in Phase 1a and 1b clinical trials following oral administration.(2,9,10) In a multi-center, open-label Phase 2 clinical trial (PhaseOut DMD), ezutromid demonstrated a statistically significant and meaningful decrease in muscle regeneration and muscle inflammation after 24 weeks of treatment. However, these effects were not observed after 48 weeks, and the development of ezutromid was discontinued.(11) Although found to be safe and well tolerated in patients, the exposure of ezutromid was found to be lower than anticipated in a Phase 1 healthy volunteer trial and a Phase 1b trial of 12 DMD patients, and drug levels reduced significantly following repeated dosing.(9,10) Ezutromid also has sub-optimal physicochemical properties and is extensively metabolized in humans to the corresponding 1,2-dihydrodiol metabolites DHD 1 and DHD 3, both of which show reduced or no activity.(1,10,12) Taken together, these findings can rationalize the initial positive benefit followed by the lack of sustained efficacy in the Phase 2 clinical trial.
    As a result of these findings, a medicinal chemistry program was conducted to develop second-generation utrophin modulators, with improved physicochemical properties and metabolic stability compared to ezutromid, whilst maintaining a good safety profile. Optimization was focused around replacing the sulfone moiety (Region A, Figure 1), in order to improve the compound’s aqueous solubility, and the naphthalene moiety (Region B, Figure 1), which was found to be the metabolic hotspot.(12)

    Chemistry

    The general preparation of amide benzo[d]oxazoles, discussed extensively in previous publications,(13) was applied here to generate naphthyl amide derivatives 2, 3, and cyclic sulfonamide 4 (Scheme 1). Sulfonamide 5 was derived from the respective 2-aminophenol via a modification of Method B (Schemes 1 and 2).
    A range of methods were used to access the phosphinate esters of the benzo[d]oxazoles based on a common general route (Scheme 2), and these methods are described in detail in the Supporting Information. In brief, intermediate 2-arylhalobenzo[d]oxazoles (6–8a, 15–26a, 28–29a, 31–45a, 51a, 55a) were derived either from the condensation of 2-aminophenols with the respective carboxylic acid chloride followed by dehydration (Methods A and D) or from the respective aldehyde with a consecutive oxidation step (Methods B and C). The final products were obtained from the halo-derivatives via a Pd-catalyzed functionalization with phosphinate esters or phosphinic acids (Methods E–G, 6–26, 28–29, 31–55). Phosphinic acids were converted to the corresponding esters through the formation of the phosphinic acid chloride and reaction with the appropriate alcohol (Method H, 26b, 55b). Where scale-up synthesis was required for in vivo studies, the procedures were further optimized.(14)
    Detailed synthetic procedures for analogues 27 and 30 are reported elsewhere.(14,15) The synthesis of 26 was described previously;(16) however, a significant improvement in yield was observed when the compound was synthesized via the phosphinic acid route.

    Replacement of the Sulfone Moiety (Region A)

    Sulfones are known to form strong intermolecular interactions in the solid state, often leading to low solubility.(17) For this reason, replacements of the sulfone group of ezutromid (1) with other hydrogen bond donor groups such as amides (2, 3), sulphonamides (4, 5), and phosphinates (6, 7) were investigated in order to establish the impact of altering Region A on activity and solubility (Table 1). The ability of the compounds to increase utrophin levels was investigated using the previously reported cell line H2K-mdx utrnA-luc (H2K), a murine myoblast cell line that contains a stably integrated 8.4 kb fragment of the human utrophin promoter linked to a firefly luciferase gene.(1,12) The replacement of the sulfone moiety was well tolerated in all cases and even led to an apparent increase in activity in the cases of amides (2, 3) and phosphinates (6, 7) (Table 1). Amides (2, 3) and sulfonamide 4 had similar solubility to ezutromid, while phosphinate 7 was found to be significantly more soluble (Table 1).

    Phosphinates are an under-represented class of molecules as drugs, with most phosphorous-containing drugs containing the phosphate or phosphonate structure (nucleoside prodrugs and bisphosphonates).(18,19) The angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor fosinopril includes a phosphinate motif as a prodrug, the active species being the phosphinic acid (fosinoprilat).(20) Opportunity to study the underexplored chemical space in combination with the improved solubility observed from the preliminary studies led to the selection of the phosphinate as a sulfone replacement which was carried forward into more detailed structure-activity relationship (SAR) studies (Table 1, analogues 6–14). It was found that the phosphinate ester substituent was well tolerated in positions 5 and 6 of the benzoxazole ring (6 and 7, respectively), while moving it to position 4 (8) led to loss of activity. Whilst bulky P-alkyl substituents including i-Pr and t-Bu were tolerated (11–13), the corresponding P-phenyl derivative (14) showed no activity. Ethyl phosphinate esters (9, 10) showed similar potency to their respective methyl ester counterparts (6, 7). Subsequent optimization studies were performed using the methyl ethylphosphinate scaffold.

    Replacement of the Naphthalene Substituent to Reduce Hydrophobicity (Region B)

    As the naphthalene ring of ezutromid had been identified early on as the main site of metabolism,(12) the variation of this region was investigated next (Region B, Figure 1) in order to improve metabolic stability in the new series. The replacement of naphthalene with heterobiaryls such as quinolines and isoquinolines was an approach to mitigate the cytochrome P450 oxidative metabolism(12) (Table 2). In a parallel approach, halogen substituents were used to block the metabolic hotspots in the phenyl ring (Table 2).

    The binding of analogues 7, 15, 20, 21, 28, 30, 32, 40, and 41 to human and mouse plasma proteins varied, ranging between 80 and 99% bound fraction in humans and 72–99% bound fraction in mice (Table 3). The fraction of the compound bound to plasma proteins did not appear to be linked to phosphinate regiochemistry (5- or 6-) nor to the substituent in position 2. The most highly bound compound appeared to be the naphthalene analogue 7, and the least were quinolines 20 and 21 and fluorophenyl derivatives 27 and 32.
    The modifications greatly improved metabolic stability in most cases, apart from 28 which was cleared very quickly in mouse microsomes (Table 3). No patterns were apparent in terms of the phosphinate or aryl/heterobiaryl/styryl substituent nor for the phosphinate regiochemistry. The most metabolically stable compound in both mouse and human liver microsomes was the styryl analogue 40, while quinoline 20 had the lowest intrinsic clearance in mouse liver microsomes and the 2,3-dichloro analogue 35 in human liver microsomes.

    Phosphinate Selection: Utrophin Protein Upregulation in Dystrophic Mouse Muscle Cells

    Acknowledging the limitations of reporter gene assays,(21,22) we sought further confirmation of utrophin modulation activity. The quantification of utrophin protein levels by western blot in LUmdx cells treated with the selected analogues 27, 30, and 41 was used as an orthogonal method to support the progression of these molecules to in vivo evaluation. We observed an increase in the utrophin protein in all three cases. Phosphinates 27 and 30 increased utrophin up to 2.5-fold in a concentration-dependent manner, while 41 increased utrophin 2-fold at 1 μM, but at 10 μM, utrophin came down to basal levels (Figure 2). 27 and 30 were therefore selected for progression into murine in vivo PK and efficacy studies.

    Characterization and In Vitro Evaluation of Phosphinates 27, 30, and Their Enantiomers

    As phosphinates have a P-stereogenic center, some insights into the profile of the two enantiomers were required in order to assess whether to progress the racemic mixture or a single enantiomer. The separation of the enantiomers was performed using preparative chiral HPLC,(14) the specific optical rotations of 27 enantiomers were measured, and the enantiomers were assigned as (−) and (+), respectively (Table 5). The absolute configuration of the enantiomers was later inferred through an enantioselective synthesis of (+)-30 and (−)-30.(14) The enantiomers of 30 were found to have similar levels of activity to the racemate (H2K EC50s were 1.28, 1.39 and 1.20 μM for 30, (+)-30 and (−)-30, respectively).(14) In contrast, (+)-27 appeared to be slightly more active in the H2K-mdx reporter gene assay than enantiomer (−)-27 and the corresponding racemic mixture, with a eudismic ratio of 5 (Table 5).

    PK and Safety Profile for Racemic and Enantiopure Phosphinates 27 and 30

    The PK of the enantiomeric pairs and racemic mixtures of 27 and 30 were investigated in the mdx mouse (Table 7). In general, oral exposure was found to be low to moderate for 27 and enantiomers at 5 mg/kg, but higher for 30. The half-life was found to be short in all cases, but 27 and its enantiomers were cleared more rapidly than 30. Interestingly, the less-lipophilic 27 had a higher volume of distribution, potentially attributed to its lower PPB and, hence, greater unbound fraction; however, in all cases, a favorable distribution to tissues was predicted. The oral bioavailability of 30 and enantiomers was also found to be superior to that of 27. In general, oral bioavailability was good (>50%), apart from (−)-27, where it was 39 and 45% at day 1 and day 7, respectively. Overall, this suggested that phosphinate 30 had a preferable pharmacokinetic profile to 27. The reason for the observed decrease in AUC for (+)-30 and increase in AUC for (−)-30 after 7 days dosing was not clear. Despite this apparent difference, as discussed later, (+)-30, (−)-30, and the racemic mixture showed no significant differences in activity and efficacy in vivo in the mdx mouse.(14)

    The mutagenic potential of the leading phosphinates was evaluated in an Ames assay using genetically modified Salmonella typhimurium LT2 strains TA1535, TA1537, TA98, and TA100, and Escherichia coli WP2 strain uvrA/pKM101 pKM101 as indicator organisms. In most cases, no consistent increase in numbers of revertant (histidine or tryptophan-independent) colonies was seen with any of the five indicator strains either in the presence or absence of S9 microsomes. Thus, it was concluded that none of racemic 27, 30, (−)-27, (+)-30, and (−)-30 appeared mutagenic under the conditions used in this study. Curiously, (+)-27 showed a clear concentration-related increase in numbers of revertant colonies (histidine-dependent) for indicator strain TA98 at and above a dose of 160 μg (0.501 μmol) per plate in the presence of S9 microsomes. The increase reached 3.5 times the solvent control value at 500 μg (1.57 μmol) per plate. For this reason, 30 was initially selected for progression to a murine in vivo efficacy trial.

    Phosphinate 30 is Identified as an Advanced Molecule but Proves to be Hepatotoxic in Rats

    The in vitro and in vivo profile of 30 (also termed SMT022357) has been described in detail previously.(1,14) In brief, 30 was found to increase the utrophin protein in vitro and in vivo, ameliorating the pathology in skeletal, respiratory, and cardiac muscle in the mdx mouse.(1) No significant difference was observed in the activity and efficacy of the two enantiomers, and activity was shown to be comparable to that of the racemic mixture.(14) However, 30 was found to be hepatotoxic during a maximum tolerated dose (MTD) study in rats (data not shown). Although the 100 mg/kg dose level was tolerated for both enantiomers, adverse findings were apparent based on the evidence of liver toxicity that included liver enlargement and correlative histopathology findings. As a result, the therapeutic window for this compound was deemed too small to progress further.
    A more detailed analysis provided evidence of hepatobiliary toxicity as γ glutamyltransferase (GGT) and total bilirubin (TBILI) were significantly elevated at both dose regimens of 30 (300 and 100 mg/kg, Table S1). This was in good agreement with the cholestatic injury observed during the MTD study. Enantiomer (−)-30 appeared to be less toxic; however, this was attributed to the difference in toxicokinetics between the two enantiomers (Table S2), as (+)-30 showed greater exposure than (−)-30. It was noted that the two enantiomers had similar PK profiles in the mdx mouse,(14) although this was not the case in rats.
    In order to gain insight into the mechanism of hepatotoxicity and to determine whether it could be mitigated, representative analogues were tested in HepRG spheroids, a 3D hepatocyte model where several cell health parameters can be measured simultaneously (Table S3). Although the limited number of analogues tested did not allow clear structure-toxicity relationships to be observed, 30 and its enantiomers were amongst the most toxic analogues tested, inducing the same type of cell damage as shown by an increase in oxidative stress and a decrease in glutathione (GSH), DNA structure, and cellular ATP. Another trifluorosubstituted analogue 29 induced similar types of damage as 30. Analogue 27 and its enantiomers were found to have only minimal hepatotoxicity. From the results obtained, it was apparent that some analogues were significantly less toxic than others, and there was no pattern in inducing the same type of cell damage.
    The nature and location of the damage caused in the rat liver during the MTD study suggested P450 bio-activation to a hepatotoxic metabolite. As a result, metabolite identification (MetID) studies were undertaken in samples from this study. It was found that 30 was metabolized mainly to a glucuronide adduct of an oxidation product (M1) and to the corresponding phosphinic acid (M3). Further metabolites identified were GSH-conjugates (M7–8, Figure 3, Table S4).
    The metabolic profile of 30 was further studied in vitro in 4 different species to evaluate if this profile was unique to rats and if there were significant differences in the metabolites produced in primates. The metabolites of the two enantiomers in human hepatocytes were also determined. In all cases, the main metabolic routes were oxidation and conjugation with glucuronic acid (Table S5). Glucuronides were detected in larger quantities in rat and dog hepatocytes. No significant differences were observed between the two enantiomers (data not shown). Interestingly, the corresponding phosphinic acid M3 was not detected in the in vitro incubations.

    De-risking Genotoxicity of (+)-27

    While 27 and (−)-27 proved to be clean in the Ames assay, (+)-27 (also termed SMT022332) was shown to have weak mutagenic potential after metabolic activation with rat microsomes. In order to progress this potential candidate further it was necessary to understand whether this effect was specific to rats. Similar studies incubating (+)-27 with human, hamster, and mouse S9 microsome fractions were all negative for mutagenicity in the TA98 strain (Table S6). In light of these new data, (+)-27’s initial positive Ames result appeared be specific to rat metabolism.

    Safety Pharmacology of Phosphinate 27 and Its Enantiomers

    Phosphinate 27 and its enantiomers were not found to inhibit the most common P450 isoforms involved in drug metabolism and, as such, they have a safe profile in terms of predicted drug–drug interactions based on P450 inhibition (Table S7). Furthermore, 27 and its enantiomers also did not interact with a broad selection of ion channels (Chart S1A–C). When the two enantiomers were screened against a selection of 76 GPCRs, they were found to have an encouraging profile (Table S8): the only notable activity observed was antagonism of the 5-HT2B receptor (EC50 50 μM and 9.3 μM for (−)-27 and (+)-27, respectively). Similarly, the two enantiomers did not show any agonist or antagonist activity against a panel of nuclear hormone receptors (Table S9).
    On the basis of these data, 27 and its enantiomers were progressed to in vivo efficacy studies. Phosphinate 27 and its enantiomers had already been shown to have an acceptable PK profile in the mdx mouse (Table 7), but a more detailed evaluation of the two enantiomers and evidence to demonstrate upregulation of utrophin in vivo were sought.

    Phosphinate 27 and Enantiomers In Vivo Efficacy Study in mdx Mice

    Follow-up pharmacokinetic studies (data not shown) established that q.d. dosing at 30 mg/kg p.o. of 27 or its enantiomers would have adequate exposure to upregulate utrophin levels. Indeed, a statistically significant 2.2-fold upregulation of utrophin protein (p = 0.01) was observed in the extensor digitorum longus (EDL) muscle of 7 week-old mdx mice, dosed p.o. q.d. with 30 mg/kg 27 from 2 weeks of age for 5 weeks (Figure 4A). In the same study, using the same dosing regimen, (+)-27 induced a 1.9-fold increase in utrophin protein (p = 0.03), while (−)-27 increased utrophin 2.1-fold in the EDL muscle (p = 0.06) (Figure 4A). Considerable variability was observed amongst animals in the (−)-27 treatment group, which could account for the lack of statistical significance.
    As a next step, we sought to determine if this increase in the utrophin protein translated into a functional improvement in the muscle. In the absence of dystrophin, muscles become unable to sustain the mechanical stress caused by eccentric contractions, thereby showing an excessive force drop.(23,24) The reduction of force drop can thus be used in the mdx mouse model as a means to quantify the potential of new therapeutics for DMD. Ex vivo analysis of the EDL muscle showed 31.8% (p = 0.001) and 29.4% (p = 0.004) force drop, following dosing of 27 and (+)-27, respectively (Figure 4B). Interestingly, (−)-27 did not provide any significant improvement compared to the control mdx mice (44.4% and 44.9% for (−)-27 and control, respectively) (Figure 4B), whilst 27 and (+)-27 were found to protect against the muscle damage and improve the muscle function by increasing the muscle’s resistance to the stretch-induced damage (29 and 35% improvement for 27 and (+)-27, respectively). Overall, the in vivo efficacy data of 27 and (+)-27 were found to be similar to that of the previous advanced molecule 30.(14)

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Upregulation of utrophin is a promising therapeutic approach for DMD, which would be applicable to all patient populations. Following clinical evaluation of the first-generation compound, ezutromid, it became apparent that there were liabilities within ezutromid’s structure that could be improved, such as its metabolic profile and poor solubility. For this reason, a second generation of utrophin modulators was developed, based on bio-isosteric replacement of the sulfone group with a phosphinate ester and replacement of the naphthalene with a haloaryl substituent. The new analogues had significantly improved physicochemical and ADME properties, which was reflected further in the pharmacokinetic profile of the most advanced lead compounds. Indeed, both phosphinate analogues 30 and 27 showed significantly improved in vivo murine exposure compared to ezutromid. More importantly, analogue 30 showed utrophin protein upregulation in mdx mice, alongside alleviation of the dystrophic phenotype. However, further progression of this lead compound was halted when dose-limiting hepatotoxicity was found. After establishing that hepatotoxicity is not a general attribute of the series, another compound, 27, was chosen for further progression. Although both enantiomers of 30 showed similar profiles in vivo, only enantiomer (+)-27 was shown to upregulate the utrophin protein and improve physiology in mdx mice. Analogue 27 and enantiomers exhibited a safe profile and limited off-target effects, highlighting their potential utility as next-generation utrophin modulators for progression toward future DMD therapy.

    General Chemistry Methods

    All reactions involving moisture-sensitive reagents were carried out under a nitrogen or argon atmosphere using standard vacuum line techniques and glassware that was flame-dried and cooled under nitrogen before use. Water was purified using an Elix UV-10 system. All other reagents were used as supplied (analytical or HPLC grade) without prior purification. The final compounds were purified to ≥95% and characterized by a combination of 1H NMR, 13C NMR, high-resolution mass spectrometry, and high-performance liquid chromatography. NMR spectra were recorded on Bruker AVANCE III spectrometers (at 400 or 500 MHz) using the deuterated solvent stated and at rt. The field was locked by external referencing to the relevant deuteron resonance. Accurate mass measurements were run on either a Bruker MicroTOF internally calibrated with polyalanine or a Micromass GCT instrument fitted with a Scientific Glass Instruments BPX5 column (15 m × 0.25 mm) using amyl acetate as a lock mass. For preparative HPLC, the instrument used was a Waters Acquity H-Class consisting of an Acquity UPLC PDA Detector, an Acquity Quaternary Solvent Manager, and an Acquity QDa mass detector. Two general methods were used: (1) a basic method, where the column was an XBridge C18 10 μm particle size (30 × 100 mm); 5–95% gradient of MeCN (0.2% NH4OH) in H2O over 14 min, and the flow rate was 40 mL/min; (2) an acidic method, where the column was an Sunfire C18 10 μm particle size (30 × 100 mm); 5–95% gradient of MeCN (0.1% HCOOH) in H2O over 14 min, and the flow rate was 40 mL/min. Thin-layer chromatography was performed on aluminum plates coated with 60 F254 silica. Flash column chromatography was performed either on Kieselgel 60 silica on a glass column or on a Biotage SP4 automated flash column chromatography platform. Melting points were recorded on an EZ-Melt Automated Melting Point Apparatus (EZ Melt) and are uncorrected. Optical rotations were recorded on a PerkinElmer 241 polarimeter with a water-jacketed 1 cm cell. Specific rotations are reported in 10–1 deg cm2 g–1 and concentrations in g/100 mL.
    The intermediates that appear with no analytical data were quickly identified with LC–MS before progressing to the next step.
