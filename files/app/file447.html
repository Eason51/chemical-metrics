    Abstract

    In the present paper, we described the design, synthesis, SAR, and biological profile of a novel spirocyclic sulfone series of γ-secretase inhibitors (GSIs) related to MRK-560. We utilized an additional spirocyclic ring system to stabilize the active chair conformation of the parent γ-secretase inhibitors. The resulting series is devoid of the CYP2C9 inhibition liability of MRK-560. A few representative analogs were assessed in a nontransgenic animal model of Alzheimer’s disease (AD), demonstrating reduction of amyloid-β (Aβ) in the CNS after acute oral dosing. A spirocyclic phosphonate was identified as the optimal ring system for both potency and pharmacokinetics. Compared to GSIs studied in the clinic, representative spirocyclic phosphonate 18a(−) features improved selectivity for the inhibition of the PS-1 isoform of γ-secretase (33-fold vs PS-2), which may alleviate the adverse effect profile of the clinical GSIs.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the most common form of dementia in the elderly. It causes problems with memory, thinking and behavior. These problems get worse with increasing age. AD is the sixth leading cause of death across all ages in the United States.(1) As the elderly population continues to grow, the prevalence of AD will increasingly be a major burden to the nation’s health care system.
    It is known that accumulation and deposition of Aβ plaques leads to the damage and death of neurons, which is correlated with severity of AD.(2) An important step in the formation of Aβ peptides is the secondary processing of amyloid precursor protein (APP) fragments by γ-secretase, which is a multiprotein enzymatic complex containing presenilin (PS), aph-1, nicastrin, and pen-2.(3) Thus, γ-secretase is one of the critical enzymes required for the generation of Aβ peptides, which play a key role in AD. Inhibition of γ-secretase is considered to be a disease-modifying approach for this disorder(4) because small-molecule GSIs have been shown to lower levels of plasma, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), and cortical Aβ peptides in the animal models.(5) However, in addition to APP, γ-secretase cleaves a number of other endogenous substrates,(6) of which cell-surface receptor Notch has the most physiological significance. Cleavage of Notch by γ-secretase generates Notch intracellular domain (NICD), which relocates to the nucleus of the cell and acts as a transcriptional factor. Adverse effects (AE) of GSIs stemming from the inhibition of Notch processing have been well-characterized in vivo.(7-9)
    Notch-related AE are viewed as a major obstacle for the development of GSIs for the treatment of AD.(10) Semagacestat (LY-450139, Figure 1), one of the first GSIs to enter clinical trials,(11) failed to achieve a useful therapeutic window, leading to discontinuation of phase III clinical trials with this compound.(12) Because of the Notch-related AE, the AD research field has shifted in recent years from GS inhibition to GS modulation(13) and inhibition of BACE.(14) However, retrospective analysis of data from the failed Semagacestat trials points to knowledge gaps which must be addressed before prematurely abandoning efforts toward development of GSIs for the treatment of AD.(15) A review of recent advances in the identification of GSIs was published in a Journal of Medicinal Chemistry perspective.(16) The same review also discussed the application of GSIs beyond AD, such as for the treatment of leukemia(17) and breast cancer.(18)
    Interestingly, Notch-sparing GSIs have been discovered. Begacestat(19) (GSI-953, WAY-210953, Figure 1) and avagacestat(20) (BMS-708163, Figure 1), both belonging to the sulfonamide GSI class, have respective selectivities of 14-fold(21) and 193-fold(20) favoring inhibition of APP cleavage vs Notch. The in vitro selectivities of these compounds have translated into improved safety profiles in animal models.(22) Unfortunately, clinical trials with Notch-sparing GSIs still have not come to fruition, possibly because the reduction of Aβ in the CNS compartment has not been robust at the studied doses,(23) which were limited by AE.
    Similarly to Notch-sparing GSIs, MRK-560 (Figure 1), a representative of sulfone GSI class, has also demonstrated an improved therapeutic window in animals, as after chronic administration in mice it did not show the typical Notch AE.(24) This observation was surprising because MRK-560 did not show selectivity in the inhibition of APP vs Notch cleavage in in vitro assays.
    However, in a follow up study, MRK-560 was found to inhibit the isoform of γ-secretase composed of presenilin-1 (PS-1) 37-fold more potently than that of presenilin-2 (PS-2).(25) As separate studies of the PS-1 and PS-2 knockout animals lead to suggest that these isoforms have different pharmacologies, these findings taken together imply that PS-1 selectivity may provide for the improved safety profile of MRK-560.(25)
    The improved safety profile of MRK-560 makes it an attractive starting point for further optimization, and previously two novel series of γ-secretase inhibitors related to MRK-560 were described. Thus, Xu et al. made an additional connection between the C-2 atom of MRK-560 with the difluorophenyl ring (structure I, Figure 2).(26) Sasikumar et al. demonstrated that trifluoromethanesulfonamide can then be moved to positon C-3 of the scaffold, generating series II.(27) Several representative compounds I and II were tested in the TgCRND8 mouse model, an animal model of AD, and demonstrated the ability to reduce Aβ levels in vivo.
    Capitalizing on the previous research results, herein we describe the synthesis, SAR, and biological profile of a novel spirocyclic series of γ-secretase inhibitors III.(28) The novel spirocyclic series III was designed by borrowing SAR elements of previously known sulfone GSIs but adds a spirocyclic ring to position polar groups at the C-3 position of the scaffold while maintaining the preferred cyclohexane conformation. A few representatives of the series were assessed in a wild-type (nontransgenic) animal model of AD and demonstrated reduction of Aβ after acute dosing.

The same synthetic sequence (b–d) was used to elaborate the alkene in 5 into a leaving group and close to the six-membered ring via intramolecular alkylation, providing intermediate 6. Acetonide 6 was then hydrolyzed under acidic conditions to the diol 7.
    The synthesis of diols 11 and 13 is described in Scheme 2. 3-Allyldihydrofuran-2(3H)-one was deprotonated with LDA in THF at −70 °C and condensed with the vinyl sulfone 1(26) through a Michael reaction to afford compound 8 as a mixture of diastereomers. Hydroboration of compound 8 followed by mesylation, or hydroboration-iodination of 8 (“Kabalka protocol”(29)) was used to prepare compound 9, which was treated with t-BuOK to give the ring-closed product 10. Fortuitously, 10 was separable from the alternative diastereomer by a combination of silica gel chromatography and crystallization.

To prepare 11, lactone 10 was reduced with lithium aluminum hydride. For the preparation of 13, lactone 10 was converted to enol ether 12 through reduction to the corresponding lactol, followed by ring-opening of the lactol to expose the aldehyde by trapping of the free primary hydroxyl as its TBS ether. Wittig olefination of the aldehyde led to enol ether 12. Attempts at acidic hydrolysis of enol ether 12 resulted in a complex mixture of products; however, oxymercuration–reduction provided a clean conversion of enol ether to the alcohol. Finally, the TBS group was cleaved by TBAF to give the requisite diol 13. The advanced diol precursors 7, 11, and 13 were then transformed into the spirocycles (Scheme 3) to provide the final targets.
    In Scheme 3, diol 7 (n = 1, k = 1) or 11 (n = 1, k = 2) was treated with methanesulfonyl chloride and triethylamine to give monomesylated intermediates which were converted to compounds 14a,b by reaction with sodium hydride in DMF. Diol 13 (n = 2, k = 2) was treated with excess of methanesulfonyl chloride and triethylamine to produce a bis-mesylated intermediate, which was treated with sodium sulfide and pyridine in ethanol to give compound 15. Compound 15 was oxidized to compound 16 with OXONE in acetone and water. Diol 11 (n = 1, k = 2) was treated with TsCl and pyridine to give a bis-tosyl intermediate which was treated with trifluoromethanesulfonamide to give compound 17. Diols 7 or 11 were treated with methylphosphonic dichloride and pyridine to give compounds 18a and 18b, respectively. The phosphorus atom presents an additional stereogenic center in the molecules 18a and 18b, and in the case of the six-membered ring in 18a, a single diastereomer was isolated, whereas for 18b an inseparable mixture of diastereomers was formed.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Herein we disclose the design of a series of γ-secretase inhibitors, III (Figure 2), utilizing the cyclohexane framework A of MRK-560 (Figure 3). Although molecule A itself is a potent γ-secretase inhibitor in vitro,(30) it requires a polar appendage in order to gain oral bioavailability (Table 1, entry 2),(31) and in the case of MRK-560, this role is played by the trifluoromethanesulfonamide group (Table 1, entry 1). Without the polar appendage, molecule A experienced extensive metabolism at the methylene carbons of the cyclohexyl ring in in vitro metabolic stability assays.(31) In addition, its poor aqueous solubility may have negatively impacted oral absorption.

    Teall et al.(30) showed that the conformation of the cyclohexane ring in the framework A has an important effect on the potency of γ-secretase inhibition, and in the active conformation the inhibitors maintain the equatorial position of the arylsulfone group. This observation was confirmed by Jelley et al.,(32) who introduced a substituent, R, at position 3 of the cyclohexane (Figure 3, structures B). For molecules with relative cis- stereochemistry of R and the sulfone, equatorial conformation B-cis-eq was thermodynamically preferred, and these compounds showed high γ-secretase potency. On the other hand, trans- stereochemistry of R and sulfone led to a steric clash between R and the difluorophenyl group in the B-trans-eq conformation, resulting in a conformational “flip” of the cyclohexane ring. The ensuing conformation B-trans-ax lost γ-secretase activity due to the axial positioning of the sulfone group.
    The conformational “flip” similar to the one occurring for B-trans (Figure 3) may also occur in the case of gem-substitution or a spirocycle attached to the position 3 of the cyclohexyl ring. To counterbalance this tendency, in addition to the spirocycle attached to the C-3, our design III (Figure 2) utilizes an additional ring, connecting C-2 with the difluorophenyl group. This connection was shown by Xu et al.(26) to stabilize the active chair conformation of the γ-secretase inhibitor.
    For the in vitro assays of γ-secretase activity, we utilized the same assay systems as used by Xu et al.(26) The first-tier assay was conducted in cellular membrane preparations obtained from human embryonic kidney (HEK293) cells, transfected with the substrate of γ-secretase. These membrane preparations could be stored frozen for months and provided the reading of activity after 1 h of incubation with the inhibitor at 37 °C. The active compounds were then assessed in a more labor-intensive whole-cell assay based on HEK293 cells, which required a 6 h period of incubation. For the compounds tested, a consistent inhibition of production of both Aβ40 and Aβ42 were observed, indicating that the series acted as inhibitors rather than modulators of γ-secretase. Selected compounds were also tested in reconstituted γ-secretase systems prepared on the basis of either PS-1 or PS-2 isoforms of presenilin. These systems were obtained by expressing the individual constituents of γ-secretase into Sf9 cells(33) and allowed to determine selectivity of the inhibitors for the given isoform of presenilin.
    Geminally disubstituted bis(hydroxymethyl) derivative 7 (Table 1, entry 3), prepared as a synthetic intermediate for the construction of spirocycles, lost ∼17-fold of potency compared to A (entry 2). This outcome could be possibly attributed either to the overall steric bulk of the hydroxymethyls or to specific unfavorable interaction of the OH groups with the enzyme. Both issues were addressed in spirocycles 6 and 14a,b, where the hydroxyls were capped and the steric hindrance gradually reduced. The activity of the resulting compounds inversely correlated with the steric bulk of the spirocyclic ring, oxetane 14a being the most active. As expected based on previously published data for a related sulfone series,(26) the (−)-enantiomers were more active in comparison to 14b(−) and 14b(+).
    Although 14a and 14b(−) reached in vitro potency levels comparable to MRK-560 and showed good cellular penetration as evidenced by data from the whole-cell γ-secretase assay, they demonstrated only a modest effect on Aβ in vivo possibly due to poor pharmacokinetics as seen with A (entry 2). We concluded that, unlike the trifluoromethanesulfonamide group in MRK-560, polarity of a single oxygen atom in the spirocycle was not sufficient to confer good oral bioavailability; therefore, we continued to explore other polar groups. Increased polarity, however, often negatively affected γ-secretase potency in the in vitro assay. For example, introduction of a carbonyl group in 10 resulted in a ∼15-fold loss of potency compared to 14b(−), sulfone 16 was 2-fold less potent than thiopyrane 15, and the potency of spirocyclic triflamide 17 also was inferior to triflamide-containing MRK-560. Nevertheless, we were pleased to see that, as expected, polarity had a favorable effect on pharmacokinetics as demonstrated by the excellent rat AUC of 16.
    Ultimately, we identified cyclic phosphonates as in 18a(−) and 18b as optimal groups for both potency and pharmacokinetics. After oral dosing in rat, these compounds had AUCs superior to MRK-560, and the cyclic phosphonate appeared to be stable in vivo, as there was no detectable circulating metabolite 7, corresponding to cleavage of the phosphonate moiety. Unfortunately, in the case of 18b, the seven-membered spirocyclic ring contained a mixture of diastereomers at the phosphorus center and we failed to find conditions to separate the diastereomers of this compound for further characterization. This was in contrast to the six-membered phosphorus-containing ring in 18a, which had a tendency to form the spirocycle in such a way that the methyl group at phosphorus would occupy the least sterically hindered equatorial configuration. Enantiomerically pure (after chiral chromatography) 18a(−) was dosed orally at 10 mg/kg in wild-type rat and demonstrated 39% reduction of brain Aβ42 levels 3 h after dosing compared to vehicle-treated animals.
    Gratifyingly, the spirocyclic analogue 18a(−) (entry 11) demonstrated a high selectivity ratio favoring PS-1, which is characteristic of MRK-560 and is thought to provide for the improved side effect profile of MRK-560.
    Although full characterization of the safety profile of our spirocyclic GSI series in preclinical species is out of scope of the present study, it is worth highlighting that the series did not exhibit significant hERG inhibition and resolved the CYP2C9 inhibition liability associated with MRK-560 (Table 2). Evidently, the clean CYP2C9 profile of our compounds is a consequence of the newly formed spirocyclic ring because comparison of MRK-560 with a monocyclic analogue 19(26) indicates that the cycle connecting C-2 with difluorophenyl has minimal effect on CYP2C9 and repositioning of the triflamide from C-4 to C-3 as in 20(27) results in only a modest improvement, whereas a spirocyclic triflamide in 17 leads to complete abrogation of CYP2C9 activity within the range of our assay.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, using MRK-560 as the lead structure, we designed a novel series III by incorporation of two additional rings on its scaffold, including the cyclization of C-2 to difluorophenyl to stabilize the active chair conformation of GSIs and installation of a spirocyclic heterocycle attached at C-3. The series resolved the CYP2C9 inhibition liability of MRK-560 and demonstrated a high ratio of PS-1 vs PS-2 activity, potentially leading to a safer drug candidate. We identified spirocyclic phosphonates as optimal groups for both potency and pharmacokinetics. A few representatives of the series were assessed in a nontransgenic animal model of AD and showed efficacy by demonstration of partial reduction of Aβ in the brain and CSF after acute dosing in rat.
