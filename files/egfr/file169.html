    Abstract

    Pancreatic cancer is one of the most common cancers with an extremely low survival rate. Metastasis, as one of the key reasons of cancer-related death, is found in more than 50% pancreatic cancer patients at diagnosis. Novel therapeutic targets and drugs blocking cancer metastasis are urgently needed. Herein, we report a series of 1,5-diaryl-1,2,4-triazole derivatives as potent antimetastatic agents. Lead compound 6y displayed effective antimetastatic activities in pancreatic cancer in vitro and in vivo. Concomitant studies indicated that 6y probably binds with myoferlin (MYOF), a novel potential antitumor metastasis target, which regulates vesicle trafficking and metastasis-related proteins. Subsequent biophysical and biochemical methods verified that 6y bound to MYOF. Mechanism studies revealed that 6y inhibited pancreatic cancer metastasis through reversing the epithelial mesenchymal transition, inhibiting the secretions of matrix metalloproteinase and blocking the receptor tyrosine kinases. Our findings suggest that targeting MYOF with 6y may be a promising therapeutic strategy to prevent pancreatic cancer metastasis.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Pancreatic cancer is projected to be the second leading cause of cancer-related death by 2030 in the U.S. based on its annual percentage increase in incidence and death rates.(1) One of the major causes of death is tumor metastasis. Pancreatic cancer has already metastasized in 52% of patients at diagnosis, among whom the 5 year survival rate is only 3%.(2) Despite prominent improvement in cancer treatment, metastatic pancreatic cancer remains a major challenge. Systemic chemotherapy is still one of the most common and effective treatment strategies, especially for patients with metastatic pancreatic cancer. However, only eight drugs were listed on the National Comprehensive Cancer Network guidelines for treatment of pancreatic cancer (Figure 1). Among them, gemcitabine-based therapy is the standard-first-line therapy for advanced pancreatic cancer. Nevertheless, the 1 year survival rate of patients under standard treatment was only 15–20%, and the median survival was 5–6 months.(3) Few options are available for patients who have failed gemcitabine-based therapy. Therefore, innovative and effective drug candidates are greatly needed, especially for those patients with metastatic pancreatic cancer.
    Tumor metastasis is a process of interplay between cancer cells and the host distant site, which is also responsible for more than 90% cancer-related deaths.(4) Novel strategies and agents that could prevent or subdue tumor metastasis are urgently needed in drug development. Phenotypic assay has the advantage of identifying small molecules with a novel target, which are more likely to possess therapeutically relevant molecular mechanisms of action.(5,6)
    Distribution and degradation of key membrane receptors play crucial roles in tumor cell invasion and metastasis.(7) Myoferlin (MYOF), an endocytosis and vesicle-transport-related membrane protein, has been reported to be prominently overexpressed in pancreatic cancer,(8,9) breast cancer,(7) B-cell lymphoma, and lung cancer(10,11) while being absent in most normal tissues.(12) MYOF has been indicated to be involved in cancer cell migration and invasion by regulating the activities of metastasis-related proteins, such as epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR),(12) vascular endothelial growth factor receptor 2 (VEGFR2),(13) and matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs).(14) It has been reported that MYOF induces epithelial to mesenchymal transition (EMT),(15) promotes the secretions of MMPs,(16) and regulates activities of receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs),(14) which indicate that MYOF may act as one of the key regulators of cancer metastasis. Furthermore, the amplification of MYOF was revealed as a negative prognostic factor in pancreatic cancer.(7,8,17−20) MYOF is essential for the maintenance of a branched mitochondrial structure as well as a high oxidative phosphorylation activity in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, affecting the energy metabolism of pancreatic cancer cells.(8) Thus, prevention of the tumor-promoting function by novel MYOF modulators is a promising therapeutic approach for metastatic pancreatic cancer. Nevertheless, the crystal structure of MYOF is not available to date, which impedes the use of structure-based approaches for the design of MYOF inhibitors.
    Based on our previous structure–activity relationship (SAR) study on a series of 2,3-diaryl-4-thiazolidinone derivatives, compound 2a was discovered to potently inhibit tumor growth and metastasis.(21) Here, we introduced the medicinally privileged structure of the triazole fragment to the core skeleton of hit compound 2a to improve the antimigration activities and optimized the physicochemical properties by replacing the diphenyl rings with hydrophilic heteroaromatics. To evaluate the potential antimigration effects of designed compounds, the phenotypic assay was first applied for the initial evaluation of final compounds. More than thirty diaryl-substituted triazole derivatives were designed and synthesized to investigate the corresponding structure–activity relationship, including the following aspects: (1) further optimizing on the halogen-substituted phenethyl group tail (R1), (2) substituent position alteration or replacement of the methoxyl group and homologizing the diaryl substitution by other heterocycles (R2, ring A and ring B), and (3) replacement and modification of the 1,2,4-triazole with its isomers (R3 and ring C) (Figure 2).
    Concomitant with the study reported here, another part of structural optimization that focused on the tail of hit compound 2a was also carried out, leading to the discovery of compound 2b, which was identified with promising antitumor effects on breast cancer through targeting the MYOF C2D domain.(15) Based on previous results about the target identification of 2b, three compounds with potent antimetastatic activities were then selected for evaluation of their potential binding affinities through the MYOF-C2D-based surface plasmon resonance (SPR) assay. Further, compound 6y, as one of the potential leads against tumor metastasis, which possesses favorable binding affinity as well as physicochemical properties, was also studied with saturation transfer difference nuclear magnetic resonance (STD-NMR). Biological research subsequently showed that the anticancer activity of 6y was positively correlated with the expression of MYOF in pancreatic cancer cells, and 6y seemed to be insensitive to MYOF-knockout cells and restored its activity in MYOF-rescued cells. The animal model evidenced its ability to impede metastasis in vivo, presumably through its inhibition of MYOF. The specific antimetastasis molecular mechanisms of 6y were also explored in pancreatic cancer.

    Chemistry

    The heterocyclization of 1,2,4-triazoles was carried out following the reported literature procedures.(22) Compounds 6a–z were prepared as shown in Scheme 1. 2-(7-Aza-1H-benzotriazole-1-yl)-1,1,3,3-tetramethyl-uronium hexafluorophosphate (HATU) and N,N-diisopropylethylamine (DIPEA) were selected as coupling reagents to couple amidines and substituted-aryl carboxylic acids to form acylamidine intermediates 3. Aryl hydrazine and acetic acid were added to the crude solutions of 3 and then heated to afford 4. The 1,2,4-triazoles were generated in one pot with high tolerance for various functionalities and byproducts that could be easily removed. Compounds 7a–d were prepared using a similar synthetic route with 6k or 6p by substituting para-anisic acid and methyl 3-hydrazinyl-benzoate with monomethyl isophthalate or pyridine-3,5-dicarboxylic acid monomethyl ester or 6-(methoxycarbonyl)picolinic acid and (4-methoxyphenyl)hydrazine, respectively. Demethylation of 6p in the presence of BBr3 in dichloromethane (DCM) led to 8. Finally, compound 9 was synthesized similarly with 5-bromonicotic acid, followed by the Pd-catalyzed decarboxylative coupling reaction of potassium oxalate monoesters (Scheme 2).(23)

    Ten compounds were afterward synthesized with modifications on the halogen-substituted 2-phenylethyl tail, and their antimigration activities were examined (Table 1, 6e–n). The replacements of the phenyl ring with other polar heteroaromatics such as pyridine (6e and 6f) or thiophene (6g) unfortunately resulted in an almost complete loss of antimigration activities against cancer cells (IC50 > 20 μM), which indicated that the existence of a phenyl ring in this position is necessary. The elongation of one methylene chain (6h and 6i) conferred weaker activities, whereas two methylene groups’ elongation (6j) produced similar activities compared to 6c. Intriguingly, the nonsubstituted counterpart (6k) showed about 5-fold antimigration activity of compound 6j (bromo substitution on the p-position of the phenyl ring). To enrich the diversities of linkers between the amide group and the phenyl moiety and to optimize the physicochemical properties, the oxygen atom was incorporated into the carbon linker (6l and 6m), which minimized perturbation of the overall compound structure, but the potencies of antimigration were markedly reduced compared to 6k. Further linker expansion (6n) also led to diminished activity. The 4-phenylbutyl was thus retained in the following optimization.
    Subsequent replacements of 1,2,4-triazole with other N-heteroaromatics, such as pyrazole, imidazole, and 1,2,3-triazole, resulted in remarkable loss of antimigration activities in most cases (data not shown). Another 1,2,4-triazole analogue (7a, Table 3) showed about 2-fold less activity than 6k as well. Meanwhile, 2-methyl substitution of the 1,2,4-triazole scaffold was modified as reported in Tables 2 and 3 (see R3). One methylene extension (6p) exhibited improved activities (Table 2, 6p vs 6k, 6o, 6q), about 4-fold more active than 6k. The ethyl replacement on the isomer (7b) also showed a marked improvement (Table 3, 7b vs 7a). Introduction of the geminal methyl group and tethering gem-dimethyl into a cyclopropyl derivative showed little improvement in potency (6r, 6s vs 6p). These results suggested that ethyl substitution was preferred for R3 among the groups investigated (Scheme 3).

    The subsequent modifications on the diaryl substitutions of 1,2,4-triazole were mainly focused on the aromatic ring A and its substitutions (Table 2, 6t–z, 8). The para-methoxyl group on ring A was replaced by ethoxyl (6t), difluoromethoxyl (6u), trifluoromethoxyl (6v), and hydroxyl (8). Among the four groups, only the analogue with hydroxyl (8) showed similar potency to 6p. Meanwhile, the phenyl ring A was replaced by a more hydrophilic group like pyridyl (6w, 6x) to curb the hydrophobic properties. The position of nitrogen in the pyridyl group greatly impacted the activities of compounds (6w vs 6x), which suggested the possibility of improving the antimigration activity by introducing the 2-pyrimidinyl group (6y). Gratifyingly, compound 6y (IC50 = 0.11 μM) exhibited a comparable antimigration activity to positive control 1d (IC50 = 0.16 μM) and improved physicochemical properties according to the calculating model (Table S1). Replacement of ring A with the pyrazinyl ring resulted in modest activities (6z vs 6w, 6y). The aromatic ring B was explored as reported in Table 3 (7c, 7d, and 9), where the incorporation of the pyridyl group resulted in reduced activities (7c, 7d vs 7b, 9 vs 6p).

    Compound 6y Showed Potent Anti-invasion and Antimigration Activities against Different Pancreatic Cancer Cells

    To further evaluate the anti-invasion activities of the 1,2,4-triazole compounds, several compounds with representative structures were selected for the investigation (Table 4). Compounds 6p, 6x, and 6y displayed anti-invasion IC50 values in the submicromolar range (0.18, 0.15, and 0.12 μM, respectively). Notably, among the investigated compounds, 6y exhibited the greatest potency in suppressing cancer cells’ migration and invasion, showing comparable activity to that of 1d. To further validate the antimigration activity of 6y, two other pancreatic cancer cell lines MiaPaCa2 and Capan2 were selected in the transwell assay (Figure 3). The results showed that treatment with 6y concentration-dependently reduced migrated cell numbers of both MiaPaCa2 and Capan2 cells, indicating its strong inhibitory effect on motility of pancreatic cancer cells.

    Cellular Toxicity Assessment of Compound 6y by the 3-(4,5-Dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-5-(3-carboxymethoxyphenyl)-2-(4-sulfophenyl)-2H-tetrazolium (MTS) Assay

    Since compound 6y displayed promising antimigration and anti-invasion activities as well as physiochemical properties (Table S1), its toxicity was initially assessed in the cellular model. Human pancreatic cancer cell lines (Capan2, MiaPaCa2, and PANC1) and normal human cell lines (HPED6-C7, human normal adult fibroblast (HAF), and MCF-10A) were used to determine the half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) by the MTS assay (Table 5). The results showed that compound 6y displayed IC50 values of 2.85–0.84 μM in three pancreatic cancer cell lines and 8.54–27.56 μM in three normal human cell lines. To rule out the possibility that the cellular toxicity of 6y led to inhibition of cell migration, we performed an additional experiment by treating PANC1 with different concentrations of compound 6y (0.1, 0.3, and 1.0 μM) for 18 h. As shown in Figure S1, compound 6y had very weak antiviability effects against PANC1 under these concentrations during the period. The results suggested that compound 6y was tolerant to normal cell lines at effective concentrations against cancer migration, and the cellular toxicity of compound 6y against PANC1 did not affect its antimigration result.

    Binding Affinity between Compounds and MYOF in SPR Experiments

    To explore the possible targets of this series of compounds, the SPR-based biosensor assay of the MYOF-C2D domain interaction with three representative compounds was performed based on our previous results of the target identification of 2b (Figure 2).(15) Compound 6k, as the first identified 1,2,4-triazole derivative with the IC50 value below 1 μM against the migration assay of the PANC1 cell line, was included in SPR experiments. Since the substitution of ethyl in ring C resulted in an about 4-fold increase in biological activities (6p vs 6k), compound 6p was added to help us observe the corresponding binding affinity change. Compound 6y, possessing the best antimigration and anti-invasion activities and the most favorable physicochemical properties, was also selected for evaluation. As illustrated in Figure 4, the modification from methyl to ethyl substitution on the 1,2,4-triazole resulted in over 10-fold improvement in the binding affinity (Figure 4, 6p vs 6k), consistent with the increase in antimigration activities (Tables 1 and 2, 6p vs 6k). Compound 6y also showed concentration-dependent and reversible binding with the MYOF-C2D protein, with a KD value of 0.094 μM.

    STD-NMR Spectroscopy Investigation of the Binding Interactions for the 6y-MYOF Complex

    STD is one of the most powerful and robust NMR techniques for the investigation of ligand–receptor interactions.(24) STD-NMR was thus employed as an orthogonal technique to observe the direct binding between compound 6y and MYOF. In agreement with the SPR experiment, the direct binding of 6y to MYOF was observed in the STD spectrum. As illustrated in Figure 5, STD for the pyrimidine and methoxyl displayed weaker signals, indicating that the pyrimidine (ring A) and methoxyl moieties are more solvent-exposed compared to ring B. By contrast, proton 1 at the para-position of the phenyl ring tail and protons at the ethyl substitution on ring C displayed the strongest STD signals, suggesting that these protons are buried in the protein. In the absence of the protein three-dimensional structure, the STD data help rationalize SPR data. Substitution of methyl on ring C with ethyl increased the binding affinity by about 10-fold, in agreement with the STD-based observation that this moiety is buried in the protein. The results of STD-NMR indicated that the 4-phenylbutyl tail and the 3-ethyl substitute on the 1,2,4-triazole bind into the pocket of MYOF-C2D. Combined with SPR results of compounds 6k and 6p, we speculate that there may exist a narrow hydrophobic pocket wherein the ethyl group was buried.

    Activity of 6y Is Positively Correlated with the Expression of MYOF

    Compound 6y was shown to bind to MYOF via both SPR and STD-NMR experiments and displayed favorable biological activity and physicochemical properties. Therefore, it was selected for further target validation by biological experiments. To determine whether the biological effect of 6y is mainly due to binding with MYOF, sensitivity test of 6y was performed in MYOF-knockout and MYOF-overexpressing cells (Figure 6). The results showed that MYOF-knockout PANC1 cells reduced migration, demonstrating the critical role of MYOF in migration. Genetic ablation of MYOF in the PANC1 cell line caused cells to become less susceptible to 6y. Nonetheless, genetically rescued expression of MYOF restored the antimigration activities of compound 6y, suggesting that 6y inhibited cancer cell migration through targeting MYOF (Figure 6B,C). To rule out the possibility that MYOF abrogation could simply cause resistance to multiple types of anticancer agents, 1g (docetaxel, Figure 1), an antimigration agent that inhibits microtubule depolymerization, was added to the MYOF-knockout cells (Figure S2A,B). The results showed that 1g significantly inhibited migration of MYOF-knockout cells.

    CYPs Inhibitions of Compounds 2a, 6k, 6y, and 7a

    Drug-related adverse effect is a major challenge for drug development and may be attributable to the inhibition of cytochrome P450 enzymes.(25) Compounds 2a, 6k, 6y, and 7a with various concentrations ranging from 0.05 to 50 μM were used to determine the inhibition effects on human CYP enzymes. As shown in Table 6 and Figure S3, compound 2a had moderate inhibition effect on the CYP2C9 isozyme (IC50 = 5.35 μM). However, compound 6y had weak or little inhibition on tested human CYP activities (IC50 values > 20 μM), with reduced inhibition on CYP2C9.

    Compound 6y Blocked Pancreatic Cancer Metastasis and Prolonged Overall Survival in BALB/C Nude Mice

    Since 6y significantly inhibited the migration and invasion of pancreatic cancer cells and was shown to bind to MYOF, we then assessed its antimetastasis activity in vivo utilizing luciferase-based noninvasive animal bioluminescent imaging in a pancreatic cancer pulmonary metastasis model on BALB/C nude mice. PANC1-luciferuse (PANC1-luc) cells were injected into the tail veil of the BALB/C nude mice (n = 8). The mice were then averagely assigned into four groups according to the fluorescence value of mice: the negative control group (dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)), the positive control group (1d, 25 mg/(kg day)), and the 6y-treated groups (25 and 50 mg/(kg day)). Lung metastasis was monitored every 10 days by bioluminescent imaging. As shown in Figure 7A,B, compound 6y dose-dependently inhibited pancreatic cancer pulmonary metastasis with superior antitumor metastatic activity compared to the positive control at the same dosage. On day 20, the average luminescence was reduced by about 3-fold and 5-fold in treatment groups (25 and 50 mg/(kg day), respectively) (Figure 7B), indicating significant reduction in lung metastasis burden. After 3 weeks, the survivals were 25% (the negative control), 60% (the positive control), 75% (6y, 25 mg/(kg day)), and 87.5% (6y, 50 mg/(kg day)) (Figure 7C), evidencing that compound 6y significantly prolonged the overall survival. Moreover, the mice in the treated groups showed no significant loss in weight (Figure 8A) and displayed no signs of toxicity exhibited by pathological examination of various major organs such as the heart, lung, liver, spleen, and kidney (Figure 8B). Taken together, these results suggested that compound 6y possesses potent antimetastatic activities in vivo and can improve overall survival without significant toxicity.

    Studies of the Possible Mechanism of Compound 6y against Tumor Metastasis

    EMT is the process of loss of cell polarity and generation of mesenchymal and migratory phenotype, which drives cancer cells toward the invasive phenotype in tumor metastasis.(26) Since it has been reported that genetic ablation of MYOF reverses EMT in breast cancer cells,(16) whether 6y impacts the EMT process of PANC1 cells was explored. Western blot results revealed that the expression of the epithelial cell marker E-cadherin was significantly upregulated. Conversely, fibronectin, a mesenchymal cell marker, was observed to be drastically reduced in a concentration-dependent manner with 6y treatment (Figure 9A). In summary, these results indicated that 6y could reverse pancreatic cancer cells to the epithelial state. In the process of tumor metastasis, cancer cells secrete a variety of MMPs to degrade the extracellular matrix and basement membrane for efficient migration and invasion.(27) Research has revealed reduced secretions of MMPs in MYOF-depletion cancer cells.(16) Then, we collected and concentrated the cell culture medium of 6y-treated PANC1 cells. As shown in Figure 9B, 6y gradient-dependently inhibited the secretions of MMP1 and MMP2 in PANC1 cells.
    Moreover, MYOF acts as a key regulator of RTK recycling, whereas VEGFR and EGFR are two major RTK drug targets in pancreatic cancer clinical treatment.(14,28) It is not surprising that ablation of MYOF in vascular endothelial cells led to instability and rapid degradation of the VEGFR2.(13) MYOF deficiency also resulted in impaired degradation of phosphorylated EGFR, causing prolonged/aberrant signaling, notably resulting in the inhibition of EMT and migration in cancer cells.(12) These studies revealed that MYOF served as a cancer-related protein likely via regulating tumor-associated endocytic processes. On the basis of this hypothesis, we examined the effect of 6y on VEGFR and EGFR. As shown in Figure 9C,D, Western blot assay revealed that 6y diminished the levels of phospho-VEGFR2 and VEGFR2 in PANC1 cells. 6y treatment in PANC1 cells caused sustained EGFR phosphorylation upon 10 ng/mL EGF stimulation, switching off the EGFR signal pathway. These results indicated that 6y suppresses tumor metastasis in a dose-dependent manner through both VEGF and EGFR signaling pathways.
    To prove that compound 6y regulates these migration-related factors by targeting MYOF rather than directly affecting expressions, secretions, or phosphorylations of these factors, experiments on MYOF-knockout PANC1 cell were performed in comparison. As shown in Figure S4, treatment of different concentrations of compound 6y had almost no effect on expressions of E-cadherin and fibronectin, secretions of MMP1 and MMP2, expressions as well as phosphorylations of EGFR and VEGFR2 in MYOF-knockout PANC1 cells. These results suggest that compound 6y regulates these migration-related factors by targeting MYOF rather than directly targeting these factors.
    Moreover, two migration-associated proteins that are not under the control of MYOF were also examined (Figure S5). The results showed that treatment of 6y had no significant effect on the expression levels of Snail1 and Twist1. Besides, the expressions of two other proteins (myosin IIA and dynein) that regulate vesicle trafficking were detected after treatment with different concentrations of 6y (Figure S6). The results showed that 6y did not affect their expression. These data indicated that compound 6y is more specific for MYOF inhibition.

    Conclusions

    This study characterized a series of diaryl 1,2,4-triazole derivatives that inhibited migration and metastasis against pancreatic cancer in vitro and in vivo through targeting MYOF. Moreover, their antimigratory structure–activity relationship was explored. Among them, 6y inhibited pancreatic cancer cell migration and invasion with IC50 values in the nanomolar range. The SPR assay showed that 6y can bind to MYOF-C2D with nanomolar affinity. STD-NMR experiments indicated that the phenyl tail is the most buried region of the inhibitor and that ring A appears the most solvent-exposed moiety of the compound. Also, compound 6y showed little activity against the MYOF-knockout cell line and restored the activity in the MYOF-rescued cell. More importantly, compound 6y potently blocked PANC1 metastasis in vivo without showing obvious toxicity. The MYOF inhibitor is likely to impart its effects by reversing EMT and inhibiting the secretions of MMP1 and MMP2 while affecting the phosphorylation of VEGFR2 and EGFR. Therefore, a novel therapeutic strategy to prevent pancreatic cancer metastasis can be developed based on our findings. In addition, the implication of MYOF overexpression in various types of cancer warrants further evaluation on the effect of these series of 1,2,4-triazole compounds against other types of cancers.

    Biological Evaluation

    Cell Lines and Reagents

    Human pancreatic cancer cell line PANC1, MiaPaCa2, Capan2, human normal breast epithelial cell line MCF-10A, human normal pancreatic ductal cell line HPDE6-C7, and human normal adult fibroblast (HAF) were obtained from American Type Culture Collection (Manassas, VA) and cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium media (Gibco) in a 37 °C and 5% CO2 incubator. Mitomycin C was purchased from Sigma. Matrigel was purchased from BD Biosciences (San Jose, CA). The pcDNA3.1-myoferlin HA vector was obtained from Addgene. Primary antibodies: MYOF (ab76746, Abcam), fibronectin (ab137720, Abcam), β-actin (A5441, Sigma), MMP1 (ab137332, Abcam), MMP2 (ab37150, Abcam), E-cadherin (3195, CST), VEGFR2 (9698, CST), phospho-VEGFR2 (2478, CST), EGFR (4267, CST) and phospho-EGFR antibodies (3777, CST), Snail1 (3879, CST), Twist1 (46702, CST), myosin IIA (3403, CST), dynein (ab23905, Abcam). The AQueous One Solution Cell Proliferation Kit was purchased from Promega (MTS reagent, Madison, WI). BugBuster 10× protein extraction reagent was purchased from Merck.

    In Vitro Transwell Migration Assays and Invasion Assay

    Transwell migration assays were performed in 24-well plates following the manufacturer’s instruction. Briefly, PANC1, MiaPaCa2, and Capan2 cells were pretreated with 20 ng/mL mitomycin C for 2 h to exclude the effect of cell proliferation. Then, cells were trypsinized and seeded in upper chambers of a 24-well transwell chamber with 300 μL of basic medium containing different concentrations of compounds (0, 0.16, 0.8, 4, 20 μM or 0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.4 μM). The lower chambers were filled with 600 mL of total medium containing the corresponding compounds. After incubation at 37 °C (18 h for PANC1, 24 h for MiaPaCa2, and 36 h for Capan2), noninvasive cells were scraped with a cotton swab, and invaded cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde and then stained with 0.1% crystal violet. Images were photographed using a digital camera attached to an OLYMPUS inverted microscope, and migrated cells were quantified by manual counting. Invasion assay was performed as the transwell migration assay except that the upper surface of the transwell membrane was coated with 20 μL of Matrigel (1.5 mg/mL) and incubated for 15 min before the cell seeding. Three independent experiments were performed.

    Cell Viability Assay

    In brief, cells were seeded in 96-well plates with an appropriate cell density. After 12 h of incubation, various concentrations of drugs were added for 48 h incubation. Then, 20 μL of MTS reagent per well was added and incubated for 45 min at 37 °C. Spectrophotometric absorbance at 490 nm was measured with a microplate reader (SpectraMax 190; Molecular Devices). The IC50 was calculated using GraphPad software. Three independent experiments were conducted in triplicate.

    Knockout of MYOF by sgRNA

    sgRNA directed against human MYOF was inserted into the lentiCRISPR v2 vector (oligo 1: 5′-CACCGGGCTTGCCAAATTTCGTTTT-3′ and oligo 2: 5′-AAACAAAACGAAATTTGGCAAGCCC-3′). HEK293T cells were cotransfected with the lentiviral expression plasmid, plus packaging plasmid psPAX2 and envelope plasmid pMD2.G. After 48 h of transfection, the supernatant was harvested for MYOF-knockout cells.

    Overexpression of MYOF

    In a six-well cell culture plate, 3 μg of pcDNA3.1-myoferlin HA vector or equivalent empty vector was transfected into MYOF-Knockout PANC1 cells using 6 μL of Lipofectamine 2000 reagent (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. After 48 h of transfection, the migration abilities of cells were tested.

    Cloning, Expression, and Purification of MYOF-C2D Protein

    Human MYOF-C2D gene sequence composed of bases 3445–3912 of MYOF CDS was ligated into the BamHI and XhoI sites of the pGEX4T-1 vector (forward primer: 5′-CGGGATCCCGAGTGCCACCACT-GTGTTCGGAG-3′; reversed primer: 5′-CCGCTCGAGCCTCAGAATCAGCTCTGCAGTT-3′). Recombinant plasmid was transformed into BL21 (DE3) chemically competent cells (TransGen Biotech) for protein expression. Then, 0.5 mM isopropyl β-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) (Sigma) was added to induce protein expression when the optical density value of 100 mL of culture medium reached 0.7. After an additional 4 h post induction, cells were centrifugated and resuspended in 5 mL of lysis buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.4, 1 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), 150 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1% NP40) and followed by ultrasonic cell lysis (300 W, twice for 10 min). Inclusion bodies (IB) were collected and thoroughly resuspend in 5 mL 1× IB wash buffer (20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 10 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100). IB solubilization buffer (10 mL, 50 mM N-cyclohexyl-3-aminopropane sulfonic acid, pH 11.0) with 0.3% N-lauroylsarcosine was added into inclusion bodies and incubated for 15 min. The solution was centrifugated, and the supernatant containing the solubilized protein was transferred into a clean tube. Solubilized protein was dialyzed in dialysis buffer (20 mM Tris–HCl, 0.1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), pH 8.5) for 6 h at 4 °C. The dialysis continued through two additional changes (3 h each) with the dialysis buffer lacking DTT. Reduced glutathione (1 mM) and oxidized glutathione (0.2 mM) in the dialysis buffer were used to dialyze the refolded protein overnight at 4 °C. Then, glutathione agarose gel beads were incubated with protein overnight at 4 °C to purify the refolding glutathione S-transferase (GST)-tagged MYOF-C2D protein. The unbound protein and impurities were washed off with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). The GST-tagged MYOF-C2D protein was eluted with an elution buffer (20 mL, 10 mM reduced glutathione, 50 mM Tris, pH 8.0) and concentrated with an ultrafiltration column at 5000g, and the eluent buffer was replaced with PBS simultaneously. The purified protein was quantified and detected by coomassie blue staining and Western blot.

    Surface Plasmon Resonance Assay

    Experiments were performed at 25 °C with a Biacore T200 instrument (GE Healthcare) as previously described.(29) GST-tagged MYOF-C2D protein in sodium acetate buffer (pH 4.5) was immobilized on the sensor chip (CM5) for 7 min using the standard amine coupling kit (GE Healthcare). To deactivate the unreacted esters and remove the unbound protein, the surface of the flow cell was then blocked with ethanolamine for 7 min. A blank flow cell was activated and deactivated in the same manner with GST-tagged peptide immobilization.
    Gradient concentrations of compounds that were dissolved in degassed and filter-sterilized PBS buffer were injected into the channel and analyzed at 25 °C. The bulk effects were corrected with solvent correction, and the reference and blank were subtracted from the binding response curves. Data processing and affinity analysis were performed using BIA evaluation software in a 1:1 Langmuir binding model.

    Saturation Transfer Difference NMR Spectroscopy

    D2O 99.9% and DMSO-d6 were purchased from Sigma. PBS buffer (50 mL) was prepared with NaCl (400 mg), KCl (10 mg), Na2HPO4 (72 mg), and KH2PO4 (12 mg), and the pH was adjusted to 7.5 by hydrochloric acid. The NMR sample contained 6 μM MYOF-C2D protein, 145 μM 6y in 10% D2O, 1% DMSO-d6, and PBS buffer. NMR spectra were acquired in a Bruker spectrometer operating at a proton frequency of 600 MHz equipped with a z-gradient cryoprobe. The NMR experiments were performed at 293 K. Water suppression was achieved using excitation sculpting for both the 1D and STD experiments. 1D and STD experiments were performed using identical experimental conditions (spin lock, interscan delays). For the STD experiment, selective saturation of the protein was achieved at 0 ppm, whereas off-saturation was achieved at −15 ppm. Saturation time was set to 2 s.

    Microsomal Incubation

    Microsomal incubation was carried out at 37 °C in a mixture of 200 μL in 0.05 M Tris–HCl buffer (pH 7.4) consisting of pooled HLMs (0.5 mg/mL) and an NADPH-regenerating system including MgCl2 (5 mM), G6P (10 mM), G6PDH (0.4 U/mL), and NADP (1 mM). Substrates were added into the incubation system, and the final concentrations were obtained (10 μM phenacetin for CYP1A2, 20 μM bupropion for CYP2B6, 20 μM tolbutamide for CYP2C9, 5 μM dextromethorphan for CYP2D6, 20 μM chlorzoxazone for CYP2E1, and 10 μM midazolam for CYP3A4). The ratio of the organic solvent was not higher than 1% (v/v) in the incubation system.(30) The reaction was initiated by addition of NADP after preincubation for 5 min. The incubation process was performed for 20 min and stopped by adding 200 μL of ice-cold acetonitrile. Then, 20 μL of internal standard solution was added (3-acetamidophenol 2 μg/mL, chlorpropamide 2 μg/mL, and mebendazole 2 μg/mL). The solution was mixed thoroughly and centrifuged for 15 min at 16 000g at 4 °C. The supernatant (80 μL) was transferred to a cell plate, and 2 μL was injected into the LC–tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS) system. The inhibition IC50 values were fitted by nonlinear regression analysis using GraphPad Prism 6.0 (GraphPad Software Inc., CA).

    Pancreatic Cancer Lung Metastasis Animal Model

    Eight-week-old BALB/c nude mice were purchased from National Rodent Laboratory Animal Resources (Shanghai, China). Animals were housed in a specific pathogen-free room within the animal facilities, and studies were performed according to the guidelines approved by the Institute of Biomedical Sciences, East China Normal University. Next, 1 × 106 PANC1-luc cancer cells were intravenously introduced into the mice. On the next day (day 0), the mice were divided into four groups according to the fluorescence value of mice. All mice in drug treatment groups were intraperitoneally injected with 50 μL of 6y (25 and 50 mg/kg) or 1d (25 mg/kg) every day, with an equivalent volume of DMSO serving as the control group (n = 8). The bioluminescence of cancer cells in lungs was monitored every 10 days by an IVIS Imaging System (Xenogen Corporation, Alameda, CA). When the mice showed near-death indicators such as loss of mobility and temperature drop, they were euthanized immediately in consideration of animal ethics. The Kaplan–Meier survival curve was used to estimate the survival curve during the treatment.

    Histological Analysis

    The tissues of hearts, livers, spleens, lungs, and kidneys were harvested and fixed with 4% formaldehyde for 12 h, processed with tissue dehydration, and embedded in paraffin blocks. Paraffin-embedded tissues were cut into 4 μm sections and mounted on glass slides. The tissue sections were hydrated through decreasing concentration of alcohol baths and stained with hematoxylin and eosin. After tissue dehydration, the slides were mounted with xylene-based mounting media and covered with coverslips. Histopathological changes were observed and captured with a Leica DM 4000B photomicroscope.

    Western Blot Assay

    Pancreatic cells were lysed with a radioimmunoprecipitation assay buffer supplemented with phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride and a proteinase inhibitor cocktail and a phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (Sigma). Protein concentrations were determined using a Bicinchoninic Acid Protein Assay Kit (Thermo Scientific) and equalized before loading. Total cellular protein samples were applied to 8–12% sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) gels and transferred onto nitrocellulose membranes. Then, membranes were blocked with 5% bovine serum albumin in PBS and probed with specific primary antibodies overnight at 4 °C. Membranes were incubated with florescence conjugated secondary antibodies and detected by the infrared dichroic laser scanning imaging system (Li-Cor, Odyssey).

    MMP Detection Assay

    In the test for the secretions of MMP1 and MMP2, wild-type or MYOF-knockout PANC1 cells were serum starved in basal media with different concentrations of 6y for 24 h. Then, the conditioned media were concentrated 50-fold with Amicon Ultra-4 10 kDa centrifugal filters (Millipore, Billerica, MA), and equal volumes of concentrated media were reconstituted in the sample buffer and resolved by SDS-PAGE.

    Statistical Analysis

    Grouped data are expressed as mean ± standard deviation. In the transwell migration assay, the invasion assay, and the cell viability assay, significance between groups was analyzed by one-way ANOVA, and lung metastasis animal experiment was measured by repeated measurement ANOVA using IBM SPSS Statistics software. All experiments were performed at least three times except animal experiments. p values less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant.
