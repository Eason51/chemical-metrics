    Abstract

    On the basis of the efficient substrate for p60c-src protein tyrosine kinase (PTK) YIYGSFK-NH2 (1) (Km = 55 μM) obtained by combinatorial methods, we have designed and synthesized a series of conformationally and topographically constrained substrate-based peptide inhibitors of this enzyme, which showed IC50 values in the low-micromolar range (1−3 μM). A “rotamer scan” was performed by introducing the four stereoisomers of β-Me(2‘)Nal in the postulated interaction site of the peptide inhibitor 23 (IC50 = 1.6 μM). This substitution led to selective and potent inhibitors of p60c-src PTK; however, no substantial difference in potency was observed among them. This and the results of the “stereochemical scan” performed at residues 2 and 7 of 3 (peptides 19−21), which form the disulfide bond, may suggest that the enzyme active site does not have rigid topographic requirements and thus is able to achieve important conformational changes to bind the ligand as long as the pharmacophore pattern in the inhibitor is conserved. Two new potent iodo-containing nonphosphorylatable tyrosine analogues were also incorporated into our lead inhibitory sequence 23, producing the most potent inhibitors for p60c-src PTK identified thus far in our studies. Compounds 29 and 30 exhibit IC50 values of 0.13 and 0.54 μM, respectively. Peptide 29 is 420-fold more potent than the parent peptide 1. Selectivity studies of peptides 23−30 toward p60c-src, Lyn, and Lck PTK showed in general high Lyn/Src and moderate Lck/Src selectivity ratios.We found that the χ1 space constraints of the specialized amino acids, introduced at position 3 of the peptide lead 23, were not as important as the configuration of the Cα of that residue to recognize the subtle chemical environment surrounding the active site of Src and Lck PTK, as reflected on the obtained Lck/Src selectivity ratios.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Phosphorylation is one of the major posttranslational modifications which regulate many cellular processes such as cell cycle, growth, and differentiation.1 Protein tyrosine kinases (PTKs) are key enzymes which phosphorylate specific tyrosine residues within the sequence of a wide variety of functional proteins and have been found to be a common mechanism for transmitting mitogenic signals and regulating numerous cellular processes. A potential role for certain PTKs in tumorogenesis is evident from their ability to transform normal cells to a neoplastic phenotype when expressed in a mutated unregulated form or to an abnormally high level.2 Therefore, the development of inhibitors of PTKs has been considered as a promising approach for the design of antiproliferative drugs.3
    PTKs are divided into three categories:  (1) membrane receptor-linked PTKs, such as epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) and insulin receptor; (2) non-receptor-linked cytosolic PTKs, such as Abl, Fes, and Wee1; and (3) non-receptor-linked membrane-anchored PTKs, such as the Src-family PTKs, among which Src, Lyn, Fyn, Lck, Blk, Yes, Hck, Fgr, and Yrk have been identified.4 All members of the c-Src family possess a variable region followed by SH3 and SH2 domains, the catalytic (kinase) domain, and a short carboxy terminal tail.5
    The overactivation or mutation of proto-oncogenes encoding protein kinases can result in signaling activation causing cell transformation. Indeed half of the proto-oncogenes identified to date encode proteins having PTK activity.6 This potential to transform normal cells is compatible with existing data implicating tyrosine phosphorylation and dephosphorylation as events intimately involved in growth regulation and mitogenesis.7
    Since it has been suggested that inactivation of a single oncogene-encoded protein may be sufficient to reverse cell transformation,8 the development of inhibitors of these protein kinases offers an alternative to the treatment of cancer,9 as well as for other proliferative diseases including psoriasis,10 atherosclerosis,11 and restenosis.12 The potential utility of PTK inhibitors as anticancer agents has been extensively reviewed.13
    The normal cellular homologue (c-src) of the Rous sarcoma virus oncogene (v-src), the first molecularly defined proto-oncogene, encodes the p60c-src PTK.14 Human p60c-src PTK belongs to the third class of PTKs described above and has been implicated in the development of leukemia, breast, and colon cancer.15
    Natural product screening programs have identified a number of substances which inhibit or disrupt cellular processes mediated by this enzyme, many of which are phenolic in nature and compete with ATP for binding to the PTK.16 Unfortunately, the inhibitory efficacy of these species, even though their IC50 values are in the low-micromolar and medium-nanomolar ranges, is markedly impaired under physiological conditions due to the high intracellular concentrations of ATP.17 An alternative and preferred approach is to utilize peptide−substrate-based inhibitors which compete only with the protein and peptide PTK substrates. This approach had been relatively unexplored,13 mainly because of the lack of suitable short peptide substrates (i.e., eight residues or less),18 wherein the phosphorylation site of the sequence can be replaced for nonphosphorylatable tyrosine analogues to produce inhibitors. In the past few years, random combinatorial peptide library methods have been shown to be useful tools in the identification of peptides ligands for macromolecular targets.19,20 Lam et al.21 using the “one-bead one-peptide” concept20 have identified, after screening a limited heptapeptide library (500 000 beads), a peptide with the sequence YIYGSFK-NH2 (1) as an efficient substrate for p60c-src PTK with a Km value of 55 μM.
    Over 70 analogues of this lead were designed and synthesized on beads and their phosphorylation on solid supports by p60c-src PTK has been quantitated.22 Figure 1 summarizes the conclusions obtained from the structure−activity relationship (SAR) studies for the single l- or d-amino acid substitutions in 1. These results were used as the starting point of our inhibitor design, as described below.

    Results and Discussion

Cleavage of the assembled peptides was achieved using anhydrous hydrogen fluoride (HF) and scavengers. Peptides 25−30 were prepared using an Nα-Fmoc strategy in disposable polypropylene columns. Cleavage from the resin was achieved by treatment with trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) and an appropriate scavenger cocktail (see the Experimental Section).

    The results obtained in Lam's study22 (Figure 1) showed that the residues Ile2, Tyr3, and Gly4 are important for the biological activity of 1. In addition, the Tyr3 residue was shown to be the phosphorylation site of this substrate. The importance of the Gly4 residue may suggest the presence of some type of β-turn stabilizing the secondary structure of these peptides in which Gly4 could be, due to favorable steric reasons, the i+1 or i+2 residue of the β-turn. From a molecular recognition perspective,24 it can be argued that the most important features of the β-turn are the relative dispositions of side chains bonds 1, 2, 3, and 4 (Chart 1). Bonds 2 and 3 are important because they govern the placement of the side chains of residues i+1 and i+2, whose exposed nature makes logical recognition sites. Bonds 1 and 4 are important because they determine the position of any binding groups before and after the β-turn. Therefore, to determine if β-turns were present in the peptide substrates studied by Lam et al.21 and hence may be important for p60c-src PTK binding, we synthesized a series of four linear analogues of substrate 1 and the corresponding cyclic disulfide derivatives 2−9 (Table 1).
    In the cyclic analogues, a Cys residue was introduced at position 7 for disulfide bond formation as well as an extra Lys residue at position 9, to account for the pharmacophoric distances which may be important at the basic core of the peptide. To preserve the hydrophobic nature at position 2, shown to be important for activity, we introduced the chimeric amino acid penicillamine (Pen) at that position, which provided as well the SH group necessary to form the disulfide bond with Cys7. Recently, Lam et al.25 have reported that replacement of Tyr3 with (2‘)Nal in substrate 1 resulted in a moderately potent and specific inhibitor of p60c-src PTK with an IC50 value of 66 μM. YI-(2‘)Nal-GSFK was shown to be a competitive inhibitor for p60c-src PTK, when 1 was used as a substrate, and showed no activity toward cAMP-dependent protein kinase (a representative of serine/threonine kinases). Thus, we decided to incorporate this amino acid into peptide analogues 4 and 5. Using a solution-phase phosphorylation assay,26 the IC50 values of peptides 2−9 were determined using 1 as a substrate. The Tyr-containing inhibitors are not phosphorylated, as no new spots besides that of the phosphopeptide corresponding to the substrate were detected on the TLC of the solution-phase phosphorylation assay, as compared with the control containing only substrate (with no inhibitor). For the (2‘)Nal compounds, of course, phosphorylation is not possible. As shown in Table 1, the inhibitory potency was retained and in most cases improved by the introduction of the conformational constraint, which supports the theory of a β-turn stabilizing the secondary structure of the linear peptides. The reduced potency of peptides 8 and 9 shows the importance of Gly4 in both the linear and cyclic forms. Compound 5 showed a 28-fold increase in potency compared with the parent peptide 1 of this series.
    At this point, it was decided to explore other structural and functional aspects of these cyclic inhibitors. Thus we selected compound 3 (IC50 = 3.0 μM) as the new lead. The following points were addressed and are discussed below (results are shown in Table 2).
    1. Nature of the Amino Terminus. In peptides 10−12 the importance of the Tyr1 residue and of the acetylation of the N-terminus, in the inhibitory capacity of 3, was studied. As shown in Table 2, it was observed that acetylation and/or deletion of Tyr1 in compound 3 resulted in loss of inhibitory potency (8−9-fold in the case of 10).
    2. Nature of the Basic Core. One important criteria for successful PTK inhibitors is, among others, cell permeability. Therefore, in peptides 2−9 the presence of a highly hydrophobic core with two Lys residues at the C-terminus suggests potential problems for membrane crossing. In peptides 13−16 we studied the effect, on the bioactivity of these analogues, of the substitution of Lys9 by the less lipophilic and more basic amino acid Arg. After this modification, a 4- and 2-fold increase in potency for 10 and 11, respectively, was observed. In the case of 3, the parent peptide of this series, this substitution produced the potent inhibitor 16 with an IC50 value of 1.2 μM, which represents a 46-fold increase in potency compared with 1.
    3. Stereochemical Scan at Positions 2 and 7. Although conformational effects, due to changes in the configuration of a particular residue, may not be immediately apparent in linear peptides, this type of substitution has been shown to be extremely important in cyclic peptides as is demonstrated for the case of DPDPE, a potent and selective δ-opioid receptor agonist.27 Peptides 3 and 19−21, produced from the l- and d-configuration scan at positions 2 and 7, showed no significant difference in inhibitory potency. This result may confirm the existence of a β-turn that is being stabilized by the disulfide bond formation, in which the stereochemistry of the side chain group that is out from the turn (i residue), in this case Pen2, has no significant influence on the potency of the inhibitor. This result suggests the possibility of using d-amino acids at these positions to enhance the metabolic stability of future analogues.
    On the basis of the results shown in Tables 1 and 2, peptides 22 and 23 were prepared incorporating the most effective structural and functional features (pharmacophore pattern) which appear to be important for binding and inhibition of p60c-src PTK, namely, the Lys8-Arg9 sequence at the basic C-terminus, the (2‘)Nal residue at position 3, and the d-configuration of the Pen2 residue. Similarly, we observed IC50 values in the low-micromolar range (1.5−3 μM) for these two peptides which, to some extent, validates our design methodology.
    Compound 23 (IC50 = 1.6 μM) was selected as the lead for the next series of peptides analogues which incorporated topographically constrained amino acids and potentially nonphosphorylatable tyrosine analogues at position 3.
    Topography can be defined as the relative, cooperative, three-dimensional arrangements of the side-chain groups in a polypeptide.28 The topography of side-chain groups plays a critical role in peptide−receptor interactions. The investigation of the topographical aspects of bioactive peptides offers great potential in the design of potent and selective peptides.29 This has been shown, for example, by our group in the case of highly selective ligands for the δ-opioid, melanotropin, and glucagon receptors.30 In these studies it has been shown that the preferred topography of the side-chain groups can be examined by appropriately constraining, biasing, or fixing side-chain conformers in the gauche(−) (χ1 ≈ −60°), trans (χ1 ≈ ±180°), or gauche(+) (χ1 ≈ +60°) conformations generally found in amino acid residues.
    These β-methyl amino acids (Figure 2) were designed to be used as tools to favor one of the three possible rotamers of the χ1 side-chain torsion angles (Figure 3). It could be assumed that the β-methyl susbstituent would favor, for steric reasons, the rotamer which places the β-proton between the NH and CO groups (Figure 4). However, in the case of the β-MePhe isomers, recent NMR and theoretical studies31 have shown a more complicated rotamer equilibrium for the β-methyl-substituted side chains.
    In this case, the β-methyl substituent does not always favor one particular χ1 rotamer but rather always eliminates the χ1 rotamer which places both bulky β-substituents in a gauche orientation to the carbonyl group; i.e., a trans rotamer (χ1 ≈ 180°) of both erythro isomers (2S,3S and 2R,3R), a gauche(+) rotamer (χ1 ≈ 60°) of the (2S,3R)-isomer, and a gauche(−) rotamer (χ1 ≈ −60°) of the (2R,3S)-isomer, while allowing the other two conformers in each case. Although an extensive conformational analysis has not yet been performed in the case of the four isomers of β-methyl-2‘-naphthylalanine, we decided to study the effects of these topographically biased amino acids, on the inhibitory potency of 23 toward p60c-src PTK. Therefore, we introduced the four isomers of this specialized amino acid at position 3 of this lead. Assuming a similar conformational behavior of this aromatic amino acid to the already studied β-MePhe isomers, we expected to observe a preference of the enzyme toward one of the four peptides containing a different isomer at this position. However, in contrast to the case of membrane-bound receptor ligands,30 a significant difference in inhibitory potency among the four peptides was not observed (Table 3). Both erythro isomer-containing peptides were shown to be the more potent. Peptide 25 showed an IC50 value of 1.0 μM which is a 2.1-fold increase in potency compared with its corresponding χ1 unconstrained peptide 24 and 55-fold compared with 1. The IC50 values for the (2R,3S)- and (2R,3R)-β-Me(2‘) Nal analogues showed a 3-fold difference in potency which revealed a slight topographical requirement of the enzyme active site to the orientation of the naphthyl ring at position 3. In this regard, almost no improvement in bioactivity was observed in the case of peptides 27 and 28 which contain an l-configuration at the Cα of β-Me(2‘)Nal.

    Perhaps the results can be explained on the basis of previous studies32 which have shown unusual broad active-site substrate specificity for p60c-src PTK. This was explained in terms of a “dual specificity”; i.e., the interaction enzyme−substrate is not fixed to a prescribed site but instead fluctuates according to the structural nature of the substrate. This broad specificity was explored in terms of the chain length between the phosphorylatable function and the adjacent peptide bond, but our results support this hypothesis in terms of topographical space as well.
    These results may suggest that the enzyme p60c-src PTK, unlike some types of membrane-bound receptors,30 is able to perform important conformational changes molding itself around the inhibitor during the binding process which may avoid, to some extent, the differentiation of distinct topographies on the interacting side chains of the inhibitor.
    As mentioned above, another rational approach33 in the search for potent inhibitors of PTKs is the introduction of nonphosphorylatable tyrosine mimetics in the phosphorylation site of suitable substrates. Clearly, PTKs have evolved to specifically coordinate an aromatic hydroxyl moiety within the enzyme active-site region. If the hydroxyl group of tyrosine is retained and additional functionalities are incorporated in the aromatic nucleus, this could preclude phosphoryl transfer or it may actually enhance enzyme activity.
    On the basis of the results obtained, the position 3 in these inhibitors is thought to be the residue that resembles the phosphorylation site of the substrate YIYGSFK-NH2 (1) and therefore the key interacting side chain of these peptides at the enzyme active site. Thus, it was decided to incorporate the nonnatural amino acids 3-iodotyrosine and 3,5-diiodotyrosine, as potential nonphosphorylatable tyrosine mimetics, at position 3 of the lead inhibitory sequence 23. The introduction of these two sterically demanding groups ortho to the aromatic hydroxyl group may interfere with the ability of this group to engage in productive hydrogen-bonding interactions with the active-site residues of the enzyme.
    This substitution led to the potent peptides 29 and 30 with IC50 values of 0.13 and 0.54 μM, respectively. Compound 29 is 10-fold more active than its Tyr3 analogue 16 and 420-fold more active than 1, the parent peptide of this study (Table 3).
    This result may indicate a different role of the bulky iodo atoms at the active site of p60c-src PTK, which does not involve stereoelectronic effects that could affect the hydrogen-binding properties of the phenolic OH. These compounds represent the most potent substrate-based inhibitors obtained so far, even more potent than the branched peptide inhibitor reported by Lam et al.34 with an IC50 value of 0.6 μM.
    Selectivity Studies. Selectivity is a very important part in the design of PTK inhibitors, because they will interact at the peptide−substrate binding pocket rather than at the conserved ATP binding site. The cdc.2(6−20) peptide KVEKIGEGTYGVVYK (Km = 100 μM), derived from p34cdc2, has been reported to be a specific and efficient substrate for the Src family PTKs.35 In this study, we decided to use this peptide as a substrate to evaluate the selectivity of our inhibitors among Src family PTKs. It is because Lyn and Lck kinase preparations contain some contaminating Ser kinases, which precludes the use of 1 as a substrate. Table 4 shows that peptides 23−30 present a high Lyn/Src selectivity ratio. The highest selectivity for p60c-src PTK was observed for the χ1-unconstrained analogue 24, in which no inhibition of both Lyn and Lck was observed at concentrations up to more than 800 μM. On the other hand, the enzymatic specificity of the χ1-biased inhibitors 25−28 is high in the case of Lyn (no inhibition above 800 μM) but only moderate for Lck (no inhibition up to 250 μM).

    The χ1-constrained peptides 25 and 26 with d-configuration of the Cα at position 3 present, respectively, the lowest and highest IC50 values for p60c-src PTK inhibition which, as mentioned before, may represent a slight topographical recognition of the enzyme active site toward these two isomers in which the χ1 conformer populations are supposed to be different. However, in terms of Lck/Src specificity, it was observed that the presence of the β-methyl group produces a negative effect which makes inhibitors 25 and 26 less selective. This is particularly clear in the case of the χ1-unconstrained (no β-methyl group) analogues 23 and 24 with l- and d-configurations at Cα of (2‘)Nal3 residue, respectively. Peptide 24 shows a much higher Lck/Src selectivity, considerably different to the one observed for the l-isomer-containing analogue 23. These data mainly indicate two interesting points. First, in terms of selectivity, the χ1-constraints do not significantly enhance, to an important extent, the specificity of the inhibitors toward p60c-src PTK. Second, in the χ1 unconstrained peptides 23 and 24, the absence of the methyl group at the β carbon of that side chain greatly increases the Lck/Src selectivity ratio, further showing that the d-configuration of the Cα at position 3 makes compound 24 a potent and highly selective inhibitor of p60c-src PTK.
    Compounds 29 and 30 were shown to be the most potent inhibitors of p60c-src PTK found in our study, showing no inhibition of Lyn PTK at concentrations up to 800 μM. However, as shown in Table 4, they show a poor Lck/Src selectivity ratio which may indicate that the role of the iodine atoms may be common in both Src and Lck active sites.

    Conclusions and Future Work

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    On the basis of the peptide substrate for p60c-src PTK 1, obtained from a limited combinatorial library, we have designed conformational and topographically constrained potent/selective inhibitors for this enzyme. The conformational constraint, introduced via a disulfide bond, produced a slight difference in potency between the linear inhibitors and the cyclic counterparts, which suggests that the conformation of these inhibitors at the binding site of the enzyme may involve some type of β-turn. Further structural and functional modifications of one of the most potent cyclic inhibitors produced the new lead 16 with an IC50 value of 1.2 μM.
    The structural modifications that appeared to be key for binding and inhibition of p60c-src PTK were incorporated into 16, producing compound 23 (IC50 = 1.6 μM) which was used as the lead for the “rotamer scan” at position 3. Thus, the four isomers of β-methyl-2‘-naphthylalanine were incorporated at that position of peptide 23. The results obtained from this study indicate that the topographical requirements at the enzyme active site appear not to be very rigid, as shown by the IC50 values obtained for the analogues 25−28. Further studies are underway to determine if the lack of difference in potency observed among these inhibitors is due to the ability of the enzyme to perform important conformational changes of its interacting side-chain residues at the active site or to low-energy barriers of the rotamer populations of the (2‘)Nal side chains of peptides 25−28.
    Selectivity studies performed for the inhibitors 23−30 indicate, in all cases, a high Lyn/Src selectivity ratio. We observed, in the case of Lck, a negative effect of the β-methyl group in the selectivity Lck/Src, in which the χ1 space constraints do not significantly enhance the specificity of the peptides 25−28 toward p60c-src PTK, as does the Cα configuration at the same residue of the χ1-unconstrained analogues 23 and 24. This may suggest that the orientation, relative to the peptide backbone, of the whole side chain of residue 3 is more important than its χ1 space topography in recognizing the binding groups at the enzyme active sites of Lck and Src PTKs.
    The introduction of two new nonphosphorylatable tyrosine mimetics into the lead sequence 23 produced the two most potent substrate-based inhibitors identified in our study. Compounds 29 and 30 showed IC50 values of 0.13 and 0.54 μM, respectively. These two peptides are respectively 420- and 100-fold more active than the parent peptide of this study (1). However, they show poor Lck/Src selectivity ratios. These data show that some of these substrate-based inhibitors may be good candidates to be used as peptide leads for the design of potent and specific peptidomimetic blockers of p60c-src PTK activity.
    On the other hand, we have underway a cocrystallization study of p60c-src PTK with some of these inhibitors, which will give more tools to understand the structure and function of the catalytic site (kinase) as well as for the development of effective antiproliferative drugs. Our research will continue toward the design and synthesis of a peptide mimetic molecule which, based on the presented results, could be a nonpeptide β-turn mimetic which would not only enable control of the turn type formed but also allow for optimal functionalization of the turn mimetic with the appropriate groups. This type of compound may present a higher inhibitory potency and selectivity for p60c-src PTK, be metabolically more stable, and be suitable to act on intact cells.

    Peptide Synthesis. (A) Using the Nα-t-Boc strategy, compounds 2−24 were synthesized in a glass manual peptide synthesizer, using p-MBHA substituted at the level of 0.32 mmol/g of resin. The following Nα-t-Boc amino acids with the indicated side-chain protecting groups were used:  Arg(Tos); Lys(ClZ); Ser(O-Bzl); Tyr(2,6-Cl2-Bzl); Cys(p-MeBzl); Pen(p-MeBzl); and d-Pen(p-MeBzl). The p-MBHA resin (0.1 g) was neutralized with 10% DIEA in DCM (2 × 10 mL) followed by washings with DCM (4 × 10 mL) and DMF (2 × 10 mL). The first amino acid (2.2 equiv) was coupled to the resin using HOBt (2.2 equiv) and HBTU (2.2 equiv) as coupling reagents and DIEA (3 equiv) in DMF for 1.5−2 h. Deprotection of the Boc groups was performed by 50% TFA in DCM, followed by treatment with 20% DIEA in DMF for 10 min to neutralize the resin.
    The subsequent Nα-t-Boc amino acids were coupled to the growing peptide chain under the same conditions mentioned above. The couplings were monitored by the Kaiser test.37 After the last synthetic cycle, the N-terminus was deprotected and the resin washed with DCM (2 × 10 mL), ethanol (2 × 10 mL), and DCM (2 × 10 mL) and dried under vacuum overnight. The peptide was cleaved from the resin by stirring with anhydrous HF for 60 min at 0 °C, using a 1:1 (v/v) mixture of p-cresol/p-thiocresol as scavengers (1 mL/g of peptide-resin), followed by the removal of the scavengers and HF under high-vacuum conditions. To ensure complete removal of the scavengers and nonpeptide material, anhydrous ethyl ether (5 × 30 mL) was added to the vessel and the mixture filtered using a course glass frit. The crude peptide was dissolved in glacial acetic acid, lyophilized, and purified by RP-HPLC using the conditions indicated above.
    (B) Peptides 25−30 were prepared using an Nα-Fmoc strategy in disposable polypropylene columns. The following side-chain protecting groups were used:  Arg(Pbf); Cys(Trt); d-Pen(Trt); Tyr(t-But); Lys(Boc); and Ser(t-But).
    The Rink-amide-MBHA resin (0.1 g) with a substitution level of 0.41 mmol/g of resin was Fmoc-deprotected by washing twice with 3 mL of 20% piperidine in DMF while shaking the columns on a Labquake for 5 and 15 min. The Nα-Fmoc amino acids (2.5 equiv) were added sequentially using DIC (2.5 equiv) and HOBt (2.5 equiv) as coupling reagents. The Nα-Fmoc group deprotection was carried out by 20% piperidine, in two cycles as mentioned above. After the last coupling cycle the Nα-Fmoc terminal group was deprotected and the resin washed with DMF (5 × 10 mL), DCM (3 × 10 mL), EtOH (2 × 10 mL), and DCM (2 × 10 mL) and dried under vacuum overnight.

The crude peptides were recovered by tert-butylmethyl ether precipitation and dried under vacuum overnight.
    Cyclizations. The disulfide bond in all the cyclic peptides synthesized in this work was formed at pH 8.5−9.0 using the high-dilution method37 (0.1 mmol/L) with different proportions of H2O/MeCN mixtures, depending on the hydrophobicity of the peptide. A 0.01 M solution of K3[Fe(CN)]6 (10 mL/0.1 g of peptide) was used as the oxidizing agent.
    After 2 h stirring at pH 8.5−9.0, the pH of the mixture was adjusted to 4 by addition of 10% aqueous AcOH solution, and the ferro- and excess of ferricyanide were removed by stirring the solution with 9 mL settled volume of Amberlite IRA-68 anion-exchange resin. The mixture was stirred for 2 h; later the resin was filtered and washed with AcOH/H2O (95:5) solution (60 mL/0.1 g of peptide). The solution was evaporated down to ca. 100 mL and lyophilized. The resulting cyclic peptide was purified by RP-HPLC using the conditions described above.
    Phosphorylation Assays. All the phosphorylation assays were performed at 25 °C in a final volume of 20 μL of MES buffer (30 mM MES, 10 mM magnesium chloride, and 0.4 mg/mL BSA, pH 6.8) containing a specific PTK, 10 μM [γ-32P]ATP (specific activity, 25 Ci/mmol), and a specific peptide substrate with or without an inhibitor. The concentrations of PTKs used were as follows:  30 units/mL for p60c-src PTK (900 000 units/mg), 90 units/mL for Lyn (6157 units/mg), and 90 units/mL for Lck (2500 units/mg). The reactions were initiated with the addition of the corresponding PTK. Reactions were allowed to proceed for 10 min and were then stopped with 20 μL of 150 mM phosphoric acid. The terminated reaction mixtures (5 μL) were then spotted onto the anion-exchange TLC plates which had been previously soaked with 0.5 M NaCl for 5 min and rinsed with double-distilled water. The loaded TLC plates were air-dried for about 10 min. Chromatography was performed using 15 mM phosphoric acid and 0.25 M NaCl as the mobile phase for about 15 min at room temperature to separate 32P-labeled phosphopeptides from free [γ-32P]ATP and other 32P-labeled components such as [32P]PPi.26 The dried TLC plates were then exposed to the storage phosphor screen for 1 h at room temperature. The exposed screen was read by the 425S PhosphorImager (Molecular Dynamics, Sunnyvale, CA), and the spots corresponding to the phosphopeptides were quantitated. For the background correction, [γ-32P]ATP and a PTK were mixed together in the absence of any peptide substrate and incubated under the same conditions described above. The IC50 values were estimated by nonlinear regression data analysis using the GraFit program (Erithacus Software Ltd., Staines, U.K.).
