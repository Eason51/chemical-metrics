    Abstract

    A new water-soluble conjugate, consisting of a chlorin-e6 photosensitizer part, a 4-arylaminoquinazoline moiety with affinity to epidermal growth factor receptors, and a hydrophilic β-d-maltose fragment, was synthesized starting from methylpheophorbide-a in seven steps. The prepared conjugate exhibited low levels of dark cytotoxicity and pronounced photoinduced cytotoxicity at submicromolar concentrations in vitro, with an IC50(dark)/IC50(light) ratio of ∼368 and a singlet oxygen quantum yield of about 20%. In tumor-bearing Balb/c nude mice, conjugate 1 preferentially accumulates in the tumor tissue. Irradiation of the nude mice bearing A431 xenograft tumors after intravenous administration of the prepared conjugate with a relatively low light dose (50 J/cm2) produced an excellent therapeutic effect with profound tumor regression and low systemic toxicity.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Photodynamic therapy (PDT) was developed more than 30 years ago and has now become a well-proven modality for efficient treatment of various oncological(1,2) and nononcological(3,4) diseases. PDT has been successfully applied for the treatment of many types of cancers including skin, head, stomach, colon, bladder, brain, breast, and lung cancers.(5)
    Generally, PDT is based on the light (650–800 nm) activation of a photosensitizer (PS) in the presence of oxygen, which causes the formation of cytotoxic reactive oxygen species (ROS) such as singlet oxygen (SO) and free radicals.(6) The antitumor mechanism of PDT action includes (a) oxidative damage of tumor tissues leading to necrotic or apoptotic cell death,(7) (b) antiangiogenic effects,(8) and (c) activation of immune responses.(9)
    Although PDT belongs to perspective and innovative approaches in cancer therapy, a number of difficulties hamper its broader application in clinics, e.g., nonselective PS uptake by tumor tissues resulting in undesirable side effects such as prolonged skin and eye phototoxicity, as well as photoallergic reactions.(10) Besides, there are problems related to low efficiency of PDT in the treatment of large solid and disseminated tumors.(11)
    As a solution, a more selective delivery of a PS in the body is worth consideration. The tumor/normal tissue distribution of a perspective therapeutic substance can be tuned by conjugation of biologically active “warheads” (like photosensitizers) with tissue-specific moieties such as antibodies,(12−14) carbohydrates,(15−17) vitamins,(18−20) peptides,(21,22) or ligands of specific cellular receptors.(23−25) Besides, it has been shown that the conjugation of a photosensitizer with another chemotherapeutic compound might give a synergetic dual-mode action with enhanced therapeutic efficacy.(26,27)
    Herein, we describe the synthesis and biological evaluation of the chlorin-e6 conjugate 1 with a 4-arylaminoquinazoline ligand equipped with a hydrophilic maltose moiety (Figure 1a).
    Photosensitizers derived from chlorin-e6 (Figure 2) belong to natural metabolites of chlorophyll-a and can be easily activated within a phototherapeutic window (>650 nm). They ensure efficient ROS generation and are quickly eliminated from the patient’s body. Importantly, these structures also allow relatively straightforward chemical functionalization.(28) Due to these useful features, a range of natural, semisynthetic chlorins (talaporfin,(29) verteporfin,(30) Tookad,(31) and photodithazine(32)) have been developed and clinically applied for the treatment of several types of cancers.
    It is known that rapidly growing tumors tend to express receptors of epidermal growth factor and vascular endothelial growth factor (EGFR and VEGFR, respectively).(33) Among the high-affinity ligands of these receptors are 4-arylaminoquinazolines such as vandetanib [Figure 2, approved in 2011 by Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of late-stage medullary thyroid cancer], which blocks the activity of the corresponding tyrosine kinases and therefore hinders downstream signal transduction.(34−36) To improve the selectivity of chlorin accumulation in tumors, we performed the conjugation of the chlorin-e6 photoactive core with a 4-arylaminoquinazoline ligand, structurally similar to vandetanib, and studied tumor specificity of these conjugates (Figure 1b, structure 2).(37,38) In those studies, we observed good selectivity of compound 2 uptake by EGFR-expressing A431 cancer cell line, as well as pronounced accumulation of 2 in tumor tissues in vivo. The internalization of cationic porphyrins is dramatically dependent on and can be tuned by the number of positive charges and their localization.(39) However, the presence of three positively charged ammonium fragments in the compound, bringing about its aqueous solubility, have led to profound side effects in vivo, making it useless for further development as an antitumor drug.
    Generally, the outcome of PDT highly depends on the lipophilic/hydrophilic profile of a photosensitizer. Excessive lipophilicity leads to the increase of the dark toxicity of a photoactive drug mediated by its aggregation in the biologic liquids. Poorly soluble aggregates accumulate in a patient’s body and induce prolonged light sensitivity.(40) On the other hand, a successful drug has to be sufficiently hydrophobic to cross cellular membranes.(41) In the search for the optimum, a number of porphyrinoid-based glycoconjugates have been reported and evaluated.(42) In some cases, a sugar moiety attached to the porphyrin core provided amphiphilicity of the conjugate, necessary for the desired pharmacokinetic profile of a PDT agent.(43)
    Based on this, we replaced cationic ammonium fragments in conjugate 2 by carbohydrate scaffolds, as shown in structure 1. We also expected that, due to the presence of sugar fragments, conjugate 1 would have affinity to carbohydrate receptors, such as lectine-specific ones, or carbohydrate transporters, widely expressed on the surface of tumor cells.(44)
    To make the target molecule more flexible and therefore better soluble, we used a longer poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) linker, connecting the parts of the molecule through an ester bond. The ester bond is proved to be cleavable under physiological conditions.(45) Thus, esterase-mediated cleavage of conjugate 1 could provide independently acting poorly soluble photosensitizer as well as a 4-arylaminoquinazoline inhibitor of tumor vascularization. Thus, increased antitumor efficiency might be expected.

    Chemistry

    4-Arylaminoquinazoline 3 was synthesized from the vanillic acid according to the known procedure (Scheme 1).(46)
    To evaluate whether ligand 3 plays a certain role in the selective delivery of the conjugate into (V)EGFR-expressing cancer cells, we prepared conjugate 6 of chlorin-e6 with β-d-maltose without a quinazoline fragment (Scheme 3).
    Azide-containing disaccharide 4 was prepared from commercially available β-d-maltose in three steps (Scheme 2). At the first stage, β-d-maltose was acetylated.(47) The following azidation [TMSN3/SnCl4 in dichloromethane (DCM)](48) and protective group cleavage(49) afforded target compound 4 in 64% overall yield.
    Conjugation of azide 4 with alkyne-containing chlorin 5(38) using copper-mediated 1.3-dipolar cycloaddition using a CuSO4 (0.2 equiv)/sodium ascorbate (AscNa) (0.4 equiv) catalytic system in the presence of tris[(1-benzyl-1H-1,2,3-triazol-4-yl)methyl]amine (TBTA) ligand (0.2 equiv) in dimethylformamide (DMF) at 50 °C led to conjugate 6 in 65% overall yield (Scheme 3).
    Notably, conjugate 6 exhibited sufficient solubility in water (∼10–3 mol/L) to be tested in aqueous medium in vivo.
    For the synthesis of conjugate 1 with the EGFR/VEGFR ligand, propargyl-containing pheophorbide 7(38) was subjected to nucleophilic ring-opening(50) under the treatment with 2,2′-(ethylenedioxy)bis(ethylamine), followed by the in situ zinc insertion, giving chlorin metallocomplex 8 in 72% yield after two steps (Scheme 4). The treatment of amine 8 with succinic anhydride led to the quantitative formation of acid 9.
    In the next steps, the Steglich esterification(51) of chlorin-e6-containing building block 9 with quinazoline 3 afforded ester 10 in 58% yield (Scheme 5). Finally, copper(I)-catalyzed alkyne–azide cycloaddition (CuAAC) transformation of alkyne 10 and maltose azide 4 gave conjugate 1 in 88% yield.
    Conjugate 1 demonstrated acceptable solubility in water (∼10–4 mol/L). To evaluate its behavior in biological tests, we prepared a 5% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)–water stock solution that was found to be sufficiently stable for prolonged storage.

    Both compounds manifested strong fluorescence in water with the maxima in the red region (∼646 nm) with the relative quantum yields (to rhodamine 6G) of 1.2 and 6.7% for 1 and 6, respectively. Conjugate 1 demonstrated weaker fluorescence than free photosensitizer 6, which can be attributed to its partial quenching by the arylquinazoline part of the molecule, as well as reduced water solubility and possible aggregation of 1 in the solution.
    The singlet oxygen (SO) generation quantum yields of conjugates 1 and 6 were determined via chemical methods using SO sensitive traps such as 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran (DPBF) for DMSO solution and potassium anthracene-9,10-diyl-bis-methylmalonate (ADMA) for both water and DMSO solutions. The solution of phthalocyanine ZnPc in DMSO was used as a standard (for details, see Experimental Section). The obtained values of SO generation quantum yields in DMSO solution (21% for 1 and 20% for 6) and in aqueous solutions containing 1% DMSO (20% for 1 and 28% for 6) are in good agreement with the literature data for zinc-containing chlorin complexes.(53) Notably, there is no significant difference in SO generation yields between DMSO and aqueous (1% DMSO) solutions of conjugate 1. However, compound 6 generates SO in aqueous solution more efficiently than in DMSO. This difference can be explained by the lesser extent of aggregation of more hydrophilic conjugate 6 in H2O.

    Cellular Uptake Study

    The ability of compounds 1 and 6 to penetrate into and be accumulated by living cells was studied on cell lines with different expression levels of epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR): Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells (EGFR-negative)(54) and A431 (human epidermoid carcinoma, high levels of EGFR expression). The level of the EGFR expression was experimentally proved by flow cytometry with immunofluorescence staining (Figure S19).
    When conjugates 1 and 6 were incubated with CHO and A431 cells, both compounds demonstrated a higher level of accumulation in A431 cells (Figure 4b), with predominant localization in the perinuclear region (Figure 4a). Figure 4b shows the differences in the accumulation of compounds 1 and 6 in A431 and CHO cells qualitatively. As the fluorescence of 6 at maximum is ca. 5–6 times stronger, it can be concluded from the intensity comparison that the concentration of 1 in both cell types is significantly higher than that of 6.
    The co-localization analysis with dyes, specific to particular cellular organelles, has shown that both compounds in living cells are mainly localized in lysosomes and other vesicles, presumably endosomes, as well as in the Golgi apparatus (Figure 5). That allows the active adenosine 5′-triphosphate (ATP)-dependent cellular uptake of 1 and 6 to be assumed.

    Photodynamic Cytostatic Activity in Vitro

    A standard 3-(4,5-dimethyl-2-thiazolyl)-2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetrazole bromide (MTT) assay was used to determine the concentration of 1 and 6 required to inhibit cell viability by 50% after the incubation in the culture medium for 24 h. Along with the dark cytotoxicity, we determined the light cytotoxicity of these compounds under irradiation with a dose of 20 J/cm2 (λ = 655–675 nm, power 32 mW/cm2) (Figure 6).
    Compound 6 exhibited pronounced dark toxicity at concentrations of 30–60 μM (Table 2). Irradiation of cells in the presence of 6 at a dose of 20 J/cm2 led to approximately 40 times increased toxicity; obtained IC50(light) values are about 1 μM for both CHO and A431 cell lines. As expected, conjugate 6 demonstrated in these studies no specificity toward EGFR-positive cell line A431.

    For 4-arylaminoquinazoline-containing molecule 1, we observed remarkably dropped dark toxicity with the increase of photoinduced activity. Thus, conjugate 1 was slightly toxic for A431 cells and nontoxic for the EGFR-negative CHO line in dark conditions. However, it exhibited pronounced light toxicity toward EGFR-positive A431 (Table 2), not in accordance with its cellular uptake (Figure 4b). This might be caused independently by the kinase-inhibiting moiety after its cleavage. In the case of conjugate 1, we observed excellent IC50(dark)/IC50(light) ratios of ∼368 and 250, respectively, for A431 and CHO cells, while the similar parameters for compound 6 were significantly lower (∼41 and 47, respectively).

    In Vivo Antitumor Potency

    After intravenous administration into the nude mice bearing A431 xenograft tumors, conjugate 1 demonstrated predominant tumor-specific accumulation (Figure 7A); the tumor-to-normal tissue ratio was 1.65 (1 h point) and 2 (24 h point) when estimated by epifluorescence imaging. A significant amount of conjugate 1 was observed both in tumor and normal tissues 24 h after injection with almost complete clearance after 4 days.
    Photodynamic therapy (PDT) was performed at 4 h time point when conjugate 1 showed peak tumor accumulation (Figure 7B). Irradiation of the tumor area at a dose 50 J/cm2 (620–655 nm, power density 100 mW/cm2) led to fluorescence decrease (to ∼30% from the starting level) because of photobleaching of conjugate 1 (Figure 7B). Partial recovery of the fluorescence level after the completion of the procedure was recorded.
    Monitoring of tumor behavior proved high potency of performed PDT in the group of animals injected with 1 followed by light irradiation (Figure 8). The growth of the implanted tumors was inhibited with the formation of a scar at the tumor site. The total tumor volume rapidly decreased and did not exceed ∼15% of the tumor volume in the control animal group at 7–9 days after the photodynamic treatment (Figure 8B). Notably, the applied light dose (50 J/cm2) is relatively low in comparison to the common clinical practice (100–150 J/cm2).(55,56)
    Interestingly, conjugate 1 demonstrated moderate therapeutic activity against A431 tumors also without light irradiation; the index of tumor growth inhibition was about 40%. We attribute this effect to the tyrosine kinase-inhibiting activity of the EGFR/VEGFR-targeted ligand. The demonstrated effect of 1 itself was less than the adopted threshold of 50%, admittedly being considered as a significant antitumor activity.(57) However, we presume a synergistic effect of the chlorin part and the EGFR/VEGFR-targeted ligand in compound 1.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, we have designed and synthesized a novel water-soluble conjugate constructed on a base of PDT-active chlorin photosensitizer, linked with a quinazoline EGFR/VEGFR-affine ligand. Tuning of its aqueous solubility was achieved by the introduction of a maltose derivative as a hydrophilic group. As expected, increased toxicity toward EGFR-expressed A431 cancer cells in comparison with the EGFR-negative CHO cell line was observed for the obtained molecule. The new conjugate exhibited an excellent IC50(dark)/IC50(light) ratio of ∼368 for the A431 cell line with photoinduced cytotoxicity at submicromolar concentrations. PDT on nude mice bearing A431 tumors proved this conjugate to be a highly potent photoactive drug. Comparative analysis of light and dark toxicities on animal models demonstrated two independent ways of anticancer action: photoinduced cell toxicity and tumor cell growth inhibition due to the presence of the EGFR-specific quinazoline fragment. These results show that the EGFR-targeted quinazoline–chlorin conjugate 1 is a perspective multimodal agent for PDT with relatively high singlet oxygen quantum yield, excellent dark/light toxicity ratio, and good water solubility.

    General Procedures

    1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on an Agilent DD2 400 MHz spectrometer. Chemical shifts (δ) are reported in parts per million (ppm) for the solution of compounds in CDCl3, DMSO-d6, or CD3OD, with the residual peak of the solvent as an internal reference. J values are in hertz. Mass spectra (MS) were recorded using the matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization (MALDI) method on a time-of-flight Bruker Microflex LT mass-spectrometer and the electrospray ionization (ESI) [high-resolution mass spectrometry (HRMS)] method on a Finnigan MAT 900 spectrometer. Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) analyses were carried out on Merck TLC Silica gel 60 F254. Column chromatography separation was performed using Macherey-Nagel Kieselgel 60 (70–230 mesh). The purity of the targeted compounds was analyzed by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) (Knauer Smartline S2600) using a Si column (Eurospher 100-5 Si, column 250 × 4 mm2) at a flow rate of 0.6 mL/min: method A [gradient mode from MeOH (0.1% Et3N)/DCM (1:1, volume ratio) to MeOH (1% Et3N)/DCM (9:1, volume ratio)] or method B [gradient mode from MeOH (0.1% Et3N)/DCM (3:7, volume ratio) to MeOH (1% Et3N)/DCM (9:1, volume ratio)]. All tested compounds were found to be ≥95% pure. Commercially available reagents (Aldrich, Alfa Aesar) were used without additional purification. Solvents were purified according to the standard procedures. Petroleum ether (PE) used had a boiling point of 40–70 °C.

    Cell Lines and Culturing Conditions

    Cell lines of human epidermoid carcinoma A431 and Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) (both lines obtained from Russian cell culture collection of vertebrates) were cultured in Eagle’s minimum essential medium (MEM) (PanEco, Russia) with 10% (v/v) fetal calf serum (HyClone) and 2 mM l-glutamine in 5% CO2 at 37 °C. At each passaging stage, the cells were treated with trypsin–ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) (1:1) solution (PanEco, Russia). All cells were washed with 10 mM phosphate-buffered saline (PBS).

    Study of Cellular Uptake

    Cells were seeded in glass-bottom 96-well plates (Corning) at the density of 9 × 103 cells per cell with 24 h incubation in 5% CO2 at 37 °C. The medium was then exchanged with fresh serum-free growth medium containing 5 μM of a tested compound (200 μM per well). Cells were incubated for 4 h.
    Cells were imaged using a laser scanning confocal microscope Axio Observer Z1 LSM 710 NLO/Duo (Carl Zeiss, Germany) equipped with C-Apochromat 63× water immersion objective lens with numerical aperture 1.2. Fluorescence was excited at 633 nm and recorded in the range of 650–735 nm.
    The fluorescence intensity of the cytoplasmic region of the cells was measured using the ZEN 2012 program; at least 10 cells in two to three fields of view were analyzed.
    For analysis of intracellular localization, the cells were incubated with the tested compound at 5 μM for 4 h and then stained with the following dyes according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Thermo Fisher Scientific): 0.5 μM LysoTracker Green DND-26, 0.5 μM MitoTracker Green FM, 0.5 μM ER-Tracker, and 5 μM BODIPY FL C5-ceramide complexed to BSA for the Golgi apparatus. Fluorescence of stained organelles was excited by an argon laser at 488 nm and recorded in the range of 500–550 nm.

    Cytotoxicity Study

    The effect of tested compounds on cell viability was estimated using the microculture tetrazoline test (MTT).(60)
    Cells were seeded in 96-well plates at the density of 4 × 103 cells per well with further 24 h incubation. The medium was then exchanged with fresh serum-free growth medium containing tested compounds in different concentrations. After 4 h incubation, the medium was exchanged with full fresh growth medium.
    To estimate the photoinduced toxicity of the tested compound, we exposed the cells to light irradiation (655–675 nm, 32 mW/cm2, 20 J/cm2) using a light-emitting diode (LED) light source providing a homogeneous light distribution in 96-well plates.(61)
    Irradiated cells were then incubated for 24 h before cell viability was measured. For the measurement, the cells were incubated with serum-free medium containing 0.5 mg/mL MTT reagent [3-(4,5-dimethyl-2-thiazolyl)-2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetrazole bromide, Alfa Aesar, U.K.] for 4 h.     The same procedure was performed for the estimation of the dark toxicity of the conjugates, except for that there was no cell exposure to LED light.
    Cell viability was expressed as the ratio of the optical density of treated and untreated cells (in percentage). Three independent experiments (all in triplicate) were performed. Data analysis and calculation of inhibition concentration IC50 were performed using the GraphPad Prism 6 software. To obtain dose–response data and calculate the concentration of half-maximal inhibition (IC50), a four-parameter model for the lognormal distribution was used.

    Three groups of animals were formed: “1” group (administration of 1 without PDT, n = 4); “1 + PDT” group (administration of 1 followed with PDT, n = 3); and control group (administration of the solvent, n = 3).
