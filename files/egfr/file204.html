    Abstract

    We introduce a novel strategy to sample bioactive chemical space, which follows-up on hits from fragment campaigns without the need for a crystal structure. Our results strongly suggest that screening a few hundred or thousand fragments can substantially improve the selection of small-molecule screening subsets. By combining fragment-based screening with virtual fragment linking and HTS fingerprints, we have developed an effective strategy not only to expand from low-affinity hits to potent compounds but also to hop in chemical space to substantially novel chemotypes. In benchmark calculations, our approach accessed subsets of compounds that were substantially enriched in chemically diverse hit compounds for various activity classes. Overall, half of the hits in the screening collection were found by screening only 10% of the library. Furthermore, a prospective application led to the discovery of two structurally novel histone deacetylase 4 inhibitors.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The ability to efficiently access a small molecule to perturb a biological state in a desirable manner stands as one of the long sought after goals in drug discovery. As a first step, an approach that has proven useful in modern drug discovery is to identify a protein that can be targeted in order to reverse a diseased state to a more desirable state. Once a putative protein target has been identified, the next challenge is the identification of chemical matter that binds to it and perturbs its activity. Indeed, vast amounts of resources have been invested in exploring how to best access a small molecule to perturb a target of interest. Although approaches have spanned seemingly unrelated research areas such as virtual docking(1) or de novo drug design(2, 3) in the computational field and high-throughput screening(4) (HTS) or DNA-encoded chemical libraries(5) in the experimental arena, they all share one goal: to efficiently sample as much relevant chemical space as possible in order to access the most desirable ligand for the target at hand. Fragment-based lead discovery (FBLD) is an approach that shares this goal, and in many embodiments represents a synergistic marriage of experimental and computational techniques.(6-8)
    There are a number of advantages of FBLD that have strengthened its place in the modern toolbox of lead discovery. First, for a given sized library of compounds, fragments cover a greater fraction of possible chemical space when compared to typically larger drug-sized compounds.(8) Second, fragments tend to have a high ligand efficiency(9) for a target, and in many cases this high ligand efficiency can be maintained as the fragment is developed into a lead.(10) However, despite these desirable aspects, there are a number of aspects of FBLD that have limited its potential impact on lead discovery. First, in many cases, a cocrystal structure is necessary for fragment hits to be progressed. When the binding mode of a fragment is unknown, the number of possible ways to grow the fragment is seemingly unlimited and can hinder its progression in a drug-discovery setting. Therefore, it is important to fix the fragment in the correct binding mode before growing it. This often relegates fragment screens to targets that have a soakable crystallography system established. Second, even with the aid of a crystal structure, it is often very difficult to follow-up a fragment hit and to grow or evolve it into a leadlike compound. For example, the fragment or analogues of the fragment may not be amenable to synthesis. Third, when a path to growing the fragment can be envisioned, in practice the evolution of a fragment into a leadlike compound is resource-intensive and time-consuming. There is frequently a need for chemical synthesis to access a compound with potency similar to a compound that might be identified through other means such as HTS. Fourth, it is hard to imagine certain types of chemotypes ever being obtained through FBLD, for example, a complex natural product with a few fused rings or a macrocyclic compound. This suggests that FBLD has bounds in terms of what subset of the chemical space it can capture.
    We sought to develop a method that leverages the advantages of FBLD while overcoming a number of barriers that can hinder its success. In the first step of our approach, information about desirable substructures is extracted from a fragment screen, and this information is used to select larger compounds containing these substructures (chemistry-based expansion). In the next step, the larger compounds are tested for activity, and information regarding the biological and biochemical activity enriched in the actives is used to select a subsequent set of small molecules for evaluation (biology-based expansion). At no point is crystallographic information employed in the models. We found that in the first expansion (chemistry-based) the desirable linking of the fragment substructures is revealed. Furthermore, in the second step, we were able to uncover the biological fingerprint of the active hits, and by expanding on the basis of these fingerprints, active compounds were identified that contain chemotypes not covered by the original fragment library. Indeed, some of these chemotypes are complex natural products that one would never imagine finding as a result of an effort initiated by FBLD.
    Overall, a retrospective evaluation of our approach led to the conclusion that, by screening only 10% of the compound collection, half of the hits in the collection can be captured. Furthermore, we successfully employed our approach in the prospective identification of histone deacetylase (HDAC) 4 inhibitors, as reported herein.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    With this work, we wanted to learn if and how FBS hits can be expanded to cover a broad range of bioactive compounds. We tested our novel FBS follow-up strategy using a combination of chemistry- and biology-based expansion on 13 different benchmark sets covering targets from multiple protein families (Table 1). For all but one target, a fragment campaign testing the Novartis fragment core library of ∼1400 fragments was run in-house. For one target, a significantly smaller library of 466 fragments was tested. Furthermore, for all FBS targets, a corresponding HTS campaign was carried out, which allowed us to retrospectively evaluate the ability of our approach to enrich small-molecule subsets with hits and to extrapolate to a chemical space greater than 3 orders of magnitude. To assess better the observed performance of our method, we compared it to two control strategies that used only chemical information for fragment-hit expansion. Special emphasis was given to the ability of the different approaches to sample chemically diverse bioactive molecules. Furthermore, we investigated our method in molecular detail by investigating the cocrystal structures of compounds identified by the method at various steps of the chemical space navigation for a kinase campaign. Finally, we prospectively tested our method against HDAC4.

    Global Performance

    We use an iterative expansion process to select a small-molecule subset for screening (Figure 1A). In the first step, we apply virtual fragment linking(11) (VFL), a method that was introduced to learn bioactivity-relevant substructures from fragments for the subsequent selection of a focused set of small molecules. More precisely, substructures (ECFP4(12) features; see the Methods) are systematically extracted from active and inactive fragments to build a naı̈ve Bayesian (NB) classifier(13) that is applied to rank the small-molecule screening collection. The top 5000 molecules, which typically contain multiple active fragment substructures, are then chosen for testing. In a second expansion step, bioactivity profiles termed HTS fingerprints(14, 15) (HTS-FPs) of active and inactive compounds are used to train a second NB classifier that is ignorant of the chemical structure of the molecules. Rather, the classifier learns activity patterns that distinguish active from inactive compounds based on historical HTS campaigns run at Novartis. The remaining screening collection is subsequently ranked using this classifier. Small molecules that show characteristic bioactivity patterns of active compounds from the tested 5000 compound set are picked for the next screening round. As controls, we tested two methods that employed only chemical expansion (Figure 1B,C).
    To obtain a first performance assessment for each benchmark set and the VFL + HTS-FP method, we selected the top 1, 5, or 10% of the screened collection by two-step expansion and calculated the percentage of all active compounds that was found in the selected subset (Figure 2). Because the total number of screened molecules differed for the 13 benchmark sets, selected compound subsets were also variable in size. For example, 1% of the screened compounds ranged from 8813 molecules for the serine protease set (SER PROT) to 13 257 molecules for the HDAC set. On average, 14.7, 35.3, and 45.6% of all hits were recovered by screening only 1, 5, or 10% of the full sets. For the kinase set SER/THR 4, our method showed a surprisingly low performance compared to other kinase sets. This was most likely due to the combination of a limited fragment library screened in this single case (only 466 fragments) and, for kinases, a comparably low hit rate (Table 1). The low percent of hits found for the HMET set may be a result of different domains being screened in FBS and HTS. The average performance of the 11 remaining sets was 16.4, 38.8, and 49.5%, respectively. Taken together, screening only 10% of the full library led to the discovery of half of the hits.

    Kinase Case Study

    We wanted to explore on a structural level how substructures identified as active from FBS were ultimately incorporated into subsequent compounds identified during expansion. Fragments tend to bind to a hot spot of a protein, and we wondered whether the active fragment substructures contained within small molecules were also located in this region or whether there was no relationship. To do this, we analyzed fragment and small-molecule hits that would be identified by our method (FBS, VFL, HTS-FP) for the SER/THR 1 campaign; the hits had been cocrystallized with either the target of interest or other kinases. Because we cannot disclose the identity of the SER/THR 1 target, a tyrosine kinase-like kinase (TKL), we chose to study hits from this analysis in complex with either other TKL members (interleukin-1 receptor-associated kinase 4 (IRAK-4)) or related tyrosine kinase (TK) members (lymphocyte-specific protein tyrosine kinase (Lck), epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR)).(16) Analysis of fragment cocrystal structures with SER/THR 1 revealed that fragments bound mostly to the hinge region of the kinase, forming hydrogen bonds with backbone amides. Interestingly, enriched fragment substructures in small molecules obtained from VFL also resided in this region, or hot spot, whereas other moieties on the compounds explored other regions of the binding pocket. For example, representative VFL hit 4 is depicted in complex with Lck, where it is apparent that the enriched fragment substructures (colored green in the 2D structure) maintain hydrogen bonds with the Lck backbone in the hot spot (Figure 3A). When biology-based expansion was subsequently used, novel chemotypes such as that demonstrated by staurosporine 5 (Figure 3B) also explore this hot spot as well as other regions of the binding pocket. Furthermore, we realized that compounds that were identified from biological expansion often contained substructures that had been enriched in fragment hits in the hot-spot region while exploring other parts of the binding pocket with substructures that FBS identified as unfavorable for the hotspot. For example, Gefitinib 6, which is depicted in complex with EGFR (Figure 3C), contains favorable fragment substructures that interact intimately with the hinge region as well as unfavorable fragment substructures that reside away from the hot spot. Although it might be due solely to chance that these unfavorable substructures were present in the biological expansion hits, it is notable because the presence of the unfavorable substructures would preclude them from being identified earlier during the chemical expansion. Thus, the biological expansion can “save” compounds containing favorable substructures that might have been missed in the chemical expansion as a result of the concomitant presence of unfavorable substructures because the presence of the latter substructures (whether they reside in the hinge region or not) will lower the overall score of the compound during chemical expansion.
    In summary, this case study illustrates that substructures enriched in FBS hits have been observed to reside in a similar region when contained in compounds identified by VFL. Furthermore, it demonstrates that the subsequent biological expansion identifies novel chemotypes that explore this region, and other regions of the binding pocket, as well as identifying compounds that were missed by VFL because they paired both favorable (exploring hot spot) and unfavorable (exploring other binding-pocket regions) fragment substructures.

    Performance Contributions

    We then wanted to investigate, more generally, how the individual parts of our approach contributed to overall enrichments. For this purpose, numbers of active molecules found by the different steps were analyzed in more detail, and single components of the method were replaced in control calculations.

    (i) VFL

    First, we analyzed the number of active molecules found in the individual expansion steps when a fixed-sized subset of 20 000 molecules was selected per benchmark set (5000 molecules were chosen by chemistry-based expansion and 15 000 molecules were chosen by biology-based expansion; Table 2). In the first step, the minimum number of discovered active compounds was 16 for the set DH. Hence, in all cases, sufficient numbers of active compounds were found to build an active training class for a bioactivity-profile-based small-molecule model. On average, active compounds were 4.4-fold enriched in the sets of 5000 (Table 2), in accordance with the previously demonstrated ability of VFL to extrapolate from fragments to small molecules and enrich subsets with bioactive compounds.(11) In only one instance (IG set) did VFL retrieve less active compounds than expected by random compound selection (i.e., the fraction of active molecules in the selected set was lower than in the entire screening collection). To understand better the failure of VFL in this case, we analyzed the NB classifier built on the active and inactive fragments for the target IG. We observed that only very few substructures obtained positive weights (i.e., were identified as favorable) in the training of the classifier. In other words, there was not much structural consensus among the FBS hits, and not many chemical features could be detected that were enriched among the active fragments. Furthermore, enrichment factors were very modest for the sets HDAC and HMET (1.16 and 1.26, respectively). For HMET, the inability of VFL to lead to a substantial increase in hits might be attributed to different protein domains used to identify ligands in FBS and small-molecule HTS. For SER/THR 4, using only 466 fragments to train the classifier led to a 2-fold enrichment of active compounds.

    (ii) Biology-Based Expansion

    Compound sets were then expanded by training classifiers on the bioactivity profiles of the 5000 tested molecules. Compound subsets selected on the basis of VFL + HTS-FP outperformed sets assembled by selecting the 20 000 compounds best-ranked by VFL (Figure 1B) (i.e., an expansion strategy using the chemical information of the fragments only) by up to 24-fold (Table 2 and Figure 4). Although, from a practical point of view, testing all molecules in one step might be preferred, the results clearly demonstrate that an iterative expansion process increased hit rates substantially. For VFL + HTS-FP, when considering the 20 000 tested compounds as a whole, top enrichment factors of 30.4, 19.9, and 17.2 were obtained for the sets HDME and SER/THR 1 and 2, respectively. To see whether the combination of two expansion steps that use orthogonal views on molecular similarity was crucial to the success of VFL + HTS-FP, we replaced the biology-based expansion step by another chemistry-based expansion step (i.e., we replaced HTS-FPs by structural features (ECFP4 fingerprint) to select 15,000 molecules in the second step (VFL + ECFP4; Figure 1C)). We observed that bioactivity profiles as small-molecule descriptors consistently led to the discovery of more active compounds than structural fingerprints (i.e., VFL + HTS-FP exceeded the performance of VFL + ECFP4) (Figure 4). Importantly, the higher number of retrieved hits also translated to a higher number of discovered chemotypes, here measured as the number of unique Bemis and Murcko scaffolds(17) found among the active molecules. For five data sets, VFL + HTS-FP discovered more than 30% of the active scaffolds in the screened collection by testing only 20 000 molecules (Figure 4). In absolute numbers, a maximum of 2857 different active scaffolds were identified for the set SER/THR 1.
    To visualize how VFL + HTS-FP samples bioactive chemical space, we projected active compounds in three dimensions using multidimensional scaling (MDS; see Methods)(18) on the basis of pairwise compound structure similarities (Figure 5). Active molecules (all points in the plot) were colored according to whether they had been discovered by fragment-hit expansion or not. Compounds identified by chemistry-based expansion (VFL) were colored in red, compounds found by biology-based expansion (HTS-FP) were colored in green, and active compound that remained unidentified were colored in blue. For the data set IG, active compounds populated two distinct regions of chemical space (Figure 5A). Chemistry-based expansion of fragment hits was able to identify only active compounds concentrated in a very narrow area of chemical space. However, as nonstructural descriptors, HTS-FPs in biology-based expansion were able to reach out to active compounds further away in chemical space (Figure 5A, left), and chemically very diverse hits populating both active islands in chemical space were found.

    (iii) Differences in Sampling of Chemical Space by Chemistry- and Biology-Based Expansions

    By contrast, when structural descriptors were used for the second expansion step (strategy VFL + ECFP4; here molecules shown in green were discovered by the second chemistry-based expansion step), the discovered active compounds mainly surrounded the hits found by the first expansion step, and only a very small and focused region of the bioactive chemical landscape was explored (Figure 5A, right). Accordingly, VFL + HTS-FP sampled a much broader region of chemical space than VFL + ECFP4 that used chemical information only. Similar trends were found for HDAC (Figure 5B). The hits found by VFL clustered together in a very narrow region of chemical space. Whereas biology-based expansion was able to retrieve hits from very different regions in chemical space (Figure 5B, left), a second chemistry-based expansion step mainly led to the discovery of active compounds that were closely located to each other (Figure 5B, right) These observations show that it is the combination of orthogonal views on molecular similarity (chemical and biological) in the two hit-expansion steps that is crucial for a successful follow-up on fragment hits.

    (iv) Screening Set Sizes

    For SER/THR 4, screening of only 466 fragments still led to a 2-fold enrichment of hits by VFL. To analyze required fragment library sizes for our approach more systematically, we recalculated results for VFL + HTS-FP for a test set of 20 000 molecules when only half of the Novartis core library was used in FBS. (For each of the 12 data sets that had used the full fragment library, three trials with a random selection of only half of the fragments were carried out, and results were averaged over the three trials; Supporting Information Table S1.) Perhaps surprisingly, numbers of active compounds found by VFL were very similar for the differently sized input libraries. Screening a few hundred fragments generally led to the identification of enough active fragments that patterns of chemically favorable substructures emerged, and meaningful classifiers could be built to prioritize active small molecules for testing.
    Moreover, to explore whether the number of small molecules selected by chemistry-based expansion and used as input for biology-based expansion is a decisive factor in VFL + HTS-FP, we increased the number of molecules selected by VFL to 10 000 (i.e., the overall tested 20 000 molecules were now equally split into two sets of 10 000 compounds). Hit numbers were comparable to those achieved by the 5000/15 000 compound subset split, although a slight preference for the original split could be observed (Supporting Information Figure S1 and Table S2). By contrast, decreasing the number of compounds selected by VFL to 500 or 1000 generally led to a substantial loss in the number of active molecules found after biological expansion to 20 000 molecules (Supporting Information Figure S1). Still, active compounds were enriched in comparison to a random compound selection, especially if only a few hundred or thousand compounds were selected in the biological expansion. Reducing the numbers of molecules screened in the individual steps might be desirable for academia or start-up companies that do not have the same screening capacities as large pharmaceutical companies. Therefore, to provide guidelines and set expectations for how many hits can be obtained with a reduced number of screened molecules, we report enrichment factors and absolute numbers of active molecules for different combinations of smaller-sized screening sets in step 1 and step 2 in Supporting Information Table S3.

    Comparison to Current Practice

    The current practice in lead-discovery projects in industry is to screen a diverse set of small molecules to find initial hits and then to expand using chemical similarity. To assess whether screening a focused set designed by chemical expansion of fragment hits had a significant benefit over a rather random selection of small molecules to begin the lead-discovery workflow, we repeated our experiments, but this time we chose 5000 random compounds for the first round of testing that were then expanded by HTS-FP or structural fingerprints (strategies random + HTS-FP and random + ECFP4). Consistent with the observation that hits were generally enriched within the VFL-based selected subsets of 5000 molecules, VFL + HTS-FP performed mostly better than random + HTS-FP (higher hit rates in 9 of 13 data sets; Supporting Information Figure S2A and Table S4). The most notable exception that strongly favored a random selection of initially tested compounds was the data set IG for which VFL had failed. Strikingly, the picture was completely different when chemical similarity was used for small-molecule expansion. Here, a random selection of compounds led to much higher enrichments of active molecules (Supporting Information Figure S2B and Table S4). Because random compound selection in the first step led to the identification of rather structurally diverse active molecules, the subsequent chemistry-based expansion was not biased toward a particular region but was able to retrieve active molecules scattered over large parts of chemical space (Supporting Information Figure S3).
    Accordingly, to alleviate the strong bias toward structurally similar molecules introduced by VFL-based compound selection, we decided to change our selection strategy in a control experiment: instead of selecting the top 5000 molecules, we picked the 5000 most diverse among the top-ranking 50 000 compounds. Indeed, in line with our hypothesis, improved hit rates were obtained for chemistry-based expansion (Supporting Information Figure S2B and Table S4) and almost reached hit rates observed with our VFL + HTS-FP method. By contrast, biology-based expansion did not further benefit from a more diverse hit set from the first step because the method was already able to hop in chemical space and was less affected by the chemical location of the hit molecules that were expanded (Supporting Information Figure S2A and Table S4).
    Taken together, considering all possible strategies to sample bioactive chemical space, best enrichments of active compounds and structural diversity were obtained when FBS was carried out and followed by VFL and HTS-FP-based expansion. VFL led to an enrichment of active compounds in the chemistry-based expansion, and this additional information about active molecules in the training set was then exploited by the biology-based classifier in the second expansion step. Because this step is structure-independent, it reached out to different regions of chemical space. Thus, our method retained knowledge gained from the FBS screen (using VFL as the first expansion step) and at the same time retrieved diverse bioactive compounds (using HTS-FP as the second expansion step). The ability of VFL + HTS-FP to hop in chemical space also has direct implications for the design of fragment libraries, which is currently often focused on diversity, to have as many fragment chemotypes to follow up on. With our findings in mind, it may be more desirable to focus on diversifying a subset of chemotypes in terms of their substitution patterns and substituent types. Once active fragments have been identified, more chemotypes will be discovered during the biological expansion (using HTS-FP), so it is not necessary to include all of these chemotypes in the initial library. Of course, one caveat that one has to keep in mind is that the same fragment library is usually screened against a range of diverse targets. Hence, including less chemotypes in the initial library might come at the risk of identifying very few or no hits for some projects.

    Prospective Testing: Identification of Novel HDAC4 Inhibitors

    For histone deacetylase 4 (HDAC set in the benchmark system), we prospectively applied the biology-based expansion step in VFL + HTS-FP. Out of ∼150 000 compounds not originally screened, we predicted 47 small molecules to be active, which we subsequently screened in an HDAC4 fluorescence-intensity assay. Two compounds showed substantial activity: in dose–response experiments, IC50 values of 6.6 and 9.0 μM were determined for 3-benzoyl-6-chloro-7-(2,4-dioxopentan-3-yl)-1-benzofuran-4,5-dione (7) and 2-chloro-3-[(4-methylphenyl)amino]-1,4-naphthoquinone (8), respectively (Figure 6).
    Remarkably, the two identified hits 7 and 8 had very low structural similarity to all active fragments and small molecules found in the chemistry-based expansion of the VFL + HTS-FP approach; the highest ECFP4 Tanimoto coefficient(19) (Tc; see Methods) between one of the hits and an active reference structure was 0.239. Similarly, the hit structures were very different from all publicly known HDAC4 inhibitors in the ChEMBL(20) and GVK(21) databases. Supporting Information Figure S4 displays the closest structural neighbor for both hit compounds determined on the basis of ECFP4 fingerprints and Tanimoto similarity. Both compounds lack acidic groups, which raises the hypothesis that they possess a novel binding mode. For 7, the Michael acceptor moiety and/or the 4,5-dione motif, which overlap, appear to be important for activity because we also tested the aromatic hydroquinone analog 3-(3-benzoyl-6-chloro-4,5-dihydroxy-1-benzofuran-7-yl)pentane-2,4-dione (9), which did not show any detectable activity (Figure 6). We also tested two analogs of 8, 2-amino-3-(4-chloroanilino)naphthalene-1,4-dione (10) and 2-chloro-3-[(4-nitrophenyl)amino]naphthalene-1,4-dione (11). We found that replacing the methyl by a nitro group at the para position of the phenyl ring led to a slight increase in activity (IC50 value of 4.8 μM for 10). By contrast, replacing this methyl group by a chlorine atom while simultaneously changing the chlorine substituent at the naphthoquinone moiety to an amino group led to activity loss (no detectable activity for 11; Figure 6). With a molecular weight as low as 297.7 Da, 8 has a good binding-efficiency index(9) (BEI) of 16.95, which is close to the reported median BEI of 18.4 calculated for leads in successful drug-discovery programs (BEI is calculated as the ratio of the pIC50 to the molecular weight in kDa).(22)
    Although the size of the tested compound set was very small and cannot provide statistical significance, it should be noted that the performance of the HTS-FP expansion matched the hit rates observed in benchmark calculations. For the HDAC set, a hit rate of 3.8% was observed for the biology-based expansion step in retrospective simulated virtual-screening experiments; in the prospective evaluation, the discovery of two active ligands among 47 tested compounds corresponds to a hit rate of 4.3%. Furthermore, it should be noted that the two identified inhibitors were confirmed as hits in multiple dose–response measurements, whereas the hit definition in the benchmarking system was based on single-dose experiments (see Methods).

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    FBS has become increasingly utilized in the pharmaceutical industry. Different strategies have been introduced to grow binders from FBS into larger, more potent molecules. However, in many cases, the sampled chemical space is still limited by the size and diversity of the fragment library. We have therefore developed an in silico workflow that, on the basis of VFL and HTS-FP, departs from fragments and arrives at diverse chemotypes not necessarily assembled from active fragment substructures. X-ray structures demonstrate our method’s ability to find bioactive compounds that were informed by, but not limited to, fragment-binding modes. A prospective application of our approach revealed two novel histone deacetylase 4 inhibitors that were structurally very distinct from all active fragments and small molecules found by chemistry-based expansion.
    HTS-FPs that are used in the second step of our approach are derived from Novartis screening campaigns and are proprietary. However, with increasing amounts of screening data being released to the public domain, similar bioactivity profiles can be generated from public sources, such as the NCI60 data set(23) and HTS data in the PubChem bioassay database.(24) Even more data is anticipated to be available in the future because many open-access screening efforts are ongoing (e.g., Lilly’s PDD initiative(25) or the LINCS project at Harvard Medical School(26)). Hence, the methodology presented in this article is applicable in both academic and industrial institutions.
    Our method offers an alternate path that may adapt FBS to drug-discovery settings where other lead-discovery methods might get more attention otherwise. We could demonstrate that by screening about 10% of the screening collection we can expect to find half of the primary hits contained in the screening deck. That being said, we currently consider the introduced VFL + HTS-FP approach best practice for the integration of FBS and small-molecule screening into a common workflow.

    Virtual Fragment Linking and HTS Fingerprints

    Virtual fragment linking was introduced by Crisman et al. to bridge the gap between low-affinity fragment hits and larger, generally more potent, small molecules.(11) The key idea is to learn structural features that distinguish active from inactive fragments and then prioritize small-molecule structures for screening in which these features are enriched. For doing so, circular atom environments (ECFP4)(12) are calculated for active and inactive fragments and used as molecular descriptors to train a naı̈ve Bayes classifier. Subsequently, the Bayes classifier is applied to rank small molecules according to their likelihood of being active.
    HTS-FPs are primary-assay-based compound-bioactivity profiles that have been introduced by Petrone et al.(14) They capture the activity of a compound across 234 biochemical and cellular assays that have historically been run at Novartis and can be used to compare molecules based on their previously profiled interaction with the proteome. To have a consistent reference frame across all assays, activity outcomes for a compound are reported as Z scores. Because the screening library is constantly changing with new compounds being added and others being depleted, most compounds have not been tested in all assays and hence their bioactivity profile vectors are incomplete and contain missing values.

    Small-Molecule Subset-Selection Strategies

    To explore the utility of fragment-based screening as starting point to design focused small-molecule subsets, we evaluated the following strategies to follow-up on FBS and cherry-pick N compounds from the full screening deck for experimental testing.
    (i)

    VFL + HTS-FP: The N compounds are picked in an iterative fashion by screening two subsets. First, the M best compounds from VFL are selected and tested. HTS-FPs of assayed active and inactive molecules are then used as descriptors to train a second Bayes classifier. The remaining screening database is ranked according to this classifier, and the best N–M compounds are picked for experimental testing.

    (ii)

    VFL: The N top-ranked compounds from VFL are selected.

    (iii)

    VFL + ECFP4: Same strategy as VFL + HTS-FP, but this time ECFP4 fingerprints are used instead of HTS-FPs to train the second Bayes classifier.

    In this study, presented results were mostly obtained with M set to 5000. For this setting, explored values for N ranged approximately from 9000 to 130 000.

    Data Sets

    To assess the ability of our different strategies to efficiently sample bioactive chemical space, we assembled 13 benchmark data sets. For 12 sets, the Novartis fragment core library consisting of about 1400 structures was screened against a specific protein target, whereas for one data set a smaller collection of 466 fragments overlapping only partly with the core library was tested. For a fragment, the average number of heavy atoms and the average molecular weight were 14.8 and 211.7 Da, respectively. Furthermore, for each target, a high-throughput campaign had been run at Novartis (Table 1). Hit numbers varied significantly (i.e., between 23 and 642 active fragments were found for different proteins). All assays considered in this study were cell-free and designed to detect direct target-ligand interactions. For the set HMET, a single protein domain was used in the FBS campaign, whereas the corresponding HTS campaign was run on a protein complex containing this domain as a subunit. A fragment was considered a hit if project teams considered it to be a hit in at least one technology. For example, if a biochemical and NMR screen were employed for a given campaign, all hits from either technology were considered hits for model building. The cutoffs for calling a fragment a hit for a given technology were chosen by each of the individual project teams. For small molecules, a Z score equal to or smaller than −3 was used as the hit-calling criterion. Furthermore, two different small-molecule sets were created for each protein target. Because we used primary assay data, we were careful not to bias our results by false positives so that, in addition to the full set of molecules tested against a target in an HTS campaign, we built a subset that excluded all compounds with a hit rate of at least 10% across the 234 assays in the HTS-FP. However, results for sets with or without these frequent hitters were very similar. Hence, for brevity, only data for sets excluding frequently hitting molecules were reported herein, with an exception being made for the kinase case study. Because a lot of kinase assays are included in the 234 HTS-FP assays, compounds that inhibit multiple kinases can easily be discarded by a rather conservative 10% hit rate cutoff for the identification of frequent hitters, although they are true kinase binders. Therefore, the original nonreduced data set was used for this case study, for which reported results were obtained by selecting 5000 molecules by chemistry-based expansion and 15 000 molecules by biology-based expansion.

    Calculations and Implementation

    For each data set, the three described strategies (VFL + HTS-FP, VFL, and VFL + ECFP4) were applied in simulated virtual-screening trials to assemble small-molecule subsets of 20 000 molecules. Numbers of active molecules in selected subsets were determined retrospectively, and enrichment factors over the number of hits expected by a random selection of 20 000 molecules were calculated. Furthermore, control calculations that replaced the chemistry-based expansion of fragment hits by random compound subset selection were carried out. For each data set, three different random subsets of 5000 molecules were assembled, and the results were averaged over the three independent trials. NB classifiers were built with PipelinePilot v8.0(27) using standard settings and allowing missing descriptor values for HTS-FPs. Perl scripts were used to organize the results and facilitate the analysis.
    To visualize bioactive chemical space for individual data sets, we represented molecules by their ECFP4 fingerprints and calculated coordinates in 3D space using MDS, a dimension-reduction technique that tries to transform a set of data points to fewer dimensions while retaining the distances between objects as accurately as possible. Coordinates in 3D space were determined with the R Classical MDS component in Pipeline Pilot and default settings that minimize the sum of the squared differences between the distances in the original and the new coordinate systems.

    Identification of HDAC4 Inhibitors

    We prospectively evaluated the biology-based expansion step of our VFL + HTS-FP approach on the HDAC4 data set. We expanded hits from an HDAC4 fragment campaign by VFL and selected the 10 000 highest-scoring small molecules that were part of the small-molecule set reported in Table 1 and that had been tested in an HDAC4 HTS campaign in the past. Forty eight active compounds (Z score ≤ −3) were among the selected subset and then served as a positive training class for an HTS-FP Bayes classifier, whereas the remaining 9952 inactive compounds served as negative training examples. The classifier was used to rank 157 005 compounds that had not been tested previously in the HDAC4 campaign. We selected the top 160 compounds and calculated Bemis and Murcko scaffolds.(16) For each scaffold, we just retained the highest-scoring compound, thereby reducing the list to 95 molecules. We then discarded structures that could possibly interfere with a fluorescent assay readout using the PAINS(28) substructure and internal filters. Of the remaining 78 compounds, 47 were in stock with sufficient quantity for experimental testing. Each compound was tested at eight different concentrations up to 10 μM in an HDAC4 fluorescence-intensity assay (see Experimental Section). Two of the 47 compounds showed moderate activity against HDAC4 and were both retested at higher concentrations together with three structural analogs. On the basis of dose–response experiments with compound concentrations up to 30 μM (see Supporting Information Figure S5 for dose–response curves), IC50 values of 6.6, 9.0, and 4.8 μM were determined for 7, 8, and 10, respectively.
    For structural comparisons to known HDAC4 inhibitors, compounds were represented by ECFP4 fingerprints, and pairwise Tc values were calculated. The Tc value for a compound pair is the ratio of the number of structural features shared by two molecules to the union of structural features present in the two molecules.(18)

    Experimental Section

    Compound Data

    The tested compounds 7, 9, 10, and 11 were available in the Novartis corporate compound collection, whereas 8 was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (S703729; CAS number: 62101-46-6) and used without further purification. Purity was confirmed by 1H NMR analysis of this known compound.(29) The four compounds from the Novartis library were >95% pure as analyzed via LC–MS and 1H NMR (Supporting Information Figure S6).

    HDAC Assay

    HDAC4 activity was detected by a two-step enzymatic assay based on successive cleavages of a substrate peptide. An ε-trifluoroacetylated (TFA) peptide (Ac-Gly-Ala-Lys(TFA)-AMC) was used as an HDAC class IIA-specific substrate.(30) First, the trifluoroacetyl group on the ε-amino groups of lysine was cleaved by HDAC4, which unmasked the trypsin cleavage site. In a second step, the deacetylated substrate was cleaved by trypsin, resulting in the release of fluorogenic AMC (7-amino-4-methylcoumarin). HDAC4 inhibition by an added compound generally results in a decreasing amount of fluorogenic AMC.
    All enzyme reactions were performed in reaction buffer consisting of the following components (final concentrations): 25 mM Tris, pH 8.0, 137 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl, 0.05% (w/v) BSA, 0.005% (v/v) Triton-X-100, and 1 mM MgCl2.
    Compounds were diluted in 384-well compound plates to obtain eight-point serial dilutions for 40 test compounds in 90% DMSO as well as 4 reference compounds plus 16 high controls and 16 low controls. Assay plates were prepared by dispensing 50 nL of compound solutions into 384-well plates (Greiner, reference 784900) using a Hummingbird nanoliter dispenser (Cartesian Technologies Inc.). All following pipetting steps were carried out with the help of a nanodrop (Innovadyne Technologies Inc.), and the fluorescence detection step was performed on a Pherastar fluorescence reader (BMG).
    Recombinant HDAC4 was expressed in full-length form (a.a. 2–1084) in Sf9 insect cells (obtained from ATCC) using baculovirus generated with the Bac-to-Bac system (Invitrogen). A 0.15 mg/mL stock solution was produced and stored at −80 °C. Trypsin was purchased from Worthington Biochemical Corporation (no. 3740). A 20 mg/mL stock solution was prepared in 1 mM HCl, which was stored at −20 °C or used as a fresh solution prepared in assay buffer. The substrate (Ac-Gly-Ala-Lys(TFA)-AMC) was purchased from Biosyntan. A 10 mM stock solution prepared in 90% DMSO was stored at −20 °C.
    Enzyme reactions were prepared as follows: 4.5 μL of a 10 μM peptide substrate solution and 4.5 μL of a 0.1 nM enzyme solution were added to the assay plates containing 50 nL of compound solutions, and the plates were incubated for 120 min 30 °C. Enzyme reactions were terminated by the addition of 4.5 μL of assay buffer containing a pan-HDAC inhibitor at high concentration and 100 nM trypsin for 15 min at 30 °C. Fluorescence-intensity readout was at 450 nm (10 nm bandpass) following excitation at 350 nm.

        Lahm, A.; Paolini, C.; Pallaoro, M.; Nardi, M. C.; Jones, P.; Neddermann, P.; Sambucini, S.; Bottomley, M. J.; Lo Surdo, P.; Carfi, A.; Koch, U.; De Francesco, R.; Steinkuehler, C.; Gallinari, P.
        Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America (2007), 104 (44), 17335-17340CODEN: PNASA6; ISSN:0027-8424. (National Academy of Sciences)
        Previous findings have suggested that class IIa histone deacetylases (HDACs) (HDAC4, -5, -7, and -9) are inactive on acetylated substrates, thus differing from class I and IIb enzymes. Here, we present evidence supporting this view and demonstrate that class IIa HDACs are very inefficient enzymes on std. substrates. We identified HDAC inhibitors unable to bind recombinant human HDAC4 while showing inhibition in a typical HDAC4 enzymic assay, suggesting that the obsd. activity rather reflects the involvement of endogenous copurified class I HDACs. Moreover, an HDAC4 catalytic domain purified from bacteria was 1,000-fold less active than class I HDACs on std. substrates. A catalytic Tyr is conserved in all HDACs except for vertebrate class IIa enzymes where it is replaced by His. Given the high structural conservation of HDAC active sites, we predicted the class IIa His-Nε2 to be too far away to functionally substitute the class I Tyr-OH in catalysis. Consistently, a Tyr-to-His mutation in class I HDACs severely reduced their activity. More importantly, a His-976-Tyr mutation in HDAC4 produced an enzyme with a catalytic efficiency 1,000-fold higher than WT, and this "gain of function phenotype" could be extended to HDAC5 and -7. We also identified trifluoroacetyl-lysine as a class IIa-specific substrate in vitro. Hence, vertebrate class IIa HDACs may have evolved to maintain low basal activities on acetyl-lysines and to efficiently process restricted sets of specific, still undiscovered natural substrates.
