    Abstract

    There are currently no approved disease-modifying osteoarthritis (OA) drugs (DMOADs). The aggrecanase ADAMTS-5 is key in the degradation of human aggrecan (AGC), a component of cartilage. Therefore, ADAMTS-5 is a promising target for the identification of DMOADs. We describe the discovery of GLPG1972/S201086, a potent and selective ADAMTS-5 inhibitor obtained by optimization of a promising hydantoin series following an HTS. Biochemical activity against rat and human ADAMTS-5 was assessed via a fluorescence-based assay. ADAMTS-5 inhibitory activity was confirmed with human aggrecan using an AGC ELISA. The most promising compounds were selected based on reduction of glycosaminoglycan release after interleukin-1 stimulation in mouse cartilage explants and led to the discovery of GLPG1972/S201086. The anticatabolic activity was confirmed in mouse cartilage explants (IC50 < 1.5 μM). The cocrystal structure of GLPG1972/S201086 with human recombinant ADAMTS-5 is discussed. GLPG1972/S201086 has been investigated in a phase 2 clinical study in patients with knee OA (NCT03595618).

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Human osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common form of arthritis and a major cause of disability worldwide.(1) Current clinical management is inadequate and mainly focused on symptom relief using drugs often associated with side effects.(2)
    OA is a whole-organ disease characterized by the progressive degradation of cartilage and significant alterations in subchondral bone and synovium.(3)
    Aggrecan (AGC) is the major structural proteoglycan found in articular cartilage. In OA, increased aggrecanase activity is observed.(4) Inhibition of pathological aggrecanolysis has therefore been proposed as a promising therapeutic approach to delay or even halt OA progression.
    A disintegrin and metalloproteinase with thrombospondin motifs (ADAMTS) subtype 5 (ADAMTS-5) is a member of a family of zinc-dependent endopeptidases with key functions in organ and tissue homeostasis.(5,6) In mice, genetic deletion of ADAMTS-5 resulted in protection from cartilage degeneration, reduction of subchondral bone thickening, and attenuation of OA-induced mechanical allodynia.(7−10) ADAMTS-5 gene polymorphisms have been associated with human OA susceptibility,(11) while in human OA cartilage explants, silencing of ADAMTS-5 by siRNA led to reduced aggrecan loss.(12) Based on this evidence, ADAMTS-5 is a promising molecular target for the discovery of disease-modifying OA drugs (DMOADs), agents that have so far failed to achieve regulatory approval.
    To our knowledge, despite several drug development programs, only one ADAMTS-5 small molecule inhibitor has entered clinical development for the treatment of OA, AGG-523, a dual ADAMTS-5/4 inhibitor developed by Wyeth/Pfizer (Table 1).(13−20) Several pharmaceutical companies have opted to develop monoclonal antibodies that neutralize ADAMTS-5,(21) demonstrating the challenge to develop small molecules targeting this enzyme.
    Table 1. Activity, Selectivity, and Binding Mode of Advanced ADAMTS-5 Inhibitors Developed for Oral Treatment
    Nonselective inhibition of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) results in musculoskeletal syndrome,(22) indicating that selectivity over other MMPs and A disintegrin and metalloproteinases (ADAMs) is an essential requirement for the development of a well-tolerated ADAMTS-5 inhibitor for OA.(6) Owing to the nature of classical zinc binding groups (carboxylic acid, hydroxamic acids, and reverse hydroxamate) and the high lipophilicity featured by most of the inhibitors of this target class, identifying candidates with moderate plasma protein binding and good oral PK properties has been challenging.(23)
    Here, we report the discovery, SAR, ADME, PK, and selectivity profile data for GLPG1972/S201086, a novel, potent, selective, orally available ADAMTS-5 inhibitor that has been investigated in a phase 2 clinical trial (NCT03595618) in patients with knee OA. GLPG1972/S201086, further referred to as GLPG1972, was codeveloped by Galapagos and Servier.

    Hit Identification

    An HTS campaign was prepared for approximately 50 000 compounds. With the goal to enrich this screening library with metalloproteinase-like inhibitors, a ligand-based virtual screening workflow was implemented. Its core component comprised 2 elements. The first was a structural filter aiming at selecting compounds with zinc binding group (ZBG) moieties (carboxylic acid, hydroxamic acid, hydantoin, trifluoromethyl-ketone, sulfonamide, etc.). The second was a two-class categorization model (Naı̈ve Bayes classifier) built with PipelinePilot (Dassault Systems BIOVIA, version 8.0, San Diego, CA) using extended-connectivity fingerprints (ECFP6),(24) as descriptors for 212 metalloproteinase inhibitors (IC50 < 1 μM) extracted from PubChem (“active”) and 6891 inactive compounds of similar molecular weights (“inactive”). The area under the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve of this model was 0.95, characterizing an excellent discriminatory power (0.5 for random, 1 ideally).(25) For instance, by setting a selecting cutoff at 4.26, a good split between active (194 true positives and 18 false negatives) and inactive (640 false positives and 6251 true negatives) compounds was obtained.
    The HTS campaign was based on inhibition of ADAMTS-5 protease activity using a fluorescent-based assay and was initially conducted using the matured form of rat recombinant ADAMTS-5 (rn-ADAMTS-5; AA 262–564). Hits were confirmed by dose–response curves using human recombinant ADAMTS-5 (hs-ADAMTS-5; AA 262–567).
    Following the HTS campaign, a hydantoin series originating from the statistical model was identified, and its activity was confirmed after resynthesis. While NH hydantoins had been reported as ZBGs,(26,27) they have been less explored than carboxylic and hydroxamic acids as metalloproteinases inhibitors. Interestingly, during the course of our investigations, Eli Lilly also reported a hydantoin ADAMTS-5 inhibitor.(16,20)
    Our series displayed a similar potency against rn- and hs-ADAMTS-5 (Figure S1). The properties of compound 1, selected as the hit compound from the hydantoin series, are summarized in Table 2. Compound 1 had a very attractive overall profile, displaying promising ADME properties, low PPB, druglike physicochemical properties, and multiple vectors for potency and selectivity optimization. The hit was assessed in a panel of MMPs and did not show counter-selectivity (Table 2).

    Hypothesized Binding Mode of Compound 1

    Since compound 1 was evaluated as a racemic mixture, one of the earliest questions to be addressed was whether the stereochemistry of the chiral carbon had an impact on the on-target potency and what would be the absolute configuration of the active stereoisomer. Based on the high-resolution X-ray structures of ADAMTS-5 published earlier by Pfizer(28,29) and Wyeth,(30) docking studies could provide a clear answer. Metalloproteinases, including MMPs, ADAMs, and ADAMTSs, exhibit a homologous fold and a characteristic active site with three histidines (numbered 410, 414, and 420 in human ADAMTS-5) coordinating a zinc ion. Assisted by a conserved glutamic acid residue (Glu411 in this case), this zinc ion polarizes and activates a water molecule enabling its reaction with a peptide bond of the substrate (proteoglycans in this case). The substrate itself is bound in an extended conformation as if it prolonged a beta-sheet of the catalytic domain by one additional beta strand. Specificity is provided by several pockets occupied by the side chains of substrate residues. By convention, protease pockets are numbered S1, S2, and S3 from the cleaved bond to the N-terminal residue and S′1, S′2, and S′3 from the cleaved bond to the C-terminal. The S′1 pocket of metalloproteinases in particular is typically well-defined and relatively hydrophobic and varies significantly between members of the superfamily, which explains why it is often targeted to improve both potency and selectivity.(14) In order to dock compound 1 to an X-ray structure of ADAMTS-5 (PDB ID 3B8Z), two constraints were used: first, the hydantoin moiety should bind in a similar way to an ADAM17 (aka TACE) inhibitor published earlier (PDB ID 3L0 V),(27) adopting a tautomer form that allows hydrogen bonding with Glu411 and coordination of the zinc by the N-acyl amide nitrogen. An additional hydrogen bond between the remaining hydantoin NH and Gly380 locks the hydantoin ring in a well-defined orientation. The second constraint imposed that the carbonyl of the piperazine-amide acts as a hydrogen bond acceptor orienting the phenyl-piperazine group in the S′1 pocket described above. Upon docking (Dassault Systems BIOVIA, Discovery Studio Modeling Environment, Release 3.1, San Diego: Dassault Systems, 2011), only the R configuration could meet both geometrical requirements as shown in Figure 1. Indeed, if the first condition is met for the S enantiomer, it is impossible to also meet the second condition since the hydantoin substitution would point 180° away from the direction of the S′1 pocket.

    SAR Optimization

    Phenyl Substitution

    Based on the docking studies, the S′1 pocket was occupied by the aryl-piperazine moiety. Given that only limited space appeared to be available in the vicinity of the piperazine group, we first explored phenyl substitution and replacement (Table 3). Our SAR optimization indicated that halogen atoms and small groups were preferred in the para position, with CF3 being tolerated, but not OMe (compounds 2–6). A broad range of substituents (halogen atoms, OMe, large Ph groups, etc.) were tolerated in the meta position (compounds 7–10), whereas ortho substitution tended to reduce the potency (compounds 11 and 12). Furthermore, phenyl replacement by cycloalkyl or heteroaryl groups was associated with a decrease in potency, except for the 3-pyridine (compounds 13–16). Fluoro and chloro substituents in meta and para positions resulted in the best potency, and disubstitution afforded an increased potency of one log unit (compounds 17 and 18).

    Hydantoin C5-Substitution

    Human AGC Neoepitope ELISA

    To further evaluate the potency of the series, key compounds were tested in an ELISA assay which evaluates the cleavage of full-length AGC. The ELISA assay more accurately captures the activity of inhibitors on the target as it involves the full-length human ADAMTS-5 protein (vs truncated form for the fluorescent-based assay) and AGC, an endogenous substrate of ADAMTS-5.
    The activity of the hydantoin series in the human AGC neoepitope ELISA was confirmed and found to be comparable to the activity measured in our fluorescent-based assay. Thus, for compound 28, an IC50 of 29 nM was measured in the AGC ELISA assays. When the assay was performed in the presence of human serum, a limited decrease in activity was observed, which nicely reflects the moderate PPB of representative compounds 19, 21, and 28 (Figure 3 and Table 7).

    Selectivity versus Other Proteases

    [R]-18 and 29, two analogues with different substitutions on the C5 hydantoin and in the aryl region, were evaluated against a broad panel of proteases in a biochemical assay: ADAMTS-1, and −4; tumor necrosis factor-α-converting enzyme (TACE); and MMP-1, −2, −7, −9, −13, and −14. These proteases were selected to cover closely related, as well as various families of proteases (Table 8).
    Table 8. Activity of Two Representative Compounds across a Panel of Proteases
    The selectivity of these two compounds was moderate against ADAMTS-4 (2–12 fold) but excellent across the rest of the panel of proteases assessed. The selectivity ratio, defined as IC50 against protease X divided by IC50 against ADAMTS-5, for compound 29 was at least 12 against ADAMTS-4 and at least 19 against the other proteases tested. In addition, a larger set of compounds was assessed against ADAMTS-1, TACE, and MMP-2 (data not shown). Some of the compounds displayed moderate selectivity ratios (<10) against TACE and MMP-2. To optimize the selectivity, substitution and replacement of the piperazine moiety were explored because it occupies the entry of the S′1 pocket, a region known to drive both potency and selectivity within the metalloproteinase family.(30) Of all the modifications investigated, only the replacement of the piperazine with an (S)-methyl-piperazine (compounds 37 and 39) maintained potency, with the (R)-methyl enantiomer (compound 38) being 5-fold less potent. Replacement of the piperazine with a piperidine (compounds 28 vs 40) led to a slight decrease in potency (4-fold) against ADAMTS-5 (Table 9).

    Further Potency Optimization

    One of the most effective ways to improve potency is to reduce the strain energy coming from the difference between the energy of unbound low-energy conformations and that of the bioactive conformation. With three consecutive carbon–carbon torsions, the linker between the hydantoin ring and the piperazine-amide offers a good opportunity to probe, control, and eventually reduce this strain. Consequently, substitutions on the linker were extensively explored, initially by adding small substituents to each carbon of the ethylene linker and evaluating the resulting diastereomers. In addition, substituents on the linker were connected with the C5-hydantoin carbon to generate the spiro analogues. From this endeavor, only substituents in the α position to the carbonyl group improved potency, and only one of the four possible diastereomers was active. Although the introduction of small substituents had a favorable impact on potency, it was in general accompanied by a decreased metabolic stability in rat liver microsomes, or reduced selectivity (Table 11). However, derivatives with alkoxy methyl substituents in the α position of the amide group displayed improved in vitro metabolic stability but showed unsatisfactory oral systemic exposures in rat.

    Mouse Femoral Head Cartilage Explant Assay

    Following evaluation of the various SAR combinations, the compounds with the highest potency and selectivity were assessed in mouse cartilage explants. Mouse femoral head cartilage was cultured as described by Stanton et al.(8) Femoral head cartilage was collected from C57BL/6 mice at 3 weeks of age (2 hips/animal). Hip cartilages (2 hips/well) were then stimulated with or without human IL-1α media in combination with different concentrations of the tested compounds. After 3 days of incubation, the GAG concentration was measured in the supernatant.
    There was a good correlation between inhibition of ADAMTS-5 activity in hs-ADAMTS-5 preparations and the mouse cartilage explant assays, although a 100-fold shift was observed (Figure 4), most likely reflecting easier access of the compound to its soluble target in a biochemical assay than in a 3D matrix environment. An IC50 below 50 nM against hs-ADAMTS-5 equated to an inhibition of over 50% at 10 μM in the mouse cartilage explant assay. These data also suggested that an IC50 below 10–20 nM was required to achieve low μM activity in the mouse cartilage explant assay. ADMET and rat PK profiles were established for the most active and selective compounds, including GLPG1972 (Table 12).

    Cocrystallization of ADAMTS-5 with GLPG1972

    The binding mode of GLPG1972 was resolved by cocrystallization with ADAMTS-5-(262–480)L282K followed by X-ray diffraction (resolution 2.25 Å, Figure 5). In agreement with the hypothesized binding mode, the hydantoin ring adopts a tautomeric form that enables its coordination with the zinc ion via the nitrogen of the N-acyl amide and the H-bonding with Glu411. Also, in line with interactions described above, three additional hydrogen bonds are preserved: the hydantoin amide nitrogen interacts with Gly380 and the carbonyl oxygen of the piperazine amide with both backbone NHs of Leu379 and Gly380. Interestingly, the methyl group is axial with respect to the piperazine ring and develops hydrophobic contacts with Leu443. Consequently, the slightly polarized hydrogen of its α carbon is equatorial and interacts with the carbonyl oxygen of Ser440 with an almost ideal geometry. Hydrophobic contacts are observed between the cyclopropyl ring, which occupies the S1 pocket, and Met381, between the piperazine ring and His410, and with the difluorophenyl ring embedded in the S′1 pocket (Figure 5).

    Chemistry

    The synthetic discovery route used to prepare GLPG1972 is depicted in Scheme 1. It consisted of four linear steps and one chiral separation by supercritical fluid chromatography (SFC) to give GLPG1972 with an overall yield of 6%. Condensation of ketone 57 with tert-butyl-2-bromoacetate 58 gave γ-keto ester 59 that underwent a Bucherer–Bergs reaction to provide racemic hydantoin 60. The tert-butyl ester group was removed under acidic conditions, affording the carboxylic acid intermediate 61. Separation of enantiomers by chiral SFC was run on a 600 g scale, and (S)-61 acid was isolated with high enantiomeric excess (ee = 99.1%). The phenyl-piperazine building block (S)-65 was prepared in two steps (Buchwald cross coupling followed by Boc deprotection), starting from commercially available and enantiomerically pure (S)-3-methyl-piperazine-1-carboxylic acid tert-butyl ester. Finally, the two enantiomerically enriched intermediates (S)-61 and (S)-65 were coupled using EDC.HCl/HOBt to yield GLPG1972.

    Conclusion

    Following an HTS campaign, a hydantoin series was identified with modest potency against ADAMTS-5 but excellent ADME properties. Subsequent SAR optimization resulted in the discovery of GLPG1972, which displayed high potency against rn- and hs-ADAMTS-5 protein preparations as well as in a mouse cartilage explant assay. In addition, GLPG1972 featured an excellent selectivity profile versus metalloproteinases, with good plasma exposure in rats and dogs after oral administration, and high stability in dog and human liver microsomes and hepatocytes. Detailed pharmacology data on GLPG1972, including animal models with surgically induced OA, will be presented elsewhere (manuscript in preparation). GLPG1972 is a druglike small molecule with the potential to be a first-in-class disease-modifying therapy for the treatment of OA. It has been investigated in a phase 2 clinical trial (Roccella study; NCT03595618).
