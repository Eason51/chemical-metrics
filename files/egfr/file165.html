    Abstract

    Heterobifunctional molecules, which recruit E3 ligases to ubiquitinate a target protein of interest, have found wide application as both biological tools and molecules with the potential to have clinical effects. In their recent paper, Yamazoe et al. report a heterobifunctional molecule that recruits the phosphatase PP1 to promote the dephosphorylation of pAKT to give AKT. This Viewpoint seeks to place this work in the wider context of heterobifunctional molecules and looks ahead to new possibilities presented by these results.

    Proteinâprotein interactions (PPIs) play many important roles in cellular homeostasis and function. It is estimated that the human protein interactome comprises up to 650â¯000 pairwise interactions.(1) Consequently, aberrant PPIs are associated with many disease states,(2) which has led to a significant focus on the development of synthetic molecules that either inhibit or promote PPIs. Inhibition of PPIs by small molecules can be difficult, with the often shallow interacting surfaces being described as âundruggableâ.(2) However, progress is being made in this field, with a number of PPI inhibitors in clinical trials, especially in the area of epigenetics.(3â7)

    There has also been much interest in molecules that can promote PPIs to evoke a desired biological effect.(8) Such molecules are sometimes referred to as chemical inducers of dimerization (CIDs) or molecular glues. Naturally occurring CIDs include a wide range of natural products, notably cyclosporin A, rapamycin, and FK506.(8â10) Rapamycin and cyclosporin A function by forming a ternary complex between themselves, the FRB domain of mTOR, and FKBP12. FK506 functions in a similar manner but recruits calcineurin rather than mTOR. There has also be significant work conducted on the 14-3-3 family of proteins, which are a class of eukaryotic regulatory proteins.(11)

    Immunomodulatory drugs (IMIDs), including thalidomide, lenalidomide, and pomalidomide, are monovalent promoters of PPIs. They function by binding to the cereblon (CRBN) subunit of the CRL4ACRBN E3 ligase complex and recruiting neosubstrate proteins, which include Aiolos (IKZF3), CK1Î±, GSPT1, and Ikaros (IKZF1). This results in polyubiquitination and subsequent proteasome-mediated degradation of the neosubstrate.(8) The teratogenic activity of thalidomide has recently been attributed to its ability to promote degradation of the neo-substrate SALL4.(12,13)

    The ability of thalidomide, and its derivatives, to recruit CRBN has been harnessed in proteolysis-targeting chimeras (PROTACs). These are heterobifunctional molecules that comprise a ligand for a protein of interest (POI) linked to a ligand for an E3 ligase (Figure 1B). By bringing the POI into proximity with the E3 ligase, and associated degron, polyubiquitination of the POI is promoted, leading to proteasome-mediated degradation of this protein. In addition to CRBN, Von HippelâLindau disease tumor suppressor protein (VHL) has also been widely applied in this context. Another six E3 ligases have also been harnessed in PROTACs.(14,15)

    PROTACs that promote the degradation of a wide range of compounds have been reported.(8,15,17,18) An incomplete list of these targets includes the androgen receptor (AR), estrogen receptor (ER), the BET bromodomain-containing proteins,(19) TRIM24,(20) BRD9,(21) BRD9 and BRD7,(22) and STAT3.(23,24) Excitingly, both ER-degrading PROTACs(25) and AR-degrading PROTACs are in clinical trials.(26)

    While the addition of multiple ubiquitin units marks the POI for degradation, the addition of the initial ubiquitin monomer, usually to a lysine residue, can be viewed as a post-translational modification (PTM) to the POI. This gives rise to the possibility that heterobifunctional molecules could be developed that allow modulation of other PTMs. In their article published in this issue of the Journal of Medicinal Chemistry, Yamazoe et al. extend the concept of modulating protein PTMs to the dephosphorylation of the POI.(16) Their aim was to design a heterobifunctional molecule comprising a ligand for the POI linked to a protein phosphatase-binding moiety. It was hypothesized that such molecules would promote dephosphorylation of the POI. The POIs selected were AKT (protein kinase B, PKB) and epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), which are both oncogenic kinases. Kinase activity is often enhanced by phosphorylation, and therefore bifunctional molecules that reduce phosphorylation of the kinase of interest provide a way to regulate its activity. AKT functions by binding to phosphatidylinositol 3,4,5-trisphosphate (PtdInsP3) through its pleckstrin homology domain, which results in recruitment to the cell membrane.(27,28) Once recruited, AKT is phosphorylated and modulates the function of a large number of downstream proteins.(29) EGFR is a transmembrane receptor tyrosine kinase that dimerizes upon activation, leading to a wide range of effects including angiogenesis and cell proliferation. Protein-phosphatase 1 (PP1) was selected to dephosphorylate the targets as it is widely expressed and has broad activity.

    To determine whether this concept was plausible, a chloroalkyl-modified allosteric AKT inhibitor (1, Figure 2) was synthesized that would react selectively with a HaloTag(30) protein in cells. The PTEN-deficient cell line, LNCaP, was transfected with a vector for a HaloTag-PP1-FLAG(31) fusion protein. When the transfected cell lines were treated with 1 (10 Î¼M, 8 h) a significant reduction in the level of pAKT was observed, compared to untransfected cells. It is presumed that 1 reacted with the HaloTag-PP1-FLAG fusion protein, conjugating the allosteric AKT inhibitor, promoting recruitment of pAKT to the fusion protein, leading to dephosphorylation.

    Investigations into the development of a bifunctional molecule that could replicate the reduction in pAKT focused on linking an allosteric AKT inhibitor to a peptide that possesses a PP1 recognition motif. Initial compounds that incorporated a 20-amino acid peptide showed insufficient cell permeability to be functional. However, linking of the minimal PP1 recognition motif, RVSF, to the allosteric act inhibitor gave compound 4a (Figure 2). When applied to LNCaP cells (10 Î¼M for 8 h), compound 4a caused a reduction in AKT phosphorylated at T308 and S473. The inactive compound 4b, which includes the RVSA peptide sequence in place of RVSF, did not affect pAKT levels. Interestingly, a heterobifunctional molecule that employs an ATP-competitive AKT inhibitor, in place of the allosteric inhibitor, was less effective. This observation is attributed to the known effect of ATP-competitive AKT inhibitors promoting an increase in pAKT, counteracting the effect of PP1.

    To demonstrate that another target could be dephosphorylated, EGFR was next investigated. AZD-9291 (Tagrisso) was conjugated to a HaloTag-reactive chloroalkyl group, giving compound 7 (Figure 2). Application of 7 (10 Î¼M, 8 h) to HaloTag-PP1-FLAG transfected HCC827 cells showed a significant reduction of EGFR phosphorylation at Y1068. Some of this reduction could result from a reduction in EGFR trans autophosphorylation. However, the reduction in pY1068 was more significant in the transfected cells. This observation is particularly interesting given that PP1, a serine/threonine phosphatase, appears to be dephosphorylating a tyrosine residue as a result of the induced proximity to Y1068.

    This work is important because it demonstrates that heterobifunctional molecules can be used to manipulate PTMs beyond ubiquitination. Phosphorylation has long been recognized as a key PTM, which modulates function in a wide range of cellular contexts including disease states.(32) It is exciting to imagine that heterobifunctional molecules can be developed that will promote phosphorylation through harnessing of kinase activity, in addition to this report that demonstrates phosphate group removal. There are many other PTMs that are also important within cells, and it stands to reason that it might be possible to develop heterobifunctional molecules that manipulate further PTMs. The progression of PROTACs into clinical studies promotes confidence that heterobifunctional molecules might represent, more generally, a new approach to obtaining biological effects, and consequently therapeutic approaches, that would be impossible using traditional, occupancy-based inhibition.
