    Abstract

    In recent years, several attempts have been made to develop graphical methods for the analysis of structure–activity relationships (SARs) in increasingly large and heterogeneous compound data sets. Among others, these approaches include extensions of conventional R-group tables and graph representations for the analysis of active analogs. Herein, we introduce AnalogExplorer as a new method for the graphical exploration of analog series. AnalogExplorer consists of three graphical components and is methodologically distinct from previous SAR visualization techniques. It is designed to deconvolute large series of analogs and systematically analyze and compare analog series contained in structurally heterogeneous data sets. In addition, analog subsets forming activity cliffs and R-groups responsible for cliff formation are easily identified in AnalogExplorer graphs. The design of AnalogExplorer is described in detail, and exemplary applications are discussed. In addition, the implementation of AnalogExplorer is made freely available.

    1 Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The transformation of hits into leads and further optimization of leads are central tasks in medicinal chemistry.(1) These processes involve the generation of series of analogs of preselected active compounds to increase their potency and improve other optimization-relevant features such as solubility or metabolic stability. In the practice of medicinal chemistry, analogs are typically designed and generated with the help of R-group tables that list core structures of individual series, R-groups at predefined substitution sites, and potency values of analogs. R-group tables represent a conventional data format for the exploration (and exploitation) of structure–activity relationships (SARs) during chemical optimization efforts. However, R-group tables are difficult to use when compound series become large, multiple series are compared, or structurally heterogeneous compound sets are analyzed.
    As rapidly increasing amounts of SAR data become available, computational approaches have been introduced to access SAR information contained in compound data sets and visualize SARs.(2, 3) These include methods for the design of global activity landscape representations of compound data sets, which provide integrated views of similarity and activity relationships,(4) as well as methods for the graphical analysis of local activity landscape features and analog series.(2-4) A few approaches have been specifically introduced to further extend the R-group table format or provide alternative ways to graphically analyze analog series and their SARs. For example, so-called SAR maps have been developed for visualizing single analog series in a rectangular matrix format reminiscent of R-group tables.(5) Compounds forming a series share the same core, which is represented by the maximum common substructure (MCS), and contain R-groups at two predefined substitution sites. In the matrix, rows contain all unique R-groups attached to a given site of the MCS and columns contain all R-groups attached to another site. Accordingly, each cell in the matrix is associated with a compound consisting of corresponding substituents. Cells are colored according to compound potency values or computed selectivity scores.(5) Two derivatives of these SAR maps have subsequently been introduced including a map that displays the biological activity profiles of compounds in a series across a panel of assays or targets(6) and, in addition, a map accounting for single R-group polymorphism by analyzing compounds that only differ at a single substitution site.(7) The SAR map and its derivatives provide compound-centric views for analyzing analogs with only one or two substitution sites.
    Other MCS-based visualization methods that conceptually depart from R-group table formats have also been introduced. For example, the combinatorial analog graph (CAG) evaluates the contributions of individual substitution sites and site combinations to compound activity within series and identifies substitution patterns responsible for SAR discontinuity.(8) In this case, all compounds sharing the same MCS are compared in a pairwise manner and analogs with structural variations at one, two, or maximally three sites are identified. In the graph, each node represents one or more pairs of compounds that differ at corresponding substitution site(s). Since compounds are compared in different substitution site contexts, a compound can appear multiple times in different nodes, which are colored according to SAR discontinuity scores.(8) Hence, the CAG is a compound pair-based approach for the identification of key substitution site(s) with significant contribution to SAR discontinuity. Another MCS-based graphical method termed directed R-group combination graph (DRCG) focuses on R-group combinations in analog series.(9) Nodes in the hierarchical DRCG represent all R-group combinations derived from an analog series, subsets of these R-group combinations, and the corresponding compounds. The graph reveals subset relationships between R-group combinations and potency changes associated with the addition or removal of substituents. Its primary focal point is the consideration of R-group combinations rather than compounds.
    An alternative approach, the similarity–potency tree (SPT), was introduced to mine SAR information for any given (reference) compound and its chemical neighborhood in a compound data set.(10) All compounds that exceed a predefined structural similarity threshold when compared to the reference compound are identified and organized in a tree structure with decreasing similarity to the reference. Compounds in an SPT are colored according to their potency values. The SPT reveals analogs of the reference compound in the form of horizontal tree patterns. From horizontal and vertical patterns, SAR information can be extracted. Another reference compound-based graphical representation termed chemical neighborhood graph (CNG) is designed to identify compounds in data sets that have many structural neighbors with significant potency differences.(11) Both the SPT and CNG representations can be rationalized as chemical neighborhood graphs that identify subsets of analogs from which SAR information can be extracted.
    In addition to graphical methods for the organization and display of analog series and chemical neighborhood graphs, the matched molecular pair (MMP) formalism(12) has also been adapted for the systematic study of analogs. An MMP is defined as a pair of compounds that only differ by a structural change at a single site.(12) The exchange of a pair of substructures that converts MMP compounds into each other is referred to as a chemical transformation.(13) The SAR matrix data structure is designed to identify and systematically organize analog series with core structures forming MMP relationships and reveal associated SAR information.(14) By application of a two-step MMP fragmentation scheme, compounds with analogous core structures and conserved or varying substitutions are arranged in an R-group table-like matrix format. Cells in a matrix are colored according to compound potency values, and the matrix captures all possible combinations of structurally analogous cores and available substituents, many of which might still be virtual, hence providing compound design suggestions.(14)
    Herein, we report a new computational methodology for the graphical representation and analysis of analog series that is distinct from currently available approaches and data structures, as discussed above. The method termed AnalogExplorer is compound-based (rather than compound pair- or substituent-based) and systematically explores substitution sites or site combinations in analog series, regardless of the number of substitution sites they might contain or the structural diversity of R-groups. AnalogExplorer organizes analog series into subsets having varying R-groups at the same site(s). A given analog only appears once in the graph. The approach can be applied to analyze individual analog series, irrespective of their size, or systematically investigate all analog series available in a given compound activity class, including structurally related and distinct series. Given its unique design, AnalogExplorer further extends the current spectrum of methods for the analysis of analog series and adds to the chemoinformatics tool box for medicinal chemistry applications.

    2 General Methods and Compounds

    2.1 Definition of Analog Series

    Analog series were defined on the basis of molecular scaffolds. From compound activity classes, all unique scaffolds were extracted following Bemis and Murcko.(15) These hierarchical scaffolds were generated by removing all R-groups from compounds while retaining all rings and linkers between rings. Compounds represented by the same scaffolds were grouped into individual analog series. For the definition of analogs series, ring-containing scaffolds were required to contain more than six atoms (i.e., they had to be larger than a six-membered ring). In addition, each qualifying series had to contain at least 10 analogs. It should be noted that this scaffold-based approach was only required for the initial extraction of analog series from compound activity classes. By contrast, the AnalogExplorer method is MCS-based, as further discussed below.
    For specific AnalogExplorer applications, multiple analog series with activity against the same target and structurally related scaffolds were also identified. Scaffolds of analog series that were only distinguished by a chemical modification at a single site (i.e., the exchange of a substructure) were identified using the MMP formalism. For the generation of scaffold-based MMPs, two transformation size restrictions were applied that were originally introduced for the identification of MMP-based activity cliffs(16) (i.e., pairs of structurally analogous compounds having a large difference in potency(17)). Accordingly, the maximal size of an exchanged fragment was limited to 13 non-hydrogen atoms and the size difference between two exchanged fragments was set to maximally eight non-hydrogen atoms. Systematic fragmentation of single exocyclic bonds was carried out for MMP generation. MMPs were produced using an in-house version of the algorithm by Hussain and Rea(13) that utilizes the OpenEye toolkit.(18)

    2.2 R-Group Decomposition

    For each qualifying analog series, the MCS was determined. The MCS often corresponds to the signature scaffold. However, depending on the substituent distribution within a series, its MCS might be larger than the signature scaffold (e.g., if all analogs contain one or more conserved R-groups). The MCS was then used for R-group decomposition in order to identify all available substitution sites and corresponding R-groups for each compound. For this purpose, the MCS was mapped onto each analog in a series. R-groups were identified and assigned to numerically indexed substitution sites. If multiple mappings of the MCS onto a compound were possible because of structural symmetry, all possible mappings were determined and the mapping yielding substitution sites with the smallest sum of indices was retained. Figure 1 illustrates the generation of the MCS with substitution sites from a small set of analogs.
    Routines for scaffold and MCS identification, R-group decomposition, and indexing of substitution sites were implemented in Java using the OpenEye chemistry toolkit.(18)

    2.3 Selection of Analog Series

    On the basis of scaffold analysis, a total of 247 analog series with activity against 67 different targets were selected from ChEMBL (release 18).(19) These series had to meet the following selection criteria. Only compounds with direct interactions (i.e., assay relationship type “D”) with human targets at the highest confidence level (i.e., assay confidence score 9) were collected. As potency measurements, assay-independent equilibrium constants (Ki values) and assay-dependent IC50 values were separately considered. Only compounds with explicitly defined Ki or IC50 values were selected. Approximate measurements such as “>”, “<”, and “∼” were disregarded. Compounds with multiple Ki or IC50 measurements for the same target were retained if all the values fell within the same order.

    3 Design of AnalogExplorer

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    3.1 Subset-Based Display

    For graphical analysis, an analog series is organized into subsets on the basis of the MCS. Each subset comprises compounds having varying R-groups at the same substitution site or site combination. Mapping of an analog to the MCS of the series determines its subset membership. Each analog of a series belongs to one and only one subset.

    3.2 Graph Design

    All graphical components of AnalogExplorer, as discussed in the following, were implemented using the open source Java package JUNG.(20) A representative complete graph is shown in Figure 2a. The graph captures a series of 59 analogs (androgen receptor antagonists). Mapping of analogs to the MCS of the series identified five substitution sites (R1–R5). In the complete graph, each node represents a given substitution site or unique site combination and all compounds with varying R-groups at the site(s). The root node 0 corresponds to a compound having no R-group at any site (if available). The node 1 represents analogs that only contain R-groups at R1, the node 12 analogs with R-groups at R1 and R2, and so on. For an MCS with n substitution sites, the total number of possible nodes is calculated as the sum of the root node and all possible combinations consisting of 1 to n sites. Hence, for the MCS with five substitution sites shown in Figure 2a, the complete graph contains a total of 32 nodes. Only a subset of these nodes is populated with analog subsets. The AnalogExplorer graph incorporates several design elements.
    First, nodes are scaled in size according to the size (number of analogs) of the subset they represent. Five canonical node sizes are applied, as indicated in Figure 2a. Thus, large nodes represent substitution sites that have been well explored chemically and small nodes sites that have only been little explored. “Empty” nodes have unit size and are not populated with analog subsets (i.e., the corresponding substitution sites are not explored).
    Second, nodes are colored according to the mean potency values of their analogs using a continuous color spectrum from red (minimal potency within the analog series) to yellow (medium potency) to green (maximal potency within the series). Empty nodes are colored white.
    Third, the border thickness of colored nodes reflects the potency range covered by analogs comprising the corresponding subset, as also indicated in Figure 2a. If the potency of all analogs falls within the same order of magnitude, no scaling is applied; if their potency values differ by 10- to less than 100-fold or by at least 100-fold, thick black borders and thick red borders are used, respectively. Thus, nodes with thick red borders correspond to subsets of analogs containing activity cliffs (with respect to our preferred potency difference criterion).(16, 17)
    In the AnalogExplorer graph, nodes are arranged in different layers, depending on the number of substitution sites. From the top (node 0) to the bottom, the number of substitution sites increases, i.e., layer 0 consists of only the root node, layer 1 of all nodes with one substitution site, layer 2 of all nodes with two sites, and so on. In addition, nodes are connected via edges according to subset relationships among the substitution sites, i.e., if substitution sites defining a node are a subset of other site combinations. For example, node 1 is a subset of nodes 12, 13, 14, and 15, and node 12 is a subset of nodes 123, 124, and 125. Therefore, edges in the graph reflect hierarchical relationships between nodes in adjacent layers.
    The layout of the complete treelike directed acyclic graph is determined by the DAGLayout algorithm of the Java package JUNG.(20) To reduce the complexity of the graph representation, the direction of the edges is not displayed. The algorithmically generated graph layout can be interactively edited.

    3.3 Reduced Graph

    The complete graph discussed above provides a hierarchy of all possible substitution sites and site combinations and reveals sites that have not yet been explored in a given analog series. For the analysis of an existing series, empty nodes and edges between them are omitted (while retaining the graph layout), which produces the AnalogExplorer reduced graph, for ease of interpretation. The reduced graph for the set of 59 androgen receptor antagonists is depicted in Figure 2b, which consists of 10 populated nodes.

    3.4 R-Group Tree

    Another graphical component of AnalogExplorer, termed R-group tree, is designed to represent a subset of analogs with given substitution site(s). In Figure 3, the R-group tree of node 245 in Figure 2b is shown, which represents seven analogs with varying R-groups at sites R2, R4, and R5. These sites are arranged in different layers, and all R-groups are shown. The site order is determined by the total number of R-groups available at each site. If the same number of R-groups is found for multiple sites; these sites are ordered according to their numerical indices. For example, all analogs in Figure 3 share a hydroxyl group at R4, three unique R-group patterns at both R2 and R5. Accordingly, the corresponding tree starts with the root node followed by R4, R2, and R5. Individual branches in the tree represent sets of R-groups contained in available analogs. The nodes in the bottom layer represent individual analogs and are colored according to their potency values (applying the same continuous color spectrum discussed above). Nodes in intermediate layers represented subsets of analogs containing given substituents at the specified site(s) and are colored on the basis of their mean potency. For example, the left-most node in the R2 layer is associated with three analogs having a hydroxyl group at R4 and two methyl groups at R2.

    4 Applications of AnalogExplorer

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    AnalogExplorer can be applied in different ways. The analysis of individual analog series represents a primary application. In addition, the methodology can be used to systematically analyze and compare all analog series that are contained in a given compound data set. Furthermore, for series of analogs with activity against multiple targets, AnalogExplorer can be used to compare SAR information on a series for different targets (target-based SAR comparison). Moreover, it can be applied to compare SAR patterns for structurally related analog series with activity against a given target (series-based SAR comparison). In the following, exemplary applications are presented using reduced graphs.

    4.1 Individual Analog Series

    AnalogExplorer analysis is applicable to any given series of analogs, irrespective of its size and the complexity of the underlying substitution patterns.

    4.1.1 Serotonin 2c Receptor Antagonists

    Figure 4 shows the reduced graph for a series of 45 analogs active against the serotonin 2c receptor. The MCS of the series contains eight variable substitution sites (R1–R8). Hence, the complete graph contains a total of 256 possible nodes (site combinations). However, only 10 site combinations (including the root node) are populated with analogs in the reduced graph (with maximally two sites per combination). The majority of analogs (41 of 45) contain substituents at R3 and its site combinations R1 and R3 (13), R2 and R3 (23), 34, 35, 36, 37, and 38. Thus, in this series of analogs, only substitution site R3 is chemically well explored. Site 3 and site combination 13 represent subsets of analogs that have significantly different potency values and form activity cliffs. These subsets are identified in the graph as primary focal points for SAR analysis.

    4.1.2 Histamine H4 Receptor Antagonists

    Another exemplary series of 52 analogs, histamine H4 receptor antagonists, is shown in Figure 5a. The analogs combine maximally three of five available substitution sites. In total, 12 sites or site combinations are observed, nine of which represent multiple analogs. The majority of site combinations are associated with subsets of potent analogs. Four site combinations (15, 25, 35, and 45) represent multiple analogs with potency differences of at least 2 orders of magnitude that form activity cliffs. In particular, R-groups at different sites of the phenyl moiety and a substituent at the para position of the piperazine ring yield analogs with large potency differences. Representative activity cliffs formed by analogs having different R-groups at these sites are shown in Figure 5b. The methyl group at R5 is conserved in all eight analogs. A carboxyl group at R1 and R4 and a methoxy group at R2 and R3 yield only weakly potent compounds. By contrast, halogen atom substituents (bromine at R1 and fluorine at R4) and smaller R-groups at R2 (hydroxyl group) and R3 (methyl group) produce analogs with much higher potency. Hence, the graph representation identifies activity cliff inducing R-groups at specific sites.

    4.1.3 Cannabinoid CB2 Receptor Antagonists

    Figure 6 shows the reduced graph for a series of 80 analogs that are antagonists of the cannabinoid CB2 receptor. The series contains nine variable substitution sites. A total of 26 site combinations are explored, revealing diverse substitution patterns at varying site(s). Given the distribution of mean potency values, the series is characterized by strong SAR heterogeneity. Among all 26 populated site combinations, 15 represent subsets of multiple analogs. Site combinations 12 and 124 both represent 10 analogs with consistently high potency. The example illustrates how AnalogExplorer graphs deconvolute analog series with complex substitution site patterns and help to prioritize subsets with different substituents at specific site(s) having desired activity.

    4.1.4 Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor erbB1 Antagonists

    In Figure 7, a large series of analogs is shown with 128 antagonists of epidermal growth factor receptor erbB1. The MCS of this series contains eight variable substitution sites. A total of 17 sites or site combinations are populated with analogs, three of which (26, 267, and 1267) are each associated with more than 15 analogs. These three site combinations represent analog subsets spanning large potency ranges with varying mean potency values. In the graph, these site combinations form a path. Hence, R-group patterns of the associated subsets can be directly compared to study SAR determinants.

    4.2 Multiple Analog Series with Activity against the Same Target

    In addition to individual series, AnalogExplorer can also be applied to compare multiple analog series extracted from a compound activity class.
    As shown in Figure 8, five series (A–E) of SRC tyrosine kinase inhibitors comprising a total of 122 compounds are subjected to AnalogExplorer analysis. Four of these series (A–D) have related core structures (identified by MMP analysis). Each series contains from 13 to 44 analogs with four to seven variable substitution sites. Series A, C, and E have comparable numbers of analogs. However, the topology of the corresponding reduced graphs differs. The largest series containing 44 analogs produces the most complex graph among these series, with 19 explored site combinations. In addition, the graphs reveal different SAR characteristics for individual series. For example, analogs in series B, C, and E have comparable potency, but analogs belonging to series E are overall more potent than those in series B and C. By contrast, analogs in series A and D have varying potency and thus higher SAR information content. In series D, node 134 corresponds to an analog subset with activity cliffs, which becomes immediately apparent in the graph. The comparison of the graphs also reveals the differences in substitution site complexity among these related series and different degrees of chemical exploration.

    4.3 Analogs Series with Multitarget Activity

    Individual series of analogs with activity against different targets are also graphically analyzed. In this case, graphs having conserved topology are compared to evaluate target-specific differences in SAR patterns.

    4.3.1 β2 and β3 Adrenergic Receptor Antagonists

    Figure 9a shows reduced graphs for a series of 30 analogs with activity against the β2 and β3 adrenergic receptor isoforms. The MCS of the series contains four variable substitution sites. For graphical analysis, the potency spectrum is standardized for both targets with respect to the overall least and most potent compound. In general, the analogs are more potent against the β2 adrenergic receptor than the β3 receptor but populated substitution sites display similar potency value distributions in both targets. For example, analogs at site 2 are more potent than those at site 4, followed by analogs at site 3. Site combination 34 produces activity cliffs for both receptors. These two potency value-based graphs for side-by-side comparison are further combined into a single selectivity value-based graph, as shown in Figure 9b. The selectivity values of compounds are calculated as the potency differences between two targets. Nodes in the graph are colored according to the mean selectivity values of corresponding analogs using a continuous color spectrum from red (i.e., most selective for β3 adrenergic receptor) to yellow (nonselective) to green (most selective for β2 adrenergic receptor). Consistent with the observations made above, more analogs are found to be weakly selective for the β2 over the β3 adrenergic receptor.

    4.3.2 Inhibitors of Tyrosine Kinases ABL and SRC

    The graph comparison for a series of 38 analogs with activity against kinases ABL and SRC is shown in Figure 9c. These analogs have R-group variations at seven sites. Different SAR patterns are observed for ABL and SRC. For example, analogs with R-group variations at sites 37, 137, 347, 357, and 367 are weakly potent against SRC but have medium to high potency against ABL, this revealing selectivity trends of analogs in these subsets for ABL over SRC. This observation is further corroborated by selectivity value-based graph in Figure 9d. In addition, different substitution sites are identified that correspond to analogs with large potency differences for a given target including sites 123 and 134 for kinase ABL and SRC, respectively. Thus, these site combinations represent target-specific SAR hotspots.

    4.4 Combined Analog Series

    Structurally related series of analogs might also be combined for AnalogExplorer analysis and compared to individual series. For example, for four of the series shown in Figure 8 (including A, B, C, and D), a combined MCS with four variable substitution sites can be generated that represents a large series of 101 SRC tyrosine kinase inhibitors. The MCS representing the combined series and its reduced graph are shown in Figure 10. In the combined series, six substitution site combinations are populated with analogs. Three of these site combinations (123, 234, and 1234) represent analog subsets with large potency differences that contain activity cliffs. The reduced graph of the combined series provides an alternative and more general view of the SAR information associated with these related analog series and helps to identify site combinations that are generally preferred. Substitutions of the smaller consensus MCS capturing four series might now involve small rings, as revealed by comparison of the consensus MCS with the MCS of the individual series. Hence, it provides an alternative perspective for analog design taking information from all four series into account.

    5 Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Methods for SAR visualization have become popular in recent years. This can at least in part be attributed to the rapid growth of compound activity data, especially for current pharmaceutical targets, and the ensuing difficulties of analyzing and comparing active compounds using conventional data formats such as R-group tables. In this work, we have introduced AnalogExplorer for the systematic graphical exploration of analog series. The approach differs from previously reported SAR visualization methods that focus on series of structurally similar or analogous active compounds such as SAR maps, combinatorial analog graphs, or similarity–potency trees. AnalogExplorer is composed of three graphical components including the complete graph, which provides a detailed organization of the “analog space” available to a given core structure and its substitution sites and identifies unexplored regions of the analog space, the reduced graph, which focuses on available analogs, their structural relationships, and SAR patterns, and the R-group tree, which provides high-resolution views of subsets of analogs with varying R-groups at defined substitution sites or site combinations. Nodes are associated with compound structures that can be interactively viewed. AnalogExplorer avoids structural redundancy in graph representations, which often hinders interpretation (i.e., each analog and substitution site combination occurs only once in the graph). The approach is equally suitable for the exploration of individual analog series (including large series) and the comparative analysis of structurally distinct or related series contained in heterogeneous compound data sets. For practical applications, the number of substitution site combinations that can be graphically explored and the number of analogs that can be represented are not limited. Graph complexity is much more determined by the number of substitution sites and site combinations within a series than the number of analogs. However, as we have shown, series with up to nine different substitution sites can be easily handled. Furthermore, the methodology can be easily applied to monitor evolving compound data sets on a time course. If multiple analog series share activity against the same target, they usually have different composition, might be chemically explored to different degrees, and display different SAR progression. Such differences can be visualized and assessed by comparing reduced graphs. In addition, analog subsets that form activity cliffs and represent SAR hotspots can be immediately identified in reduced graphs. High-resolution views of such analog subsets are provided by R-group trees of selected nodes. Furthermore, AnalogExplorer can also be applied to assess complex SAR information for a given analog series that is active against multiple targets. In this case, reduced graphs with conserved topology and one-to-one node correspondence are compared that immediately reveal target-specific differences in SAR patterns. We emphasize that AnalogExplorer is primarily introduced for SAR visualization. However, compound potency can of course also be replaced with any other optimization-relevant properties, such as selectivity, as also presented herein.
    In summary, given its potential applications, the AnalogExplorer approach should considerably aid in the exploration of large analog series and provide new features for SAR visualization. As a service to the scientific community, a Java implementation of AnalogExplorer routines is made freely available via the ZENODO open access platform.(21)
