    Abstract

    The cytosolic Hsp90-selective inhibitor TAS-116 has an acceptable safety profile and promising antitumor activity in clinical trials. We examined the binding characteristics of TAS-116 and its analogs to determine the impact of the ligand binding mode on selectivity for cytosolic Hsp90. Analyses of the co-crystal structure of Hsp90 and inhibitor TAS-116 suggest that TAS-116 interacts with the ATP-binding pocket, the ATP lid region, and the hydrophobic pocket. A competitive isothermal titration calorimetry analysis confirmed that a small fragment of TAS-116 (THS-510) docks into the lid region and hydrophobic pockets without binding to the ATP-binding pocket. THS-510 exhibited enthalpy-driven binding to Hsp90α and selectively inhibited cytosolic Hsp90 activity. The heat capacity change of THS-510 binding was positive, likely due to the induced conformational rearrangement of Hsp90. Thus, we concluded that interactions with the hydrophobic pocket of Hsp90 determine potency and selectivity of TAS-116 and derivatives for the cytosolic Hsp90 isoform.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Heat shock protein 90 (Hsp90) is a molecular chaperone essential for the maintenance of three-dimensional (3D) structure and the localization of proteins.(1,2) There are four isoforms of Hsp90: Hsp90α and Hsp90β, which localize to the cytoplasm and nucleus, TRAP1 in the mitochondria, and Grp94, also known as gp96, in the endoplasmic reticulum.(3) Cytosolic Hsp90 mediates the proper folding and functions of a diverse range of client proteins including the tyrosine kinases ALK, FLT3, and MET and the growth factor receptors EGFR and FGFR, which have all been implicated in oncogenesis.(4,5) The inhibition of cytosolic Hsp90 impairs the correct folding and maturation of many oncoproteins required for cancer growth and survival. Therefore, Hsp90 is a promising target for drug development in cancer therapy, and Hsp90 inhibitors have been tested in clinical trials.(6−9)
    Hsp90 requires ATP to drive its chaperone activity.(10) ATP hydrolysis causes large and dynamic conformational changes in full-length Hsp90 dimers, and conformational changes are also observed in an ATP lid region of the N-terminal domain (NTD) of Hsp90.(11) Several Hsp90 inhibitors bind to the ATP-binding site of Hsp90 NTD, as shown by co-crystal structural analysis.(12−14) Due to the flexibility of the structure of Hsp90, the shape of the pockets is highly dynamic.(15) A recent paper has indicated the dynamics of conformational changes in the interaction between HSP90 and inhibitors using a principal component analysis of molecular dynamics simulations.(16) Thus, the structural classes of Hsp90 inhibitors are diverse. Hsp90 inhibitors that bind to the Hsp90 NTD are classified based on the binding mode of the compounds. Geldanamycin analogs, radicicol, AUY922, and other resorcinol-type Hsp90 inhibitors are classified as type 1 inhibitors, which occupy the ATP-binding pocket (the first pocket).(12,17) Purine analogs, such as BIIB021, BIIB028, and SNX-2112, interact with the first pocket and also the second pocket, which is formed when the Hsp90 ATP lid region adopts a helical form.(14,18,19) Compounds that bind to both the first pocket and the second pocket are classified as type 2 inhibitors. Hsp90 inhibitors in clinical development have a distinct pattern of isoform selectivity. 17-AAG and AUY922 have pan-Hsp90 inhibitory activity. BIIB021 and SNX-2112 inhibit not only Hsp90α and Hsp90β but also moderately inhibit Grp94 and TRAP-1. The functions of TRAP-1 and Grp94 are poorly understood. However, the gut-specific deletion of Grp94 results in the loss of intestinal homeostasis, and the liver-specific Grp94 knockout in PTEN-null mice accelerates liver tumorigenesis. TRAP-1 is reported to protect against mitochondrial apoptosis.(20) Therefore, the isoform specificity of Hsp90 inhibitors may impact both adverse effects and antitumor efficacy of these agents.(21,22)
    The orally available Hsp90 inhibitor TAS-116 (Figure 1a) is in clinical testing.(23,24) In the preclinical antitumor efficacy studies, TAS-116 showed the potential to be the best-in-class Hsp90 inhibitor as an antitumor agent. In addition, TAS-116 prolonged survival in fourth-line treatment settings for patients with metastatic or unresectable gastrointestinal stromal tumors in a Phase III clinical trial, which is currently ongoing. TAS-116 is the most selective compound for the cytosolic Hsp90 forms among the known Hsp90 inhibitors.(25) As noted by Ernst et al., the structure of Hsp90 must be considered in design of isoform-selective inhibitors.(26) Recently, Uno et al. reported a structure–activity relationship analysis of TAS-116 analogs.(27) However, a biophysical characterization of the isoform selectivity of TAS-116 analogs has not been performed. We previously reported use of biophysical methods, including calorimetric analyses, for small-molecule inhibitor identification.(28−31) Biophysical information on specific inhibitors is helpful for the selection and design of new hit or lead compounds for other target proteins. In this study, we examined the characteristics of binding of TAS-116 and its analogs to Hsp90 using structural, biological, and biophysical analyses, with particular emphasis on calorimetric and competitive analyses.

    Results

    Characterization of Binding Modes of TAS-116

    A co-crystal structure of the very close analog of TAS-116, 16d (Figure 1), with the human Hsp90α NTD has been reported recently (PDB ID: 5ZR3).(27) The structure of another TAS-116 analog, THS-828 (Figure S1a), in complex with human Hsp90α NTD has also been reported (PDB ID: 3WQ9). Based on the binding modes of these inhibitors (Figure 1b), TAS-116 is expected to bind to the first and second pockets and also to a hydrophobic pocket, which we refer to as the third pocket, of Hsp90 (Figure 1b,c). When the amido group of TAS-116 was changed to carbonic acid (THS-2415, Figure S1b), no binding to Hsp90 was observed (data not shown). This result indicates that similar to other Hsp90 inhibitors, the hydrogen-bonding network with D93, T184, N51, and water molecules in the ATP-binding pocket of the Hsp90 NTD are critical for binding of TAS-116 to Hsp90. TAS-116 docked model was constructed from a 16d co-crystal structure (PDB ID: 5ZR3) by modifying the methyl group to an ethyl group, and energy minimization was performed using the Molecular Operating Environment (MOE) software package. In the model, hydrogen-bonding network in ATP pocket and π–π interactions with F138 and a hydrogen bond with Y139 in the second pocket are retained (Figure 1d,e). Water-mediated interaction with Gln23, which was reported in the literature about other types of third pocket inhibitor, was not observed in the co-crystal structures of TAS-116 analogs.(32) After optimization, Gln23 directly interacts with the nitrogen atom of the imidazole-pyrazole group of TAS-116 unlike co-crystal structures (Figure 1f). Gln23 seems flexible and might contribute to the transition state of the interaction between TAS-116 analogs and Hsp90 NTD.
    To investigate the contribution of the amino acids of Hsp90α to the interaction with TAS-116, a site-directed mutagenesis study was performed. Four sites (Q23, N105, F138, and Y139) were selected for mutation based on the interactions observed in the crystal structure of the THS-828 and Hsp90 complex (PDB ID: 3WQ9) and 16d and Hsp90 complex (PDB ID: 5ZR3). Q23, F138, and Y139 are expected to interact directly with TAS-116. From the literature about the third pocket inhibitor,(16,32) F138 is expected to have hydrophobic interaction with TAS-116. N105 is one of the constituent residues of ATP-lid region, and the local structure is highly flexible and forms a helix when Hsp90 takes helical form.(33) N105 faces the solvent front in the helical form and seems not to interact directly with ligand (Figure 2b). Therefore, N105 was selected as a residue which is expected not to interact with TAS-116. Although D93 also interacts with TAS-116, we could not prepare D93A mutant due to low expression level. We successfully prepared Q23A, N105A, F138L, and Y139F mutants of Hsp90 and performed a previously described competitive binding assay(25) to evaluate interaction of Hsp90 inhibitors 17-AAG, AUY922, BIIB021, SNX-2112, and TAS-116 with wild-type and mutant proteins. AUY922 and 17-AAG are type 1 inhibitors, and BIIB021 and SNX-2112 are type 2 inhibitors (Figure S1). The relative IC50 values are summarized in Figure 2c, and plots are shown in Figure S3. The perturbation effect for helical form specific inhibitors (TAS-116, SNX-2112, and BIIB-021) by Q23A mutation is greater than that of type 1 inhibitors (17-AAG and AUY-922), even though SNX-2112 and BIIB-021 do not interact with Q23. These results indicate that Q23 affects formation of the helical form and is not crucial for the interaction with TAS-116. Unexpectedly, Y139F mutation does not weaken the inhibitory effects of SNX-2112 and TAS-116, both were considered to form hydrogen bonds with Y139. Although the binding of other inhibitors was reduced, TAS-116 bound to the F138L mutant with affinity similar to its binding to the wild-type Hsp90. F138 forms a hydrophobic interaction with type 1 inhibitors.(12) In contrast, the type 2 inhibitors, BIIB021 and SNX-2112, interact with F138 through π–π interaction (PDB: 3QDD and 3R92).(19,34,35) From our modeling study, we expected that the interaction between TAS-116 and F138 would be critical; however, as shown in the mutational study, the binding of TAS-116 to human Hsp90α does not depend strongly on the interaction with F138 (Figure 2b). This low dependence of TAS-116 on F138 may be a property acquired by TAS-116 through the use of the third pocket. The shape of the third pocket is not constant in many crystal structures of the helical form Hsp90 NTD. Therefore, we hypothesized that the shape of TAS-116 is the key to bind and stabilize the helical form with the third pocket of Hsp90 NTD.
    Kinetic analysis using surface plasmon resonance (SPR) demonstrated that TAS-116 has a slow off-rate from the human Hsp90α NTD (Figure 2a). The off-rate of TAS-116 was slower than that of type 1 inhibitor 17-AAG and type 2 inhibitor BIIB-021 (Figure S2). Another type 2 inhibitor SNX-2112 showed a slower off-rate (Figure S2), which has a similar structure and binding mode with TAS-116. From the literature, changes in conformations and internal dynamics of Hsp90 are reported to be induced by inhibitor bindings, and some residues are proposed as hot spots.(16) As TAS-116 is not the highest affinity binder among known Hsp90 inhibitors (Figure S3), the slow-off rate is likely due to the unique binding mode of TAS-116.

    Characterization of Isoform Selectivity and Thermodynamics of THS-510

    The Hsp90 inhibitory activities of TAS-116 among Hsp90 isoforms have been reported previously. The Ki values of TAS-116 for Hsp90α, Hsp90β, Grp94, and TRAP-1 are 34.7 nM, 21.3 nM, >50,000 nM, and >50,000 nM, respectively, in a competitive binding assay using FITC-labeled geldanamycin.(25) TAS-116 analog 16d is reported to bind preferentially to the cytosolic isoforms.(27) Like TAS-116 and 16d, THS-828 (Hsp90α, 13 μM; Hsp90β, 17 μM; Grp94, >300 μM; TRAP-1, >300 μM) also binds to the cytosolic isoforms. To further study the unique binding characteristics of TAS-116, we prepared a fragment of TAS-116 that includes only the moiety that binds to the third pocket; this analog was named THS-975 (Figure 3a). In addition to the THS-975, THS-510 was synthesized (Figure 3d). The binding activity and isoform selectivity of THS-975 and THS-510 were analyzed using the competitive binding assay. The IC50 values of THS-975 for Hsp90α, Hsp90β, Grp94, and TRAP-1 were around 300 μM (T/C (%) showed 51% at 300 μM), 218 μM, >300 μM, and >300 μM, respectively (Figure 3b). The IC50 values of THS-510 for Hsp90α, Hsp90β, Grp94, and TRAP-1 were 293 μM, 89 μM, >300 μM, and >300 μM, respectively (Figure 3e). Although both THS-975 and THS-510 are smaller and their activity were very weak compared to TAS-116, THS-975 and THS-510 clearly bound to the cytosolic isoforms Hsp90α and Hsp90β but not to TRAP-1 or Grp94.
    The direct binding of THS-975 and THS-510 to the human Hsp90α NTD was investigated using isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC). The calorimetric data were analyzed using a single-site binding model to obtain the thermodynamic parameters of the interaction between the human Hsp90α NTD and these fragment compounds. In the case of THS-975, although we obtained the exothermic binding reaction, the binding stoichiometry was not 1:1, and the binding enthalpy change was small. In other words, the pyrazole compound is not suitable for more quantitative and precise evaluation such as temperature dependence and competitive assay, as shown in this study due to unexpected errors. On the other hand, we quantitatively analyzed the binding of THS-510 to HSP90 in ITC. As shown in Figure 3f, moderate binding of THS-510 was observed (KD = 2.7 μM) at 25 °C. The stoichiometry of binding calculated for THS-510 from the ITC data was 0.72 ± 0.03; therefore, THS-510 likely binds human Hsp90α in a 1:1 molar ratio. The thermodynamic parameters of the interaction between THS-510 and human Hsp90α NTD were ΔG of −7.6 ± 0.1 kcal/mol, ΔH of −5.7 ± 0.2 kcal/mol, and – TΔS of −1.9 ± 0.1 kcal/mol, indicating that complex formation was primarily enthalpy driven. This suggests that hydrogen bonds or van der Waals stabilize the interaction between the human Hsp90α NTD and THS-510. It is noteworthy that this interaction also involved a slight entropic gain.

    Exploration of the Binding Mode of THS-510 Using Competitive Thermodynamic Profiling

    To estimate the contact region of THS-510 on Hsp90, a competitive binding analysis with known Hsp90 inhibitors was performed using ITC. We hypothesized that if a highly potent inhibitor of Hsp90 occupies the same binding site as THS-510 on Hsp90, the titration of THS-510 into Hsp90 prebound to the potent inhibitor will not cause exothermic reactions. In contrast, if the binding pockets of the potent Hsp90 inhibitor and THS-510 are different, THS-510 will bind to Hsp90 in the presence of the other inhibitor. We selected Hsp90 inhibitors of different types for this analysis: The type 1 inhibitors AUY922 and 17-AAG are expected to occupy the ATP-binding pocket because geldanamycin binds the ATP-binding pocket (PDB: 1YET). BIIB021, a type 2 inhibitor, binds to the Hsp90 helical form and occupies the ATP-binding pocket and the second pocket (PDB: 3QDD).(17,19)
    When THS-510 was titrated into Hsp90α prebound to AUY922, an exothermic reaction was observed (Figure 4a). However, 17-AAG, which binds to the same site as AUY922, did not cause an exothermic reaction (Figure S4). These results indicate that THS-510 does not occupy the ATP-binding pocket of Hsp90α. In contrast, addition of THS-510 to Hsp90α prebound to BIIB021 did not result in an exothermic reaction (Figure 4b), suggesting that THS-510 and BIIB021 have overlapping binding sites. As BIIB021 interacts with both the ATP-binding pocket and the second pocket of Hsp90α, our data suggest that THS-510 binds to the second pocket of the helical form of Hsp90α NTD but does not bind to the ATP-binding pocket.
    We hypothesized that the helical conformation of Hsp90 results from a conformational rearrangement of some residues of the ATP lid region. THS-510 was manually placed on the second and third pockets, and the complex was minimized using MOE (Figure S5A,B). In the energy-minimized structure, the imidazole ring of THS-510 interacts with F138 in the second pocket. A hydrogen bond and van der Waals interactions were observed between the ligand and Hsp90 in the third pocket. These noncovalent interactions probably contribute to the binding enthalpy of THS-510. The structural alignments of the structure of the complex of THS-510 with Hsp90 with the structures of BIIB021 and AUY922 in complex with Hsp90 suggest that the binding site of THS-510 partially overlaps with that of BIIB021 (Figure S5A) but does not overlap with the binding site of AUY922 (Figure S5B).

    Thermodynamic Characterization of TAS-116 and Its Analogs

    To characterize the biophysical properties of the binding of TAS-116 to Hsp90, we examined the thermodynamics of the interaction of Hsp90 with TAS-116 and its analog THS-510. Binding reactions of both ligands to human Hsp90α NTD were exothermic. The tight binding of TAS-116 to the human Hsp90α NTD (KD = 0.17 μM) was enthalpy driven at 25 °C (Figure 5a).
    To characterize the molecular mechanisms of the binding of THS-510 and TAS-116 to Hsp90, we examined the heat capacity changes (ΔCp) over the temperature range from 20 to 30 °C using ITC (Figure 5b, Figure S6). Unexpectedly, binding of THS-510 to the human Hsp90α NTD had a positive ΔCp value (0.56 kcal/mol·K), whereas that of TAS-116 had a negative ΔCp value (−0.29 kcal/mol·K). The negative value of ΔCp is suggestive of binding to a hydrophobic buried region, as previously described for ligands with binding that involves driving solvent molecules out of a pocket.(32) There are previous works that described positive ΔCp thermodynamics for the protein–small ligand interactions; the positive heat capacity change is thought to result when the ligand binds to an exposed nonpolar surface.(36,37) Based on the model structures, we calculated the solvent-accessible surface area (ASA) of Hsp90 in each complex. As shown in Table 1, the nonpolar and polar ASAs of Hsp90 in the THS-510 complex (5809.7 and 4979.4 Å2) were larger than those in the TAS-116 complex (5785.9 and 4900.9 Å2), respectively. This difference was derived from the empty ATP-binding pocket of Hsp90. We speculate that the hydrophobic surface of the ATP-binding pocket is exposed when the THS-510 binding induces α-helix formation of the ATP lid region.

    Based on the putative binding mode of THS-510, we proposed that there would be an affinity enhancement if THS-510 was linked to a compound that filled the ATP-binding pocket of Hsp90. Actually, THS-762 (Figure 5c), a conjugate of THS-510 and THS-828, showed the affinity enhancement. ITC revealed that the reaction of THS-762 with human Hsp90α NTD was exothermic (Figure 5a). We could not perform the ITC experiment with THS-828 due to its low solubility. In comparison with the thermodynamic parameters of THS-510, THS-762 had a higher binding affinity, with an entropic gain and an enthalpic loss (Figure 5a). In THS-762, hydrogen-bond networks between the benzamide moiety, Asp93, and water molecules are expected to contribute to the enthalpic gain, such as the case of TAS 116 analog 16d,(27) and the cyclohexanol group at the solvent front may contribute to the entropy gain. The decrease in favorable binding enthalpy means that enthalpy–entropy compensation occurred in the binding. Thus, TAS-116 is a favorable inhibitor, possessing the enthalpic contribution based on THS-510.

    Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The cytosolic Hsp90-selective inhibitor TAS-116 has a best-in-class potential as an anticancer drug; clinical trials are ongoing.(23,24) In this study, we investigated the modes of binding of TAS-116 and its analogs. Co-crystal structures of Hsp90α with the TAS-116 analogs THS-828 (PDB ID: 3WQ9) and 16d (PDB ID: 5ZR3)(27) showed that the compounds bind to the Hsp90α helical form and interact with the ATP-binding pocket. Unlike the other tested inhibitors, TAS-116 bound to the Hsp90α mutant F138L almost as well as it bound to the wild-type protein. By binding to the third, hydrophobic pocket on Hsp90, TAS-116 has a reduced dependency on F138 compared to the other inhibitors tested, although modeling suggests that TAS-116 forms π–π interactions with F138. We also analyzed a fragment of TAS-116, THS-510, which includes only the moiety that binds to the third pocket. ITC experiments showed that THS-510 binds Hsp90α in an enthalpy-driven manner. THS-828, 16d, TAS-116, and THS-510 all show cytosolic Hsp90 selectivity, implying that inhibitors that bind to the third pocket are highly selective for cytosolic Hsp90. Tricyclic compounds have also been reported to bind Hsp90 in the third pocket region.(32)
    SPR analysis showed that TAS-116 has a very slow off rate, likely because it binds to Hsp90 in a conformation-specific manner. We hypothesize that TAS-116 functions as a conformation lock for cytosolic Hsp90. As conformational change in Hsp90 is needed for its ATPase activity, TAS-116 might block this conformational change. Paralog inhibitors such as the Grp94 inhibitor PU-H54 and the TRAP1 inhibitor BIIB021 bind mainly in the ATP-binding pocket (Figure S7).(38) In addition, it was reported that XL-888 occupies the ATP-binding pocket, the second pocket, and the extended space of the second pocket of Hsp90 in the co-crystal structure,(39) but the helical structure of the ATP lid region was disordered. Therefore, the large conformational changes of the ATP lid in Hsp90 and the positions of some residues in the second pocket and the third pocket are crucial for the isoform selectivity of Hsp90 inhibitors. As the roles of Hsp90 isoforms TRAP-1 and Grp94 are not fully understood, whether or not cytosolic selectivity will reduce the risk of adverse effects or influence antitumor activity are not known. TAS-116 and analogs will provide the opportunity to study the significance of isoform selectivity. In situ analyses of the kinetics of isoform-specific inhibition at the cellular level using fluorescently labeled derivatives of TAS-116 that retain isoform selectivity may clarify the functions of Hsp90 isoforms in more detail.
    In this study, we utilized a combination of biophysical methods to analyze the binding modes of the evaluated inhibitors. Although ITC is a powerful tool for investigation of label-free investigation of ligand interactions with proteins, issues with compound solubility or aggregation may limit its use. We were not able to detect the thermodynamic binding energy of THS-828 to Hsp90 because THS-828 was not soluble. This makes it difficult to compare thermodynamic binding parameters of a series of compounds. However, thermodynamic analysis does provide information that can guide drug design. For example, knowledge of whether binding was enthalpically or entropically driven was suggestive of modes of action. Moreover, competitive ITC analyses revealed that THS-510 did not interact with the ATP-binding pocket. Based on this observation, hybrid compounds of THS-510 and the ATP-binding pocket binders were synthesized, which were bound with higher affinity to Hsp90 than THS-510. From this result, it is expected that a hybrid design combining THS-510 and other Hsp90 inhibitors will provide a novel type of Hsp90 inhibitor. In the case of Hsp90, co-crystal structures helped shape the design of the hybrid compound. However, even in the absence of co-crystal structures, competitive ITC analysis can reveal binding locations of lead compounds.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this study, the cytosolic Hsp90-selective inhibitor TAS-116 and analogs were analyzed using biophysical techniques including ITC. The combined use of competitive ITC analysis and molecular modeling enabled the prediction of binding modes and revealed that compounds that bind to the third binding pocket of Hsp90 interacted with an enthalpy gain and induced the rigid conformation of ATP lid region. The third binding pocket of the compound is thought to determine the cytosolic Hsp90 binding selectivity and activity. A deeper understanding of isoform selectivity of Hsp90 will facilitate the design of additional compounds and will lead to a greater understanding of the roles of Hsp90 paralogs in diseases.
