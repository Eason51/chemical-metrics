    Abstract

    It is very promising to target the extracellular domain of epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) for developing novel and selective anticancer therapies. Herein, we report the discovery of a novel small molecule, M-2-5, from a one-bead-two-compound (OBTC) cyclic γ-AApeptide library. The molecule was found to bind tightly to the extracellular domain of EGFR. Intriguingly, this molecule could also effectively antagonize EGF-stimulated EGFR phosphorylation and downstream signal transduction. Furthermore, together with its remarkable resistance to proteolytic degradation, M-2-5 was shown to effectively inhibit cell proliferation and migration in vitro and suppresses the growth of tumor in the A549 xenograft model in vivo, highlighting its potential therapeutic application for cancer treatment.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    As a member of the receptor tyrosine kinase family, epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) plays a crucial role in key cellular transduction pathways and regulation of growth and differentiation of many cell types.(1,2) Previous studies suggest that EGFR is abnormally expressed in a wide variety of human epithelial malignancies, such as glioma, lung cancer, and colon cancer, and therefore has been extensively used as the target for the development of drugs for the treatment of cancer, which is caused by epidermal growth factor receptor upregulation.(3−6)
    EGFR, also known as ErbB-1, is a transmembrane protein that has a ligand-binding extracellular domain, a transmembrane domain, and an intracellular domain with kinase activity. As such, monoclonal antibodies that bind to the extracellular domain, as well as small molecules targeting the intracellular tyrosine kinase domain of EGFR, are the two main approaches currently undertaken to inhibit EGFR functions for cancer therapy.(7,8) However, small-molecule EGFR inhibitors such as erlotinib, gefitinib, afatinib, and so on,(9−11) which are known to target the intracellular tyrosine kinase domain of EGFR and thereby inhibiting EGFR autophosphorylation and downstream signal transduction, lead to rapid intrinsic or acquired resistance. Meanwhile, these small tyrosine kinase inhibitors lack specificity, and as a result, they generally exhibit severe toxic side effects, limiting the prolonged use of these drugs in clinical therapy. On the other hand, anti-EGFR monoclonal antibodies, such as cetuximab(12) and matuzumab,(13) which could recognize the extracellular domain of EGFR exclusively with high affinity and specificity, have already been successfully developed for clinical therapeutic strategies.
    Despite the success of EGFR-targeted drugs, there are high demands for EGFR therapeutics with lower cost and improved efficacy. Peptides that bind to the extracellular domain of EGFR have been developed through phage display or virtual screening;(14,15) however, they were subsequently investigated as EGFR-targeting ligands, whereas their own ability to mimic monoclonal antibody and affect EGFR signaling was not reported. Meanwhile, the peptides are susceptible to rapid proteolytic degradation. An alternative strategy could take advantage of peptidomimetics that mimic the structure and function of peptides. Recently, we have designed a class of peptidomimetics, γ-AApeptides, which are resistant to enzymatic hydrolysis.(16−18) γ-AApeptides are able to adopt robust helix stabilized by sulfonamido moieties and hydrogen bonding,(19−22) allowing the rational design of protein domain mimetics for the recognition of protein surface and disruption of crucial protein–protein interactions.(18) Additionally, their vast chemodiversity and modular synthetic capability warrant γ-AApeptides excellent candidates for the creation of combinatorial libraries, which could provide unnatural ligands binding to biomacromolecules including nucleic acids and proteins with high affinity and specificity.(23) Indeed, the unique macrocyclic γ-AApeptides framework provided a novel molecular scaffold with good biocompatibility and high stability for studying the biomacromolecular interaction and developing potential drug candidates and molecular probes.(24−26) In this work, we report our effort in the identification of γ-AApeptides-based ligands that recognize the EGFR extracellular domain through the screening of a one-bead-two-compound (OBTC) γ-AApeptides combinatorial library with a diversity of 320 000. Fortunately, we were able to discover novel cyclic γ-AApeptides that exhibited excellent binding activity to EGFR. It is intriguing that one cyclic γ-AApeptide could effectively inhibit EGF-stimulated EGFR phosphorylation and block its downstream signaling pathway. Moreover, this compound could efficiently inhibit cell proliferation and cell migration and exhibited potent efficacy in the inhibition of tumor growth on an A549 lung cancer xenograft model.

    Library Synthesis and Screening

    The OBTC γ-AApeptide library was prepared on the solid phase following the previously reported protocol.(24) In the library, each TentaGel bead was manipulated to bear two layers, with a cyclic γ-AApeptide on the outer layer and a unique peptide coding tag attached onto the inner layer. The cyclic γ-AApeptide was constructed through combinational synthesis using five γ-AApeptides building blocks and eight side chains, and the cyclization was achieved through the formation of the thioether bridge by sulfur-mediated SN2 reaction (Figure 1A). As a result, the OBTC combinatorial library was expected to have a theoretical diversity of 320 000 compounds which were displayed in triple. The linear peptide coding tag contained seven α-amino acid residues, which specifically codes every cyclic γ-AApeptide on the same bead.
    The library was first assessed for its quality after preparation. The MALDI-TOF MS/MS analysis of 10 randomly selected beads revealed that nine beads give unambiguous fragmentation patterns, indicating that the library quality is satisfactory. The on-bead screening for the extracellular domain of EGFR was subsequently conducted.(24−26) In brief, the OBTC library was first incubated with Fc-Tagged EGFR extracellular domain and then incubated with Dylight 549 conjugated Goat antihuman IgG Fc secondary antibody. Beads showing strong red fluorescence (Figure S1, Supporting Information) were fished out from the library and then treated with guanidium chloride (GdmCl) to denature any binding proteins. Next, the beads were treated with CNBr to cleave off the encoding peptide tags from the inner layers, which were sequenced by tandem MS/MS of MALDI afterwards. The chemical structures of five beads could be unambiguously determined.

    Cyclic Peptides M-2-5 Binds to EGFR and Inhibits EGFR, AKT, and ERK Phosphorylation In Vitro

    We first determined whether the identified hits could bind to EGFR in vitro. To this end, fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) labeled hits (Figure S2 in the Supporting Information) were resynthesized and tested for their binding affinity toward the extracellular domain of EGFR using a fluorescence polarization (FP) assay. To our delight, two exhibited high binding affinity toward EGFR; M-2-2-F bound to EGFR with a KD of 0.55 μM (Figure 1B) while M-2-5-F had a relatively better binding with a KD of 0.43 μM (Figure 1C), and the others show a quite weak binding to EGFR (Figure S3 in the Supporting Information).
    The promising binding affinity prompted us to test the activity of the hits M-2-2 (Figure 2B) and M-2-5 (Figure 2C) at the cellular level. It is recognized that the extracellular domain of EGFR contains four subdomains, with two of them being used for ligands binding and one of them is engaged in heterodimerization and homodimerization.(27,28) The EGFR ligand family is known to include seven transmembrane precursor proteins, including epidermal growth factor (EGF), transforming growth factor-α (TGF-α), and others.(29,30) Our cyclic AApeptides, which may bind to any sits on the extracellular domain of EGFR, may compete with natural ligands involved in EGFR binding, resulting in inhibition of the EGFR-mediated signaling pathway including tyrosine kinase activation (Figure 2A).
    To determine the effect of both M-2-2 and M-2-5 on the phosphorylation level of EGFR (P-EGFR), starved A549 cells were pretreated with M-2-2 or M-2-5 for 4 h before further stimulation with 100 ng/mL of the natural ligand EGF, and cell lysates were analyzed by western blotting. A549 cells were chosen due to their elevated expression of EGFR. As shown in Figure 2D, stimulation of cells with EGF led to significant tyrosine phosphorylation of EGFR. Interestingly, M-2-2 could enhance the activation of EGFR in the presence of EGF at a concentration of 20 μM (Figure 2D,E), suggesting that M-2-2 is a potential activator for EGFR phosphorylation by acting as functional mimetics of EGFR ligands. Intriguingly, at the same concentration, M-2-5 blocked a vast majority of EGFR activation (Figure 2D,E), which indicated that M-2-5 is a potent inhibitor of EGFR. Although M-2-2 is of considerable interest for EGFR activation, which is beyond the scope of this article, we focused on attention on M-2-5 by investigating the ability of M-2-5 to inhibit EGF-stimulated tyrosine phosphorylation of EGFR and downstream ERK and AKT pathways in a dose-dependent manner. Pretreatment of starved A549 cells with M-2-5 (0–50 μM) for 4 h (Figure 2F) resulted in a dose-dependent inhibition of EGF stimulation of EGFR phosphorylation (IC50 ∼ 17.7 μM) and an almost full blockage at 25 μM or more. Moreover, downstream phosphorylation of both ERK and AKT were found to be inhibited by M-2-5 as well with a similar potency (IC50 for ERK is 11.2 μM; IC50 for AKT is 8.75 μM) in a concentration-dependent manner (Figure 2F,G). Together, these data indicated that M-2-5 is an effective inhibitor of EGFR and its downstream signaling pathways.

    M-2-5 Inhibits Cancer Cell Proliferation and Cell Migration In Vitro

    As cell proliferation is an important aspect of tumorigenesis, the cell proliferation assay was conducted to analyze the impact of M-2-5 on EGF-driven proliferation of A549 cells in vitro. A549 cells were treated with serum-reduced EGF-containing (100 ng/mL) DMEM in the presence of M-2-5 at different concentrations (0–25 μM) for 48 h. As shown in Figure 2H, upon the stimulation of EGF, A549 cells demonstrated a significantly enhanced proliferation; however, M-2-5 exhibited a strong antiproliferative activity with an IC50 of 8.23 μM (Figure 2H) and a complete suppression of cell proliferation at ∼10 μM compared to the control group that without EGF stimulation. Indeed, M-2-5 exhibited excellent selectivity toward cancer cells over normal cells (∼8 fold), as its IC50 for human embryonic kidney HEK293 cells was found to be 62.1 μM.
    We next investigated M-2-5’s inhibitory effect on cell migration of A549 cells, which plays an important role in tumor metastatic activity. First, a scratch-wound assay was established to investigate cell migratory behaviors. As expected, the percent of the wound closed was notably decreased upon treatment with 25 μM M-2-5 (15.2%) for 30 h, leading to 40% decrease compared with untreated control A549 cells (55.3%) (Figure 3A,B). Similar suppression in migration ability was also observed in transwell assay, as shown in Figure 3C. Compared with the untreated control group, the number of migrating cells were distinctly increased to 133.9% in the presence of EGF (Figure 3D), which were dramatically decreased to 64.8% with M-2-5 (25 μM) treatment. Taken together, M-2-5 has been shown to significantly inhibit A549 cell proliferation and cell migration ability.

    Stability of M-2-5 toward Enzymatic Hydrolysis

    As M-2-5 is a promising ligand to block EGFR signaling in vitro, we thus set out to further evaluate its enzymatic stability against pronase (the enzyme that is capable of digesting regular peptides into single amino acids), which could provide a strong basis for their potential biological activity in vivo. As anticipated, incubation of M-2-5 with pronase for 20 h did not lead to detectable degradation (Figure S4 in the Supporting Information), demonstrating its remarkable resistance to proteolytic degradation.

    Parallel Artificial Membrane Permeability Assays (PAMPA) of M-2-5

    The parallel artificial membrane permeability assay (PAMPA) is widely used as a high-throughput permeability assay to evaluate drug candidates.(31) As such, we conducted both the blood–brain barrier (BBB) parallel artificial membrane permeability assay (PAMPA-BBB) and the gastrointestinal tract (GIT) parallel artificial membrane permeability assay (PAMPA-GIT) to further evaluate the potential of M-2-5 as a drug candidate. The PAMPA-BBB is used to predict the ability of a drug to cross the BBB.(32) The PAMPA-GIT is used for the evaluation of how the oral drug candidate might be absorbed across the entire GIT.(33) In the PAMPA-BBB assay, we used Verapamil and Lidocaine as the positive and negative control, respectively. Verapamil can easily cross the BBB,(34) exhibiting a very high permeability (Papp) value in permeability assay (Papp > 20 × 10–6 cm/s), whereas Lidocaine could hardly pass through the BBB. As shown in Figure 4A, Verapamil in the concentration of 50 μM exhibited a favorable Papp ((169 ± 4) × 10–6 cm/s), as we expected, Lidocaine displayed a lower Papp value ((94 ± 3) × 10–6 cm/s) even with a quite high concentration. Surprisingly, M-2-5 showed a fabulous high Papp value of 266 (±6) × 10–6 cm/s even in the concentration of 50 μM. The result demonstrated that M-2-5 possesses the promising potential to be further investigated as a novel drug candidate. For the PAMPA-GIT assay, it is significant to test different PH conditions since human intraluminal pH is different in stomach, duodenum, ileum, cecum, and rectum.(35) Carbamazepine and antipyrine were included as the positive and negative control for the assay. Carbamazepine is fully orally bioavailable (>90% absorption in the human gastrointestinal tract); however, antipyrine is considered as a poorly orally bioavailable compound that gives low Papp values. As shown in Figure 5B, M-2-5 exhibited favorable Papp without pH-dependence. The findings indicated the M-2-5 has the potential of oral bioavailability for future drug development.

    Short-Term Therapy of M-2-5 In Vivo

    Based upon the observed strong inhibition of cell proliferation and migration, the ability to inhibit EGFR cell signaling pathways in vitro, as well as the remarkable stability against proteolytic degradation, we next evaluated the antitumor activity of M-2-5 in a A549 lung tumor xenograft mouse model in vivo. PBS was used as a negative control, while C225 (cetuximab), a monoclonal antibody, was used as a positive control, which was approved by FDA for treating lungs, head, and neck cancer. Mice were intraperitoneal administered with PBS, C225 (40 mg/kg) and M-2-5 (10, 50 mg/kg), respectively, every 2 days for seven times. After another 6 days post injection, mice were then sacrificed, from which tumors were resected. As shown in Figure 5A, a substantial reduction in the tumor volume was observed comparing the PBS group with the group receiving C225 therapy. Though no significant differences were found for 10 mg/kg of M-2-5 treatment, the injection of M-2-5 in high concentration (50 mg/kg) demonstrated significant suppression of tumor growth. In addition, injection of the compound did not affect the body weight gain of A549 tumor xenografted mice (Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). To confirm the expression level of P-EGFR and downstream signaling, total proteins were then extracted from the tumor tissues in each group and subjected to immunoblotting of P-EGFR, P-ARK, and P-ERK. Western blot analysis (Figure 5B) show that either C225 or M-2-5 (50 mg/kg) causes a dramatic decrease in P-EGFR and P-AKT, whereas M-2-5 (50 mg/kg) treatment exhibited slightly weaker efficiency in the inhibition of P-ERK compared to C225, which may account for the difference on inhibition of tumor growth. In addition, the level of phosphorylation for EGFR in A549 tumor tissues was investigated by immunohistochemical staining. Consistent with western blot analysis, the staining intensities of P-EGFR, P-AKT, and P-ERK were decreased, respectively, compared with the PBS group (Figure 5C). Overall, the in vivo studies revealed that M-2-5 exhibited similar antitumor efficacy compared with cetuximab in the A549 xenografted model. Compared to cetuximab, M-2-5 has much smaller molecular weight and therefore low production cost. Together with its similar function to monoclonal antibody and its high resistance toward proteolytic degradation, it could be further explored as promising anticancer agents for clinical drug development.

    Molecular Docking Study

    As M-2-5 could directly target the extracellular domain of EGFR and show comparable in vivo efficacy, its binding mode maybe analogous to that of cetuximab. As such, we carried a molecular docking study and tried to gain insight into the binding of M-2-5 toward EGFR. As shown in Figure 6, the docking of M-2-5 with the extracellular domain of EGFR (PDB: 1YY9) suggested that M-2-5 could occupy the cetuximab binding site of EGFR. It was observed that an amino group and a carbonyl group of M-2-5, respectively, oriented to form two hydrogen bonds with glutamine side chains in EGFR (Q384 and Q411). It is noticed that Q384 is also the key residue for the interaction between EGFR and cetuximab.(36) The docking results strongly supported the potential of M-2-5 as a novel artificial antibody targeting EGFR.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, through screening an OBTC combinatorial library, a novel cyclic γ-AApeptides M-2-5 was identified to inhibit EGFR phosphorylation. Our result implicated that M-2-5 could interfere EGFR-EGF binding, resulting in inhibition of EGFR activation and downstream signaling pathways, as well as suppression of cell proliferation and migration. In addition, unlike regular peptides, M-2-5 is completely resistant to proteolytic degradation. More importantly, M-2-5 also shows excellent antitumor efficacy in vivo. Compared with known small molecular inhibitors of EGFR, which are mainly tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs), M-2-5 has a few advantages. First, unlike those tyrosine kinase inhibitors which targeting kinase site in the intracellular domain of EGFR, M-2-5 targets the extracellular domain of EGFR, and therefore it does not have to have good cell permeability. Meanwhile, it is known that tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) lack good selectivity as the kinase sites are highly conserved; this is why generally TKIs exhibit high toxicity and frequently lead to drug resistance. On the contrary, M-2-5 shows excellent selectivity between cancer cells and normal cells. The ability of M-2-5 to mimic monoclonal antibody, as well as its small molecular weight and high stability, could enable it a promising candidate for the discovery of novel anticancer agents. The result also suggested that the approach of OBTC γ-AApeptide library provides an effective strategy for discovering human cancer therapy.

    One-Bead-Two-Compound Library Synthesis, Screening, and Analysis

    The one-bead-two-compound library was synthesized as our previous work(24−26) and the scheme for library synthesis is provided in the Supporting Information.
    For the EGFR-targeted library screening, it contains two main parts, prescreening and screening.(37,38) First, for the prescreening, all of the TentaGel beads were swelled in DMF for 1 h. After being washed five times with Tris buffer, the beads were equilibrated in Tris buffer overnight at room temperature, followed by incubation with the blocking buffer (1% BSA in Tris buffer with a 1000× excess of Escherichia coli lysate) for 1 h. After a thorough wash with Tris buffer, the beads were incubated with Goat antihuman IgG Fc cross-adsorbed secondary antibody, Dylight 549 (1:1000 dilution), for 2 h at room temperature. The beads were washed five times with the Tris buffer and then the beads emitting red fluorescence were picked up manually and excluded from formal screening.
    Second, for the screening, the rest of the beads after prescreening were washed with Tris buffer and treated with 8 M guandine·HCl at room temperature, after 1 h, the beads were washed by DI water (5×), tris buffer (5×), and DMF (5×). The beads were then incubated in DMF for 1 h, followed by washing and equilibration in Tris buffer overnight. The beads were incubated in 1% BSA/Tris buffer and 1000× excess of E. coli lysate for 1 h at room temperature. After washed five times Tris buffer, the beads were incubated with EGFR protein at a concentration of 50 nM for 4 h at room temperature with a 1000× excess of E. coli lysate. After the thorough wash with Tris buffer, the library beads were incubated with and Goat antihuman IgG Fc cross-adsorbed secondary antibody, Dylight 549 (1:1000 dilution), for 2 h at room temperature. The beads were washed five times with the Tris buffer and then the beads emitting red fluorescence were picked up for future analysis.
    For the hit structure analysis, each hit was transferred to an Eppendorf microtube and denatured in 100 μL 8 M guanidine·HCl for 1 h at room temperature. The bead was rinsed with Tris buffer 3 × 10 min, water 3 × 10 min, DMF 3 × 10 min, and ACN 3 × 10 min. At last, the resin was placed in ACN overnight in each microtube and then ACN was evaporated. The bead was incubated in the cocktail of 5:4:1 (v/v/v) of ACN/glacial acetic acid/H2O containing cyanogen bromide (CNBr) at a concentration of 50 mg/mL overnight at room temperature. The cleavage solution was then evaporated, and the cleaved peptide was dissolved in ACN/H2O (4:1) and subjected to MALDI-TOF for MS/MS analysis.

    Synthesis of Cyclic γ-AApeptides

The Fmoc protection group was then removed, followed by the desired building blocks needed for the sequence synthesis. After the γ-AApeptides were cyclized, the Dde group was removed and Fmoc-β-Ala was added, the Fmoc protection group was then removed and FITC (2 equiv) and DIPEA (6 equiv) in DMF were added to the resin and shaken for 12 h at room temperature. The FITC-labeled cyclic γ-AApeptides was cleaved by 1:1 (v/v) DCM/TFA containing 2% triisopropylsilane. The crude was purified by the Waters HPLC system.

    Transwell Assay
