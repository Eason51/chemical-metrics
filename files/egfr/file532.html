    Abstract

    Residence time and more recently the association rate constant kon are increasingly acknowledged as important parameters for in vivo efficacy and safety of drugs. However, their broader consideration in drug development is limited by a lack of knowledge of how to optimize these parameters. In this study on a set of 176 heat shock protein 90 inhibitors, structure–kinetic relationships, X-ray crystallography, and molecular dynamics simulations were combined to retrieve a concrete scheme of how to rationally slow down on-rates. We discovered that an increased ligand desolvation barrier by introducing polar substituents resulted in a significant kon decrease. The slowdown was accomplished by introducing polar moieties to those parts of the ligand that point toward a hydrophobic cavity. We validated this scheme by increasing polarity of three Hsp90 inhibitors and observed a 9-, 13-, and 45-fold slowdown of on-rates and a 9-fold prolongation in residence time. This prolongation was driven by transition state destabilization rather than ground state stabilization.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Early stage drug discovery programs are still mostly guided by binding affinity optimization. However, in recent years it has been recognized that the lifetime of the binary drug target complex (the drug residence time) is a key determinant for clinical success of drug candidates.(1−3) On the basis of the relationship between kinetic parameters and affinity, the residence time can be increased by either ground state (GS) stabilization(4) or transition state (TS) destabilization (Figure 1).(5−9) According to the transition state theory,(10) the TS represents the highest barrier during the binding event. The binding free energy (ΔGD) relates to the energy difference between the unbound and bound state and represents the GS. Improved interactions in the drug receptor complex (ΔΔGD) stabilize the GS and thus can increase the ΔG⧧off and the residence time (Figure 1). Destabilization of the TS by unfavorable interactions along the binding pathway (ΔΔG⧧on) can likewise increase ΔGoff and the residence time (Figure 1).
    Lapatinib was the first drug approved by FDA, whose label includes long residence time as mechanism of action. Lapatinib is only 1.6-fold more affine than erlotinib, hence showing similar GS stabilization. However, lapatinib dissociates 70-fold slower from the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR). The slower dissociation derives from the considerably higher energy barrier (TS) along the binding pathway, which lapatinib has to overcome (Figure 1). As the energy barrier, and therefore the destabilization of the TS, is directly linked to kon, lapatinib shows a 43-fold slower on-rate.(11) According to the principle of microscopic reversibility, barriers along the unbinding pathway are the same as on the binding pathway. Hence a better molecular understanding of the physicochemical features determining kon will eventually contribute to a more detailed model of the dissociation process.
    The importance of kon has been neglected for a long time, as it was considered to be invariant within lead-like series. Furthermore, any limitations could in theory be overcome by dosing at higher concentrations. Within the public–private partnership K4DD (Kinetics for Drug Discovery)(12) so far 387 drug-like compounds have been tested against pharmaceutically relevant targets resulting in 847 sets of kon, koff, and KD values.(13) Interestingly, the variance in affinities and on-rates was nearly identical. Both parameters cover 7 orders of magnitude, which is in good agreement with Schoop and Dey’s observation of an overall on-rate range of 102–108 M–1 s–1.(6)
    Apart from its contribution to residence time, kon itself is emerging as a key parameter in drug design. Several studies have indicated that kon can be equally important for target occupancy as koff.(14−16) Most recently Sykes et al. published the indisputable clinical relevance of kon. Measured on- and off-rates for antipsychotic D2 receptor antagonists showed a significant relation between kon and extrapyramidal side effects (EPS) in the clinic. Interestingly, no correlation between koff and EPS was found.(17) Furthermore, fast kon has been shown to be of critical importance for the efficacy of the anticoagulants ximelagatran and dabigatran,(18) which is related to their rapid onset of action. Moreover, Walkup et al. introduced a mechanistic PD model, which relies on koff as well as kon values.(19)
    These findings ask for a deeper understanding of structure–kinetic relationship (SKR) in order to rationally optimize the on-rate. One great obstacle for a detailed analysis is the nature of the elusive, not directly observable TS that determines association kinetics.(20) In this context, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations are used to gain information about energy barriers during the binding event. These simulations have identified energetically costly conformational changes of the protein, including loop–helix conversions, during the binding event.(5,21−23) MD studies have also identified desolvation effects as a critical barrier in the binding event. Dror et al.(24) reported hydrophobic ligand and receptor desolvation of the extracellular vestibule of the β2-adrenergic receptor as the main rate-limiting step in the ligand–receptor association. In another comprehensive analysis, Spagnuolo et al.(8) introduced steric clashes based on a TS model to successfully slow down the on-rate. In contrast to binding affinity data, which is abundantly accessible in the public domain, only limited sets of kinetic data from lead-like series are available for comprehensive SKR analysis.
    To contribute to a better SKR understanding, we analyzed a large collection of 176 kinetically characterized inhibitors of the model protein Hsp90, generated within the K4DD consortium.(12) Those inhibitors were derived from three lead-like series, with X-ray resolved binding modes. From SKR analysis and MD simulations we inferred a polar desolvation scheme to rationally slow down on-rates. Following that scheme we chemically modified three ligands and in fact observed a 9-, 13-, and 45-fold slowdown in on-rates. Most interestingly, in addition to the slow on-rate, one compound showed a 9-fold prolongation in residence time, despite loss of affinity (GS destabilization).

    Hsp90 Inhibitor Data Set

    The oncology target heat shock protein 90 (Hsp90) was an early example for the importance of binding kinetics for in vivo activity.(25) This target, among others, has been chosen by the K4DD consortium to shed light on the phenomenon of binding kinetics.(12) The N-terminal part of Hsp90 forms a flexible nucleotide binding site, which is in the focus of our study.
    In the framework of the K4DD project(12) we had the opportunity to work with a highly consistent data set of 176 Hsp90 inhibitors provided by Merck KGaA. In addition to SPR data, also eight newly resolved X-ray structures were available for structural insight. This comprises a unique data set, which offers the opportunity to systematically analyze molecular features driving binding kinetics. Chemistrywise the data set covers 73 indazole-, 74 resorcinol-, and 29 quinazoline-inhibitors. The indazole(26) and resorcinol(27) scaffolds are displayed in Figure 2A,B. In general, the molecular scaffolds vary on two substitution sites, R1 and R2. Cocrystals of the various scaffolds show good overlay of the R1 and R2 substitution sites (Figure S1 in Supporting Information), with R1 pointing into the hydrophobic binding cavity and R2 pointing toward the hydrophilic pocket entrance (Figure 2C,D).

    Global SKR Analysis on the Hsp90 Inhibitor Data Set

    A set of four easily interpretable physicochemical descriptors was computed for the entire Hsp90 data set (n = 176) (see Supplementary Methods in Supporting Information). We conducted Pearson correlation analysis of the descriptor set against on-rates. The analysis exhibited a significant negative correlation for molecular weight (R = −0.64; P < 0.001) and polar surface area (R = −0.52; P < 0.001), whereas no correlation was observed for compound lipophilicity and molecular flexibility (Figure S2).

    Comparative, Local SKR Analysis of “Cavity-Varying” and “Entrance-Varying” Inhibitors

    We selected 13 indazoles (Table 1) and nine resorcinols (Table 2), which vary only at one substitution site (R1). The substitution on R1 points into the hydrophobic cavity, once the inhibitor is bound to Hsp90 (Figure 2C,D). Local SKR analysis of these “cavity-varying” subsets revealed significant correlations of the on-rate with molecular weight (R = −0.76; P < 0.01) and polarity (R = −0.72; P < 0.01) for the indazoles (Figure S3). Furthermore, we performed similar Pearson correlation analysis on 25 indazoles (Table 3) possessing a varying substitution on R2- the site facing toward the hydrophilic pocket entrance (Figure 2C,D). In contrast to the “cavity-varying” subsets, these “entrance-varying” data set showed very poor correlation (R = −0.20; P = 0.33 for polarity and R = −0.37; P = 0.07 for molecular weight) between chosen descriptors and the on-rate (Figure S3). The kinetic maps of these series can be found in Figure 2E. The negative correlation between polarity and the on-rate, observed within the “cavity-varying” subsets, prompted us to extend our descriptor set, employing desolvation free energy (ΔGdesolv) (see Supplementary Methods). Pearson correlation analysis between ΔGdesolv and the log10-transformed on-rate showed a strong discrepancy between the “cavity-varying” subsets (indazoles, R = −0.66, P < 0.05; resorcinols, R = −0.85, P < 0.01), on the one hand, and the “entrance-varying” indazoles (R = −0.10; P = 0.62) on the other hand (Figure 2F). The correlation results suggest that stripping off water molecules from polar ligand moieties while entering a hydrophobic cavity or traversing a hydrophobic entry pathway might present a considerable enthalpic barrier that slows down the on-rate.

    Polar Desolvation Scheme Identifies Slowest Associating Inhibitor

    To validate the desolvation scheme, we screened the Merck in-house chemical library for validation compounds. We extracted three indazoles carrying R1 substituents, with gradually increasing desolvation barriers. Starting from compound 2a, we selected its kinetically uncharacterized polar analogues 2b and 2c to complete a matched molecular triplet, the “five-membered ring triplet“ (Figure 3A). In the same manner, starting from compounds 3a and 3b, we picked compound 3c to constitute the “six-membered ring triplet” (Figure 3A). Subsequently, the respective desolvation free energy (ΔGdesolv) of those compounds was calculated (Figure 3A and Table 4) (see Supplementary Methods) and SPR analysis was performed (Figure 3C).

    In both triplets we observed a stepwise reduction of on-rates (Figure 3B), as the desolvation penalty of the R1 substitution increased. In total, kon decelerated stepwise 13-fold within the “five-membered ring triplet”. The most striking slowdown was the observed 45-fold deceleration within the “six-membered ring triplet”. Compound 3c turned out to be the slowest of all 176 Hsp90 inhibitors in the entire data set, showing a kon as slow as 1653 M–1 s–1 (Figure 3B).
    Slowdown of on-rates has frequently been related to structural protein adaptations during the binding event.(5,21) Thus, we calculated the rmsd differences of the pocket shaping residues for the cocrystals of compound 3b and its methoxy derivative 3c. The pocket shapes turned out to be close to identical with a pocket atom rmsd of 0.3 Å (Figure 3D), which rules out that the change of 1 order of magnitude in association rate is related to different pocket rearrangements. The carbonyl oxygens in 3b and 3c show crystal water interaction, proposing unchanged solvation of the oxygen throughout the binding event (see Figure S5 and Supporting Information). This interaction was also found among other Hsp90 inhibitor cocrystals (Figure S6).

    Nine-Fold Residence Time Slowdown Due to TS Destabilization

    Within the “six-membered ring triplet“, we also observed a 9-fold decrease in residence time (Figure 3E and Table 4). Similar to the on-rates, the residence times decreased stepwise with increased polar desolvation penalty. To investigate the influences of GS stabilization and TS destabilization on the residence times, we calculated the free energy values for kon and KD, respectively, according to Schoop and Dey(6) (Figure 3F). The results show that the increased residence time in 3c was due to a TS destabilization, while the GS was unchanged.
    Recently, Spagnuolo et al.(8) described the discovery of a slow dissociating inhibitor class on InhA in which the slow residence time was also driven by TS destabilization (Figure 1). Their study as well as our observations demonstrates the contribution of TS destabilization to a compound’s residence time. However, despite TS destabilization, we also observed invariant residence time when comparing compound 2a to 2c. In these cases, the destabilization of the TS was accompanied by a simultaneous destabilization of the GS, resulting in similar residence time (Figure 3F).

    Quinazoline Regioisomers Validate the Influence of a Hydrophobic Pocket Environment

    For a further strengthening of the polar desolvation hypothesis, regioisomers with similar physicochemical properties were selected from the Merck in-house compound library. The new compound set comprised three quinazolines possessing local variation of their polar substituent in either R1 or R2 (see Figure 4A for chemical structures of 6a–c). On one derivative (6b) the hydroxyl group is attached to the quinazoline scaffold (R2 substitution site); in the other (6c), the hydroxyl group is attached to the isoindoline moiety (R1 substitution site). Assuming a common binding mode of quinazolines (Figure 4B and Figure S7), the hydroxyl group of 6b points toward the hydrophilic pocket entrance and the hydroxyl group of 6c points into the hydrophobic cavity. SPR measurements showed a kon of 2.56 × 105 M–1 s–1 for the unsubstituted quinazoline 6a. The additional hydroxyl group on the isoindoline in R1 (compound 6c) slowed down the association rate 9-fold (Figure 4C, red chart). Despite sharing identical physicochemical properties with 6c, compound 6b showed a 2-fold acceleration of association rate (Figure 4C, red chart and Table 4). However, the slowdown of kon comparing 6a to 6c did not influence the residence time of the compound pair (Figure 4C, gray chart). The destabilization of the TS barrier was compensated by GS destabilization (Figure 4C, blue chart), most probably due to the unfavorable placement of a hydroxyl group in a hydrophobic environment.

    MD Simulations Confirm a Polar Desolvation Barrier as the Rate-Limiting Step along the Binding Event

    To get a better understanding about the barriers along the binding event, a set of MD simulations(5,24,28−31) using the quinazoline 6a (R1 = H, R2 = H) and two polar hydroxyl analogues 6b (R1 = H, R2 = OH) and 6c (R1 = OH, R2 = H) were performed. Starting coordinates were derived from PDB code 5OD7 (see Supplementary Methods). We chose a combination of steered MD simulations and umbrella sampling to determine the TS.(32) Employing steered MD simulations was one of the first attempts for detection of unbinding pathways in kinetic studies.(33,34)
    The potential of mean force (PMF) was calculated, which discloses energy changes along the binding pathway (Figure 4D, Experimental Section, and Supporting Information). In addition, we examined the solvation state of the hydroxyl group of 6b and 6c during the simulation path (red line in Figure 4D). While 6b showed interactions of the hydroxyl group with at least two water molecules at all times, the hydroxyl group of 6c had to strip off all its water molecules to enter the hydrophobic binding pocket.
    Starting from the bound complex of the quinazoline to Hsp90 (Figure 5A), the simulations showed a distinct energy barrier at 6.2 Å. Compound 6c shows the highest energy barrier along the pathway (red arrow in Figure 4D), which is in accordance with 6c possessing 9-fold and 20-fold slower on-rate than 6a and 6b, respectively (Table 4). Compound 6c is the only quinazoline with a hydroxyl substitution on the isoindoline moiety, which is embedded inside the hydrophobic pocket when bound. The high energy barrier detected using MD corresponds to the isoindoline moiety entering the hydrophobic binding pocket during the association process (Figure 5B).
    To put the TS (6.2 Å) into context, we compared it to a position of the ligand before entering the hydrophobic pocket. At this position (8.5 Å), 6c’s hydroxyl group on the isoindoline shows hydrogen interactions with water molecules (Figure 5C). Consequently, 6c’s hydroxyl moiety becomes desolvated upon entering the hydrophobic pocket (Figure 5B, TS). We conclude that the disruption of hydrogen bonds between the hydroxyl group of 6c and surrounding water molecules poses a considerable desolvation penalty. This penalty cannot be compensated by polar interactions of 6c at the TS as it lacks interaction partners in the hydrophobic pocket (Figure 5B). Broken uncompensated water interactions typically render as enthalpic barriers in thermodynamic decomposition.(35) Hence, we assume that the increased energy barrier (ΔG⧧on) observed in 2c, 3c, and 6c is of enthalpic nature. Our MD findings provide a mechanistic model for the observed kon slowdown suggested by our SKR studies (Figure 2F). Desolvation has been recognized as an important factor influencing the kinetics of ligand binding.(35,36) However, these studies focused on kinetic barriers due to water reorganization, including “pocket dewetting” upon ligand binding.(24,32,37) In our study we aimed to increase our understanding for the kon variance within a group of highly similar compounds, rather than a single binding event. In this respect, pocket desolvation does not provide a rationale for the large kon derivations in the data set (Figure S8). Our analysis led to the conclusion that enthalpic barriers due to polar ligand desolvation in an unfavorable hydrophobic pocket greatly impact the association kinetics. A literature search for enthalpic (ΔH⧧on) and entropic (−TΔS⧧on) contributions to the association barrier ΔG⧧on identified experimental data on various drugs and drug-like molecules for the drug targets MAP38α,(38) thrombin,(39) gpH3 receptor,(40) HIV1 protease,(41) and FGFR1(42) (see Table S1). Interestingly, in all those cases the enthalpic contribution to the association barrier was dominant.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we demonstrate a guideline that introduces enthalpic desolvation penalties to rationally slow down the on-rate of drug like compounds binding to the hydrophobic pocket of the heat shock protein 90. We prospectively validated the scheme by introducing polar moieties to indazoles and quinazolines. In fact, their corresponding polar analogues showed a kon slowdown by 13-, 45-, and 9-fold, respectively. One of the compounds turned out to be the slowest associating inhibitor among the 176 compounds encompassing Hsp90 data set. In addition, the introduced desolvation barrier, resembling a TS destabilization, led to a 9-fold prolongation in residence time, despite affinity loss.
    These findings are of substantial interest, as hydrophobic areas constitute an integral part of druggable sites in proteins. To our knowledge, this is the first validated scheme to prospectively optimize kon by chemical modification of drug-like molecules within a lead-like series. The work presented here provides guidance to chemists to optimize on-rates and therefore enables rational drug design of binding kinetics of drugs.

    Molecular Dynamics Simulations

    For this study, a center-of-mass (COM) pulling simulation followed by umbrella sampling was used to study three complex structures. The crystallographic structures of these complexes were obtained from Merck and modified using PDB code 5OD7, resolution of 1.62 Å (see Supplementary Methods). For the simulations, the GROMACS 5.0.4 package(43−46) was used in combination with the GROMOS54a7 force field(47) to define the parameters for the protein and the compounds. The force field has proven successful in a number of studies, including studies to quantify protein–ligand interactions.(48) The parameters for the compounds were derived based on the analogy of functional groups of the force field (see Table S2).(49−51) For the protein, the protonation state of charged amino acids was chosen to describe them at neutral pH. Therefore, Lys and Arg were protonated whereas Glu and Asp were deprotonated. The tautomeric form of His was selected based on a geometric criterion to allow for an optimal hydrogen bonding conformation.
    The complexes were positioned in a rectangular box using periodic boundary conditions. For the box dimensions a minimum protein-wall distance was set (1.0 nm), and in the pulling direction, the pulling distance (2 times the pulling distance) was additionally taken into account in order to avoid periodicity artifacts. Sodium and chloride ions were added to gain a neutral system, and the simple point charge (SPC) water model(52) was used. The system was energy minimized with the steepest descent method, followed by a two-step equilibration cycle. The first step was performed in an NVT ensemble for 100 ps followed by a second equilibration step under constant pressure (NpT, 1 bar, 1 ns). For the regulation of the pressure the Berendsen barostat(53) was chosen with a coupling constant of 5.0 ps and the V-rescale temperature coupling(54) (0.5 ps) was used to maintain the temperature constant at 300 K. During these steps, the atoms of the complexes were position restrained. To constrain the covalent bonds, the LINCS algorithm(55) was used. For the nonbonded interactions the short-range cutoff was set to 1.2 nm and the particle mesh Ewald algorithm(56) was applied to calculate the electrostatic interactions beyond this point. For the COM pulling simulation the protein was kept rigid and the compound was pulled out of the active site with a pull rate of 0.001 nm·ps–1 and a force constant of 1000 kJ mol–1 nm–2. The pulling distance was set to 3.0 nm to ensure that at the end of the simulation the compound is not in contact with the protein anymore. The compound was pulled out of the protein along a single reaction coordinate (y-axis). For the pulling a harmonic potential was used. Umbrella sampling was used to find energy changes along the pathway of the compounds. To sample all relevant configurations along the pathway, the spacing between the sampling windows was set to approximately 0.06 nm. For each window an NPT equilibration step (100 ps) and an umbrella sampling simulation (10 ns) were performed. At least 470 ns of umbrella sampling simulations were performed for each compound to calculate the potential of mean force (PMF). The PMF was computed with the weighted histogram analysis method (WHAM)(57) by selecting a series of overlapping umbrella sampling simulations with a COM distance between 0.3 and 3.0 nm. The free energy was set to be zero at 0.3 nm, and the PMF curves were smoothed by applying the Savitzky–Golay filter(58) in R. Additionally the Bayesian bootstrapping (200 bootstraps) of complete histograms was used to determine the statistical uncertainties of the PMF.(59) Further analyses include the calculation of the amount of water molecules in the vicinity of the compounds (see Figure S9). Water molecules having the oxygen atom within a distance of 0.36 nm to all atoms of the compounds were taken into account. Additionally, the surrounding water molecules of the substituted hydroxyl group were determined.
