    Abstract

    The specific targeting of oncogenic mutant epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) is a breakthrough in targeted cancer therapy and marks a drastic change in the treatment of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). The recurrent emergence of resistance to these targeted drugs requires the development of novel chemical entities that efficiently inhibit drug-resistant EGFR. Herein, we report the optimization process for a hit compound that has emerged from a phenotypic screen resulting in indazole-based compounds. These inhibitors are conformationally less flexible, target gatekeeper mutated drug-resistant EGFR-L858R/T790M, and covalently alkylate Cys797. Western blot analysis, as well as characterization of the binding kinetics and kinase selectivity profiling, substantiates our approach of targeting drug-resistant EGFR-L858R/T790M with inhibitors incorporating the indazole as hinge binder.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Targeted cancer therapy relies on the identification and effective modulation of druggable oncogenic drivers that induce the development and growth of tumor cells.(1) The targeting of protein kinases has been one means of addressing this line of investigation, particularly the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR).(2) This ErbB family transmembrane tyrosine kinase receptor plays a pivotal role in controlling cell proliferation, differentiation, survival, and migration in many epithelial cell types.(3-5) Dysregulation of EGFR can aberrantly trigger the EGFR-dependent pathways and is found to be a oncogenic driver in a subset of major cancer types such as breast, lung, and gastric cancer.(6-9) In a subset of non-small cell lung cancers (NSCLC), mutations in the EGFR kinase domain, mostly consisting of the point mutation L858R or exon 19 deletion (delE746_A750), induce a constitutive activation of the PI3K and rasGAP/kRas signaling pathways.(10, 11) Patients harboring these activating mutations dramatically benefit from treatment with reversible first-generation EGFR inhibitors (TKIs) such as gefitinib(12) (1) with objective response rates of 50–80% as compared to ∼35% with standard platinum-based chemotherapy.(13-16) Despite this substantial clinical benefit, patients eventually develop resistance and progress after a median 9–12 months. EGFR genotyping that was done with biopsies taken from progressing lesions of resistant tumors revealed point mutation of the gatekeeper residue (T790M) in 60% of the patients, thereby providing a strong rationale to target T790M mutations to overcome resistance.(17-19) The mutation that involves replacement of the threonine with methionine is thought to lead to increased affinity to ATP, but it also provokes a steric repulsion with 4-aminoquinazoline-based drugs such as gefitinib, thereby inducing less favorable binding geometry that diminishes the efficacy of these drugs.(20-22)
    Second-generation EGFR inhibitors such as afatinib(23) (2) possess a Michael-acceptor that covalently modifies a unique cysteine (Cys797) in the ATP binding cleft of EGFR to trap the inhibitor and to maximize the drug-target residence time.(24, 25) These covalent inhibitors showed promising effects in preclinical studies, but their efficacy in patients was insufficient for beneficial treatment of T790M drug resistance. On-target toxicity represented the dose-limiting factor and prevented clinically effective concentrations as the wild-type form of EGFR is likewise inhibited, which is correlated with severe side effects during therapy (e.g., skin rash and diarrhea).(26, 27)
    Currently, patients who suffer from the T790M drug resistance can be treated with third-generation EGFR inhibitors comprising CO-1686/rociletinib (3) and AZD9291/osimertinib (4), compounds that have been developed to selectively target the gatekeeper mutated variant of EGFR.(28-33) These inhibitors are structurally distinct from the classic 4-aminoquinazolines and feature a Michael-acceptor for covalent modification. The clinical success of these drugs and the insights we gained so far from second-generation EGFR inhibitors that failed in T790M drug-resistant patients identified the following crucial features required to sufficiently target EGFR-T790M: (i) a novel scaffold to specifically target the gatekeeper mutant of EGFR by avoiding a steric clash with Met790; (ii) sparing EGFR wild-type inhibition; and (iii) incorporating a reactive electrophile to covalently modify Cys797 to achieve maximum drug-target residence time.(34) However, the development of rociletinib (3) has been discontinued due to its metabolic instability. A non-covalent metabolite has been identified that causes raised blood glucose levels related to inhibition of the insulin-like growth factor 1 receptor and the insulin receptor.(35) Together these findings highlight the need for the development of novel chemical entities to efficiently inhibit gatekeeper mutant drug-resistant EGFR. Additionally, patients that have been treated with osimertinib (4) revealed tumor progression and drug resistance after mutation of the reactive cysteine to a less nucleophilic serine side chain (EGFR-C797S).(36) Targeting EGFR-C797S drug resistance in NSCLC patients will be one of the next challenges in personalized therapy and drug design.
    We recently reported an optimization process for a hit compound that has emerged from a phenotype screen of 80 NSCLC cell lines against approximately 1500 compounds. Combining a structure-based design approach together with X-ray crystallography, we developed a series of covalent indazole-based compounds, for example, compound 5, characterized by a strong kinase inhibition of the drug-resistant mutant of EGFR-L858R/T790M and a remarkable mutant-selectivity profile. Despite promising results in the biochemical evaluation, we observed only poor cellular potency. Conformational analysis of this compound class revealed a relatively high flexibility, and characterization of the binding kinetics showed an accordingly slow rate of covalent bond formation.(37) These results prompted us to reposition the acrylamide warhead to reduce the molecules flexibility and achieve an optimized alignment between the reactive cysteine of the receptor and the Michael-acceptor of the inhibitor (Figure S1).
    Herein, we present the structure-guided redesign of indazole-based derivatives with an optimized spatial arrangement to covalently target drug-resistant EGFR. Design, synthesis, and biological evaluation led to a series of novel analogues of our previously reported scaffold. These compounds elicit a potent inhibition of T790M mutant EGFR in biochemical settings that translates nicely into improved activity on pertinent NSCLC cell lines.

    Rational Design of Functionalized Indazole EGFR Inhibitors

    Focusing our efforts on designing inhibitors with improved activity toward cancer prominent mutants of EGFR, we already investigated the solid contribution of engineered cSrc-kinase (T338M/S345C), a reliable model system for the EGFR-T790M mutant.(38) As a result of these efforts, we recently solved the co-crystal structure of an inhibitor with a 5-methylpyrazole group as the hinge-binding motif that covalently addresses Cys345 while avoiding steric clashes with the Met338 gatekeeper (these residues correspond to Cys797 and Met790 in EGFR, respectively) (PDB ID: 5D11). In the process of hit optimization, we replaced the methylpyrazole moiety with the larger indazole ring to enlarge the hydrophobic space next to the nonpolar methionine gatekeeper side chain, resulting in compound 5 (Figure 1). This modification preserved the aminopyrazolic moiety as a hinge-binding element and increased the spatial occupancy of the kinase ATP pocket, resulting in significant improvements of selectivity and potency.(37) Inspired by the potential of the indazole to be a hinge binder as well as serving as an eligible structural platform for subsequent chemical modifications, we used the co-crystal structure as a starting point to design new covalent EGFR inhibitors. We manually fitted 6 and 7 to the closely related 5-methyl-3-aminopyrazole inhibitor in complex with drug-resistant cSrc (PDB ID: 5D11) to allow for the aminoindazole to form hydrogen-bond interactions with the kinase backbone. In a next step, we aligned this structure to the ATP pocket of T790M mutant EGFR kinase domain (PDB ID: 5J9Y) to result in proposed binding modes for 6–7 in this mutant kinase (Figure S1B). Because the correct orientation of the electrophile toward the cysteine thiol group is a fundamental parameter that we aimed to optimize, we envisaged that attaching the reactive acrylamide to the aniline ring would be beneficial to reduce the flexibility of the reacting partners and to align them in the proper juxtaposition (Figure S1). To increase the solubility of the compounds, we inserted a solubilizing group projecting outward into the solvent channel of the catalytic cleft. The indazole scaffold furnished the proper spatial and chemical features to accommodate the space between the hinge region and the gatekeeper residue, without undergoing steric clashes with the methionine side chain. Moreover, this scaffold offered the possibility for further chemical functionalizations that could increase the binding affinity. On the basis of the generated structures, we directed our attention to analogues with aromatic extensions in the 6-position of the indazole to extend toward the binding site between the gatekeeper residue and Lys745, as observed with previously reported crystal structures (PDB ID 5J9Y) (Figure 2A–C).(39) Aromatic substituents in the 5-position of the indazole core were predicted to interact with the Met gatekeeper and can occupy the space around Lys745 (Figure 2D).

    Synthesis of Functionalized Indazole EGFR Inhibitors

    Substituted compounds, 6 and 7 (Figure 3), and unsubstituted compounds, 30 and 31 (Figure S1), were synthesized according to a common synthetic approach. Starting from the commercially available 6-bromo-1H-indazole (8) and 5-bromo-1H-indazole (9), a selective iodination in the 3-position was fulfilled by treating the unprotected indazoles with iodine under basic conditions.(40, 41) To avert undesired reactivity in the subsequent palladium-catalyzed chemical modifications, we proceeded protecting the free indazolic NH as tetrahydropyran-2-yl (THP) derivative. It is known that the insertion of this group can generate a mixture of N-1 and N-2 protected regioisomers when carried out under mild acidic catalysis;(42) therefore, to promote the formation of the thermodynamically more stable N-1 derivative, we used more harsh conditions such as CF3SO3H as catalyst in refluxing chloroform.(43, 44) Leveraging the different reactivities of the halogen leaving groups (I ≫ Br) in cross-coupling reactions and the activation of the 3-position of the indazole core,(45, 46) we regioselectively achieved the corresponding Buchwald amination products (14 and 15) by reacting the intermediates 12 and 13 with 4-fluoro-2-methoxy-5-nitroaniline using Xantphos as Pd ligand, as reported in the literature.(32, 46-48) The so-obtained synthons were still endowed with a reactive halogen atom and were then used as common building blocks to substitute the indazole scaffold in 6- and 5-positions by Suzuki reaction with proper boronic acids/esters.(49) Intermediate 14 served also as a building block for compound 16l and underwent a further Buchwald reaction with 3-fluoroaniline at 90 °C. Nucleophilic aromatic substitution with N1,N1,N2-trimethylethane-1,2-diamine(32) on 16 and 17 and the following nitro group reduction afforded the anilines 20 and 21 in good yields. Finally, acylation of the amino group with acryloyl chloride and removal of the THP protecting group by TFA in dichloromethane led to the desired series of covalent inhibitors (6 and 7) equipped with the Michael-acceptor warhead (Scheme 1).
    For the synthesis of unsubstituted compounds, 32 and 33 (Figure S1), the commercially available 3-bromo-1H-indazole was THP protected at the N-1 position using a catalytic amount of para-toluenesulfonic acid (pTSA) in dichloromethane at room temperature.(46) As previously described, the so-obtained intermediate (23) underwent Buchwald amination with 4-fluoro-3-nitroaniline or 4-fluoro-2-methoxy-5-nitroaniline, affording compounds 24 and 25, respectively. Introduction of the alkyl amine ortho to the aromatic nitro group and its subsequent reduction gave the amines 28 and 29. Each of these was then reacted with acryloyl and propionyl chloride and THP deprotected with TFA, providing the desired unsubstituted indazolic inhibitors 30 and 31.

    Biochemical and Cellular Activity Characterization of EGFR Mutants

    First, we evaluated the inhibitory potency of our indazole-based compounds library in biochemical systems such as wild-type EGFR and the corresponding cancer-relevant mutant forms L858R and L858R/T790M. The activity of the inhibitors was determined as a function of the phosphorylation state of an artificial substrate whose levels were determined by homogeneous time-resolved FRET methodology. All compounds tested were shown to have a purity of more than 95% by means of NMR and LCMS analysis.
    To assess whether the orientation of the Michael-acceptor warhead allows for covalent bond formation with Cys797, we initially synthesized and tested unsubstituted indazole derivatives (30a,b and 31a,b) on our EGFR mutants (Table 1). Consistent with the formation of a covalent adduct, we observed a notable inhibitory activity only for compounds equipped with the acrylamide moiety (30a and 31a), while the reversible counterparts (30b and 31b) were completely inactive in inhibiting any form of EGFR. To our delight, 31a showed selectivity over EGFR wild-type, inhibiting solely the L858R and L858R/T790M mutants with IC50 values of 3352 and 462 nM, respectively. Moreover, 31a was almost 6-fold more potent than 30a, indicating the importance of the methoxy group for inhibitory activity. Therefore, we synthesized a focused set of derivatives equipped with a Michael-acceptor, based on the methoxy-bearing phenyl core (Table 2). According to our structural analysis, we considered compounds endowed with aromatic ring systems in the 6-position of the indazole as they had suitable hydrophobic and steric characteristics to accommodate the back pocket (Figure 2A–C). As compared to the unsubstituted inhibitors, the introduction of a 2-naphthyl substituent, resulting in compound 6a, provoked an increased activity of 85 nM against EGFR-L858R/T790M, and compound 6b, possessing the corresponding 1-naphthyl substituent, showed inhibitory activity in the single digit nanomolar range in the biochemical assays. Unfortunately, the selectivity over wild-type EGFR was slightly reduced with inhibitor 6b. However, the marked gain in biochemical potency during the development from inhibitor 30a to 6b is in good accordance with their ability to inhibit the growth of tumor cells, as determined in the EGFR-L858R/T790M drug-resistant cell line H1975 and in the EGFR-delE746_A750 activating mutation-carrying HCC827 cells utilizing the CellTiter-Glo assay. Compounds 30a and 31a showed only weak activity in the H1975 cellular assay with EC50 values of 6926 and 3527 nM, respectively, but inhibitors 6a and 6b displayed activities of 1610 and 1102 nM, respectively, and, even more remarkably, corresponding values of 2348 and 121 nM against the HCC827 cell line. To assess selectivity over EGFR wild-type as well as cellular potency, further derivatives containing heteroaromatic substituents were synthesized. Subsequent biological testing revealed a 2-fold increased cellular potency against H1975 cells while retaining biochemical activity for inhibitor 6c containing an isoquinolin-4-yl substituent. Interestingly, 6c showed increased activity (EC50 = 659 nM) against mutant kRas-bearing A549 cells, which might indicate off-target inhibition (Table S1). However, replacing the isoquinoline moiety with a N-methylindol-3-yl function resulted in a selective inhibitor 6d, with an IC50 of 4.8 nM against EGFR-L858R/T790M and 9.3 nM against wild-type EGFR. Moreover, 6d showed good activity in the HCC827 and H1975 cell lines with EC50 values of 400 and 417 nM, respectively, while having only a moderate effect on A431 cells harboring EGFR-wt (2206 nM) as well as in kRas mutant cell lines A549 and H358 with EC50 values between 2100 and 2600 nM. A comparable trend was observed with compounds containing pyridine substituents. Inhibitor 6e, bearing the pyridin-3-yl moiety, showed moderate activity in biochemical (15 nM against double mutant EGFR) and cellular (1041 nM in H1975 cells) testing, while 6f with a pyridin-4-yl substituent exhibited selectivity over wild-type and efficacy in perturbing cell growth. EGFR wild-type, L858R, and the double mutant were inhibited with IC50 values of 30, 14, and 5 nM, respectively, and 6f showed good inhibition of HCC827 and H1975 cells with 106 and 489 nM, respectively, while A431 cells were perturbed only with an EC50 of almost 4000 nM. To further explore the chemical space of the nitrogen-containing aromatic compounds, we prepared compounds 6g,h endowed with dimethylaniline substituents. They displayed only weak inhibition, both in the biochemical setting with IC50 values of 22 nM (6g) and 72 nM (6h) and in cellular tests with EC50 values of 1321 nM (6g) and 1050 nM (6h) against the drug-resistant mutant EGFR. Additionally, we aimed to determine whether hydrophobic groups could have beneficial effects on selectivity through their interaction with the methionine gatekeeper side chain, and therefore we included fluorinated compounds 6i–k. Among these, compound 6i containing the meta-fluorobenzene substituent was most active against EGFR-L858R/T790M with IC50 values of 5 nM in the biochemical assay and an EC50 of 608 nM in the cellular assay, but unfortunately this compound targeted EGFR wild-type to the same extent. Further investigations were directed toward secondary amine-bridged residues in the 6-position, which possess some degree of flexibility and nicely occupy the back pocket, as shown by structural analysis (Figure 2D). A corresponding compound (6l) was prepared by introduction of a meta-fluoroaniline group via Buchwald amination. This derivative showed inhibition in the subnanomolar range of all variants of EGFR in the biochemical evaluation and cellular EC50 values of 191 and 22 nM against relevant H1975 and HCC827 cell lines, while sparing the wild-type with an EC50 value of 3103 nM in A431 cells. Moreover, no off-target toxicity was observed with 6l, as determined in A549 and H358 cells, showing EC50 values of 1637 and 2650 nM, respectively.

    We then set out to investigate the effect of substitutions in the 5-position of the hinge-binding indazole moiety. Again, the 2- and 1-naphthyl bicyclic groups were introduced and resulted in compounds 7a and 7b. The introduction of a 2-naphthyl nucleus (7a) provided a strong inhibition of the activating mutant and drug-resistant EGFR variants, with IC50 values of 2 and 1 nM, respectively. Notably, 7a was 8-times less active toward EGFR wild-type (IC50 = 8 nM). The corresponding 1-naphthyl derivative 7b exhibited an inhibitory effect in the double-digit nanomolar range on EGFR-wt (IC50 = 33 nM), while preserving a relevant efficacy in targeting the activating and drug-resistant mutants (IC50 = 12 and 3 nM, respectively). Interestingly, 7a was twice as potent against the H1975 cell line as compared to 7b (EC50 values of 960 and 2077 nM, respectively) and perturbed A431 cells only with an EC50 of 1765 nM. Inhibition of EGFR-L858R/T790M in the subnanomolar range, while retaining selectivity over wild-type (IC50 = 7 nM), was achieved by the introduction of a para-fluorobenzene residue (7c). Cellular characterization revealed remarkable EC50 values of 694 and 459 nM against EGFR mutant H1975 and HCC827 cells, respectively, while a 3–4-fold higher concentration (2070 nM) was required to affect the wild-type-bearing A431 cell line.
    Altogether we synthesized 12 compounds with different substituents in the 6-position of the indazole ring. The compounds bearing a N-methylindol-3-yl (6d) and pyridin-4-yl moiety (6f) showed remarkable selectivity over wild-type, being active against the double mutated EGFR with IC50 values below 5 nM biochemically and between 400 and 500 nM against the corresponding cell line. Particularly high activity was displayed by compound 6l bearing a flexible secondary amine-bridged meta-fluorobenzene moiety. 6l showed intense inhibition in the biochemical settings and cellular activity below 200 nM in drug-resistant H1975 cells. Three additional derivatives in the 5-position were also characterized, and two of them showed inhibition of EGFR-L858R/T790M in the low or subnanomolar range as well as having a good selectivity profile.

    Western Blot Analysis of Cellular Activity on NSCLC Cell Lines

    We further characterized compounds 6d, 6l, 7a, and 7c for their potential to inhibit the phosphorylation of the EGF receptor as well as its downstream enzymes by means of Western blot analysis (Figure 3). Treatment of H1975 cells with 6d and 7c induced a remarkable dose-dependent reduction of EGFR phosphorylation with an EC50 between 100 and 1000 nM, being consistent with the EC50 of 417 and 694 nM, respectively, as determined in the CellTiter-Glo assay. Accordingly, the downstream phospho-Akt level was reduced to the same extent. The inhibitory effect on EGFR wild-type phosphorylation was determined in A431 cells and was affected only at higher concentrations of 10 μM, further underscoring the mutant-selective inhibition profile of these compounds. Although 7a showed excellent inhibition of EGFR mutants in the biochemical context, this compound affected the viability of tumor cells only with EC50 values between 1 and 2 μM, as measured in the CTG assay. Decreased pEGFR and pAkt levels with an EC50 between 1 and 10 μM in the relevant cell lines nicely reflected these results. However, compound 6l displayed an EC50 value of 191 nM against H1975 cells and accordingly affects phosphorylation of EGFR, Akt, and Erk1/2 at a concentration of 100 nM. Selectivity over EGFR wild-type was found to be around one order of magnitude. Altogether, this representative set of indazole-based inhibitors revealed a good correlation of the impact on cellular viability with the impact on phosphorylation of EGFR and its downstream cascade.

    MS-Based and Kinetic Characterization of Covalent Bond Formation

    Treatment of EGFR-T790M with compounds 6d, 6f, 6l, 7a, and 7c and subsequent ESI–MS analyses resulted in a mass increase corresponding to the single-labeled receptor as compared to a DMSO-treated control sample (Figure S2). These studies confirmed the formation of a covalent adduct between these inhibitors and the EGFR kinase domain in the context of the gatekeeper mutation. Covalent binding requires the initially reversible alignment of the inhibitor in the binding site, providing a distinct orientation of the reactive acrylamide toward the cysteine side chain. To further investigate the binding characteristics in detail, the Ki and kinact parameters were determined, reflecting reversible contributions and the rate of covalent bond formation (Table 3). Compounds 6d and 7a revealed remarkably fast covalent bond formation rates in EGFR-L858R/T790M as compared to previously reported compound 5. Compounds 6d and 7a showed values of 0.37 and 0.50 min–1, respectively, while compound 5 exhibited a rate of 0.14 min–1, thereby indicating that the position and flexibility of the warhead are perfectly optimized to target the reactive cysteine. The affinity was determined to be moderate with 148.7 and 28.0 nM. Moreover, 6l, the most potent inhibitor in the cellular evaluation, was also tested and was shown to have a more pronounced reversible binding, as Ki values are in the single digit nanomolar range and the covalent binding rate is 0.17 min–1. This compound was proposed to occupy the backpocket due to more flexibility of the aromatic moiety, so that the high degree of reversible binding proved this hypothesis to be valuable. Altogether, the profile of kinetic binding properties of the tested compounds demonstrated their good selectivity for the double mutant EGFR over wild-type, in accordance with the biochemical IC50 values.

    As binding of these compounds was shown to be highly dependent on covalent bond formation by means of kinetic characterization, we consequentially tested a set of representative compounds against the drug-resistant triple mutant EGFR-L858R/T790M/C797S. As expected, the compounds were shown to have no inhibitory effect against cysteine mutant EGFR (Table S2), further highlighting the importance of covalent bond formation for inhibitory activity.

    Pharmacokinetic Characterization and Kinase Selectivity Profiling

    To evaluate the pharmacokinetic characteristics, we set out to investigate the stability of a representative set of inhibitors in human and mouse microsomes and plasma, respectively (Table S3). To our delight, these compounds showed remarkable high microsomal stability and accordingly low clearance as compared to third generation TKIs. Moreover, all indazole-based inhibitors tested revealed stability of >75% in human and >90% in mouse plasma. Additionally, we assessed the selectivity of inhibitor 6l at 1 μM screening concentration in a kinase profiling comprising 50 tyrosine kinases (Figure S3). EGFR and its mutant variants (EGFR-L861Q, L858R, T790M, and L858R/T790M) were significantly inhibited (>95%), as well as ErbB family members ErbB2 and ErbB4. Only kinases bearing the reactive cysteine in the same position as compared to Cys797 in EGFR displayed marked inhibition of more than 85% (TXK, BLK, BMX, JAK3, and BTK), and the remaining kinases tested show inhibition of below than 50%. These findings further strengthen our previously described findings that our indazole-based EGFR inhibitors depend on the covalent bond formation with a reactive cysteine to achieve marked target inhibition.

    Discussion and Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Covalent inhibitors for therapeutic treatment of kinases have proven to be functional to circumvent competition with high cellular ATP levels and exhibit a prolonged occupancy of the binding pocket.(24) Besides this, the relentless incidence of acquired secondary mutations, crippling the ongoing first-line treatments, highlights the need for new drugs that can specifically target these aberrations.(50) To address this concern, the third generation of EGFR inhibitors, such as osimertinib (4), well exemplifies the potential of covalent drugs in targeted cancer therapy, and this compound is presently on the leading edge of drug refractory NSCLC treatment.(29, 33) The covalent mode of action to overcome the increased ATP affinity along with the interaction with the methionine gatekeeper leads to a gain in potency and selectivity toward the T790M-EGFR mutation. Thus, these new compounds can provide valuable insights for rational design of further inhibitors with different binding modes.
    In the wake of efforts to identify molecular entities targeting cancer relevant variants of EGFR, we recently presented some new inhibitors with a unique aminopyrazolic hinge-binding element. They demonstrated selective inhibition of the L858R/T790M double mutation in a biochemical setup, but did not achieve any considerable activity in corresponding NSCLC cell lines. This outcome was supposed to be attributed to a high degree of flexibility that afforded conformational energy to achieve the optimal binding pose and alignment of the Michael-acceptor toward the reactive cysteine. Consequentially, slow rates of covalent bond formation required the redesign of this compound class.(37) Modeling studies led to novel compounds 6–7, which lack a flexible linker for the positioning of the reactive warhead and are endowed with aromatic ring systems in the 5- and 6-positions of the indazole core to accommodate the back pocket. We synthesized an appropriate library comprising 15 compounds and obtained derivatives that effectively inhibit the drug-resistant mutant variant of EGFR while being selective over wild-type. The representative compounds 6d and 7a (Figure 2B,D) have been further investigated and exhibited fast bond formation with EGFR-L858R/T790M, thus substantiating our approach of targeting Cys797 with conformationally less flexible compounds that bring the reactive counterparts in close mutual proximity. Cellular viability of H1975 cells is affected especially by 6-substituted compounds 6d and 6l with effective concentrations of 417 and 191 nM, respectively, while A431 wild-type cells were only moderately affected. Among the derivatives containing substituents in the 5-position of the indazole scaffold, the inhibitor 7c showed potent inhibition of the viability of H1975 cells with an EC50 of 694 nM and was also active against HCC827 cells with 459 nM. Additionally, these inhibitors were shown to suppress the phosphorylation of EGFR and its downstream cascade in the context of the gatekeeper mutation by means of Western blot analysis. We provide a good pharmacological profile for a representative set of inhibitors as well as a selectivity profile within a set of tyrosine kinases for compound 6l, which highlights the covalent mode of action of these inhibitors because the identified off-target kinases possess a reactive cysteine in the same position as compared to EGFR. In summary, we have developed novel selective indazole-based inhibitors of drug-resistant EGFR-L858R/T790M that displayed increased potency to inhibit the corresponding H1975 cells by optimized positioning of the acrylamide to covalently target the reactive cysteine in the binding site. However, the moderate potency of these inhibitors gives room for optimization by exploring the SAR around secondary amine bridged indazoles as exemplified by compound 6l.

    Activity-Based Assay for IC50 Determination and Kinetic Characterization

    IC50 determinations for EGFR and its mutants (Carna Biosciences, lot13CBS-0005K for EGFR-wt; Carna, lot13CBS-0537B for EGFR-L858R; and Carna, lot12CBS-0765B for EGFR-L858R/T790M) were performed with the HTRF KinEASE-TK assay from Cisbio according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, the amount of EGFR in each reaction well was set to 0.60 ng of EGFR-wt (0.67 nM), 0.10 ng of EGFR-L858R (0.11 nM), or 0.07 ng of EGFR-T790M/L858R (0.08 nM), respectively. An artificial substrate peptide (TK-substrate from Cisbio) was phosphorylated by EGFR. After completion of the reaction (reaction times: 25 min for wt, 15 min for L858R, and 20 min for T790M/L858R), the reaction was stopped by addition of buffer containing EDTA as well as an antiphosphotyrosine antibody labeled with europium cryptate and streptavidin labeled with the fluorophore XL665. FRET between europium cryptate and XL665 was measured after an additional hour of incubation to quantify the phosphorylation of the substrate peptide. ATP concentrations were set at their respective Km-values (9.5 μM for EGFR-wt, 9 μM for EGFR-L858R, and 4 μM for EGFR-L858R/T790M), while a substrate concentration of 1 μM, 225 nM, and 200 nM, respectively, was used. Kinase and inhibitor were preincubated for 30 min before the reaction was started by addition of ATP and substrate peptide. An EnVision multimode plate reader (PerkinElmer) was used to measure the fluorescence of the samples at 620 nm (Eu-labeled antibody) and 665 nm (XL665 labeled streptavidin) 50 μs after excitation at 320 nm. The quotient of both intensities for reactions made with eight different inhibitor concentrations was then analyzed using the Quattro Software Suite for IC50-determination. Each reaction was performed in duplicate, and at least three independent determinations of each IC50 were made.
    For kinetic characterization (kinact/Ki), the inhibitors were incubated with EGFR-wt, -L858R, or -L858R/T790M, respectively, over different periods of time (2–90 min), whereas durations of enzymatic and stop reactions were kept constant as stated above. A 6-fold dilution series (eight data points per IC50 curve) starting at 20 μM final compound concentrations was applied. Compound dilutions were generated using the acoustic dispensing system “ECHO 520 Liquid Handler” from Labcyte (Sunnyvale, CA) and the according dose–response software “Echo Dose-Response v1.5.4”. Calculated IC50 values were plotted versus incubation time, and data were fit as described in the literature to determine kinact and Ki (Krippendorff, B. F.; Neuhaus, R.; Lienau, P.; Reichel, A.; Huisinga, W. J. Biomol. Screen.2009, 14, 913–923).

    Mass Spectrometry Experiments

    We used the drug-resistant mutant variant of EGFR (EGFR-T790M) for MS experiments. We incubated a mixture of 52 μM of protein with 100 μM of inhibitor in buffer (25 mM TRIS, 250 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1 mM TCEP, pH 8) on ice for 1 h. We analyzed the aliquots by mass spectrometry using a Finnigan LCQ Advantage Max mass spectrometer from Thermo. Deconvolution and visualization were performed using MagTran.

    MLM Stability Phase I

    Metabolic stability under oxidative conditions was measured in liver microsomes from different species by LCMS-based measuring of depletion of compound at a concentration of 3 μM over time up to 50 min at 37 °C. On the basis of the compound half-life t1/2, the in vitro intrinsic clearance CLint was calculated.

    Plasma Stability

    Plasma stability was measured by LCMS-based determination of the percentage of remaining compounds at a concentration of 5 μM after incubation in plasma obtained from different species for 1 h at 37 °C.

    Cell Culture

A431 cells were cultured in DMEM high glucose media (Life Technologies, Germany), and HCC827, H1975, A549, as well as H358 cells were cultured in RPMI media (Life Technologies, Germany), both containing l-glutamine and supplemented with 10% FBS (GE Healthcare, Austria) and 1% PenStrep (Life Technologies, Germany) in a humidified incubator at 37 °C and 5% CO2.

    Viability Assay

    Cells were seeded on day 1 at cell numbers that ensure linearity and optimal signal intensity (150–300 cells/well, 25 μL). After culturing for 24 h in serum- and antibiotics-containing media in humidified chambers at 37 °C/5% CO2, the cells were incubated for 96 h with EGFR inhibitors in serial dilutions (14 nM to 30 μM) with DMSO and staurosporine as control. Compound dilutions were generated using the acoustic dispensing system “ECHO 520 Liquid Handler” from Labcyte (Sunnyvale, CA) and the according dose–response software “Echo Dose-Response v1.5.4”. Viability studies were carried out on day 5 using CellTiterGlo Assay (Promega, U.S.) that is a homogeneous method of determining the number of viable cells in culture. It is based on quantification of ATP, indicating the presence of metabolically active cells. For these studies, CellTiterGlo Reagent was prepared according to the instructions of the kit and diluted 1:1 with the complete growth medium suitable for the corresponding cell line. Thereon, reagent and assay plates were equilibrated at room temperature for 20 min. Equal volumes of the reagent were added to the volume of culture medium present in each well (25 μL). The plates were shaken for 2 min on an orbital shaker to induce cell lysis. The microplates were then incubated at room temperature for 20 min for stabilization of the luminescent signal. Following incubation, the luminescence was recorded on an EnVision microplate reader (PerkinElmer) using 500 ms integration time. The data were then analyzed using the Quattro Software Suite for EC50-determination. As quality control, the Z′-factor was calculated from 16 positive and negative control values. Only assay results showing a Z′-factor ≥ 0.5 were used for further analysis. All experimental points were measured in duplicates for each plate and were replicated in at least two plates.

    Western Blotting

    Cells were plated in six-well plates on day 1 at 400.000 (H1975) and 500.000 (A431) cells per well and incubated at 37 °C and 5% CO2. On day 2, cells were starved overnight with media containing 0.5% FBS to be incubated on day 3 for 1 h with EGFR inhibitors following an additional 30 min of EGF stimulation at 50 ng/mL. The medium was discarded, and cells were washed once with DPBS 1x. Using 100 μL per well of lysis buffer, cells were then scraped off and lysed while rotating (Stuart Scientific, Staffordshire, UK) for 1 h at 4 °C. Supernatant was transferred into a new tube, and protein concentrations were determined with the Bio-Rad Protein Assay (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA). Loading buffer containing 0.5 M (v/v) Tris-HCl pH 6.7, 10% (v/v) SDS, (w/v) DTT, (v/v) glycerol, and 0.05% (w/v) bromophenol blue was added, and lysates were incubated for 5 min at 95 °C.
