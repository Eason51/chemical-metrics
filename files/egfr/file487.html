    Abstract

    We designed and synthesized quinazolin-2,4-dione-based hydroxamic acids to serve as selective competitive inhibitors of histone deacetylase-6 (HDAC6). The most potent and selective compound, 3d (IC50, 4 nM, HDAC6; IC50 > 10 μM, HDAC1), substantially increased acetylation of α-tubulin instead of histones in the lung cancer cell line, LL2. Paclitaxel in combination with 3d had a synergistic anticancer effect on reduction of programmed death-ligand 1 expression in LL/2 cells. When given orally, 3d was mainly found to locate in the liver and lungs, at a concentration 18- to 70-fold greater, respectively, than in plasma. As an orally active HDAC6 inhibitor, 3d (20 mg/kg) potentiated paclitaxel antitumor activity (percentage tumor growth inhibition, 67.5%) in a xenograft syngeneic non-small cell lung cancer mouse model.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Histone deacetylase-6 (HDAC6) is the most studied member of the HDAC family, but, unlike what its name implies, HDAC6 deacetylates α-tubulin, HSP90, cortactin, and peroxiredoxin, and not histones.(1,2) A HDAC6 knockout mouse develops normally with no obvious phenotype aberrations, whereas other types of HDAC knockout mice display severe defects.(3) Consequently, inhibitors of HDAC6 have been proposed to be more appropriate targets for drug development because the pan-HDAC inhibitors that have been developed to date have proven to possess undesirable side effects.(4,5) Compounds that inhibit HDAC6 have been shown to decrease the negative effects associated with disease models including autoimmune disorders, Alzheimer’s disease, and cancer.(6−8)
    Selective positive effects on HDAC6 by siRNA or HDAC6 inhibitors include inhibition of tumorigenesis, inhibition of metastasis, and increased sensitivity of tumors to other anticancer agents.(9−11) Several inhibitors selective for HDAC6 have been developed to treat cancers including an inhibitor that bears a substituted urea linker with antimelanoma activity.(8) Another inhibitor, ACY-1215, is being tested clinically in combination with bortezomib, a proteasome inhibitor, for multiple myeloma.(12) In addition, data have accumulated suggesting that prostate cancer may be treated with the HDAC6 inhibitor, tubacin, alone or in combination with a cytotoxic agent.(11) Although LBH589 is a pan-HDAC inhibitor, it is therapeutically responsive to LNCap and PC-3 prostate cancer cells by inhibiting HDAC6.(13) HDAC6, as a microtubule deacetylase, is crucial to the regulation of epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) trafficking and EGFR degradation in lung cancer cells.(14) Combined treatment of ACY-1215 and sorafenib, a tyrosine kinase inhibitor, overcomes HDAC6-mediated resistance in non-small cell lung cancer cells.(15) Remarkably, Nexturastat A, a potent inhibitor of HDAC6 having an immunotherapeutic activity, downregulated protein programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression by deactivating the STAT3 pathway.(16) These studies highlight how HDAC6 inhibitors can regulate multiple pathways involved in carcinogenesis, tumor growth, and metastases.
    We have reported a series of quinazolin-4-one-based inhibitors selective for HDAC6.(7) For the study reported herein, we designed and synthesized a new set of quinazoline-2,4-dione-based HDAC6 inhibitors with scaffold hopping of substituents on our previously developed quinazoline-4-one derivatives (Figure 1). The hydroxamic acid and benzyl groups in the previously constructed derivatives maintained their original positions as a zinc-binding group and a linker, respectively. The benzyl-containing linker was moved to the N-1 position of the quinazoline-2,4-dione core, which is a more central location. In addition, the phenyl rings of the quinazoline-2,4-dione core were substituted, one at a time, with various functional groups to explore the HDAC inhibitory profiles of the resultant compounds. Selected compounds, which showed a good inhibitory activity, were then tested for cytotoxicity against lung cancer cells.

    Chemistry

    The syntheses of 3a–c began with the substituted anthranilic acids 1a–c (Scheme 1). Compounds 1a–c were first coupled with phenyl chloroformate; then, amide was coupled with phenethylamine and cyclized under basic conditions to afford the intermediates 2a–c, which were alkylated with ethyl 4-(bromomethyl)benzoate using NaH in an ice bath to obtain the corresponding ester intermediates. These esters were transformed into hydroxamic acids via hydrolysis, amide coupling, and reductive deprotection (3c) or via deprotection by BBr3 (3a and 3b). The synthesis of 3d and 3f began with a ring opening of isatoic anhydride with a phenethylamine at the C-4 position. Then, the product was coupled with phenyl chloroformate and cyclized under basic conditions to obtain 2d, which was then alkylated with ethyl 4-(bromomethyl)benzoate or ethyl 3-(bromomethyl)benzoate to afford the corresponding esters. These esters were transformed into the hydroxamic acid derivatives 3d and 3f. 3-Methoxy aniline was coupled with phenyl chloroformate and then transformed into the disubstituted urea, which was ortho-carbonylated through palladium-catalyzed C–H activation(17) and cyclized under basic conditions to afford 2e. Compound 3e was synthesized from 2e in a similar way as mentioned above.
    In addition, we individually modified positions 6 and 7 of the quinazolin-di-one or replaced the phenyl group with a pyridine of the quinazolin-di-one core (Schemes 1 and 2, respectively). Compounds 1d, 1e, and 1f were each coupled with phenethylamine and then cyclized with ethyl chloroformate under basic conditions to give 2f, 2g, and 2h, respectively. These intermediates were then subjected to conditions similar to those shown in Scheme 1 to afford 3g, 3h, and 3i, respectively.
    Intermediate 2b was alkylated with ethyl 4-(bromomethyl)benzoate to give the ester 4. The chloro group of 4 was converted into a hydroxyl, methyl, or cyclopropyl by palladium catalysis under Buchwald–Hartwig condition, Negishi, or Suzuki reaction to afford 5a, 5b, or 5c, respectively,(18,19) and these derivatives were then converted into the hydroxamic acids 3j, 3k, or 3l, respectively (Scheme 3).
    To explore the structure–activity relationship of substituents on the phenyl ring of the phenethyl group, the fluoro or methoxy-substituted compounds 3m–3r were synthesized under conditions similar to those of 3d and 3a (Scheme 4). The phenyl group of the linker or cap was also replaced with thiophene to generate 3s, 3t, or 3u, respectively (Scheme 5). The benzyl group was removed by BBr3 as the final step because a Pd-catalyzed reduction did not give the desired product and might have been caused by thiophene poisoning.

    HDAC Inhibition Profile

    We first screened the compounds that contained substitutions on the phenyl of the quinazolin-di-one core (Table 1). Compound 3d, with a nonfunctionalized quinazolin-di-one core, was the best inhibitor of HDAC6 (4 nM) and had the most selectivity over HDAC1 (2790-fold). Introduction of a chloro (3a) or fluoro (3g) at the C-6 position was detrimental to HDAC6 inhibition. Compared with 3g, shifting the fluoro to C-7, which afforded 3c, significantly increased the inhibition of HDAC6 and HDAC8. However, the enlargement of the C-7 group in 3e, 3h, 3k, and 3l did not improve HDAC6 inhibitory activity. Regarding the electronic effect, the electron-withdrawing group in 3h substantially reduced HDAC6 inhibition to 636 nM and selectivity over HDAC1 (13-fold) compared with the electron-donating substituents in 3e and 3j. Compounds 3k and 3l, containing a methyl and a cyclopropyl, respectively, had generally comparable HDAC-inhibition profiles. The data shown in Table 1 indicated that the core size of the quinazolinone cap determined if it could properly be accommodated at the rim of the HDAC6 active site, with an increase in bulkiness being harmful for HDAC6 inhibition. Consequently, 3d was used as the lead compound for further modification.

    In addition to SAR on the phenyl group, the orientation of the zinc binding group and the introduction of heterocycles on 3d was further investigated (Table 3). First, we moved the hydroxamic acid group from the para position to the meta position of the phenyl to give 3f. Intriguingly, the meta-oriented hydroxamic acid abolished the inhibition of HDAC6 inhibition and gave an inhibition profile slightly selective for HDAC8, which suggested that the catalytic site of HDAC6 is not as flexible as that of HDAC8 and does not allow for a conformational change.(20) Second, replacing the phenyl ring of the core structure with a pyridine to produced 3i reduced the activity toward HDAC6 around sevenfold but did not affect the activity against HDAC1. However, 3u with a thiophene ring strengthens its interaction with HDAC8 compared to 3i. Finally, by replacing the phenyl linker with 2,4-disubstituted thiophene to give 3t which possesses a similar inhibitory profile as 3f, that might be because of their comparable orientation of hydroxamic acid group. Remarkably, 3s with 2,5-disubstituted thiophene linker reduced the selectivity for HDAC6 over HDAC1 and HDAC8 (1147- and ∼fivefold, respectively) provided a dual HDAC6/8 inhibitor.

    HDAC6 Kinetic Activity Study

    To delineate the interaction between some of our synthesized compounds and HDAC6, the enzymatic activity of HDAC6 was measured in the presence of a substrate at different concentrations and with different concentrations of the inhibitors. In addition to full-length HDAC6, the activity of the catalytic domain 2 (CD2) from HDAC6 was studied (CD1 does not have catalytic active).(21) The Ki values of the potential inhibitors were similar to that of tubastatin A, with 3d being the most potent (Table 4). The inhibition data were consistent with a competitive inhibition model for full-length HDAC6 and for CD2. The v values for CD2 were ∼50% those of full-length HDAC6 for all inhibitors. The Km values for the substrate were of the same order of magnitude as were the Ki values for the quinazolinone derivatives.

    More Potent Inhibitors of Recombinant HDAC6 also Decreased Lung Cancer Cell Viability in Vitro

    A 3-(4,5-cimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyl tetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay was used to characterize the cytotoxicities and IC50 values of some of the HDAC6-selective inhibitors (Table 5). Human lung cancer A549 cells were treated with serial dilutions of the HDAC6 inhibitors at 37 °C for 72 h. Compound 3i had the greatest activity against the cells with an IC50 value of 2.8 μM. Although, the cytotoxicities of the HDAC6 inhibitors were not as potent as that of the pan-HDAC inhibitor, suberanilohydroxamic acid (SAHA), it showed better safety without severe adverse effects than pan-HDAC inhibitors.(12,22)

    Combined Cytotoxic Effect of Paclitaxel and 3d on Cultured Mouse Lung Cancer LL/2 Cells

    As described above, our data indicated that the HDAC6-selective inhibitors potentially had a useful anti-lung cancer activity. To analyze the combined effect of paclitaxel (PAX) and 3d, we employed the MTT assay for cell viability. Because the IC50 values for PAX and 3d were 10 nM and 7.87 μM, respectively, we performed the combination experiments with 10 nM PAX and 4 μM of 3d. Compound 3d (4 μM) or PAX (10 nM) when used separately killed between 10 and 20% of the LL/2 cells by day 3, whereas when used together, they killed 80–90% of the cells by day 3. To assess the combined efficacies of 3d or 3r with PAX, LL/2 cells were treated with 3d or 3r separately and in combination with PAX for 3 days. The combined treatment of PAX and 3d or 3r resulted in a significantly greater inhibition of LL/2 cell viability when compared with the treatment of either inhibitor alone (Figure 2B,C). Further, increasing concentrations of 3d (0–10 μM) and PAX were cultured in LL/2 cells for 72 h, and the viability was analyzed by MTT to determinate the combination effect. When 3d was combined with PAX, the IC50 of 3d and PAX in LL/2 cells was found to decrease significantly. Synergism was evaluated by Chou–Talalay method and showed the combination index (CI) < 1.0 (Figure 2D). Taken together, the data show that the selective HDAC6 inhibitor, 3d and 3r in combination with PAX, had a synergistic effect on the viability of LL/2 cells.

    Selective HDAC6 Inhibitor, 3d, Increased α-Tubulin Acetylation

    To further confirm the specific inhibitory effect of 3d on HDAC6 activity, acetylation of α-tubulin in the human lung cancer cell, A549, was accessed. A dose-dependent increase in α-tubulin acetylation was observed when the cells were treated with 3d (Figure 3A). Besides 3d, another HDAC6 selective inhibitor, ACY1215, was also analyzed. In addition, 3d also increased α-tubulin acetylation in a time-dependent manner (Figure 3B). A substantial increase in α-tubulin acetylation was seen when the cells were treated with compound 3d for only 1 h. Conversely, 3d had no effect on the acetylation of histone H3 and histone H4, which suggested that it is a selective HDAC6 inhibitor.

    Combination Treatment of 3d and PAX Enhanced α-Tubulin Acetylation

    Because previous studies indicated that 3d and PAX induced α-tubulin acetylation, we investigated the effects of 3d and PAX alone and when combined on the microtubule system in LL/2 cells. Little tubulin acetylation was observed when the cells were treated separately with 10 nM PAX or 0.8 μM 3d as α-tubulin acetylation was increased only 7.28- and 42.66-fold, respectively, in comparison with the control. However, α-tubulin acetylation was increased to 74.72-fold when the cell was treated with both PAX and 0.8 μM 3d (Figure 4). There is a similar phenomenon that was observed when the cell was treated with the both PAX and 4 μM 3d. Our data, therefore, suggested that treatment with 3d and PAX had a synergistic effect on the acetylation of α-tubulin in LL/2 cells.

    Combined Treatment of 3d and PAX Increased the Number of Abnormal Nuclei in LL/2 Cells

    A relatively small concentration of PAX induces mitotic block at the metaphase/anaphase stage and results in abnormal cell division and formation of multiple nuclei.(23) To test whether the combined treatment of 3d and PAX had a synergistic effect on aberrant cell division and cell viability, LL/2 cells were treated for 24 h with 10 nM PAX or with 0.8 μM 3d alone or in combination, and the morphology of their nuclei was visualized by 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole staining. An increase in the number of multinucleated cells was observed when the cells were treated with 3d and PAX (Figure 5).

    Combined Treatment of 3d and PAX Induced a Synergetic Apoptotic Response in A549 and LL/2 Cells

    Human and mouse lung cancer cells (A549 and LL/2, respectively) were incubated with indicated concentrations of 3d and/or PAX for 24 h (Figure 6). The relative proliferation of each cell sample was analyzed daily using the MTT assay. Apoptotic and dead cells were identified by flow cytometry with propidium iodide and annexin V staining. Treatment with 4 μM of 3d did not cause apoptosis, and treatment with 10 nM PAX induced apoptosis in 4 and 2.9% of the LL/2 and A549 cells, respectively. However, the combined treatment with 4 μM of 3d and 10 nM of PAX induced apoptosis in 8.8 and 5.4% of the LL/2 and A549 cells, respectively. In addition to inducing apoptosis, the combined treatment of 3d and PAX also resulted in more dead cells.

    Combined Treatment of 3d and PAX Decreased PD-L1 Expression

    LL/2 cells were incubated with 3d, tubastatin A, which is also selective for HDAC6, or the pan-HDAC inhibitor, SAHA for 24 h (Figure 7, top panel). Expression of PD-L1 was then assessed by Western blotting. Tubastatin A and 3d inhibited PD-L1 expression, whereas SAHA did not. To determinate the effect of PAX and 3d on PD-L1 expression in LL/2 cells, the cells were incubated with 3d and/or PAX for 24 h, and PD-L1 levels were determined by Western blotting (Figure 7, lower panel). Interestingly, when the cells were treated with PAX alone, PD-L1 expression increased, suggesting that PAX may be the cause of immune-resistance seen in patients who receive chemotherapy.(24) Conversely, PD-L1 expression decreased when the cells were treated with PAX and 3d, suggesting that 3d may decrease PAX-induced PD-L1 expression and thereby avoid inducing immune-resistance and improving the antitumor response.

    Effects of Combined Treatment of 3d and PAX on LL/2 Tumors in an in Vivo Syngeneic Xenograft Mouse Model

    Given the promising results of the in vitro experiments reported herein, the combined use of 3d and PAX was assessed in an LL/2-tumor syngeneic subcutaneous xenograft C57BL/6 mouse model. Tumor growth in the mice was not inhibited by 20 mg/kg of 3d or 10 mg/kg of PAX when the compounds were given singly. However, in combination and at the same doses, PAX and 3d decreased tumor growth by 67.5%. In addition, a single dose of 80 or 40 mg/kg of 3d showed LL/2 cell tumor growth inhibition (TGI) of 63.4% in the mice, suggesting that 3d has an antitumor activity (Figure 8).

    In Vivo Tissue Distribution of ACY-1215 and 3d in Mice

    The tissue distribution of 3d and ACY-1215 administrated orally to mice was assessed. The concentration of 3d in the liver was greater than in any other tissue, including the lung and kidney, however, the concentration of 3d in the lung was 18.8-fold greater than in the plasma. Comparing the AUC0–24 of ACY-1215 with that of 3d (Figures 9 and 10) in mice, the AUC0–24 of 3d was greater than that of ACY-1215 in all organs except for the kidney. The AUC0–24 ratio of 3d to ACY-1215 in the lung was 2.1-fold and 4.4-fold in the liver (Table 6). Even though 3d had poor exposure levels in the plasma and decreased dramatically within 24 h after an oral administration, the results informed that 3d may tend to distribute into the peripheral tissues instead of staying in the plasma.

    In Vivo Metabolism of 3d

    Because the unsubstituted phenyl ring of 3d may undergo hydroxylation or the hydroxamic acid may be hydrolyzed into carboxylic acid in vivo, the biological samples after 3d orally 40 mg/kg in mice were used to assess the possible metabolites of 3d. The hydrolysis metabolite of 3d had been measured in various tissues; however, the hydroxylated metabolite could not be found. The concentration of hydrolysis metabolite in every tissue was significantly higher than the parent form, especially the liver, which plays an important role for xenobiotic metabolism (Figures 10 and 11). The results suggested that a large amount of 3d may experience a first-pass metabolism and be biotransformed into hydrolysis form by the hepatic isozymes.

    In Vivo Tissue Distribution of Combined Treatment of 3d and PAX in Mice

    The tissue distribution was assessed at 1 h after dosing 40 mg/kg of 3d orally combined with or without PAX intraperitoneal injection 10 mg/kg administrated to mice. Compared with single oral dosing, combined treatment increased the concentration of 3d in the tissues from 1.6- to 12-fold, except for the liver, at 1 h after dosing (Figure 12 and Table 7). This phenomena was also observed in one phase I study of vorinostat. The AUC and half-life of vorinostat in the plasma were increased when coadministered with PAX, but PAX pharmacokinetics was not affected by vorinostat.(25)

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We designed and synthesized quinazolin-2,4-dione derivatives to determine if they could act as potent and competitive HDAC6-selective inhibitors. Compound 3d (IC50, 4 nM; Ki, 1.58 nM) showed the greatest selectivity for HDAC6 compared with that for HDAC1 (2790-fold) among the derivatives. In vitro studies of using 3d revealed that it had a modest cytotoxicity against non-small cell lung cancer cells; however, 3d in combination with PAX had a synergistically increased anticancer activity. Time-course and dose-dependent Western blot studies demonstrated that 3d increased Ac-tubulin within 1 h of treatment in LL/2 cells and with only 1 μM dose. Remarkably, PD-L1 expression in LL/2 cells was also reduced after incubation with 3d. Tumor growth in a syngeneic mice model was substantially suppressed by a single oral treatment of 3d, and in combination with PAX, its anticancer activity was synergistically increased. As an orally active HDAC6 inhibitor, 3d had better penetration in the mouse lung than it did ACY-1215. In summary, this study suggested that the quinazolin-2,4-dione derivative, 3d, may be an orally active and safe therapeutic agent for the treatment of non-small cell lung cancer.

    HDAC Inhibition Assay

    HDAC inhibition assays were performed by the Reaction Biology Corp. (Malvern, PA) using full-length recombinant hHDAC 1, 6, 8, and 11 that had been expressed in Sf9 cells via baculovirus vectors. Substrate I, a fluorogenic peptide based on the p53 residues 379–382 (RHKKAc), was used for the HDAC 1, 6, and 11 assays, and substrate II, a fluorogenic diacyl peptide based on the p53 residues 379–382 (RHKAcKAc), was used for HDAC8. The reaction buffer was 50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 137 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl, 1 mM MgCl2, 1 mg/mL BSA, and 1% (v/v) DMSO (final concentration). The compounds and a substrate were delivered sequentially into the enzyme mixture, and each reaction was incubated for 2 h at 30 °C. IC50 values were generated from the resulting 10-dose plots based on threefold serial dilutions starting at 30 μM.

    Plasmid Construction

    The nucleotide sequence of CD2 from hHDAC6 (amino acid residues 450–850) was PCR-amplified from the pcDNA-HDAC6 FLAG clone (product number 30482; Addgene), and the product was subcloned into a pET-28b vector (Novagen). An insert that included EcoRI and HindIII restriction sites was created and PCR-amplified by the polymerase chain reaction using primers 1 and 2, respectively, 5′-TGA ATT CAT GGA GGA GGA CAA TGT AGA GG-3′ and 5′-CGA CAA GCT TTT ACA ACT TAG AAC TGG AGG GTC C-3′. This vector and the PCR fragment were each digested with EcoRI and HindIII, purified by agarose gel electrophoresis, and joined by reaction with T4 ligase DNA ligase. The plasmid sequence was confirmed by Mission Biotech.

    Purification of CD2 of hHDAC6

    CD2 was expressed in Escherichia coli BL21 (RIL). Expression was induced with 1 mM isopropyl-1-thio-β-d-galactose, after which the cells were incubated at 16 °C overnight. The cells were harvested by centrifugation at 6000g for 15 min at 4 °C, and the pellet was suspended in 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 250 mM NaCl, 5% (v/v) glycerol, and 2 μM β-mercaptoethanol (β-ME). The cells were sonicated, and the lysate was centrifuged at 12 500g and 4 °C for 30 min. The supernatant was passed through a Ni-NTA affinity column, which was washed with 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.0, 250 mM NaCl, 2 μM β-ME, and 20 mM imidazole, and then CD2 was eluted with a step gradient of increasing imidazole concentration (20–500 mM imidazole in 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.0, 30 mM NaCl, and 2 μM β-ME). The protein was stored in 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.0, 100 mM NaCl, 5% (v/v) glycerol, and 2 μM β-ME (buffer was exchanged through a desalting column, GE Healthcare).

    HDAC6 Enzyme Kinetic Studies

    For the HDAC6 inhibition assays, each inhibitor, after dilution, at various concentrations, into 120 μL of 15 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 250 μM EDTA, 250 mM NaCl, and 10% (v/v) glycerol, was mixed with 4 nM hHDAC6 (Merck Millipore) or 5 nM CD2 at rt for 10 min. Enzyme activity was then measured using the Fluor De Lys HDAC substrate (Boc-Lys (Ac)-AMC, Biovision; final concentrations: 5–35 μM) and incubated at 37 °C for 30 min. Reactions were stopped by adding 80 μL of 0.1 mg/mL trypsin, 50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 100 mM NaCl, 4 mM of the HDAC-inhibitor TSA, and 30% (v/v) isopropanol. After a 30 min incubation at 37 °C, the release of AMC was monitored by measuring the fluorescence at λex = 350 nm and λem = 440 nm using a Tecan microplate reader. Fluorescence intensity was calibrated using known concentrations of AMC. AMC signals were recorded against a blank containing buffer and substrate. The inhibition data were best fit to a competitive inhibition pattern, and all experiments were repeated three times.

    Assessment of Tissue Distribution of ACY-1215, 3d, and Hydrolysis Metabolite in Mice

    All of the animals were performed in accordance with the guidelines of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of the Institute (IACUC). Male C57BL/6 mice weighing 0.02–0.03 kg were purchased from LASCO (BioLasco Taiwan Co. Ltd., Taipei, Taiwan) and housed under a standard 12 h light/dark cycle. The mice were allowed access only to water for 12 h before experimentation. For ACY-1215 and 3d tissue distribution study, the mice received an orally administered 40 mg/kg dose of ACY-1215 or one of 3d (18 mice per group). At 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 6, and 24 h after dosing, three mice from each group were sacrificed. For 3d combined with PAX treatment study, mice received an orally administered 40 mg/kg dose of 3d combined with 10 mg/kg intraperitoneal injection of PAX (3 mice per group). At 1 h after dosing, the mice were sacrificed. In both studies, the mice were perfused with ice-cold normal saline for myocardial perfusion. Plasma, lung, liver, and kidney samples were collected and stored at −80 °C until analysis. Plasma was separated from blood by centrifugation (3000g for 15 min at 4 °C). To determine the ACY-1215, 3d, and the hydrolysis metabolite of 3d concentrations in the plasma, a 50 μL plasma sample was added into a fourfold volume of acetonitrile containing 200 ppb vorinostat supplied by TCI as an internal standard and vortexed for 1 min. After centrifugation at 12 000g for 6 min, the upper layer of the mixture was filtrated through a Minisart RC4 syringe filter (0.22 μm, Sartorius Stedim Lab Ltd, UK). A sample of each organ, lungs, livers, and kidneys, was weighed and added into deionized water (2 mL/g) and homogenized using an Active Motif Q120AM ultrasonicator at 30% power for two 20 s periods with a resting period of 10 s between two sonication periods. Vorinostat (2500 ppb, 10 μL) was added into each homogenate to serve as an internal standard; then, a threefold volume of EtOAc was added, and each mixture was vortexed for 1 min. After centrifugation at 3800g for 10 min, the organic layer of each sample was placed into a clean tube and evaporated under a stream of air at 40 °C. Each residue was suspended in 200 μL of acetonitrile. After centrifugation at 12 000g for 6 min, the upper layer of each solution was filtrated through a Minisart RC4 syringe filter. Each sample was subjected to LC/MS/MS to quantitate the concentrations of the drugs extracted from the plasma and tissues.

    LC/MS/MS

    Compounds were separated through a Kinetex C18 column (50 mm × 3 mm i.d.; 2.6 μm particle size; Phenomenex, Torrance, CA, USA) protected by a Phenomenex Gemini C18 guard cartridge. The elution solvents were 0.5% (w/v) formic acid in acetonitrile (solvent A) and 0.5% (w/v) formic acid in deionized water (solvent B). The column was equilibrated in 90% B for 2 min. Between 2 and 3 min, the percentage of B was decreased to 0% and then held at 0% B for an additional 2 min. Between 5 and 5.5 min, B was increased to 90% and then maintained at 90% B for 1 min for a total time of 6.5 min. The flow rate was 0.8 mL/min; the column temperature was 40 °C; and the sample size was 10 μL. Multiple reaction monitoring mode via a positive ESI was applied to detect ACY-1215, 3d, hydrolysis metabolite of 3d, and vorinostat at the transitions m/z = 434.10 → 274.10, 416.05 → 150.05, 398.90 → 265.05, and 265.05 → 232.15, respectively.

    Cell Lines and Culture Medium

    Mouse Lewis lung carcinoma cells (LL/2, ATCC CCL-81T) and human lung carcinoma cells (A549, ATCC CCL-185) were maintained in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) and RPMI-1640 medium, respectively, supplemented with 10% (v/v) fetal bovine serum (Gibco), 100 units/mL penicillin G, and 100 μg/mL streptomycin (Gibco). Cell cultures were maintained at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2 and were subcultured one to three times a week.

    MTT Assay

    Cell-doubling rates and viability were evaluated by the MTT assay. Briefly, cells were grown in wells of 96-well plates (initially, 5000 cells per well). Cells were allowed to attach for 24 h and then exposed to fivefold diluted compound or only to the vehicle (DMEM with 0.1% DMSO). After 72 h, 20 μL (5 mg/mL) of MTT was added into each cell culture. After a 5 h incubation at 37 °C, the supernatant of each culture was carefully aspirated, and the resulting formazan crystals were dissolved in 100 μL DMSO at 37 °C for 10 min with a gentle agitation. Absorbance of each solution was measured at 570 nm using a Molecular Devices I3 microplate reader. Results are presented as the mean of three independent experiments. IC50 values were calculated from the relative viability values and concentrations of the compounds by regression analysis.

    In Vitro Combination Assay

    The inhibitory effect of drug combinations on cell proliferation was assessed by crystal violet staining. Briefly, LL/2 cells were seeded into the wells of 24-well plates (5000 cells per well). Cells were allowed to attach for 24 h and were then exposed to HDAC6 inhibitor and/or PAX for up to 4 days. At the end of the 5 day period, the cells were fixed with 3.7% (w/v) formaldehyde at rt for 30 min and then stained with 0.02% (w/v) crystal violet. The cells were then washed with water, after which the plates were dried at rt. The CI was calculated by CompuSyn software (ComboSyn Inc, Paramus, NJ, USA) according to the Chou-Talalay method.

    Western Blots

    Cells were lysed in 50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 1% (v/v) Triton X-100, 0.1% (w/v) SDS, 1% (w/v) sodium deoxycholate, 1 mM phenylmethanesulfonylfluoride (Sigma-Aldrich), and a protease inhibitor cocktail (Sigma-Aldrich), and the protein concentration in each lysate was measured by the BCA protein assay (Bio-Rad). Proteins were resolved by SDS-PAGE [12.5% (w/v) acrylamide]; transferred onto a polyvinylidene difluoride membrane (Merck Millipore); blocked with 5% (v/v) skim milk in 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, and 0.5% (v/v) Tween-20; and reacted with anti-β-actin (1:2000 dilution; Cell Signaling, with β-actin serving as the internal control), anti-acetyl-α-tubulin (Lys40; 1:1000 dilution; Cell Signaling), and anti-acetyl-histone H3 (Lys9; 1:1000 dilution; Cell Signaling). The secondary antibody used was Europium-labeled goat antirabbit IgG (1:5000 dilution; Molecular Devices).

    LL/2 Tumor Syngeneic Xenograft Mouse Model

    Seven-week-old C57BL/6 mice were subcutaneously injected with 100 μL of an LL/2 cell suspension (equivalent to 5 × 106 cells). PAX (10 mg/kg) was intraperitoneally injected once daily for 5 days (10 mg/kg IP QD × 5) (n = 8). Mice were orally dosed with 20, 40, or 80 mg/kg 3d once daily for 21 consecutive days (20 or 40 or 80 mg/kg oral QD × 3 weeks) (n = 8). TVs were measured twice weekly throughout the duration of the experiment.
