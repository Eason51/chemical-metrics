    Abstract

    We present CSD-CrossMiner, a novel tool for pharmacophore-based searches in crystal structure databases. Intuitive pharmacophore queries describing, among others, protein–ligand interaction patterns, ligand scaffolds, or protein environments can be built and modified interactively. Matching crystal structures are overlaid onto the query and visualized as soon as they are available, enabling the researcher to quickly modify a hypothesis on the fly. We exemplify the utility of the approach by showing applications relevant to real-world drug discovery projects, including the identification of novel fragments for a specific protein environment or scaffold hopping. The ability to concurrently search protein–ligand binding sites extracted from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) and small organic molecules from the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD) using the same pharmacophore query further emphasizes the flexibility of CSD-CrossMiner. We believe that CSD-CrossMiner closes an important gap in mining structural data and will allow users to extract more value from the growing number of available crystal structures.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The two major crystallographic databases of the world, the Cambridge Structural Database(1) (CSD) and the Protein Data Bank(2, 3) (PDB), have long reached a size and coverage that make them invaluable resources for structure-based design. Exploitation of this structural data has led to the generation of practically useful empirical conformation rules(4, 5) and interaction preferences.(6-9) More and more refined analyses are being carried out today, complementing first-principles approaches(10) and supporting various aspects of small molecule drug discovery.(11)
    It is surprising, then, that the arsenal of methods to query the CSD and PDB is still limited in functionality: first, query engines are specific for either database, ConQuest(12) being the gold standard for CSD queries. A unified search engine does not yet exist, yet both databases often yield complementary results, whether comparing atom contact statistics, searching for interaction motifs, or generating ideas for a specific structure-based design project. Second, a broad array of tools have been developed to query the PDB,(13, 14) but these tools remain highly segregated and are based on alternate annotation(15) and simplification models.(16) Substructure-based interaction queries(17) cannot be combined with more fuzzy queries comparing protein cavities,(18) with scaffold hopping(19) or with queries based on protein motifs.(20, 21) Lastly, the lack of intuitive query editors and the lack of interactive query response are impediments to iterative refinement of complex 3D searches.
    In view of these gaps, we have set out to design and implement a versatile method to expand the possibilities of 3D searching. Our interest in such an expansion is 2-fold: we wish to provide a tool that enables an exploration of protein–ligand complexes beyond the classic concept of pairwise interactions and that can serve as an idea generator for structure-based projects. To this end, we chose to use the well-established technique of 3D pharmacophore searching as our basis, due to its conceptual clarity, because it is amenable to 3D query design and because it is scalable in terms of specificity and may be combined with substructure queries. An efficient database and query scheme in conjunction with careful interface design enable fast and highly interactive queries.

    Methods

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    Structural Databases

    The method has been designed to operate on any database of molecular structures as long as 3D coordinates for the atoms are available. Here we specifically analyze small molecule structures stored in the CSD and protein–ligand binding sites extracted from the PDB. For the CSD (version 5.36 including the November 2014 update) a subset has been created containing structures that (a) are not organometallic, (b) have an R-factor of at maximum 10%, (c) for which 3D coordinates have been determined, (d) have no disorder, and (e) are not polymeric. This resulted in a database containing around 234 400 structures. Protein binding sites without hydrogen atoms were extracted in MOL2 format from Proasis,(22) a curated version of the PDB, and stored in an SQLite(23) database. Database entries were generated for all HET molecules, except metals and commonly found small ions, as of June 16, 2015. Those HET groups with less than 5 or more than 100 atoms were removed. All molecules within a cutoff radius of 6 Å around the selected HET group were included in the respective database entry. This database consists of 210 200 binding sites derived from around 58 300 PDB entries.

    Feature Annotation

    Each entry in a structural database is annotated with a set of feature points which are used to perform the actual 3D search. The method distinguishes between base and virtual feature points, and a specific feature type may consist of a single or multiple of these feature points. Similar to other published methods,(24-26) substructure-based features are defined by a hierarchy of SMARTS(27) patterns which can be tailored by the user. For molecular fragments from the PDB, for which we do not use explicit hydrogen atoms, we define hydrogen bond donors and acceptors based on expert knowledge about predominant protonation and tautomer states. The SMARTS list was defined to cover nitrogen heterocycles(28) and functional groups occurring in a set of protein–ligand binding sites.(29) It was then extended wherever an assignment was considered incorrect in our test applications. Abstract feature representations are derived from the substructures by applying a point generation rule. Point generation rules define how the feature points will be determined from a set of atomic coordinates. For example, virtual hydrogen, lone pair, or centroid positions can be assigned to represent donor, acceptor, and ring features, respectively. While in this work exclusively substructure-based features are used, the approach can deal with other point-based features such as molecular surfaces represented by surface points and corresponding projected points along their surface normal. All features assigned to a database entry are preindexed and stored in a feature database which is stored in memory at run-time. Table 1 illustrates the features used in the application examples (additional features and the full list of SMARTS patterns can be found in the Supporting Information).

    Fingerprint Generation

    In order to quickly decide whether a database entry has to be considered for the 3D search step, a feature presence fingerprint is precalculated. For each database entry, this fingerprint stores the presence or absence of feature types in a small molecule, a protein component, or any of the two.

    Pharmacophore Query

    The locations of features to be searched for are specified by tolerance spheres (see Figure 1a). Since feature types may be represented by multiple feature points, e.g., for a directional feature such as a hydrogen bond donor, the same number of tolerance spheres is used to specify the query. The radius of each tolerance sphere reflects the uncertainty in the exact location of the feature and can thus be used to control the specificity of a query. Different types of constraints can be applied to the query (see Figure 1b). For pairs of query features, an intra- or intermolecular constraint can be defined, which requires the respective feature points in a matching structure to be located in the same or in different molecular structures. If no such constraint is defined, hits of both kinds can be found. For databases containing protein and small molecule annotations, each query feature can additionally be constrained to be part of a protein, a small molecule component, or any of the two. Combinations of the intra-/intermolecular constraints with the protein/small molecule annotations allow for highly tailored interrogation of nonbonded interactions (see Figure 1c). Excluded volumes are not explicitly supported but can be emulated. For example, an excluded volume for a ligand in a protein–ligand binding site can be defined by a protein “heavy_atom” feature at the desired excluded position.

    Searching

    The database search starts with the conversion of the pharmacophore query defined in Cartesian space by tolerance spheres into a distance space representation, thereby eliminating translational and rotational degrees of freedom. For each pair of tolerance spheres (neglecting pairs that form part of the same feature), a distance constraint is derived by measuring the distance between the two sphere centers and adding, as well as subtracting, the sum of the sphere radii to arrive at upper and lower bounds, respectively (if the spheres overlap, the lower bound will be set to zero). Additionally, each distance constraint stores whether an intra- or intermolecular constraint has been defined for the respective pair of features and whether a feature is constrained to be part of a protein or small molecule component. Given this information, the fingerprint described above can also be generated for the pharmacophore query. For each feature type present in the query, the respective bits of the feature presence fingerprint are set taking the respective molecule components into account. The query fingerprint can then be compared to the respective fingerprint of any database entry. Each bit set in the query fingerprint is required to be present in the database entry fingerprint. Only if the fingerprint comparison passes, a database entry is subjected to a 3D search using the precalculated feature points. A constraint-aware backtracking algorithm similar to the one described by Chisholm et al.(30) is used to search for matching structures in distance space. Given a specific database entry and the query, a match protocol is generated by sorting the pharmacophore features in the query with respect to (a) a decreasing number of constraints, (b) an increasing number of feature points available in the database entry, and (c) increasing smallest widths of any of the distance constraint ranges a pharmacophore feature is involved in (for a run-time comparison see the Supporting Information). A depth-first-search (DFS) is then started from the feature involved in the highest number of constraints. Every time the current match is expanded to include a feature point of the next feature in the match protocol, all distance constraints (including the check for intra-/intermolecular) to all feature points already part of the current match are evaluated and only if all constraints pass, the match is expanded. For small molecule crystal structures the search engine additionally takes symmetry-related copies of the feature points into account and by default considers 26 unit cells around the central unit cell (unless the query is intramolecular only). Full matches are recorded if (a) they are not symmetry-related to previous matches and (b) they pass a Cartesian space overlay check. The latter check is necessary, since not all matches in distance space correspond to matches of the original query in Cartesian space. For example, the mirror images of a tetrahedral four-point pharmacophore cannot be distinguished in distance space. Thus, a gradient-based overlay technique has been developed which checks if a match in distance space can be overlaid onto the pharmacophore in Cartesian space (see Supporting Information). If this overlay is possible, i.e., a rigid-body transformation can be found that overlays a given set of points onto the pharmacophore spheres such that all points from the match lie in their corresponding pharmacophore spheres, the match is recorded.

    Match Processing

    Since for a single database entry multiple matches may have been identified, for each match the Kabsch algorithm(31) is used to calculate the minimum overlay root-mean-square deviation (rmsd) of the point coordinates in the match, with respect to the pharmacophore sphere centers. This is used to obtain a unique ranking of the matches. Although the search method will have checked that all matched points could lie inside their corresponding tolerance spheres, they may not necessarily do so after the application of the Kabsch algorithm. Nevertheless, the ranking obtained by this method follows the user’s intuition in most cases. Additionally, the rmsd-based ranking allows the user to, for example, only keep the top n matches per structure. The molecular structures corresponding to a feature database entry are loaded from the structural database (residing on disk), and for each pharmacophore match, the matched structures are overlaid onto the pharmacophore using the transformation obtained by the Kabsch method. By default, a bounding sphere is calculated for the pharmacophore and only the parts of the molecular structures inside the bounding sphere are kept. Ring systems (plus attached terminal substituents) are always kept as intact units if at least one ring atom is inside the bounding sphere. For bonds which have one atom located inside and the other one located outside the bounding sphere, the latter atom will be replaced by an R-group symbol.

    Diagrams

    A force-field based diagram generation method is used to create a 2D representation of each pharmacophore match. First, all base features of the pharmacophore query are projected into the 2D plane. The generated 2D coordinates are then used as a template in the diagram generation process to place the atoms of a 3D pharmacophore match in the same region in the 2D layout. Since the same template is used for all matches, the resulting diagrams are aligned in the same way. An example is presented in Figure 2.

    Interactive User Interface

    The design of an interactive user interface was a key component of making the above-described functionality available in an intuitive and easily accessible fashion. In this context, the meaning of interactive is not to be mistaken for real time. While in the interactive interface, the user can interact with the search query at any time; complete search results will not necessarily be available instantaneously. Once a feature database has been created for a given structural database, this features database is stored in memory. The user then starts building a search query from scratch or based on a reference structure, which is automatically annotated with the feature types stored in the database. Tolerance sphere positions and radii can be modified using the mouse, and as soon as the interactive editing mode is activated, any change in the topology or the geometry of the pharmacophore query triggers a new database search. Any pharmacophore match found will be immediately displayed in the 3D view, giving the user instant feedback on the kind of hits to be expected from the current query setup. In the background, all hits of a search (up to a user-defined rmsd limit) are collected and up to a user-defined number of structures (typically around 100–500) are displayed in the 3D view as well as in a results browser showing the 2D diagrams along with the structure identifier and overlay rmsd. While the hits are generally sorted according to the overlay rmsd, they can also be clustered on-the-fly with respect to a molecular fingerprint(13) at a user-specified Tanimoto threshold. In order to speed up the database search for large databases, which may contain several hundred thousand structures, the user can dedicate several CPU cores to the search if available.     Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    CSD-CrossMinera was designed to query structural databases in an efficient, intuitive, and highly flexible manner. Due to the generality of the approach, we foresee applications in small molecule drug discovery, in protein design, and in materials science. Here we focus on examples of relevance to medicinal chemistry. Several of these involve the identification of molecular fragments binding to closely related protein environments. A second set of examples illustrates the use of CSD-CrossMiner as a scaffold hopping tool, either with intramolecular constraints only or in combination with a protein environment. All case studies involve targets of previous drug discovery projects. The detailed pharmacophore queries used in this section can be found in the Supporting Information.
    The following examples show results from both PDB and CSD searches. To be able to query both databases with the same program, our algorithm has to correctly assign feature types for CSD structures, which typically contain explicit hydrogen atoms, as well as for PDB structures, which are stored without this information in our database. We provide an extended set of SMARTS-based feature definitions for both worlds depending on whether hydrogen atoms are present or not in a molecule. For structures without hydrogens feature type assignment is less obvious, and expert knowledge about preferred protonation and tautomer states is used to classify substructures as hydrogen bond donors and acceptors. It is also noteworthy that our definitions cover nonclassical interaction partners, such as polarized C–H groups acting as hydrogen bond donors. The full list of feature type definitions can be found in the Supporting Information.

    Fragments Binding to Related Protein Environments

    A standard task in structure-based drug design is “pocket filling”, i.e., the identification of ligand moieties that are complementary in shape and interaction types to a given protein environment. Common questions are “What does ligand group X interact with?” and “Which other ligand group X could be suitable in this protein environment?” Often we do not want to limit ourselves to a certain interaction type for group X. There are several features that make CSD-CrossMiner particularly suitable to address such questions. First, molecules are annotated as being protein or small molecule enabling protein–ligand interaction searches. Second, a special feature type “heavy_atom” exists which matches all non-hydrogen atoms without further restriction. This allows for queries in which one interaction partner is kept very general. Third, the query can be set up directly in 3D space by means of imported reference structures. This is a big advantage over 2D query builders implemented in other programs, which require complex geometric definitions to represent 3D relationships within a binding pocket. In the following, we will illustrate the power of CSD-CrossMiner with three different queries in which one interaction partner is defined while the other is completely open.
    Inhibitors of the cysteine protease cathepsin L typically form a covalent bond with the active site Cys 25 and gain additional binding affinity by occupying the S2 and S3 pockets.(32-35) The S3 pocket is characterized by a planar amide backbone fragment involving Gly 67–68 at its base, while the carbonyl group of Gly 61 exposes a hydrogen bond acceptor into the binding site (Figure 4). This pocket has been used previously as a model system to study stacking effects on amide groups(36) as well as halogen bonding(33, 37) in detail. With CSD-CrossMiner we can look for ligand fragments that are known to bind to similar protein environments. Figure 4 illustrates how we define a CSD-CrossMiner query based on the key residues Gly 61, Gly 67–68, and Leu 69, which define the shape of the S3 cavity. We did not include the side chain of Glu 63 due to its high flexibility as observed in crystal structure overlays. To ensure that solutions are enriched with ligand fragments interacting with all specified protein residues, we included two ligand “heavy_atom” features into our intermolecular query.
    As similar S3 pockets are present in related cysteine proteases such as cathepsin K or S, complexes with these proteins turn up at the top of the hit list. Clustering the solution space in real time helps to remove redundant binding site information and allows the user to quickly grasp the diversity in ligand topology and protein–ligand interactions. Figure 5 shows three solutions to our query that appear in the top 25 entries of the hit list after clustering with a Tanimoto threshold of 0.7 and removing all cathepsin L, K, and S structures. Interestingly, our pharmacophore motif defined for the S3 pocket in cathepsin L also matches the S1 pocket of serine proteases, e.g., factor Xa structure 2ei7, a relationship we were not aware of before. Here, the indole heterocycle stacks on one of the backbone amides which form the wall of the S1 pocket, while the indole NH donor interacts with a backbone carbonyl oxygen acceptor. Other matches from non-cathepsin binding pockets are a chlorophenyl fragment in HIV-RT (PDB code 3dm2) and a triazole heterocycle in cruzain (PDB code 4klb). The chlorophenyl is a motif that is already known to interact very strongly when attached to the pyrrolidine scaffold displayed in Figure 4.(33) Compared to a methyl group it provides a ∼500-fold affinity gain. This was rationalized by favorable stacking of the phenyl group to the amide backbone and a close to ideal halogen bond interaction of the chlorine atom with the carbonyl oxygen atom of Gly 61. In HIV-RT, too, a halogen bond with the carbonyl backbone of Val 179 can be identified while the phenyl interacts with the amide of the Asn 103 side chain in a more perpendicular fashion.
    Another site for molecular recognition is the S4 pocket of the serine protease factor Xa. It is composed of the side chains of Tyr 99, Phe 174, and Trp 215, providing an aromatic cage with opportunities for both edge-to-face and stacking interactions. Strong gains in binding affinity can be achieved by interacting with this pocket, and the vast majority of potent factor Xa inhibitors extend into this region (Figure 6).(38) Using the S4 pocket as reference for a CSD-CrossMiner query, we can see that such aromatic cages are not unique to factor Xa but occur in a larger number of functionally unrelated proteins as recognition motif for small molecule fragments. Among them is choline-binding protein L which binds a tetraalkylammonium moiety in a box of two Trp and one Tyr residues. In independent studies of cation−π interactions, the Diederich group identified quarternary onium ions as very efficient binders to the S4 pocket of factor Xa.(39) Other examples with aromatic cages are the first tudor-like domain of spindlin 1 and a back-pocket in the DFG-out binding site of the protein kinase lok (Figure 6).
    β sheets have pleated patterns of laterally connected β strands with shallow pockets about 7 Å wide. These pockets can form part of ligand binding sites. In p38 MAP kinase, the lipophilic back pocket separated from the hinge region by the gatekeeper residue is partially formed by two β strands. Which ligand motifs interact with this environment? Figure 7 shows the CSD-CrossMiner query for this question using the donor/acceptor pattern of the protein backbone and a single “heavy_atom” ligand probe. Hits show a propensity for halogen binding, small alkyl groups, hydroxyl functionalities, and (hetero)aromatic rings. In the latter case, the aromatic plane is oriented almost symmetrically between two adjacent faces of the β sheet with angles of 60–70°. The ligand substructures that are displayed in Figure 7 occur in at least 100 matches of our query. While this is not a solid statistical analysis, it still indicates a trend for favorable interaction with β sheet pockets. This is often enough for idea generation in a medicinal chemistry program. The binding pockets that match our query are mostly from the kinase family (e.g., p38, abl, braf, egfr), but also other targets (e.g., HIV-RT) have cavities in which ligand atoms interact with β sheets.

    Scaffold Hopping

    Scaffold hopping is defined as replacing an active central element of a molecule with a novel one, with the aim of identifying patentable structures or improving molecular properties.(40, 41) An integral part of 3D scaffold hopping algorithms is the matching of substructures to exit vectors that connect the core with the substituents of the template molecule.(42, 43) In CSD-CrossMiner we have the possibility to define an exit vector pharmacophore which allows the user to combine scaffold hopping with other powerful features of our tool. The two examples below, from 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase 1 (11β-HSD1) and from kinase drug discovery, illustrate the concept.
    11β-HSD1 is a central enzyme in the regulation of intracellular cortisol concentrations and has been targeted with the aim to treat the metabolic syndrome. It reduces the carbonyl group of the substrate cortisone to the hydroxyl analogue cortisol by means of NADPH as a cofactor. Some of the first inhibitors of 11β-HSD1 used an amide carbonyl group to mimic the cortisone carbonyl interactions with protein residues Ser150 and Tyr163, which are important for preorganizing the substrate for catalysis (Figure 8).(44) While the carbonyl oxygen serves as a central anchor point, the amide exit vectors appropriately position two hydrophobic residues in the steroid binding cavity of 11β-HSD1. We were wondering about other small molecule motifs that could engage in two intermolecular hydrogen bond interactions while preserving at least one of the exit vectors and constructed the pharmacophore query displayed in Figure 8. The same query can be used for searching the PDB and the CSD, which extends the search space significantly and which is to our knowledge not possible with any other tool. The results of the CSD-based search returned several interesting hits summarized in Figure 9. Apart from the secondary amide used in the template, sulfonamides and aliphatic hydroxyl groups fulfill our query definitions. Most interestingly, a tetrazole motif is identified from the search in which the two intermolecular hydrogen bonds are formed by two adjacent nitrogen atoms with exposed lone pairs instead of a single oxygen atom. This structural insight opens up a plethora of heteroaromatic rings as scaffold hopping possibilities. Indeed, a triazole was used as central scaffold in 11β-HSD1 inhibitors from Amgen,(45) and the complex structure (Figure 9d) showed the same hydrogen bond arrangement as found in our search.
    Scaffold hopping through design of a “pseudo ring” formed by an intramolecular hydrogen bond is an elegant approach to broaden the intellectual property space for kinase hinge binding motifs. An example of this is the replacement of the pyrido[2,3-d]pyrimidin-7-one core displayed in Figure 10a by a single aromatic ring with an additional intramolecular hydrogen bond from a pyrimidin-4-ylurea motif yielding equipotent tyrosine kinase inhibitors.(46) We aimed at reproducing this finding by running a pharmacophore search in the CSD involving planar ring, acceptor, and exit vector features (Figure 10). Results of this search are summarized in Figure 10b−d and show that solutions with an internal hydrogen bond are retrieved among top-ranking matches.
    We can reuse this query and ask which kinase hinge binding scaffolds are known in the PDB that contain such a ligand pharmacophore. To this end, only two protein features for the hinge hydrogen bond donor and acceptor involved in interactions with the reference ligand of Figure 10 need to be added. Figure 11 contains representative examples of kinase scaffolds that match this pharmacophore, illustrating the diversity of ligand topologies that can be used.

    Conclusions and Outlook

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    We have presented an approach that enables interactive navigation of the CSD and the PDB through the same interface. Essentially, the approach combines well-known concepts (fingerprints, 3D pharmacophores, SMARTS-based substructure definitions) with each other to achieve an unprecedented degree of interactivity and usability. High query precision can be achieved by substructure-based feature definition, while the spherical 3D constraints allow for a user-definable amount of fuzziness. Search results are fed back immediately such that iterative query refinement becomes a normal way of working. Molecular fingerprint-based clustering and structural annotations, e.g., information on the drug target, serve to narrow down potentially large lists of hit structures. The optional specification of intra- and intermolecular constraints between pharmacophore features and the ability to restrict features to be part of a protein or a small molecule enable a plethora of different query types: applications may range from the retrieval of sets of overlaid structures sharing common binding patterns to scaffold hopping, cavity comparisons, and the analysis of complex multicomponent nonbonded networks. Most interaction queries are immediately applicable to the PDB and the CSD, substituting a protein interaction partner by the crystal environment. We believe that this flexibility sets the stage for broad applications for structure-based design and solid state modeling alike.
