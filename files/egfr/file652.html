    Abstract

    Biphenyl-4-carboxylic acid-[2-(1H-indol-3-yl)-ethyl]-methylamide 1 (CA224) is a nonplanar analogue of fascaplysin (2) that specifically inhibits Cdk4–cyclin D1 in vitro. Compound 1 blocks the growth of cancer cells at G0/G1 phase of the cell cycle. It also blocks the cell cycle at G2/M phase, which is explained by the fact that it inhibits tubulin polymerization. Additionally, it acts as an enhancer of depolymerization for taxol-stabilized tubulin. Western blot analyses of p53-positive cancer cells treated with compound 1 indicated upregulation of p53, p21, and p27 proteins together with downregulation of cyclin B1 and Cdk1. Compound 1 selectively induces apoptosis of SV40 large T-antigen transformed cells and significantly reduces colony formation efficiency, in a dose-dependent manner, of lung cancer cells. It is efficacious at 1/10th of the MTD against human tumors derived from HCT-116 and NCI-H460 cells in SCID mouse models. The promising efficacy of compound 1 in human xenograft models as well as its excellent therapeutic window indicates its potential for clinical development.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Mammalian cell division is a highly controlled process, and the loss of this control results in the development of a cancer phenotype. Mammalian cells undergo a proliferative cycle composed of various stages (G0, G1, S, G2, and M phase), and the transition of cells from one phase to another involves various check-point mechanisms, including cyclin-dependent kinases, checkpoint kinases, and their partner cyclins. The malfunctioning of any of these checkpoint mechanisms leads to uncontrolled cell division and proliferation.(1-5) Cyclin-dependent kinase 4 and its cyclin partner D1 (a check-point mechanism) control the transition of cells from G1 to S phase of the cell cycle.(6-9) Active Cdk4–cyclin D complexes inactivate retinoblastoma protein (pRb) by phosphorylating it specifically at Ser780 and Ser795, which allows the G1/S transition of the cell cyle to proceed.(6, 10) Hyperphosphorylation of pRb leads to a loss of control over gene transcription through the E2F family of transcription factors, which ultimately turns into uncontrolled cell division. In contrast, pRb phosphorylation by Cdk2 or Cdk3 along with their cyclin partners, A and C, is not sufficient to inactivate pRb’s functions.(11-13) A large number of human tumors (around 96%) lose their normal cell cycle transition check-point mechanism due to a variety of genetic and biochemical adaptations, including hyperactivity of Cdk4, downregulation of Cdk4’s positive regulator p16INK4A, and mutations in pRb. The vital role of Cdk4–cyclin D1 in tumor development has been confirmed by experiments in Cdk4 and cyclin D1 knockout mice, which have been shown to be resistant to tumor development. On the other hand, antisense nucleotide-based targeting of cyclin D decreases tumor size and growth in vivo. On the basis of these experimental observations, the Cdk4–cyclin D complex is considered to be a promising target for combating cancer.(14-16)
    Inhibition of Cdk4–cyclin D1 with small molecules has been an area of major interest in the field of anticancer drug discovery during the last 2 decades. There have been numerous scientific reports highlighting the role of Cdk4–cyclin D1 inhibitors in cancer treatment.(17-22) Pfizer’s palbociclib (6-acetyl-8-cyclopentyl-5-methyl-2-{[5-(1-piperazinyl)-2-pyridinyl]amino}pyrido[2,3-d]pyrimidin-7(8H)-one; PD-0332991), a selective inhibitor of Cdk4 and Cdk6, has received FDA approval for the treatment of patients with breast cancer.(23, 24) Other Cdk4/Cdk6 dual inhibitors, LY2835219 (N-[5-[(4-ethyl-1-piperazinyl)methyl]-2-pyridinyl]-5-fluoro-4-[4-fluoro-2-methyl-1-(1-methylethyl)-1H-benzimidazol-6-yl]-2-pyrimidinamine methanesulfonate) and LEE011 (7-cyclopentyl-N,N-dimethyl-2-((5-(piperazin-1-yl)pyridin-2-yl)amino)-7H-pyrrolo[2,3-d]pyrimidine-6-carboxamide), are in phase I and III clinical studies for breast cancer (http://clinicaltrials.gov).(25, 26)
    The main objective of our studies was to develop potent and specific small molecule inhibitors of Cdk4–cyclin D1 based on the structure of fascaplysin (2), which is a pentacyclic quaternary salt originally isolated from the Fijian sponge Fascaplysinopsis Bergquist sp.(27) It is known to possess antimicrobial, antimalarial, and antiacetylcholinesterase activities.(27-29) It also displays potent cytotoxicity against small cell lung cancer cells and induces cell cycle arrest in G0/G1 at lower concentrations and in S-phase at higher concentrations.(30) Compound 2 also showed antitumor effects in a sarcoma mouse model through apoptotic and antiangiogenic pathways.(31) Fascaplysin is a specific inhibitor of Cdk4–cyclin D1, with an IC50 value of 410 nM.(9, 32) It inhibits Cdk4–cyclin D1 in vitro and blocks the growth of normal and cancer cells at G0/G1 phase of the cell cycle, which correlates with the accumulation of hypophosphorylated pRb, implying that there is no phosphorylation of pRb at Cdk4-specific serine residues.(9) Compound 2 has also been reported to inhibit Cdc25B with an IC50 of 1 μg/mL (3.26 μM).(33, 34) An analogue of fascaplysin, 1-deoxysecofascaplysin A, has been reported to inhibit the growth of the MCF-7 breast cancer and OVCAR-3 ovarian cancer cell lines in vitro.(35)
    Nonetheless, it is unlikely that fascaplysin will ever be used therapeutically as an anticancer agent because it is highly toxic. The potential for its planar structure to intercalate double-stranded DNA has been suggested as a possible explanation for its unusual biological activity and toxicity. The DNA binding property of fascaplysin is similar to that of structurally related DNA intercalating agents like cryptolepine and ellipticine.(36) We have explored the possibility of separating the DNA intercalating ability of fascaplysin from its potent Cdk4-specific inhibitory activity. The aim of the current study was, therefore, to devise nonplanar (nontoxic) Cdk4–cyclin D1 inhibitors based on the structure of fascaplysin.(37-40)
    Biphenyl-4-carboxylic acid [2-(1H-indol-3-yl)-ethyl]-methylamide 1 (CA224)(41) was initially identified as a Cdk4–cyclin D1-specific inhibitor lacking the ability to intercalate DNA. As expected, compound 1 manifested its Cdk4-specific inhibitory ability by blocking cancer cells at G0/G1 phase of the cell cycle. Surprisingly, however, compound 1 also inhibited the cells at G2/M phase in a Cdk-independent manner. Further investigations showed that compound 1 inhibited the growth of a panel of 10 cancer cell lines (some of which were potentially resistant to cancer chemotherapy) in the low micromolar range. The validity of these results was corroborated by the observation that compound 1 has the ability to diminish the colony formation efficiency of cancer cells. A Cdk-independent G2/M block was found to be associated with its anti-tubulin activity. Compound 1 inhibited the polymerization of tubulin in vitro and also showed an enhancing effect on tubulin depolymerization in live cells. In human xenograft models, compound 1 was found to inhibit the growth of HCT-116 and H-460 tumors at 1/10th of the MTD, 100 mg/kg. Here, we present the in vitro and in vivo anticancer activity of compound 1 in detail.

    Chemistry

    Our efforts toward discovery of nontoxic analogues of fascaplysin led to the identification of compound 1, a nonplanar analogue of fascaplysin (2). Briefly, the synthesis of compound 1 involves three steps starting from commercially available tryptamine (3). The treatment of tryptamine (3) with ethyl chloroformate produced carbamate ester 4, which, on further reduction using LAH, led to formation of N-methyl tryptamine 5. Compound 5, on reaction with 4-biphenyl carbonyl chloride (6), produced compound 1. The detailed lead optimization and SAR have been described in previous publications.(37-41) Scheme 1 shows the synthesis of compound 1. It is worth noting that nonplanar analogues with opened C and D rings of fascaplysin (2) demonstrated a loss of DNA intercalation activity. Among C and D-ring opened analogues, compounds with para-substituted E rings (structures shown in Table 1) maintained their ability to inhibit Cdk4 in a selective manner.

    Selective Inhibition of Cdk4–Cyclin D1

    A series of new compounds based on the structure of fascaplysin was identified as specific inhibitors of Cdk4–cyclin D1 (Table 1). Compound 1 was found to be the most potent inhibitor of Cdk4–cyclin D1 (IC50 = 6 μM) as well as selective when the Cdk4 IC50 was compared with the IC50’s obtained from assays with Cdk2–cyclin A, Cdk1–cyclin B1, and Cdk9–cyclin T1. Compound 1 was superior to its other two structural analogues, N-(2-(1H-indol-3-yl)ethyl)-4-chloro-N-methylbenzamide (CA223, 7) and N-(2-(1H-indol-3-yl)ethyl)-4-tert-butyl-N-methylbenzamide (CA225, 8),(41) with >6-fold better activity against Cdk4–cyclin D1 (Table 1). Unlike fascaplysin, compound 1 does not intercalate DNA.(41) Compound 1 was also tested against 58 represenative kinases at Millipore Bioscience Division, UK. It was found that compound 1 was inactive at 10 μM against all kinases, including Cdk5–p35, Cdk6–cyclin D1, Cdk7–cyclin H, EGFR, GSK3β, MAPK1, MEK1, PDGFR, Plk3, PKA, PKCα, IGF-1R, and others. Results are shown in the Supporting Information. These results support compound 1’s selective ability to inhibit Cdk4–cyclin D1 in vitro while having a reduced or no affinity for other kinases tested.
    In order to understand the observed selectivity toward Cdk4–cyclin D1 versus Cdk2–cyclin A, molecular modeling studies were carried out. The ATP binding sites of these two Cdks are well-conserved and share 45% sequence homology with each other; however, compound 1 displayed varying degrees of affinity to these Cdks. These two Cdks differ from each other primarily by the following three sequences: 94–97(Glu-His-Val-Asp)Cdk4/81–84(Glu-Phe-Leu-His)Cdk2, 101–102(Arg-Thr)Cdk4/88–89(Lys-Lys)Cdk2, and Glu144Cdk4/Gln131Cdk2. Compound 1 interacts flexibly with the ATP binding pockets of Cdk4 and Cdk2 with 84-fold selectivity toward Cdk4 due to the flexible conformational movement of compound 1’s amide bond (1 interacts with Cdk4 and Cdk2 in two different conformational states), which allows free rotation of the biphenyl ring. This subsequently leads to a loss of major hydrophobic interactions with Cdk2. Two conformations of compound 1 include (a) a trans conformation (green ligand in Figure 1a; dihedral angle, Ψ = −151.5), which was found to interact selectively with the side chains of Arg101 in Cdk4 by a hydrophobic π–cation interaction. However, in Cdk2, this interaction is missing because the corresponding residue, Lys88, is far away from compound 1’s binding cavity. (b) A cis conformation (orange ligand in Figure 1a; dihedral angle, Ψ = 2.6) that interacts with Cdk2. On the other hand, the trans conformation of compound 1 is stabilized in the Cdk4 binding site because the negatively charged side chain of Glu144 opposes the biphenyl aromatic ring. In the case of Cdk2, the cis conformation is stabilized because the biphenyl aromatic rings are well-accommodated by the corresponding neutral residue, Gln131. A comparison of the binding site amino acids of Cdk4 and Cdk2 is shown in Figure 1c.

    Cancer Cell Growth Inhibition

    All fascaplysin analogues, including the most potent compound in the series, compound 1, were tested against a panel of 10 different cancer cell lines (some of which are known to be relatively resistant to known chemotherapeutic agents) for their ability to inhibit cancer cell growth in vitro. The inhibitory effects of the compounds were quantified using MTT assay, and IC50’s were determined (i.e., the concentration, expressed in micromolar, of a compound at which 50% of cell growth is inhibited). The results of these cell proliferation assays indicated that compound 1 inhibits the growth of cancer cells in vitro at low micromolar concentrations (Table 2). Among all of the analogues,(41) compound 1 was found to be the most potent molecule even at the cellular level. The inhibition of cell growth was both p53- and pRb-independent, with the latter indicating that Cdk4–cyclin D1 inhibition is not the only cellular target for the mechanism of action of these molecules.

    Compound 1 Retains the G0/G1 Block in Serum-Starved p53-Null Calu-1 Cells

    Compound 1, although identified as a Cdk4-specific inhibitor in the in vitro enzyme assay, generally tends to block cancer cells more profoundly at G2/M than at G0/G1 phase of the cell cycle. Moreover, compound 1 inhibits tubulin polymerization in vitro with a higher potency than it inhibits Cdk4–cyclin D1. In Calu-1 cells, the mitotic spindle checkpoint is impaired, implying that these cells cannot be blocked at G2/M. Hence, Calu-1 cells were treated with compound 1 after they were released from synchronization at G0/G1 (by serum-starving the cells using 0.1% FBS for 24 h). At IC50 and IC70 concentrations of compound 1, the G0/G1 block was either partially or full maintained.(40) Because the maintenance of the G0/G1 block after serum starvation requires Cdk4 to be inactive, these results indicate that compound 1 probably inhibits cellular Cdk4 at these concentrations and thereby maintains the G0/G1 block. In the absence of any other kinase inhibitory data, the results obtained with Calu-1 cells confirm the Cdk4 inhibitory potential of compound 1 in live cells.

    Compound 1 at Its IC50 Concentration Induces a Profound G2/M Block in Asynchronous A549 (p53+) and NCI-H1299 (p53-Null) Cancer Cells

    Incubation of A549 (p53+) cells with compound 1 at its IC50 concentration for 24 h induced a profound block at G2/M, as indicated by the percentage of cells at G2/M. As seen in Figure 2, at the IC50 concentration of compound 1, 89% of cells appear to be arrested in G2/M phase (Figure 2B), and at the IC70 concentration, 91% of cells were blocked at G2/M (not shown). Incubation of NCI-H1299 (p53-null) with the IC50 concentration of compound 1 resulted in the accumulation of a large number of cells (71% cells) at G2/M (Figure 2D). These results confirm that a profound G2/M block can be observed in cells (A549 and NCI-H1299) where the mitotic spindle checkpoint is intact.

    Nocodazole- and Paclitaxel-Induced G2/M Block Is Maintained by Compound 1 in NCI-H358 Lung Cancer Cells

    NCI-H358 (p53-null) cells were treated with nocodazole (1 μM, a suboptimal concentration) for 18 h in order to induce a partial block at G2/M so that treated cells are minimally stressed. When the blocked cells were released in fresh medium, they readily entered the cell cycle without any apoptosis. When blocked cells were released in the presence of compound 1 for 12 h, the cells maintained the G2/M block, and >50% of G0/G1 and S phase cells entered G2/M (compare Figure 2E–H). Even though only the nocodazole treatment experiment is shown, similar results were obtained when paclitaxel-blocked cells were released in the presence of compound 1 for 12 h. These observations suggest that, at least in p53-null/pRb-null NCI-H358 cells, compound 1 maintains the pro-metaphase block induced by nocadozole or paclitaxel during mitosis.

    Compound 1 Blocks NCI-H358 Cells in G2/M after Release from Hydroxyurea-Mediated G1/S Cell Synchronization

    Hydroxyurea (250 μM, 18 h) was used to block cells at G1/S (77%; Figure 2J), a stage of the cell cycle where Cdk2-specific inhibitors normally act. When released in the presence of compound 1, cells proceeded from G1/S, confirming that compound 1 does not inhibit cellular Cdk2. Cells ultimately accumulate at G2/M (72%; Figure 2L). These results again indicate that compound 1 has an inherent tendency to induce block at G2/M phase of the cell cycle, at least in cells where the mitoic spindle checkpoint is normal.

    Selective Killing of SV40 Large T-Antigen Transformed Normal Mouse Embryonic Liver Cells by Compound 1

    SV40 large T-antigen inactivates tumor suppressor proteins p53 and pRb and thereby transforms normal cells into tumorigenic cells. We used normal mouse embryonic hepatic (liver) cells, BNL CL2, and its SV-40 large T-antigen transformed counterpart, BNL SV A.8, to study the effects of compound 1. The normal cells, upon 48 h incubation with 1, exhibited prominent G2/M arrest at both the IC50 and IC70 concentrations, with less than 5% of cells detected in sub-G0/G1 phase (Figure 2N) and more than 50% of cells appearing at G2/M phase of the cell cycle. Interestingly, in the SV40 large T-antigen transformed cell line, significant apoptotic cell death was observed. After 48 h of treatment at the IC50 concentration of compound 1, 31% of cells were detected in sub-G1 phase (Figure 2P), indicating apoptosis. The percent apoptosis increased further from 31 to 44% at the IC70 concentration after 48 h of incubation (data not shown). Similar results were obtained using normal and SV-40 large T-antigen transformed human lung fibroblast cells, WI-38 (data not shown).

    Effect of Compound 1 on the Cellular Level of Cyclin B1, Cdk1, p53, p21CIP1/WAF1 (p21), and p27KIP1 (p27) in p53+ Cells

    Western blot analyses of p53+ cells, A549 and LS174T, after treatment with compound 1 (at the IC50 concentration) for 24 h demonstrated a more than 10-fold induction of p53. Therefore, it is likely that the global Cdk inhibitor p21CIP1/WAF1 (p21) was also induced. The levels of p27KIP1 (p27) were also elevated after compound 1 treatment (Figure 3). The proteins Cdk1 and cyclin B1 were downregulated in the treated cells as compared with that in untreated control cells. Repression of cyclin B1 and Cdk1 and elevated levels of p21 and p27 are a possible explanation for the G2/M block seen in A549 and LS174T cells that bear functional copies of the tumor suppressor protein p53 (Figure 3).
    The effects of compound 1 on the cellular level of cyclin B1, Cdk1, p53, p21CIP1/WAF1 (p21), and p27KIP1 (p27), in p53-null cells, were also investigated. Western blot analyses on proteins from MIAPaCa-2 cells (carrying a p53 mutation) showed that p53, p21, and p27 levels remained unchanged, indicating that the p21 and p27 induction seen in p53-positive cells is probably p53-dependent. Interestingly, cyclin B1 and Cdk1 levels were elevated, and phosphorylation of Cdk1 at Tyr15 remained unaffected (data not shown), indicating that Cdk1–cyclin B1 is still active in p53-mutated cells after compound 1 treatment.

    Cell-Free Tubulin Polymerization Assays in Vitro Indicate Inhibition of Tubulin Polymerization by Compound 1

    Compound 1 inhibited the growth of cancer cells in vitro at relatively low concentrations compared to those at which it inhibited Cdk4–cyclin D1. FACS analysis and mitotic index experiments (data not shown) indicated that compound 1 blocked cell growth at the pro-metaphase stage of the cell cycle. In addition to these observations, the 2–3-fold increase of compound 1’s IC50 in cells with an impaired mitotic spindle checkpoint suggested another cellular role for compound 1, possibly as an anti-microtubule agent. We investigated the action of compound 1 on tubulin polymerization in vitro. The results indicated a strong anti-tubulin activity of compound 1 (Figure 4a), whereas fascaplysin did not show any interaction with tubulin (data not shown). Representative polymerization curves of compound 1, paclitaxel, and nocodazole are shown in Figure 4a. Compound 1 inhibits the polymerization of tubulin, which can be concluded from the dose-dependent decrease in Vmax (mOD/min) and the reduction of final polymer mass (Figure 4a). When tested at four different concentrations, compound 1 decreased the Vmax from 17 mOD/min to 6.2, 2.1, 1.1, and 0.4 mOD/min at 2.5, 5, 10, and 25 μM, respectively. As a consequence of decreased Vmax, up to an 80% reduction in the final polymer mass was observed.
    Microtubules, the key components of the cytoskeleton, are made up of α/β-tubulin heterodimers. Microtubule assembly has been targeted using a number of polymerization inhibitors and inducers by binding at different sites including (a) the colchicine binding site (at the interphase of the α/β-tubulin heterodimer) and (b) the taxol and vinblastine binding site deep inside β-tubulin. The mechanism of inhibition of tubulin polymerization involves binding at the interphase of α/β-tubulin and forming a complex with tubulin like that with colchicine. This complex is added to the microtubule assembly, where it induces unfavorable conformational changes in tubulin dimer (M-loop) and thus further polymerization gets stopped. Furthermore, the tubulin–polymerization inhibitor complex perturbs microtubule growth by sterically blocking further addition of the tubulin dimers to form the microtubule assembly.(42-44) From molecular docking studies performed at the colchicine binding site, it was observed that compound 1 binds at the α/β-tubulin interphase by H-bonding. Compound 1 interacts with Thr353 of β-tubulin by H-bonding. In addition to this, the hydrophobic biphenyl ring fits in the hydrophobic core of β-tubulin formed by Leu248, Ala250, Leu252, Cys241, Leu255, Ala316, Ala317, and Ala354 (Figure 4b). Interestingly, these interactions were missing with fascaplysin. The binding of compound 1 at the interphase of α/β-tubulin is supposed to induce conformational changes in the protein that further perturbs tubulin polymerization, preventing microtubule assembly.

    Compound 1 Inhibits Paclitaxel-Mediated Tubulin Polymerization and Enhances Tubulin Depolymerization in Live Cells

    The tubulin polymerization experiments performed on whole cells (A549 and NSCLC, with an intact mitotic spindle checkpoint) confirmed the observation that compound 1 inhibits the polymerization of tubulin. Moreover, compound 1 also enhances the depolymerization of stabilized tubulin protein. The polymerized and depolymerized (soluble) forms of tubulin were gauged (via western blotting) from the accumulation/disappearence of tubulin protein from the pellet and supernatant fractions of cell lysates treated with the IC50 concentration of compound 1. In the first set of experiments, where cells were treated with compound 1 in the presence of the microtubule-stabilizing agent paclitaxel, compound 1 prevented tubulin polymerization (mediated by paclitaxel) in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 5, upper two panels). In the second set of experiments, where intracellular tubulin was stabilized with paclitaxel and then subjected to compound 1 treatment, an enhancement of tubulin depolymerization was observed with increasing concentrations of compound 1 (Figure 5, lower two panels).

    Compound 1 Inhibits Colony Formation Efficiency of A549 and Calu-1 Cells

    The ability of compound 1 to kill cancer cells was explored using a long-term survival assay that monitors a cancer cells’ colony-forming capacity in vitro. A549 (with a normal mitotic spindle checkpoint normal, pRb+) and Calu-1 (with an impaired mitotic spindle checkpoint, pRb+) cells were used in colony formation assays. The colony formation efficiency of A549 and Calu-1 cells is significantly reduced after compound 1 treatment (Figure 6). The concentration at which colony formation efficiency is reduced by 50% is comparatively lower than the IC50 concentration for cell growth inhibition in the MTT assay, indicating that a large number of cells loose the ability to form colonies or do not survive for a long time after compound 1 treatment. For A549 cells, a 50% reduction in colony formation efficiency was observed at an average concentration of compound 1 of 1.4 μM (cell growth inhibition IC50 = 3.5 μM), and for Calu-1 cells, a 50% colony formation efficiency reduction was observed at an average concentration of compound 1 of 3 μM (cell growth inhibition IC50 = 11.5 μM) (Table 2 and Figure 6), indicating that compound 1 may be quite efficacious in in vivo mouse tumor models.

    Compound 1 Induces Apoptotic Cell Death in Cancer Cells

    When A549 cells were treated with increasing concentrations of compound 1 for 24 h, a dose-dependent induction of fragmented nuclei, disrupted cell membrane, and apoptotic cell death was observed (Figure 7A, c–e). The induction of apoptosis in NCI-H460, NCI-H358, and NCI-H1299 cells was also measured with flow cytometry (Figure 7B). A dose- and time-dependent increase in apoptotic cell death was observed.

    Aqueous Solubility, Cytochrome P450 Liability, and Caco-2 Permeability of Compound 1

    The solubility of compound 1 in water, phosphate buffered saline (PBS), simulated gastric fluid (SGF), and simulated intestinal fluid (SIF) was found to be 80, 125, 125, and 5 μg/mL, respectively. In cytochrome P450 liability studies, at 10 μM, compound 1 showed 50, 14, 51 and 19% inhibition of CYP3A4, CYP2D6, CYP2C9, and CYP2C19, respectively. In a permeability experiment, the efflux ratio [Papp (B–A)/Papp (A–B)] was 1.1, indicating that compound 1 is not a substrate of efflux pumps.

    In Vivo Experiments: Pharmacokinetics and Maximum Tolerated Dose (MTD)

    In order to check the plasma exposure of the compound in animals, the pharmacokinetics of compound 1 was studied in BALB/c mice following a single 10 mg/kg dose administered orally (PO) and a 1.0 mg/kg dose administered intravenously (IV). The time versus plasma concentration profile of compound 1 is shown in Figure 8. Compound 1 showed good plasma exposure, with a Cmax of 190 and 371 ng/mL (537 and 1048 nM) by the PO and IV routes, respectively. The AUC0–∞ values were 182 and 189 ng h/mL (514 and 534 nM), respectively.
    The MTD determination studies were performed in Swiss albino mice for 2 weeks. The concentration at which compound 1 should be tested in vivo was thus ascertained. A loss of body weight in the animals was considered to be a measure of overtoxicity for the test compound. The concentration of the compound at which ≥10% weight loss was observed was determined and designated as the MTD, although a weight loss that is below 20% of the animal’s initial weight is usually harmless, and animals can recover once the treatment is stopped.(45) The toxicity results obtained from these studies indicated that for compound 1 the MTD in mice was ∼1000 mpk (mg/kg). On the basis of the MTD experiment and pharmacokinetics data, compound 1 was tested at 1/10th of the MTD concentration, i.e., 100 mg/kg, in in vivo xenograft studies.

    Effects of 1 on HCT-116 and NCI-H460 Tumor Growth

    The severe combined immunodeficient (SCID) mouse, lacking both T and B immune cells, is an established model to study the in vivo efficacy of molecules that have potential for use in the treatment of human cancers. When evaluated, compound 1 showed statistically significant (p < 0.05) tumor growth inhibition (Figure 9A) at 1/10th of the MTD concentration (100 mg/kg) in the HCT-116 tumor model. Mice injected with compound 1 exhibited approximately 80% tumor growth inhibition as compared to that of untreated mice injected with vehicle solution alone. In the second set of experiments, the antitumor potential of compound 1 was confirmed when it was proven to be highly efficacious in NCI-H460 tumor models at a 100 mg/kg concentration (Figure 9C,D). These results indicate that compound 1 has strong anticancer properties in vivo when administered at a concentration 10 times less than its MTD. Thus, with these studies, compound 1 has shown efficacy as a new class of anticancer compound.
    During this study, the effect on the animal’s body weight during compound treatment was also noted. The weight loss observed in treated animals was found to be ≤10% of the starting weight of the animals (Figure 9B) in the HCT-116 tumor model. This loss of weight is statistically insignificant, indicating that compound treatment caused no major toxicity or harm to the animals. The body weight of the animals at the beginning of the treatment was measured, and this was considered to be 100%. The percent weight loss or gain was calculated using the initial weight as a reference. Similar results were obtained in the NCI-H460 tumor model (data not shown). Compound 1 shows minimal toxicity in animal models and can be tolerated up to 1000 mg/kg without any significant toxicity.

    Conclusions

    In summary, we have presented data supporting the potential of compound 1 as a novel anticancer agent. The finding of its dual inhibition of both Cdk4–cyclin D1 and tubulin polymerization may address the problems of resistance that currently limit the efficacy of valuable anti-microtubule agents. The promising efficacy of compound 1 in xenograft models (HCT116 and NCI-H460 tumor models) and its excellent therapeutic window indicate the promise of this compound regarding its development as an anticancer agent.

    Flow Cytometric Analysis

    The untreated (control) and treated (with test compounds) cells were harvested by trypsinization, washed once with PBS, and then fixed in 70% chilled (−20 °C) ethanol for a minimum of 1 h. After the fixation step, cells were centrifuged for 5 min at 3000g at room temperature, and the pellet was then suspended in PBS containing 50 μg/mL propidium iodide (Sigma cat. no.     Tubulin Polymerization Assay in Vitro

    Purified tubulin was obtained commercially (Cytoskeleton Inc., Denver, CO, USA), and the polymerization assays were carried out according to a method previously described.(46, 47) The tubulin polymerization assay is based on an adaptation of the original method of Lee and Timasheff,(47) which demonstrated that light is scattered by microtubules to an extent that is proportional to the concentration of the microtubule polymer. The resulting polymerization curves are representative of three phases of microtubule polymerization, namely, nucleation, growth, and steady-state equilibrium. Paclitaxel and nocodazole were used in the assay as a known enhancer and inhibitor of tubulin polymerization, respectively. The ability of compound 1 to inhibit tubulin polymerization in vitro was determined according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, tubulin protein (3 mg/mL) was polymerized in GTP buffer (80 mM PIPES, pH 6.9, 2 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM EGTA, 10.2% glycerol, and 1 mM GTP) in the presence of a range of concentrations of compound 1 at 37 °C in a temperature-regulated Biotech spectrophotometer. The absorbance kinetic (at 340 nm) of 61 cycles for each sample was studied, and the readings were recorded at intervals of 1 min.

    Western Blot Analysis to Test Effects of Compound 1 on Tubulin Polymerization and Depolymerization of Stabilized Tubulin in Live Cells

    A549 cells were plated at a concentration of 10 000 cells per well in 1 mL of complete growth medium in 24-well/15 mm plates. The plates were incubated for 24 h for stabilization. In the first set of experiments, the cells were treated for 30 min with 10 nM paclitaxel and different concentrations of compound 1 (simultaneous treatment of paclitaxel and compound 1). In the second set, in order to study the effect of 1 on the stabilized form of tubulin, the cells were treated first with 10 nM paclitaxel for 30 min. The cell monolayer was then washed twice with sterile PBS, and fresh growth medium containing different concentrations of compound 1 was added. The plates were further incubated for 30 min, the cell monolayers were washed twice with sterile PBS at room temperature, and then 100 μL of tubulin extraction buffer (1 mM MgCl2, 2 mM EGTA, 0.5% NP40, and 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 6.8) supplemented with 2 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride and a protease inhibitor cocktail (Sigma cat. no. P8340) was added per well. After a brief and vigorous vortex, the cell lysates were incubated at room temperature for 5 min and then centrifuged at 16 000g for 10 min in order to separate the soluble and polymerized tubulin fractions. Each supernatant and pellet fraction was mixed with 10× sample buffer, heated for 7 min at 95 °C and resolved on 10% SDS-polyacrylamide gels. The resolved proteins were then subjected to western blotting (as described above) with a specific α-tubulin antibody (B-7, Santa Cruz Biotechnology cat. no. sc-5286).

    Detection of Apoptosis Using DAPI Staining

    Apoptosis and nuclear fragmentation were detected using DAPI staining followed by observation under a fluorescence microscope.(48) A549 cells were seeded at a concentration of 50 000 cells per well in 35 mm/6-well plates in a total of 2 mL of complete growth medium. After a 24 h stabilization period, cells were further incubated in the presence of different concentrations of compound 1 for 24 h. The smear formed on the slide was mounted in medium containing 1 μg/mL DAPI and covered with coverslip. The slides were observed using a fluorescence microscope (Olympus), and a minimum of 500 nuclei were counted for each sample.

    Clonogenic Assay

The plates were incubated for 24 h for stabilization and further incubated with a range of concentrations of fascaplysin and compound 1 (0.1–10 μM) for 24 h. Plates were then gently washed with PBS, replaced with fresh medium, and incubated at 37 °C.     Determination of Aqueous Solubility

    The thermodynamic aqueous solublity was determined using a 96-well plate protocol. The dissolution media, PBS (pH 7.4), SGF (pH 1.2), and SIF (pH 6.8), were prepared as per USP procedures. Briefly, compound 1 was loaded into a 96-well plate in the form of methanolic solution, followed by evaporation of solvent to obtain 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 25, 40, 80, 160, and 300 μg of compound in solid form in the wells. Thereafter, 200 μL of dissolution medium was added to the wells, and plates were shaken horizontally at 300 rpm for 4 h at room temperature (25 ± 1 °C). The plates were covered with aluminum foil and kept overnight at room temperature for equilibration. Later, the plates were centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 20 min (Jouan centrifuge BR4i). Supernatant (50 μL) was withdrawn into UV 96-well plates (Corning 96-well clear flat bottom UV-transparent microplate) for analyses with a microplate reader (Molecular Devices, USA) at the corresponding λmax of the sample. The analysis was performed in triplicate for each compound. The solubility curve of concentration (μg/mL) vs absorbance was plotted to find the saturation point, and the corresponding concentration was noted.

    hrCYP P450 Isoenzyme Assay

    hrCYP P450 isoenzymes were aliquoted at the total concentration required to conduct the study and stored at −70 °C until use. The total assay volume was adjusted to 200 μL and consists of three components: cofactors, inhibitor/vehicle, and enzyme–substrate (ES) mix. Fifty microliters of working cofactor stock solution was dispensed to all of the specified wells in a black Nunc microtiter polypropylene plate. Fifty microliters of diluted working concentrations of test compounds/positive control/vehicle was dispensed in triplicates to the specified wells as per the plate map design. The reaction plate with the cofactor and test item was preincubated at 37 ± 1 °C in a shaking incubator for 10 min. Simultaneously, the ES mix was prepared by mixing the hrCYP P450 isoenzyme. The remaining volume was made up with buffer and preincubated for 10 min at 37 ± 1 °C. One-hundred microliters of ES mix was dispensed per well as per the plate map design and incubated at 37 ± 1 °C in a shaking incubator for a predetermined time. A set of controls was incubated with hrCYP P450 isoenzymes and substrate without the test or reference item. A set of blanks was incubated with the substrate and test or reference item in the absence of hrCYP P450 isoenzymes. The reaction was terminated by adding specific quenching solutions (for CYP1A2, CYP2C19, and CYP3A4, 75 μL of 100% acetonitrile; for CYP2C9, 20 μL of 0.25 M Tris in 60% methanol; for CYP2D6, 75 μL of 0.25 M Tris in 60% methanol). The reaction was quenched by thoroughly mixing the final contents of the wells by repeated pippeting using a multichannel pipet. The product fluorescence per well was measured using a multimode reader at excitation and emission wavelengths of the respective hrCYP P450 isoenzyme fluorogenic metabolites. Data was analyzed using Excel, and the percent inhibition was calculated.(49)

    Caco-2 Permeability Assay

    A permeability study was conducted with a Caco-2 monolayer cultured for 21 days (TEER full form values >500 cm2 in each well) by adding an appropriate volume of buffer (HBSS buffer containing 10 mM HEPES) containing the test compound to the apical chamber. A test sample was taken from both the apical and basolateral chambers at 0 and 90 min after incubation at 37 °C and analyzed by LC–MS/MS. The same experiment was repeated by adding an appropriate volume of buffer (HBSS buffer containing 10 mM HEPES) containing the test compound to the basolateral chamber. The AUC defined the net influx and outflow of the test compound across the Caco-2 cell monolayer.

    Pharmacokinetics Study

    The pharmacokinetics study of compound 1 was carried out in BALB/c male mice (4–6 weeks of age) by administering the compound PO and IV at doses of 10 and 1 mg/kg, respectively. Plasma samples were collected at time points between 0 and 24 h and analyzed by LC–MS-MS. Mean plasma concentration was calculated, and data was further analyzed for PK parameters using WinNonlin 5.3.

    Maximum Tolerated Dose (MTD) Studies for in Vivo Experiments

    Swiss albino mice were used to determine the maximum tolerated dose of the compound. Compound 1 was weighed and mixed with 0.5% w/v carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) and triturated with Tween 20 (secundum artum) with gradual addition of water to make up the final concentration. Care was taken not to exceed >0.25% Tween 20 in the final formulation of compound 1. In this study, 6 animals per group were administered compound 1 at different doses for 5 days (Q1D × 5) via an intraperitoneal route. Animals were monitored for weight loss, morbidity symptoms, and mortality for up to 2 weeks, which was the end of treatment. Weight loss was considered to be significant for a mean animal weight drop of ≥10% and was considered to be highly significant when the drop was ≥20%.

    In Vivo Efficacy Study in SCID Mice

    The in vivo efficacy of compound 1 was determined in two human xenograft models: HCT-116 and NCI-H460.

    HCT-116 Experiments

    A group of 60 SCID (strain-CBySmn.CB17-Prkdcscid/J, The Jackson Laboratory, stock no. 001803) male mice weighing 18–25 g, 6–8 weeks of age, was used for the studies. Human colon carcinoma HCT-116 cells (ATCC cat. no. CCL-247) were grown in McCoy’s 5A medium supplemented with 10% FBS (Sigma-Aldrich). The cultured cells were injected subcutaneously into the dorsal side of the SCID mice at a dose of 6.6 × 106 cells in 0.2 mL of suspension. When tumor growth reached to about 4–6 mm in diameter (about 5 days), the animals were randomly divided into eight groups, with each containing 7 mice. The treatments were continued for 9 consecutive days intraperitoneally.

    NCI-H460 Experiments

    A group of 65 SCID (strain-CBySmn.CB17-Prkdcscid/J, The Jackson Laboratory, stock no. 001803) female mice weighing 15–24 g, 6–8 weeks of age, were used. Human non-small-cell lung carcinoma NCI-H460 (ATCC cat. no. HTB-177) cells were grown in RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS (Sigma-Aldrich). The cultured cells were injected subcutaneously into the dorsal side of the SCID mice at a dose of 5.3 × 106 cells in 0.2 mL of suspension. When tumor growth reached about 4–6 mm in diameter (about 6 days), the animals were randomly divided into eight groups, with each containing 6 or 7 mice.     Tumor Weight Measurements

    Tumor size was recorded at 2–5 day intervals. Tumor weight (mg) was estimated according to the formula for a prolate ellipsoid: length (mm) × width (mm)2 × 0.5, assuming specific gravity to be 1 and π to be 3. Tumor growth in compound-treated animals was calculated as T/C (treated/control) × 100%, and growth inhibition percent (% GI) was 100 – % T/C.(50-52)

    Body Weight Measurements

    Body weights of the animals in different treatment and control groups were monitored by taking the measurements daily during the treatment schedule. By considering the body weight at the start of the treatment as 100%, the percent weight loss was calculated on subsequent days of treatment.

    Statistical Analysis

    Data from each experiment was analyzed by Microsoft Excel 2000. Statistically significant differences were identified and analyzed using Student’s t test for comparisons versus control group.(50-52)

    Molecular Docking and Molecular Dynamic Simulation

    The available crystal structures of Cdk4 are in the apo-form and have several missing residues; thus, they cannot be used for molecular modeling.(53) In the present study, we used a hybrid homology model of Cdk4 described by Shafiq et al.,(32) which was developed from the Cdk4–cyclin D apo-crystal structure (PDB ID: 2W96) by incorporating positions of missing gaps and activation loops from Cdk2 (PDB ID: 1FIN).(54) This hybrid homology model was subjected to Protein Preparation Wizard for H-bond optimization, heterogeneous state generation, protonation, and overall minimization. A grid file of the docking was constructed using XYZ coordinates of the N atom of Val96, with a binding site having a 12 Å radius grid box (X = −10.521, Y = 208.683, Z = 107.944). For Cdk2 docking, the Cdk2 apoprotein (PDB ID: 1FIN) was subjected to Protein Preparation Wizard for filling missing loops and side chains (using prime), ionization, H-bond optimization, heterogeneous state generation, protonation, and overall minimization. A grid file of the docking was constructed using XYZ coordinates of the N atom of Leu83, with a binding site having a 12 Å radius grid box (X = −10.406, Y = 209.105, Z = 107.576).
    For tubulin docking, the tubulin–colchicine complex (PDB ID: 1SA0) was retrieved from the Protein Data Bank.(55) In this complex, the protein is heterodimeric in nature, consisting of two α-chains (451 residues), two β-chains (452 residues), and the Stathmin like domain (142 residues). The crystal structure was subjected to Protein Preparation Wizard for filling missing loops and side chains (using prime), ionization, H-bond optimization, heterogeneous state generation, protonation, and overall minimization. All other ligands, water, and ions were removed except colchicine. A grid file for docking was constructed considering the colchicine ligand as a centroid having a grid box of 10 Å size at the interphase of α/β-tubulin (C and D chains). All ligands were sketched in Maestro, prepared using LigPrep, and docked by Glide molecular docking software in XP mode.
    The Cdk4–compound 1 docked complex obtained from XP docking was subjected to system builder, in which TIP4P-Ew was used as an aqueous solvent model. A cubic box having a 12 Å radius was used to define the core, and the overall complex was neutralized by adding one Cl– counterion for simulation. Furthermore, this complex was minimized by steepest descent method followed by the Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno algorithm with a convergence threshold of 2.0 kcal/mol and 1000 iterations. MD simulations were carried out at normal temperature and pressure (300 K and 1.01325 bar, respectively). The thermostat and barostat method used was Langevin, with the ensemble pathway comprising NVT (constant number of particles, volume, and temperature) and the isotropic coupling method. The overall model system was relaxed before a 10 ns simulation, and Coulombic interactions were defined by a short-range cutoff radius of 9.0 Å and by a long-range smooth particle mesh Ewald tolerance to 1 × 10–9.
