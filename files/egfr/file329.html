    Medicinal Chemistry Out of Africa

    Drug discovery in Africa has, historically, largely been dependent on international funding agencies, programs, and pharmaceutical industries. This is alarming given the fact that Africa bears over 20% of the world’s disease burden. Furthermore, scientific output from Africa represents less than 1% of the global share and disproportionately emerges from South Africa and Egypt, where academic institutions as well as science and technology receive significantly more attention compared with other African nations.(1,2) Africa’s extensive biological diversity and traditional remedies have previously highlighted terrestrial and marine bioactive natural products.(3) However, modern drug discovery approaches have recently driven African medicinal chemical research outputs in more universal, relevant, and exciting directions. Since African populations carry the brunt of global infectious diseases, it is unsurprising that most publications focus heavily on diseases caused by parasitic, microbial, and viral infections such as malaria, leishmaniasis, tuberculosis, and HIV. Despite funding and technological setbacks, there have been some phenomenal efforts from African scientists that have brought valuable insights into both phenotypic and target-based drug discovery approaches, each contributing unique classes of compounds in the search for promising drug candidates.

    In this Virtual Issue, the Journal of Medicinal Chemistry and ACS Medicinal Chemistry Letters have selected and compiled 21 papers published between 2016 and 2021 that showcase outstanding medicinal chemistry research from Africa. These 21 publications represent the depth and diversity of medicinal chemistry research in Africa, with therapeutic foci ranging from infectious diseases that have traditionally plagued the continent to noncommunicable diseases such as cancer and hypertension.

    One of Africa’s greatest success stories in small molecule drug discovery is that of the team from the Drug Discovery and Development Center (H3D, http://www.h3d.uct.ac.za) at the University of Cape Town (UCT), South Africa. Previous work by this team put the first African-optimized compound into human trials for the treatment of malaria. H3D led an international project team involving the Medicines for Malaria Venture (MMV) that discovered the first ever small molecule clinical candidate, for any disease, researched on African soil by an African drug discovery center.(4) The malaria clinical candidate (MMV390048) reached phase II human trials in African patients. This work has advanced basic and clinical sciences at several levels. At a basic science level, mechanism of action studies leading to the identification of the human malaria parasite Plasmodium falciparum phosphatidylinositol 4-kinase (PfPI4K) as the target of MMV390048 were, to the best of our knowledge, the first time in which a chemical proteomics approach was used to identify a malaria drug target. At a clinical level, the progression of MMV390048 to phase II human clinical trials makes this compound the first and only PfPI4K inhibitor to have progressed to human clinical studies. Furthermore, it is noteworthy that MMV390048 is the first known Plasmodium kinase inhibitor to enter the clinic.

    Indeed, antimalarial drug research is of critical importance to prevent and treat the >220 million cases that arise globally each year and to combat resistance, particularly in Africa, which carries over 90% of the global burden of this disease.(5) The chemical series that delivered MMV390048 was originally identified via whole-cell phenotypic screening against P. falciparum, an approach that has become a popular way to discover new series of compounds active against the human malaria parasite. More recently, H3D scientists have published two notable articles on novel phenotypically identified Plasmodium-active series.

    Nchinda et al. reported structure–activity relationship (SAR) studies on 2,6-disubstituted imidazopyridine compounds with potent activity against asexual blood stage (ABS) parasites (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.8b00382). Optimization of pharmacological properties including metabolic stability and aqueous solubility translated to in vivo efficacy in the humanized P. falciparum SCID mouse model for malaria.

    Another chemical series recently explored by H3D is the diazaspiro[3.4]octanes, a novel sp3-rich scaffold, published in 2021 by Le Manach et al. (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.1c00034). SAR studies were employed to design compounds with low nanomolar ABS activity as well as potent gametocyte-sterilizing properties and transmission-blocking activity. The authors also delved into mechanistic studies via resistance selection and cross-resistance studies, which established that the P. falciparum cyclic amine resistance locus is implicated in the mechanism of resistance.

    There is a great deal of interest in understanding parasite biology and determining drug mechanisms of action (MoAs). Several recent publications from South Africa focus on specific Plasmodium drug targets, namely, hemozoin formation, microtubules, and kinases.

    During the ABS, parasites digest host hemoglobin and convert the cytotoxic heme byproduct into insoluble and inert hemozoin. This process has been the focus of countless studies, particularly as it remains a nonmutable drug target and since the common clinically used 4-aminoquinoline antimalarials are believed to act on the hemozoin formation pathway. However, there are few reports on SARs and cellular mechanisms of non-quinoline hemozoin-inhibiting antimalarials. Wicht et al. identified inhibitors of β-hematin (synthetic hemozoin) formation via high-throughput screening and evaluated two novel chemical series as tool compounds in studies that included rational design, SAR analysis, and assessment of intracellular mechanisms. The first Article focuses on a series of benzamides (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.6b00719) while the second Letter analyzes triarylimidazole derivatives (DOI: 10.1021/acsmedchemlett.6b00416). Interestingly, significantly larger amounts of free heme were required for both chemotypes to disrupt parasite growth compared with the quinolines. These reports highlight the complex relationship between compound accumulation, heme binding, and inhibition of this relevant anti-Plasmodium drug target.

    Since the publication of those reports, there have been additional studies on non-quinoline chemical series that inhibit hemozoin formation. One such study published in 2021 by Dziwornu et al. revealed a novel series of benzimidazoles with phenolic Mannich base side chains (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.1c00354). These compounds display dual asexual blood and sexual stage activities, for which SAR studies are reported. A subset of derivatives also demonstrated microtubule inhibitory activity in ABS parasites and stage II/III gametocytes. In addition, the frontrunner compound possessed high efficacy in P. berghei in vivo efficacy studies and low mammalian cell cytotoxicity.

    Plasmodium kinases are another important group of potential therapeutic targets. In 2018, González Cabrera et al. published a Perspective on parasite kinase inhibitors, focusing on calcium-dependent protein kinases (PfCDPK), cGMP-dependent protein kinase (PfPKG), glycogen synthase kinase (PfGSK), never-in-mitosis gene A (PfNIMA/NEK), and phosphatidylinositol kinases (PfPI3K and PfPI4K), among others (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.8b00329). Many of these kinase targets were identified via reverse genomic experiments, which allowed for classification of 36 kinases as essential for parasite survival in the ABS. Further phenotypic validation and kinase binding studies have also been carried out, and new inhibitors with anti-Plasmodium activity have been found via screening compound libraries against Plasmodium kinases. An important consideration when targeting kinases is sufficient safety margins since selectivity over the human ortholog can create challenges. The authors review cases where selectivity has been achieved, occasionally with the help of in silico docking against the two orthologs, and examples of medicinal chemistry for notable kinase-inhibiting series. Indeed, kinases with defined essentiality, stage specificity, and information on the mechanism of kinase function offer great potential for future development of antimalarials.

    While South Africa is at the forefront of malaria drug discovery research in Africa, other African countries have achieved notable research outputs on other parasitic diseases and vector control. An article by Knutsson et al., which includes several authors from Kenya, presented the discovery of novel phenoxyacetamide inhibitors of essential enzyme acetylcholinesterase 1 (AChE1), a validated insecticide target in mosquitoes (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.8b01060). They report on rational structure-guided design, synthesis, biological evaluation, and SARs for their chemical derivatives, which showed enzyme inhibition against both the wild type and resistant mutant, as well as moderate in vivo activity with good selectivity over human AChE. The potential of these AChE1 inhibitors for controlling mosquito species that transmit malaria, dengue, and Zika virus infections is discussed.

    Other major NTDs caused by parasitic infections in African tropical regions are leishmaniasis and human African trypanosomiasis (HAT). A recent Viewpoint by three authors from Ethiopia describes the urgent need for new effective drugs for cutaneous leishmaniasis (DOI: 10.1021/acsmedchemlett.0c00446). They discuss the disturbing statistics and symptoms of this disease as well as the lack of funding and the “fatal imbalance” against diseases critical to developing countries. Treatments, based mainly on the antimonial compounds, have been in use for over seven decades, present serious adverse effects, and are becoming less effective with the rise of resistance. Alternative treatments are limited, despite a huge disease burden and strategies to address this need are failing. As a result, there are opportunities for highly driven researchers in developing countries to identify cost-effective and sustainable solutions, particularly given the untapped biodiversity of these regions. It will be crucial to prioritize support for research institutions as well as to build partnerships and capacity in developing countries.

    Similarly, HAT, which is transmitted by the bite of tsetse flies, is treated by limited drugs that are threatened by resistance. International collaborations between scientists in Nigeria, Europe, and Japan led to an investigation on a chemical series of 2,4-dihydroxybenzoic acid- and salicylhydroxamic acid-based trypanocides with triphenylphosphonium and quinolinium lipophilic cations (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.6b01740). They synthesized derivatives with low nanomolar potency against wild type and resistant strains of trypanosomes (T. brucei and T. congolense), high selectivity, and no cross-resistance to existing trypanocidal drugs. The authors explore SARs and report on mechanistic studies, highlighting the potential for future development of this chemotype.

    Tuberculosis (TB), a disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb), continues to be one of the leading causes of death and morbidity globally. The disease claimed at least 1.2 million lives in 2019, 25% of which were from Africa.(6) African researchers have been engaged in early stage TB drug discovery to identify and progress new chemical series with potentially novel modes of action lacking cross-resistance with existing drugs.

    In one such study led by H3D, phenotypic screening against Mtb identified a cluster of 2-pyrazolylpyrimidinones with potent antitubercular activity and a novel mode of action (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.0c01727). Detailed SAR studies identified potent compounds with good physicochemical properties and in vitro microsomal metabolic stability with moderate selectivity over cytotoxicity against mammalian cell lines. While further optimization of pharmacokinetic properties is needed, this study has laid important groundwork to identify a compound suitable for in vivo efficacy studies in TB animal models.

    In another study published by H3D, a SoftFocus library of approximately 35 000 compounds was screened against Mtb (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.7b01347). Hits that were suspected to function via common or promiscuous modes of action were excluded, leading to the prioritization of a 6-dialkylaminopyrimidine carboxamide series for hit-to-lead optimization. Chemoproteomic analysis led to the identification of two putative targets of the series, both with unknown function, while initial SAR studies resulted in compounds with moderate to potent antitubercular activity and improved physicochemical properties.

    Inspired by a molecular hybridization approach, in which pharmacophores of different bioactive molecules are combined to produce hybrid compounds with improved properties compared to the parent molecules, researchers in South Africa designed and synthesized a series of styryl hydrazine thiazole hybrids based on the natural product dehydrozingerone (DOI: 10.1021/acsmedchemlett.6b00088). The in vitro antimycobacterial activity of the series was evaluated, with one compound exhibiting promising activity under hypoxic conditions and against drug-resistant isolates of Mtb. These findings highlight the benefit of incorporating a hydrazine linkage to combine the styryl portion of dehydrozingerone and the thiazole core, providing a good starting point for further lead optimization.

    Finally, a team based at the University of KwaZulu Natal in South Africa evaluated a series of N-trifluoromethylthiosulfonimidamide derivatives for antibacterial activity against Mtb and a host of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria (DOI: 10.1021/acsmedchemlett.9b00285). While two compounds showed potent antimycobacterial activity comparable to WHO-recommended first line antibiotic ethambutol, the same compounds were also found to be cytotoxic in a mammalian cell line. Matched-pair SAR analysis gave the unexpected conclusion that the trifluoromethylthio moiety was responsible for the cellular and bacterial toxicity. This observation calls for careful considerations before implementation of this functionality in compound design.

    Over the past 30 years, significant progress has been made in the treatment of human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), the causative agent of AIDS. Although there is not yet a cure for HIV infection, there are currently six main classes of antiretroviral drugs that target different stages of the viral life cycle. In low-to-middle income countries, as part of combination drug treatment, patients will usually be prescribed a non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor (NNRTI). Investigators in South Africa designed two generations of benzimidazolone derivatives as HIV NNRTIs (DOI: 10.1021/acsmedchemlett.8b00549). While the first-generation benzimidazolone inhibitors were found to be potent inhibitors of wild-type HIV reverse transcriptase, they were ineffective in the presence of common resistance mutations such as K103N and Y181C. However, a second-generation benzimidazolone inhibitor remained active against these resistant strains as well as the problematic K103N/Y181C double mutant.

    Antimicrobial resistance to existing antibiotics is a growing crisis. Not only are new antibiotics exceptionally costly to develop, but they are usually limited to a narrow spectrum of chemical diversity. Researchers in Egypt previously showed that phenylthiazoles require two important structural features necessary for antibacterial activity, viz. a nitrogenous and a lipophilic component. In this study, advantageous moieties were combined to generate a new hybrid scaffold of 5-pyrimidinylbiphenylthiazole with the objective of enhancing both activity against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and drug-like properties (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.7b00392). The enhancement in antibacterial activity of one of the analogs was validated in a C. elegans model of MRSA in which the compound generated a more pronounced decrease in MRSA burden compared to vancomycin. Despite the poor aqueous solubility and limited permeability of these compounds, they hold potential for use in topical formulations for treatment of uncomplicated skin and soft-tissue infections caused by MRSA.

    Unlike the infectious diseases describe above, noncommunicable diseases (NCDs) are not passed from person to person. The four main types of NCDs (cardiovascular diseases, cancers, chronic respiratory diseases, and diabetes) together represent the leading cause of death globally. While the WHO estimates that deaths from NCDs in Africa are likely to increase by 27% over the next decade, NCDs already cause over 50% of all reported adult deaths in some African countries such as Mauritius, Namibia, and the Seychelles.(7)

    Colorectal cancer is one of the leading causes of cancer-related deaths. A main problem for its treatment is resistance to current chemotherapies, for which the development of new drugs is urgently required. However, the success rate of new candidate cancer drugs in clinical trials is dismal. As a result, three-dimensional (3D) cell culture models have been proposed to bridge the current gap between in vitro chemotherapeutic studies and in vivo patient studies, due to shortcomings in the physiological relevance of the commonly used two-dimensional cell culture models. In a study conducted at North-West University in South Africa, LS180 colorectal cancer cells were cultured as 3D sodium alginate-encapsulated spheroids (DOI: 10.1021/acsmedchemlett.0c00076). The model was viable for up to at least 20 days and revealed the development of resistance to the treatment in the same way as previously observed in patients. Hence, these results indicated that the in vitro LS180 sodium alginate-encapsulated spheroid model can be used for future evaluation of the efficacy of chemotherapeutic compounds.

    It has now been well established that modulators of carbonic anhydrase (CA) metalloenzymes represent an important class of anticancer agents. Researchers in Egypt synthesized novel benzofuran-based carboxylic acid derivatives featuring benzoic or hippuric acid moieties connected via an ureido linker (DOI: 10.1021/acsmedchemlett.0c00094). Some benzofuran-containing carboxylic acid derivatives acted as submicromolar CA IX inhibitors with a selective inhibitory profile over the off-target isoforms CA I and II. These compounds were also examined for their antiproliferative action against human breast cancer cell lines, and one of these compounds, in particular, displayed promising antiproliferative, cell cycle disturbance, and proapoptotic actions in cells.

    The Hippo signaling pathway facilitates the degradation of the oncogenic transcriptional coactivator, Yes kinase-associated protein (YAP). Dysregulation of this pathway is associated with tumorigenesis and cancer progression. The authors of this study identified a closely related derivative of an oxadiazole-based EGFR/c-Met dual inhibitor, differing only at its terminal amide moiety, which exhibited cogent antitumor activities while devoid of the original compound’s capacity for EGFR/c-Met degradation (DOI: 10.1021/acsmedchemlett.9b00501). This derivative exhibited a unique ability to facilitate YAP degradation through the activation of LATS1, although the underlying mechanism remains unclear. Oral administration of the compound was effective in suppressing xenograft tumor growth in mice and showed a satisfactory safety profile. The compound identified in this study may aid in the advancement of new therapeutic strategies for cancer treatment by restoring the Hippo pathway regulatory function to facilitate YAP degradation.

    Celecoxib is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) approved for the treatment of pain and swelling associated with rheumatoid arthritis or osteoarthritis. Through systematic rounds of chemical modifications of celecoxib and iterative target-directed screening, this study identified a novel series of trisubstituted pyrazolines as potent and selective phosphodiesterase 5 (PDE5) inhibitors that lack cyclooxygenase type 2 (COX2) inhibitory activity (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.0c01120). These results reveal a novel strategy to inhibit PDE5 with unprecedented potency and isozyme selectivity.

    Nuclear hormone receptors, including the liver X receptors, represent a large family of ligand-activated transcription factors that have emerged as important drug targets. In a Miniperspective (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.8b00045), the authors highlight advances from the past decade in the development of synthetic liver X receptor modulators with a particular emphasis on SAR of the most active and promising modulators. They note that advances in knowledge of receptor structure and function help to move the field forward, as these can be leveraged to inform target-based design approaches.

    Inhibitors of the angiotensin-I converting enzyme (ACE), a zinc metalloprotease, have been developed to treat hypertension and cardiovascular disease. To overcome nonselective inhibition of both catalytic domains of ACE, which results in clinically adverse effects, a diprolyl chemical series was modeled using docking simulations (DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.7b01478). The series was expanded based on key target interactions involving residues known to impart N-domain selectivity. Several diprolyl compounds were synthesized and tested for N-domain-selective ACE inhibition, with one compound displaying potent inhibition and 84-fold more selectivity toward the N-domain. A high-resolution crystal structure of the complex with the N-domain revealed the molecular basis for the observed selectivity, which could serve as an excellent starting point for further SAR studies to develop a viable N-domain-selective ACE inhibitor.

    These publications reveal the exciting yet nascent state of medicinal chemistry research in Africa. While research funding in sub-Saharan Africa comprises only 0.5% of the region’s GDP compared to a global average of 1.8%,(8) an anticipated increase in the use of computational tools and artificial intelligence is likely to accelerate and democratize medicinal chemistry research, leading to further important contributions from the continent.

    We are proud to bring you these preeminent publications from Africa and hope that you will be encouraged by the world-class medicinal chemistry research that is taking place on our continent. We take this opportunity to thank scientists based in Africa, who have made many notable contributions to the Journal of Medicinal Chemistry and ACS Medicinal Chemistry Letters over the years as authors and reviewers.
